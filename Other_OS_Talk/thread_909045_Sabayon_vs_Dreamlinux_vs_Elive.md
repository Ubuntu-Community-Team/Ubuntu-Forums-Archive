### Post by Tux Aubrey on 2008-09-03
Those are, IMO, three very different distros.  Sabayon is Gentoo based and very fully featured.  I've not used it except as a Live DVD.

DreamLinux is Debian based with a Xfce WM and it is fairly light.  I tried the most recent release and found it buggier than earlier ones. I think they are having a few translation problems with the English language version (it is Brazilian). It is very beautiful but I think there are "better" Xfce distros (Like SAM - which is a lightweight PCLinuxOS and so similar to Mandriva at its core)

eLive is also Debian-based and lightweight.  It was the distro that initially turned me on to Enlightenment but I think there are better implementations now (Maryan, OpenGEU and OzOS - which are all Ubuntu-based and use a more up to date and stable e17).

### Post by handy on 2008-09-03
On my 2nd machine, which has drive drawers & runs multiple OS's; I run Sabayon 3.4f, specifically for Cedega & the Oblivion game, as all of the 3.4 versions of Sabayon handle my graphic card from hell straight up & quickly, even the liveCD gets it right!  Sabayon handles the hardware on that machine better than any other Linux distro' or BSD I have tried.

The current version of DL, fails when X has to deal with my graphics card.  I could get past it with some work, but I couldn't be bothered.  The previous versions of DL installed perfectly on the same machine & impressed me, though I still personally prefer Sabayon.  By the way, the latest Sabayon 3.5 fails when X has to deal with my graphics card.  Though I don't care, 3.4f runs Oblivion fine, which is all Sabayon has to do for me on that machine.

I recommend whatever distro' suits your hardware, because in the end hardware compatibility is the crux of the biscuit (as Frank Zappa once said).  One distro' (in my 2nd machine's case, Sabayon 3.4) will just install & recognize all of your hardware, & another can give you days of brain ache, as you endeavor to solve the problems presented.

Your choice, the hard way or the easy way.

Personally I like Arch, because the installation process demands that you customize Arch to suit yourself, nothing more or less, & if you follow the [***_Arch Beginners Guide_***]("http://wiki.archlinux.org/index.php/Beginners_Guide"), it is really quite straight forward, even easy.
