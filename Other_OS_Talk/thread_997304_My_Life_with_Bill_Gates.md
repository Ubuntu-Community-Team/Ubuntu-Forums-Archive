### Post by JaniceMorgan on 2008-11-29
My Life with Bill Gates – and how I learned to love Unix (and Mac)

My life began actually before Bill Gates and before his products were known in the public – which means that we talk about the late 60ies up to 1981. (Personally I’m very old and much older than Bill). That was a time with mainframes and strange people (today called nerds), talking about UNIX, CP/M, Basic, Assembler, Pascal and processors like Intel 8080, Motorola 68000, Zilog Z80 and some new companies like Microsoft and Apple – however mostly “Buzz” words or names only a lucky few had the slightest idea what was and what they could do.

The turning point came in 1976 with Microsoft and the IBM PC in the early 80ies – and with DOS. On top of DOS came then the Windows Graphic User Interface, from 1985. Since those years, people started using PCs and Microsoft products in particular. I did too – even it still was pioneering years.

From the early 90ies the new computer wave took speed and almost all operating system market share landed in the Microsoft basket with its Windows 3, 95, 98, and so on, including their word processing, excel, etc. I believe that their global peak market share has been as high as impressing 98% in 2005 – but nowadays decreased to some 90% - a trend that seems to continue.

In 2005 the computer world however came to a slow change. Many were tired of the Microsoft dominance and bad quality, but alternatives were still sporadic, complicated and mostly incompatible. The new turning point came primarily from the UNIX world. The Linux people (finally) realized that without a new Graphic User Interface they would never win or be accepted. At the same time Apple changed to the Intel processor platform (a PC type of computer) with their new MAC OS X operating system (at the moment called Leopard). Where or how all this will end needs a fortuneteller.

I’m definitely not that fortuneteller, but would like to tell how it worked and works for me, as I’m one of those who changed habit. OK, maybe a little easier for me than most, as I have been around all of the “computer age” and tried and tested it all over the past 5 decades. I always loved to hate Windows, but used it since it was widely compatible with all kind of PCs, the peripheral and it had a wide spectrum of all the different software I used to use.

The main Windows problems were and still are: All its limitations in handling files, file names, RAM size, storage systems, etc. I always felt that Windows were similar to a house build on mud, but in need to be made roomier, higher and expanded all the time – with internal and external problems easy imaginable. 

Even 40 years old next year, it took UNIX far too long to get “user friendly” in the world of PC users. Apple (Mac) however always did a lot to it and Linux in the late years has done a lot too. My first new century experiments were with all the new and different Linux versions – of which I after intensive testing find Ubuntu an outstanding development. The same goes for the free and ever better Open Office from Sun Microsystems – a real good Microsoft Office competitor, also under Windows – or I believe – under any operating system.

After looking close upon the Mac change to Intel in 2005, I (in 2006) bought my first Mac as a “test” product, to get some further experience and to realize where the limitations were. As my work demands total compatibility with the Windows world, this aspect was of major importance.

The past years I have learned to love my Mac. Even an experienced computer user, it took me some month before I got totally used to the differences in use of the hardware as well as the software. Apart from the slightly higher price you have to pay for a Mac computer, you mostly get a slightly better overall quality; even I still miss some of the latest PC (especially Notebook) practicalities or “gimmicks” on my Mac. Why the hell do they still ignore an onboard memory card reader? I hope Apple will learn to keep up with that as well. To me it seems that they increasingly try to meet (or even exceed) the market needs.

What about Windows? I still have one HP Notebook running Vista and one own build desktop running XP. I also still have only one Mac, but not for long, as I’m in the market for two new – the new multi many processor MacPro and the new MacBook Pro 17”, both expected to arrive early 2009. Beside that I have one 4 year old Dell desktop and one 7 year old Dell Notebook – both now running Ubuntu. The old Dell Notebook is funny, because a Windows based computer is normally totally outdated after 2-3 years. Replacing Windows with e.g., Ubuntu on these “old” computers gives you all the original speed and power back, as any UNIX based operative environment has very low demand on processor speed, memory and storage.  My advice: Don’t kick old PCs out. Installing Ubuntu is fast, simple and free and it includes a bunch of practical and good software too. Any basic needs is fully covered here – and its easy to learn.

More about Windows.  Most people and experts says that Windows XP was the best Windows ever. Yeah, but its still and definitely an operating system on crutches – in this case maybe only two, where older ones needed 3 or 4 crutches (referring primarily to Windows 2000). The most terrible issue is that the “new” Vista simply is: Terrible – and on at least 3 crutches. It’s just a nicer colored version of the same instability as all the former ones had – and even slower and or more demanding. On top of that its only able to address less than 3GB RAM – and that’s really great nowadays… where almost any Notebook is supplied with 4GB RAM. OK, Microsoft claims that its Vista 32bit version can address 4GB RAM. True or not true, the common user will only read between 2.7GB and 3.5GB if he/she checks.

OK, believe me – I know what I talk about. Any Windows version offers you everything from blue screens, overall sorts of in-understandable error messages, useless help, instability to in-compability, ton of driver problems, takes l-o-n-g time to boot (great time for coffee making), long time to shut down, updates and service packs which destroys your networking, connectivity, drivers, etc. Everybody using any kind of new software or hardware knows that, and is happy if this day’s session goes without a crash.

What’s wrong at Microsoft? Is one of the biggest companies in the world not able to make good products? Yes and no. Microsoft is not able to produce an operating system able to serve a modern computer – and I have my own private theory about that: When we got Windows 3, 95 and 98, all the PC based computers wasn’t better than that. During the first part of the 2000 years, PCs improved and developed very fast – with more processors, graphics, memory, storage and overall. Since Windows is based upon a “dead end” or muddy fundament, it’s not able to keep up (sorry for the words Bill). We have seen that very clearly with Vista and I now play fortuneteller – and risk to forecast that we will se the same pattern with the in 2009/2010 Windows 7.  

The best thing a Windows user can do is never to make updates… and that’s not a way to work.
Sure the third party software industry is happy with Windows, as they can develop and develop an ever increasing number of Windows maintenance utilities you cant live without – to repair this and that. From registry to all sort of daily cleaning, since Windows never do any “house-cleaning”.  I’m very familiar with those cleaning processes, since I have always been installing and de-installing lots of software – and Windows leaves a mess after such. Last bad thing I would like to say about Windows is, that it always becomes slower and slower day-by-day. If you wish to keep its “speed”, you will have to make a clean re-install once a year.

Do I think Microsoft only makes ****? No, they do one of the worlds best “Office” programs, if not the best. Damn expensive when you compare with what’s out there for free – and especially Open Office. I always was of the opinion that those guys developing Microsoft Office should be the guys making the operating system. Microsoft also makes other (not bad) software. I have used their Microsoft Mobile in several generations of PDAs and Mobile Phones. It works, but I’m not impressed. Maybe Linux is the one to watch here too. The iPhone already is established, but there are still lots of room for improvement on the hardware side (no consumer changeable battery, no memory card slot, limited camera quality) – apart from their (most) stupid way to sell it. No wonder its one of the most hacked hardware products nowadays.

The funny thing about Microsoft Office is, that it’s a very popular product for Macs (the Office 200eight). Personally I would love to see the Mac version to be a little closer to the one for PCs (the Office 2007). Microsoft also makes a selection of some pretty (acceptable) good hardware products.

Even UNIX is designed 40 years ago, it seems to me to be the only realistic basis platform for “small computers” in the years to come – unless somebody develops a brand new and even better operating system from scratch.

To you who consider to go away from Windows (I know you are many out there). Apart from the higher price for the Mac, it’s worth all the money. Like to use Mac OS X  (Leopard) on PC instead? Legally it’s not possible and illegally it’s still not working very well. The Mac OS X is still too different (e.g., BIOS demand), but I believe that future versions will be more “PC compatible” – Apple like it or not. We have already seen some Mac clones out there. I would install on a PC, but if Apple is not becoming too nasty with their hardware pricing, I will probably not.

Maybe you can learn from my Mac experience, what it can do and does. I already said that I’m constantly installing and testing lots of software. All sort of – and it has so far not created one single hiccup. The Mac OS X never offers you any stupid messages or errors – and I’m sure not missing them. It’s speedy up and down. Up in less than 30 sec and down in maybe 5-10 sec. I never understood people who close their Mac (the lid) to make it sleep.

My Mac runs Windows (Vista and XP) faster than any fresh installed Windows on a PC. I have less from its crazy messages and so far no blue screen or freeze-in or similar. Here I run some very few applications not available for Mac yet (some CAD and other tech software). There are basically two routes to go, running Windows (and other OS) on a Mac – one is using a program called Parallel and the other is using VMware. I use the last one and never tried the other. There are many advantages using this kind of so-called virtual desktop. You can run almost any (if not ANY) operating system and or application, without getting it mixed up with what else you do. You have full exchange between the Mac OS X and whatever you run as and on the virtual desktop. I also use VMware on PC, but it’s primarily for fun – and legally it will not run Mac OS X. Its possible to get software (e.g., CrossOver) enabling you to run (some) Windows software directly under the Mac OS X, but I prefer the “virtual solution”. When I tell people – or show them how easy it is to run everything on a Mac, they don’t believe it – but it is fact. 

So, on my Mac I can do everything I could on a PC with Windows – plus a lot more. Today I have XP, Vista, the coming Windows 7, the old “retro” Windows 3.1, IBM OS/2 (anybody remember that?), Ubuntu and Solaris 10 on my Mac. This is probably not the end of it. If you are into games and believe that will only work in Windows – you’re wrong – the world is rapidly increasing the number of titles released for Mac. The trend is further that most new software is developed on a so-called universal platform enabling any software to be installed under Windows or Mac.

Do ever never try to use such utilities as a boot manger for different operating systems on Mac – or any other computer running something else for that matter. Keep your partitions clean and with one operation system only. That will keep you free of any kind of trouble or messy partitions or hard drive. A virtual manager is much more flexible and you can change everything as often as you like.


Final words: Changing habits is not always a piece of cake. You have to spend a little effort, as some of the Mac functions are little different from what you are used to. You will probably be positively surprised how little and less it is. I find it however interesting to switch between PC, Mac and different operating systems. After a little while its comparable to drive/have different cars – you can do it in your sleep.

Traveling in e.g. Europe working on my Mac in meetings or elsewhere, I meet many comments like: “Is it still possible to buy Mac?” or “ Mac is for girls, students and gays only!”. My normal answer is: “In a few years from now you will run Mac too”.
John Morgan
