Woody, you are operating on the assumption that every new idea is a good idea. I can understand someone wanting to put out their own "distro shingle" (hell, I want to as well!), but as others have already said, RL's idea has been done already.

You have, at this point:

[LIST]
[*]Linux Mint: For an alternate take on the Ubuntu desktop.
[*]Ubuntu Studio: For the creative types.
[*]Ultimate: For those who want it all, now.
[*]Ubuntu MCE: Drop in Media Center version.
[*]Ubuntu Gamer: Planned release for the gamer set.
[/LIST]

Add to this Lin/Freespire, Mepis, SymphonyOS, and all of the other distros sprouting up using Ubuntu as a base, and you really need to have:

1. A good idea.
2. A niche that noone else is serving. 
3. Dedication.
4. Knowledge of what it takes to service a distribution.

Now, let's examine the idea itself.

RLbuntu would be, to my understanding:

1. Similar in size to the Ubuntu distribution.
2. Contain all of the multimedia codecs.
3. Contain all of the popular Multimedia, Office, Graphics, Games, and Education apps.

Do you see a disconnect here? Ultimate does that, and the disc size is >700mb. So much for keeping the size down. And #2 can get you in legal hot water, particularly if you include mp3 encoders and libdvdcss. 

What is the size of a base install, without a desktop? 250-300mb? Ok, let's say it's 300mb. Add Xorg, and you are at 350mb let's say. Add a desktop, let's say we go light and chose Icewm, that's 360mb. Add Open Office, and that's 400+mb...let's say 450mb. 

So, assuming you are using an alternate type install, you have 250mb to do all of what RL wants to do to fit on a normal CD. That's very tight. If you do a Live CD, you still run up against size limitations, because you need to have a fairly stripped install to get all that done. 

So, what people are saying is: it's not possible, given the parameters. I think most people here are supportive of new projects, but the project lead needs to be realistic about what they are trying to achieve, and their end goals.

### Post by Tux Aubrey on 2007-07-29
I agree with posters who have raised issues about objectives and technical skills here.  There have been heaps of Ubuntu-based rebuilds already (I can't quite bring myself to elevate Reconstructor projects to the status of "distro", sorry).  Many were started with plausible and worthy aims.  Very few, IMHO, will make their first anniversary - which is probably a good thing but disheartening for those who have invested so much time and energy in their project.

The idea of just replacing the default apps with others or including non-free stuff by default are the worst possible reasons to start a new "version".   

And Ubuntu's release cycle is relentless.  No sooner have you put together a suite of tools than you are rebuilding it for the next release (or getting so far behind you loose your users).

Look at Fluxbuntu - IMO one of the better and more grounded attempts at a genuinely different Ubuntu.  It is still stuck as a beta on a 6.06 base having been passed on the straight by both edgy and feisty.  Since Joe Jaxx started that project, he has been overtaken not only by Ubuntu itself but by a host of others with similar aims (but not necessarily off a pure Ubuntu base).  I have been trying Mepis's Antix lately and it fits almost exactly with what I was hoping Fluxbuntu would be.

The OP and others of a similar mind should familiarise themselves with what is already out there (I put together a list - which is no doubt already out of date - [here]("http://cafelinux.org/distropedia/?q=node/76#galaxylist")).

Surely joining one of the projects that has already got off the ground and has a roadmap would be a better use of time and enthusiasm.  Maybe Dr Mo and MetalMusicAddict could use some help with Ubuntu Studio?  That's another one that at least is offering something different from just more and different apps.
