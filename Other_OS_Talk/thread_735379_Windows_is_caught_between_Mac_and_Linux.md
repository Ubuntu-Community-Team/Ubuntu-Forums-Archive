### Post by JAPrufrock on 2008-03-26
MS is getting squeezed from both ends.  Apple is increasing market share from the high end- the fast, expensive, state-of-the-art machines bundled with OSX.  At the same time Linux is squeezing from the bottom end because it is cheap ($0) and can run on slower and less memory-hungry machines.  Of course Linux has the disadvantage of not having a lot of the killer apps that MS OSs have developed for them (like the Adobe stuff).  Same with printer drivers, wireless drivers, etc.  When Linux comes pre-installed, like on Asus eee, some of the driver problems go away.  However, there's still the apps problem.  But that will go away when the Linux distros capture enough of the OS market share for companies to start writing software for Linux.  The question is, when will that happen?  I don't know, but I'm sure MS is looking nerviously through it's rear view mirror.

note: the apps problem isn't as big as some people think.  The Gimp is a great graphics tool for most users.  I read that the next soon to be released open office is supposedly much improved, and competitive with MS Office.  Firefox is a great browser, better than IE in my opinion.  So let those users who need a special killer app stay with MS.  The rest of the world can move to Linux and use the money that they save for something more important than bloated sofware.

Let me start by saying I have no real problem with Windows (at least not XP) and I recognize Microsoft's contributions to information technology.  That said...

I recently read an academic book concerning the effect of media on culture, and one part dealt with technology (specifically, the "new" technology that was about to take over the world when the book was written in 1996)

One of the most important lines from the book:  "Every technology has a philosophy, expressed in how we use it ..."  The philosophy (or one of many philosophies) of the internet, like it or not, is "free information," in every sense of the phrase.  This will sound crazy to some, but Microsoft is philosophically irrelevant.  

This doesn't mean that it's going anywhere anytime soon, only that its business model and past behavior (fighting over patents and restricting computer usage to what it deems acceptable in the fine print of its license documentation, for example) are at odds with the so-called philosophy of the internet.  I can't speak for Mac, though it's safe to say their philosophy is not exactly geared towards flexibility or freedom with regards to hardware.

This was what I meant earlier about the philosophy of open source driving web 2.0 -- everything is free and collaborative
