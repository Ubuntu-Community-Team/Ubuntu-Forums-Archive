### Post by spectrevk on 2008-05-13
> **arman.haghi said:**
> I second that; more GNU/Linux distros give us choices, hardly about "threats" to others.

The only threat I see is distros that disregard the call to have free-only software; they show an advantage to a newbie user over distros who listen to the call and are free of non-free software. Thoughts?

Isn't that a charge levelled at Ubuntu for using proprietary drivers? Frankly, I don't think that's entirely fair, speaking as someone who is a recent adopter of Ubuntu (I have a few aborted past attempts at getting into unix/linux OSes). The purpose of an OS is to serve the user, not the other way around; someone who has a piece of hardware that needs a proprietary driver shouldn't be penalized for owning the "wrong" hardware. I feel the same way about non-free file formats like DivX encoded AVIs. They're extremely common in some communities, and new users are going to be judging an OS experience by how much works right out of the box, and how easy it is to add new features.

Having to search forums for a workaround just to get your wireless card working, or to be able to play half of the video files you brought over from your windows system is, simply put, a negative user experience. I understand and respect not including non-free software in the base installation, but making exceptions for hardware drivers seems reasonable, and I certainly would like to see a straightforward method for adding those file formats without a newbie user having to search through support documentation. At the very least, including that info in the initial documentation would be good. I don't mind searching for info, but that's just how I roll. What about someone who just wants their DVD playback to work out of the box? That doesn't seem unreasonable. Yes, opposing these closed file formats is a noble cause, but the fight is with the companies that own the formats, and the content producers who use the formats, not the end-user. The end-user experience should be seamless, IMO.
