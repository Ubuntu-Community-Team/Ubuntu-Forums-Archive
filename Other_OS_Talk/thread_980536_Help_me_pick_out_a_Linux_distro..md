### Post by L815 on 2008-11-12
Here are my recommendations based on my experience:

- Debian: Stable depending on which version you get. It is vanilla, meaning you get a base gnome, and at times, have configure things yourself.

- Fedora: Bleeding-edge, not always stable, and you have to get used to rpm. Package repo doesn't seem that large in comparison to debian based ones.

- Linux Mint: Like Ubuntu, easier to use. Has codecs installed already, and a few mint tools to help you configure your system. Very novice friendly.

- Arch: This distro is fun if you have time to build your system. Install is command line, and everything is configure by you. But once it is configure, it is easy to pick up. Packman (package manager) is easy to work with.

Gentoo is not easy, no matter how you look at it.  I personally think that the graphical installer is more trouble than it's worth - they should have stuck with stage 3 installs.  Gentoo isn't the stablest system either - it tracks the bleeding edge pretty closely.

These are the stablest distros I've tried, with comments:
[B]
Slackware[/B]: Slackware is my overall favorite distro.  It's stable, powerful, and vanilla.  However, it isn't easy.  Slackware is usually considered **the** distro for learning about Linux, because it offers vanilla packages and totally manual configuration.

**Debian**: There are many things I don't like about Debian, and I'd never use it on my personal system.  However, these are personal quibbles, and you may like it for its vast repos and solid base system.

**CentOS**: This free Red Hat clone is extremely solid and ready to roll.  Designed for the enterprise, this distro is made to last.  It isn't quite as easy as Ubuntu or Mandriva, but it's easier than Slackware.

**DesktopBSD**: Though not Linux, both FreeBSD and NetBSD are very stable.  However, they are probably a bit hard for your purposes.  DesktopBSD provides FreeBSD with a GUI out-of-the-box.  Hardware support is a bit behind Linux, but this is a very good and very solid system.

**Vector**: Based off Slackware, Vector is solid.  All the versions provide a thorough, complete experience.  Reasonably easy for a newish user.  It's packages can be a bit old, but that's the price of stablity.

### Post by sultanoswing on 2008-11-13
I use and love Arch linux on my desktop (Ubuntu on my laptop). I highly recommend giving Arch a try - it's the 'ground-up-only-what-you-want' simplicity and efficiency of Gentoo without the hassle and error-prone-ness of Gentoo and compiling everything from source (although my mate who uses Gentoo just couldn't get into Arch - he prefers the minutiae of setting every c-flag and kernel option etc.).

Arch's biggest advantage for me is that it's a rolling release i.e. you get OpenOffice 3, Firefox 3, newest kernels, latest GNOME etc. without waiting for the next milestone release like in Ubuntu or Fedora. I also haven't had any major problems due to 'bleeding edge', like I did last time I tried Fedora i.e. Fedora 9. 

You'll also get to learn a good deal about how your OS works which will ultimately help you to keep it working - kind of like learning a bit about maintaining your own car!

Have a look:
[http://wiki.archlinux.org/index.php/Arch_Linux](http://wiki.archlinux.org/index.php/Arch_Linux) (What is Arch)
[http://wiki.archlinux.org/index.php/Arch_vs_Others](http://wiki.archlinux.org/index.php/Arch_vs_Others) (Arch Compared to Other Distros)
