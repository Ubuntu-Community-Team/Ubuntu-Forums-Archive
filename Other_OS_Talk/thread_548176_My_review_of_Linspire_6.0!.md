Steer clear of Linspire 6.0!
Sep 09 '07

The Bottom Line Steer clear of these guys.

History of Linspire

Linspire is a company that makes a proprietary Linux operating system of the same name.

The company started out several years ago, calling their operating system Lindows, and their website was set up to give the appearance that Lindows was an entirely new operating system, designed by them (hardly a mention of Linux to be found), and that they were designing this operating system in order to be mostly Microsoft Windows compatible.

Well, back at the time (around 2001 if I remember right), I was dual booting Windows Me (for games), with Mandrake Linux Gaming Edition (shipped with Transgaming WineX), and decided to check LindowsOS out, since they were giving the preview builds away for free.

What I found in Lindows, was that it was really a modified copy of Debian Linux, running a modified version of the K Desktop Environment, set up with the free/open source WINE (Windows like subsystem for Unix-like systems), and the main difference between it, and my copy of Mandrake Linux, was primarily that Lindows had a proprietary package manager, with a lot of commercial software, to sell you, and that you further needed to pay them a subscription fee to access their package repository, even to download free software, or to have access to the privilege of paying for closed-source commercial software.

If you're in for a laugh, I found the complete original Lindows home page (January 19, 2002) in the Wayback Machine at [www.archive.org:](www.archive.org:)

[http://web.archive.org/web/20020119144709/http://lindows.com/](http://web.archive.org/web/20020119144709/http://lindows.com/)

So after the free preview version was over with...

Lindows started demanding that you pay them $49.99 for a digital download, or $59.99 to get a box with a manual and the CD-ROM, after you got that all set up, you were also expected to pay them (if I recall right), $39.99 a year to access their software repository, or $49.99 a year to be eligible for a discount on the for-pay software in their repository (I have to pay more to get a discount?).

Well, needless to say, I was not having any of that, and continued right on using Mandrake Linux, right up through version 10.1, even going so far as to even pay money for boxed sets with official Mandrake support, as well as their whole software repository on the DVD (which is a God-send if you're on dial up). (By the way, if you want a box set of their new Mandriva Linux, you have to buy it from them, because Microsoft strong armed Staples and Best Buy into not selling Linux sets anymore.)

But back to Lindows, so here comes Microsoft, suing Lindows for trademark infringement (LindowsOS might confuse customers wanting Microsoft Windows, yeah, right!), anyway, that lawsuit turned into a settlement, with Microsoft paying Linspire (new name) a couple cool million to change their name.

Linspire released version 5.0 (My review):

[http://www.epinions.com/content_296356908676](http://www.epinions.com/content_296356908676)

Anyway, long story short, Linspire 5 was buggy, broken, slow, and I couldn't get it to work with most of my hardware (including my ethernet port), the only reason I tried it was because I had a special electronic coupon code letting me download it free of charge.

Fast forward to where Linspire is now (September 2007)

After signing one of those shady "patent deals" with Microsoft, and deciding that Debian's glacial development pace is difficult to schedule their own releases after, Linspire decides to "build" (see; break) the Ubuntu Linux operating system, which is what I presently use, and releasing version 6.0 of Linspire based on it.

But don't let that fool you into thinking that they have nothing but love for Ubuntu, after hijacking 99% of their operating system to base Linspire on...

Here are some quotes, and my replies: ([http://www.linspire.com/products_linspire_whatis.php](http://www.linspire.com/products_linspire_whatis.php))

"It begins where the others end..."

Ubuntu ends where it ends because for all practical purposes, it is a complete system already, Linspire might tweak this or add that, or change the names of some of the software packages around a little bit, but if they add anything at all, it's their proprietary Click 'n Run APT frontend, and some fluff that is a proprietary program that replicates the free/open source functionality of Ubuntu.

"We add CNR, with over 20,000 software packages for easy, one click installation"

Pulling up Synaptic Package Manager in Ubuntu, I have 21,107 packages at my fingertips, for easy, one click installation.

"We add KDE, for an easy-to-use, and comfortable interface for Mac and Windows users!"

[http://www.kubuntu.org](http://www.kubuntu.org)

If I already use Ubuntu:

I open my terminal and type "sudo aptitude install kubuntu-desktop" and hit enter.

And now I have KDE! (Although I much prefer GNOME, thank you.)

"We add enhanced applications...modified copies of Firefox and Thunderbird...along with Linspire-sponsored programs like Nvu, and Lsongs and Lphoto."

Why do Firefox and Thunderbird need "modified", what did you do to them?

Lsongs and Lphoto are unnecessary, Ubuntu has F-Spot photo manager, The Gimp, and Rhythmbox.

Nvu is a nice program, for what you see is what you get web page editing, standards compliant nonetheless, based off Mozilla Composer, not that Linspire would tell you they didn't write the thing.

Oh look, I found a DEB package for Ubuntu here:

[http://www.getdeb.net/search.php?keywords=nvu](http://www.getdeb.net/search.php?keywords=nvu)

Kompozer, an ENHANCED version of Nvu, for Ubuntu.

NEXT!

We Add Proprietary Software!

Which is a major reason for me deciding to leave Windows?

What do they add?

JAVA, Adobe Flash, Acrobat Reader.

Let's see, Ubuntu has open source PDF reader, so I won't need Acrobat, Firefox offers me a choice between GNU GNASH and Adobe Flash (I use Flash, but the open source GNASH is getting better), and JAVA, which comes either by installing it's package in Ubuntu, or by installing the Ubuntu Restricted Extras metapackage, which also gives me access to Microsoft fonts, codecs for every type of video and audio file I can think of, and more.

"We also give you one-click access to PURCHASE proprietary software, such as Parallels, Win4Lin, StarOffice, and more..."

Come to think of it, I do use Sun StarOffice, there was a glitch in Staples' cash register system and I got it for a penny, but Ubuntu has the open source version of Star Office (Open Office) which is 98% identical, and is what Linspire comes with, unless you pay for Star Office.

Parallels and Win4Lin, require you to BUY A WINDOWS LICENSE, to run in a virtual machine, and with Vista, it is illegal to virtualize a copy of Windows unless you buy the $400 ULTIMATE EDITION. Yummy!

When I want to run Windows software, WINE typically does just dandy (and it's free), although there are proprietary versions of WINE called Cedega and Crossover Linux.

All of them emulate Windows API's on Linux, but Cedega is optimized for games, Crossover mainly supports office applications (but will run some games, like Half-Life 2 and World of Warcraft), and part of that price is official support.

I did pay for Transgaming Cedega (I mentioned it under it's old name, WineX, earlier), and guess what, they sent me a nice DEB package built for Ubuntu.

We Add Codecs,
Adding proprietary codecs expands software compatibility and provides a better overall experience so users can play MP3 files, watch DVDs, play Windows Media files, watch QuickTime movie trailers, and more."

Get the Ubuntu Restricted Extras package, in Add/Remove, in the pull down menu select "view all programs", then type "restricted", just check the package, and hit Apply.

You can get free DVD playback by installing LibDVDCSS (there are DEB packages around for that, but Ubuntu can't officially give them out.)

Linspire tries to sell you a copy of Intervideo LinDVD.

We Add Drivers
Adding proprietary software drivers expands hardware compatibility and provides optimum 3D graphic card support, better WiFi support, and more.

System/Administration/Restricted Driver Manager, click, oh, now I have video.

Open a terminal, sudo apt-get install bcm43xx-fwcutter, now I have wireless.

Speaking of which, even though Linspire had all those proprietary drivers, it STILL failed to detect my display, or my wi-fi in the laptop.

We Add Confidence...Microsoft IP Protection

All other Linux distributions are illegal pirate h4x0r operating systems, we bend over for Bill Gates!

We take the money you paid us, and paid Microsoft protection money in their little racketeering operation, for patents they likely don't have, and would certainly never challenge Linux with in court.

Properly acquiring the blahbity, legal, (snore), codecs, walkitywalkwalk, I care?

Survey Says?

Linspire 6.0 is kind of like Kubuntu, that supposedly just works out of the box, but usually really doesn't.

Linspire costs $49.99 to download, or $59.99 for a CD, Canonical will mail you a Kubuntu CD free of charge.

Linspire doesn't have Synaptic or Adept, instead forcing your dependence on a proprietary software installer.

Freespire is the "community" version of Linspire, it's essentially Kubuntu rigged with Click 'n Run and no support from Linspire.

I guess technically Linspire qualifies as a "Linux" based operating system, but instead of promoting freedom, it goads the user into buying more licenses for more broken, closed source, proprietary software.

Linspire makes the baby Jesus cry.

Nothing to see here, move along....
