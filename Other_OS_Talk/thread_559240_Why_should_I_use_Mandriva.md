### Post by AdamWill on 2007-09-27
I'd say the most obvious thing Mandriva has that Ubuntu doesn't is the Mandriva configuration tools. Ubuntu doesn't have many of its own configuration tools, it relies mainly on third party ones (which certainly do a good job as far as they go, and to which Ubuntu developers do contribute). Mandriva has a fairly wide ranging set of home-grown tools which have been written over the course of Mandriva's existence (since 1998 ) and cover a lot of ground you can't really cover on Ubuntu without hunting down obscure third party utilities that may not necessarily have been customised to Ubuntu, or by doing things manually by editing configuration files.

Other than that, Mandriva has more extensive backports repositories. These are repositories containing updated versions of popular applications for the latest stable release. Mandriva also has (I believe, though obviously I'm biased :>) a slightly better hardware detection database.

There are other differences, but those are what I'd say are the most obvious. And of course, I wouldn't be the person to tell you what Ubuntu might do better than Mandriva. :D

What you're doing is the most sensible thing: try Mandriva out and see if it offers you anything Ubuntu doesn't. You may find it does, you may find it doesn't, you may love it, you may hate it. But hey, Ubuntu and Mandriva are both free, so it's not like you've got anything to lose =)

Let us know how it goes, I'd be interested in your thoughts. Note that a new version of Mandriva - 2008 - is coming out very soon, probably next week. You might as well wait for that to do your trials.

### Post by AdamWill on 2007-09-28
wthanna: the commercial edition of Mandriva Linux 2008 will include licensed multimedia codecs from Fluendo. all editions of Mandriva have always played MP3 out of the box. getting codecs for other stuff (AAC audio etc) for the Free and One editions is not quite as easy as with Ubuntu's codec assistant, I have to admit, but it's not too hard (you just have to find the third party repository where they live, and install 'em). stupid legal issues.

Mandriva's free-of-charge single-CD edition, One, and its commercial editions, have always installed and configured proprietary drivers automatically.

Mandriva was the first major distro to include 3D desktop acceleration technology (with the inclusion of Compiz in Mandriva Linux 2007, released last year around this time). Mandriva includes a tool called drak3d which lets you enable or disable 3D effects and switch between Xgl and AIGLX, it's run on first boot if your card is capable of 3D effects. In 2007.1 it let you switch between Beryl and Compiz too, but now that's obsolete with Compiz Fusion.

I've always thought it'd be fun to have a distro showdown with hardware detection - take a bunch of test systems covering a wide range of hardware, built by an independent lab, and compare concurrent releases of the major distros to see how well they detect and configure the hardware. I'm pretty confident Mandriva would come off very well in such a test, but we don't know till someone does it =) as far as I know no-one's ever done a really decent scale test.

### Post by AdamWill on 2007-10-02
Yes, that's correct. All Mandriva development is done in Cooker. All releases - stable and alpha / beta / RC - are simply snapshots of Cooker. When we go to a beta cycle we change the focus of Cooker from "package the newest version of everything and introduce packaging improvements" to "fix bugs", so the snapshots get progressively more stable. So current Cooker is actually more stable than RC2, and is very close to 2008 final. If you want to check on the progress of bug fixes since RC2, update to Cooker.

Of course, as soon as 2008 comes out, we unfreeze Cooker and go back to the bleeding edge, package-everything-right-now approach in Cooker, so if you want to stick to the stable 2008 release, you should switch your repositories from Cooker to 2008 as soon as that happens (I usually post a notification in the official MDV forums advising people when they should switch to the stable repos). But if you want to stay on the bleeding edge, you can stick with the Cooker repos, just don't be surprised when stuff breaks. :)

### Post by AdamWill on 2007-10-05
Compiz entered Mandriva on August 11, 2006, so it was in Mandriva Linux 2007, released in October 2006. The first public release was in February 2006. Mandriva was the first major distribution to ship with Compiz included and a configuration tool to allow it to be easily enabled.

NTFS-3G entered Mandriva on July 20, 2006, so it was in Mandriva Linux 2007, released in October 2006. The first public release was on July 14, 2006.

mindtrick: public release is due early next week. Today 2008 will be released to early seeders - a small group of a few hundred users who volunteer to download the release early and then seed it constantly, increasing the base of available seeders when the torrents go out to the public. We introduced this system a couple of releases back on the suggestion of a user, after we had some problems with the first couple of releases we did via BitTorrent: we'd have two systems seeding, and 10,000 people all trying to download from those two systems at once. As you can imagine, this did not go very well. :)

### Post by AdamWill on 2007-10-06
I'm not a big fan of 'community' packages. Once you accumulate a few of them from different places, they inevitably start causing problems with each other, problems with official package updates, and problems with distribution upgrades (I've heard the vast majority of broken upgrades from one Ubuntu release to another are caused by the influence of 'community' packages). We found this out the hard way with Mandriva. Ultimately I think a better approach is to make the official packaging system open and flexible enough to take care of most needs.
