There's a couple things it would take.  Windows is "good enough" for the average user, and the average user is going to use Windows because it came with the PC (they aren't going to look for alternatives if they haven't thought about switching already).

1) games support
and
2) business applications support

1) many many many games require DirectX, so there's  two things that can remedy that: an open-source implementation of DirectX to be used with (an improved) WINE, and/or games developers realizing that OpenGL is just as good and has a broader market potential.  WINE is making progress, but it's not there yet.

2) specialized desktop business apps generally don't run in Linux.  I'm not talking about office apps; think Quickbooks, which is estimated to be used in something like 50% of all small businesses in the US.  There is NOTHING for Linux that comes close to the feature set and ease of use that Quickbooks offers.  There are some teams working on projects with compete potential, so it's only a matter of time, but it's going to be hard to get those small business owners to switch to something completely different, even if it is as easy to use.  There's also a lot of industry specific programs that have no comparable Linux counterpart.  BUT here's the key: get Linux on business desktops for the average office worker, and they will be much more likely to consider it at home.  Especially if that new pc they want is cheaper with a pre-installed Linux.  So once again it comes back to getting the developers of commercial software to port their products to Linux. 

It all comes back to commercial developers (especially Intuit: Quickbooks, Quicken and TurboTax are all very popular with no real Linux alternative.  They would definitely capture the Linux market, or at least those Linux users willing to pay for their software).  They don't develop for Linux because there's not enough market, but if they did develop for Linux, there'd be a larger market, and no reason for Windows.  Ironic that MS .NET programs and Mono could spell the end of MS Windows.

Well, there's my two cents.
