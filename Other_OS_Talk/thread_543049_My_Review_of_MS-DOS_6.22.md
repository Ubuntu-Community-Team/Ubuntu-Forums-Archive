Below, I will go into nine, detailed reasons why I think DOS is better than Windows, and in certain cases, Windows Vista, here goes.

9 Reasons Windows Vista can suck it!

1. It's easier to get MS-DOS installed.

There is no product activation wizard, and there is no point in the installation where it prompts you for a serial number, come to think of it, I do believe I copied my MS-DOS floppy disk set from my Aunt, who copied it from work, who had 50 copies running on their office PC's, from when the manager copied all the disks from her neighbor in exchange for baking her a cherry pie.

Windows Vista: $400 a copy.
MS-DOS: Free

In Windows Vista, I think it's possible to bypass Activation by challenging it to Battleship and Twister. (No, wait, I'm thinking of Bill and Ted vs. The Reaper)

In Windows Vista, you have to walk up to Product Activator and use your 1337 Gun Kata skills.

[http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tINWl0gzQWI](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tINWl0gzQWI) (Warning: Violence)


2. MS-DOS has better memory management than Windows Vista.

Try as I might, I don't think I could manage to use more than 8 Megabytes of RAM under DOS.

Sure DOS can only directly address 640 kilobytes of RAM, but if you use HIMEM.SYS and EMM386.EXE, you can load most of DOS, your TSR's and drivers, etc. into RAM beyond the 640k barrier, thus having 580 kilobytes of RAM left, which still makes DOS comparable to the amount of free RAM you have left after Vista boots up, with the advantage going to DOS, because you still have "Extended RAM" left to play around with, in a never-ending supply.

DOS has no SWAP file, because it does not need one, if you run out of memory completely in DOS, it crashes, but if you run out of memory in DOS, you probably need to update your computer to one that was made after 1988.

Speaking of memory, DOS can only access partitions up to 2 gigabytes, this is not a problem, because between Windows Vista, System Restore (Which, if you've ever used it, usually manages to break things even worse), and the files Vista litters all over your disk, you'll have about two usable gigabytes of disk space left after not too long.

3. It's easier to do common tasks in MS-DOS.

To launch Notepad in Windows Vista, you have to navigate through no less than 4 steps to get through the Start Menu (I'm sorry, the Mac OS orb rip-off menu), 5 if Windows has "intelligently" hidden it from you. "Personalized Menus"

In MS-DOS, you type "edit" and hit enter.

Or say I wanted to load the X-Wing video game.

"cd Xwing"
"xwing"

Or say I wanted a new folder:

md "folder"

Fear my leetness.

Windows Vista - Order an Ubuntu CD, and reboot with the disc in the drive.

5. Web Browsing

In DOS, you can browse the web, and not get spyware and pop-up's, Windows Vista has yet to duplicate this functionality.

Using Arachne add-on's, you can listen to your mp3's without DOS asking you about license files.

6. MS-DOS includes better programming tools.

7. Freeing up disk space in MS-DOS

MS-DOS shipped with disk compression software, first "Doublespace", which Microsoft stole source code from Stac Electronics' "Stacker" for, they got sued and Stac won, so then they wrote their own code, and called it "Drivespace".

If you ever want to corrupt the contents of your hard drive beyond repair, use Drivespace, Windows 95 and 98 also shipped with Drivespace.

The best way to free up disk space using drivespace is by navigating to the DOS directory:

"cd dos"

And using the following commands:

"del drvspace.exe"
"del drvspace.bin"

In MS-DOS, you can delete anything you want, and nothing will stop you.

In Windows Vista, you may not remove Microsoft's crappy web browser, or their media player, or anything else you don't like, that's sitting there, wasting disk space, cause let's face it, the only way to improve IE or Media Player would be by deleting them.

8. System Requirements

MS-DOS made it through six major revisions as a stand-alone product (eight if you count the ones that Windows piggybacked on), and the system requirements never went up, you could run DOS with 640k of RAM and an Intel 8086 processor.

Windows Vista requires a PC with a processor 300% faster than XP needed, with at least 1,000% more RAM.

9. Boot up times

DOS boots in about 15 seconds, on my Canon laptop running an 80486 DX 66, with 16 MB of RAM.

Windows Vista takes about 3 minutes, on my faster desktop, with an Athlon 64 3200+ and 2 gigabytes of RAM.

(Yes, I still have a machine running DOS, the FreeDOS operating system, which is far superior to anything that ever came out of Redmond)

In DOS, you never had to screw around with your "Start Up Programs", in Windows, everyone hooks in their craplets to load up immediately, as soon as you start the system.
