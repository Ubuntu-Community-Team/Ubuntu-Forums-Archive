### Post by ZylGadis on 2007-01-18
I don't really want to argue GPL2 vs GPL3, as it makes no sense to just repeat stuff you can read elsewhere. Consider, however, the following example that I just thought of:

Some (most) FLOSS people dislike the BSD license - and its compatibles, because they consider it to be too allowing. The BSD license essentially gives everyone every possible right, only copyright is kept. That is how Apple was able to take BSD Unix, make a proprietary version of it, and build OSX on top. That is also how Cedega managed to leech Wine before Wine became GPLed.

On the other hand, people like GPL2 because GPL2 keeps the rights allowed and those disallowed in balance: you, the author, give others lots of rights, with the exception of the rights that they may turn against you and in effect trample **your** rights - like making a proprietary spin-off of your code and start fattening their own wallets with your work. Again, just look at Cedega.

It's much like democracy - talk/do whatever you want, unless you start abusing someone.

However, with hardware-based DRM, the big corps finally found a loophole in GPL2, which means they can trample the users' and authors' rights much like if the software was released under BSD. GPL3 aims to close that loophole, thus reinstating the necessary and healthy balance.

I don't really see GPL2 being a viable option nowadays at all. Those people who don't want restrictions shold migrate to BSD. Those people who believe that ideal freedom lies somewhere between complete anarchy and total despotism will use GPL3. GPL2 is simply dead with the discovery of hardware-based DRM, because it cannot uphold the aims of its creators any more; once again, those aims were a healthy balance between the rights of the author and the rights of the user.

Corporations naturally dislike any GPL, because the first priority of corporations is to make money, and the easiest way to make money is to take someone else's work and leech off of it. GPL2 used to prevent that; now it cannot any more. GPL3 aims to prevent that once again.

Guess what? Corporations are your enemy - no matter whether you are a user, or an author. Corporations trying to leech FLOSS are the middleman that sits between the producer and the consumer and makes life difficult for both. So it is very natural that "very sizable" companies don't like GPL3 - it would be suicidal for them to.

The question is, **why do you care about entities whose purpose is to trample you?**
