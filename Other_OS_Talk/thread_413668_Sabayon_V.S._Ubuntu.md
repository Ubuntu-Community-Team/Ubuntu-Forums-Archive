### Post by pxc on 2007-06-12
Hi everybody. I know this thread is mostly dead, but I found this googling "Linux Mint vs SabayonLinux" as one of the top results, and it's probably even more "relevant" to "SabayonLinux vs Ubuntu" searches, and, being the arrogant person that I am, I decided my opinion would be useful to others in the future--so much so that I registered just for this post (although you may see more of me soon. [PS: That's one beast of a run-on sentence!]).

I'll start off by saying that I have more experience with SabayonLinux than Ubuntu (I'm writing this from a Sabayon machine), so I might be biased towards it, and I probably have different opinions of how a distro should look and feel than most of you.

**Good Things (according to me) About SabayonLinux**
[list]Very helpful (and very knowledgeable) community.[/list]
[list]Very bleeding edge packages (most packages are added to the Sabayon overlay (Gentoo-speak for secondary repository) within two days of their release, and often less than one day.[/list]
[list]Official distro of the Beryl project (and maybe Coral, soon. [Coral is the leading candidate for the new name of the Compiz and Beryl projects after they complete the merge])[/list]
[list]Incredibly flexible package management (for example, Amarok, a music player that I absolutely *love*, one that has been described as "*the* killer app" for Linux, is a KDE application. Because of this, in Ubuntu, you have to install more or less half of KDE to install it. In SabayonLinux, or any Gentoo-based distro, since you compile from source, you can compile Amarok without KDE support, and you only have one extra dependency: KDE-libs. You can even compile Amarok to use GStreamer, so you don't even need a new engine.)[/list]
[list]Sabayon (Gentoo/Portage) has the most massive repository. **Period**. I have *never* been unable to find a package in Sabayon, between Portage and the Sabayon overlay.[/list]
[list]Comes with KDE and Fluxbox, two WMs that, between them, should work for a machine of any age and/or power.[/list]
[list]Comes with full multimedia support. This means that all codecs and drivers--including proprietary Windows formats and proprietary drivers (wireless, video, etc) will work "out-of-the-box."[/list]

**Bad Things (according to me) About SabayonLinux**
[list]Slower Package Management:
[list]Slow Searches (emerge --search can take a *long* time[/list]
[list]Slow Portage Syncs (repository refreshes)[/list]
[list]Sometimes very slow installs (OOo, for example, requires up to 9GB of free space and sometimes over that many hours to compile)[/list][/list]
[list]Very bloated installs: the DVD comes with all sorts of crap (four+ desktop environments, three+ office suites) that you may, or more commonly, may *not* need.[/list]
[list]Relatively tiny community: although SabayonLinux is growing quickly (watch for it on DistroWatch!), its community is still teency-weency compared to Ubuntu's.[/list]

**Proposed Solutions for the Bad Things about SabayonLinux (to my knowledge)**
[list]Slow Searches: there is are two applications, esearch and eix, that both implement indexed (and near instant [faster than Synaptic <although I think apt-cache search is the same speed>] package searches). I've used esearch myself, and it works great.[/list]
[list]Slow Portage Syncs: there's nothing for it, as far as I know. :([/list]
[list]Slow Installs: There are two possible solutions to this. One is implemented, but limited, and the other is in development, but powerful.
[list]Solution One: Portage has two ways to emerge binary packages. One is emerge -K, and the other is that certain packages with long compile times (OOo, Firefox) have a package with the same name ending in -bin, ie. mozilla-firefox-bin openoffice-bin[/list]
[list]Solution Two: Project Entropy. This is a Sabayon project that implements a special binmerge function that will integrate with Portage. It'll be handled in Sabayon's own repositories. I'd keep an eye on this if I were you and your primary problem was slow installs/broken compiles.[/list][/list]
[list]Bloat: The miniEditions. Typically released two weeks after a stable release, they are a single-CD release that comes only with the KDE and Fluxbox (good ol' Fluxbox is practically weightless) desktop environments. If you prefer Gnome, you can uninstall all of KDE in a snap, and, unfortunately, start Gnome compiling and go to bed ;).[/list]

### Post by pxc on 2007-06-12
I decided to break this into two posts, for organization and so I don't have to worry about the post not going through or something and losing all of it. Anyway, here's my opinion on Ubuntu, laid out in the same format as my previous post on Sabayon.

**The Good Things (according to me) about Ubuntu**
[list]Strong Package Management: I love deb compared to RPM.
[list]Synaptic is easy to use, and stable.[/list]
[list]Aptitude is an elegant way to install packages from the command-line, and works on *all* Debian and Debian-based systems.[/list]
[list]Ubuntu packages install quickly.[/list]
[list]Ubuntu packages are very ubiquitous, so much so that if a package for a given application is not in the repository, there's one out there somewhere.[/list][/list]
[list]Simple, clean configuration, look, and feel. Ubuntu is certainly not bloated.[/list]
[list]Massive community (who, according to everything I've heard, are very friendly and helpful)[/list]

**The Bad Things (according to me) about Ubuntu**
[list]Ubuntu lacks support for proprietary codecs and drivers. This means post-install, I have a bunch of installation and configuration to do. It's easy, and it's a hassle. In my opinion, this means Ubuntu's multimedia is broken, regardless of the philosophical and legal reasons behind it.[/list]
[list]Ubuntu's package management is simplistic to the point that it lacks functionality as compared to Portage.[/list]
[list]Ubuntu's packages are not always the latest-and-greatest, even on the "unstable" repositories.[/list]
[list]Ubuntu's KDE edition, Kubuntu is, quite frankly, pathetic.[/list]

**Other Bad Things (for me) About Ubuntu**
Don't let these decide which you pick, just keep them in mind. They're really pet peeves of mine more than anything else, and most will be fixed in upcoming releases.
[list]Ubuntu's Savage video drivers are broken. My old PIII laptop has a Savage card. I don't like that.[/list]
[list]The Ubuntu kernels (and they're brethren manifested in Ubuntu-derivatives) have issues with a phantom floppy disk they think is on my laptop and can't read. My laptop's BIOS doesn't have anywhere to disable floppy drives. I've heard blacklisting the floppy module can fix this--I'll try it[/list]
[list]I can't stand (Ubuntu's settings for) Gnome. At *all*. Nautilus makes me want to kick people, *especially* with the default settings that Ubuntu gives it. This wouldn't be such a big deal if Kubuntu didn't suck.[/list]

**Proposed Solutions For The Bad Things About Ubuntu (to my knowledge)**
[list]Broken codecs and drivers: LinuxMint! LinuxMint is an Ubuntu-based distribution that, in my opinion, takes all of the good things that Ubuntu has and fixes most of the problems it has. It's just different enough to be good for my tastes *and* still feel like Ubuntu.[/list]
[list]Stupid Gnome configuration and crummy KDE version: LinuxMint... again! I've played with LinuxMint just a little bit lately, and I was surprised to find that I actually *like* their rendition of Gnome! Their KDE version is also supposedly better than Kubuntu, although the Feisty equivalent isn't out yet, so I can't try it myself.[/list]
[list]Stale packages: LinuxMint!! LinuxMint has an additional "romeo" repository for new and untested packages.[/list]
