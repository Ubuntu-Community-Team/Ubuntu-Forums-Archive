### Post by ezsit on 2008-08-21
> So what package management are you using? Did you come up with your own? Becasue if you didnt your based off of either Debian or Redhat.

Not necessarily. SuSE started with Slackware and adapted .rpm as their package management system. Most LFS distros go with .tar.gz (or .tgz) and these have no direct relation to Slackware. Slackware, even though it uses .tar.gz (or .tgz) compression format for its' packages, does not really have a package management system per se, just a way to install, update, and remove individual packages. Sure, others have grafted a package management system into Slackware, but the granddaddy is pure at its roots.

Just because a distro uses .rpm does not mean the system was based off of Redhat (Ark and OpenSuse are two top examples that come to mind, and Caldera's OpenLinux from years back is another example of distros that used .rpm packages but had no direct relation to Redhat lineage - Caldera did with their first ever release, but quickly switched to LST Linux from Germany for their second release on forward).

The choice of package format does not imply the basis for the entire distro. Startup scripts, configuration files, filesystem hierarchy, and installation routines are all better indicators of what distro any particular distro chose to emulate or copy.

### Post by bilijoe on 2008-09-01
I am really glad to see RedPandaFox step up and rescue this project.  I'm not sure exactly why, but I have become rather attached to it.  And you're right, RedPandaFox, about this being a BIG project.  

A word of advice; do not ignore the fact that, no matter how you may like to structure this in your mind, it is a business, with associated costs (e.g., the forum), the need for some degree of management (at least coordination), facilities, etc.  Right now, all these considerations might be trivial.  For instance, I'm reasonably sure you are all working out of your own spaces, on your own computers, but (assuming you succeed, which I am assuming you will) the time will come when you are going to need a test bench, and a budget that will allow you to go out and purchase whatever hardware you need to [try to] duplicate a bug, or some problem someone is having.  And, sooner or later, you are going to need someone [else] to answer the phone (or do some other infrastructure type task).  

Anyway, my point is (and this comes from first hand experience with several successful and several unsuccessful start-ups), it is dangerous to the health of the "company", and fiscally irresponsible, to wait to worry about these things, until the need arises.  My carefully considered opinion is that you should enlist another volunteer who is NOT a Linux person, maybe even not so much a computer person, but a BUSINESS person.  The business aspect of a project like this is like a tiger, laying, essentially invisible, in the grass, patiently waiting for the time to strike.  When it does strike, it is about as easy to deal with as an uncaged tiger.  And the more quickly the success comes, the sooner the tiger will strike.  

Two of the three start-ups I have been close to, collapsed like a house of cards when, seemingly suddenly and unexpectedly, the comfortable little programming group had to act like (become--overnight) the "business" it really was.  Programmers and their ilk are generally very poor businesspersons.  There's a reason for "Business" to be a specialty at the collegiate level.  Universities don't have Schools of Business for no reason.  They have them because there is a genuine need for people who love "doing business" (for one thing), and who have gone to the trouble of studying, learning, and gaining experience in the details of doing business.  Details that are anything but obvious to most people.  

Every business I have ever been involved in at a high enough level to have to participate in the "doing business" part of having a programming company, or a remodeling company, or a bicycle accessories company--it doesn't matter--business is business (that's why business schools don't specialize)--I hated it, every bit of it.  So, eventually, I always quit.  Until finally, I had the good sense to go to work for a business where the business of doing business was handled by people hired explicitly and exclusively to deal with the doing business part of the business (have I used the word "business" enough times yet? pant, pant).  

Anyway, my advice is to get a business person on your team.  You may not NEED one for some time, but if they are a volunteer (which should be the case, at least at first), they'll just have little to do, so it'll be no big deal.  But when the time comes that you DO need one, it'll be invaluable to already have one who is at least familiar with what's going on--one who won't have to take the [possibly unavailable] time to familiarize themselves with what's going on.  'Nuff said?

Best of luck to all those who are putting effort into this Nisus-Linux project.  I'm not good for much (except expressing my often controversial opinion), but I have had over 25 years of experience with Software companies, both large and small, and nearly 40 years total experience with various businesses (including the computer stuff, else those numbers would make me older than the hills), including 7 start-ups (4 of which were successful, one spectacularly successful).  So, if there is anything I can do to help, let me know and, if I can help, I will be happy to.
