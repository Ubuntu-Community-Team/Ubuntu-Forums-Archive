Computing in the year 2007: Why the future isn't what it used to be

In 1990, my dad upgraded to the first IBM PC-Compatible our family had ever used, the price tag was about $1,200 (roughly $2,100 in today's money, adjusted for inflation).

The PC was a Packard Bell with an Intel 486 SX 25 Mhz processor, 4 MB of RAM, and a whopping 100 megabytes of hard drive space, it came with MS-DOS and Microsoft Windows 3.1, and a display capable of 640 x 480 resolution at 16 colors, or in MS-DOS (most advanced video games were DOS based back then, Windows simply lacked the ability to run anything much more advanced than Solitaire), 256 colors at 320 x 200.

By any standards today, that thing was an abacus, but I did sure like it back then, I played many "multimedia" (buzzword of the 1990's) video games in DOS, in fact, the only thing Windows was any good for was to run a word processor or Quicken (Not much has changed, huh?).

Sitting there and playing all these games, such as Return to Zork, Wolfenstein 3d, Corridor 7: Alien Invasion, Commander Keen, DOOM, Panzer General, Doom II: Hell on Earth, Companions of Xanth, X-Wing, Tie Fighter, X-Wing vs. Tie Fighter, and more SSI Advanced Dungeons and Dragons games than you can shake a stick at, the possibilities looked fairly endless for the PC, remembering that just 6 years before this, computers were all a novelty, flashy toys like the Apple Macintosh, that didn't really do much other than cost money (Not much has changed there either.).

Flash forward through Windows 95, Windows 98, and Windows Me, all the way to my systems running XP.

With Windows XP, everyone is running a fully 32-bit operating system, despite the fact that 32-bit CPU's had been sold since 1987. (Windows 9x/Me were 16-bit MS-DOS extensions with some 32-bit functionality)

The price of the typical PC, adjusted for inflation, is roughly 20% of the cost of one 17 years ago, but nearly all those cost savings have come from OEM's using chitzy hardware, made in China, and assembled by slave labor, of women and children.

The price of a Windows license, has increased, even adjusting for inflation, if you want to use any of the "advanced" functionality of Vista (advanced, in respect to other Microsoft operating systems, where every other platform had pretty much all of Vistas features years before Vista was out)

So the biggest cost component of a PC, is not any of the hardware, but your Microsoft Windows license, which cost absolutely nothing to copy and image the hard disk with.

And the biggest part of the ruse, is that even though there are more than 20 times as many PC users now vs. 1990, Microsoft hasn't given us a break on Windows pricing, they're just selling 20 times as many copies of Windows, at ever increasing prices.

Also, most of the technical problems that had put a crimp on the PC's multimedia functionality had been overcome, these included insufficient RAM, slow video cards, slow processors, dial up internet connections, no access to equipment that could burn CD's or DVD's, and oh yeah, Peer to Peer file sharing was still relatively new,

In otherwords, most of the technology that people now own a PC for, has all come together since the turn of the century, now that systems are able to do what the user wants, we face "intentional design flaws", these include:

Damnable Restrictions Malware

Video game installers that infect your computer with viruses that damage your hardware and make your PC run slower. (Some of these "copy protection drivers" forced Windows to read from DMA DVD-Rom drives in PIO Mode, thereby destroying them, the most notorious for doing this is the Starforce malware.

Subsystems embedded in the OS itself that monitor what you are doing and intentionally crash the system if they think you're up to no good. (Windows Vista)

Subsystems in the OS that spy on what the user is doing, what hardware and software they have, and what media that have been playing, and sends the information to Microsoft. (Windows Vista)

Subsystems in the OS that are designed to delete files on your machine, at the command of Microsoft. (Euphemistically called "Windows Defender".)

What is the poor PC user to do?

Well, at the risk of becoming a broken record, get Linux.

But what Linux?

Well, a Linux distribution that is not based in the United States and subject to our arbitrary and asinine legal system.

Microsoft sees the writing on the wall, people are catching on to their shoddy business practices, but they aren't quite sure what to do.

Microsoft has tried to kill off free and open source competition in the following ways:

Launching a web site, packed full of lies and FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt), designed to wage a smear campaign against Linux.

Building undocumented, poorly documented, and incorrectly documented features into Windows, so that Windows API reverse engineering efforts (such as Wine, a Windows like subsystem for Linux/UNIX, Mac OS) would be exceedingly difficult.

Paying SCO to launch their lawsuit against Linux vendors, alleging patent infringement on SCO's UNIX source code, which their case has slowly, but surely fallen apart, after being shot down in chucks by a judge, who apparently does not suffer from rectal-cranial inversion. (Who knew there were any?)

When that failed, Microsoft started signing back-alley deals with Linux vendors like Linspire, Novell, and Xandros, with wording that effectively meant those vendors were acknowledging the violation of patents that Microsoft supposedly has for functionality in Linux systems, in return, users of these systems got the Microsoft Tahoma font, and WMA codecs, woohoo! (Yet another victory for the idiots that want to turn Linux into "Poor Man's Windows")

Most reputable Linux ditributors have told Microsoft to go (you know) themselves, as many times as they like......because Microsoft likely has no such patents, and wouldn't even think about engaging Linux in a court battle even if they did, because Linux has friends, and those friends have more patents than Microsoft, certainly covering more things in Windows, that infringe on things in Linux.

Therefore Microsoft is waging this battle to give potential Linux converts another barrage of FUD to think about, thus causing them to stay on Windows.

But the biggest thing, by far, that soils Linux's reputation is the way the patent and legal systems are set up in the United States.

Linux distributions like Fedora don't include any officially supported way of getting codecs, DVD software, or proprietary drivers.

Why?

Because Red Hat (Fedora's commercial sponsor), is based in the United States, and subject to the insanity of the US legal system, therefore they're too afraid to innovate on their operating system, because someone, somewhere, might have a patent, so Red Hat doesn't feel the need to stick it's head out on the chopping block and give the users what they want, because it would almost certainly bring a lawsuit down on Red Hat.

Sure you can tack support for all this stuff and more on, after the fact, by using unofficial software repositories, but still those are not always such a great idea (can conflict with software already in your distribution).

Novell's OpenSuse is getting more and more like that too.

And with the idiot judges and best politicians money can buy, packing the benches with more of them every day, why take the risk?

We are talking about a court system that:

Gives multi-million dollar judgements TO spammers and spyware vendors FROM the victims! (While you're at it, why don't you just give Charles Manson a full pardon and the Nobel Peace Prize)

Allows the RIAA, MPAA, BSA, and other companies that should not be allowed to exist, per the RICO Act, the right to file SLAPP suits against ordinary citizens.

Thinks it has the right to try and enforce United States law on companies in other countries.

Let OJ Simpson go.

And many other, as Carlos Mencia, would so eloquently put it, "der der derr" moments.

Which is why you'll see me using Ubuntu.

Ubuntu is a great OS in it's own right, but I probably never would have moved over to it if the United States Government didn't hamper the efforts of US Linux vendors, while at the same time, rewarding Microsoft for abusing it's monopoly.

In another 17 years, people will probably come to accept the fact that the PC is just a device that disables anything Microsoft, the Business Software Alliance, the Recording or Motion Picture Associations of America don't want running, and the fact that the PC gained popularity by enabling it's users.

What a country we live in!

Final Thoughts

I was going to write another article around this next bit, but I think I'll just make it a footnote in this one.

I love Bit Torrent!

Bit Torrent has taken the blood, sweat, tears, and high bandwidth bills of distributing large files, and done away with them.

But the man behind the Bit Torrent protocol is an evil, greedy, money grubbing, SOB, that has sold out to the MPAA and RIAA.

The official Bit Torrent client is now closed source and contains spyware, logging everything you transfer, and I'm also sad to say that this horse's butt also bought and destroyed the best Bit Torrent client ever, uTorrent.

So, in closing, use Azuereus or Shareaza or something on Windows, or Deluge, Azureus, Ktorrent, Bit Tornado, etc. on Linux, but stay FAR FAR away from the official BitTorrent (or uTorrent) software.

I leave you now, with a verse from the good book (of Mozilla that is):
