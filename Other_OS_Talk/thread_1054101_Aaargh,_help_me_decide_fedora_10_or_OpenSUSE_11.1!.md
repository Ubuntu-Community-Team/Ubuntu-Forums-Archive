### Post by RichardLinx on 2009-01-29
I've had very little experience with Fedora so no comment there. openSUSE in my experience has been great, the package management was easy enough to understand. YaST  is a very complete GUI tool. And OpenOffice on SUSE is great. openSUSE gives you the choice of a DVD or CD. So If you don't mind a larger download you'll have the choice of installing GNOME, KDE 3.5, KDE 4.x.x and 'Other'. I'm using the KDE version and am very happy with my OS. 

My impressions from the point of an Ubuntu user:
1. Install was easy. 
2. Updating the first time was strange, It asked me to accept some sort of certificate, accepted and updates continued with no problems.
3. Graphics card installation was easier then Ubuntu. "One click" installation.
4. All hardware was detected out of the box a part from my DUO card reader which no distro seems able to notice.
5. GUI looks nice and professional, YaST is a vey comprehensive GUI tool as well. (I'm hoping UBuntu will implement something similar in the future).
6. For some reason I was impressed by OpenOffice.
7. Obtaining "illegal" codecs was a little more effort, I had to enable third party repositories (pacman) because they weren't available in SUSE' official repos.
8. I'm a GNOME user who wanted to give KDE a try, so there was a small learning curve but I got use to the menu and apps quickly. I think KDE 4.2 is great.

I also installed a copy of openSUSE 11.0 GNOME about a month ago but never really got into it. It was on a dual boot machine but I was to lazy to get into and kept going into my Ubuntu partition. If KDE isn't your thing, from what I saw of the GNOME version it was obvious the SUSE developers put effort into it, they did a great job.

### Post by Down8ve on 2009-02-02
I've been hopping a lot of late.  I liked the simplicity of Gnome and used it for quite a while, mainly with Ubuntu-based distros.  When the KDE4 talk started, I paid attention, as most of my work is done on a Mac.

KDE4 was not ready until some of the 4.2 release candidates began to appear.  I installed two releases of the Fedora-based KDE4.2 RCs and liked what I saw, but there were various holes in doing day-to-day things that caused me to revert back to Mandriva 2009.0 KDE4.1, which was not too bad.

Fedora's use of PackageKit was slow and unable to install many packages at once without freezing, no matter what I tried.  Some folks are happy with the "fastest repo" plugin.  Good for them.

I was frustrated with openSuse.  I've tried to migrate to it many times from the 'buntus, but always had a "gotcha" with enough small elements due to my own ignorance of how to use Yast to configure it properly.

This all changed last week.  As of this moment I have installed the recent live CD of openSuse KDE4.2.  This is great.

I've had no issues using Kdenlive, the terrific video editor.  No issues burning CDs, opening macro-laden powerpoint documents, annotating PDFs, etc.  The Mplayer plugins make web media work fine.  

The best part is the elegance of the KDE4.2 desktop.  This reminds me of a Mac, but it is far "faster" to do things, and very configurable.  The latest demonstrations of the Koffice apps show much in common to the way Apple's iWork apps work and are truely a joy to use.  Suspend to ram.disk works, too.  Even with my Nvidia driver.

I'd give openSuse KDE4.2 LIVE a serious look.

I also know about the upcoming releases of Mint KDE4, Klikit and PCLinuxOS, which will be KDE3 based.  They are fine, but there is much to be said for a distro that has the force and organization of Red Hat or Novell behind it.  As nice as Mepis and PCLinux may seem, the recent lag in development of these made-by-one-dude systems worry me a bit.

### Post by mthei on 2009-02-02
I'm sure you've had enough replies in the last four days since your original post, but I'd like to give one more vote for Fedora. I had used it for nine months, and had only given me one issue (a udev update caused some problems with unmounting CDs/DVDs, which was fixed a week later). However, I never had an update destroy the entire system, and was always able to use my computer normally. Frankly, I really like YUM. It does everything that aptitude does, but with a clearer output, although it is a bit slow, as every time it's used, it updates and re-downloads the package database. That's easy to get around though, by pointing YUM to a mirror closest to you, or installing the yum-fastestmirror plugin. RPM also makes it easier to upgrade packages installed outside of the repository (such as Opera, or Flash).
The only reason I would advise anyone against Fedora is if you're married to the idea of using KDE. I had tried KDE4 in F9, and I wasn't too impressed with it. Can't say how 4.2 is working out for it.
And I had left Fedora months ago, as the Livna and Dribble repositories had given me some sub-par packages, which I assume is taken care of now, as at the time, the developers working there were in the process of creating RPMFusion.
This post probably wasn't terribly helpful and I'm sure I had repeated some points mentioned several times (didn't bother reading the whole thread past the first post), but I figured one more positive post about Fedora couldn't hurt.
