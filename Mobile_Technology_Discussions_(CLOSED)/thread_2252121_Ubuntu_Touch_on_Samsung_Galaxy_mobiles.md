### Post by grahammechanical on 2014-11-09
You need to understand the purpose for developing Ubuntu Touch. It was to give to OEMs a Ubuntu image that could be pre-installed on retail mobile devices released by OEMs. It was never the purpose to produce a version of Ubuntu that could be installed by users on any and every mobile device on sale in the same way as Ubuntu desktop can be downloaded and installed on compatible desktop hardware.

Originally it was intended to do this work in secret and not to release information about it until the code was finished and polished. But people complained about the secrecy. So, all this work is now being done in the open and still people complain.

Ubuntu is open source. The code is available to people like you but that is not enough for you, is it? You want Ubuntu developers to divert from their goal of getting Ubuntu phones and tablets to market to help you with your experiments.

The 3 Nexus devices are just reference devices. They are used by the developers to test the code as they develop it. These Nexus devices will be obsolete by the time Ubuntu phones get to market. In fact they are already being outclassed by newer devices in the market place. So, you are wrong in thinking that the development of Ubuntu Touch is solely for a handful of Nexus devices. You are not understanding the purpose for developing Ubuntu Touch in the first place.

The Ubuntu SDK is there to make it easy for application developers to write apps for Ubuntu phones and tablets and get those apps into the app store. Retail mobile devices can succeed or fail as a  commercial product due to the number and quality of the apps in the app store. The Ubuntu SDK is based upon QTCreator. So it is not really a new SDK. But it is new in the sense that it is still under development. It could become a powerful Software Development Kit in time.

The same could be said of Ubuntu Developer Tools Center which has the purpose of turning Ubuntu into a platform for Android developers. In fact there is an aim to make Ubuntu the platform of choice for software developers. But it all takes time.

[https://wiki.ubuntu.com/ubuntu-developer-tools-center](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/ubuntu-developer-tools-center)

You speak of Open Source as if that puts some kind of obligation on Ubuntu developers to do what you want. The "Free" part of Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) also gives the developers of Ubuntu the freedom to work in directions of their choice. Which is not what you would want. You claim freedom for yourself but deny it to others.

We look down at our feet to see what we are walking on. We look up to see where we are going. We look up at the starts and become aware of both the possibilities and the limitations of human activities.
