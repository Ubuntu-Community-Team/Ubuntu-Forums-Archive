### Post by Angharad on 2015-05-17
It's time for me to come clean on my BQ Aquaris Ubuntu edition. And this review is timely, with the announcement of the Meizu today.

Well, it is back in its box, ready to be put up on Ebay or sold to any other interested party. I gave it a fair trial, for over a month. But I am afraid it's not the hardware that let it down for me, it's the software. I bought it because the touch screen my old Nokia N900 (Maemo/Debian) was on its way out.

1. Not truly multi-tasking. For example, a process running in a Bash shell will suspend when the window is not active. That's a bummer when you're listening to music through a custom application you wrote in Bash (yes, I do that, and no, there is no other app that will do exactly what I want as far as I can see).

2. The gesture based interface was continually frustrating me. Not because it wasn't easy to learn the gestures - it was. Rather because getting the right gesture takes considerable skill sometimes. For example, the difference between switching scopes and revealing all running apps. Or trying to type on the virtual keyboard (which I hate anyway, but I know it's hard to get a real keyboard on phones these days), horizontal, without activating the launcher when you try to hit the space key.

3. I found myself only using one scope - the App scope. Maybe if I had given it time, I would have found scopes more useful. But to be honest, I think they're a gimmick. I am prepared to accept that time might prove me wrong though.

4. Suspend when idle - suspends. It suspends processes you have running too. Like that music I was listening to in my Bash shell.

5. Solution to 4 is to set to never suspend. But then the screen stays on - albeit at low power. But when the phone is by the side of your bed at night (with a bash-scripted alarm program to fire off that stream I want to wake me up), that is not good. Turning it upside down merely causes clicks as the screen registers touches from the surface it is on, so is not a solution either. This also wastes battery.

6. The calendar app is a nightmare to use. Last time I was standing at a desk making an appointment, the lady asked (as always), would I like a card. I said no, I'll put it in my phone. 2 minutes and some embarassment later, I said, actually yes, I will take the card after all. (who on earth thought those spinners were a good idea?)

I had sort of managed to cope with all of the above, by using my poor dying Nokia N900 just for the music whilst on my home wifi, and the BQ for my phone and occasional other apps. And I could always take the card for the appointment and add it to my calendar when I got home.

The BQ is fine for making calls, it is also fine at SMS. I received one call, which I failed to answer in time, and that surprised me, as I had picked up the phone and pressed a button a couple of times. Oh well, must have been my fault, I concluded. But the final straw for the BQ was a week ago when an incoming call came, the phone was at hand, and I spent many times pressing the green "answer" button, and getting no response. The phone simply ignored my pressing the button. Now this is clearly a (fairly major) bug which I assume others have not experienced, or it surely would have made the forums by now! So, completely exasperated, when I got home, I went onto Ebay and bought another Nokia N900 for £30 - 1/10th of the price I paid for my original N900, 5 years ago.

I am very disappointed in the Ubuntu phone. I had very high hopes for it. After all, I run Ubuntu (and only Ubuntu) on my desktop and laptop. And I believe in what Canonical are trying to do to bring Linux to the masses, and see them as the best hope at present. I hope it improves, but to do so it is going to have to change, as well as fix bugs. I won't be hurrying to buy another Ubuntu phone. Which is a big shame.

With my replacement N900, I have come home. Big sigh of relief.
