### Post by Ken UK on 2015-04-10
I just thought this might be the best way to give some feedback as I couldn't see any other formal way to do it (they should problem set something like that up if it doesn't already exist eespecially in these early stages).

I bought the phone because I had an old Windows Nokia phone I've had for acouple of years which cost me about £100 new. Knowing that you can probably guess I'm not one to be fussed about big expensive phones with lots of apps etc. I just need to call, text and go on the web to look stuff up, anything else is a bonus. For that reason I thought I'd take the plunge and switch to the BQ phone full time because I knew it would meet my minimum requirements and using it full time would be the best way to test it out.

It terms of the hardware I was very pleased especailly for the price. The build quality is good, definitely better than my old phone. The screen is clear, responsive/accurate to touch and seems durable, I can wipe off marks easily. It also seems reasonable in bright sunlight.
I really like the blue LED indicator which allowed me to see if the phone needed my attention without me disturbing it, a very nice feature,the only fault with it was from a software side (see below). I liked the speakers, very loud and pretty good quality although I think it's ashame they don't both  work together for music, videos etc. I assume the second one is for loud speaker during calls (I never use it so I don't know). The battery life is not amazing but it's reasonable, the typical capacity you can expect from a smartphone these days. I was able to watch videos aloud off youtube for quite along time and if I didn't use it heavily it could last multiple days.
The only gripe I have is with the camera, it is pretty poor. The photo quality isn't good and the fact that most of my photos are unclear or blurry underminds the fairly high resolution of the photos. I also find myself having to regularly touch the screen to make sure it is in focus which I find slightly awkward (the unfortunate side effect of not having a physical button I guess). My old phone's camera was much better in terms of quality and ease of use which is ashame as I often need to take photos on the fly when I don't have a proper camera around.

In terms of the OS and software I would say that I'm starting to see the potential but I don't think it's glaringly obvious by looking at it in its current state. It feels like it is still in very early stages i.e. alpha stage/unfinished/work in progress, so it's not too easy to see how well this will actual work once it gets going properly. The other thing that bothers me slightly (especially now the phone is on sale fully now) is that it doesn't clearly state anywhere that this is the case and that it is aimed at developers and enthusiasts which would result in disappoint for most normal users. There are some nice features which as fully working within the phone (I love the pull out apps bar on the left) but more features than not are either buggy or not fully implemented. You get a taste for scopes but they are currently only very superficial and 90% useless so for that reason I have barely tried them at all. Before I see them as something useful they will need to:
[LIST]
[*]Show a large range of data i.e. browse lots of videos, not just a handful.
[*]The central scopes (Today, NearBy etc.) need to be completely customisable in terms of types of content.
[*]Have some depth i.e. not just show abit of information and then a link to the website which the information came from.
[/LIST]

The gesture driven interface is definitely more difficult to get use to and use than your typical button based interfaces and it took me awhile to get use to the different types of gesture (i.e. different lengths of stroke) for the different types of app and scope. However I feel that after I got more use to it I had more direct control and speed to manoeuvre around the phone which I imagine will really benefit quick, skilled and coordinated users. The screen was definitely responsive enough to implement the gesture based system and be responsive.
I didn't like the fact I couldn't find a way to easily close an app when I'm finished with it without first displaying all the apps ruinning which means often I couldn't be bothered to close apps and would leave them open until later when I needed to display all apps anyway. I suppose this prevents you from easily closing an app and if there is no extra resource drain it's not a problem but the nature of phones means that you commonly need to switch between apps and leaving aload open despite not needing them to be open seems messy. I suppose that is the purpose of scopes, to reduce this switching between apps malarkey.
I didn't like the notifications drop down too much. it is a nice idea but you can't just partially pull it down and quickly reply to a message, you must let it take over your whole screen which is still an improvement over stoping listening or running whatever you are doing but I think it could be better, often I didn't need to use the whole screen to see and reply to a text. I didn't like that I could only see the first part of long text messages also because it means I would still need to go into messages to see the full thing even if I don't need to review other older texts. Notifications only seemed to work for phone functionalities (i.e. text, calls etc.) and not for anything else such as Facebook.
I found accessing anything outside the scopes and digging deeper such as finding the email adress for a contact was slow and at times this made the phone feel sluggish as I'm sat there waiting for the contacts app to open just to get some basic fundmental information. The same applies to lots of other things such as making a call, changing settings outside the ones accesible at the top etc. This gave the phone a general feeling of feel disconnection between many features and a feeling of many things (especially fundimental ones) being a hassle.
I was disappointed that there was a Gmail app but no proper email client such as some type of mobile thunderbird. This did mean that the email address in the contacts app were useless beyond just viewing.
Placing your curcur while writing/editing text is terrible at the minute and needs to be fixed as well as copy and pasting of text.
My final criticisim would be that contacts need to have more types of information beyond just phone numbers, addresses and email addresses such as company etc. It's ashame UbuntuOne closed down because that would have been ideal as a platform to support Ubuntu Phone in a similar way to iCloud, including contacts.

Mobile internet for me in the UK on O2 seemed initially poor to non-existant. I can only guess that the 3G wasn't working correctly and so it was trying to use 2G instead despite the phone correctly identifying my APN as the O2 monthly one. This also means that the location data for things like NearBy was almost always wildy off, sometimes suggesting I was 200 miles away from where I actually was. In the end I just turned off 3G to save battery as it wasn't any use but I am planning on giving the mobile internet and location based feautres another try.

I am very busy and I've not had time to do lots of research and purposeful testing so my comments are based on a purely typical end user perspective.
I do realise that these are very early days and they are probably already working on the things I've mentioned above but I just felt I should hightlight them anyway just incase.

In conclusion I like the phone but it is not ready for end users yet. I can't wait for things to be fully developed and mature because it's obvious to see that this system is different to the others.

P.S. for all those eager to get the phone but are outside the EU, you shouldn't worry because by the time you get a Ubutnu phone it will be better tested and more ready for end user use, you're not missing out on too much.

Regards,

Ken
