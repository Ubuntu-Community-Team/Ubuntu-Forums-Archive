### Post by duncedrive on 2016-02-24
People seem to forget some of the points regarding a phone... There's of course, two names, so two coins to a mobile phone. The FEATURE phone, and the SMART phone. We live in an era where the smartphone is predominant, offering things like app stores, an easily navigable interface, integration with multiple devices through Bluetooth, and even the ability to be used as your credit card, in place of your wallet.

The FEATURE phone was a popular thing back in the start of the millennium, and in some nations, it remains dominant, like in Japan, where smartphones are being designed with things like the clamshell design popularized by feature phones. Smartphones are being built to accommodate the crowd of feature phone users.

For what you mention, Jordan_Roberts, a regular phone in that case is a feature phone. It's all tied to the use case of the user, of course. A regular phone to robcar is apparently a smartphone with access to applications like Whatsapp, and Spotify.

Ubuntu is definitely missing some things that would make it a potential competitor. For me, my phones need to be able to do these 3 things specifically.
[LIST=1]
[*]Run Skype
[*]Run an IRC client, even through Mibbit on the web browser
[*]Run TeamViewer
[/LIST]

Knowing Skype and TeamViewer, in their current states, can't run as APKs, and can't run as X apps properly (especially Skype which is somehow worse off than the Mac version), Ubuntu Phone OS is out of the question as a daily driver for me.

Spotify and SoundCloud and the like aren't important to me, I can live without them. I do want access to my music library at the least, and I'm sure that'll get stabilized. Beyond that, the push for X app support, and Ubuntu Software Center being made front and center as the de facto App Store of Ubuntu Phone is going to be important to make Ubuntu Phone even slightly competitive.

For now, Ubuntu Phones are the type of thing to just keep around. Watch updates, keep the device updated, keep watching for substantial changes.

### Post by duncedrive on 2016-03-04
I actually...**absolutely despise Swype**. I find it on some devices, and if I have the ability to, I will remove it, even if I have to root it.

Something I found out that surprises me, though, is the BlackBerry Keyboard for Android. The BlackBerry Priv is the first Android-only BB phone, and when you aren't using the physical keyboard, you can use a virtual keyboard. It's literally the same as the one on all-touch BB10 devices. While I could only use it a few times because I don't have any Lollipop-based Android devices (my only choice is being sent back because I ordered a clean ESN device and didn't get that), I found it to be very nice.

It turns out BlackBerry worked with Nuance to make the BlackBerry Keyboard, and so that keyboard is based on Swype technology. I suppose it is because BlackBerry's keyboard feels more intuitive than swiping on the keyboard, but even my favorite keyboard, the Google Keyboard, which offers a swipe-typing method, is nothing compared to BlackBerry's efforts.

If you can, I'd highly recommend you find an APK of the BlackBerry keyboard. Again, it only works on Lollipop and over, but if you have that, you're golden. It's a great keyboard, and if Swype is essential to you, I don't think you'll be missing out.
