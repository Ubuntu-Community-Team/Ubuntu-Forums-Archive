### Post by CatKiller on 2008-01-07
> **imT said:**
> i'm just wondering why the ability to play games plays such an important part in choosing the os,

It's not necessarily statistically clear. For example, a lot of people who have specific problems with Ubuntu and eventually work their way here are ex-Windows people who quite like to tinker. If they didn't like to tinker, they probably wouldn't have decided to, and managed to, install Ubuntu.

The category of Windows users who like to tinker has a huge overlap with those people who like to play games on their computer. If you don't like to tinker, then you won't have the latest-and-greatest hardware, and so you won't be able to play the latest-and-greatest games.

Hence, we get a lot of posts here about "make my Windows games work on Ubuntu", which isn't necessarily indicative of the Ubuntu-using population as a whole. For example, there are four people who use computers in my house. All the computers run Ubuntu. I'm the only one that plays games on the computers with any regularity. I'm also the person that configures and fixes the computers. I'm also the only one that posts on this forum. I doubt that this correlation is atypical.

I hope this makes it clearer.

### Post by Praadur on 2008-01-07
If this question had been asked three or more years ago, I would've had a much different answer.

I would've noted that the PC has rich gaming experiences like Vampire: The Masquerade, Fallout, Ultima Online, the LucasArts adventures, and so on that really can't be matched.  I am a gamer by heart and I'd be forlorn without having any games at all around to play, I enjoy interactive stories and that's all there is to it.

However, the climate today is very different, with things to consider...

i.) The likes of Fallout has been replaced with Oblivion.  The rich tapestry of story-driven games these days seems to have been replaced with FPS, RTS, or action RPGs which are poor shadows of the likes of Baldur's Gate II.  The adventure games and story driven RPGs that I enjoyed playing are pretty much dead as far as commercial PC gaming is concerned.  The only one I can think of actually enjoying on the PC lately is Neverwinter NIghts 2.

ii.) Most innovation in the gaming field and indeed, the most interesting games and even MMOs these days comes from indie developers, the tech involved there isn't too great and wine can run such games beautifully.  Just look at the likes of the IGF and compare that with what's on the shelves for the PC currently.

iii.) Vista appeared and Microsoft decided that XP was dead, I've talked to a lot of people and nobody seems to actually like Vista at all.  So people begin to ask themselves whether Vista or one of the consoles will become their new gaming platform.

iv.) The current generation of consoles is amazing, the X-Box 360 wasn't bad as a mid-range PC replacement but the PS3 is nothing short of spectacular.  It has games which support mods, of all things!  Moreover, it has downloadable content and games that have seamless multiplayer capabilties (like Burnout Paradise).  Not to mention that the PS3 has some corkers that match the very best of anything a high-end PC   Not to mention that there are actually some story-driven games, or at least games that have very charming characters, like Uncharted.

v.) The primary market of PCs these days tends to be the humble MMO but due to MMOs using older technology to support people who can't afford top of the range machines, wine is catching up.  It's easier to catch up with items like that instead of DX10 Vista games and whatnot, and most MMO developers have said that their primary target platform is XP anyway, even for many of those still in development.

So these days, picking an OS just for gaming isn't as important, because consoles with the power of PCs exist and I feel that Ubuntu can hold its own when it comes to what people play on PCs these days.  The big names are putting out Linux clients of their FPS titles and wine is becoming more and more capable at running the popular MMO games, with more graphically complex entities such as Everuqest II recently having seen breakthroughs.

So these days it really doesn't matter as much whether one picks Windows, Linux, or Mac, for these reasons alone.  I'd honestly say that preferring Windows for the games is becoming something of a fallacy, as the only people that do these days (in my opinion) are those who're clinging to the latest hardware, and gritting their teeth and putting up with the likes of Vista in order to get their eye-candy.  I can only wonder how long they'll last before finally giving out and jumping ship to either this or the next generation of consoles (which will likely be a factor more powerful again).

I chose Ubuntu because I thought it was the right OS for my computers (desktop and laptop), and because I just felt that I was needlessly punishing my poor computers by keeping Windows around.  I certainly want to see more support for games in Ubuntu but it's less the commercial pap really and more the inventive little indie titles.  They interest me and I really do love blowing 10 minutes here or there on games like that.

After all, life on the PC can't *all* be about word documents, IRC, and web browsing... can it?
