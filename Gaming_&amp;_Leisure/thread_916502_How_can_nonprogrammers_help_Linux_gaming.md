### Post by Abras on 2008-09-11
Recently, perhaps because of some of the threads on this very forum, I have noticed an increased interest in helping open source games and the Linux gaming community in general. Unfortunately, many people interested in helping out do not possess the necessary skills (like programming, art, etc) to help the game developers directly.  So what are these people supposed to do? Well, I think it's important  to realize that there many other ways to help out "the scene" (or maybe put more accurately, create a scene) than just contributing art and code. I don't know what they all are, of course, but I've listed a few below. And I hope you guys can put forth some other suggestions.

[LIST]
[*]Script/Documentation Writing - The best programmers and artists are rarely the best writers :). So if you write fairly well you may want to join an existing game team and fix up or create the game's documentation or script. Granted most open source games don't have stories but doc writing is always important.
[*]Buy/Play existing games - Well this one is easy enough. Silly as it may seem, simply playing games on Linux helps the scene by making companies and free game devs notice and possibly invest more time and/or money into the Linux games scene. 
[*]Spread the word - Just like Linux itself there are no commercials for open source games. So its our job to publicize and cover individual games and the scene in general as much as possible.
[*]Offer support/help - There's no help line to call when an open source game doesn't work, so most people turn to the internet (specifically forums like this one) to get help. And a forum's only as helpful as the people on it.
[*]Encourage the devs - As silly as this may sound, emailing a developer of a open source game to tell them how much you really enjoyed their game can really do wonders. They get no money for their efforts, after all, so one of their biggest forms of compensation is knowing that people are enjoying their game. 
[/LIST]
Well, that's my short list, but I'm sure there are plenty more. And feel free to elaborate on anything I've already mentioned. Really, I just hope this thread takes off. The Linux games world definitely needs help and obviously it rests with all of us to change that. (No pressure, heh.)

### Post by MaximB on 2008-09-11
Ok ...those are good for FREE and Open Source games.
I want to add something regarding commercial games.

Email the commercial games developers asking for a Linux client for their game.
Post "Will there be a Linux client" threads in their forums (others may join the thread) or join existing threads of that sort in the forums.
Buy games that you like that have a NATIVE Linux client.
Encourage the developers (Indie devs are easier to convince) to make Linux client.
 
**_Some guidelines_**

To not waste your time for nothing you must know who to email/which games has a chance for a Linux client.
So ....

1. Games that were/being developed using OpenGL (not just directX) might have a Linux client.
2. Games that using the Torque Game Engine (TGE) might have a Linux client (games that where/being developed with the Torque Game Engine Advances (TGEA) will not have a Linux client because garagegames stopped supporting Linux in their "advanced" engines).
3. Indie game developers are much easier to convince making a native Linux client.

Now go and infest them with Emails and threads ;)

### Post by curvedinfinity on 2008-09-15
> **Caedis said:**
> Exactly, agreed.

In terms of my condition, being a commercial game developer for Linux, I was thinking about this. I'm personally finding that there is a bit of resentment in the OSS community toward commercial developers, and that's a problem when it comes to games.

I think I have finally realized the defining thing about what makes games different from other software, and why OSS isn't succeeding with game development yet. 

See, OSS works because its made in pieces. Each piece of the big puzzle is honed through time so it gets better and better. One hour of work put toward a piece of the puzzle "sticks" -- it never needs to be duplicated until the whole piece is obsoleted. Overall, the development is slow, but because work never needs to be duplicated, the end result just gets better and better.

Games are different though, because in order to be entertaining, one game has to be completely different from another. That means that work doesn't "stick" from one project to another, and thus the evolutionary model of OSS never has enough time to "bake" before it is already obsolete. There simply aren't enough man-hours to go between enough projects to keep people constantly entertained.

Back to the resentment thing, its tough. There are plenty of people who think money is evil. Immediately after someone shares such a viewpoint, I tend to think to myself, "so how do you live?" -- Doing a service in exchange for money is a fact of life for everyone on the planet, regardless of whether that service is being a cashier, assembly line worker, or even a programmer. More to the point, a service is different from an idea. You can sell a service, while keeping an idea free.

With that in mind, to my pragmatic sense, OSS's goal is to help give information to less fortunate people, so they have more opportunities in life. If that truly is the end-goal of OSS, then I think it is important to convince OSS game developers that it is morally acceptable to sell the **service** of making games as long as information is shared freely. If that happens, more developers will be able to work full-time, thus the number of man-hours devoted would be much higher. That has the duplicity of increasing the number of games available and also increasing the amount of educational resources available (because OSS developers like to teach). More educational resources means more new developers and thus the cycle repeats.

There are however some models for those kind of games :
1. Release the source code for free but leave the art part closed - like ID software does with their games.
People will still buy the games and make new mods, games using the open source code of the game and engine.
2. Once upon a time I published a thread in this forum about a game company with a great business model but very poor implantation.
This small company made a game demo (closed source if I recall) and wanted to sell the source code of their full game - one big payment for GPLing the code, just like it was with Blender.
This model is great IMO, but their game was very simple and not better then our current already "opened" games - this was their mistake.
If there was (or will be) a company that will make a quality game that many would love then they could sell the source code and cover the costs of development, like it almost was (we almost managed to gather the money in time) with some mmorpg game (that was surprisingly using directx only).
This model can work very well and support the free source ideology well.
It is "legal" to pay money for development as long as the source code stays free ;)

Those are the two options I see so far for free games other then mmorpgs.

### Post by calimer on 2008-09-21
There is a ton that non programmers can do.  I mainly work with 3D games but I think a lot can be applied 2D games as well.  I'm going to break down the different jobs that I see.

Programmer - codes the engine and/or game code and various scripts.

Game Designer - In charge of the game design documents, first the concept document and then the game design document.  Responsible for listing what jobs needs to be done and how things are to work across the program.  As should be responsible for creating a basic gameplay prototype.  Also responsible for storyline, dialog, etc.

Level Designer - Sometimes is a game designer too.  Creates the 3D or 2D Levels for the game.  Sometimes does basic scripting.  Responsible for fleshing out the game designers concepts.

3D artist - Responsible for creating the models.  There can be many different types of 3D artists including character modelers, map model modelers, animations, etc.

2D artist - Several areas to this as well.  Can create textures, skins, skyboxes, sprites, etc.  Also concept artists.

Project Lead - Responsible for organizing the project and making sure that things are moving forward properly.  Also responsible for making sure the team is communicating.

Promoter/PR - Get the word out on the project.  Try to get people interested in the project by sending our press releases, optimizing the webpage, setting up interviews, etc.  Also along with the project lead, might be responsible for recruiting.

Sound Artist - Creates the music and/or sound effects for the game.

Writer - Could have a dedicated writer to create the backstory, dialog, etc.

There are tool solutions for each of these as well that are free.
3D Artist - Blender
2D Artist - The Gimp, inkscape
Level Design - Platinum Arts Sandbox, GTK Radiant, etc
Audio - Audacity, free sound sites

Hopefully this helps.  I might stop back to update this too.  Take care and best of luck in game creation :D
