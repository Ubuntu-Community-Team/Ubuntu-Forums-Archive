### Post by samjh on 2007-02-10
I'm more for a tightly-integrated and powerful gaming development framework that can make porting from DirectX easier than what it is now.

Basically, something like SDL, but more stable and powerful.  At the moment DirectX is the king of the game development hill -- but it's for Windows only.

If Linux is to have the same kind of cutting-edge mainstream games as Windows it needs to:

1. Have a set of powerful, stable, and integrated development APIs for native Linux development, and/or porting of DirectX and console developed games.

2. Solve "dependency hell", and problems caused by differing package formats and installation among Linux distributions.

3. Accept closed-source, non-free software, so that game developers can actually profit from making games for Linux.

### Post by samjh on 2007-02-10
You misunderstand me.

I'm saying that games which are both open-source and free (ie. no cost to purchase) cannot generate profit.  This includes subscription-based products, since you can't generate financial profit without collecting money.

Red Hat, SUSE, Novell, etc. make open source products, but they are also sold for prices comparable to the likes of Apple and Microsoft.  They also operate on a service-oriented business model, where the most substantial revenue is generated by on-going product support and service.  But again, these involve the collection of money from the consumer.  To make it absolutely clear, none of those companies give away their products and services for free (community-support open-source projects, like Fedora and OpenSuse obviously don't count, as they are not used for generating revenue, but only as word-of-mouth marketing).

How can a game company make a profit if it doesn't sell its products and services at a price high enough to at least break-even with their expenditures?

Also making the source code of your front-line cash cow completely open source, is pretty much just giving away your propriety work to competitors.  Why do you think companies like Id Software only release old game engines under GPL?  Why not release the Doom 3 engine as open source?  Because they either include encumbered technologies, or more likely than not, releasing them will be giving too much away to their competitors.

### Post by Breepee on 2007-02-11
I'm under the impression that lots people don't understand the difference between OpenGL and DirectX. DirectX is a *huge* library of API's that make everything you'd want as a gamedeveloper easier. Direct3D is the Microsoft alternative for OpenGL, just like DirectSound is for OpenAL. Yet, Direct X includes DirectPlay, DirectMusic, DirectInput, etc, etc, etc. SDL makes an effort to make an alternative for all this API goodness, but according to people from the industry DirectX is just make life for gamedevs easier (than SDL).

Have a look at XNA Studio, Microsoft latest. You can almost click and drag an UT2004-like  game together in 15 minutes, with multiplay and support for the most diverse inputmethods. It makes developing complex games (which all current commercial titles are) very easy.

The reason DX is big is no conspiracy theories, it's just much better than anything any other platform has to offer (in Linux's case, SDL).

And please: OpenGL and Direct3D are the two 3D methods, DirectX is much much more than D3D. Most Windows games that do use OpenGL by the way, use DirectX for stuff like multiplayer and sound, just because it's easy and good (enough).
