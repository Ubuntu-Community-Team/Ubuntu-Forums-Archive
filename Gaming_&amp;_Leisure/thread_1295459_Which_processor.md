### Post by shaggy999 on 2009-10-19
It looks like if you were to swap CPUs the best you could get for $100 is the Intel Pentium Dual-Core E6500 Wolfdale @ 2.93GHz.

The cheapest Core2Duo is the E7500 Wolfdale @ 2.93 GHz for about $120.


Your CPU
========
800 MHz FSB
2x2MB L2 Cache
HT - No
VTx - Yes
95W


E7500 (Cheapest Core2Duo)
=========================
1066 MHz FSB supported
3 MB L2 cache
HT - No
VTx - Yes
65W

CPU is slightly faster (2.93 GHz vs 2.8 GHz) and the FSB is 1066 vs 800, which should result in less latency, but you now get 3 MB of unified cache versus 2 MB cache per processor. Technically you're getting less cache, but all of it is now addressable by both processors. The coin can flip either way on whether it helps or hurts performance. The 65W thermal envelope means that this processor can probably overclock way better than that Pentium D, so if you get an aftermarket cooler you could probably overclock to 3.5 GHz or so if you were so inclined and actually had a motherboard that supported overclocking.

My personal opinion: Save the $100. Come back when you got $250 and can get a nice high-end Core2Duo like the E8500 and an aftermarket cooler for overclocking.

> **gadget3000 said:**

I think I will save up to get a Core 2 Duo and then a 64-bit motherboard as, hopefully, more drivers will support it by then.

Plus I would rather have more cache than a higher clock speed unless its a massive difference. Although Core 2 Duos usually have about the same cache as the Pentium D, am I right in thinking that the Core 2 Duos will have either more or larger registers?

8xxx-series Core2's have 6 MB unified cache
7xxx-series Core2's have 3 MB unified cache
Pentium D has 2 MB cache per core totaling 4 MB

So a low-end Core2 has 25% less cache than a Pentium D, but it's fully addressable by both processors so cache can't be "wasted". High-end Core2's have 50% more cache than Pentium D and fully addressable as well.

Unified cache is really such a nice feature with multi-core CPUs because a lot of stuff is still single-threaded and so when one core is idle the other CPU can take up all the cache that would otherwise be wasted if it was only addressable by the idle cpu.

As for AMD, I haven't played with them since the original Athlon days, but if you are going to get a brand new motherboard you might want to look at AMD as well since they tend to be cheaper. But I don't think they are as good quality. I remember awhile back, maybe 8 or 9 years ago one of the major hardware sites showed a video of what would happen if you took the heatsink/fan off of a cpu while it was running Quake 3. They showcased a P3, P4, Athlon, and one of another of AMD's processors.

The P3 turned itself off to avoid damage, crashing the system, but saving the chip

The P4 throttled back the juice to reduce temperature and continued to chug away at very slow speeds until the heatsink/fan was placed back.

Both AMD processors fried. That's when I realized the difference in quality between AMD and Intel. They both perform about the same, but Intel piles in a lot of extra little features to deal with things that other people don't really think about. They also tend to run on less power.

But AMD is still a worthy look, particularly if you're on a budget.
