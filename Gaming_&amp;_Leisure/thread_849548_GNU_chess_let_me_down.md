### Post by samjh on 2008-07-04
Most club players should be able to beat GNU Chess on "easy" setting.  I can do it sometimes, about one in four or five games (played chess for my school 10+ years ago).

A lot of Masters play exhibition matches against computers (see: [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_chess#Computers_versus_humans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_chess#Computers_versus_humans).  So beating a computer isn't impossible, just hard.

GNU Chess is a pretty mediocre engine. Version 4 is ranked 149th and version 5 at 190th in the WEBC ratings list (WEBC are for engines whose source code can be provided to tournament referees, so you won't find commercial engines in the list): [http://wbec-ridderkerk.nl/html/BayesianElo_ed14.htm](http://wbec-ridderkerk.nl/html/BayesianElo_ed14.htm).  Spike, Ruffian, Crafty, etc. are heaps harder.  This rating list includes commercial engines: [http://www.computerchess.org.uk/ccrl/4040/rating_list_all.html](http://www.computerchess.org.uk/ccrl/4040/rating_list_all.html).  The ELO rating for each engines gives a rough indication of its quantified strength.  For comparison, the current world champion, Viswanathan Anand, is rated at 2798.

### Post by samjh on 2008-07-04
> **hayden92 said:**
> Although two things still bothers me... 
Why do computers not beat humans every time if they are perfect? Or are they not perfect?

And how do certain engines beat others? If they all know the same rules?Computers excel in caculations.  They can calculate combinations move-for-move farther than any human brain can in a given time period.  However, humans are better at intuitively evaluating positional play.

In short, computers are better at tactics; humans are better at strategy.  So when you have a human who has excellent tactical ability (even top club player have very good tactical ability), combined with brilliant strategic insight (essential for Master-level play), computers can be beaten.  Humans can see a position and evaluate one or two strategies which are likely to yield the most favourable results, and then evaluate tactical lines for them.  Computers tend use brute-force to just calculate whatever is possible, with more advanced algorithms using positional evaluation to attempt to prune lines of play that aren't likely to yield good results.

Also computations are expensive.  They increase in complexity exponentially per depth.  Most PC-based engines will struggle to calculate further than 6 moves within a reasonable time.  So engines with more efficient algorithms will win.  This usually means computer engines need to be programmed to evaluate positions strategically instead of brute-forcing.

When Kasparov played Deep Blue, IBM engineers had the help of US chess grand master, Joel Benjamin, to tune the engine on-the-fly to adapt to Kasparov's style.
