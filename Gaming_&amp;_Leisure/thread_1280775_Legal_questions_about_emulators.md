### Post by donkyhotay on 2009-10-02
Of course, you have to consider the source since that those links are to nintendo & CNN, both of which have a biased view debate as to whether information can be 'owned', and copyright law. Nintendo absolutely forbids *any* open source software on it's hardware, they have denied games from small developers that used open source libraries/tools on their wii downloads channel because of it. Unfortunately copyright law is a complete utter mess and there is no real answer. One the one hand you are allowed to make copies for fair use such as backups or for compatibility purposes, on the other hand the DMCA forbids cirucmvention of any security which includes taking it off the disc/cartridge and putting it on a ROM. So you have one law that says you can and another that says you can't. Which one do you follow? If you ask nintendo, RIAA, MPAA, etc. they're going to say if you even think about doing that you owe them $500 for each nanosecond you entertained the idea, more if you actually do it. On the other hand are users that say @#$% that, I paid for my legit copy of zelda2 and I can play it on my computer for compatibility purposes if I want since my NES died. By the same token it is *technically* illegal to even play a movie DVD on linux since it has to override security built into DVD's (windows and osX pay money to make it legal for them) which is why you can't play DVD's with the default install of ubuntu and have to get the medibuntu repos and install some extra libraries.

> **Bölvaður said:**

It is *most definitely *true.  The distribution and downloading of copyrighted materials is illegal.  Period, end.  What *is *legal, on the other hand (but would still find its fair share of debate), is the copying and backing up of said materials for your own personal use as long as you do not redistribute it, as pointed out by donkyhotay.

If you need more recent, *unbiased *documentation, please see this: [http://gaming.wikia.com/wiki/Emulator](http://gaming.wikia.com/wiki/Emulator)

If you do decide to seek rom images out, you do put yourself at risk-- even though it is highly unlikely that you would get sued over it.  Instead of suing downloaders, companies like Nintendo and Sega usually take action against the sites that host the roms by issuing cease and desist orders which force them to remove download links.  So what you may find out is that certain roms just aren't available.

If you still have unanswered legal questions about your specific region, doing a search for "international copyright laws" would be a good place to start.
