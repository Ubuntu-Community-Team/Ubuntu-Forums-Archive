About the games;
> Penumbra: Overture is a first person adventure game which focuses on story, immersion and puzzles. Instead of using violence to progress the player has to use his/her wits to guide Philip on his quest to unravel the past.


Like all good nightmares, Philip's begins with something all too real - his mother's death. The days following the funeral are characterized by nothing, save for an incessant feeling of abandonment. Until, that is, he receives a letter from a dead man. 

Philip's father left before he was born, taking his reasons with him. Now, here he is, opening up the door from beyond the grave. That door leads to more questions, and those questions lead to Greenland. Philip follows the clues - they're all he has left. 

On leaving the final signs of human civilization behind him, in search of the location mentioned in his father's ambiguous notes, Philip wonders if he's left some part of his humanity behind as well. Soon, that will be the least of his fears. 

Now, Philip needs your help. He's found an inexplicable metal hatch, in the middle of a frozen wasteland. Inside, is something yet more unfathomable. 

Step into the unknown.

### Post by Brain-free on 2008-11-21
Having played both Overture and Black Plague, I feel I need to clarify some things.

First things first, the "weapons" you have in the game (pick-axe, dynamite etc.) are usually used for anything but killing. In order to hit an enemy it needs very good coordination of mouse moves and usually the enemies need more than 10 blows. Even if it's dynamites we 're talking about.

Secondly, there are no such things as knives, chainsaws or other blood-spattering weapons. 

So, do you kill enemies? Yep, open a valve, light a match, throw a bridge and many other indirect ways I don't recall.

Besides Penumbra, I've also played Resident Evil and Alone in the Dark and in my opinion, none of these 2 games (which are fine by all means, especially Resident Evil 2) manage to capture this game's atmosphere.

And this is not because of gore or decapitating zombies but it's purely based on mindf***ing, your story interlaced with other on-going stories that makes you fear of other human presences or feel terribly sorry for them. And even though I like to think I have the stomach for these games, there were times when I was saying "F**k it, this is... it's just too much".

So, what I'm saying is that the game will be as good as you want to immerse into it. Or no good at all if the lack of bullets nags you. I would personally recommend to play the demo and decide for yourself. And although Requiem seems more puzzle-oriented, (which is not necessarily a bad thing) the previous two games are worth checking out.




Brain "Oh my god, I've never wasted so much gray matter in describing a horror game" Free
