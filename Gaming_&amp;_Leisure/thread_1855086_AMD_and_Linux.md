### Post by cwwilson721 on 2011-10-07
Rather than saying "FooBar SUCKS!", lets get into this a little deeper.

Intel: While their graphics and HD playback has improved somewhat with the newest "Sandy Bridge" CPUs, their performance in OpenGL (the Linux 'standard') leaves ALOT to be desired. The actual implementation of OpenGL by Intel has issues on both the driver side, and the hardware side, not to mention the actual speed of OpenGL 3D speeds is VERY subpar. As a gaming GPU, in Linux, you can toss Intel into the "only if I cannot do anything else" pile, and move on from there.

AMD/ATi: They make some of the best hardware out there. As far as speed, heat, power requirements, and price, they cannot really be beat. (Hold your comments. There is a rather large BUT coming). Tom's Hardware usually recommends them on their Graphics "Best Buy" lists, for most price points. And here is the big BUT: Tom's Hardware only reccomends for Windows. And, as all here can attest, Windows ain't Linux. 

The issue in Linux is the proprietary drivers for AMD. While the open source AMD drivers can (and do) run some 3D OpenGL games rather well, for most serious gaming, you need the Catalyst Drivers. And AMD lags quite a bit when it comes to providing up-to-date and easy to install and use drivers for the Linux world. They have made great strides in this area recently, but are rather questionable about how this actually works. If you have an 'older' AMD/ATi card/chip, it may not be supported AT ALL AT ANY TIME. You might be totally out of luck. While the same chip/card can run a title very well in Windows, it may not be supported at all in Linux. And then there is the actual drivers themselves, which have been riddled with issues about installing and running. It seems to have no rhyme or reason if your specific system will install the drivers, and if they do install, if they will work.

With that said, however, AMD/ATi has a VERY good product, that if it DOES work for you, will work very well.

Nvidia: Has the hardware on par with AMD/ATI, and MILES better than Intel. Support with the proprietary drivers on older hardware is FANTASTIC. And ease of driver install couldn't be easier. What else is there to say? I ONLY use Nvidia (when I have a choice) on my machines.

So, in conclusion, it goes as such:

Intel: Okay for day to day use, or even HD movies (most recent Sandy Bridge. Forget anything older), but NOT FOR GAMING

AMD/ATi: IF you can get the Catalyst drivers to install and run correctly, they are VERY GOOD performance wise. If you do have Catalyst issues, tho, you may have to wait awhile for them to be resolved. And older chips/cards may not have a chance.

Nvidia: Great hardware, easy to install and use drivers, and great performance. Support in Linux is top-notch.

So, it goes: Nvidia > AMD/ATi > Crayons > Intel

### Post by efikkan on 2011-10-08
Intel integrated graphics is not a real alternative for gaming. The performance is low, lacking support for modern OpenGL specifications, and the latency to the shared memory is considerably higher than dedicated solutions. A low-end solution from nVidia is a superior choice, even it you are not gaming and doing something like a media center, a low-end CPU + GT 520 will be cheap and handle any video like a charm.

**ubupirate:**  nVidia's products are not horribly overpriced, that's not true at all. In some price segments AMD offer better performance for your money in general performance, but you have to remember their OpenGL performance might be quite different from the general Direct3D performance. AMD is getting decent performance in OpenGL 2.x nowadays, but with modern OpenGL (which more and more games use) AMD's performance is poor compared to nVidia. In the higher segments, nVidia does offer good performance for the price in general performance (such as GTX 560 Ti, GTX 580 etc.), and considering the fact that these would greatly outperform the competitors from AMD under GNU/Linux, nVidia is as of today the safe choice.

Although this might change in the future, those of you who games such as Rage might have discovered that nVidia has superior performance, because AMD has poor OpenGL 3.x+ performance (Windows platforms). I'm hoping that as a result of this top gaming title that AMD will actually improve their OpenGL performance on different platforms, so we GNU/Linux users might benefit from this in the future.

Why do I know this about OpenGL performance on platforms you might think? Bacause I'm an OpenGL programmer.
