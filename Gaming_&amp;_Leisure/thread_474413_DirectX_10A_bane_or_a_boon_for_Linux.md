### Post by cogadh on 2007-06-15
I was just as horrified as you by Shadowrun's minimum requirements (I was the one who posted them), but what Microsoft is doing is just smart business that is bad for the consumer's wallet. 

Analysts predict that the number of PCs in homes worldwide will double to about 1.3 billion by 2010, but they don't predict that Microsoft's 95% market share will decrease significantly. Since there are going to be about 650 million new PCs purchased over the next three years and Microsoft is forcing Windows system manufacturers to adopt Vista exclusively, they are creating an incredibly closed but profitable Windows gaming market, which will likely force those, like myself, who are resisting Vista to upgrade both hardware and OS. It's called planned obsolescence, auto manufacturers have been doing it for years, now Microsoft and the PC hardware manufacturers are following suit. It sucks, but that is the way business works.

In the immediate future, we may see some influx of OpenGL gaming on a minor scale (due to the updated OpenGL versions coming soon), but as has already been pointed out, DX is just too cost effective right now for OpenGL to really take off. Once the PC sales numbers for the next two quarters come in, game developers will see that the Vista market is growing too fast and too large to ignore and will end up embracing DX10. In the end, Windows XP and Linux users will lose out.

### Post by Kazade on 2007-06-22
One thing that Ubuntu should do is reach out to game developers to develop cross-platform games. 

OpenGL is as powerful as D3D simply because they are both just APIs to the hardware. As soon as a feature is released that is advertised as being in D3D it is also available as an extension in OpenGL (OpenGL has extensions for Geometry shaders etc.), in fact a lot of the time it is the other way (remember D3D has annual releases) OpenGL extensions are sometimes added by vendors before they are implemented in D3D .

There are more than worthy open alternatives to all parts of DX. SDL and OpenGL provide an excellent means to get cross-platform high performance graphics, combine that with libraries such as DevIL(image loading), OpenAL, HawkNL(networking) and ODE (physics engine) which all follow the same naming conventions as OpenGL and you have a complete API toolset.

The only other thing missing on Linux is the tools. There are some good 3D modellers out there, Blender and Wings3D for example so that's not really a problem, then of course the GIMP is as good as (if not better than) Photoshop. The tools that are missing are things like shader editors, texture editors for producing normal and specular maps. Specialized tools are what's missing, but this is the key thing YOU CAN USE WINDOWS FOR THIS.

So to sum up, there is NO reason to use DirectX, ever. There are open alternatives to everything and they are cross-platform so they'd reach a wider audience.

Improving gaming on Linux needs several things:

1. Consolidate all of the above mentioned libraries together into some kind of tool kit preferably integrated into a cross-platform IDE (Code::Blocks?)
2. A website to host the development of cross-platform games and the possibility of selling via that site.
3. Mark Shuttleworth to start lobbying for support for cross-platform game development

Linux is ready for gaming, we just need the big developers to realize that it is and its easy to develop cross platform.
