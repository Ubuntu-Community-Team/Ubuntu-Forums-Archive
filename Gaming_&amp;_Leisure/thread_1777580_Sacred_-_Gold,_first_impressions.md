### Post by handy on 2011-06-07
I bought this some weeks ago when it was on sale at Tux Games. It cost me about $18- Oz, including freight from the UK to Oz. :)

I've tried various RPGs over the last 25+ years & always there was a problem, whether it was unreliability, or dreadful playing ergonomics (NwN comes to mind), or just a poor game with no depth (NwN2 comes to mind).

So when Sacred - Gold, came up at the right price I thought I'd give it a shot, & I'm so glad I did. :)

Firstly I installed it on the 24" iMac (it took a few inserts before the Sony optical drive would read the DVD) after that it all went fine, upgrades over the net & all. Until I ran it full screen or windowed & found that I had too much mouse lag to be able to play the game. I expect that it is the open-source driver stack that I'm using for the AMD/ATi Radeon 2600 Pro / 256MB, GPU. If anyone knows a solution I'm all ears?

So, not to be thwarted, I installed the game on my old home built box; Athlon64 3500+, 2GB RAM, AGP slotted nVidia XFX 7950GT / 512 RAM. After multiple inserts to get the Pioneer (this time) DVD drive to recognise the media, the installation went well. Except that when I try to play in full screen mode, the graphics system (GPU / 24" Samsung monitor) & game combination positions the game such that the game display is offset down a couple of inches. This cuts off the bottom of the game display & makes it unplayable. (Unfortunately this problem can't be solved via simple monitor display adjustments.)

So, to windowed mode, which is certainly not my first preference, & it works fine. But the window is 1024x768 & can't be resized. Another "so", I commented out my normal "Modes" line in the xorg.conf, which usually runs the system at 1920x1200. & made another that starts with 1280x1024.

This gives me a working solution, with the largest playable window I can use. Not perfect, but good enough.

Since then I've spent a few days playing the game, & am still learning, though I've certainly learned plenty.

I'm playing a Dwarf, which I've set up as a "Trader" Dwarf, (they can get at the Trading skill at level 3, long before any of the other types of characters you can play) who's at level 17, with 17 skill points added to Trading, though with other buffs his Trader skill is currently running at 20. 

This gives my Dwarf a great advantage when using the merchants & once you get your character to Braverock Castle (big place), & find the merchant in the merchant square (not the one up by the portal, which you want to activate as you'll be using it a lot in the future) & standing right next to him is Henno the Trader, with a really quick & easy quest for you to do. Do that & when you return to Henno, he becomes a merchant too. This gives you the ability to stand between the two merchants & check one out, close him, check the other, close him, check the other. Each time you close one & check another trader, the stock is reset. :D

This is called "Bravemart" by many players. It is a great way to get good equipment.

I've been playing at Bronze (the easiest level) so far, though I expect that by about level 20, I'll have the Dwarf well enough equipped to export his character, & then start a new game using him at whatever level he's at, with all equipment & points & such, & this time begin at the Silver difficulty, with levelled up opposition that is more aggressive, harder to kill, & healing doesn't work as well. :)

Anyway, overall, the mechanics of the game play is simple & well thought out. There is plenty to think about re. the way you want to build & play your character. The ability to both wear rings & amulets & to be able to incorporate them via a blacksmith into your armour & weapons, really works great. It is easy to see that it is of prime importance that you buff up with these if you want to be able to survive into the more difficult levels of the game, even at Bronze, let alone the levels of difficulty that you can use beyond it.

I've really only barely scraped the surface of this game, as each of the 8 character types plays very differently, & requires a whole new way of dealing with the game, which they start differently as well.

Then there is the ability to export characters & use them for multi-player via LAN, or on-line.

Anyway, enough of this, I've got some adventuring to do... ;)

Addictive game. :)
