### Post by desktorp on 2011-06-11
From a marketing perspective, I hate that they call it a gaming pc when it's featuring an overclocked Sempron processor.  I suppose it could be based on the style of the case.  Marketing fluff aside, I would not foresee any problems.  It's all relative to what you want it to do.

All of the new desktop CPUs generally feature 64-bit capability, this one included.  A lot of people opt for 32-bit operating systems anyhow, because of software availability.  This is a very quickly vanishing problem, though some snags can still frustrate people.  It's also about RAM.  In 32-bit Ubuntu, you will get somewhere around 3.2GB of ram, even though there is physically 4GB installed.  So it's much less of an incentive, until you pass the 4GB barrier.

Just for an example, I have an Intel Atom 330 + Nvidia 9600GT running Ubuntu 10.04 and it's great.  It runs Minecraft, plays videos, streams, etc.. (it also runs 24/7 as a file server) I chose to use 64-bit after a while, because I finally got comfortable with the whole Flash situation.  Flash used to be much more of a hangup than it is now.  Make sure to get the 64-bit "preview" of Flash, if you do go that direction.

At least they're telling you it's an overclocked Sempron, I guess.. some people would just call it a 3.0ghz AMD and be done with it.  :o

Agreed on that. I have an i3 processor (dual core, but can run 4 threads at once with hyper-threading), the i5 and i7 also have a power-boost feature. But even with the i3, when a process gets in an infinite loop (uses 100% of CPU) I only notice it about 5 minutes later, because the fans begin to blow a little bit more, I don't notice anything about the speed. Once I've had 3 processes running in an infinite loop and didn't notice it.

I would advise dual core even for normal desktop usage. The advantages of quad and hexacore over dualcore are minimal (at least for now, until better software is written to handle all the cores as efficient as possible).
