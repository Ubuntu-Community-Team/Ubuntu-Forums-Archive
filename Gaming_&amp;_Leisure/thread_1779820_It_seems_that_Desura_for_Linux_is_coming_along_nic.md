### Post by Quadunit404 on 2011-06-11
[http://www.desura.com/groups/linux-gamers/news/desura-linux-development-we-have-ui](http://www.desura.com/groups/linux-gamers/news/desura-linux-development-we-have-ui)

The Linux Engineer of DesuraNET, Keith Poole, has shown off new screenshots of Desura for Linux, apparently running on Ubuntu 10.10(?) and showing off how basic the UI is right now. Well, at least they have a somewhat functional UI. Apparently, even though the Linux client is based on wxWidgets and GTK+ it won't respect your GTK+ theme and use the same appearance as Windows. I guess. Remember, this is still under heavy development and it could end up respecting your GTK theme later on.

If you don't know what Desura is, it's basically like Steam, except all the games are indie games developed by smalltime "companies" or single people in their free time, these games are all DRM-free and many of these games are either free or cost a few bucks, usually $15 or less, sometimes more. It also makes mods easier to access, as DesuraNET also owns ModDB and therefore both the Desura and ModDB communities are heavily tied, whereas with Steam you're on your own when you want to mod your games. Personally, I can't wait until this reaches at least public beta on Linux as it'll be a great stride in establishing Linux as a gaming platform.

The webpage of Desura is somewhat disturbing to me. Why are they listing games which aren't sold / distributed via Desura ? :confused:

It would be completely suficent to list them on an additional page (e.g. supported / launchable games).

Their approach seems to me like they want to fill up their homepage with well know titles (e.g. Portal 2) to capture the interest of peoples. 

To me the biggest issue still remains unsolved ! Why do I need a distribution platform if I do not have much to distribute ?

While Desura (or any other similar Linux-supporting service) is a welcome product, we still do not have much valuable AAA titles on Linux which are published at a close timeframe to the Mac / Windows release. Most of the stuff commercially available is either Indy Stuff (which is no a bad thing) or overprized Windows titles ported over Linux (Sacred, Knights & Merchants, etc). The rest of games is either completely free or Windows stuff people play under WINE.

Lets face it: Linux is not a primary target gaming platform for bigger gaming companies / publisher and probably never will be ! 

As much as I dislike Cedega for their behaviour: They did a smart move concentrating on building up a publishing platform for Intel CE4000 devices plus using Cedega as base for a development / emulation environment. Gametree Linux is similar to this. It shall enable Windows-based game developers to run their games on Linux with no or only minor changes.

Imagine someone like Crossover stepping in to provide a stable custom-build QA-checked version of WINE with focus on performance and compatibility (with a certain set of supported games). Someone who coaches Windows-based development companies to make their title run within this special stabilized version of Wine. Every company could package their game with a defined version of WINE with a static set of libs and distribute them.

Now imagine someone like Crossover works closely together with someone like Desura to distribute the packaged software. If this specific WINE-version would be released in an open-source manor (e.g. as a patch set to a certain WINE version), also Windows freeware devs could gain advantages. They could use this as base for packaging their games also for Linux.

At least in theory I see some clear advantages:
We (the Linux guys) could see more game-titles on Linux being closely released to the Windows counterparts.

They (the Windows devs) could bring their titles to another platform (more customers) without too much modifications to the code (depending on the used game engine, D3D stuff used and so on). Less modifications would mean less workhours and thus less money to spent.

They (the "Crossover"-alike company) could earn their money by providing consulting / support on how to adapt either the Windows-based game and/or adapting the "stable" version of WINE to make the game run. Somewhat similar to the "offer" of Codeweavers to provide companies support porting their software to WINE: [http://www.codeweavers.com/services/engagements/wine_port/](http://www.codeweavers.com/services/engagements/wine_port/)

They (the "Desura" company) could earn (more) money by being the premier platform providing working Windows titles on our beloved Linux. 

There could also be companies similar to LGP "porting" over games from Windows to Linux as contractors.

Just my 200U$ :)
