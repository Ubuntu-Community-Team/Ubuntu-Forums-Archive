### Post by tokyovigilante on 2006-08-25
> **enigma_0Z said:**
> I looked at the dual cores, and they are just waaaaay out of my price range (you're talking about the Athlon 64 X2, right?).

I still wholeheartedly recommend the 3800+ X2. The performance gain is huge compared to the price differential, I can transcode DVD->MPEG4 while running WoW at 40fps under Linux. The performance gain under Linux with multicore is far greater than in Windows, because of Linux's superior multitasking and NUMA support.
> **enigma_0Z said:**
> 
2 GB of RAM was another thing I was thinking about. I was planning on getting 1 GB (x1). I looked at the docs on the motherboard and got the impression that it would not work without pairs. However, I've reviewed it and I do think (as I did before) taht 1 GB (1x) would be the better idea...

2 GB is better than 1 GB just because of simple maths, you can get twice as much into RAM (ie run twice as much at the same time) before you need to page to disc.

DDR is a standard feature of PC 3200 RAM (the kind you need). Basically, the system bus runs at 200 MHz, and the RAM shifts data on both the up and down voltage changes of the cycle, to achieve a 400MHz memory speed (hence Dual Data Rate). 

Dual Channel requires two identical DIMMs, and uses them both in parallel to achieve faster access times. 

Both are good, so the ideal config is two 1GB DIMMs, for a total of 2GB running at 400MHz with dual channel. You can use 2x512 for 1GB of dual channel, or get a single 1GB with the intention of adding another as price permits, as I have.
> **enigma_0Z said:**
> 
I was planning on a 939, is the AM2 better?

Socket 939 and AM2 have the same chips, the difference is that AM2 supports the new DDR2 RAM, which gives higher bus speeds (533, 667 and 800 MHz, compared with DDR which gives 400Mhz maximum. The prices for chips and motherboards seem comparable, but the RAM is more expensive. I'm unsure of the performance gain, but certainly its less than a faster processor and I/O speed.
> **enigma_0Z said:**
> 
About DDR & Dual channel, the papers say to get the "exact same RAM". Does this *really* matter?
 Yes, see above.
> **enigma_0Z said:**
> I
About 32 bit vs. 64 bit, is there any real performance gain running 32 bit apps on a 64 bit processor? I've got an AMD T-Bird @ 1GHz. And what stability/compatability issues can I expect from a 64 bit system (if any)? I know that there is a Ubuntu 64 bit.

Yes, though it is minimal. The real point is that there are no available 32 bit chips from AMD, so it is a moot point. The real issue is whether you want to run 32 bit or 64 bit Ubuntu on them. I highly recommend running 64-bit. Theres not a lot of gain in browsing, word processing and gaming, (indeed I'm running 32-bit Firefox for plugin compatibility) but the real gains come from numerically-intense calculations, such as video-encoding and processing. Working with 64 bit floating point and integer calculations is up to 4x faster on 64-bit native. Stability is absolutely not an issue, I haven't had a kernel panic or system crash 
period.
> **enigma_0Z said:**
>  
Once again, SATA when I can afford it. I agree that it is the better performer, but I'd rather get a new CPU & stuff and rely on RAM (I'll deal with loading times! :mrgreen: ).

Honestly I really think you should get SATA-II. ATA is obselete, and your motherboard already has SATA-II support. The price difference for the Hitachi ATA vs SATA is negligible, and considering hard drives are the slowest part of any system, this is the most important component to maximise. ATA has a maximum throughput of 1.3GBit/s, whereas SATAs is 3.0GB/s. SATA also has native command queueing, which in a nutshell reorders the disc read requests from the OS to optimise the movement of the drive heads and speed access. Your system will be noticeably more responsive with SATA, you'd be surprised how dependent on disk I/O performance is, especially if you're gaming.

You have to consider not just price, but value for money, its a substantial investment, and with a bit of tweaking, you'll have a machine that runs faster and lasts longer before obselecence.
