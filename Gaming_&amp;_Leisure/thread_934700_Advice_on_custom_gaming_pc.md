### Post by HappyCheeze on 2008-09-30
The thing that worries me is your PSU (Power supply unit). Its designed for Crossfire, which is ATI's own thing. 

You've selected two Nvivia Geforce cards which means if you plan on SLI, you'll want an SLI ready psu. The picture of the box for the psu doesn't say, usually theres a easy to see circle in a corner that says something like "SLI READY". 



PCIE is its own unique slot that is specifically dedicated for grapics cards ONLY. It has a very fast transfer to meet the demand for the card. 

To determine how much voltage you'll need, you can use a wattage calculator. 

I use Newegg's 

[http://educations.newegg.com/tool/psucalc/index.html](http://educations.newegg.com/tool/psucalc/index.html)

Your motherboard will fit in your case. You can tell by clicking on specifications, and looking down at "Form Factor" It is ATX, which is one of a few different standards of motherboard sizes.  Your case is an ATX midtower, meaning it has the proper dimensions.

With those two THICK graphics cards the space might get a little tight, so maybe a full tower case or a special SLI case might be better for a pair of those fat cards.

Other than that, things looks pretty good. I haven't used that GPU myself (I'm using an 8800 GT), I think I have that same or very similar ram, the OCZ Vista upgrade, very nice. One thing you can do is google search for benchmarks for your graphics cards and CPU and even ram and mobo and see how well they perform. People are constantly testing the newest hardware to help folks like us decide whats best for our gaming performance and money.


I hope this helps, if you have more questions lemme know.

### Post by HappyCheeze on 2008-10-01
Gimmick or no, you need a PSU that has the specific voltage requirements for an SLI setup (the power is going to all the right places, or in this case the graphics cards). A friend of mine built a machine, powered it on and it ran choppy and couldn't detect the GPU, even though he had more than enough voltage for the components. After some research, sent back his psu and ordered an SLI ready psu and it worked fine. 

Do some homework to reduce hairpulling moments.
