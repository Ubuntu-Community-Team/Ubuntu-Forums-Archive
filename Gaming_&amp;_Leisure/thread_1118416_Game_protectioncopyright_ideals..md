### Post by TheBuzzSaw on 2009-04-07
Piracy is not the end of the software industry; low quality is. Piracy is the modern scapegoat for content creators. If sales are down, "somehow it *must* relate to piracy". Silly companies such as the RIAA and the MPAA equate one instance of piracy to one "lost sale", but they operate on the premise that if they were able to somehow block all piracy, every pirate would give up and go buy the product. That mentality is simply incorrect. If you stop 100 pirates, you're lucky to recover one sale from that. Piracy is indeed unethical and dishonest on a number of levels, but I find the corporate reaction to be far more inappropriate. The Dark Knight had record-breaking sales at the box office; it also had record-breaking downloads over BitTorrent. Must be a paradox...

Now, when it comes to game protection, I say give it up. The only way to secure revenue is to **give customers a reason to buy**. Believe it or not, not *everyone* in the world is a freeloading cheapskate. (Actually, that's what cracks me up about these hardcore government crackdowns. Many of these "pirates" are 14-year-olds who have no money to buy the content anyway.) If a game has a large world, I like having a full color detailed map! If the game is complicated and/or has lots of numbers/stats, I like having a manual detailing those statistics. If the game is from a company I like, I purchase the "special edition" that comes with extra shiny packaging.

The only practical way to combat piracy is (as has been mentioned already) online play. MMO games feature natural protection since the creators have complete control over the server and who accesses it. Other online games simply require a valid game key to access (this is especially handy in that it empowers the moderators to ban game keys at will based on cheating, harassment, etc.).

The problem with *any* kind of protection on **single player games** is that it blocks the paying customer and not the pirate. On one side, we have the guy who spent $50 fiddling with the online activation and/or ensuring that the disc is in the tray; on the other side, we have the pirate who is enjoying the game hassle-free: no online activation and no disc. There is no form of DRM (copy-protection) that pirates have not cracked. Not only that, pirates crack these protections usually within **days** of release. EA's Spore was cracked *before* it was released.

Games fail due to a plethora of issues. Piracy has a *minor* impact on sales; the increased publicity that piracy provides usually negates that anyway.
