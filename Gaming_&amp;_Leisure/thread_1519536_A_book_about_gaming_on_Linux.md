### Post by echolinux on 2010-06-29
Thanks for all of these great suggestions, sakuramboo! I'll definitely get in touch Linux Game Publishing and Ryan Gordon. I've got a huge list of open source games I'm going to start working through and I'm definitely interested in speaking with those developers.

It's true that my focus is mostly on larger game companies, though, and my premise is this: people mostly develop games for the best system to handle them, and a system can only be competitive if there is investment into it. If people don't invest in Linux with an eye towards gaming, that function of Linux will die. At the top of the Kickstarter page, I reference Commodore 64, which faded due to old age. My own experience was with 3D0. That console was pretty awesome, and I felt it was ahead of its time, but developers didn't make games for it and it faded.

There will probably continue to be open source game developers... but what if Microsoft made DirectX free tomorrow? Or what if more engines like Unreal were made free and usable for developers? Part of the motivation for developing a game on Linux is to use a free toolset, but what if you could get a free toolset that will help your game appeal to a wider marketshare?

These thoughts make me want to help draw more attention to the value of gaming on Linux, and native games certainly do that. But the market weight of Activision/Blizzard, Valve, and EA can't be ignored either. I guarantee there's at least someone at those companies thinking about this, and I wonder what their thoughts are.

You've given me a lot of good leads, sakuramboo. I do remember the Cedega + WoW incident several years ago (which didn't affect me, though I was playing WoW + Cedega at the time--I feel that Blizzard handled that affair very well in the end), and I think that any time a company shows willingness to work with the community is a positive. Will that willingness still be there in 5 years, or 10? That's what I hope to find out.

It very indirectly supports Windows, but that discussion is pretty tangential to the book. The bitter truth is that mainstream games are more popular than open source games, and getting mainstream games and, more importantly, mainstream developers working on Linux is one of the keys to increasing adoption rates of Linux. Wine/Cedega are gateways to getting people to switch from Windows to Linux, and I'm subsequently a big fan of them.

> **MaximB said:**

Think of it more as a story about the development of gaming on Linux and the future of gaming on Linux. There will be a bit of introductory stuff, a la

[LIST]
[*]Why would I want to game on Linux?
[*]What is Wine? What is Cedega?
[*]Are open source games any good?
[/LIST]

That sort of thing. Note that this isn't an outline, nor are these chapter titles--development's too early, and it's too close to bedtime for me to think super-clearly :-P But that's the introductory gist.

It's not a technical manual, though some familiarity with the fact that Windows is an operating system, rather than A Computer in and of itself will help. The primary focus is on development: what is the future of gaming in general, with a particular eye towards Linux. When a professor at a gaming college teaches, do they talk about OpenGL or DirectX? Do their students know about Linux? Do game companies see a future there, or are they drinking the Microsoft Kool-Aid? Do Linux developers care about encouraging gaming? I want to pull together as comprehensive a view as possible to get a sense for whether Linux gaming will still be around in 5-10 years and what it might look like, or whether we'll have degenerated to a single point for games (be it console or an PC). Things have shifted a lot over the last several years, but if you follow the money and the planning, patterns will start to emerge. Where are the companies putting their money? That's what I'll be looking at.

My hope and intent is to say yes, Linux will be viable, and it's in the best interest of companies to embrace OpenGL and engagement with the community. But if every company I talk with says they're going the opposite direction... well, we'll see.

Originally Wine was licensed with a BSD license, i.e. a much more permissive open source license that doesn't actually require that changes are made available, or even the original code. Basically, when Cedega started they took the Wine source-code, made some changes and marketed it as a completely new product. This is why Wine is now licensed under the GPL.
