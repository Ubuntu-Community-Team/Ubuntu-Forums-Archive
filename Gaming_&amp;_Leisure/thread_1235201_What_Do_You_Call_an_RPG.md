### Post by coldReactive on 2009-08-08
This is what I consider to be a JRPG:

    * Turn-Based or using a battle system similar to Tales of Vesperia/Star Ocean
    * Stats System (HP, Attack Power, Defense Power, etc.)
    * Job Class System (Certain Jobs have different weapons, in JRPGs, it is uncommon to be able to change your job class.)
    * Level System for characters (higher levels produce better stats for your character)
    * Money system
    * At least two characters that you can control. These characters are involved in a rich storyline.
 

This is what I call an Action RPG:

    * Action, real time usually (Like Kingdom Hearts)
    * Stats System
    * Job Class System
    * Level System (NOT A RANK System, a regular level system that can power your stats) still for characters (if this isn't included, might as well call it just a plain action game.)
    * Money system
    * At least ONE character that you can control. This character has a storyline of course.


This is what I call an SRPG:

    * Strategy Grid System where you command characters to move to certain blocks/etc. of the grid.
    * Stats System
    * Job Class System (Most SRPGs have the ability to change Job Class.)
    * Level system still for characters (remove this and it's just a strategy game if you ask me.)
    * Money system
    * At least 2 to 5 characters that you control (notice lack of CAN.) Characters that you control at the start of the game set the story.


Notice commonplaces each RPG has? The back bone of RPGs then in my eyes is as follows:

    * Stats System
    * Job Class System
    * Level System that, when at higher levels, powers up your character permanently for the rest of the game (unlike Dawn of Mana.)
    * Money System
    * At Least One character with a story to tell. 


And there you have it.

### Post by shaggy999 on 2009-08-09
Personally, I think a "Role-Playing Game" aka RPG is any game that allows me to take on an a character and experience as integrated of an experience that technology can allow me at the time.

I think experience points and such are simply an old way of doing things that people haven't been able to shake off. Personally, I think dinking around trying to get "experience points" is stupid and arbitrary. They were fine in the days of the NES/SNES/D&D days, but really not all that interesting anymore. I still play FFVI, but I expect more from modern RPGs. RPG developers are stuck in the 70s rolling dice.

I would consider a game such as "Assassins Creed" to be an RPG because I really feel like I'm an assassin and I can decide how I want to go about doing things (sort of). Character "development" is done by giving the character new moves and weapons as the player progresses rather than handing out XP. Similiarly I consider a game such as "Far Cry 2" to be an RPG because I take on the role of a mercenary and go about things the way I want. Do I want to go in with heavy artillary guns blazing or bring in lightweight stealth weapons and perform the misison at night? Both games I would probably specify more specifically as "Action-RPG". Neither are "true rpgs" in the traditional sense, nor are they rpgs in a modern sense, but they are closer to what I think of as an rpg. I'm into playing characters - not churning out XP.

The point of role-playing is to BECOME the character. THINK like the character. If a game world allows you the freedom to do that, I consider it in some sense to be an RPG. XP is not required, and in some ways actually *hinders* role-playing. "I've reached the bad guy, oh no! My level isn't high enough to defeat him!" Then I have to go grind xp for an hour just to continue the story. As homestar would say, "BORE-ING!"

You'll notice over time that developers that really want to push the role-playing part of role-playing games are going to push away from the idea of XP. The progression of Elder Scrolls III -> IV -> Fallout 3 from Bethesda is a perfect example. Sure, Fallout 3 has "stats" like strength and charisma, but the character REALLY grows by gaining new abilities (hacking computers, for example) and 'perks'. Changing your 'base' stats has much less of an effect on your character.

I hope to see more games like Fallout 3 that stress multiple ways to do things, open game worlds, huge dialog trees, and actually allowing a player to make choices that develop a character.

Now that I've said all that. I'm going to go back to FFVI, which I was playing on zsnes. :)

### Post by Naegling23 on 2009-08-11
an rpg can really be anything, the base of it is that there is character building, ie, progression, leveling, increased power/stats, or whatever you want to call it.  Beyond that, there can be any sort of variation.  Games like deus ex were essentially first person RPG's in that you assumed a character, and "built them up" during the progression of the game.  

I think a lot of people put RPG's into two main styles, I would call them eastern and western (easter refering to the asian contenant, and western being north america).  The western rpg's tend to be DandD based, think lord of the rings, beowulf, grittier, and more adult looking, swords, bow and arrows, and dragon type stuff (neverwinter nights, WoW, everquest, etc).  The eastern ones tend to be more anime style, futururistic, brightly colored, guns, spaceships, and main characters with oversized weapons and bright pink and or blue hair.  (im looking at you final fantasy).

But you also see a lot of offshoots and new takes on rpg games, like eve online, or the star wars rpg games.  Heck if you want to get technical, you might have to include gran turismo as an rpg too.
