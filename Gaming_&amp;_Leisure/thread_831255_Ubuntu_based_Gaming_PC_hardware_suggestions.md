### Post by tbrminsanity on 2008-06-16
I have a couple of suggestions for you:

Don't go with a ATI graphics card like I did.  It is a pain in the butt to setup perfectly for gaming and as a result I still don't have full 3D acceleration (even with the official drivers).  You will have to ask around if the NVidia cards are any better.

Compare the price with a bunch of places as you may find better deals at tigerdirect.com or ncix.com .  Don't be afraid to have parts from a bunch of different companies (especially if it saves you a few $ in the end).

If you plan to dual boot (Win/Lin) I strongly suggest setting up Windows before installing Ubuntu.  It will save you a lot of heart ache.  Like wise make a list of all the programs you use now and want on the new system.  If you have to backup any files before hand the better (an ounce of preparation is better then years of regret).

Upgrading is a perfect time to optimize your personal data.  Go through your personal files and only save the stuff you still need.  I had files I hadn't touched in 3 years on my system wasting space.

I'm a bit of a sound freak and I don't like listening to my computer hum away beside me.  I got an acoustic case and I don't think I could ever get another case.  This gets me on another point, save anything you may need from your old computer for your new one (big one is extra screws for your drives [it makes them quieter]).

You will have to check with your local area but my city had a plan for recycling old computer parts and I was able to get $100 for the metal in my old machine.  I saw it as the final gift from my old trusty computer.

### Post by Cappy on 2008-06-17
I am using an AMD computer. If you choose AMD then you can pick the Asus M3A-H (or the M3A) which is linux apic and acpi compatible (my last one wasn't). It also has an onboard linux distro you can boot into making it ideal for fixing boot problems that can't be corrected from the grub editor.

Edit: Oops, I didn't see you wanted a micro-ATX; The Asus M3A-H is a full ATX motherboard. **Edit: The M3A-H has a clock drift of 4 minutes a day. There is a fix here: [http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=835288](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=835288)**

Graphics cards:
If it has to work out of the box then get a Nvidia 8600; they are in the $80-100 range.

The 8800s are about $150 for the cheap ones.

Finally, the 9600s are nice for $150 but you would have to install drivers with a program that does it for you [called Envy]. The 9600s are more powerful than the 8800s.

I don't know about the other games, but I know that the 8600 can run TF2 on Full AA, Full AF + Highest Settings without a hitch. I suspect similar out of the other games.

As for 64-bit/32-bit ubuntu ... I run 64-bit. I would say 90% of the people who have problems with "64-bit Ubuntu" would have the same problem if they did the same thing on 32-bit ubuntu. There's nothing difficult about 64-bit on Hardy so I would say give it a try. Most anything that had to be done in the past , such as installing flash, is done by automated scripts now.
