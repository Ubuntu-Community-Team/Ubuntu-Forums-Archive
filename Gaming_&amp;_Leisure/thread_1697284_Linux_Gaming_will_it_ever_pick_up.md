### Post by sakuramboo on 2011-03-02
To OP:

There is more to the answer than just a simple Yes or No. First, you need to look at the past and present before you can look at the future. Let us look at the past.

Loki Games ported Windows games to Linux. They were quite good at it. But, they spent more than they were making and ultimately closed shop. The outcome of which made other larger development companies get the impression that Linux games just don't sell.

Now, the real question is, why didn't they sell? Truth be told, they actually did. Loki just thought that they would sell better. This hurt the Linux gaming market in quite a big way.

However, some of the bigger companies understood what really happened with Loki and decided to keep on going with Linux support. Id and Epic both stuck true to the porting of Windows titles to Linux. They learned early on that in order to sell, they need to port, not just the game, but license the engine as well. This means developing in a way to allow them easy porting (remember, we are not just talking about Windows and Linux but consoles too).

Sadly, Epic was not able to bring UT3 over to Linux. This hurt the image of Epic Games to the Linux community. As for why? Well, that is still up in the air, but I remember reading something about a lawsuit causing the hold up and ultimate cancelling of the port, but I could be wrong on that one.

Meanwhile, Id not only continues to port their titles, but after the shelf life of their engine is over, they open source their engine. This means that Linux WILL see their titles, eventually. Whether using their original game files or creating our own art, we will see it.

Now, let us look at the present.

Linux Game Publishing is, in my opinion, doing an amazing job. They may not actually do the ports of some of the titles, but they make sure to make those titles available. Sure, their titles may be old and/or out dated, but I would gladly pay full price for an old game, so long as it shows my support for Linux. But, I digress.

The area that really seems to be "getting it" is the indie market. However, even in there, there is plenty of feet stopping and close minded attitudes towards Linux.

### Post by sakuramboo on 2011-03-03
> **slooksterpsv said:**
> EDIT: Granted it's all about "business", but still, it wouldn't take too much effort to port it to Linux (I wouldn't think).As I said in my post, it depends on how the engine was written. Companies like Id and Epic develop their engines with cross-compiling in mind. This makes porting rather trivial. Especially when they disconnected the client from the engine. This makes game development ten times easier when it comes to porting. Basically, drop in an OpenGL renderer, change some library calls and file structure and you are done (there is way more to it than just that, but to just give an example).

The porting issue comes from poor programming. If a program was written on the fly, quick and dirty, good luck porting it. This would pretty much require rewritting of the entire code. But, if the code was written to be compartmentalized, all that would be needed would be extra #include <> lines and more if statements to determine which drivers/OS/features it needs to use.

So, the difficulty of porting comes from the developers. Not all games have separate server/client applications. Most FPS's now a days do, but then porting rests on the ability of the engine. If the engine supports cross-platforms, then a client can be made on any platform. But, many platform games do not have separate client/servers. Everything is tied together. These types of games become harder and harder to port once the use of the WinAPI gets used.

This is also why many still don't believe that Valve is porting their Steam Engine. The Steam Engine Clients are so heavily tied into Windows that porting it is a serious undertaking. And the question is, who benefits from such a task? In the short run, Valve would take a serious hit, financially. In the long run, Valve would be really lucky if they break even.
