Warning: Wall of Text
What follows is one mans journey to justify the Linux gaming market! Read and rejoice (or sigh, your choice)!

How can the Linux gaming market be proved if we can only buy Windows versions of most games?

The Dilemma:

John  Carmack has said that porting their games to Linux hasn't been a  financial decision but rather to strengthen their code because they  don't see much on the Linux sales front. However seeing as Linux users  have to buy the Windows version of their games and then download a  separate binary it seems like his statistics would be off.
Their only  definite stats are Quake Live players and to be quite honest it is  probably the most useless statistic they could have. The Quake-like  games scene is probably the MOST SATURATED and over represented aspect  of Linux gaming out there and I see no reason why people would switch to  quake live from one of the many clones (or otherwise they are pretty  bored of that type of game altogether (I played Quake Live for a few  hours... then I went back to Nexuiz... and then got bored and played  something else and haven't been back since)).

Or what about the many players who use Wine to play many of their games? Once again, counts as a Windows sale.
How  about the users who dual boot Windows and Linux and would stop using  Windows altogether if their favorite games were available for Linux?  Their sales also count as a Windows sale.
And of course their is a  lack of a good games distribution system such as Steam which automates  the download and install process for accessibility purposes.

Anyone  who follows the Indie games scene can see that Linux versions are on  the rise and are consistently profitable despite lacking large amounts  of advertising. Many would dismiss it saying "the whole Linux gaming  market goes behind each indie game" and that the sales of a commercial  game "would be to small to make back the porting costs". To which I'd  say that this has not been tested PROPERLY (as in native Linux AAA title  that has publicity behind it and is released at the same time as it's  Windows counterpart).
They would also site Loki software going  bankrupt in attempts to cater to the Linux market. To which I respond  "it was a bunch of games that came out years earlier and Linux wasn't  nearly as mature as it is now!"

I'm sure I could find more  examples but I hope the point is brought out that it is an up-hill  battle to prove that Linux has a viable gaming market.

The upside:

Here are a few points in favor of Linux gaming's viability that I have come up with:
1. Indie scene for Linux is proving strong and serves as a decent indicator that people are looking for good Linux native games.
2.  Linux users tend to be on the more tech savvy side of things, even the  beginners. Tech savvy people are a lot more likely to enjoy gaming... I  don't need to back up that point... shhhh! Therefore the amount of  people on Linux likely to buy games would be higher in comparison to the  people on Windows likely to game.
To explain better: lets say  Windows has 90% market and Linux has 5%. Of the 90% Windows has only 10%  will likely game. Of Linux's 5%, 50% would be likely to game. Therefore  of the total gaming market 9% will be Windows users and Linux will be  2.5%. Thats a heck of a lot more viable sounding to me! (Both of the  Linux and Windows numbers are likely higher than their real values,  these are only selected for simplicities sake.)
3. Wine seems to be  full of people working hard to get their games working (quaint compared  to the epic scale of point 2's overblown explanation (in length, not in  validity)).
4. The sheer number of dedicated Linux gaming sites and  "Linux version?" threads on forums all over despite the "small" Linux  gaming market.

Well I've nearly beaten this horse to death (thats horrible!) but there is one last section to this rant of mine.

What can we do?:

This one is tricky because I can't simply say "Buy AAA Linux native games" because there aren't any.

What  you can do however is pick some of your favorite games that you have  running on Wine, or your empty except for gaming Windows partition, or  your downloaded binary games and run (metaphorically) over to the  developers forum and post screenshots with a topic like "I bought the  Windows version but I'm playing on Linux, count me as a Linux sale!" and  maybe just maybe something might happen if enough people do this (being  realistic it'd take quite a few people to do this, like tens of  thousands on multiple forums).
Now if that sounds like a lot of work  (and to be reasonable, it is. Good luck finding an ID Software forum, I  was looking for a half hour before this and... wait, maybe the Bethsoft  forums now that they merged... hmmm... oops, just thinking aloud. Back  to the post!) you can do two other things.
Post in the Steam forums thread about [bringing Steam to Linux]("http://forums.steampowered.com/forums/showthread.php?t=1173683")  (187 pages long as of writing), and could also put in a request to  Ubuntu to make getting gaming content a priority at the store being  added to the Ubuntu Software Center (if Steam wont come to Linux than  Linux should create it's own alternative! Thats what Linux has done in  the past and it's worked out pretty good so far. Hows the music store  doing by the way? Should add some game soundtracks (Killer 7 soundtrack  is awesome (Listening to that right now in fact (Arrgh! Digression! Back  to post!)))).

And to end it all...

What are your suggestions? (Also many tldr and ridicule for the made up on the spot statistic under the label of "2.")

### Post by Tijok on 2010-10-01
I think that point 1 is the greatest possible outlet to show market viability of Linux gaming. Why? I'm so glad that you asked!

1. The Indie Film scene.
This may seem like a stretch, but hear me out. In the nineties we all know that indie films saw a huge boost and became well known for taking small amounts of money and making huge ROIs if/when they succeeded. Indie gaming has seen a similar path, but is not quite there yet. The success of Plants vs. Zombies, World of Goo, the Humble Indie Bundle, and others has certainly helped, and the small dev teams that usually develop these games are a golden opportunity for investors strapped for cash.

2. Casual Gaming.
With the two areas of indie gaming and casual gaming beginning to look more like a venn diagram rather than a divided realm, indie gaming opens itself up to a HUGE population of users known to spend reasonable amounts of money an unreasonable amount of times (farmville).
Combine this with the ever increasing ease of use, security, and stability of Ubuntu systems, and you have one happy (and paying) housewife (or househusband).

3. Counterculture Sticks Together
As you hinted at, Linux is quickly making close friends with many Indie devs, if this relationship can remain strong, both platforms and markets can help bring each other users. By sharing and approaching the market from two completely different angles, a larger share for both will obviously be possible and likely.

Also, I've noticed a few more of [these types]("http://games.slashdot.org/article.pl?sid=09/03/10/0453237") of articles indicating a strong possibility of native DirectX support, and that only makes things easier, right?
