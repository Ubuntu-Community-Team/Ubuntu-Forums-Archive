Between a AAA high budget mainstream Windows/console game with eye popping visuals and an interesting indie RPG for Linux, I will gladly spend my money on the latter.

### Post by burkdecroix on 2010-02-12
One thing I think is lacking from the Linux-native games is story.

Independent of genre, there are a lot of great engines out there (both 2D and 3D) that provide excellent entertainment value and fall under the category of free and open source software. A majority of these games, however, don't have much in the way of storyline. They point their focus at the mechanics of the engine, and in a lot of the more popular cases offer only muliplayer style gameplay. In my opinion, even the singleplayer games that are available tend to feel like playing around with a game engine that has a simple set of rules for completion (i.e. puzzle games, arcade games, flight and driving simulators). I'm not saying this is a bad thing. In fact, it's how great engines are made even better (through successive modification). But I would like to see more games that use an involving plot and interesting characters to distract from the underlying engine, and create a more immersive singleplayer experience.

There are a few excellent games out there already that sport amazing story. The Battle for Wesnoth, for one, is an amazing engine that has several great storylines for the player to get into. Others like Fall of Imiryn and Adonthell sport creative stories as well, though I haven't played them yet.

I know that native support implies that the game runs without an abstraction layer (such as an emulator), and that there are plenty of wonderful, story-driven Linux ports of commercial (and even independent) games out there: FreeDink, Doom 3, Cave Story, etc.. But I feel that there is a lack of plot, either from the developer or the community for games and engines developed (at least initially) for Linux and other open source operating systems.

Now, I understand that creating an involving storyline and interesting characters can be more of an undertaking than designing a game on top of an engine, but there are solutions. Any game that a designer puts creative effort into deserves compensation, and not every game for Linux has to be FOSS. Also, at a time where distributions like Ubuntu/X.org are reaching their maturity when it comes to graphical support, focusing development (and marketing) on Linux compatibility for a multiplatform game with great story could score some serious popularity. With the amount effort put into designing gameplay, story, and the appropriate hype, it would more than warrant charging for an end product of quality.

Too Long? Synopsis: Games with story that are designed for (not ported to) Linux (not ruling out multiplatform design). Charging for them would not be ridiculous.
