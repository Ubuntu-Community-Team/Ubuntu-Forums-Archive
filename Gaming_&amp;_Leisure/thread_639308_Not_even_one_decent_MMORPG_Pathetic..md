### Post by keyboardashtray on 2007-12-13
I can't believe that there isn't even one halfway decent MMORPG floating around the FOSS world. I mean, even when I was on Windows I was surprised no one was attempting to take the throne from World of Warcraft. A very playable game but seriously lacking in real character development. The market is screaming for competition. But not even a token MMORPG for GNU/Linux? C'mon.

Runescape. That is all there is, is freakin' Runescape, if you want a playable game. The rest are perpetual alphas and betas that will never be playable.

Planeshift looks like it might have potential until you dig a little deeper and see it has been in development for years and has gone and is going nowhere.

Regnum isn't really an option when the unsupported Linux client only works for a couple graphics cards. Even if it did work, it looks like a Guild Wars clone, which is a crap game from the immersion-PVE viewpoint. No character development, etc.

Eternal Lands I'd play if it was 2D. Unfortunately, it reminds me of the early nineties 3D games that turned me away from anything 3D for more than a decade. You can't look up - it is hard to feel like you have even basic control of your character when your eyes are glued to the ground. One of those games that should serve as a reminder that you can't do 3D half assed and retain playability. Doesn't help that the NPC's refer to you as noob, etc. If I want that I'll just play Runescape.

I was surprised to see one that had slipped past the radar actually end up in the Ubuntu repos, The Mana World - hopes were quickly shattered. No magic. Little research affirms that it's just another in the pile of never-will-be-done. 

In desperation I reached for Tibia, a game that I've known about for years and refused to try because it seemed so lacking in character creation. My jaw dropped when problem after problem led to my discovery that Tibia, of all games, requires a select few graphics cards (like Regnum). Dear Lord, that game doesn't deserve to require a monitor, let alone fancy-dancy graphics cards. I almost used Wine for it but had to stop myself, remembering what I would be looking forward to.

What happens? Why do these games get to, what looks like to me, 90 percent complete, and then just lose all their steam? They get the hard parts done, and then just give up and putz out right at the finish line - actual game content.

There doesn't need to be some grand 3D venture. Geez, Runescape is testament to that, that a game doesn't need graphics to get big. So why can't someone slap together something and actually get a playable game out there? 2D but with actual content - don't just stick someone in a pile of rats and expect them to figure it out.

I see all these counter - posts saying "well, get involved with one! That's the great thing about FOSS" blah blah blah - if anything this has changed my idealistic opinion on FOSS slightly. When you look around and see games that have started before the millenium and haven't made crap for progress, it's not exactly inspiration to get involved. Not to mention the fact that you don't have to dig very deep on any of these games' forums to see that they are pretty much as close minded about feedback as your average commercial venture.

### Post by cogadh on 2007-12-13
Personally, I think the "RPG" should be removed from "MMORPG". No one actually role plays in them, not like in a real role playing game. Real role playing games involve small groups of people playing as their individual characters through an actual story with a defined beginnining, middle and end. MMOs have lost that story element completely (persistent subplots do not make a story). The only multiplayer games I have played that still maintain the RPG elements are not really MMOs, but just regular multiplayer games, like Neverwinter Nights and Vampire: Redemption.

That being said, I don't think the open source model is really the right one for making an MMO, which is why there aren't any really good open source MMOs. They require too much financial investment and ongoing commiment for the average open source developer. Just maintaining the game servers can be a huge expense and continuously updating the game with new content is a career in itself.

In a console game, there is one main problem in the world, and eventually your goal is to end that problem. In MMORPG's, they can't really have an end goal, because the world has to go on (you don't beat these games). So usually they involve quests, or series of quests, that hint to a grandiose goal.

So bearing that in mind, WoW *does *have a backstory and plot, maybe not the richest, but it's easy to bash WoW being they are the McDonalds of mmo's. And it really looks like another language, when Dwarves speak, it looks like a Scandinavian language, Tauren like Native American/Spanish - very cool. The big ones, like power-balancing (some classes and races get treated better than others)  and an almost entirely equipment-based character advancement. Your character is able to learn new skills as they level, but there is the constant need to upgrade equipment to gain "stats" (your standard issue strength, intelligence, etc.). In that sense, it really isn't any different from the old console RPG's - they were just as bad, you pretty much got a new suit of armor every other time you unlocked a new town in the old Final Fantasy games.

In every other game, all spells and skills amount to just some way of either dealing damage, healing damage, preventing damage, OR augmenting or reducing the player or enemies skills, for the same intent (to increase or reduce damage). Well, in WoW there are plenty of neat abilities that go beyond simple combat.
