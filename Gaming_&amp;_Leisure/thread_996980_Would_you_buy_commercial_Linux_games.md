### Post by johnkzin on 2008-11-29
Yes, I would.  And if I had been a linux user back when (Loki?) was around, I would have bought their ports of native games.

re: "not FOSS" and "DRM":

In an ideal world, everything would be DRM-Free FOSS.  But this isn't an ideal world, and it's not just about the freedom of the user, it's also about the freedom of the developer.

To me, infrastructure and commodity components (OS, firmware, API == infrastrucructure; othello/reverse == commodity, basic office type apps == commodity, Doom3 == not commodity) should be free.

Non-commodity components COULD be free, if you can make an alternate business model out of them, like charging for support, micro-transactions, etc.  But I do firmly believe developers and investors have a right to recover their time/money investment from a project, if that's what they want to do.  The question is: what's the best/right way to do it?  Again, we don't live in an ideal world.

As long as the DRM isn't invasive (like some company putting rootkits and spyware into their DRM infrastructure), then I don't have a true problem with DRM.  I don't like it, but it's part of the "if I chose to use the fruit of their creativity, instead of creating for myself, or using a 3rd party's creation instead" tade-off.

Now, abusing FOSS through patents, and using copyrights for racketeering (like the RIAA and MPAA), those things do bother me.  I'd avoid game companies that were in that category.
