### Post by efikkan on 2010-08-04
There should be no doubt that GNU/Linux is a viable market for games.

Ubuntu has over 12 million users, over 15 million netbooks use GNU/Linux (most of these does not use Ubuntu), in addition Fedora claims to have approx. 20 million users (wich I think is a little high), but anyway there are many millions using Fedora and many other desktop distros. If you add up these numbers you'll realize there are way more than 50 million users, probably 100 million.

But anyway, what's more important is the amount of software these users will buy. We've seen it time after time, quality games wich are ported to GNU/Linux with good packaging and well covered in media sell well on GNU/Linux (and Mac). Titles include World of Goo, Mystic Mine, Osmos and of course the Humble Indie Bundle all prove that good games sell well on GNU/Linux. Many of these has sold over 20% to GNU/Linux users. Some claim this is due to the lack of games for GNU/Linux, but that's not true. The main reason is the GNU/Linux user is more interested in gaming is then willing to buy more games if they are available. The GNU/Linux ports that has been unsuccessful is mainly due to bad packaging, late release or lack of media attention.

A quality GNU/Linux port can expect to sell several hundred thousand copies for large titles, wich will cover the expense of porting the game.

Games already ported to Mac OS X can be very easily ported to GNU/Linux. The APIs used are cross platform, and if SDL or a similar library is used, the game can be more or less directly ported. Then the two main workloads will be bugfixing and packaging. Testing for a lot of distros will take some time. Games wich are available on PlayStation 3 are also easy to port, but it would take a little more effort.

Renderers wich only uses Direct3D will take up to several months for a few skilled programmers to port. The problem would be fixed functionality wich needs to be rewritten for OpenGL manually. But HLSL can be more or less converted to GLSL and then tweaked a little bit. The main problem would be other libraries used in the game.


One thing that annoys me is the people who screams they don't want to buy the games unless they are open source. These people might actually scare away game developers wich are considering porting to GNU/Linux. I do love open source software, but demanding all software for GNU/Linux to be open source is the wrong way to go. The right way to do it is to embrace the quality proprietary software we get and not try to crack it. Then maybe someday the developers realize GNU/Linux users are great customers, and then when we politely ask for DRM-free or open source, maybe there is a tiny chance they'll listen. But anyway, proprietary games will serve the GNU/Linux platform more than almost no quality games at all!

But still, the large problem is to get the major companies to realize GNU/Linux is a viable gaming platform. The problem is not the coders themselves but the management. These are the people who needs to hear we are not a group of Linux hippies wich demand everything to be open source. They need to hear the success stories from other quality games and how GNU/Linux users embrace quality software.

Actually, the reverse is true. Big budget != flexible budget. The bigger budgets get, the more risk. This makes devs/publishers angsty, making them less and less likely to bet on unknowns. Many people (including myself) bemoan the rather unimaginative state of modern mainstream gaming, and it exists precisely because of large budgets and publishers who don't want to take risks because of that.

Also, as a developers/publishers size increases, it makes that 10% more and more negligible on their balance sheet. Indie devs are actively motivated to go after that 10% as the income they get is more limited generally, and those smaller markets may also happen to be starved markets where they'll get much more publicity and demand will be relatively higher for them (which is exactly what happens with Mac and Linux).

In other words, small markets are much more attractive to small businesses, including in video games. The real interesting part is when [that market grows and so too does the business along side it]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disruptive_technology").

### Post by theraje on 2010-08-04
Keep in mind that a lot of the tech that big game development houses use is Windows-centric (DirectX, Visual Studio, et al), and their code often reflects that. Instead of using cross-platform windowing kits and such, they tend to write code for .NET or the MS-Windows API. This makes the code tightly wound to Windows systems, so much so that changing it would take a LOT of man-hours.

MS has held the desktop graphics market by the ... well, you know ... for a number of years. This is probably because of the poor state of graphics programming between DOS and Windows 95. MS developed DirectX (at the time known as WinG, IIRC) and billed it as a sort of de facto standard for high-speed graphics in Windows 95... and at the time, Windows was the king of the consumer desktop market. Bada-bing, bada-boom, MS locked themselves in place to corner the market.

Also, colleges in the US tend to favor MS-related technologies because of the perks involved. So, we have a lot of people coming out of college with skills in MS tech, but less skill in cross-platform technologies such as OpenGL and SDL. It would take a metric tonne of time and money (because time is money in business) to retrain their developers and have them start cranking out fresh libraries and code.

And let's not even mention the game console or Japanese game development worlds...

But yeah, the Linux gaming market is best served by independent and small developers. I think it would change if, as has been previously mentioned, the "Big Boys" would realize that open source software can sell. Release the engine code for free, and sell the game content that goes into it. This has worked well in the past for those who have tried it, but unfortunately, those developers had more say in their work, and less pressure from men in $10,000 monkey suits to get their game out tomorrow.
