It comes with a few adapters ... including a couple power adapters that go from the standard 4-pin to the 6-pin that the cards use nowadays, a mini-HDMI -> HDMI, and DVI -> VGA.  Just make sure you have a 500W or better (preferably 600 or so, just to be safe) PSU, and enough power connections.  Check out the last pic  =o)

Those adapters are fairly cheap, if you need/want to pick up one separately just in case:

[http://www.newegg.com/Product/ProductList.aspx?Submit=ENE&DEPA=0&Order=BESTMATCH&Description=DVI+to+VGA&x=0&y=0]("http://www.newegg.com/Product/ProductList.aspx?Submit=ENE&DEPA=0&Order=BESTMATCH&Description=DVI+to+VGA&x=0&y=0")

### Post by 1clue on 2011-02-07
I would stay away from the old analog connector entirely.  Stick with an entirely digital signal path.

I use an nvidia gt200 with dual monitor support and I like it, but I don't game so I have no idea how it performs that way.  It handles movies really well.

I do with video cards what I do with other hardware that I want decent current performance without any specific desires:  "Go for the knee."

Start listing cards starting at the top and working down.  There is a point below which you get a lot of performance for a little price increase, and above which you get a little performance for a lot of price increase.  That's the knee.  Best bang for your buck.
