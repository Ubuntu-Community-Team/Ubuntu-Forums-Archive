### Post by hikaricore on 2010-05-20
The only thing preventing porting of modern games to linux is the non-interest of the larger gaming comanies to do so.
It has absolutely nothing to do with licensing or any such nonsense.

Many of the games you listed are playable though dosbox or wine or console emulators, some even already have official native port or reverse engineered engines.
For example the original simcity has gone opensource as micropolis and was ported to all platforms a couple years ago.  Simcity 3000 had a commercially available linux port.
NWN has a native port, I personally own it.

That said many of these games that you consider "abandonware" are not such and are still owned by the companies that made them or by those who bought them out.

### Post by rjaguar3 on 2010-07-22
> **mepatuhoo said:**
> as i know of some copy rights for phones that ran out after a set amount of years and at that time every phone company started using the tech.

I can't tell exactly what you mean without further clarification, but I believe the rights you are referring to in this case are not copyrights but patents, which act under completely different rules and grant broader protection (to ideas, methods, processes, and the like, which copyright does not protect) for a shorter period of time (currently 20 years from filing).

>  that term extension act sounds interesting i should read up on that act. is that law effect all copyrights or only a sub set of copyrights as in software and movies? (being from Disney i would think it was a focus on media but i could be wrong)

All copyrights that were still effective in 1998 (not expired, not lapsed, not forfeited, renewed where applicable) were retroactively given a 20-year extension of term.  All works created after 1998 automatically receive this longer term.
