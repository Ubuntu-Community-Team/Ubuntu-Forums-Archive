### Post by cogadh on 2007-06-15
Wine is the original, Cedega and CrossOver are just offshoots of that project. Wine is updated regularly (monthly) and nearly every update includes some new DirectX functionality. CrossOver supplies the major funding for Wine and only just recently added the gaming functionality that Wine has been working on. Cedega is specifically designed for installing and running games, but it does not have the regular update cycle that Wine has and doesn't necessarily contribute anything significant back into the Wine tree (I'm not a big fan of Cedega). Both CrossOver and Cedega have a user-friendly GUI, while Wine is a old school CLI application.

The major difference between Wine and the others is cost. Wine is free (open source and cost free), Cedega and CrossOver are both paid applications. Cedega has a $5 US monthly fee (minimum three months) and CrossOver is $39.95 US for the standard edition.

If you really want to get into gaming, but don't want to learn to use the terminal interface, I recommend CrossOver, not Cedega. At least the money and effort spent on CrossOver goes right back to the source project, Wine. I prefer to use Wine, it works fine for everything I use it for (KOTOR 1 & 2, Half-Life 1 & 2, other FPS and RPG games).
