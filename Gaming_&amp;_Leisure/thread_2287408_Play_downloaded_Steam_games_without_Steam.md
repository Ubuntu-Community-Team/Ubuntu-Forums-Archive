### Post by efflandt on 2015-07-20
Only "some" Steam games can run independently of Steam (typically single player only games) and that may be rare. I think the only game I have like that is "Sir You are being Hunted" (which alternately has a 64-bit Linux version), but not sure if that changed or will change now that they are beta testing multi-player. Most multi-player games typically need to keep track of achievements on Steam Cloud and items on an item server, along with Steam for connecting to game servers and VAC to avoid cheating. Without Steam you would only be able to play off-line, if even that.

### Post by mystics on 2015-07-20
From my experience with Steam, things are a complete hit-or-miss in offline mode to begin with, and I have no idea what would happen with the games that worked if the Steam client wasn't even there. Even singleplayer-only games would sometimes refuse to run because I hadn't logged into Steam in X number of days (pretty sure this happened with Portal or Half-Life 2), and that's assuming they worked for any amount of time. In short, be sure you test out your games before removing the Steam client and make sure that they'll stay working in the long run.

But, basically, Steam is a piece of DRM. It's a dressed up piece of DRM, and it's more appealing than the alternative DRMs, but you should still expect to occasionally run into the problems associated with it being DRM.

### Post by oldrocker99 on 2015-07-24
GOG does sell nothing but non-DRM games; when they released the original Witcher game with no DRM, sales went through the roof, which confirmed Red Projekt's aversion to DRM was justified (GOG is a wholly-owned subsidiary of Red Projekt, developers of the Witcher games). 

I am a happy Steam customer; yes; it's proprietary. However, the freedom of GNU/Linux includes **my right** to install and use closed-source, proprietary software (such as 99% of the Steam games for Linux are, also, as are 99% of the games GOG sells) if I choose to. It also includes the right of others to keep any closed-source software off their computers. If I wanted an OS which is 100% FOSS, I'd choose to use Debian and use only FOSS drivers, and that's definitely a great way to go for those who use their freedom for their own computers . I am no big fan of DRM, but I also feel that programmers deserve to be paid for their efforts, especially the smaller game developers (and I'm talking about the companies which have fewer than five employees, and there are a lot of those: Illwinter, PHR00T, etc). I gladly contributed to the Kickstarter campaigns for Wasteland 2, Pillars of Eternity, War for the Underworld, and Torment, as well as some others which weren't as successful. (I'm looking at you, American McGee and Akaniero.)

I gladly paid a small amount for the FOSS Vulture Nethack and Slash'Em, to help the two-man developer team improve the already swell interface. And the GNU license doesn't say that they can't sell their program if they make the source code available, and they do. And yes, it's in the Ubuntu repos already, and yes, I paid for it gladly. By the way, the beloved 3D modeling and animation program Blender is available **for free** on Steam, which will certainly increase its user base.

I don't understand the reason to make a big deal on how Steam operates. How often are you really offline long enough that Steam won't let you play your game? But for a multiplayer game... Requirement to use steam should not be a concern. At least with Steam you have a guaranteed content delivery platform, cloud, social network and more that you can rest assured will continue on for ages.

Sure, you're "dependant" on Steam, but they are providing you a reliable service in exchange meaning in the long term view of things - you have less risk of 'losing access' to your content (those games with multiplayer features that no longer function because the games devs retired the servers? Yeah you lost access to content you paid for)
