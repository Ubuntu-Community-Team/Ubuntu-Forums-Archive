### Post by leech on 2006-05-01
Here are the reasons I've found, after thinking about this for a very long time.

1) Support.  With so many different linux distributions out there, having technical support for games is a nightmare.

2) Drivers.  This kind of goes with the support thing.  What are game developers supposed to do, tell everyone to buy nVidia?  I don't think that would go over too well.  Then again, maybe ATI would get a clue then...

3) Copy Protection.  Look at all the crazy copy protection that are on games for Windows.  Star Force anyone?  No sane person that knows anything about computers wants that crap on their system.  Since most people who run linux are a bit more sane and know more about computers... no Star Force for them.  (Ok, maybe not the more sane bit...)

4) Market Share.  As stated before, there is a confusion on how much market share there is.  Personally, I think there are a lot more desktop linux users than anyone realizes.  Think China, or other non-USA countries.  Linux is huge elsewhere, much like in the Atari ST/Amiga days, they were always much bigger in Europe than here in the US.  Probably explains why Cold War was made for Linux (Mindware I think is in Czech.)  It's hard to determine how many people have downloaded Linux, and you can't go by how many are purchased or sent out to people.

5) OEMs.  Right now there aren't many OEMs that sell Linux pre-installed.  Yes ,this also helps get commercial games.  Think if nVidia or ATI started selling their cards with direct Linux support and packaged games with them.  Hmmm, I sense a sweet Quake 4 marketing scheme there.  Package up Quake 4, a 7800GT and an Ubuntu CD.  A Package for Humans, who want to blow up Troggs (or whatever they are called in that.)

6) APIs.  Most developers have had DirectX pushed down their throats so much that they don't even realize that OpenGL 2.0 can do just as much.   Thaat's why NWN2 probably won't see the light of day on Linux (though at one point in the Faq, they state that the OpenGL part of the engine is being upgraded as well.  We'll have to see on that one...)  The thing is, a lot of game engines out there support both OpenGL and DirectX.  Rainbow Six 3: Raven Shield is for the Mac, yet was never ported to Linux (and doesn't work under Wine or Cedega either).  It's an Unreal Engine based game.  Prime example of something that could very easily be ported, but won't because of the publisher.  World of Warcraft is another one that is API neutral and could have been ported...

7) Cedega/Wine.  Why should a developer spend all that extra time to make a native game port when it can be emulated just fine.  World of Warcraft again comes under this section.  From what I have seen on the net, there was a Linux native version during the beta, but it was killed off.  I think it's because Blizzard works with Transgaming to get it to work under Cedega.  The reason I think this?  Because Transgaming posts on their website "Fixes to work with 1.10 patch of WoW" before the 1.10 patch even came out!

8) Dependencies.  A lot of the old Loki games are dependent on certain libraries that are now pretty much out of date and they require some tweaks here and there to get working on a modern distribution.

9) $$$$$.  Last but not least, money.  Whether it's in the form of Microsoft paying out deals to get exclusive rights (I know they do it with Xbox, wouldn't surprise me if they did it with computer games) or that the publishers themselves feel that Linux users want everything for free.  I don't know if Microsoft pays out for computer games or not, but I wouldn't be terribly shocked if that were the case.  Look again at NWN2.  Considering that we know OpenGL can do just as much as DirectX can, and the engine that they used was pure OpenGL to begin with, yet they are taking all the extra time and development costs to make it DirectX.....  Neverwinter Nights I tend to think is one of the best games for Linux.  Not to mention the huge modding community for it, and all those servers that run on Linux.....

Leech

### Post by leech on 2006-05-01
Actually the Amiga pretty much kicked the PC's butt until about the 486+VGA era.  Even then, you had to fork out a whole lot more cash to get a decent gaming platform on the PC.  You could practically pick up the entire Amiga for the price of a good stereo sound card.

Much like many things in this world though, the crappy things win out, because people don't research what they buy.

Good examples of this is Windows winning out over other Operating Systems (OS/2 for example), Dos vs. Unix, Beta vs. VHS, PPC vs. x86....  The list goes on.

Anyone who remembers the olden days of the PC when we had the Old Mac, Atari ST and Amiga against the IBM compatible computers, will know that with any of the Motorola based systems you never had to worry about IRQs or setting up DOS drivers for video etc.  In fact even direct ports of games were twice the size on PCs as they were on other platforms.  Ultima 6 was 4mb on the PC but only 2mb on the ST, and it was a direct port.
