### Post by NinjaNumberNine on 2009-12-30
I don't doubt that you'd be better, I have next to no art skills. And yes, it would take a ton off the load, now we have 2 working on the audio, and a graphics designer. For me, it doubled, (the graphics) nay triples the development, a three step process:
**Brainstorm.**
Get new ideas, how possible/practical?
**Create the character/tile.**
Open the GIMP or TileStudio and begin the masterpiece.
**Import.**
(this is about as long of a step as the other ones, but NovaShell worlds are about 80 clicks away (not quite that bad, and I've made a shortcut by now)  and I had to organize it all to be findable. The BIGGEST downfall of NovaShell that I have noticed, possibly fixable, is that it lacks an import preview. I'll have to ask Seth about it- maybe he can help? (He's the designer of NovaShell)  
Now that you can see the tiles in the game window, you have to put "feet" to them- add collision data, material type, etc. This is pretty straightforward for tiles, but when you get into the players it's more difficult- you organize the art in its respective folder for each act that player will do.
**Give the player a "brain".**
A player might look pretty cool just pasted there on the map, but 9 to 10 you're going to want it to move. And move when it's told. (or a key is pressed) 
That requires the manual making of two scripts- a *.lua file(scripted profile, controls reactions, gravity, input and so forth) and an *.xml file. (controls all things visual, and sets animation properties like playback speed, attack sequence movement, and collision data.)
Game Organization
Finally, compile the game (build levels, secret places, special actions) in such a way that it will be entertaining as well as challenging.

So, portets, welcome to the team!

### Post by Nevon on 2009-12-30
> **NinjaNumberNine said:**
> That is VERY well done, regardless.
Thanks, but it's really just a glorified doodle. Creating original artwork is much harder than working from a sketch.

> **NinjaNumberNine said:**
> The problem is, everything looks so glossy in vector... Is that at all avoidable?
Definitely avoidable. It all depends on whether or not you overuse highlights. If you look at the example I made, the character doesn't look very glossy at all, does it? That's because I haven't added any pure-white highlights at all. The only highlights and shadows I've added are simply darker or lighter shades of whatever the underlying color is. 

For example: [glossy](http://i50.tinypic.com/2r6p4hy.png) & [not glossy](http://i46.tinypic.com/2em0rgo.png).

> **NinjaNumberNine said:**
> It can, if I remember right, be stretched to infinity and still be smooth? That throws pixels in the dust...
Well, kinda sorta. You have to stretch it when it's in vector form, and then export it as a rasterized image. So you couldn't just take the image I posted and shrink/stretch that to infinity, as it's already been exported as a png. Also, you can't shrink it down to infinity. Depending on the amount of details, things will get lost when it's scaled down. Nevertheless, it's a lot more flexible than rasterized images.

> Could I use the image you just made to render all the animation sequences? TY! :)
