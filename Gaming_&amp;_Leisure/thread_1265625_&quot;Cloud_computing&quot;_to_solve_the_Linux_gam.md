[http://kotaku.com/5181300/onlive-makes-pc-upgrades-extinct-lets-you-play-crysis-on-your-tv]("http://kotaku.com/5181300/onlive-makes-pc-upgrades-extinct-lets-you-play-crysis-on-your-tv")

In case you haven't heard, there is at least one company out there with what seems to be a very interesting idea: using giant server farms to do game processing for you and forwarding you the image to your screen.

So, if you are using a netbook you pull up the OnLive browser and voila! you can now play Crysis on cheap hardware fully maxed! Of course this depends on your internet connection but it is said at the moment that they have a way to make it very fast. Kotaku tried it with Crysis Wars and said they noticed no hitches. Wow.

Now, it seems like they are only working on clients for Windows and OS X but I really hope they make a client for linux. I think this could be a big step forward for PC gaming in general (the ability to rent PC games for one!) and if it is successful this could be a huge step forward in gaming on Linux, granted we push for the Linux client to be made.

Imagine being able to play the latest PC game at max settings with only your netbook, an input device and a fast internet connection (connections are only going to get faster...). This sounds like it will be amazing if it works and if it does it might just threaten consoles like the article says.

### Post by donkyhotay on 2009-09-14
Cloud computing is the future, just look at what google is planning with their chromeOS. Although some very serious and alarming issues may arise from it, cloud computing is pretty similar to how computers are used now. Except for FOSS, almost all software you 'buy' these days are technically a service anyways, just look at steam for instance. By moving the processing of the game into cloud computing it makes games less OS dependent. Which means if software companies *plan* for their game to run that way it will no longer matter what OS the software runs on, and will actually be more likely to run on something besides windows, because the only way to play is by going online to the game servers. The downside of course is, once a game stops being profitable the servers go down and your game is gone, but thats more honest then now where 'upgrades' prevent users from using 5-10 year old software correctly.

This looks very good for certain part of gamers, a big part of them.

Positive :
[LIST=1]
[*]OS dependent
[*]Lower cost on buying games
[*]Don't have to go to the store to pick up a game (and go back with a long face if it had sold out).
[/LIST]

Negative :
[LIST=1]
[*]Lag on controls
[*]Lag on video
[*]Having to own a credit card or something similar to buy online (security issue?)
[*]Internet reliability (local constructions might interrupt your game for... even few days)
[/LIST]

Lag is not important for some gamers. Role playing gamers will most certainly feel less impact than first person shooter gamers, where even a slight lag in mouse movements means going from a high accuracy to extremely low. Also there will be a lag on the video which still is less important than the lag on the controls.

For RTS this will not be too big of a problem unless if you play starcraft2 or some other where micro management is the key.

Driving games will also suffer from this lag but it might not be too bad if the game server is in your vicinity. For pro gamers this will be unacceptable but compared to FPS and RTS it will not be that bad.

Avid gamers that have a higher understanding of the game they play than others will suffer the most, as the hand-on skills that they have gained will be revoked (all sort of types of aiming, trickjumping and mirco).

Thankfully, most gamers dont know the game mechanics or even would realize if there would be this kind of lag. Controls would feel very funny, but only for those that know _exactly_ how the game should be like.


So if you are into competitive gaming, then this is an invitation to your funeral.
If you are a casual gamer that likes shiny stuff and just want things to be easy to understand and to win missions, then this is Valhalla.

### Post by handy on 2010-02-09
With Cloud computing, you have paid a corporation to provide you with a service, this service, I think, has a hidden cost. In that it has essentially taken away some of your personal freedom, & weakens individual freedom on the whole.

How come?

The corporation gives you the ability to do something on your computer that you otherwise would have to do differently, or not at all.

So what's the cost?

The cost, *potentially*, is the continual erosion of our freedom of choice.

If the cloud comes to dominate the software world; which is what the software manufacturers (amongst others) desire, people will become locked into the software that is produced by the Adobe's, MSs, EAs & the like of this world.

Such a domination of the BIG money players would serve them & severely limit their competition.  

The FOSS people would find it very hard to compete on this stage I think. Even though FOSS systems my be what so much of it is running on; this would be unseen & unknown to the average cloud software subscriber/consumer. 

This is a part of the reason why I am against the adoption of the Cloud. 

I think that in the long run, we will have lost a good deal more freedom of choice, than what we gain.  In the long run we may loose many of those that do & would continue to provide us with choices, these choices would definitely diminish, as the those behind the Cloud work to dominate the world of IT.

Once again, I hope I'm wrong.
