By Mike Gunn
We are always a bit dubious about peripherals for consoles. Often they are very gimmicky and do little for your gaming experience. With this in mind, I opened the FragFX Piranha from the team from Splitfish. Other than its go faster red colour, what is immediately evident is that this is a serious piece of kit.

Technical Specifications
The unit is separated into two parts, both of which are connected to the one USB cord. The first part is a hand control unit, kinda like the left-hand side of a normal PlayStation controller, that has the standard left hand thumbstick, the D-Pad, and triggers. It also houses a tiny glowing LED PlayStation button and the select button, both within easy reach of your thumb.
There are also two non-standard buttons; a mouse sensitivity dial and the Frag button, which is set below the normal two trigger buttons.
Split off from the main cord is also a mouse - a rather unusual component of a PlayStation controller. It has the standard left and right mouse buttons, two centre buttons (start button and R3), and - on the left-hand side of the mouse - the standard four face buttons from a regular controller.
The unit is however not just restricted to shooters as the mouse can also be set up for use with the Playstation 3 browser menu's such as the store and the internet. The unit is designed to be firmware upgradeable, and you can also download software to reassign the buttons if you are not happy with the stock layout.

Comfort
Both units are comfortable to hold, although the mouse is of a simple shape with no ergonomic curves. To a man, we all commented on the position of the PlayStation buttons (X, Square, Circle, and Triangle) on the left-hand side of the mouse. They feel about 1cm too far back. Rather than a simple thumb push, you are forced to bend your thumb to reach the button you want. Its a small niggle but during extended play your thumb can feel cramped.
Robustness
The unit itself is robust, and has a generous three metres of cord between your console and the controller, with a further one metre to the mouse. Both are of the rigid, plastic-coated variety and survived our stretch test (a sharp and firm tug to determine if there is any stretch and a source of weakness). A word of caution, though; the cable as it enters the mouse is not protected by any sort of grommet and we fear, over time, that this will be a source of failure through use.

Operation
The sensitivity dial works like a charm, and allows you to adjust your mouse through quite a large scale of speed. It was interesting that the dial on the controller adjusted the mouse. [PS3 Controller Charger](http://www.egamebiz.com/ps3-controller-charge-station.html)The Frag button on the controller is used when you want to move from fast movement to a more precise slow mouse speed to get an aimed shot in.

Overall
So that&#8217;s the basics, but how does it play? Despite the niggles it was awesome to use. Us dyed-in-the-wool PC gamers found it a lot better to use than the standard PlayStation controller, while the regular console players they were impressed by how quickly you could move your view and sight your weapon. It's best described an amalgam of whats good about console and PC first person shooter controls.
Unlike some other peripherals, this was very easy to get the hang of and very natural to use. The combination of the console speed and PC finesse more importantly gives you an edge when it comes to online play. "A snipers best friend", quipped one of our testers. Movement was very smooth and precise, and the overall layout of the buttons felt very natural.
This is a great unit. If you want to take control like never before, or if you are in transition from PC to console and struggle to point your weapon, then this is for you.
