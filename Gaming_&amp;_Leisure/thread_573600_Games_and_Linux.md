That's a moot point.  Engines like OGRE3D, APIs like OpenGL and 3D modelling/animation packages like Belnder3D are all free/open and cross platform.  Developing a game with all of these ensures very simplistic porting of your code between Windows, MacOSX and Linux.

### Post by elvis on 2007-10-11
> **Lord Illidan said:**
> Perhaps because the developers only know Direct X and the like? Certainly, if DirectX has better documentation, they would program in that.

I can tell you now that game developers certainly do not ONLY know DirectX.

If that was the case, there would be no games for Nintendo or Sony consoles.  Likewise, there are a large volume of games that are built on OpenGL already, but use proprietary engines.

More importantly, human beings have that marvelous capacity to learn abstract ideas.  That's what makes us so adaptable.  I'm quite confident anyone who has the smarts to learn DirectX/Direct3D can also learn a wide variety of other APIs and game programming systems.

The reason is ignorance, pure and simple.  There is still a stigma amongst business owners that using free/open-source software means you have to give away all of your IP.  People don't realize that the game engine and the game content are two very different things, and that making a game with a free engine does not mean you have to give your game away for free.

### Post by mivo on 2007-11-06
> **madsmeg said:**
> (i personally think Linux does have a decent market share... but thats besides the point)

It is currently difficult for a studio to even find publishers for Windows games that are not going to be a sure mega success. Take Iron Lore for example. Good people, plenty of experience. They made Titan Quest last year and it sold fairly well. Early this year they released an expansion, which sold decently, but not as well as the publisher hoped. Result: THQ, the publisher, refused to provide the money for the development of a patch even though the game was in need of one.

The PC gaming market has been shrinking in the past few years while the console market has been growing. More and more studios develop for consoles now because that is where the publishers see the future (not unselfishly, piracy is less of a problem on consoles, and it is easier to make console games because the hardware is standardized), and it is the publishers that fund the development. Even Bioware released games like Jade Empire and Mass Effect for consoles first, and only much later for the PC. I think we will see Linux versions of games only if a studio has some Linux fans who put in extra work, if the game design allows for it. We get a Linux version of Unreal Tournament 2008, but it is not even included in the box. We have to buy the Windows version for the data disks, and then download the Linux client. So, really, they are just throwing us a bone, but that's it.

We basically face two problems: The Linux market share being fairly small (smaller than Mac, if you believe the statistics, though I see no way how to reliably find out the number of Linux installations on desktops), and the PC gaming market not being in a really healthy shape. 

I'm not so unhappy with Linux gaming, though. I get to play the free Nexuiz, Neverwinter Nights (the Infinite Dungeons premium module), Dominions 3, and heck, if I wanted to I could even play WoW in Wine without real issues. There is also X3 coming out for Linux this month. I feel my gaming experience has improved because there are fewer choices, which causes me to stay longer with the games that I do have. Back when I had Windows, I'd buy 3-4 games a month, and you can guess how long I played most of them. I felt jaded.
