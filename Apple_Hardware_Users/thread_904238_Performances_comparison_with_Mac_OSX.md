### Post by MikeTheC on 2008-08-29
My own experiences with Linux on Apple hardware have thus far been restricted to PPC equipment, which means that, among other things, the degree to which there is any optimization at all is naturally less than what would likely be the case on an x86-based Mac, let alone an optimal x86 DIY box. A lot of that has to do with the fact that much of the internal hardware are things for which Apple has not published much, if anything, in the way of APIs. The fact that Linux runs as well as it does on PPC equipment is, in my opinion, a credit to the skill and determination of all those many software developers in the Linux community.

Now, that being said... I don't think I've really seen any measurable difference in performance between either OS platform on the PPC Macs I've used them on. Moreover, support for system sleep, display sleep, and 3D support have, again, been compromised to one degree or another by Apple and ATI's collective unwillingness or reluctance to share all the specs and APIs required. Naturally, support for sleep and power modes has improved considerably, but none of the PPC Macs I've owned has *ever* had any 3D support. And what started off as a relatively trivial inconvenience in not being able to play a few 3D-enhanced games, or utilize 3D-enhanced screen savers has now become a far greater inconvenience because of the increase in dependence on 3D rendering and off-loading of such tasks.

Clearly, x86-based Macs will have less trouble in this regard because the hardware is known and enjoys a much broader, thanks to being in use far beyond Apple, scope of support. In many ways, current-generation Apple equipment is Macintosh-In-Name-Only because of this fact. I don't mean that as in insult in any way, but my point being this is not equipment that Apple has somehow invented, but rather standard hardware platform technology which Apple has wisely adopted.

This is actually a boon when running either Mac OS X or Linux, because it's so much more able to be supported, and has been in front of many, many more developers than the PPC platform. Things such as GCC and many of the other development tools out there, to say nothing of the brains of the coders themselves, know x86 hardware better, and there's far many more of them. Why do you think x86 became the defacto Linux standard platform and has stayed there lo these many years?

Anyhow, my next computer will be a Mac (whether a desktop or a laptop is still an open question for me) for the benefit of running Mac OS X, but absolutely I will put Linux on it and see what it's like there. My expectation is that it will probably run better than Mac OS X, but that is yet to come for me. We'll see... we'll see...



Mike

### Post by hanzomon4 on 2008-08-29
Well it's a mixed bag...


**The Good**
Ubuntu on my 3rd gen Macbook Pro is amazing. Ubuntu looks amazing on the 1440x900 res screen; add a great theme, compiz, and the animated dawn of ubuntu wallpaper and it looks almost as great as OSX. Sound sounds good, input and output. Touch Pad works just like it does in osx, including twofinger and click right click. The webcam looks about the same in skype as it does in osx. 

**The not so good**
Wireless works but is noticeably weaker in range in Ubuntu, and this is with the new FOSS driver from athros. Battery life is horrible, 2.5hours is not enough.

**The down right dirty dog wrong**
It freezes many times a day forcing a cold boot. Usually after closing the lid. The touch pad stops working randomly, again, forcing you to reboot.

I left out the fact that getting everything to work takes a good chunk of leg work, I understand why that is and it ain't that bad. If suspend/hibernate would work as reliably as it does in OSX and my mouse stopped dying OSX would become a rare presence on this machine.
