### Post by imattux on 2015-09-30
I'm not sure what you mean by "flawless" or what you mean by "I definitely want to keep running Linux" because, well, I'm yet to meet anyone other than experienced network admins running linux servers who would describe their Linux experience as "flawless". Anyone care to chime in and say I'm wrong?

Running Linux to do just about anything usually involves tweaking, testing, lots of reading, etc, and, in my case anyway, trying different things, breaking things, applying what I've learned and trying again. Sometimes the "solution" involves a kludgy workaround, sometimes it involves using either old or bleeding-edge new, experimental software or drivers. Running OS X is a lot closer to "flawless" but I still push the limits there and occasionally break stuff. So the real question is: Why do you want to "keep running Linux?" and "what are you going to use it for? Do you want it to be your primary OS?"

Suspend/Sleep/Hibernate are all actually different and are, from what I understand, very hardware specific. My hardware will hibernate but not suspend. Macs sleep well because all the software from the OS to individual applications are written for and tested on a very limited selection of hardware. Windows boxes sleep not as, but still fairly well because it's the 800 pound gorilla. Almost everyone writes and tests almost everything to run on Windows and again, Windows has a smaller choice of supported hardware.

Linux is built to run on nearly any hardware out there, and intended to be customized as needed by the end-users or whatever third party the end users choose to pay to support them. 

I'll share my car analogy opening myself up to flaming, no doubt: 

Macs are like BMW's, Mercedes, Lexus or your favorite luxury brand. Expensive, but for those who can afford it they're worth it in that they will be very reliable for a very long time and everything "just works" well. Not many luxury car owners change their own oil or even care to know what is going on under the hood (not that some don't, but I'm generalizing). If and when they break, you need to go a specialized mechanic and parts will be expensive.

Windows boxes are more like Chevy, Ford, Honda or Toyota, the non-luxury brands. Not that there aren't many who don't tune and hot-rod their windows boxes to run faster or do specific things - gaming notably - far better than Macs and possibly for less money, but in general, windows boxes cost less, mechanics are easy to find and parts are cheap unless/until you want to start doing something very specific.

Running Linux is like driving a custom car that looks exactly like any of the other cars from the outside. It might impress some people who bother to look under the hood, other people won't understand why you didn't buy a mainstream car, but most will not notice any difference at all. You pay nothing for the OS, which makes it very attractive to those with limited budgets and/or operating on a large scale, but it takes some knowledge and work to get it running and keep it running. The reward is that sooner or later, you're going to know a lot more about what's going on beneath the hood and you'll be a lot more capable of fixing it than those who don't know anything.
