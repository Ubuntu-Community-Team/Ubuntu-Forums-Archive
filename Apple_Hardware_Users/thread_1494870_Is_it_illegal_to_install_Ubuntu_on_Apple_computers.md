### Post by srs5694 on 2010-05-27
Also, please be more accurate with terminology. An action that voids a warranty is not necessarily illegal. I can drive my car at 60 miles per hour in first gear. That will probably void the warranty, but it's not, AFAIK, illegal. I can toss garbage out of my car while driving it at 60 mph in fifth gear. That action won't void the warranty, but it is illegal.

If running Linux on Apple hardware did void the warranty, then you'd be risking free tech support and repairs within the warranty period by doing it, and you'd be risking nothing once the warranty had expired. If running Linux on Apple hardware were illegal, you'd be risking arrest and some sort of punishment (fines, imprisonment, community service, etc.) by doing it, and unless the law had some sort of time limitations, the risk would persist forever.

It's important to be clear about these two things, since they're very different.

Sorry, this topic is so incredibly odd that I thought I should post.

If Apple were to say that installation of alternate OS's was illegal, you couldn't use bootcamp.

If Apple were to say that you could ONLY install alternate OS's with bootcamp, they would run into another problem. Antitrust. They would have a very hard time telling people that they must run their software on their hardware, especially outside the US.

Also, I agree with all of the statements that voiding a warranty is different from Illegality. Apple can put whatever weird constraints they want in their warranty, as long as they are up front about them. They could make it necessary for you to hop up and down on one foot while explaining your problem to an applecare representative, if they wanted.

The forth issue, the OSX EULA, is a more touchy one. It's possible that apple could say that any OS's installed in parallel with OSX must be installed via bootcamp, or you would violate the license. That might be possible for them to do in the future, but I don't think it's the case at the moment. You can say whatever you want in a EULA, but they haven't been extensively court-tested yet, so there is no really good proof that such a clause would be legally binding. But it might be a possible lockout option for them in the future.

That being said, it would be damn silly. Nobody makes more money by restricting the capabilities of their product. Maybe by forcing purchase of another product, but not with mere restriction.
