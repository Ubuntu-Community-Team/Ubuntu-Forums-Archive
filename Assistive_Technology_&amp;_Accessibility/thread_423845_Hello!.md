### Post by Davidknight24 on 2007-04-26
Hi, I'm an optometrist in the UK with an interest in Low vision. I'm also a Linux user (Ubuntu amongst others). Many of my low vision patients have either given up using computers all together or have opted for expensive software which allows them limited functionality (text reader, Word processing, E-mail, Internet). The frustrating thing for me is that with the right software people can greatly improve their quality of life and I don't believe that money should be the barrier. Loss of vision and low income are frequent bedfellows. Essentially in an ideal world I would like to have a CD I can give them that they can put on their computer which will give them instant access to a range of accessability functions with little or no technical knowledge required. Unfortunately my programming skills are basic but I can't imagine it would be that difficult. 

I have done some web-based research and have come across a couple of projects. BLinux [http://leb.net/blinux/](http://leb.net/blinux/) and  Oralinux [http://oralux.org/](http://oralux.org/) both of which appear to be defunct (correct me if I'm wrong). The latter directs me to Ubuntu.

My primary concern is that presently the installation and setup of Ubuntu is very challenging for a person with low vision. I have not played around with Ubuntu a great deal yet but it strikes me that a simple auto-setup proceedure is lacking. A simple list of yes/no type questions could differentiate the degree of visual impairment and reconfigure the desktop appropriately. I can't see this is a big hurdle to overcome, but then I'm not a programmer! Any thoughts?
