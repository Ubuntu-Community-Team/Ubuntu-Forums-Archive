### Post by mpvano on 2006-07-10
Your card probably doesn't "talk" to your monitor to determine its capabilities. The symptom you describe is caused by the sync range being out of range of the montior's capabilities.

Don't let it run that way for long, many monitors overheat and can fail under those circumstances....

You should be able to fix the problem by reconfiguring your xserver and manually providing full details about your monitors sync rates. For older monitors it can be hard to find the details, but you can try typing it's name into google and seeing if you can find an old spec sheet (if you've lost the manual).

Once you have the horizontal and vertical sync ranges in front of you, do the following:

open a terminal window and type 

sudo dpkg-reconfigure xserver-xorg

then follow the prompts to answer the questions about your system. When you come to the description of the montor, you can enter the sync values you've obtained by telling it you want to use the "advanced" or "expert" way to set the montior type.

I also suggest picking 16 bits/pixel instead of the default 24 bits/pixel. This will help a lot on a video card that old, and especially if the machine is slow.

The next time you start up (or when you restart the X server if you know how to do that) your new settings should take effect.

If you screw up things and the xserver won't start because you typed in wacky values, then you can restart using one of the "recovery mode" choices in grub to give you a command line shell and a root terminal. If you look in /etc/X11, you will find your new (bad) xorg.conf file, and also a file called xorg.conf.200607091923 (or whatever date and time it was when you reconfigured the server). This is the old configuration. copy it over xorg.conf with the commands:

cd /etc/X11
cp xorg.conf.whateveritwascalled xorg.conf


then shutdown by typing

shutdown -h now

When you restart you should be back to your old configuration and you are ready to try again, answering the questions in the configurator program more carefully!

Don't try ithis if you don't understand these instructions - find a local nerd to help!

good luck,

Mario (a nerd)

### Post by mpvano on 2006-07-10
I don't think your problem is your monitor - You said it was a VGA!  Your monitor is more like a "super XGA" in terms of what it supports. A VGA defines a very specific (and very old and limited) monitor specification.

Your modern LCD monitor should handle almost anything thrown at it.

It sounds more like your card doesn't support READING the monitor specs dynamically, and so it can't decide what to do.

This situation also occurs sometimes when using certain cheap KVM switches because the DDC signals don't go through the switch,  and there's a long thread somewhere around here about dealing with that problem.

If you read your most recent /var/log/Xorg.0.log file you can follow the process the X server goes through in setting the resolution and see where it went wrong.

Unfortunately, I'm not an xorg expert, but I believe what you'll need to do is specify that the server IGNORE any attempt to use dynamic configuration and just use the sync values you've given it. 

Unfortunately the xorg.conf man page does NOT list all the options you sometimes need, but I'm sure there is one that tells it to ignore the DDC probing, which is the configuration method that your card probably doesn't support. It will then just shut up and generate the rates you told it in detail when you reconfigured it.

Check the logflie first to confirm just what your problem is, and if it's the absence of DDC, then  I think your best bet is to see if you can find that thread or research the term DDC online.

It might be easier just to get another card?

It also occurs to me that my understanding of your symptoms may have been wrong. A VGA (what you said you had) behaves completely differently than the analog inputs of an LCD - Don't forget the LCD can't really work at arbitrary values of resolution because it has fixed pixels.

Sorry I can't be more help than that...
