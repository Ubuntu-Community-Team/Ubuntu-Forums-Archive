### Post by philc on 2008-01-02
> **Trapper said:**
> 

More importantly though, this is just the scratch on the surface. Internet multi-media is just beginning to make major changes. There are huge ones coming soon and Nix will be on the outside looking in. What's going to be different is that Nix isn't going to be able to do hacks to utilize those multi-media services without getting in lots of legal trouble. The object here is to keep linux from being a viable internet enhanced desktop alternative and you-know-who is behind all that because they have the most to lose if Linux continues on it's current path.

I'd be interested to know about these major changes of which you speak.

Conversely, I think Linux is well placed as an Internet enhanced desktop alternative. There's lots of interest in multimedia projects, many of which are developing free open source alternatives to commercial closed source software.

In my opinion, THE most important online video plugin, Adobe's Flash, is available for the Linux platform. That's great news. Flash now supports H.264 MPEG4 content - which can be created with free video libraries such as x264. Such content can be embedded via a web page using the open source [Flow Player]("http://flowplayer.org/"). 

The BBC sees the light and reworks iPlayer to be a Flash based application, thus allowing Linux users (amongst others) to benefit from the service.

I'm not saying Flash is the best online video  solution, just the most ubiquitous, and there is Linux support for it.

If you'd prefer your media in Ogg Theora format, then use Fluendo's Cortado Java applet for playback. Although, it'd be great to see the Theora codec move forwards in terms of quality and compression to match H.264.

Then we could talk about GStreamer, the MLT Framework, FFMPEG..... the list goes on.

Anyway, it's not all doom and gloom. There's some strong multimedia Linux based projects and viewing alternatives, of which I've only just scratched the surface with this post.
