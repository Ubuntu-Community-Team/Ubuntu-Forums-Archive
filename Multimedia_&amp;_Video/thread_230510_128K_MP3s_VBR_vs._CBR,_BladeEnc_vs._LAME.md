### Post by Kensey on 2006-08-06
At the risk of starting a flamewar, I've been pondering the merits of re-ripping my CDs at 128K for car stereo use.  Currently they are ripped at 160K CBR using RipperX as a frontend to BladeEnc.

First there's the matter of bitrate.  It was suggested to me by several people that I might save a significant amount of space by encoding as VBR.  However, BladeEnc doesn't support VBR, or at least the options don't mention anything in that line.  So I tried using LAME with a maximum of 128K, minimum of 64K, quality 4.  Saved basically no space.  Tried again with quality 5.  Saved a tiny bit of space (something like 30K in a several-meg file).  Is there basically no point to using VBR when encoding at 128K?  Should I explore the depths of quality 6 or 7?  Use a lower minimum bitrate?

(This may be a pointless debate in one respect, since I don't know if my car stereo supports VBR, but my Palm's copy of RealPlayer does and I use that for music a fair bit at work and in the car as well, so it still could come into play there.)

Second: assuming it turns out that VBR saves no significant space in any realistic case at 128K, will LAME vs. BladeEnc make much difference at 128K CBR?  I see the author of Blade says *not* to use it at bitrates *less* than 128K, because it's optimized for bitrates over that threshold and will do freakish things below it.  I know people have started extended flamewars by asking questions like this, but: what has been the real-world experience of people with LAME vs. BladeEnc at 128K?  My collection is mostly alternative/rock -- think Pearl Jam, Nine Inch Nails and Dire Straits with a heavy proportion of R.E.M. and smatterings of lighter fare like Sheryl Crow, Veruca Salt and a CD of modern bagpipe music.  What sorts of noticeable artifacts have you run across in either case?
