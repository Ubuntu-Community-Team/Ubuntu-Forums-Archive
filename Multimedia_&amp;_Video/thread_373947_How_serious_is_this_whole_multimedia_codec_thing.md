### Post by Danyl on 2007-03-01
The title of this thread says it all.

I've just been wondering. Ubuntu and other companies/entities/organisations etc. behind a Linux distribution don't include support for patented multimedia codecs (such as MP3, DVD playback etc) due to legal reasons. This I completely understand.  Firstly, because as a company, you want to do things 'by the book'.  Secondly, the whole philosophy of Ubuntu Linux is to only use open-source software and hence paying a fee to such patent holders would undermine this philosophy.

For the end user though, who wants these multimedia codecs, there is the option to install the codecs using either Automatix or Easyubuntu (or manually) but how serious a legal issue is this for end users like us? I mean we're talking average people/users and judging by the popularity of programs like Automatix etc. the warning/disclaimer "installing these codecs may constitute a crime in your country" appears (at least to me) to not be too much of a worry for most Ubuntu users.

So my I'm curious:

(1) how big a legal issue is this whole installing multimedia codecs (free of charge) for you personally? Have you gone ahead and installed such codecs regardless of these legal issues? Does it worry/not worry you? 

(2) are the patent holders aware of this? (I'd like to think they are) and why don't they pursue the people behind products such as Automatix and Easyubuntu? Afterall these programs are supplying the tools for people to use their codecs without having to pay a fee. Surely they're aware of the growing popularity of Linux and the fees their missing out on?  

Just been thinking about this issue and I'm really curious as to all your opinions.

### Post by booe on 2007-03-02
> **Danyl said:**
> (1) how big a legal issue is this whole installing multimedia codecs (free of charge) for you personally? Have you gone ahead and installed such codecs regardless of these legal issues? Does it worry/not worry you?

A lot of people (most people?) would ignore some, if not most, patent laws when it comes to software. It is a very controversial issue. Most people (except IP lawyers and patent-hoarding companies trying to sue others to make money) despise software patents. You've probably heard of some of the classic cases such as a patent on "one click shopping" or something else totally obvious.

Additionally, most people wouldn't give a stuff about whether it is legal or not. The important question is: would you get caught? The answer to this (for end users) is almost always a complete "no". You would have to be sued by the patent owner, and even then, it isn't really worth their time and effort. These patent owners would rather hunt down Microsoft in the hopes of making millions... or billions... not a few thousand in loose change from a grandma Ubuntu user.

However you should definitely try and avoid breaking patents as much as possible (although in the world of ridiculous software patents, this is not possible!). The reason being one of principle. You should try and support free/open source software as much as possible. "Free" means unencumbered by patents and other stuff that no one likes, and which restricts what you can do with the software. As an example: use OGG Vorbis or FLAC for audio. Don't support companies that don't support you! Every time you use a patented piece of media (mp3, mpeg4, etc) - you are helping spread these formats! This is not the direction the open source/free scene should be headed.


> (2) are the patent holders aware of this? (I'd like to think they are) and why don't they pursue the people behind products such as Automatix and Easyubuntu? Afterall these programs are supplying the tools for people to use their codecs without having to pay a fee. Surely they're aware of the growing popularity of Linux and the fees their missing out on?

They're probably not looking for massive backlash/bad press they'd receive from hunting down some volunteer Linux programmers. If you're SCO, then you'd be an exception - and you would soak up as much bad press as possible - but this is of course a rare case!

Patent holders also would only bother with large targets with lots of cash - that are easy to sue. A project like Automatix is made by a number of people all around the world, in some countries where software patents are totally ignored (and anyone can do whatever they like without fear of being sued).

Lastly, Automatix and Easyubuntu are not directly breaking any patents. They're assisting with people who **might** use a patent without permission... but again, who said these projects adhere to US law? Automatix developers might all be from countries which don't recognize software patents - therefore, they're untouchable.
