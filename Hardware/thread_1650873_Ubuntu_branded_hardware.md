### Post by torpedolos on 2010-12-22
**What would you think of ubuntu selling a line of selected, approved and tested hardware with it's brand on it ?**

**The advantages for the users:**


a)  They would be sure, before buying this computer, that the hardware  and  the software fit perfectly together (no questions about the  graphical  card, the wifi card, the web cam, etc)

b)   They would get really  better prices than the existing offers, for  example with Dell, that  sells it's "ubuntu" computer at the same price  as the "microsoft" ones.

c)   They wouldn't be afraid of installing the system themselves.

d) To avoid giving money to Microsoft for a license that they are not going to use.

e)    To have only one contact both for hardware and software in case of   problems (a big advantage that  only  apple have at the moment).

This is what we can call a "soft migration".



**The advantages for Canonical  :**

a)  Canonical would control  the prices of the hardware sold with it's operating system, which is not the case now 

b)   Canonical would make the profit with both the hardware and the hot-line.

c)   Canonical could develop a "ubuntu community spirit", not only with the software, but with an "all-in-one" solution.

d)   Canonical could focus it's effort on the approved, selected hardware that is sold by the company itself.

### Post by ianmillington on 2010-12-22
It's a nice thought but I suspect the support angle for the hardware would be a nightmare for Canonical. It's worth bearing in mind that there are a number of suppliers who sell computers pre-loaded with ubuntu and other distros. However because of the numbers involved they tend to be smaller companies who don't get the same bulk discounts from hardware suppliers and so the machines are more expensive from the start. 

Also, I believe the mainstream builders of Windows computers like Dell receive subsidies from other software suppliers in return for putting trial versions of their software on the machines they sell (aka "crapware"). This offsets the "Microsoft Tax" to a significant degree and I wouldn't be surprised if the amount of them exceeds the price the builder has paid for Windows. As a result it may well genuinely cost more for any builder to make a ubuntu machine
