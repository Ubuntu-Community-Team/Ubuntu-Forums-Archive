### Post by Kirk Schnable on 2012-09-18
*sigh* there are so many battery myths on the Internet.

> **rullee said:**
>  - One shall only remove the battery from the computer if it is to be stored during a long period of time, or if the computer is to be docked during a long period of time.
Meh.  I guess I agree.  Lithium batteries have a certain rate of self-discharge (where they lose effective charge without being used).  I never take my battery out of my laptop, but then again, I use it very frequently.

> **rullee said:**
>  - The battery is to be completely emptied and fully recharged once every month.

The second point does not agree with the wiki article ([http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium-io ... guidelines]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery#Usage_guidelines")) that states that one should avoid draining the battery. I suppose that the manual is right and wiki is wrong, what do you think?
If a lithium battery is drained to 0%, it may never charge again.

However, that being said, 0% on your computer is not really 0%.  There will still be some charge in the cells.  If you drain it to 0%, then leave it sit on a shelf for a month, you'll kill it forever.  But if you drain it to 5% a few times a month, it's no big deal.

The reason the manufacturer wants you to drain the battery monthly is to keep the battery meter accurate.  It is supposed to prevent your computer's "battery level estimate" from being too far from the truth.


> **rullee said:**
> Anyway, when I have the possibility to charge the computer with the cable (that is most often), should I work with the cable and the battery in the computer even though it is fully charged, or should I work with the cable and remove the battery, or lastly, should I work without the cable until the battery is almost emptied, then recharge it while working and so on?
Any decent charging circuitry will stop charging your battery when it's full.   I wouldn't lose any sleep over it either way.



All of this being said, be prepared to replace your battery in 2 years.  Lithium ion batteries have an approximate half-life of 2 years, no matter how well you take care of them.  This means that in about 24 months, "100% charge" will be what "50% charge" is today.  In 4 years, "100% charge" will be like what "25% charge" is today.  

There is no avoiding the half-life of the battery, it doesn't matter how nice you are to it.

The other half-life of lithium batteries has to do with charge cycles.  This varies by manufacturer, but I usually use 1,000 charges as a half-life rule of thumb.  

So in the 2 years, if you charged it from 50% to 100% every day, that would be 365 charge cycles out of the 1,000 you can expect to get out of your battery before it is 50% deteriorated. 

tl;dr:  Your battery will be useless in 3-4 years no matter how you take care of it.

I hope I have given you some useful information.

Kirk
