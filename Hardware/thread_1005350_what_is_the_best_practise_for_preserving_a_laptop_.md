### Post by grazed on 2008-12-08
lithium-ion batteries typically have 800-1000~ charge cycles before they stop taking a charge. 

for instance, when you leave your notebook unplugged, even when it's off, the next time you plug it back in, it charges it just a little bit since it has naturally lost a small bit of power by just keeping the bios clock running. 

unfortunately, that counts as a charge cycle against the battery, and you would be far better off leaving it plugged in as much as you can.

### Post by grazed on 2008-12-08
> **eldragon said:**
> actually, best practice is NOT to drain the battery completely. when not in use for a long period of time, store with a 60~80 percent charge.

unplugging and plugging does NOT count as a charge cycle (like someone here said) otherwise id have drained my battery a long time ago :D

it certainly does.

when your power supply is disconnected, and your battery is charged, the laptop powers the bios clock and memory from it instead of the button cell on your board. also, by nature of rechargeable batteries, they will ever so slowly lose charge while not in use.

so even if both of these factors only drop your battery to 99.8%, it's still counting as a charge cycle. it is true that, that short of a cycle doesn't damage the cell connections within the battery as much as if it was a full discharge, though the degradation is still there.

also, it is -recommended- by just about all manufacturers to fully discharge your battery to 0% every few months to reset the chip within the battery. that chip inside sends out the estimated charge left within it to the computer. by only charging and recharging at random percentages, over time the chip's estimation gets thrown off, thus requiring a full discharge to reset it.

and lastly the percentage of charge to store the battery at is 40% for lithium-ion. no more, no less. that's the optimal rate to preserve cell connections. the amount you stated is common for nickel based rechargeables.
