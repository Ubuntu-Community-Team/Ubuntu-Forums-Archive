### Post by xyphur on 2010-08-26
LGA-775 is the socket type in that machine, and any place that sells computer hardware will stock a variety of heatsink-fans specifically made for that socket.

That being said, it is highly likely that all it needs is a good blast of air to clean out dust... those heatsinks (the 4-blade fan-like fin arrangement) are quite good at collecting dust. I just cleaned an uncle's machine yesterday with that exact same heatsink, and the fan speed dropped drastically from before. If it can't get air thru the fins, it can't cool the processor, and the BIOS simply keeps trying to get the temp down by forcing the fan to high.

I would suggest grabbing a can of compressed air from your local computer or office supply store, and seeing if that helps (make sure to do it outside) - otherwise a complete replacement using a better heatsink and a larger fan may be in order. (larger fans rotate more slowly, making less noise while moving more air)

Small note: If you do go the replacement route, you'll want a small syringe of thermal compound for between the processor and heatsink for the best heat transfer possible. I suggest Arctic Silver 5. You'll be using rubbing alcohol to clean the processor after removing the existing heatsink also. It must be free of all contaminants. The new heatsink may come with it's own thermal pad. They're useless. Remove it and clean the area with alcohol the same as the processor, and you'll be good to go. The thermal paste only requires a very thin layer, spread evenly on the processor. It shouldn't be any more thick than a sheet of paper.

Well, hope that helps. Best of luck. :)

### Post by dabl on 2010-08-26
You're not going to remove the fan, just to clean it (if you did, then YES you would need paste, and a bunch more instructions).  Just take the computer outside (on one of those sunny Seattle days ....), set it on a table or chair or something, take one or both sides off it to expose the innards, and then blast the dust out of it.

One caution -- when the fan gets hit with the blast of air, it will spin wildly, which you don't want.  So you need to use a clean fingertip, or better something like a new pencil where you could use the eraser end to steady the fan, you need to hold the fan blade in position so the dust actually gets knocked off.

Also do be careful to avoid touching the electronics with anything, including the nozzle of the compressed air can.

p.s. I've done the same maneuver with a leaf blower.  You just need something that blows clean air fairly hard.

### Post by xyphur on 2010-08-26
Yes, since the factory paste usually crumbles when you separate the heatsink from the processor, replacement is required.

If you first try removing the heatsink-fan, cleaning it thoroughly, remove and clean the factory thermal paste and replace with Arctic Silver 5, I'm 90% sure the temps will drop and the fan won't need to go high very often unless you're loading the processor considerably.

Also, while you've got the fan off, checking the condition of the bushing/bearing on the fan is a fairly easy and straightforward task, and will ensure smooth operation for much longer. It simply involves peeling back the sticker on the center hub of the fan. If it's a bit dry or gunky, add a drop of light oil and reseal the sticker and the fan will last you even longer.
