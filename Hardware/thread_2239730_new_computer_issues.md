### Post by em3raldxiii on 2014-08-16
If the machine will not POST, check the cables that connect the PSU to the motherboard.  There's a good chance that the machine has an additional plug on the motherboard that was missed (this has happened to me).  It's about the same size as you would plug into a high-end video card.  On many of the machines I have put together, this particular plug is located in the Upper Left corner of the board, not far from the CPU/Cooler.  If you did not miss any plugs, and all of the cables are firmly attached, then it's possible that the PSU is not powerful enough to drive the hardware.  I've seen this happen where someone was trying to power an 8-core processor with a high end vid-card, 16GB of high speed RAM with a low-end 350W PSU.  It just don't have enough power, captain!

### Post by em3raldxiii on 2014-08-17
That one is _way too big_ for almost any rig, and I stopped buying Thermaltake after I had a few of them release their magic purple smoke after only a short while.  Okay, so you have a 6-Core FX chip (is that the 125W version?) and let's say you want to max it out with 2 top-end video cards, and let's say you want to fill up 4 or 5 pcie slots with power-hungry expansions, and let's go hog-wild and say you want to throw in 4 high-speed hard drives, an optical drive, and some USB peripherals that run off the USB sockets.  Here is one that I would put on my "short list":  [http://www.newegg.ca/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16817194098](http://www.newegg.ca/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16817194098) (note that there are better prices for this, like from NCIX for example).

The one you linked *might* be okay ... it might be perfectly fine.  But for the number of watts that it's apparently providing, I would expect a "high quality" power supply to cost a bit more.  Enermax has been making high quality power supplies longer than just about any other brand that I know of, I sorta think of them as the "BMW" of the Power Supply world.  You pay more for the same amount of horsepower, but it's gonna last longer and be significantly more reliable.  You'll also note that the one I linked is a "Platinum certified."  This means a couple of things.  First, in terms of efficiency, it draws less current than bronze/silver/gold, so over time it costs a little less to run.  Compared to the Gold version, it would take several years of heavy use for it to pay for itself, so that's not really a big deal.  More importantly, though, less of the power that it's drawing from the wall socket is wasted as heat!  This makes a very significant difference with the longevity of the PSU itself, plus it can take more abuse.  The power rails are going to stay ROCK SOLID, and this is SUPER important for gaming, and graphics-intensive applications.  The capacitors are of higher build quality, the sockets and cables inside are more secure, the fan doesn't have to work as hard, and it will be a little more forgiving if it gets full of dust.  And an Enermax 850 can put out all 850 Watts for a long time, but I would not expect a Thermaltake 1050 to be able to operate at 850W for any sustained period of time.

I hope that makes sense.  So when I buy PSUs, I always go for Enermax, Antec, and _sometimes_ the top-end Corsair models (gold or platinum only).  Read the reviews on the Newegg site.  You will be more excited about this PSU than you are about any other component!  LOL
