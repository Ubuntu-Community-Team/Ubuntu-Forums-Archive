As I am to understand it:

A few years back an employee of a component company was offered a bribe to come work for another. He took the recipe for caps to the other company but the recipe he had access to was missing one ingredient as a security measure. Well, What happened is the market was flooded with bad capacitors. These caps found their way into alot of motherboards. I actually had a gigabyte board where all the caps around the processor area (all same kind) went bad. The look exactly like yours. 

Caps fail in rather 2 ways. They dry out from age or they swell and burst. A good cap should retain its flat head. 

You can always replace the cap with an equal value cap but you should have experience in board level repair before attempting this.

### Post by xyphur on 2010-10-16
I've replaced bad caps on more than one piece of equipment, both mine and others' - the most recent being an iMac G5 with 23 bad caps of 3 different values... it was a lengthy repair that began with a machine that simply wouldn't boot, or would hang trying. Now it's working 100% like new.

Seconding CharlesA, I'm 100% certain that the caps shown in your posted photo are dead.

If you are comfortable doing board-level repairs, I recommend sourcing low-ESR (Equivalent Series Resistance) caps with the same µF and voltage specs, with at least the same temperature rating as the originals.

It's not as hard as some may make it sound, but you do need to have a solid understanding of soldering, perhaps also lead-free soldering if your board claims RoHS compliance (lead-free solder requires more heat be used, so you'll need a good quality soldering iron also).

Best of luck. Keep that board from becoming landfill! :)
