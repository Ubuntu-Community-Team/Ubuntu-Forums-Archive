### Post by TBABill on 2011-03-28
You can have office and Windows on a netbook. You will have all integrated video and sound and I think you will have no optical drive either. If you require one you'll need an external. Battery life is far longer with a netbook versus a laptop. But the trade off for that is far less processing power. You'll see it more when you open multiple apps and browser tabs. Online and video performance can suffer a bit if you put a lot of strain on the processor.
 
It really depends on your needs. If a small screen is fine with you and you aren't doing much with large images, spreadsheets and other things, and also not doing memory intensive application work, then it could be a nice benefit for your needs to have a netbook instead. They're very lightweight, don't have fans (and don't run hot) and they're quiet.

### Post by wm_brant on 2011-03-28
I have two netbooks - an early Dell Mini 9 (single-core Atom with  Intel's slow integrated graphics) and a new Thinkpad X120e (dual-core  AMD E-350 with high-performance integrated graphics).

I used the Mini 9 for browsing with Firefox and for running Open Office  under Ubuntu. I never had any complaints about the performance of either  Open Office or Firefox. Both apps seemed as responsive as they are on  my quad-core desktop system. I frequently ran both Open Office Writer  and Firefox together, and had no issues with either.

The limits of the Mini 9 that bothered me were physical limits - the  Mini 9 has a keyboard that is too small to touch type on, and the screen  is both small in size (9") and low-resolution (1000x600). I always used the Mini 9 with an external (i.e., full-sized) keyboard.

My X120e addresses both issues I had with the Mini 9 - it's got a  decent, full-sized keyboard and the display is both larger (11.6"), and  higher resolution (1366x766). The unit runs both Libre Office and  Firefox well. It's currently running (gasp! choke!) 64-bit Win7 Home  Pro, and some OS functions are a little slow, but that's more of a  comment on Win7 than it is of the E-350. The X120e will be upgraded to  Ubuntu 11.04 when it is released.

Both single- and dual-core Atom processors have performance issues  running (large, hi-def) video with Intel's current integrated video. An  Atom paired with an Nvidia ION has no such problem, and neither does the  new AMD chips like the E-350. However, no netbook (and probably no  low-end laptop) is going to run complex modern games at an acceptable  speed.

An new Atom or AMD netbook is going to have less processor power than  even a new low-end laptop. However, think of it this way - a new Atom-  or AMD-based netbook has more processing power than a top-of-the-line  notebook from a few years ago. People got their job done with those, so  you can get your job done with a netbook. However, only you know how you  use a computer, and what your needs will be. 

=-=-=-=-=-

If you're otherwise happy with your Dell, I suspect you just need to buy a new battery for it. Batteries do wear out over time, and a new battery is usually cheaper than a new computer. 

Also keep in mind that netbooks rarely come with a paid for version of Microsoft Office. However Libre Office (formerly known as Open Office) is the open-source equivalent of Office, reads and write Office files, and is free. There are versions of Libre Office for both Ubuntu and Windows. Just make sure your school supports it.

However, Microsoft Office cannot be run on Ubuntu. So, if you go with Ubuntu, you either need to use software equivalent to Office - and Libre Office is excellent - or do without. I rely on Libre Office at home.

=-=-=-=-=-

Check the specs of a unit before you buy. Many of those in the link you provided have only 1GB of ram, which is not really enough. I had 2GB in my Mini 9, and that was adequate for what I did. I have 4GB in my new netbook. A 1GB system is going to run slow, particularly under Windows7, particularly running Office.
