### Post by VeeDubb on 2010-07-02
*"Processor: I hear that ARM based are best?"*

You heard wrong.  It is highly difficult and require a lot of work to even get a full linux desktop to run on ARM processors.

Virtually all netbooks use an ATOM processor, which conforms to the x86 standard, either 32 or 64bit depending on which version.

*"Hard drive: SSD? (more expensive though, but how much more?) what are the speed/power benefits?"*

You've pretty much already got it.  SSD means dramatically faster read and write access unless comparing the absolute cheapest and worst quality SSD to the very highest quality in traditional HDD, and even then, SSD still wins.  Just not by quite as much.

On the other hand, SSD cost WAY more.  You can get a good quality 500GB laptop drive for about the same price as a 32GB SSD.  If you've got money to burn, or don't mind a VERY small drive, SSD is the way to go.  Otherwise, you really have no choice.

*Battery life / Ram: Ill take as much as I can get. *

Why?  You're looking for a netbook, and you've already said you don't plan to use it for any intense processing.  I understand wanting max battery life, which will be in the 8-11 hour range, but RAM shouldn't matter to you.


Now, all that aside, you have hundreds of options, but they fall into 3 categories.

1. 7" and smaller ARM netbooks.  These are toys.  They are too small do do even light work on, and your software options will be extremely limited due to the ARM processor.  Most of the come with WindowsCE, which is the predecessor of what they put on Windows Mobile smartphones and PDAs.

2. "Typical" netbooks.  99% of these are 10" or 10.1".  Atom CPUs are the standard, and they pretty much all have the single core, 32bit Atom CPU.  Battery life varies from model to model, but can range anywhere from 2-11 hours.  Just get one with the batter life you need.  You'll also get the most variation at this level in terms of supported WiFi protocalls (B/G/N), data storage (small SSD or medium sized HDD)  The most common configurations come with 1GB of RAM.  Some have 2GB, or the ability to upgrade to 2GB.  I don't recall seeing any netboosk in this category that supported more than 2GB of RAM.

3. "Hybrid netbooks."  I just made up that term.  There are a small number of netbooks on the market, made by Asus, HP and Lenovo, which have nVidia ION graphics, support for more RAM, and larger screens (11.6-12.1")


I do NOT recommend you get #1 unless you're looking for a toy/project.

#2 is a great way to go if it's really a netbook you want.  They're pretty much all well supported, so it's just a matter of browsing through the hundred and hundreds of models out there to find one with the particular feature set you want, at a price you can afford.

If you choose to go with #3, I recommend the Asus EeePC 1201n.  It's the one I own.  It has the dual core Atom 330 with ION graphics, comes with 2GB of RAM, but supports up to 4GB, and has a 12.1" screen.  It still has a less than full sized keyboard and no optical drive, making it "technically" a netbook, but with the features above, it borders on small laptop.
