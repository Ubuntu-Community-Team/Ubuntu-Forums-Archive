### Post by Rayve on 2009-11-07
Defiant Rat,

I'm going to be doing the same in a few months.  As I understand it, an operating system, like Linux or Windows, isn't dependent upon the make of the motherboard or CPU, so long as the minimum requirements are met.  The motherboard and CPU are dependent upon each other, i.e. you can't mix an AMD motherboard with an Intel CPU, and you'll need to make sure the other components you buy are compatible with your motherboard (type of RAM, etc), but operating systems just want their minimum requirements met, they don't care about who manufactured the board.

Good luck to both of us!  :D

As an aside, you can see (hundreds of) examples of functioning builds here: [http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=361236](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=361236)

### Post by terry@softreq.com on 2009-12-19
I'm no expert on the topic, but I tend to look at the FSB (Front Side Bus) speed that the machine will operate at. 

That's the speed internally that your machine moves information between the processor, memory, graphics board and to some extent hard drive. 

For instance, many machines have processors / motherboards that  perform around 800mhz FSB.   Another machine might be able to achieve FSB speeds of 1066 while another 1333 mhz. 

Go for a class of machine (whether buying or building) that basically reaches the highest FSB you can afford.   

Obviously a machine that runs at 1333 is going to be quite a bit more expensive than a machine running at 800 mhz and quite a bit faster. 

I'm putting a machine together that is well tuned to run UBUNTU 9.10 (64 bit for AMD) running at 1333 FSB. 

Needless to say, I hope it will smoke running Ubuntu!  

My preference for processors is AMD.  Better to get a processor that achieves it's stated processor speed at low voltage and low temperature with some room for overclocking.
