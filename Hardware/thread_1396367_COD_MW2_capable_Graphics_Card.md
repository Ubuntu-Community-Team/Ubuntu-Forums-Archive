### Post by mknarr on 2010-03-20
Personally, when looking at graphic cards for a mix of Windows and Linux(Ubuntu) your best bet would be to go with an Nvidia Card because they are the best supported in Linux, and ATI is slowly starting to creep up with support.

For Cod:MW2

Make sure the video card supports:
-Vector and Pixel shadders 3.0+
-512MB card (Self preference is 1Gb)
-Before your purchase a card make sure that your power supply has the 
 proper amount of power connectors required by the video card 

RAM Wise
-I would go with at least 4GB (1066)

hope that helps

### Post by synicalx on 2010-03-21
Yeah I'd stay away from a 9500gt, and the rest of the 9xxxgt series for that matter. They're old tech nowadays and pretty expensive for what you get.

For Nvidia a GT250 or GTX260 is pretty awesome value, but for the 260 just make sure you've got a decent PSU because it's a little power hungry.

For ATI go with a 5750 or a 5770, neither need a beefy PSU but the drivers for 'nix are a little bit of a fiddle to get working.

Overall the ATI cards are faster, cheaper and more efficient (power wise anyway) and have DirectX11 and Eyefinity support (3 monitors = win) but the drivers for them under Ubuntu take about 20 minutes to install and require 2 restarts. Also if your going to be doing any CAD stuff, go Nvidia, the CUDA architecture used in the 2xx series is supposed to perform better with CAD/Autodesk.

> **Earl-Grey said:**

Persoanlly, I always go for the best motherboard/power supply I can get. The power supply is very important IMO, since it provides all the power for your system...getting a cheap one makes it more liekly that you will have issues. Better to have a good brand power supply with a stable voltage delivery of, say 500-550 watts will do everythign that you want, and its much better than a dodgy cheapo 550 (or even bigger, more watts wont make a cheap power supply any better) 

Once you have built a system, changing the CPU is easy, its normally less than 5 minutes workv. Changing motherboard is a much longer process....and its riskier. As CPUs move onward, what was a very expensive CPU one year is much more affordable the year after that, and dirt-cheap the year aftrer that.

> 
 With the GPU, I might get a cheaper or second hand card for the time being, and if I wanted to up-grade later I would be able to support a faster card, as my motherboard and CPU would be able to support it.

CPUs dont support video cards. That is the chipset. I've run a nVidia gt6600 (a 2005 card) with a pentium 3 CPU (a 1999 CPU!). With the modern PCI-e slots and cards, you can run pretty much any PCI-e card in the system (provided that the power supply an handle it). 

> **Earl-Grey said:**

That is my logic anyway. I am sure that some people would say I need to get the best GPU I can afford.

There are always people who will say that. Changing video cards is easy, probably even easier than the CPU (from the hardware install point of view, software/drivers can be a different issue....you dont have that problem with CPU upgrades) 

I've seen people buy cheap motherboards/power supplies and top of the line cards before. Even if the cheapo power supply and motherboard dont give you issues, you have a system thatsis less upgradable.
