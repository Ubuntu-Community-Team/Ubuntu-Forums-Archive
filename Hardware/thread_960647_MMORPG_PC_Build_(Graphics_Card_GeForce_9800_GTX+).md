### Post by shaggy999 on 2008-10-27
Personally, for me when I build a system I start off with some basic system specs that I want NOW while also planning for future upgrades later. For example, you might want to find a mother board that has dual PCI x16 2.0 slots and can support up to quad core processors but just use your one graphics card for now and just a slower dual core processor.

That's basically what I'm doing right now. I just picked out a mini barebones system that supports up to 8 GB RAM and 3.0+ GHz quad core but I will only be putting a 2.0 GHz dual core and 2 GB RAM for now. When prices on RAM and those CPUs bottom out I'll upgrade. Additionally my system (well, soon-to-be my system) comes w/ onboard GMA 3000 video w/ VGA graphics port (not very interesting at all) but it comes with a PCI x16 1.0 slot so later on when I'm ready I will upgrade my CRT for a DVI LCD and get a much better powered graphics card to plop in there.

Of course, I don't do a huge amount of gaming so my needs are different from others. But I usually do the following when I build a new system and I think this can apply well to budget gamers:

1) Determine Budget
2) Find high quality motherboard that meets current and future needs.
3) Put cheap stuff in there for now and wait for deals to upgrade
4) ???
5) Profit!!!

Most important components:
1) Motherboard
2) Power Supply
3) Case

I find it's easy to build a system once I've figured out what motherboard, power supply, and case I will use.

EDIT: Power supply and case really are important! You will want a GOOD quality power supply from a reputable manufacturer. The worst thing that ever happened to me was building a $2000 rig for which I tossed in some generic worthless power supply. The PSU suffered a brown out and fried my whole system. $2000 in high tech computer equipment GONE. Similiarly, a good case can be a dream to work with if it has useful features such as removable motherboard trays, wire guides, and rounded edges! I've cut myself sooo many times trying to work my hands in cramped cases that had razor edges! Nicer cases usually offer better cooling solutions as well, which is important if you're an overclocker. Seriously, motherboard, case, power supply. Most important, and probably overlooked, components of any computer.

### Post by shaggy999 on 2008-10-27
As someone mentioned before, there's no such thing as a perfect motherboard. There is, however, a perfect motherboard *for you*.

Some questions that spring to mind when I'm considering a motherboard:

1) Backwards compatibility with older hardware. Do I need IDE ports? PCI? How about Mouse/Keyboard ports or will everything be USB? If everything will be USB I recommend a _minimum_ of 6 USB ports as two will already be taken up by Mouse/keyboard.

2) Forwards Compatibility with newer hardware. What's the highest end CPU I will be able to throw into the motherboard? Is the slot/socket architecture still in use today and how long until it's phased out? I'm talking Socket A/Socket 370/Socket 478/ What's the maximum RAM capacity? If I'm using onboard video should I get a board with an expansion slot? Will I ever need Firewire or eSATA connectors? If I have a local ethernet setup at 100 Mbit/s should I get a board that also supports 1000 Mbit/s for future network upgrade? Am I using this computer as a server? If so, should I get a board w/ dual ethernet and independent buses?

3) Compatibility with Software. In my case, will everything be Ubuntu-compatible out of the box or will I need things like ndiswrapper for the onboard ethernet? Should I get an nVidia card which has good closed source drivers or an ATI card which has terrible drivers but AMD is now releasing source code for? The main concern here would have to do with onboard components (video/audio/ethernet/northbridge/southbridge).

4) Overall quality. How much thought was put into the board by the designers? Will I be able to plug in a massive heatsink and fan or will some oddly placed feature on the motherboard keep me from installing a massive cooling block? Do I even care? Are the fan and power connectors in sensible places? What kind of overclocking features does the motherboard offer? Is it energy efficient? Does plugging in a dual-slot video card block me from using other features of the board?

### Post by shaggy999 on 2008-10-27
I'll give you my case as an example:

Needs: Cheap system with decent upgrade paths for CPU/RAM/Video/HD. No need for highend gaming or overclocking. Quality of board and case in this situation take a backseat to price.

This meant I needed a motherboard that could run a wide array of processors from dual-core to quad-core. I know I want an Intel motherboard because right now Intel is kicking AMD's butt and their multi-core architecture I expect to scale better than AMD's offering. In addition, Intel generally beats AMD in the power requirement. Lower power means I don't need a beefy power supply. I know that I want a "basic" dual-core system for now but plan to upgrade to quad core when they are much cheaper. This meant I'm looking for a motherboard that supports both Intel Core 2 Duo and Intel Core 2 Quad. I know I want to start out with 2 gigs of RAM as that's standard fare on most reasonably equipped computers these days, but I want to make sure there's an upgrade path later. So I make sure that the board supports 4 GB or I actually chose 8 GB just to be safe. Onboard video for me was important because Intel has the only graphics chipsets that have full open source Xorg support and gaming is not a huge issue. I mostly play SNES games on ZSNES or old DOS games in DOSBOX. About the only "modern" game I play is HL2 and its sequels, and the Source engine scales incredibly well. That along with Google Earth and Compiz are about my only needs for 3D chipset support. So the GMA-series of onboard graphics chips suit me fine. However, most onboard graphics cards only have VGA out and I may eventually want to upgrade to a DVI LCD. I use a wireless network at home and have a PCI Wireless card, so I know I need one PCI slot for that. I am indifferent on sound. I've never used a surround sound system. So any onboard sound is fine. I do want backwards compatability with some older hardware, though. I don't want to have to buy a new SATA CD/DVD-rom drive so the motherboard needs an IDE port. I also plan to keep my PS/2 Mouse and keyboard for now, although I would eventually like to upgrade. So these are about all the things I'm looking for in a motherboard:

1) Intel Core 2 Duo & Intel Core 2 Quad Support. In particular, support for Intel's low-watt processors (E2180 2.0 Ghz is what I'm getting, basically the "Celeron's" of dual core CPUs.
2) Intel GMA onboard Graphics w/ VGA out
3) PCI Express x16 slot either 1.0 or 2.0 for future video upgrades
4) At least one IDE port
5) PS/2 Keyboard/Mouse ports
6) 6 USB ports
7) Support for up to 8 GB RAM
8) 1 PCI slot for wireless card
9) SATA ports for a modern hard drive
10) Onboard ethernet & audio
11) All onboard components supported out-of-the-box by Ubuntu

Eventually I found a barebones PC from Asus that offered ALL of those features plus an eSATA port, Firewire, 6-in-1 card reader, and a few other things in a minipc form factor for $100 ($170 - $20 sale - $50 rebate). Once I found my system I looked into processors and found the cheapest dual core that would run on it (the E2180) and selected 2 gigs of cheap-o RAM. I already have the CD-ROM drive and I just need to get a cheap SATA drive and I'm finished. In the future I will slowly replace the following things as they become cheap or I have the money:

PS/2 Devices -> USB
Dual Core 2.0 Ghz -> Quad Core 3.0+ Ghz
2 GB RAM -> 4 or 8 GB RAM
IDE CD-ROM -> SATA DVD-ROM
Onboard Video -> Dedicated Video
VGA Monitor -> DVI LCD

These are little upgrades that I can do over the next two years. The big sacrifice I made is space (almost no room to work in the case) and possibly quality of components, however I have had lots of good experience with Asus and trust their brand.

Now if I wanted a system that I would use for gaming this would have been a terrible choice (I'd definitely want a nicer case + dual PCI-Express x16 2.0 slots, no onboard video, and rock solid BIOS for overclocking).

### Post by shaggy999 on 2008-10-28
Ok, now we're getting somewhere. So you want a motherboard that has:

1) 2 PCI x16 2.0 slots for graphics cards
2) 6 USB ports, 2 of which you just need headers on the mobo for so you can plug in 2 USB ports from the front.
3) Only graphics cards use PCI x16 slots, a sound card will use either PCI or PCI 1x slots.
4) I'm assuming you're going Intel and you want an Intel Core 2 Duo or Intel Core 2 Quad? So you're going to want an LGA 775 board w/ support for top-end CPUs.
5) You had mentioned before you were going to start w/ 2 gigs of RAM w/ expandability to 8 GB I believe.

At this point you have to make a big decision. Intel is starting to phase in the X38/X48 motherboards with support for DDR3 memory and really top-end CPUs. You'll pay a hefty price initially, but you have more upgradability in the future. For most current processors DDR2 should suffice and you can buy a much cheaper board.
