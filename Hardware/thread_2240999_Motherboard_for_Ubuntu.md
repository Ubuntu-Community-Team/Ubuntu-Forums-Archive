### Post by weatherman2 on 2014-08-23
Any new motherboard and CPU you buy, if you run them in spec (not overclocked) and installed correctly with good components should be completely stable.  If not, like any other defective component, return them for replacements.  This also assumes you have a quality power supply and adequate cooling fans in the case.  Modern CPUs and motherboards, unless you get a fast graphics card that eats a lot of power, don't even get that hot anymore or use that much power, so you don't necessarily need three extra cooling fans anymore.  And you should base the size of the power supply mostly on what graphics card, if any, you get.  If you use the integrated Haswell graphics in the Intel CPU, you can get away with a small power supply.  I see 430 watt efficient power supplies on sale all the time (Corsair), and that would be plenty of power for a simple build, unless you get a separate video card.  Then use the video card's specs to choose the power supply.

I haven't built any Haswell/Socket 1150 computers so can't recommend a specific motherboard or CPU.  Obviously, the faster the CPU the better the performance but the more it's going to cost.

### Post by HugoNotte on 2014-08-27
Micheal, I have recently build a new pc for myself. I went the AMD route, since I found for what I do, the FX8320 8 core processor gives me he best bang for the buck. I bought a Gigabyte mid range motherboard and later found out, that Gigabyte's boards are not ideal for the use under Linux: you can't adjust the CPU fan via the BIOS, Gigabyte provides a little Windows program, from which you can create your own so called smart fan profile, but that's it. Even under Win7 that program crashes regularly after a few hours leaving you with very little fan control. Under Linux you can try and hack your way through to the fan control, but there is no easy way to do this. I found that under Linux the CPU runs a lot hotter than under Windows and the fan stayed at much lower rpm.

I now went and bought an Asus Sabertooth MB, which is in a higher class than the GA990XA-UD3 I had before. But what a pleasure! The BIOS let's you select a CPU fan (and chassis fan) profile and you don't need to worry about the CPU temps anymore.
As far as I have seen, even the mid range Asus boards have that feature in their BIOS, so no need to pay the extra money for a higher end board, if you don't want to. But in my experience restricting the budget to much just makes you upgrade sooner than later anyway.
