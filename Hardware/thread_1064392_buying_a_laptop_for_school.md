### Post by jbrown96 on 2009-02-09
I have to recommend a Thinkpad, even though it's out of your price range. They are extraordinarily rugged and have fantastic Linux support. Some general recommendations:
  1) Centrino is good. This is Intel CPU, wireless, audio, and mobo. They are very open-source friendly. 
  2) Intel GPUs have open-source drivers which play better with Linux, but they don't have very good performance.
  3) Computers that are sold with Linux have significantly better support (duh!).
  4) Lots of special keys (volume sliders, program shortcuts) will work but usually require workarounds. Same thing for LEDs and other displays. 
 

I do have experience working with several Linux laptops and here's what I've found.
I've configured Linux on Thinkpad T61p, HP dv6000, two other HPs, a 3 year old Dell, and Lenovo 3000. 
 The Thinkpad is mine, and I am constantly changing distros. Never had any hardware problems. volume buttons didn't work in Fedora 9, but no other issues. Like I said before, I think Thinkpads are the premier laptop.
  HPs are really good with compability. Never had any problems what so ever. They were full Centrino platforms: GPU, CPU, mobo, audio, and wireless. Couldn't be easier. The actual computers seem to be of good quality too. I don't like how their power cords connect to the computer. My girlfriend has broken two of them. 
  The Dell also had no problems, but was lacking in quality. 
  The Lenovo was the only one without Intel wireless. When I installed 8.04 on it, there were some problems. I have to edit a config file for audio and wireless was a pain for a while. On 8.10, there were no issues. Lenovo makes Thinkpads, so the two have some similar technologies. I was impressed by the battery life. 

You could always go with a netbook as well. These often come with Linux, although usually a bad distro, but they have excellent hardware support. They are small and light, which makes them ideal for class, but the keyboards can be cramped, so you should try it before you buy. I don't know if you need it immediately, but the NVIDIA Ion platform looks spectacular. Last month the platform was unveiled and according to NVIDIA there is a lot of OEM interest. They aren't available yet, so I would think about 3 months to market and 5 months for price stability. What's really cool is that they use the Intel atom CPU, which is very low power. They combine this with a 9400m GPU to give it good video and gaming performance. This should be a very powerful combo.
