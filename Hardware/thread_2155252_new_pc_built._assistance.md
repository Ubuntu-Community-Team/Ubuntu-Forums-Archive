You know what the most important part of a computer is? Power supply unit and cooling. If you skimp on the quality in that department, it doesn't matter what kind of parts you put in the casing. It'll just take longer for 'm to break. Here's your homework: [http://www.hardwaresecrets.com/article/327](http://www.hardwaresecrets.com/article/327)

I like Zalman when it comes to cooling, they have a wide selection and their fans are decent.

Gigabyte and Asus are both very good brands. I wouldn't even consider any other brands unless they paid me to use it.

Crucial is fine although I usually go with Kingston or Corsair because of their life-time warranty.
I don't like Intel, prefer AMD. Better prices and an integrated memory controller. Intel has some better performing processors according to reviewers. 

I'd do some very good research on that SSD before buying it. There's a lot of choice and I'm not convinced the technology has matured enough yet to match the reliability of regular hard drive 

I still use regular hard drives, mostly Western Digital. I bought a lot of those and they never let me down. You get far more GB per &#8364; and I think WDC still offers a 5-year-warranty on their Black line.

Don't know why you'd want to over-clock. You need hardware knowledge for that and one of the first things you wrote is that you didn't have much of that. If you're looking to void your system's warranty and generally make it very unstable, over-clocking is the way to go.

Box coolers blow. Companies want to save money and the way they do it is by shipping out sub-par CPU coolers. AMD box coolers are pathetic nowadays, I've also seen some very, very bad Intel stock coolers.

I believe someone already mentioned in this topic that you can reduce the CPU temperature well over 20 degrees Celsius by using a decent cooler. You won't "burn" your CPU with the stock coolers but you will reduce their lifespan and effectiveness by not mounting a quality cooler. Also, your computer will most likely be more quiet which is the main reason why I swap CPU coolers around. 

A thermistor stops very large currents from rushing in when you turn on the computer, it is not the same as a MOV which guards against voltage-spikes.
