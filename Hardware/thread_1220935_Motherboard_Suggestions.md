### Post by snek on 2009-07-23
Yeah you don't necessarily need to check the hardware all too much. Personally I wouldn't go for an nvidia motherboard chipset since I've had some bad experiences with them in the past (but those were windows machines tbh). Of course, nvidia vga chipsets are the best for Linux.

So far I haven't had any problems with any motherboard I've used Ubuntu on, but normally I only buy motherboards with Intel chipsets. 

The things you should really look for are the extra options to suite your needs like:
- amount of SATA connectors on the motherboard
- solid capacitors (for a long life)
- UDMA 133 or 100 (if you still have an IDE harddrive)
- 1 or 2 IDE connectors (plan on connecting multiple IDE hard drives / cd/dvd drives?)
- onboard audio (do you need SPDIF out to hook up a nice surround amp?)
- amount of RAM slots (in case you want to upgrade later)
- type of RAM (you might want to go for DDR3, or for low latency DDR2)

I've lost count on the amount of PC's I've made so far for friends and even some companies. These are basically the things I try to keep in mind when I build a new system, hope it helps you.

### Post by GoldenSun on 2009-07-23
I have had boards from biostar, gigabyte, evga, and soyo.  
I don't recommend the soyo...  And I haven't heard anything good about intel, but I have no personal experience from them.  

My friend has an Asus.  He said the only things he doesn't like is terrible tech support and he felt that he was restricted in OC'ing.  

Onboard is just fine.  I have the ati hd3300.  The drivers don't support it yet, but you can use like the hd2900 drivers and it works just fine.  Compiz, games, and movies run just fine.  Don't expect gtx 295 performance though.
