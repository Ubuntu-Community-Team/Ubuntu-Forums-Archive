### Post by sdennie on 2010-05-31
A few pieces of advice:

1) Rather than just make the decision to upgrade the CPU, I would instead run some benchmarks that you find meaningful on your machine and compare them to similar machines with slightly different configurations to determine what will give you the best bang for the buck.  A good place to start with that would be here: [http://www.phoronix-test-suite.com/](http://www.phoronix-test-suite.com/).

2) The slowest part in your computer is your hard drive.  If you aren't willing to spend the time doing #1 (I admit it's an arduous process), adding a very small Intel Value SSD (around $100) in addition to your 300GB drive and coming up with a partition layout that means you are almost always hitting the SSD will give your computer a jaw dropping speed increase for many, many tasks.  In my mind, this is upgrade #1 for anyone using a mechanical disk.

3) Because of #2, having more RAM can often make a huge difference in speed as your machine stays on longer.  As more and more of your disk is cached in memory, the machine behaves much faster.  

I recently did #2 and #3 (4GB to 8GB) in my Dell XPS M1330 and it's like having a different machine.  I completely understand the desire to go straight for the latest and greatest CPU but, unless you know you are CPU-bound, upgrading the parts around the CPU is often a much more noticeable upgrade.

If you do decide to go the processor route, I wouldn't overclock if you'd like the machine to last 10 years.  10 years is a tall order for any machine and overclocking, even if it's stable, will reduce the likelihood of reaching that goal.

### Post by an0dos on 2010-06-06
I recently upgraded my desktop computer from an old Pentium 4 to 2.8Ghz Phenom X3 with 2GB of RAM. I had to replace the motherboard, RAM, and processor. I think I paid a total of about $180. 

You may end up saving quite a bit of money if you upgrade motherboard, RAM, and processor to something that's about middle-of-the-line every 3 years or so instead of trying to buy something that will last you 10 years. Just a thought. It's a side effect of [Moore's law]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moore%27s_law").

As for PSUs, don't just look at wattage and get the cheapest one with say 750 watts. You need to look at quality. If you PSU fails, it can take your motherboard, processor, RAM, and hard drive with it. Oh, and get a modular PSU. They make your case look much cleaner inside.

This wikihow has some good information: [http://www.wikihow.com/Buy-a-Power-Supply](http://www.wikihow.com/Buy-a-Power-Supply)
