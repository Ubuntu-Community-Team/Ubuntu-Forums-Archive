### Post by nixscripter on 2009-02-04
Cool idea, but I would give you a caveat from my own experiences.

I currently own a pre-installed laptop from Dell, which shipped with Ubuntu 7.04. Everything worked, though some of it (like the proprietary modem) not very well. Mosts was open-source, some was proprietary, because Dell creates funny motherboards.

When I upgraded to 7.10 (from 7.04), all the propriety stuff broke. And as I continued to upgrade, and more broke. Not only did proprietary drivers start conflicting, but open-source stuff had Dell-specific patches in it I wasn't told about (I deduced later), and it broke.

So, I would say that if you are going to start this sort of "middle man" packaging company, you will have two significant challenges to do it right:

1. If you offer software support, you've got your work cut out for you. The more open source software you include, the more difficult it will be to support, because you will have to track that software's progress and bugs to maintain your users. The more proprietary software you include, the more difficult it will be to support, because will have to do all the development yourself.

2. The hardware *really* matters. At best, you will have to figure out a combination of hardware that runs with all open-source, unmodified stuff. At worst, you will have to write or contract your own drivers (a la Dell) which you must then control (whether you release the source or not). Most likely, you will be able to use open-source stuff, but will have to patch it, and thus do your own change management in some way for "your version" of what you ship.

I wish you luck -- and I do think it's crazy, but if you know what you're getting into, I like the idea. ;-)

### Post by nbr on 2009-02-04
I own a Dell Inspiron 1420 that came with Ubuntu 7.04.
I reinstalled 7.10 and encrypted the whole disk and everything works quite well.
I am overall confident that Ubuntu will work in Dell laptops (and other brands), just because it is getting better.
However, if I were to buy a new laptop I would do extensive research to be sure that the currently sold model works with Ubuntu.
Therefore, if you have laptops at competitive prices that you guarantee that works with Ubuntu, then you would save many people the burden of researching. 
However, as Ubuntu gets better and works with more models out of the box, your share of the market might decrease with time.
To me, the price and hardware quality would have to be very attractive to consider buying an unknown brand, as I don't care about Ubuntu support.
