### Post by zexelon on 2009-03-03
To begin, I have a history with Linux dating back about 8 years now. Linux was the first operating system I owned as a result of my inability to purchase a copy of Windows 2000 while in high school ($225 + tax is allot when you work for next to nothing and just spent all your money on hardware) so I purchases a copy of Mandrake 7.0 and spent months learning how to use this fancy new take on DLL hell (a.k.a. Dep-Hell).

Since that time I have spent years working with most of the popular versions of Linux and advocating for their use. As a result of this experience I can say without a doubt Ubuntu Linux is by far the best Linux distro available to the general public.

Ubuntu Linux is by far the easiest to install and it has made incredible strides in streamlining software installation and management.

Linux as a whole operating system (not just the kernel) has matured and come along way since I started messing around with it as a teenager, however it still has some very large hurdles to overcome before it will ever come close to reaching the mass market. 

For example, I am now on to install attempt number 3 of Ubuntu 8.10. It appears that something has been broken between the new kernel versions, xorg and the proprietary nvidia drivers in the 177 package. This is now the second time in the last 4 or so years that the graphics subsystem(s) (namely xorg and the kernel) have "advanced" and broken compatibility the commercially available drivers for the most significantly important piece of hardware in a computer! (I can live without a printer, scanner or even mp3 player) The first big issue was when xorg changed their ABI and seemed to forget to notify Nvidia and ATI. 

This latest issue is simply another glaring reason why Linux will not make it in the desktop market, especially not with power users who demand stability and reliability. User friendliness needs to be ingrained into the vary soul of developers... much like Microsoft has done and even more so like Apple has done. 

Well my third install of Ubuntu 8.10 is almost complete and I will once more battle with it to make it work... All of this simply to recover data off an ext3 drive so I can use it in Windows Vista...

Long live Microsoft Windows... Long live Linux... alas on the trailing edge...

### Post by zexelon on 2010-11-10
Almost 2 years later and this is still the case. Ubuntu 10 has made great strides towards progressing Linux and it is the OS I first recommend to anyone who wishes to use the internet... and only the internet. 

The Linux community as a whole is still completely missing the boat on taking on the desktop. With a fractured community largely run by researchers looking to push their own agendas, or borderline communist GPL3 licensing schemes, Linux is truly at the opposite end of the spectrum from the Microsft's of this world. 

This whole topic is however moot at this point as it looks as though the "desktop" as we know it is rapidly being relegated to a business tool and the consumer market is rapidly moving to always connected ultra portable computing. On this front Linux still has a significant chance if we could find someone to lead the charge and force some standardization in the community, on this front Apple is definitely the standard to live up to, but Windows 7 is coming to tablets and it promises to at least be a solid contender. 

Long live Apple... Long live Microsoft... Long live Ubuntu... alas still serving the world, but never owning it...
