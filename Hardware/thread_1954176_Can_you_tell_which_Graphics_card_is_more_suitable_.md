### Post by 1clue on 2012-04-07
Intel generally has a consistently working driver, but also generally does not have high performance.

nVidia and ATI go back and forth, generally one is better than the other at any given time but while nVidia might be better now, 6 months or a year from now ATI will likely take the lead again.  I think that generally speaking it's a bit out of sync with the hardware development.  It seems one or the other comes out with a new line of hardware, then they get the drivers working right on Windows, then Mac, and then finally they get around to Linux.  So the driver function works 6 months or a year after the card was introduced.

Generally speaking with Linux, you want mature hardware.  Don't buy a bleeding edge card unless you're ready to write your own driver for it.

I'm currently using an nVidia gt200 card and it has worked nicely for awhile.

Many of the fancier video and sound cards get support for the basic features in the driver and then all the stuff you paid extra money for are unsupported and may never get support.  Do some homework if you want high performance or new features to work, or you will waste your money.

My sound card for example has an external console that is completely useless on Linux.  I could have saved USD$100 and got the one without it.  Even though the driver says it completely supports all features, this console has never worked for me.  I've heard similar things about the fancier video cards.
