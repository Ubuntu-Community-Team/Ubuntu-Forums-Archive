### Post by weatherman2 on 2015-08-04
> **Josimba said:**
> If I want to future proof my Ubuntu laptop for the next 5 years or so.  Do I really need over 8GB of Ram and a SSD?  or can I easily get good mileage out of the Dell Laptop?  Again I thank anyone who read this far and appreciate any advice people can give me.

To me, the most important question is: how easy is it to upgrade these things?  (And what's the max RAM supported by the laptop?)  That is, how difficult is it to access the hard drive physically so I can remove it and replace it with an SSD?  Because if it's only a few screws to remove a bottom panel, then upgrading to an SSD will be super easy.  I'd never pay a premium price to Dell or someone else now just to get an SSD, when SSD prices are dropping like a rock.  Good 500GB SSDs are already $150 USD and cheaper.  If you need to wait a year, I'd expect them to be under $100.

Some newer laptops, curiously, have limitations on the max amount of RAM they support.  I find this a big limitation.  I have a slow CPU in my netbook, but I upgraded to an SSD and 16GB of RAM (very easy) - and I find it extremely usable.   Anyway - if you need/want 16GB of RAM someday, simply make sure any laptop you buy can support it (and make sure, like the hard drive, that it doesn't require removing 50 screws and the the keyboard to get to it).  Sometimes buying a new laptop with more RAM doesn't cost much more than upgrading it yourself.  Check prices.  I'm recently seeing sales on 8GB DDR3L SO-DIMM RAM for under $40 a stick, FYI.

I think people exaggerate the need for a beefy CPU and graphics card, unless they are planning to do serious gaming.  You don't need much of a CPU to write PHP and Python code.  But you will appreciate a lot of RAM so you can multi-task.  I'd appreciate a faster CPU than my netbook currently has (a dual core Sandy Bridge Celeron), but I have used it daily for a few years and have survived quite well.  An i5 dual core would be plenty for me; a quad core would be a waste.  But I don't game at all.

### Post by weatherman2 on 2015-08-04
I'd add (since this is the hardware forum) that I've upgraded the wireless cards in many laptops, too.  (I prefer Intel wireless cards, as they seem to work out of the box in Ubuntu and work reliably.)  I'd want an 802.11ac wireless card in any new laptop I buy - or at least the ability to upgrade to one later.  FYI, HP and Lenovo laptops prohibit upgrading your wireless card by design due to a "whitelist."  Dell, Toshiba, and others don't have this limitation.  That's why I will never buy an HP or Lenovo laptop.  If you do, I'd make sure it already has an 802.11ac wireless card to "future proof" it.
