### Post by northern lights on 2008-08-03
My laptop is an Asus W1/N series from 2005. The original harddrive gave up few months ago and I bought a new one.

Now, the new drive is already acting up again even though it's way too recently bought. I treat my laptop fairly careful also.

Sometimes, when powered up, the HDD will not be detected and you end up with 'Please insert proper boot device and press Enter' - we've all seen this.

The first time it happened, I didn't want to believe the new drive was gone also, so I took it out, cleaned the slot - desperate and hopeful measures.

Funny enough, after several tries of putting it back in, taking it out again and doing some more superfluous cleaning it worked. Even funnier, once booted and not powered off, the comp will run smoothly for days. However, if I shut it down, more often than not it will again not boot.

Over the past weeks I've learned to live with this problem. Today I'm not opening the laptop anymore. If it won't boot, I nowadays know fairly well where to pound the laptop with my fist to make it work. I don't literally have to whack, but without the use of decent force it won't react. Usually after one to three tries it boots. If I don't move/touch the comp other than pushing the power button it will never boot once the issue is back. Only beating it helps.

I suspect the culprit not to be the drive itsself as it is too new. I've even become used to the occasional 30 seconds I spent beating my laptop, but over the long run this practice can actually only be rather hard on the drive.

Any ideas on why shock therapy works when I wanna get my HDD to work?

By the way:
- No, I don't have another 2.5" PCMCIA HDD to test whether it's non-drive related...

### Post by bernierm on 2008-08-03
With 3.5" hard drives, this usually meant that the motor or the bearings were on the way out. The drive requires a lot of torque to start up from a stop. Once it is up and spinning, it'll keep spinning no problem.

To prevent motor burnout, the drive controller will give up if it can't get the disk up to speed in a certain amount of time. In the past, sometimes I'd toggle the machine's on/off button until the drive had enough momentum to complete it's power-up cycle (not exactly healthy for the hardware). I wonder if your 'percussive maintenance' is accomplishing the same thing. It's probably not healthy for the hardware as well.

Laptops being a bit more fickle, it could be a power issue-not enough power getting to the drive. Maybe cleaning the contacts (on the drive and connector) is worth a try?
