### Post by LeoSolaris on 2008-08-13
Here is my personal break down and reasoning:

Screen and graphics...  Go for broke here. Take the top one because you're Gimping. It is easier on the eyes, literally. 

CPU... Given the options, save here and stay standard. It is low power, and rather peppy in it's own right. The next real step up doesn't come till you spend an extra $170 (bigger cache), and it will consume more power. An extra $217 just for a little more caching and the same low power consumption could be worth it if you have that much to spare.

Memory...  Here I would save a little to tell you the truth. I have been using Ubuntu for a while now, and the only time I can get it to swap at all with 2 gigs is with VirtualBox running full tilt. For the time being I would stick to 2 g's just because if you feel you do need more, you can always upgrade it later with a couple more sticks which keep getting cheaper by the month.

Hard Drive...   This one I cannot stress enough, stick to the 7200 RPM or above. The responsiveness of the system is greatly increased. The major bottle-neck in computers is the hard drive, so the faster it's read/write times are, the faster the rest of your machine will work. If you go for less space, and add a USB hard drive or something to add room for all of the pictures you will be using as source material, it will be worth it, even with the slowest processor on that list.

Warranty... Most laptops break in 2-4 years. It's rather rare for them to break in the first year. That said however, check your credit card info to see if it has an automatic extended warranty for purchases. Mine does, and generally it is easier to use, because you do not have to mail the laptop back and forth. 

That's my two cents anyways. 

Hope it helps.

### Post by lionen on 2008-08-14
Just in case someone else comes upon this thread because they have similar needs, here is an email response I got from system76. I'm hearing some similar tips:

- Higher screen resolutions are better for graphics design applications, as they give you more pixels of detail per square inch.
- Most moderate graphics work can be accomplished with 2GB of RAM using the GIMP. Heavier stuff? Go for 3 or 4GB.
- Based on your stated needs, either Intel or nVidia graphics are fine. However, if you want higher resolution, you have to go with the nvidia
- Hard drive size: If you're a serious designer (i.e. your work = $$), I'd not recommend getting to comfortable with keeping finished projects on your on-board hard drive. If it fails, it is costly to recover your work. I'd keep non-current stuff on an external drive. So, you probably don't *need* that large of a hard drive. But if you're producing graphics frequently, you might want might to go with a moderate sized drive and then keep good backups.
- Hard drive speed might be important to you if you are doing a large volume of work. Faster speeds allow the computer to write to, and read from, the drive more quickly. If you're just doing a small amount of work, you'll probably not even notice the difference.
