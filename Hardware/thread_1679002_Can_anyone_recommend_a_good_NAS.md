### Post by perspectoff on 2011-01-31
All NAS drives require security management, and you shouldn't ignore or be put off by it. It is a networked device, after all, and not everyone secures their network properly.

At the very minimum you should set up password access to your NAS. That way, if your network is hooked up to the Internet and someone manages to hack into your router, they won't have immediate access to your files on NAS without hacking into it as well.

My ancient Buffalo LinkStation that I used for years ran Windows 2003 server. I think I got the thing (500 Gb) for about $160 several years ago. (I think their current 1 Tb drive is still about $160). While I'm not a huge fan of Windows servers in general, the thing was reliable (and is still in use by my family). However, I was diligent in learning and maintaining security features, as anything Windows-based is prone to attack if you don't. 

In spite of that, I still think an inexpensive NAS, even if Windows-server based, is worth your while. Just take your time and learn the settings.

There are only too many people willing to automate things for you, whether you like it or not. When anything is "too easy" to use, it is often too easy to hack. All software requires some learning to use. If you are unwilling to learn about your computer (including security settings), you ought to stick to pencil and paper.

A quick glance for available options include the Western Digital WorldBook 1 Tb NAS for $120, the Netgear "Home media" NAS for $180 (Netgear NAS are quite good, use RAID,  and use hot-swappable drives which can be replaced if they burn out), but usually are a little more expensive. 

If you can afford the Netgear (and are able to find one, as they are very popular), it is one of the best options. But I think the others would be fine for your needs, too. Just be sure to turn on the security.
