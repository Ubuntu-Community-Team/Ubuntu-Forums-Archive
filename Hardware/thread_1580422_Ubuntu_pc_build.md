I'll come straight out and say I'm an AMD fan,and as much as I would like to say 'AMD is faster'...its not. MHz for MHz, the iX series are faster than the Phenom II/Athlon II chips. 

Some programs are single-threaded only, other programs are multithreaded. On single threaded programs the fastest CPU will be the fastest core***** (no matter if it is a single core, dual core or quad core). As time goes by, more and more programs are moving to multithreading, so what is 'faster' now mgiht very well be 'slower' at some point in the future. 

**** BTW, 'the fastest core' (meaning 'the highest clock speed') wont always be 'faster'. Its not just clock speed, cache makes a big difference (architecture makes a huge difference as well). 

For example, on a single threaded program, a Phenom II X2 545 (1MB L2 + 6MB L3 cache, 3.0GHz) is faster than a Athlon II X2 250 (1MB L2 cache, 3.0GHz) or an Athlon II X4 640 (2MB L2 cache, 3.0GHz). Slightly improved architecture, and more cache.  

With the i5 650 (3.33GHz, turbo 3.6GHz, 4MB cache) vs the i5 750 (2.4GHz, 3.2GHz turbo, 8MB cache), the 650 should be faster on single threaded programs, but not by as much as the GHz speeds would lead you to believe (thanks to the extra cache). For a system that you are thinking about using virtualbox/vmware with, I'd go for the extra cores and cache myself. IIRC virtualisation 'likes' as much cache as possible, and the extra cores arent going to hurt either.
