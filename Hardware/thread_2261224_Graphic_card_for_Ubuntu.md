### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-01-17
You can't avoid the marketing of cards to gamers, but if you aren't going to be gaming or doing heavy video/graphics work, most any popular card that is marketed to low-end gaming should be suitable for your purposes.

Avoid the newest card releases because Linux drivers might not yet be available or reliable.  Look for a card with low power requirements and low noise levels (the fan). Nvidia cards using the company's Maxwell technology fit in that category.  For example, the 750 and 750ti cards marketed by several companies.

Remember that some cards draw power only from the slot they are inserted in, while many others must also be attached to the power supply. So be sure to check a card's specifications for the minimum power supply capacity recommended by the vendor.

Also look at a card's physical size to be sure it will actually fit in your system.

And you'll need a card with at least one output port that matches one of the input ports on your monitor. For example, if you want to use a monitor's HDMI port, then, of course, the card needs an HDMI port. (Cards and monitors typically have VGA ports but VGA is less than satisfactory these days.)

Ubuntu detects hardware as it boots and will use an appropriate open source driver for the card.  Proprietary drivers supplied by Nvidia or AMD can be installed if you wish, but the open source drivers are fine for non-gaming use.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-01-17
Well, I use an Nvidia 750ti card made by EVGA because it's low-power requirement is a good fit for the rather low-capacity power supply in my Lenovo desktop system.

Take your time researching cards.  Look for problems with specific cards once you've identified a few alternatives. Check online reviews and user comments at sites like Amazon and Newegg. What you're looking for there are trends:  Many people say it's great or many people complaining about the same problem.
