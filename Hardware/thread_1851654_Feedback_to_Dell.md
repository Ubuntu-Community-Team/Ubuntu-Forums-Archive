### Post by todd_finch on 2011-09-29
Hey guys, 

Thanks for expressing an interest in giving feedback.  Just to be clear, I'm not a Sales guy, nor am I in Tech Support.  My role is to plan and launch future Linux products at Dell.

There are lots of areas I'd like to explore, e.g. product availability in your region, pricing, marketing, usage scenarios, Ubuntu Tech Support/Services. But before we get to that, I'd like to see if you "value" the most basic thing we do with Ubuntu. 

If you download stock Ubuntu from [www.ubuntu.com](www.ubuntu.com), the OS image will probably support all of the various hardware in your computer. But the basic value proposition Dell offers is a) providing Canonical with pre-release models, getting them to test the latest version, and ensuring all of the various HW components work like they're supposed to. Afterwards, Canonical will put their official "Ubuntu Certified" stamp of approval showing this particular Dell platform was put through the tests and works well. 

Do you value Dell going through these hoops with Canonical's testing team and producing an "Ubuntu Certified" computer? If so, try to quantify how much you value it, e.g. $5, $10, etc.. I'm trying to get a feel for what this "value" is worth to you. 

Thanks in advance for your feedback, 
Todd 

PS: tidbit of info for you--Dell enables Ubuntu on 99% of all computers Dell makes. We ship many, many units with Ubuntu pre-installed and certified. Unfortunately, not in every region. And also unfortunately, some regions don't market it as well as I'd like. I don't have much control on how's that's managed. </SPAN>

### Post by todd_finch on 2011-10-10
Thanks to the Ubuntu Forum Council for opening this thread back up.  (I should have given them a heads-up that I was planning to ask some questions.)
 
Thanks to all for providing feedback.  I heard a lot on this and a few other forums, so let me repeat and summarize.  Please let me know if I got it right, or if I missed anything.
 
#1 Desire: Choice.  When you go to Dell.com, you want to see a choice in operating systems--meaning you want to see/choose Ubuntu alongside MS Windows.
 
Merchandising: You want consistent availability across regions across product lines, e.g. Inspiron, Vostro, Optiplex, Latitude.  Currently, you're not seeing Ubuntu and that's frustrating.
 
Pricing: Assuming you were comparing the exact same system with Windows, you'd be willing to pay for the hardware plus 20%-40% of the cost of Windows.  This is seen as reasonable since Dell is incurring costs working with Canonical and other hardware vendors to produce an "Ubuntu Certified" system.
 
Ecosystem: You'd like to see peripherals work better, e.g. discrete graphics cards, printers, etc.
 
Ubuntu Tech Support: In the same way Windows users are accustomed to getting OS support from the OEM (Dell), you'd like to get the same level of support for Ubuntu.  (Question I'm wondering: are you willing to pay for it?  Most of you are probably technical, so I'm guessing not.)
 
Education Industry: Factory installing Edubuntu may be a good opportunity targeting schools.
 
Future products: Tablets.  You're interested in Ubuntu on tablets.  (Please elaborate if you find think users would be willing to purchase this type of device.)
 
Tidbit--please comment: When OEM's ship a Windows machine and the user either wipes Windows and installs Ubuntu, or sets up a dual-boot machine--in either case, the OEM and companies like IDC and Gartner count that a sale for MS Windows.  In this scenario, Linux/Ubuntu doesn't get counted.  This matters a lot.  As OEM's research what types of units are purchased, these get counted as MS Windows and the cycle continues.  The only way Linux/Ubuntu gets counted is when it goes out of the factory with Ubuntu factory-installed on the hard drive.  My goal is to make Ubuntu so compelling, people will completely bypass MS Windows and order Ubuntu straight out of the factory.  Do you agree with me?  Why or why not?  
 
Thanks,
Todd Finch
(I work for Dell)
