### Post by efflandt on 2017-08-12
Good performance for doing what is the question? If all you are doing is email, web surfing, and watching streaming video, it does not require much of a computer with integrated graphics. If doing advanced graphics design or video editing, that is another matter. For example some things like blender can take advantage of fast cuda cores on a graphics card to do calculations faster than a CPU.

I don't know if you have any existing desktop PC. But for me as a gamer with a 7 year old Dell XPS 8100 with i5 650 3.2 GHz, 8 GB DDR3 1066 RAM with stock 350 watt PSU, and 3 GB GTX 1060 @ 1080p on 32" HDTV, it was worthwhile upgrading to still old i7 870 2.93 GHz (but faster turbo than the i5) and 16 GB 1333 RAM for a little over $300, which is still a bit below a newer i5 with faster RAM. But it allowed me to set higher graphics in games without losing speed, would have likely cost me more for CPU, motherboard, RAM, PSU, case to upgrade to newer i5, and some i7's alone cost more than that.

I cannot say that I am on a "fixed" income, but lost my job of the past 40 years Nov 2015 at age 63.5 before I had saved as much as I had planned to for retirement. And while I learned investing and am making more investing my IRA (retirement) money than I have withdrawn (without collecting social security yet), I still watch what I spend in case there is a downturn in the markets (still up after past week).

### Post by him610 on 2017-08-14
FWIW, I built or upgraded three machines recently - one with AMD processor, two with Intel processors. Cost to upgrade/build AMD about $100, modest Intel build about $350, high-end Intel with all new components, about $1500. 

Building a new machine is a process - something I really enjoy. :)
Establish a budget. How much do you want to spend?
Decide on your form factor: full ATX tower, mid-tower ATX, micro-ATX, mini-ITX. 
Decide on your CPU, motherboard, and GFX card. Other posters have provided plenty of advice on these.
Recommend you seriously consider a fully modular or semi-modular power supply.

Model several systems using a spreadsheet, or paper and pencil.
Review the specifications for each component. Don't forget the user reviews.
Compare the pros and cons of each system model. 
Eventually, you will conclude which system is for you.
