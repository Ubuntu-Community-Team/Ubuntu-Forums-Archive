### Post by TheFu on 2020-01-04
I really hope you didn't pay over $100 for this recently.  Pentium4 CPUs are pre-2010 and upgrading isn't worth the effort.  A used chromebook for $85 with a Celeron 2995U CPU would be 2x faster than this machine.  We replaced Mom's P4 in 2009 for a different system.

Buying a new motherboard and keeping the CPU would be a terrible idea.  What is your budget?  There are a few of articles here about how to upgrade a system.  I usually start with a ballpark budget, say $200.  Then I pick a CPU, compatible MB, and compatible RAM.  I'll reuse as much of everything else I have already.  The case, PSU, HDD, SSD, GPU, keyboard, mouse, monitor ... all of that will be reused.

To sorta get a feel for compatible components, use [https://pcpartpicker.com/](https://pcpartpicker.com/).  This is only for CPU and MB and RAM compatibility.  As for which is specifically good with Linux/Ubuntu, get the exact MB model and google for issues.  Probably best to avoid any models less than 1 yr old. New is the enemy of stable for Linux systems.  For example, the newest Ryzen systems with onboard GPUs (also called APU systems) need to have the most current kernels to work. That can make a chicken/egg problem since the installer doesn't usually have that latest kernel.  Some things are more hassle than I want to deal with and needing a very new kernel is one of those that I want to avoid.

I'm also an Intel GigE NIC bigot.  All my systems are gigE wired ethernet connected and Intel makes a good quality NIC that is extremely well supported with solid drivers.  The other vendors always seem to have some issue and $15 more for a motherboard with an Intel NIC is worth the price to me to avoid any hassles.  Plus Intel NICs offload more processing from the CPU onto the NIC, so more packets per second get handled for less CPU effort.  

When I'm picking a CPU, I'll compare passmark benchmarks between each with the price and exact model. Those 3 things help me to decide the best bang for the dollar/rupi within my budget.  A $50 CPU that does 6000 passmarks doesn't compare to an $80 CPU that does 13,500 passmarks, right?  I'd pick the $80 CPU every time.

What is a "flip card computer?"

BTW, full computers made by HP, Compaq, Dell, Asus, Lenovo, packard bell, etc. often had proprietary connectors or odd sized components that would only fit into their systems, so the ability to reuse isn't always possible.  I believe some of the desktop models have stopped doing that in the last 5 yrs, but I honestly don't know.  After being burned by a Compaq desktop purchase in 1999, I decided I would NEVER, EVER, just buy a pre-built desktop again, except from a local builder who would do the same things I would do and stay with standard components.  I'm still using a case from 1999.

### Post by Yellow Pasque on 2020-01-04
>  i want to know which motherboards are compatible with my processor
You didn't tell us what CPU you have. I am assuming it's a Pentium 4 (possibly the Pentium 4 515 from Googling). I agree with ^TheFu that it's probably not worth spending money on this system unless you can find a very inexpensive motherboard. The Pentium 4 515 doesn't even support 64-bit.

That said, the main things you need to make sure a compatible motherboard has if you want to reuse the CPU and RAM:

- LGA775 socket, also known as Socket T
- micro-ATX ("uATX")
- DDR memory supprt

That basically means you need a uATX motherboard with an Intel 915G chipset. Note that there are some other chipsets (SiS/VIA/ATI) for this socket, but I would avoid them, especially if using the integrated graphics.
If you can find some inexpensive DDR2-533 RAM (which is probably easier than finding an inexpensive motherboard), then you could get a motherboard with a 945G chipset.
There are a lot of motherboards that meet this criteria, so if you want specific models, it's probably best to give a link to the vendor you plan to use.

> full computers made by HP, Compaq, Dell, Asus, Lenovo, packard bell, etc. often had proprietary connectors or odd sized components
True, but the motherboard in question looks like a standard microATX.
