### Post by fuzzyworbles on 2011-08-15
I don't have the answer to either of your questions. Honestly, all I did was google 'ubuntu hardware compatibility list' and what came up seemed pretty official. Really, you can do well getting a used Dell. They notoriously use linux compatible hardware. If you have your heart set on building your own, you can do what I do: Check out pricewatch.com, find components  fitting your needs at a price you're willing to pay, then google the individual components to see if there are horror stories getting the particular component to work under linux, while being sure that the post is no more than, say, a year old. From the sounds of it, though, i think this procedure misses the requisite that you know what components fit your needs: e.g., what sort of motherboard? chip? memory speed and quantity, etc. 

These days, chances are much much better than years back that whatever hardware you get, it'll likely work without a hitch. However, because you've indicated frugality, you might be averse to risking your loot on the chance something doesn't work out of the box on ubuntu. This is why i'm going to advise again that you get a used Dell with an integrated intel video card (shoot, even a new low end Dell goes for under $300). 

best of luck.

### Post by .... on 2011-08-15
> email, web surfing, word processing and maybe some photo editing.I dislike Dell's support (site). I like HP the best of the big manufacturers.
Here's how I'd build it:
CPU/GPU: Least expensive Llano or Sandy Bridge I could find.
Storage: SSD plus a very quiet, 5400RPM storage drive
RAM: Start with 2x2GB DDR3. That's more than enough for the listed tasks.
Mobo: Mini or micro ATX. No need for a big case.
PSU: [http://www.silentpcreview.com/article699-page1.html](http://www.silentpcreview.com/article699-page1.html)

I'd probably also get a large CPU heatsink and quiet 120mm fan, but that's only for silence-obsessed freaks like me. The stock CPU cooler will be sufficient, but will be the loudest component by far (unless you have a crappy hrad disk or PSU fan).

Hey Bob, I thought I'd chime in as well. If you want a basic home office computer definitely go with the dell. I've installed about 30 of them and they work great. You can them here [http://www.dell.com/outlet ]("http://www.dell.com/outlet"). If you want something really cheap look at this dell website  [http://www.dfsdirectsales.com/](http://www.dfsdirectsales.com/). All of these are 2 years and older former corporate lease computers. Starting at $130 you can't get much cheaper than that. If you want to get your feet wet with building your own, which I recommend, but are a little apprehensive start with a barebones system. All you'll need to do is buy a hard drive, a DVD writer, a processor(CPU), and some memory(RAM). Here is one to look at [http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16856115040](http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16856115040). I would start with an i3 cpu, 4 GB of Corsair RAM, and 1 TB Western Digital Black. Many on here will object to my preferences regarding brand but after over 200 successful builds I stand by my choices. Don't go with a SSD hard drive to start with. Look at Newegg, Amazon, and Tigerdirect. With Tigerdirect you'll have to pay sales tax since their in Miami. Good luck with whatever you decide.
