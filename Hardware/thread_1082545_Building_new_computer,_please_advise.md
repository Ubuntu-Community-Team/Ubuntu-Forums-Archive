### Post by brokenLockpick on 2009-02-27
A few thoughts.

1. yes there are 4 GB sticks out there but they're expensive so 16GB is possible with only 4 slots

2. Considering your monitor situation and the price range of graphics cards you are looking at I would consider a motherboard with integrated graphics and then buy a discreet card when you get a better monitor, those midrange cards can vary alot in quality for a given price in a rather short time frame.

3. as far as choice of video cards I would do some serious research as to what is available in that general price range, last time I priced cards I found that at the $100ish range sometimes a few extra dollars could yeild a significantly better card.

4. consider something like this
[http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16856167037](http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16856167037)
add 2 gig stick of laptop memory decent HDD and as you know the Optical drives are cheap. The build shouldnt exceed 300 and the performance is surprising. A friend and I tried one of those with both Vista and Photoshop then with Ubuntu and Gimp and it was shocking just how well it did, while not spectacular the performance was antiquate for very little cost. It's really quite amazing what a sub 300 dollar machine can do these days. You'll probably never game on it though.

Edit: I just noticed that you had upgradability as a concern, and my final suggestion is not really upgradable save for a larger HDD, but upgrades sometimes cease to make sense. I remember the first desktop I build and my plans for it, I was going to do a round of upgrades two years later to breath new life into it. I went as far as to check the "technology roadmaps for the various companies that I was buying from and all looked as if the platform would be suitable for upgrading in two years. In reality however the hardware landscape changed so much and the companies did not follow the "roadmaps" that they had announced. Basically two years later, the processor socket was now obsolete despite the company's claims two years earlier that it would be valid for another three. The long and short of it was that it made more sense at that point to build essentially a whole new system as it would be offer far better performance for not much more money than the upgrades I'd planned on.

### Post by lindsay7 on 2009-02-28
I looked at what you are buying and it is all good,  One thing I think you need to be aware of is the dvd is an oem version and will come with no docs or cd with and support. Most of the time you do not need this stuff.  Just know you are not going to get anything but the drive. You can download whatever you need if anything.  I have built five machines in the past 12 months. and two are running ubuntu 8.10 with no problems.  They both have nvidia vid cards. The wiring of the pins for the hdd leds, speaker, power swithc. and reset switch is a little confusing and if you get the connectors backward you leds may not light when they should. Just reverse the connection,  All of the componets that you are buyings should be compatible.  The sata hard drive sometimes is not recognized by the mother board and you may need to run the driver cd that should come with it before you load windows or Ubuntu.  You also may need to "play with the bios settings"  to make sure everything is set correctly, like booting from the dvd. and also the boot order, most likey it should be set in this order, 1.cd or dvd, 2. hd where he os will be. 3. and 4. etc other devices. If you are using sata dvds and hard drives you do not have to worry about slaves and masters so you will be good to go.

And a word of advise, make sure that you turn off and unplug the power supply before you connect or disconnect anything in the case.  All of the componets hate to have power on them and be unplugged. swithching of the power switch on the back of the computer DOES NOT turn of all of the power inside the case and motherboard.

### Post by High_Speed on 2009-02-28
> **brokenLockpick said:**
> Interesting, I was thinking of one for my entertainment center so I could surf from the couch. Maybe I'll hold off a bit and see if my buddy's holds up before I drop the cash. Just out of curiosity which component failed?

Well keeping in mind that isn't the exact unit we based our builds on, but over the last 4 years at work we have experimented with dozens of builds based off mini PCs like that.   The idea was ultimately to build a small footprint PC to sit in a cramped restaurant office or even under the counter.  It's job was not to do much processing, but just to stay running and connected to the Internet at all times.

What we always found was that after cramming a CD drive and hard-drive into the case, no matter what case we used it just couldn't breathe good enough.  Heat was always a major issue.  I remember one that got so hot the IDE ribbon cabled literally melted between the board and harddrive...

Cases like that also have an external power brick that feeds an internal power distribution board.  I've seen the bricks burn up, I've seen the power distribution boards burn up, I've seen the fans burn up from having to spin at full speed non-stop trying to keep the unit cool.  From best I recall, those were the three most problematic areas of failure.  

We eventually gave up, and after two years of pleas to my boss that my support guys were getting too many support calls because of these "neat little computers" he finally agreed that the cool-factor just wasn't worth the support issues.  Then we went back to just using full-size atx mid-towers.

Anyway, your buddy might have good luck with that unit.  I honestly haven't any personal experience with that specific one.  But I would be very leery, personally.  I would definitely keep an eye on the temperature and the fans.

PS:  In our experience, our units would sometimes last about a year, but not much longer than that.
