I have a couple more things to add regarding this laptop:

1) The Seagate Momentus in mine crashed a month ago. It turned out to be not a fixable partition error or boot sector error, but a section of the drive became physically unusable. I had a month old backup and didn't loose much, but in the quest, I did find a bunch of utilities that are amazing.

2) to stevenyu
> I have recently bought the M51SN serie notebook, has anyone manage to get the internal tv tuner working under linux? I am having the sound problem, but looking at some of the early post I think I can solve it.

By tv tuner do you mean the sVidio port or an actual cable decoder in the video card? The sVideo behaves just like the other ports do regarding Nvidia and Xorg. Once plugged in, have nvidia-settings auto detect it, then configure it as you wish and restart Xorg. I do believe that plug is out only as well. 

I've actually figured out a nice way to always have 2 X screens setup in my xorg.conf file so that the DVI, VGA, and sVideo ports just plug and play. When nothing is plugged in, the laptop screen is business as usual. When I plug in one of the three addition ports and restart Xorg, the extra X server pops right up.

I prefer the extra x server to dealing with weird resolution problems and window behavior associated with twinview. Theres nothing worse then trying to do a presentation and look professional while your laptop is at 640x480. Likewise, theres nothing better then plugging in your laptop to your tv and watching a movie off your laptop (on the second x screen) while your desktop is untouched and you can surf the web and stuff (on the first x screen). Hopefully this answers your question, if not, hopefully it's useful in some way. My xorg.conf is below so you can check out how the dual x server lays out.

And the journey continues....

As mentioned before, my drive died on me. I've replaced it with a "Western Digital Scorpio WD3200BEVT 320GB 5400 RPM 8MB Cache SATA 3.0Gb/s" and I'm now running x86-64 Hardy. No problems at all so far other then just the fact that some software (flash, adobe, etc.) aren't released for x86-64 yet. I don't know if it was the new drive or the 64 bit, but boot time was cut in half and it is just overall noticeably faster; I love it. The 64 bit comes into play with compression/archiving, compiling, encoding/converting of media files, and not so much with email/internet stuff. But it's nice to know that it's there. 

I also decided to partition a little differently. Influenced by a typical linux server installation (and by the fact that I had a partition specific hardware failure and not a drive wide failure, so if I did this in the first place I could have possibly saved all my data) I decided to do the following:

I was more then generous on all fronts because the way I see it, if I'm close to filling up this laptop, then I have too much data that most likely isn't backed up. Storage is for external drives and dvd's. This laptop should be for use, not storage. 

What I had before was a swap partition and an everything else partition: 2 total. I didn't really see the logic in how partitions can actually help with regard to hardware problems in the drive if they arise. I thought that if something in the drive goes, everything goes. Turns out this experience showed me that they do act as a bit of a firewall and are worth doing. That being said, some reasoning for the sizes:

200 mb /boot - on my 3 other Ubuntu machines, 5 kernels totaled about 75 mb. round up and multiply by 2 and I'm safe forever.

8 gb swap - I have access to all 4 gb of ram now and swaps should be between 1.5 and 2 times the size of your ram. I got the space so I went for it.

15 gb / - on those same other Ubuntu machines, the / size (minus mounts and /home drive and with lots of addition programs and stuff) was between 5gb and 7gb. So 10gb is safe and 15gb means I'm safe forever.

252 gb /home - For all my stuff. This was actually what was left after I decided what everything else should be.

1 gb /tmp - Was difficult to wrap my head around. Under normal use, this is under 50 mb at all times. When you burn discs or dvd's, the temporary iso is stored here. I realized this after the fact. So I'm good for cd's but not so much for dvds. I had to change the location of my temp storage for iso burning in the gconf-editor under apps -> nautilus-cd-burner to a place in my /home directory. The catch-22 here is that 99% of the time, a small /tmp around 100 mb is cool unless you burn a dvd, in which case it needs to inflate to 5 gb to store the iso.

2 gb /var - similar weirdness. After I installed hardy fresh, I hit up synaptic and installed about 1.5 gig of libraries and stuff. I checked out my partitioning after an install spurt and this was almost full. I luckily found out it was because of all the .debs that synaptic stores and not the installed files themselves. "sudo apt-get clean" got rid of those temp files and put it back to about 20% used where it pretty much stays.

20 gb /arch - short for archive, I want to setup a type of regular backup system now where I can get regular backups into this partition. Separated from everything else, the backups can hopefully be safer if the drive goes bad. But this is of course, temporary storage until they can get onto a dvd or external drive.

Some more recent developments with regard to my hard drive failure that might be laptop model specific...SO YOUR ASUS M51Sn MIGHT BE AFFECTED. 

After some research, it turns out that the problem is most likely related to this bug ( [http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=795327](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=795327) ) which is a power saving problem with many laptops. Not exactly a bug but it's really a misconfiguration as I see it. 

As per his testing, my new hard drive (my old one as explained above had a full blown failure) gets about 90 Load_Cycles an hour. That's ridiculous and it probably expedited the death of an already flawed hard drive. I averaged probably under 4 hours of use a day and it died after 4 months.

something like this...
1) su

then reboot to this kernel (choose the right one in grub)
