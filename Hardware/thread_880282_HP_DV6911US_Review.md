### Post by squashpup on 2008-08-04
I recently bought a new laptop, and thought I'd post a short review of it here for anyone considering buying a laptop upon which to run Xubuntu. 

Here's my review of the HP Pavillion DV6911US.

Model: HP DV6911US
OS: Xubuntu Hardy
Price: $699 at Newegg with a mail-in $50 rebate.  No payments for 6 months (same as cash) with a Newegg Preferred account.

General:

Things that I like...

The nVidia graphics card is nice. It is surprising how much computing these days depends on a graphics card. Since I've gotten this computer, I've downloaded the Compiz 3D desktop (I showed pics earlier of it running on the school's old laptop), Google Earth (I could run it before, but it was marginal. Now, it works perfectly). FlightGear Flight Simulator, which is particularly awesome. It is not as advanced as Microsoft Flight Simulator, but the scenery looks real enough for the most part and the flight physics are perfect. I've been using the A-10 almost exclusively, but I've also played around with a P-51 Mustang, a 787, and a Cessna 172. I dusted off my old Logitech Wingman joystick, and have been playing this pretty hard ever since.

The 200 gb hard drive is spacious, considering that my old laptop was only 30GB. That hard drive is now in a USB enclosure, where it will stay. I can transfer all of my old files in here and take up very little room. Also, lots of room for other operating systems. Of course, I have Vista running, which I've played with all of 10 minutes, Xubuntu on another partition, and Windows XP running virtually in VMWare Server on a third partition. Keep in mind that the default install of Vista on this computer is about 25 GB (and packed with crap you don't want), so if you decide to keep it, you'll have to allow a little bit to make the partition.  I decided to give it 10 extra gb, for a total of 35.  So, even though 200 sounds like a lot, you'll lose that much right off the bat.

The DV6911US comes standard with three gb of RAM. At some point, I may upgrade, but everything is nice and snappy now. I can run Windows XP INSIDE of Linux and still allocate 512 MB of RAM to Windows XP. In Xubuntu, things start instantly. Firefox starts almost as soon as you click it.

This computer also has a built in DVD burner with Lightscribe.  I haven't tried yet to get Lightscribe working...when I do I will make another thread.  Burning CDs and DVDs works great.

The AMD Dual core processor can run XP over Linux in VMWare Server without so much as a hiccup. Network, modem, USB, power, video out, are all situated on the side of the computer, which is handy for accessibility purposes, but moves the exhaust fan to the rear of the computer, where it   will shoot hot air right onto your leg if you're actually using the computer on your lap.

Now, the things I dislike, in no particular order.

1) Touchpad.

Easily the worst touchpad I've ever used on any computer, ever, since the beginning of time. One, it's too big. A smaller touchpad is better, because it requires LESS movement to get from one side of the screen to the other. Two, it is centered to the COMPUTER, rather than the keyboard. Meaning, it's directly under my right thumb when my hands are on the home keys (IMHO, it should be centered roughly under the "B", but instead is centered between the "N" and the "M"). It is frustrating to be in the middle of typing something then end up with the cursor halfway across the screen. I guess if I practiced proper keyboarding technique and arched my wrists, I wouldn't be accidently hitting it all the time, but I don't have this problem with other laptops. It's a bad design. It should be smaller and centered under the spacebar, even if it ruins the overall look of the computer. And, anyway, I've seen computers with that arrangement and they don't look that bad. I fixed this by getting a USB Wireless mouse, which is the best invention since sliced bread. Problem solved, but it still upsets me that HP chose form over function.

2) Screen

The screen is glossy. That's stupid. It picks up every reflection and makes the screen difficult to read at times. Should be a matte finish so it kills the glare.  Hard to read in a brightly lit room.

3) No lid lock

Every laptop I've ever owned had a positive latching mechanism in the lid to keep it from being opened. This one has spring loaded hinges that will kind of snap the lid shut, albeit weakly.

4) Speakers

They don't sound bad, but I wish they were louder.

5) Windows Vista sticker which I peeled off the day I got the computer.

Overall, the things that I don't like are minor when compared to the things that I do. I have to say that, since I added the wireless mouse, I like this laptop pretty well.

Compatibility with Xubuntu is pretty good.  The wireless is not autodetected, and I tore my hair out for almost an hour trying to get ndiswrapper to work with a Broadcom driver.  I finally figured out it wasn't Broadcom.  I finally read about another person used and Atheros driver (net5211.inf and ar5211.sys...Google them if you need them).  Installed those and all works well.  Hope this advice helps someone else out.  

Ethernet works out of the box.  Modem has not been tested, but it's a Conexant.  If you know how to configure one of these, you should be OK.  I've never tried and don't think I'll plan to.

nVidia, as stated earlier, works too.  I downloaded EnvyNG and it installed everything automatically without a hitch.  Compiz works great and looks pretty.

The card reader also works flawlessly, with Xubuntu automounting SD cards and bringing them up automagically on the desktop.  

I even got the built in webcam to work, although I haven't the foggiest what I'd do with it.

The only thing I've found that doesn't work is the built in stereo microphones.  Doesn't mean, however, that you can't record in stereo, as the microphone jack will take a stereo input.  I can run this to my mixing board, and my mics out on channels 1 and 2 and record all the stereo tracks I want to in Audacity.  Cool.

Overall, I've been very happy with this computer so far.  Most things work great out of the box, and the things I couldn't fix are things that I wouldn't worry about every day and/or things that have easy workarounds.  

If anyone is interested in a mid-priced laptop capable of running Ubuntu/Kubuntu/Xubuntu and doesn't mind using a USB mouse and an external mic, you can't go wrong with the HP DV6911US.  

**** out of ***** stars.
