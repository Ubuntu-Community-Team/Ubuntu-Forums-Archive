This is why I suggested looking at dedicated review sites where empirical testing is done and the results are recorded.

You will not find laboratory results here.  You will find opinion and anecdote.  Brand loyalty plays into each of those, which heavily skews them.

If you go to sites providing empirical data, you will find that the big Noctua coolers perform just as well as the All-In-One liquid coolers.  But whether the cost of either compared to a more budget oriented product is in your best interest for your particular needs requires some due diligence on your part.  Custom loops work marginally better due to the fact that people often include huge radiators to dissipate the heat.  But those custom loops can be prohibitively expensive and in a pure cost/benefit comparison they lose badly.  Spending $1000US and more to gain 3°C is useful in very few cases.  Air or water, the ability to cool a system comes down finally to the heat transfer from a radiator to the ambient air.  Water may provide a longer time to peak temperature because of water's high specific heat, but that specific heat also means that it is reluctant to allow the heat energy to be dissipated through a radiator.  Hence the large radiators.  Air coolers peak quickly and cool quickly when the load is reduced.  Water loops peak slowly, but also cool slowly.  This can lead to the misconception that water is better because testing often is not carried on for a sufficient length of time.

The physics of heat transfer is well known.  What you want is hard numbers that are appropriate for your use case.  

You may have heard that water coolers make less noise. 
That is not borne out by experimental findings.  The AIOs are typically very noisy compared to large air coolers with similar heat transfer profiles.  You need numbers for analysis.  That is where the review sites excel.
