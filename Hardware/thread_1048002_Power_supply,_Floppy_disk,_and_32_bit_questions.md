### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2009-01-23
> **socomhacker239 said:**
> OK well i have seen post with people saying they have 300 and 400 watt power supply. Well how do you know if your set up needs a larger power supply? 

I don't have a formula, but for a basic setup (1-2 hdd's, normal or onboard video card, older system) 400 watts is plenty.  You start adding more drives or a video card with a whopping great fan then you may need more.
> 
Will it lag if the power is not efficient enough? 

IME an underpowered computer will reboot at random, lock up, or randomly "lose" devices like drives.  It's not a speed thing.
> 
Also i have 
disconnected the floppy disk from the power supply so the rest gets more power, I do not use it at all the computer came out around 2003-2005 i think so my question is can i put a built in card reader where the floppy disk goes? 

You'll have to look at the motherboard, usually the ones I've seen attach to the auxiliary USB connectors on the motherboard (looks like a row of 5 pins next to a row of 4 pins, should be labled USB).  If you have one of those on the MB, you're good AFAIK, but you may want to get a second opinion on that.
> 
Finally i have seen people talking about 32 bit and 64 bit what does that mean, What is the difference between the 2 and how can you tell which one you have?

To grossly oversimplify, it refers to the size of a processor's memory address space.  The way to tell is to find out what your processor model is and either look it up or ask around.

### Post by logos34 on 2009-01-23
General rule: the total power draw (watts) of all your components should not exceed 70% of the rating of your power supply. Good power supplies have a better effiency rating (%) than others...The higher the percentage you run at, the hotter and louder the power unit (which translates into shorter life). Things like dual-rail are also desirable, insofar as they divide up the (+12V) load more efficiently.

Don't worry about the floppy.  Yes you can put a card reader in the 3.5 in bay but it has a USB connector

EDIT: to add to what lykwydchykyn said, there's a sticky on 64- vs 32-bit you should read over in the x86_64 forum.  The big advange is it can handle more than 4 GB RAM, and the use of larger 128-bit memory registers enable faster number crunching
