### Post by ahallubuntu on 2012-08-04
I've replaced several laptop screens.  Looks like if you live in the US, you can get a used LCD panel off eBay for under $50 USD shipped for your HP.  New screens cost only a little more.  You can decide if it's worth that cost for you to replace the screen.

FYI, laptop screens are NOT very generic - you have to get a compatible match for your laptop.  Not any 14.1" screen will work even if it will physically fit.  A Dell screen may look exactly like an HP screen, both may be made by Samsung with the same connectors but with one different part number difference and that means it may not work at all when you plug it in (or only half brightness or something).

You don't have to get exactly the same screen as a replacement, just a compatible one.  Your original may be a Samsung; you may replace it with an LG that is compatible.  HP may have used compatible screens from several manufacturers in making your laptop.  If you buy from one of the big eBay laptop screen vendors, just make sure you are buying a screen that lists your exact model laptop or ask them if you aren't sure.

Still, you are taking a chance that the screen is the real problem.    It could also be the video card.  Have you tried connecting the laptop to an external monitor?  If you see no flickering there, chances are the video card is still good.

Replacing the screen varies by laptop.  In general, you need to remove the front bezel around the LCD screen.  Sometimes there are rubber bumpers glued over the screws that you have to pull off.  Remove those screws, then you have to crack the front bezel off.  That can seem a bit scary the first time you do it; you have to peel the bezel apart gently to avoid breaking it.  Try a flat screwdriver and try to pull it apart a piece at a time.  Even a guitar pick might work better.  I have cracked at least one bezel doing this (salvage laptop, I was trying to remove a screen not replace one, no loss in my case).  At worst you'll have to glue a broken bezel back together or buy a new bezel on eBay, but out of the 10+ screens I've removed, I've only ever cracked one bezel.

Once you have the bezel off, there are more screws holding the actual LCD panel in, probably on a mounting bracket (again, this varies by the model).  Once you finally get the screen loose, there are two cables attached: a two-pin inverter cable (for older CCFL screens like yours) and a flat ribbon cable attached to the back of the screen.  Getting this connector loose can be tricky; sometimes it's easy, sometimes you risk breaking it.  

When you swap in the replacement screen, you need to secure the flat data cable carefully and tape it to the back of the screen well so it will stay on even if there's some flex from opening/closing the screen later.  Occasionally the screen you buy won't be an exact fit - maybe the inverter won't screw in securely or something but generally you can just tape it in there and  it will be fine.

Lots of YouTube videos demonstrating LCD screen replacement - watch  a few of them.

### Post by trundlenut on 2012-08-05
you have a number of potential sources of the problem.  There is the lcd panel itself, the video card, the connecting wiring and the inverter and backlight.

Does tilting the screen make a difference?  Try also twisting it a bit and see what happens.

I had an older HP Compaq laptop which had a similar problem which was not an uncommon problem and was a problem with the lcd itself.  A first step may be to try changing the cable which connects the screen to the graphics card, you should be able to pick one up from ebay fairly cheap.

But you need to make sure the part number is an exact match.  If you do need to change the lcd then if you dismantle the laptop you should be able to find the part number and may be able to find out who manufactured it.
