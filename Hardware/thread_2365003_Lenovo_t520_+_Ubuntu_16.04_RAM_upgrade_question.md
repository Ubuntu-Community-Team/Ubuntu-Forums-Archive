Wanting as much RAM as possible is "extremely unusual" ? o.O

I don't know what convention brought that assumption to you..  Any 'light' combination of my hyper/paraVMs can easily take from 512MB - ~6 GB above Dom0.

Maxing out 8GB isn't even that unusual outside of VM existance (my wife sometimes maxes 8GB just by caching/buffering HD video for her streaming playlists).

In the age of N*AND storage, gaming, streaming, multiple VMs, etc., the more RAM the better!  Notwithstanding, loading into registers is *much* faster from RAM than loading suspended disk cache.  8 GB is easy to max; further consider that any number of tmpfs can hog a huge amount of RAM real-estate (i.e., mounting /var/tmp and other dirs to save your SSD from a 'thrashing' death). Consider downloading large 7-8GB .iso on top of streaming, gaming, etc., and your non-contiguous RAM is chopped up smaller than the pages needed for some inadequate dependency to write flush to those tmpfs before copy to N*AND storage, but then procs deadlock.... I digress, but you get the point, I hope.

Desiring twice as much RAM isn't ludicrous, but pragmatic, depending on your usability.

@OP:   If your hardware's firmware is compatible for larger RAM capacity of its type, try it out.  GNU/Linux just 'sees' the new address range when booting up.  If you're concerned about a potential financial loss, see if the seller allows returns or sell it off yourself; if you don't want that risk, then strictly stick with the manufacturer's published specs.

Upgrading your RAM has nothing to do with Ubuntu. RAM is just a physical memory chip. If you buy a new chip, you disassemble your laptop, plug in the RAM chip in a free RAM slot if available, or swap it with the existing RAM chip, reassemble the computer, and then turn back on the computer, that's it. The operating system will immediately detect more RAM available.

Your computer looks fine, it doesn't seem to need anything else. The only thing I noticed is it does not have a discrete graphics card, it only has the Intel graphics controller. Higher end Lenovo computers typically include the Intel card, and a discrete Nvidia card.

For any sort of hardware upgrades you should consult the Lenovo manuals, which are quite good, with 100 pages of pictures indicating how to disassemble the parts. But beware, depending on the computer, many components are soldered to the motherboard, and not intended to be replaced, including critical parts such as CPU, GPU (graphics card Nvidia), and even the RAM memory. Some things that could be upgraded are optical units (DVD) if any, hard disk drive, and wireless card. So, if your computer can upgrade something, it's probably only worth adding more RAM, and a bigger solid state drive (SSD), but other than that, I wouldn't do much to it.
