### Post by TheFu on 2016-12-17
Fast, volatile memory. Not long term memory.  
[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random-access_memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random-access_memory) explains RAM.

Why DDR2?  Because the RAM makers needed to force us to buy something new and make it a tiny bit faster than regular DDR.  DDR3 and DDR4 are similar.  Plus, they change the physical form to force using newer versions and to keep boneheads like me from putting incompatible RAM into the slots the wrong way.  In the old days, cables, RAM, CPUs were not "keyed" to force correct installation.  Putting RAM in backwards was possible and didn't work.  When I was really, really new to computers, I put in a CPU backwards and fried it. "Keying" prevents that without lots and lots of force to break something.
[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DDR2_SDRAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DDR2_SDRAM) ... might answer any specific questions about DDR2. There's a section about the 'notch' and not being backwards compatible.
