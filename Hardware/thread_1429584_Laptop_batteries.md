### Post by GregBrannon on 2010-03-14
There are many good resources on the web for taking good care of your battery - most of them even agree.  Here's an excerpt from one:

> Just like couch potatoes, batteries need exercise. The chemicals in Lithium-Ion batteries respond best to regular recharging. So if you have a laptop, don’t keep it plugged in all the time; go ahead and let it drain to about 40 or 50 percent of capacity, and then recharge your computer.

The life of a Lithium-Ion battery can be measured in charge cycles. A charge cycle occurs when 100% of a battery’s capacity is used. Let’s say you use 50% of your laptop’s battery one day, charge it overnight, and then you use 50% of the battery again the next day. Even after charging it back up again, you’ll have only had one charge cycle occur. Most laptop batteries are rated for a useful life of at least 300-500 charge cycles, but high-quality, properly maintained batteries can retain up to 80% of their original life, even after 300 cycles.

Credit: [http://blogs.techrepublic.com.com/helpdesk/?p=124]("http://blogs.techrepublic.com.com/helpdesk/?p=124")

### Post by sgosnell on 2010-03-14
Never discharge the battery completely if you can help it.  If you want to store it, let it discharge to ~50-70% and let it sit like that.  It will eventually discharge to that area anyway.  The real reason leaving it connected and charging kills the battery is heat.  LiIon batteries don't like heat, and the heat from the laptop will eventually degrade them.  Just remove the battery and let it sit when you can, then recharge it before you need to use it on battery, and recharge it periodically if you don't use it, perhaps every couple of weeks or so.

### Post by parn on 2010-05-18
Another thing about Li Ion battery is that it will start to die the moment it is assembled; the manufacturing date. No matter how well you care for the battery, it will die after a few years. The charge it can hold deteriorates through time.

My experience with my 2 notebooks, 1 of them I always take the battery out and charge it once every 2 weeks. The other, I just leave the battery in while connected to the AC all the time. Both batteries last about 2 hours initially and 2+ years later, the battery that I always take out can last for about 1 hrs 30 min while the other is slightly lower; about 1 hrs 15 mins - personally I do not see much difference.

** I never really tested it to the limit because I never let the batteries run lower than 35-40%. The time given above are estimated based on the time I actually use up 60-65% of the battery charge.

Both are Acer TM 8000 series notebooks.

All the tips posted above are valid as well. Just wanted to point out that eventually the Li Ion battery will die based on its manufacturing date regardless of best user effort to preserve it.

### Post by sgosnell on 2010-06-04
Actually, most sources will tell you to store the batteries with ~50-60% charge, not fully charged.  IMO the best way to care for batteries is to protect them from heat, and the second is to limit charge cycles.  Limiting charge cycles doesn't mean never charging, it means never letting them discharge fully.  Putting batteries in storage is a waste of money, IMO.  If you don't need a battery, don't buy one, and buy a new one when necessary.  Having one spare can be convenient if you run on battery power a lot, but having more should be necessary only in rare circumstances.

No, I don't agree with that.  Lithium Ion batteries, which are used in laptops and almost everything else these days, are not supposed to ever be completely discharged.  Doing so will kill them.  NiCd batteries do need to be discharged completely to prevent memory set, but those batteries aren't sold as laptop batteries, and you seldom see them used at all these days.
