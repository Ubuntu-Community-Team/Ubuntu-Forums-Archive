Hi there,
In some tracks I am hearing sight distortion (crackling, as though volume is too high), usually when the music becomes 'busier'. I have alot of lossless classical music and the distortion is quite apparent when listening to orchestral music. It is slight, but still there.

I have been pondering buying a new sound card for a while, and have just bought myself a shiny new pair of [Sennheiser HD558's]("http://sennheiser.com/audio-headphones-stereo-hifi-hd-558") (a cheeky Christmas present), and I want to try and figure out which part of my setup is causing the distortion. I cannot say whether or not it was there before I bought the headphones because I have only just transferred my collection to my pc.

As I understand it, this kind of thing could be caused by my source (All FLAC CD rips), my sound hardware, which is currently the onboard audio of my [ASUS Z-87 Pro]("http://www.asus.com/uk/Motherboards/Z87PRO/specifications/") (112 snr according to asus website), or my headphones. My guess is that the weak link is the onboard audio and the distortion is caused by internal interference in the case because the audio circuits are not shielded. 

In any case, how can I pin down the source of the distortion, without doing the obvious testing each part of the setup under different circumstances (which would be quite time-consuming for me).

### Post by tgalati4 on 2014-12-28
Yes, that is the dilemma with the audiophile.  When you have very accurate transponders:  speakers and headphones, it is possible that your source music is crappy.  Subtle distortion in the original recordings (or FLAC rips) are now audible and become extremely annoying.  Put a 6 Hz-capable subwoofer in a client's house and then they complain about the delivery van's idling outside--or on the recording.

I use an oscilloscope and probe each part of the signal chain to find the distortion using simple sine wave test tones.  The oscope shows the distortion before it becomes audible.  Otherwise, you will need to come up with another way to probe the signal chain.

Although it could be the internal sound card (due to lack of shielding), you may find an external, high-fidelity, USB sound card will produce the same distortion.  That would indicate that your source music has the distortion and you couldn't hear it before.

Generate some pure sine tones (1 or 2-minute tracks) using *audacity* at different frequencies (80, 160, 320, 640, etc up to 4K or 8K or higher if you can hear it).  A pure tone will "buzz" if it has distortion.  Again it is easier to see on an oscope.  If the pure tones sound clean, then perhaps your recordings or FLAC rips have the distortion embedded in them.

Get better music.
