### Post by MountainX on 2010-05-22
OK, I got the iMac. Went for the i5 afterall. It is nice. The screen is as nice as I thought, so I'm happy with the purchase and I was able to start the work I needed to do on a Mac. So all is good. 

However, OS X doesn't strike me as fundamentally any better than Ubuntu or openSUSE. In fact, my overwhelming impression is how similar they are. 

And speaking of the Magic Mouse, I prefer my existing mouse. (I use a Contour Rollermouse.) But the Magic Mouse is cool.

I don't think it would be that hard to build a Linux box that would exceed the iMac and OS X in most ways. But for an out-of-the-box experience, the iMac is very impressive. (Maybe a machine from System76 could be close in that regard... I don't know.)

Besides the initial experience, I guess the areas where Linux trails Apple -- and that we can't really do much about -- are commercial applications (such as Adobe Creative Suite, etc.) and "things just working". I haven't used the Mac long enough to know yet, but I suspect I won't have to deal with all the "papercuts" and other small annoyances that are typically part of using Linux. 

Again, maybe this isn't the best example, but sleep & hibernation don't work correctly on any of my Linux boxes (laptop or desktop). I could make a long list of things that I have never been able to get to work under Linux. Mostly they aren't that important to me. 

The few issues that are important can be solved easily by buying different hardware. (For example, USB 2.0 doesn't work on my desktop machine -- #88746 "ehci_hcd module causes I/O errors in USB 2.0 devices" -- but after 3 years I need a new motherboard anyway and I think that will resolve the issue.) 

But I have invested a ton of time and effort into resolving various issues on my Linux boxes and some of them cannot be easily resolved. Will I run into similar things with the Mac? I'm guessing there will be far fewer encounters of this type. But I think Linux could easily get to the same level as OS X with more "100 Papercut"-type projects and wise hardware selection (or using vendors like System76, I guess).

Definitely, my next machine will be a Linux box. I will run either Ubuntu or openSUSE and KDE. I'm going to use a SSD and see if I can make the machine dead silent. I'll use the best fanless graphics card I can find that will drive two very large monitors. And my goal will be to build something that the iMac can't touch. And since I have an iMac now, I can make that judgment for myself. ;) This will be a fun project and I am sure I can build a machine that gives me a better user experience than the iMac. (Anyone want to point me in the direction of the most up-to-date cutting edge Linux hardware compatibility list? How about any collections of recent systems people have built, e.g., including SSD?)

However, I also admit that the iMac met my expectations for being a really good value (the screen alone is worth the price) and for things just working out of the box. No big complaints at all. (Minor complaint that the designers at Apple seem to trump those who are in charge of usability - e.g., all ports are on back of monitor and very hard to reach.)

My conclusion is that the new iMacs represent a very good bargain. They are extremely high quality hardware, well engineered, and elegant. They also perform well. In day to day use, the iMac holds its own against the more expensive custom PC I built.

I have had a very, very difficult time installing Ubuntu on my iMac. And there are several things about OS X that I do not like. So my Ubuntu PC will be my main computer. 

But if I were starting from zero, I would buy a slightly different model iMac (one step up), add more RAM and an SSD and install Ubuntu on it. For my goals, it is hard to beat that combination.

Certainly my little experiment proves that the notion that iMacs are overpriced is an old false myth. (On the other hand, Apple accessories are overpriced. And I think the iMac has a much lower margin than other Apple products, so I don't suggest all Apple products are as good a buy as the iMac.)
