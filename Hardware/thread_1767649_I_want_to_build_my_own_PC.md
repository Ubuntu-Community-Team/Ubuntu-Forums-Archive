### Post by Gye50 on 2011-05-26
Having built and rebuilt a few PCs, but never tried to run 2 OSs off the same HD, think your old HP is due for the scrap gold pile.  Looked up the model on the net and it reminded me of a Dell Dimension i used to have--could salvage the IDE hard drive to use as a secondary drive but that's about all.  Everything else is too old to work with a new build.  If the HP cases are like Dell's then the wire connections are too proprietary to work with the ATX standard form motherboard sold in kits and the power supply probably doesn't have the watts or power control features for what you want.  The DDR memory isn't worth keeping and for $70-$120 you can buy a motherboard that runs DDR3 memory.  Maybe the CDROM is a keeper but it probably requires an IDE interface which may not be available on the motherboard you go with.  Anyway check the component prices out on NewEgg because sometimes they beat out Tiger.  Also you might check out the Intel Atom PCs depending on the intensity of the gaming you want.  Whatever you do, be sure to research it thoroughly and be careful installing the heatsink on the CPU chip--a delicate procedure.

### Post by vanquishedangel on 2011-05-26
Well hello there :)

First off I build alot of my own pc's, and I dont mean by calling tiger direct or dell and have them do it.  So it depends on what you are looking for.  The kits may offer a good rate for cheaper but I have found them to usually be lacking.  What I do to be thrifty is I look around for close out, discount, or refurbished computers and then search for upgrade and specs on them and make a decision off that.  Like my home computer is and HP dc7100. It came with 3.0 ghz processor ht, and 256 mb ram, intel 915 graphics.  I bought it for $120 and then upgraded it to a hp dc 7100 3.8 ghz ht($50 and 64 bit which the other one was not) and 4 gigs ram ($80) and an ATI Radeon HD 4350 ($20). Then I sold the replaced parts online, so the total I spent was actually like $200. (if you choose to do it this way make sure you have the system specs and part number as well as what type of socket the processor has etc, hp usually has specs on line or you can google anything)

As for the old computer I would sell it or keep it, if it is a lappy I would keep it as a back up, my computer for school is a Compaq armada m33 600mhz with 190 mb ram and an ATI rage pro,  I use it to start my assignments, it runs Lubuntu quite well with libreoffice.

As a tip: High density ram sucks and is slower than low density ram, that is why high density is cheaper, also high density ram is less compatible.  So always go for low density ram.

### Post by walt.smith1960 on 2011-05-26
Well, to be contrary, I've had quite good success with Gigabyte and AMD :cool:.  The best value is often times not the bleeding edge but one generation back.  Building a desktop PC today is pretty much plug the parts together.  It also depends on what you want your new machine to do.  The requirements for a machine intended for video editing or 3 D graphics rendering would be more $$ than one intended for mundane office-type tasks and web surfing. I agree with get a good power supply-without enough clean stable power nothing else is going to function optimally.  

Personal bias- motherboards I've had good luck with Gigabyte as mentioned or Asus.  I was working with a friend's box that had a Biostar board and Intel processor that didn't seem to perform to its specs.  I don't know why.  Discrete Nvidia graphics card & disable internal graphics.  I've heard that Crucial RAM quality control is better than average and price is competitive so that's my first choice.  I had a Phillips DVD RW that I salvaged from the old system.  I had one IDE port and the only significant upgrade would have been to BluRay which I didn't feel the need for.  The only thing I didn't get and might do differently is to get a motherboard with more than 1 PCIe slot in addition to the PCIe16 graphics adapter slot.  I was able to get a MB which had 4 DRAM slots. I can add DRAM later without removing what I already paid for.
