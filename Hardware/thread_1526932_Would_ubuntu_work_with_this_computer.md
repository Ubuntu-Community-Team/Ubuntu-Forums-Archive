### Post by Paddy Landau on 2010-07-09
> **blueysak said:**
> sorry but im a bit confused on the swap drive i am not really all that techy and if you dont swap drive would it make it worse?
Indeed, it is recommended to have a swap partition.

For a small amount of RAM (under 1Gb), the general consensus is a swap partition double the size of your RAM, or more (personally I would go for 1Gb at a minimum).

For a large amount of RAM (1Gb or more), the general consensus is a swap partition equal to the size of your RAM (no smaller, otherwise you cannot hibernate).

Not everyone will agree with those figures. However, you can't go wrong with a large swap if you have the space on your hard disk.

### Post by AltomineUK on 2010-07-09
Swap Partition is there for your comfort.
Amongst other purposes, it ensures your computer can keep on running if it ever runs out of RAM memory. 
Your computer will generally set aside a portion of your hard disk drive for this purpose, when you first install Ubuntu (or any distro). As a rule of thumb, the size of this partition is roughly twice or more the size of your RAM. You will not see this SWAP Partition on your normal Filesystem.

You don't really need to worry about this as the computer handles it completely, although there are ways to alter this.

### Post by AltomineUK on 2010-07-09
Ok...i'll put it straight.....

As you will know your computer has something called RAM in it. It stands for Random Access Memory. As the name suggests your computer "randomly" accesses this and write/read temporary data  to/from your RAM when it's running programs. We will not go into the details of what  this "data" is, but let's just say it's important for the smooth running of programs (i.e. web-browser, word-processor, photo-editor etc.) 

Now, this RAM is limited in size. For example, your PC has only got 512MB of it. Mine has 1GB of it. So people like you and me have a very tiny risk of running out of space on our RAM if we run too many programs. If we do?.... well let's just say it's not a good thing.

That's when SWAP partition comes to the rescue!!! By reserving a chunk of memory from your hard drive (1GB, 2GB, 3GB...how much ever it needs. The chunk is called the SWAP partition), the SWAP will help us avoid the danger of running out of RAM. So if our original RAM gets full, the computer will simply switch to the SWAP and start using that..... and don't worry about how much to reserve as the computer takes care of that on its own.

This might slow the computer down slightly as it is now using the Hard Drive, but hey.... we got space for our RAM. This is one of many purposes of SWAP. 

Put simply if your Hard Drive has 160GB and the computer decides to reserve 4GB of that for SWAP-ing.....the total Hard Drive your are left with is 156GB, which Ubuntu can use to store the file system.

As for your question about where this SWAP is partitioned, it is (by default) done in the hard drive or partition onto which YOU choose to install Ubuntu 10.04 OS. So where are you installing Ubuntu......in your computer's hard drive or the external drive?

Hope this helps :D
