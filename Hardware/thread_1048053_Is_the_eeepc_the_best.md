### Post by Therion on 2009-01-24
I've had my eee 1000 for about a week now and I think it's great for what it is.  It's not a full-size notebook and it's sure not a full-on tower PC.  That being said, I replaced the Xandros OS with eeebuntu (Intrepid Ibex-based) and for an extra $20 or so popped in a 2GB RAM stick.  

So far my eee 1000 is rocking my world.  Everything works right out of the box, there's a good forum for eeebuntu and I'm loving being mobile for five or six hours on a single battery charge.  The keyboard is small but it's about 95% of a regular full sized KB so if you can type it's not a huge learning curve.  I, like most people, find the right-shift key a little annoying but that's a trifle. Still, if you're looking for one, there's a nit you can pick.

For light duty tasks like surfing the 'net, checking your mail and such, the portability is awesome.  Oh, and it's damn near dead silent too.  You can hear the fan kick in every so often but your really have to listen for it.  I was surprised to see this little guy running Compiz too...  The desktop cube, open and close animations, pretty much the whole nine yards.

Keep your expectations within reason and I think you'll find they're awesome little machines.

### Post by blackened on 2009-01-24
> **WatchingThePain said:**
> Thats right there are different models and the one with a larger solid state disc (40GB) was £350 which of course is about the price of any of the cheaper laptops. You can plug in an external hard drive with the smaller HD model. The EEEpc for about £150 had a 4GB hard drive which holds the os (xandross Linux was installed) and not much else. Having said that, the speed of solid state discs is supposed to very noticable, that is they are fast...

There are two different types of solid state drives being used in netbooks: SLC (single layer cell) are generally faster than MLC (multi-layer cell). Asus has used both (sometimes both in the same machine, as I mentioned in an earlier post). That said, even the stock SLC drives are much slower than others on the market, and don't come close when compared to standard mechanical drives, though they are tremendously more shock resistant.

> The processors vary as well, of the more pricey models some have intel atom processor.

If you compare the benchmarks, the 900Mhz Celeron is cheaper and faster than the 1.6Ghz Atoms that are currently in use, though the Atom is designed to use much less power.

> **binbash said:**
> samsung or msi wind book is better than eeepc

I don't know how you can qualify this. Considering how many models (that cover the entire price spectrum) of EEE are out there, the Samsungs and MSIs are certainly going to be better than some of them, and comparable to others. The difference is that with the EEEs you can better choose the machine that fits both your computing requirements and your budget.
