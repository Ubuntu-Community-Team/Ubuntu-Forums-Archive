### Post by ian-weisser on 2015-12-01
Well, there's compatibilty and then there's compatibility.
Lots of garbage hardware includes 'Linux support', but that doesn't make it useful. 

Are products that require unskilled users to recompile the kernel considered compatible?
Are products that require additional unpackaged applications compatible?
Are products that have only partial functionality considered compatible?

What's 'Official support'?
Does that mean accepting the manufacturer's claim?
Or a functioning, maintained contribution to the kernel tree? 
Or some third-party testing or verification?

Canonical has attempted to navigate this minefield already. See [http://www.ubuntu.com/certification/](http://www.ubuntu.com/certification/)

### Post by kurt18947 on 2015-12-01
ian-weisser has it right. When I shop for new hardware I go to a prospective manufacturer/vendor's web site support section and look for linux support. If they don't mention Ubuntu I at least like to see Debian mentioned. If the web site says in effect "what is this ..... linux .... you speak of?" that manufacturer/vendor goes to the bottom of my list. For peripherals someone already owns, I've been surprised that oftentimes I plug something in and it just works. The other (obvious) thing to do is google 'device name'+linux. The hardware support situation is much better than it was 8-10 years ago.
