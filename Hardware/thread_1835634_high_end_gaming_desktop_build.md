### Post by gpost3 on 2011-08-31
I would avoid 2 or 3 or any SLi/Crossfire configuration personally altogether atleast for next two years to come. I would get the Geforce GTX 580 it will easily do 1080p gaming.

Don't bother with expensive RAM (I would personally pick the cheapest one on sale from any reputable company). Mind you there are only a few vendors who actually manufacture the RAM chips (Elpida, Micron, Samsung, Hynix) and the rest of the company simply rebadge them. 

I noticed 0 speedup when going from single channel to dual channel on my Core 2 Duo because the shared cache architecture and write-back algorithm is known to be very effective. I suspect even lesser performance gain on i5 due to the integrated memory controller and even bigger cache.

I would even go as far as saying don't even bother with overclocking because that i5 turbos to 3.7 Ghz. You can stretch it to 4.0 or beyond and get minimal speed up but jeopardizing system stability and exponentially increasing heat output not worth it IMHO. 

And as for the socket - you don't care.

### Post by gpost3 on 2011-08-31
> @gpost3 does micro stuttering is such an issue also with new gen cards? I  was planning to go single card and then upgrade to double card in one  year or so if need to improve graphic power.Yes it is an issue with newer cards as well. The thing is though that the issue is less to do with the card and more to do with the refresh rate of the LCD and drivers themselves. The source of the problem is that work load is not "evenly" distributed between two cards and in order to distribute the work load as evenly as possible requires driver optimization. Micro stuttering is very much with us today since the launch of SLi/Crossfire and while nvidia has improved its drivers "somewhat" - most users agree that it still ruins the gaming experience. Get the GTX 580 and skip on Multi GPU altogether - you will thank me later.
