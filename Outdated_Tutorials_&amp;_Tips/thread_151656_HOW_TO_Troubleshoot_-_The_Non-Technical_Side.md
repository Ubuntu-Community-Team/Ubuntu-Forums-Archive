### Post by YuHoo on 2006-03-28
To begin with, this is not a technical HOWTO. This HOWTO is a psychological one, which is as important when approaching a problem as other factors. Not small problems, I mean the medium to big that you look at and don’t know what to do. In my time of being a computer user and administering my small network, I have probably spent around 50% of my time fixing someone’s computer (amazingly this computer is usually mine). I found that psychological and simple preparation allows me to come at problems with a lot less frustration that I use to when I would wing it every time and rely on my instincts/knowledge. Of all the following 4 steps the last two are the ones that I find I need to do every single time. The first two are good for the overwhelming problem, but do not need to be followed every time. Everything that I recommend I found to help from frustrated troubleshooting, feel free to add to correct my errors. Now, before I go any further there is a very simple phrase from Douglas Adams that sums up how to troubleshoot before trouble shooting: Don’t Panic.

The first thing you ought to do is assess the time constraints. Look at the problem, poke around forums and google for 5 minutes or so. Don’t lie to yourself, estimate how long it may take for you to work on it. Usually this is off by a margin of 50%, but that doesn’t matter. If you’re looking at it and you have an appointment, it may be better to set aside some time later to deal with it. If you’re crunched for time, as I always am, don’t put it off to the last second like you would a term paper. This does not mean you have to do it now but it does mean that you need to prioritize and perhaps clear up your schedule. So the one sentence summary is: make it so you have the adequate time to fix the problem as soon as possible.

The second thing that you ought to do is relax. Many different ways are available to you. Increase the room temperature to 72 degrees, turn off the TV and listen to music, wear comfy socks… anything that reduces the stressors on your mind an body. Be aware of the lighting as well. A dark room with only your monitor can sometimes be highly stressful to your eyes. Once your eyes start to go be careful to take long blinks. Usually they are dried from not blinking often enough and the muscles are also tired from fine repetitive movements (carpal tunnel for your eyes). In addition to these environmental things you should look to your own physical needs. Comfort food is always nice for the short term annoyance, but be prepared for the long term. Make a sandwich, eat some soup, get some carbs and protein in you. Many a time I have made small errors because I was hungry. Tech support asks if your computer is plugged in because stupid things happen and are usually the cause. Don’t let stupidity happen to you (though it is impossible to avoid).

At this stage of troubleshooting you should have nothing done in respect to fixing the problem other than being relaxed and not having constraints. This is ok. As I said above, stupid errors happen, when you are relaxed and calm you do not make them. Now I come to the last thing that I have found incredibly useful for fixing a computer, a diary. Not the flowery secret coded one you have under your mattress, but a notebook recording what you’ve done. After the 109th step you’ve done that’s failed, you start to forget what you’ve tried and not tried. The records don’t have to be perfect, but a record of errors and brief summarizations of what you did. Later this will come in as very handy. As a community it also helps to recall what you did to solve a problem and what errors you encountered. Recently I installed my wifi and did not do this and currently I’m kicking myself for not doing this, this one time since I can not remember what I did. Cutting corners will always come back to get you at some time. 

Finally, don’t panic and be patient. Frustration often plagues you when it drags on and on without success. This happens to everyone so don’t worry. If you start to be overwhelmed by it, walk away. Let your mind clear for a bit, but DO NOT start something else. Grab some coffee from the kitchen or walk to dog around the block. Do something that will finish in the next 5 minutes. By following the first two steps you minimize the current level of frustration, allowing you to go longer, and the 3rd step minimizes future frustration, and finally patience allows you to avoid silly mistakes. All of these may seem silly or basic, but the silly and basic are also what usually plague the systems that hassle us. When we troubleshoot, we also access deep core components at times, this is not something you want to do when you are liable to make small mistakes. Those are the ones you can't find later and will cost you much more time. 

The true final point that is needed is humility. Never approach a problem thinking that you can solve it all by yourself. You may be able to, usually this is true. But pride will screw around with you and will prolong the problem. Accept you previous failures and grow from them and do not hesitate to call for aid. No matter how well versed you are in a system, someone will know more, and the longer you wait only prolongs your agony of solving the problem.
