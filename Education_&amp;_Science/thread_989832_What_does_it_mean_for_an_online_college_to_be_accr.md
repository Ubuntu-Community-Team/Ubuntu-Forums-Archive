One of the big differences between brick and mortar schools, and online universities, is how they quantify classroom participation. Brick and mortar classes are easy to judge who's there, and who isn't. Not as easy with an online classroom environment. So, the answer is to require significant post counts for classroom participation.

If you decide to go the online route, limber up your fingers. Having written an MBA online, I can tell you, that you'll probably have well more than 50,000 posts before you graduate. That includes classroom participation, group work, research, and general conversation with other students and staff.

All-in-all however, the online experience is very satisfying, quite unique, and yes, you can attend class "au natural" if you'd like.

### Post by Charles_bright on 2008-11-24
It means that the college is recognized by the accrediting agencies that also accredit traditional colleges, in the sense that it offers quality education that is at par with regular college. It also helps incase you want to transfer credits from one college to another, you can't do this if the college is not accredited. Also incase you'd want to apply for financial aid or other grants, this is only possible through an accredited college. Most employers also don't recognize degrees acquired from colleges that don't have legitimate accreditation.

You can get more information about why college accreditation is important to your education from: [http://www.distance-learning-college-guide.com/](http://www.distance-learning-college-guide.com/)

### Post by OldDirtyTurtle on 2008-11-25
I'd recommend starting with the end in mind when thinking about online college classes.  What is your ultimate goal?  Answer this first.  If it is a job, call a few Human Resources folks at agencies you might like to work for.  Ask them how they view online education at hiring.  If it is favorable, ask what colleges/universities they value.  Then look into those.

I can tell you that all accredited online colleges are not the same and aren't viewed the same in the job market for  hiring purposes.  My wife's department recently went through a big hiring spree.  They took all the application packets and sorted them by educational background.  All those that contained an online degree as the highest qualification were tossed in the trash.

I'm not saying that online degrees aren't valuable - just be careful.
