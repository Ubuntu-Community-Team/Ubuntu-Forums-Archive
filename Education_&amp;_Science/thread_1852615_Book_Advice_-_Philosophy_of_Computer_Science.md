### Post by matthew.ball on 2011-10-08
As F.G. suggested, you'll want to look into some metaphysics for sure - ontology and modality in particular - Russell's bundle theory provides a metaphysical basis for object-oriented programming, while work in modality lead to the idea we could capture temporal notions in a formal language (this, in turn, lead to model-checking and software verification). I could recommend some more if you're interested, but that's good for now.

As F.G. also previously mentioned, Russell's "Problems of Philosophy" is a short, and very readable book (I disagree with his opinion on Wittgenstein though, while he writes fairly cleanly, it's difficult to actually extract his key thesis from the writings, I found) - it's more general philosophy, but it will give a really good picture of how to look at things. It also displays Russell's staunch analytic approach, which is just inspiring.

However, a philosophy of computer science (not to be confused with a philosophy of information technology or whatever ikt was talking about) is going to be treated just as philosophy of mathematics is - i.e. foundations. So, in that sense, I would recommend starting with some foundations of mathematics - at the very least, become familiar with Hilbert systems for logic, natural deduction systems for logic (and the more general notion of a sequent calculus), semantic tableaux for logic, set theory and mathematical models for computation (Turing machines, lambda calculus, Post-machines, recursive functions, etc). I would also recommend, though it's probably not essential, to look into some non-classical logics, for instance, intuitionistic logic (or its parent linear logic, or the completely general mu-calculus).

In this sense, to start from the foundations of mathematics, I would recommend Russell's "Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy" - he gives the definition for the bundle theory I mentioned earlier, and introduces a Hilbert system for classical logic. As with Russell's "Problems..." it's very well written and easy to follow. The only thing Russell *doesn't* talk about here, which would be relevant, is computation. I have some stuff I could send you if you make it through that 300-page beast though (it should be available free online, just search for "Betrand Russell - Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy" or something similar).

One of the few books which really tackles the ideas in a philosophy of computer science would be [Godel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid]("http://www.amazon.com/G%C3%B6del-Escher-Bach-Eternal-Golden/dp/0465026567") by Douglas Hofstadter - this is an incredibly dense book, but it is very brilliant. There's a brief series of lectures on this book (offered by MIT's OpenCourseWare programme) available [here]("http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5jFhq3Rj6DI").

If you have any questions, either post a reply here or send me a private message. I will gladly offer you some help :)
