### Post by ctdahle on 2010-03-01
Generally 8th Grade is "Pre-Algebra", but you should already be able to do simple algebraic tasks such as factoring monomials and solving linear equations by the end of 7th grade. Advanced students should be doing Algebra 1 in 8th grade, Geometry in 9th, Algebra 2 in 10th, and starting Calculus as a Junior. If possible, you want to take an "advanced class", meaning that you are in a class with other highly motivated students who are your age, rather than taking a "regular" class that is a grade level ahead of you.

A motivated student with strong number sense and willingness to work at it diligently ought to be able knock out a year's worth of curriculum in any of these topics in 10 or 12 weeks, but you have to stay on it. Take the advice of a good teacher if you have one, but don't despair if you don't have one. Isaac Newton was just a kid when he INVENTED Calculus, and you CAN learn this on your own. You will do better if you join a study group with other motivated students and challenge each other to greater effort. 3-5 hours daily would not be too much time to spend. Check out a couple of Algebra books from your public library and work through a couple of sets of exercises in each one, then buy the one you are most comfortable with and dive in. Take lots of notes and practice describing your solutions in words.

for study advice see [http://www.purplemath.com/](http://www.purplemath.com/)

### Post by epigram on 2010-03-02
I graduated HS in 1996.  Generally most students that I knew either finished pre-calc or calc their senior year.  I wouldn't concern myself too much with AP classes though, they are nice, but their utility is a function of the math program where you plan to go.  Some programs have considerably more analysis driven calc courses and may require students from more applied AP calc courses to retake courses or sign up for review/accelerated courses.
 
So in summary it's good to get at least pre-calc out of the way before going to college so you will at least get credit for the college math classes you take, but don't fret about AP courses.  Personally, I only had pre-calc in highschool and still finished my undergrad in mathematics without staying any longer than usual.
