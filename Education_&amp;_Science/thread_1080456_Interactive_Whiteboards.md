This is also a great place to fit Ubuntu in the classroom since they'll be investing in new computers for this anyways.

I've been using interactive whiteboards a while in my teaching (Maths). There are some advantages to them, and the students really like going up to the front and interacting with the technology.  Most teachers like it.

### Post by alecspoons on 2009-05-07
I've come to this discussion whilst trying to find solutions and ideas for using Open Source (in particular Ubuntu) in UK primary schools. I am both a class teacher and ICT co-ordinator where I work and have been for ten years. 

It's been suggested here that they are more trouble than they are worth. I did some research about interactive whiteboards some time back as part of a post graduate certificate and I would refute that point of view. This is from the research I did:

"The majority of existing observations concerning interactive whiteboards is positive (Cogill, 2003). BECTA (2003) cites a range of reasons why interactive whiteboards offer benefits to the teacher and learner. The interactive whiteboard is versatile with a lot of useful applications (Smith, A., 1999); teaching time is increased by 20-25% (Walker, 2003); it supports more interaction and conversation with pupils compared to more classic uses of ICT (Gérard F. et al, 1999); the interactive whiteboard seems to make learning more enjoyable and more interesting (Levy, 2002); teachers are better able to integrate other forms of ICT into whole class teaching (Smith, H., 2001); the use of the interactive whiteboard encourages spontaneity and the dynamic use of other digital resources (Kennewell, 2001); the interactive whiteboard encourages teachers to develop, share and reuse resources (Glover and Miller, 2001) (Levy, 2002); the interactive whiteboard is easier to use for whole class teaching than an ordinary computer (Smith, H., 2001); the whiteboards inspire teachers to use ICT more in their teaching (Smith, A., 1999); the interactive whiteboard can offer a focus for teacher-student and student-student interactions (Levy, 2002); children are more able to cope at a higher level in other subjects, due to the visual impact and feedback (Smith, H., 2001); the board can accommodate different learning styles (Bell, 2002); the interactive whiteboard is an effective means of enabling students to present and discuss work (Levy, 2002). BECTA (2003) cite Goodison (2002) in saying that the interactive whiteboard removes the need for a keyboard to engage with the technology, thus increasing access for younger children and students with disabilities."

In the UK, interactive whiteboards are (rightly or wrongly) central to the classroom in almost all cases and schools have invested thousands over the last few years. I find I can think of an answer to almost all the objections to using Ubuntu and other Open Source software that a cynical colleague might pose, but the issue of using existing whiteboards in a truly interactive manner still sticks. It may be that changing to some kind of tablet system with a wireless projector is better, but it might also be a big leap for a cash-strapped school where everything (understandably) revolves around MS.

I hope there's more to say about this.

### Post by alanpt on 2009-06-10
The Primary School in New Zealand I work at use large LCD TVs plugged into Tablet PCs.  We found it to be the best option.

The problems with blackboards, whiteboards and interactive whiteboards is that you turn your back to the class and repeat the old traditional power dynamic.  For schools who have spent years developing inquiry and collaborative based teaching, this is a big step backwards. And of course in the end the only people having fun are the people up the front using it. Which is a thrill that wont last.

Using a large TV (with VGA input) you don't have to worry about curtains, or robust loud speakers.  And with a Tablet PC and a podium high desk and stool you can still face the class. If you have a long enough cord pass the tablet around.

So everyday our teachers use powerpoint (and extended desktop) for notices and timetables and class photo slideshows.

The only good thing I can say about them is the teaching software that comes with them. They are ordered in the same structured way as the UK Curriculum.
