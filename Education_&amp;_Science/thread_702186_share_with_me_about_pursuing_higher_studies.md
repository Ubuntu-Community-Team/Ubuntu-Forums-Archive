### Post by ackey on 2008-03-17
I certainly wish you luck!

I just graduated from MIT.  There are many undergraduate students there from all over the world - I'm sure MIT is understanding with application fees.  Their financial aid for international students isn't as good as it is for domestic students, but it sounds like your situation would warrant it.

Obviously your standards are high - and reasonably so!  But if you have little luck with the big name schools (MIT, Stanford, etc) don't rule out smaller universities in the US.  You may be able to get more personalized attention to your financial needs.  While going to a school like MIT or Stanford is great, they tend to be in areas where housing and the general cost of living is *much* more expensive.  A school like Wisconsin-Madison can provide an excellent social, cultural, and educational opportunities while having lower costs.

There are many opportunities at smaller schools to do great research (CERN, national labs, etc) or have good internships.  When on a career path in science, the most important thing is research!  That is what grad school is about!  Most  grad students at the "big name" research schools didn't come from the "top" US colleges.  Drive and intelligence can get you from practically any undergrad institution to a great graduate school.  And graduate school in science (like physics, not engineering) is usually fully funded!

### Post by Boelcke on 2008-03-17
I'd like to echo another poster's thoughts.  You mentioned a handful of US schools that are famous, but there are thousands that are really quite good that you might not have heard of.  If you are short on funds, but have this Internet access, I'd just compile a list of as many as you can, and start sending emails.  Who knows where it could lead if you just ask!

One such list:
[http://www.utexas.edu/world/univ/state/]("http://www.utexas.edu/world/univ/state/")

Also, I'd like to suggest that you make a plan to get yourself into the undergraduate program you're interested in, and worry about the other stuff later.  Many folks have some very interesting careers WITHOUT having to go to grad school.  "You're not really useless until you get your PhD!" has been muttered before.

Get the undergrad, and get out and work before you decide what to spend another 4-7 years of your life studying.  With one Bachelor of Science degree I've had two careers so far, and have worked in four different industries.  You never know where the next step will take you.
