### Post by gunksta on 2009-12-05
This is a challenging question to answer - Different people will have different needs. Be careful with what you get here - the individuals in this forum almost undoubtedly above-average in terms of computer literacy. Thus, the things ID'd by people here as "important to know about IT" may not be important to more casual users.

I think Samden's example highlights this remarkably well. Teaching his grandmother why she should not use the ENTER button to make something double spaced was clearly necessary - she was inappropriately applying a lesson she learned years ago typing on a type-writer. It's possible that this would be a VERY good thing to teach many older adults who are struggling in their transition to a computer-based way of producing documents.

But, if you walk into a classroom full of 18 year old college freshmen in New York City, this is probably not an useful lesson. The iPod generation is pretty comfortable using Google and opening up documents, but they are often clueless about the differences between things like RAM and the HDD (speaking from recent experience) and this could be a useful thing to teach them, while I would probably not teach my grandmother about it (see above).

You clearly need to have some ideas mapped out before the class begins and I'm not trying to discourage that, but I would recommend asking your students for ideas too. I would ask them at the beginning of the course and I would ask them again half-way through. They may be one of your best sources of information about what they are struggling with, which is really what a class like this should focus on.

### Post by samden on 2009-12-06
Gunksta is absolutely correct about different audiences needing to know different things. So for the modern audience:

The most important thing I see is to teach them not to equate "Windows" with "Computer", not to equate "Word" with "Typing a document", not to equate "Excel" with "Data analysis" etc. They need to know there are many options to do every task, and just because something is currently popular does not necessarily mean it is the best choice.

For "Typing a document" for example, Word might be a good option for short documents you need to collaborate heavily on with other Word users, or must ensure perfect compatibility for a journal submission. OpenOffice might be good for cost reasons, or to ensure you use open formats. Scribus (or Publisher, or some Adobe product) could be better to produce a brochure, newsletter, or billboard. LaTeX might be best for a thesis. Plain text might be best for some notes, or an email.

Even a typewriter may be the best option in some cases - I still use mine to fill in sticky labels for example (saves wasting time setting up the computer to format them right when I can just type them directly). Every tool has its uses.

But too many people sit down to type ANYTHING in Microsoft Word (or OpenOffice Writer) without ever even realising that there might be a far easier way of achieving a professional result for their particular task.

The same goes for spreadsheets. In my experience most people just do all data analysis in spreadsheets (generally Excel) and don't even realise there could be another way, such as a database or statistical package like R. Having learnt R I virtually never touch a spreadsheet now, and am far more productive, even with small volumes of data - but no teacher or lecturer ever suggested anything but a spreadsheet to me.

Teach them to think outside the square, and present different ways of doing things to them.

I would also add one important abstraction to this discussion - IT is in a continuous state of change/evolution.

I learned how to use a pencil and paper to write when I was a child. Now I'm thirty. In the 25 years or so since I first used a pencil, the basic interface between myself and the piece of paper has not changed in any meaningful way (in terms of using the pencil and paper). Although those who have seen my handwriting would argue that I have yet to master even the basics of penmanship, it is a skill that I have not had to re-learn or adopt to a new environment (I never owned a PDA/stylus).

IT is completely different. To effectively use IT, you must accept that you must learn new tricks and that things you learned in the past may or may not always be relevant/useful in the future. A power-user from 1990 would be utterly lost when faced with a Google homepage without a few new tricks and some basic facts of life explained to him/her.

Or compare Google to Wolfram Alpha. They are both search engines, but they function very differently. When I first tried to use Wolfram, I inappropriately tried to apply lessons I have learned using Google. Surprise! These lessons don't apply when using Wolfram Alpha. I had to re-learn how to write a query to get really good output. I have some concerns with the licensing of Wolfram, but I still think it was interesting to learn how to use it and see what it puts on the table, but before I could do this, I had to learn some new skills and, sadly, 

**RTFM**!

Which is not something that we like to say here at the Ubuntu Forums to new users, but is a practice that effective IT users need to learn ASAP.
