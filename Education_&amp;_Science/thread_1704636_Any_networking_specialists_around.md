### Post by mips on 2011-03-11
Go study at a fully accredited university. Do a bachelor of engineering (electronic) or computer science with communications subjects. Afterwards do some Cisco or Juniper certifications.

The telecoms industry is moving away from TDM based technologies to a fully packet based (TCP/IP) environment. SDH/SONET equipment these days already cater for native ethernet and the next big thing is packet over optical transport and switching in the optical domain.

Certifications are useful but more so if you have a solid underlying degree and it also improves your chances of being hired as well. Your degree is for life, certs last 2-3 years.

### Post by Khufucius on 2011-04-05
I would begin by questioning your first assumption -- that it is necessary to get a 4-year degree to be successful at system or network administration.  It is necessary?

My experience says no.

I never went to college, because I wasn't ready to commit 4 years/$50,000+ to something I wasn't 100% sure would make me happy in the long run.  Nevermind the 2 years of "general education" stuff: you'll have the privilege of being forced to pay lots of money to re-take high-school english and math, along with things like art appreciation.  No thanks.  Save yourself the time and money.

SKILL and EXPERIENCE matter to employers.

If you are capable of learning on your own, you can learn much more, MUCH faster that way.

I'm the sysadmin/network admin for a small business.  I didn't get this job by accident -- I beat out several candidates with 4-year degrees.  To be a great network admin, you need to be smart, you need to be skilled, and you need to be able to LEARN FAST, and without a teacher around to check homework.  If you are/have those things, then you can learn this stuff on your own.

If you can do that, then take the 4 years and $50k and spend it on books, certifications, and equipment to play with at home.  You'll be much better off for it.  All my training so far has cost me less than $5,000.  The rest has been paid for by employers, or by my salary.

If you study hard for 6 months, you can be ready to be a junior system or network admin somewhere.  Then you can actually get PAID to learn.  That's cool.

If you dare, here's the plan that worked for me:

1. Study your *** off for 6 months.  Read 2-3 hours per day, and spend a few hours practicing the stuff you can apply on your own computer.  Get a few good TCP/IP books (I actually think "Sam's Teach Yourself TCP/IP in 24 hours" is a GREAT one to start with).  Read a Net+ exam prep book, just for the info.  It's not that difficult, it just takes a bit of time before it all makes sense.  Just keep reading and practicing.

You can get machines to practice on (old computers, routers, etc) from most large companies.  They have to pay for disposal anyway, so they usually don't mind giving you an old machine that can no longer do its job.

2. Call small/medium businesses in your area and ask if they need a sysadmin/network admin intern for 15-20 hours per week. Be as enthusiastic as humanly possible.  If they can tell that you love IT, and you can learn fast, and you're excited to do the boring stuff that their admins don't want to do, you will get hired. Many companies will even pay you $13-15/hr for this.  Do this for free if you have to.  Experience is worth MUCH more than money at this point.

3. Now you're studying/learning both at home and at work!  Score!  Keep doing this for 6 months.

4. Talk to your boss about getting hired full-time as a junior admin.  You won't be rolling in cash, but it will enable you to do this full-time, which is the goal.  From there, you'll be set.  You'll be making some money for certifications -- work will often pay for additional certs and training -- and you'll be getting better at all the important stuff.

5. After a year or so as a junior admin, you should know enough to move up in the company, or switch to another one as a regular network admin.

At this point, you've invested just about two years and MAYBE $10,000 (if you've been spending money like crazy).  You'll already have contacts in the business, current certs and training, two years of experience on your resume, some money in the bank, and MOST IMPORTANTLY -- an idea of whether or not you want to keep pursuing a career in Networking.

If you stop right there and apply for an IT job somewhere -- after 2 years -- YOU WILL GET HIRED OVER A FRESHLY MINTED 4-YEAR COLLEGE GRAD.

Don't take this post to mean that I don't think college is a good investment -- I think that for some things, it can be (for example, if you wanted to do Project Management in a large company, and move up the corporate ladder efficiently).  It's just that IT is one of those magical fields where you can jump in and start working to see if it's for you.

And if you decide you want to move all the way up the IT ladder, you can get a PMP (proj. management) certification, or a degree in business management, or whatever the company wants from you -- but you'll be doing it with FULL knowledge of WHY you are spending the time/money.  And many companies will help you pay for it, to boot.

Anyway, I just hear almost everyone on this thread tacitly agreeing, "yup, to do networking, you need a computer-related 4-year degree."  It's just not true.

What you should do is get ABSOLUTELY clear on what you want -- it sounds like you're well on the way do doing this -- and then figure out how to get it with the minimal waste of time, energy, and money.  The path I've suggested here is just one of many ways to go about it.

### Post by gunksta on 2011-04-27
Another spin on it is this: 

Unless you get an engineering degree, a bachelor's degrees leaves you ill-equipped to actually get a job. Think about it, what job skills does a bachelor's degree in English or Psychology give you? I enjoy reading the classics (Edgar Allen Poe especially) but an English degree won't get you a job. I'm not trying to say that studying English isn't a good thing to do, because it is. But you need to be realistic about what it is going to get you.

In contrast, networking or systems administration skills can land you a real job with a real pay check. Quickly. But, it probably won't broaden your horizons like studying the classics of English literature. While nobody has ever paid me because I happen to like Poe, I am truly grateful to the teacher who introduced me to him in a college English class. My boss pays me because of my MSW and programming skills, but I am a better, more interesting person because someone made me learn things that were beyond what I would have pursued on my own. When I need to learn how to program in a new language, I can learn that on my own. I doubt I would have read Poe on a voluntary basis.

Given the state of today's job market (which probably won't change overnight) someone in your position SHOULD go to college. Make new friends and see new places. Study abroad if you get the chance and can afford it. While you're working on that degree in _?_, pick up some certifications. You can probably learn most of it on your own and having a couple of certifications (and the expertise that comes with them) will make it easier for you to land a job after graduating from college.

I had a friend who did this. She went to school and got a psychology degree. On its own, a bachelor's in psychology is basically useless. While in school she picked up an EMT B certification. After getting her bachelor's degree, she didn't want to go straight into med school even though she had the grades to do so. She took a couple of years off, upgraded the EMT license to a full paramedic's license and did that for a few years. She is now in residency and will become a OBGYN in a few years. Thanks to that EMT license, she had the skills to get a real job at the drop of a hat, even while she pursued something bigger.

In the past, having a bachelor's degree was a safe bet for landing a good job. This is less true with every passing year, but that doesn't mean it doesn't have some value.
