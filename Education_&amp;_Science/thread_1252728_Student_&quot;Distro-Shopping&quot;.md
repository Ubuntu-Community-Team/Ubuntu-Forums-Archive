### Post by gunksta on 2009-08-29
I absolutely agree with Beneblade that it is largely an issue of preference, but there are a couple of things I'd like to throw in. 

If you are a computer science student, then most any old-distro will do. All of the "major" distros will include packages (or compilation instructions) for the major databases and programming languages.

Otherwise, there is an advantage to some of the larger distributions such as Debian, Ubuntu, Fedora, etc. There are hundreds of thousands of open-source projects in the world. The vast majority of these programs will be of no interest to you. For example, I don't know squat about the options for doing sound mixing under Linux. It doesn't interest me, regardless of my OS platform. I don't need anything more than a media player to play my country/western music.

But, I do know that if I get up in the morning and really want to play with application ________x_________ tomorrow, there is probably already a Ubuntu/Debian .deb file waiting for me in the repositories. So, in choosing a distribution, I think the size of the software repositories is an important consideration. I have a great deal of respect for the volunteers who put together some of the smaller distros but I like having access to the vast (overwhelming) choices available to me in the Ubuntu/Debian world..

Secondly - Consider the quality/accessibility of the forums. If you are new to Linux, you may want help at some point. Make sure the forums are active and friendly. The Ubuntu Forums definitely fit this bill. From what I have seen, the Fedora Forums do as well. I am not familiar with the forums for other distros, and I do NOT mean to speak ill of them. But, before installing a distro as my primary workstation, I would definitely look to make sure that I could get help if/when I need it.

Thirdly, pick a distro and stick with it. If you have a secondary machine, it can be fun to experiment, but I think there is a lot to be said of learning a system and learning it well. Linux distros are more similar to one another than they are dissimilar. But, the underlying tools and config do vary. I can shoehorn Ubuntu onto just about anything with a processor. If I tried to install Fedora and ran into a problem during the installation routine, I would have fewer tricks up my sleeve. An experienced Fedora user would know what to do, but I would probably have to use Google. I know Ubuntu/Debian and I like to think I know them fairly well. But that's my choice. You may choose something different.

Finally, I would look to see how long the distro has been developed and I would consider how likely it is to continue development. It's unlikely that Ubuntu/Debian will cease development any time soon. Slackware has been around forever and always will be, simply because it's Slackware. Fedora and Suse have plenty of corporate backing (like Ubuntu) and aren't going to disappear. Other projects (Gentoo?, CentOS?) don't have the infrastructure, development community, or sheer tenacity of Patrick Volkerding. These smaller communities are more likely to fall apart or not make another release. If you're going to really learn a system, it would be nice to know that it's going to be there next year with an updated version and security patches.

OK. Those are my ten cents.

Either way. If you power your computer with a penguin, you can't go too wrong.  :-)
