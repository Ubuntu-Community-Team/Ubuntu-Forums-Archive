### Post by gunksta on 2010-11-10
Since you quoted me, I might was well throw my two cents in. By celestial mechanics, I was referring to comparatively modern ideas such as Newtonian physics, the sun at the center of the solar system, calculus etc.

But, you don't need any of these things to plot where a star will be in the night sky. You don't need to understand how/why the moon circles the earth or plot it's path through space to observe the pattern of the moon as it waxes and wanes across the sky. Similarly, the other planets appear in the night sky at known intervals which are calculated without any understanding of their location in 3D space. It's easy to record / observe the pattern and then project it into the future. In contrast, it is quite hard to plot the planets in 3D space and then use that information to predict where they will be in the night sky.

The ancient Greeks, Romans, Egyptians, Mayans, etc. etc. etc. did not know the WHY although they could calculate the WHEN/WHERE.
