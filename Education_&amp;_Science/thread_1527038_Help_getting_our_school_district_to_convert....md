### Post by yankeeboy254 on 2010-07-08
So, our school district currently runs PCs with Windows on them. Big surprise there. Our town is planning on laying off 20+ teachers (not many, we have a town of about 33k, 2k in my high school, about 5k students total in the town), and I want to try and convince our school district's IT guy (I did an interview about how the school censors many websites "inappropriate" and if they went overboard on the censorship, wit our town's IT manager), to get our schools to use Ubuntu.

The problem is, I have no idea where to start. I'm going to need:
Facts (articles from reliable sources), about Ubuntu
Need help emphasizing that they can get a refund for Windows when they next upgrade the school's computers.
Specific benefits (faster, safer, stabler) and more facts to back it up with.
Explain that there are open source alternatives to almost any program that we currently use on Windows (specifically, replacements for things like CS3, web monitoring/blocking, things for kids in younger grades).

I was thinking that:
Ubuntu would be good to put in the schools we have for grades 6-12, and maybe something like Edbuntu would go into the elementary schools.
I would emphasize openoffice, GIMP, Firefox and the reliability of the Linux kernel.
I would also need to explain how the students would transition, and how we would need to show them how to use it.
Installation would also be an issue, I think they would have to pay for support from Canonical, and for someone to set it up, especially on the school's server.

I really need suggestions on other things I would need to help me get our school district to start using Ubuntu, and explain the advantages of FOSS. 

If anyone can think of anything else, I would greatly appreciate it.

### Post by cnich23 on 2010-07-08
You need to find two or three points on why your district should start to convert, and drive them into the wall. You need to prove your points with real facts and studies from companies, secondary education, and higher education centers.

For example you can talk about ...
* Googles recent transition away from its employees using windows
* 100$ Laptop success across third world countries
* Universities computer science and mathematic departments pushing to move to open source

Your fortunate enough that most of the research has been done for you already. Hit up google ... "university convert to linux", go to your local library or school library and gain access to technical online databases to do research.

Remember it is difficult to convert all at once, especially since many students will not have time in their schedules to learn a brand new course. It may be better to start with the entry level computer students.

Hope some of this helps.

Good luck!

### Post by brallan on 2011-02-28
I was able to get folks where I work to all change over, to the point where there is only one person in 10 who still has windows or Mac even on their own laptops, but it was a process that took years. Since I was the IT guy, it was in my interest not to have to maintain any windows machines, but you never know whether the IT folks in your school will come on board, a lot will depend on that.

You might try and get  them to do a test with a couple of machines. The IT people might realize that they will have to do less maintenance on those machines.

> **DeviantGuy said:**
> Why don't you give a Live-CD to the IT guy? Let him give it a try.

I think the liveCD is great, but keep in mind that it has limitations, especially if RAM is low, or people try things that  are not on the liveCD, like flashplugin, mp3 support, etc. 

Another option is to do an install on a USBdrive (including the bootloader) rather  than make it a liveUSB and also install a lot of the toys people will  want to try out - skype, mp3 and youtube support, etc. I have [a thread which describes many of the things I did]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=969723") to make the public machines more accessible to folks. A lot of this is out of date, but the general idea is there.

At the very least, you will be able to convince people that FOSS alternatives exist and that children should have access to them alongside the proprietary equivalents. Every time I walk into a cybercafe and my only option to surf is explorer, I cringe. These days there is no excuse for not providing programs like firefox, openoffice, and vlc.
