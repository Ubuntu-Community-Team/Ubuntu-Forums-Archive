### Post by damnhappy on 2009-04-16
Hi Everyone, 
I am the director of a student support services program at a college. Part of our program has a small student computer lab with 12 desktops. They all have WinXp on them. The computers aren't that old - maybe 3-4 years old- I don't remember the exact specs. The rest of the school is replacing their computers, but I would like to switch what I have to Ubuntu. 

I am going to write a proposal up to do this.

Almost all of the specialized software on the college computers have linux versions availible, except for Microsoft Visual C++ Studio, and the Microsoft .NET studio. 

My question is what are good alternatives for these programs on the Linux?

Also, any other ideas that would strengthen the proposal would be welcomed. 

So far, off the top of my head I have:
[LIST]
[*]Cost
[*]More Secure
[*]Free Anti-virus
[*]Exposure to Open Source alternatives to Expensive Closed Software that many Students can't afford
[/LIST]

The more the merrier. 

Cons are welcome too. 

Thanks!

### Post by Rich_B_uk on 2009-04-18
I would go along with sunon on the mono shout. That's what it's there for. Anything dev level and bespoke you may need to run as an app could be checked out with wine or cedega for compatibility.

If I were you I'd riff off the whole cost & adoption thing. We're going through a difficult economic time & many organisations are adopting Linux. Look towards all the governments who are adopting this approach in all their systems. When your students graduate they will have a niche skill which will put them ahead of the pack and make them an instant choice for public services.

At the same time the computing industry has a big focus on stopping piracy; and of course when you use FOSS this becomes a non-issue. Instead of your kids passing round hooky copies of office et al you can teach them the virtues of OpenOffice.

As far as precise alternatives are concerned, if you search digg or stumbleupon you'll find one of several thousand dedicated lists. You could also come up with some unique things that Linux can do that you really can't do with Windows. For example, if you go googling, drawtop did some amazing stuff with group collaboration and drawing.

When you finish things off don't forget to remind the board that these machines are IRO 3yrs old and hard drives can't be expected to last much more than 5yrs in heavy use. When machines start failing they can be instantly brought back on line with a Live-CD or converted to thin/hybrid clients that boot Edubuntu from a central server which would easily save you $30 per unit. It all adds up.

If you need any more help feel free to drop me a PM. I'm by no means a Ubuntu/Linux guru but I have spent considerable time researching this area for my undergraduate degree and would probably be able to expand on this quite a bit.

Good luck with your endeavours! :popcorn:
