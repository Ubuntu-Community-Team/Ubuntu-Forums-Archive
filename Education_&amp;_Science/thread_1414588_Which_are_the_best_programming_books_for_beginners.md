### Post by ctdahle on 2010-02-25
I have two books that I am working through. The tough one is *The Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs* by Harold Abelson and Jay and Julie Sussman, which you can obtain on line from MIT. It started out easy enough for me, but after the first 25 or so pages, I realized that I just didn't have the background knowledge or the time to get the most out of it, besides, when I started, I wasn't quite sure what to do to get Scheme, the version of LISP used in the book, running on my machine. (still not sure how!)

Still, I keep it handy as I work my way through another text* Python Programming: An Introduction to Computer Programming* by John Zelle. I find this to be really enjoyable, informative, and challenging. Although it teaches Python programming, it's really a computer science text that uses the Python language as a structure for teaching how computers function. After just a couple of hours with it, I was able to start writing real programs and had a much better idea of how to sketch out plans for several of my harebrained schemes.

It helps that much Linux development is being done in Python, and that most Linux distros install Python and IDLE automatically. MIT has downloadable implementations of Scheme available, but since I am completely on my own learning my way around Linux, and also have very limited time to devote to this project, I am sticking with stuff is already loaded and running on my machine.

Im told that you have to have a workmanlike ability to use at least five computer languages comfortably before you can start to think of your self as a real programmer, so my goal is to master Python, and then start working through Scheme/Lisp. After that I am going to work on Perl and Java.

Anyway, I don't know if I really have a great plan, but I do know that I am learning one heck of a lot from the Zelle book, which is written at a level just enough above my head that I have to seriously think about what I am learning and reading. Just the ticket to keep aging brain cells working at their peak capacity....

### Post by ctdahle on 2010-02-28
The Python GUI module is Tkinter, or rather, Tkinter is a GUI module written in Python, that works in Python and several other languages, and since I just began reading about it today I am not far enough along in my learning to say more than that. You can learn more about Tkinter at [http://www.pythonware.com/library/tkinter/introduction/index.htm](http://www.pythonware.com/library/tkinter/introduction/index.htm)

Everything I have read so far indicates an expectation that you should be pretty adept at writing programs before you start applying GUI's to them, but I am closely following the Zelle book and it is heavily focused on theory and computer science.

Other books lead into building and using GUI tools in programs much earlier, but also treat Computer Science in general on a shallower level. Warren and Carter Sande's *Hello World* which is oriented toward games programming and is written for readers who are more focused on JUST learning to program, addresses GUI's pretty early on, earlier than Zelle.

*Python Programming for the Absolute Beginner*, by Michael Dawson, which is also more oriented toward game development, addresses GUI tools earlier on as well.

I actually have all three books and in addition to that, have been reading and working through the examples and study questions at two of the sites Memilanuk mentions as well, the **Learning To Program** site [http://www.freenetpages.co.uk/hp/alan.gauld/](http://www.freenetpages.co.uk/hp/alan.gauld/) , and also the **Think Like a Computer Scientist** site [http://openbookproject.net//thinkCSpy/index.html](http://openbookproject.net//thinkCSpy/index.html) .

I work through Zelle, and then if I get stuck or confused, I read the comparable chapter in one of the others. I'm pretty confident that working through any of the sources mentioned in this thread would be adequate to learn something about programming, but I like to get multiple perspectives, and have the confidence that if I don't "get it" the first time, the important stuff will come up again in another text. I just wish I had more time to devote to it. I'm pretty sure that you will be WAY ahead of me by next week.

### Post by spikyjt on 2010-02-28
If you decide to go with Python, I'm sure you will get lots of support from loads of people who use it, and find plenty of example of its use, particularly within GNOME and some web apps. However, if you really want to learn C++, you might want to have a look at Qt. It's an awesome toolkit, which takes away a lot of the pain of C++. It's kind of like C# but with the performance of C++. I know some people hate it, for various previous licencing reasons, and because of prejudice against C++ for various reasons. Qt brings the benefits of managed code to C++, though, and keeps the great performance and flexibility. It's really quick to pick up too, because so much is already done for you by the great API. And QtCreator is a great RAD IDE for building GUI apps particularly (although not exclusively).
C++ GUI Programming With Qt 4 - Blanchette/Summerfield is a great book that really helped me get going, but the bundled docs with Qt are awesome and come with loads of examples.
Enjoy your journey.
