### Post by hsweet on 2010-03-28
It's hard.  I know.  

I have run a LTSP linux lab at my school for 5 years, shown it off to the admins, saved ~$100k, traded in my labs newer computers for discards, spent $0 for software for 6 years etc,  all to a great big ho-hum.  Now money is tight, some teachers will probably be laid off next year and still nobody is knocking on my door. 

It is a shame though.  I wish you luck.  If you are a student and you can get the school admin or better yet a technically competent teacher involved, you can set up a LTSP lab and show it off. 

If you can get your hands on a newer computer you can make it into a server and turn a small windows lab temporarily into a Linux lab just by plugging the thing into the classroom switch.  (get permission first and make sure you know what you are doing)

### Post by ugm6hr on 2010-04-04
If money is the reason for this - find out who is responsible for IT spending at the school & ask how much the MS licenses cost for your school.

Since MS discounts educational licenses, and that there is often only money to be saved when upgrading (e.g to MS Office 2007, Windows 7 etc), you may be better off planning for when your current software support expires, or when the school is planning to buy new hardware.

Good luck.

### Post by cprofitt on 2010-04-04
1.  This is less about money (Microsoft makes the software very inexpensive for K-12 / Colleges / Universities) and more about 'comfort' and 'job security'. An IT director of a K-12 is not likely to have extensive technical knowledge or familiarity with Open Source; when they use Microsoft they feel protected from having made a 'oops' decision because everyone else is using Microsoft.

2.  Focus on the new NETP 2010 recommendations from the US Department of Education:
