### Post by cprofitt on 2010-03-15
Links:

[http://www.rit.edu/academicaffairs/centerforstudentinnovation/?cat=58](http://www.rit.edu/academicaffairs/centerforstudentinnovation/?cat=58)
*Most* co-op students who manage to land a co-op with an opensource company are required to have a paid position. To help close this gap between large companies like Red Hat and Mozilla, and small student created projects, the [LTL]("http://ltl.rit.edu/") and the [CSI]("http://innovation.rit.edu/") have developed new models for working with 501c3 not-for-profit corporations and un-funded student projects. The [FOSS@RIT]("http://foss.rit.edu/") Initiative works with professors and faculty within multiple departments, so if a student wishes to contribute to a FOSS project, or a not-for-profit FOSS corporation, they can still work for the company unpaid and receive co-op credit, with the help of these faculty advisors.

[http://foss.rit.edu/](http://foss.rit.edu/)

"The RIT [Lab for Technological Literacy]("http://ltl.rit.edu/") houses FOSS@RIT, an initiative that grew out of the [Department of Interactive Games and Media]("http://igm.rit.edu/")'s course in educational game development for the OLPC. The initiative hosts a local OLPC Users Group, helps bring FOSS speakers to campus, sponsors professional development opportunities for faculty, is the home for research and development projects and has acted to bring a regional FOSS conference to the RIT campus, all since January 2009."

### Post by oceanside on 2010-03-18
Many 'software engineering' academic programs these days make large use of open source software, since it allows for all kinds of projects where students can look at the code/understand it/change it/study the design, etc which obviously can't be done with most existing commercial software. Some of these programs even contribute and/or start open source software projects.
