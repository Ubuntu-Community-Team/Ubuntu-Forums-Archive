### Post by thtrgremlin on 2009-03-12
I was led to believe some time ago that a library local to me used and promoted Ubuntu to its patrons. I finally visited recently to discover (sadly) it was not true. However, they have a large lecture room where they have classes that anyone can sign up to teach. They have an introduction to computers classes, dance, arts and crafts, and a few book clubs, however for the most part, the calender was quite empty.

There also wasn't a single person that had ever heard of Linux or Ubuntu. Having become very active over the past 2 years, very recently on the forums, and converted / enlightened quite a few people, I feel like it would be a good time grab a broader local audience.

While I feel privileged to be very comfortable with Ubuntu, I must admit I am LONG out of touch with what it is like to be a novice computer user. I grew up with computers.

I plan on giving a basic introduction to preinstalled applications demonstrating what can be done on the computer: OpenOffice Writer, Firefox, Gimp, and RythmBox, and present Jamendo and delicately explain why people develop free software, why people want you to use it, and how everybody benefits as a community. I also have some favorite clips of Nelson Mandela and Lawrence Lessig talking about community, not to mention some classic Linux commercials.

I am concerned about being out of touch with non-computer users, but my goal here is to show people that may not have money to do big fancy things with computer that they can still do really cool things with their computer by embracing free software.

So my question is: What are novices interested in learning / hearing about? Are there any open projects to help teachers present Linux / Ubuntu / FOSS to faculty and students?

For anyone that is really new to Linux, if I am lucky enough for you to have come by this article, what made you switch or at least made you consider Ubuntu worthy of your time to learn.

Any suggestions would be useful. Thanks in advance.
