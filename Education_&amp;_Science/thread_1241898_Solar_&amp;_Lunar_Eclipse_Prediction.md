How are eclipses predicted?
Since the astronomers know with very high precision the orbit of earth around the sun, and the orbit of the moon around earth, they can predict with very high precision when exactly the 3 celestial bodies will line up to cause an eclipse. 
The formulas governing those orbits were discovered by Issac Newton. Galileo probably already knew those orbits because he spent a lot of time observing and studying them, even if he didn't know about gravity.

### Post by kharat on 2009-08-24
There are two different things. Either you want to [1] calculate from the time range or you want to [2] verify on the available eclipse info using some software. For [1] you can look for books "spherical astromony by W M SMART" or google for a book "PRACTICAL ASTRONOMY WITH YOUR CALCULATOR" ( I have forgot auther name, may be newer version is about using computers). Both the books are very popular and may be available in local library. There are a couple of more books, which may be difficult to find. But this is very long path, I suppose. For [2] there are several software available. I personally know and use are KSTARS and XEPHEM, both are very useful, many astromonical requirments. Since, lunar eclipse is by falling of shadow of earth on moon, simulation of such an event in not presently possible in software like KSTARS and XEPHEM. However for solar eclipse I run a preview, before actually observing in the sky. Which is generally very accurate.

### Post by davesmith on 2009-08-24
Yes, it is possible to calculate the relative positions of the sun and moon and earth to high accuracy even far back in the past. NASA has a website with eclipse calculations back

[http://eclipse.gsfc.nasa.gov/eclipse.html](http://eclipse.gsfc.nasa.gov/eclipse.html)

to 3000BC. But it seems that there is uncertainty in the rate that the earth's rotation is slowing down, called Delta T. So, it is possible to calculate that there was an eclipse on, say 6th August 3909BC, but because of the uncertainty we don't know where on the earth it would have been visible.
 
There are two or three solar eclipses each year, but i think there are fewer lunar ones.
