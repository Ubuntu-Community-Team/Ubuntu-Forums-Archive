### Post by sethvath on 2008-10-21
Most CIS courses already dictate that you be proficient in C/C++, Java at the end of 3 years. I currently teach photography as an adjunct professor and from what I've seen at the college most if not all of the students in engineering already know some basic form of programming. With your course level, you are expected to be proficient at them, I'll be surprised if C and/or java is not taught as part of your course. 

Name a company you would foresee yourself working for in a few years time. If you plan to get into a startup expect to know a little of everything while being a master of one specialty. 

I am not in a position to advise you of career opportunities in CIS but you would probably glean off more knowledge from any of the visiting professors teaching business entrepreneurship (if you're into a startup) or course professors in CIS. 

An inside joke between the professors is how those who are competent leave to start a company while those who cant stay on to teach. Take that into account when you ask your professors about career opportunities and what discipline to take on.

### Post by silver_lynx on 2008-10-22
Well, it depends what you want to do after you have your degree. if working for science - then it is good to know languages/technologies, that give you an overview of the basics in CS, which are - 
 - structured languages (C)
 - object oriented languages (Java)
 - client/server paradigm
 - languages for parallel systems ().
   ...

After having dug a bit in these things, you could move from thing to thing in CS easily.

I would also recommend to learn a bit of
  - design/development of compilers
  - design/development of OS

You probably won't end up developing something like IhateWindows2010 OS, but it really makes your work enjoyable. The same counts for compiler development - after knowing a bit of that, each compiler looks much more friendly. 
 
if you want to work for industry, then in my opinion it is good to know technologies like ASP.net, JSP, languages like Java, C/C++ and then some stuff from the markup world (HTML, XML, ...). These you may not end up using a lot, however it opens understanding about web services/web-applications and stuff like that, which are highly appreciated by IT system developers.
