_**Ubuntu and the Environment**_

These questions may be best answered by Canonical, but I thought I'd post them here to encourage a bit of debate.

[LIST]
[*]What is the Ubuntu community doing to reduce the environmental impact of computing?
[*]Is there a formal strategy to minimise the environmental footprint of Ubuntu/Linux?
[*]Does the Ubuntu/Linux community care about being green?
[/LIST]

I ask these questions because:

[LIST]
[*]I am turning into a bit of an environMENTALIST ;)
[*]My Ubuntu machine at work doesn't recover from suspend mode :(
[*]The Linux community may be able to out compete and out market M$ on this, whilst helping save the planet :)
[/LIST]

I guess the Linux community can help in three ways:
[LIST]
[*]Better support for energy saving technologies (Suspend to RAM/SSD, Standby, CPU throttling, hardware acceleration, virtualisation etc)
[*]More efficient code, to reduce reliance on ever faster processors.
[*]Efficient function specific distros for home media centres, mobile devices etc.[/LIST]

Perhaps the Marketing Team and/or Community Council would consider pushing for an Environment Team?

I'd love to hear the official line from a senior Ubunt/Ubunter/Ubuntee (is there a term for Ubuntu community members?!?) and also from anyone else who has their opinion to add.

### Post by gweaver on 2007-06-15
That C02 saver plugin is interesting in that it can, presumably, save significant amounts of energy just by making sure that your power saving settings are optimal.

Saving energy is obviously both a technical and educational challenge - how many people set their screens and disks to shutdown when their computer is inactive? Not many I would guess - so _maybe Ubuntu power settings should be fairly aggressive by default?_

And how many people use suspend, standby and hibernate? I know I have been put off in the past because my computers often don't wake up, and because hibernate takes too long to write the data. The _suspend problem is a software issue_ and the hibernate problem is a hardware performance issue.

I agree that hardware efficiency is key to making big advances in green computing, but good software could and should help save power across all existing and future hardware. I assume that open source software can extend the life of old systems, but IMHO software developers still have a way to go in making full use of existing power saving features and opportunities. Suspend not working is just one example - at the cool end of the scale are projects like Beryl, which in theory at least can use efficient hardware acceleration instead of inefficient CPU cycles to give me a pretty GUI whilst saving energy.

In terms of hardware improvements I hope we will see:
[LIST]
[*]Solid state disks making hibernation a viable option again
[*]Efficient power supplies, with an "off" state that doesn't draw significant power
[*]Better power saving on CPUs, GPUs, RAM
[/LIST]

Plus lots of other things that don't immediately spring to mind...

One wonders how the economics and environmental costs of re-using old kit and replacing with more efficient new kit stacks up? If I replace my 50% efficient power supply with a 90% efficient power supply how long will it take for it to pay for itself, and offset the environmental damage caused during its production?

Has anyone in the Ubuntu community done these sums? And if so is there a recommendation?
