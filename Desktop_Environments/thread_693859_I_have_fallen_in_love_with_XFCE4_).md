### Post by fredbird67 on 2008-02-25
I'll throw in my $.02 here as well.  In the four years that I've been using Linux, the last three of which I've had ONLY some form of Linux EXCLUSIVELY on my computer, I've checked out quite a few distros and different desktop environments.  Until recently, I was a big-time KDE fan.  For me, that's what I was most comfortable with, probably since that was the first Linux desktop I ever played around with, on Mandrake (you can tell that goes back a few years).

However, with all the buzz I had heard about KDE4, I naturally had to go see what it was all about.  To be quite honest, I didn't like the looks of it at all -- it made it look like they were trying to impersonate Windows Vista, and I did NOT like their choice of a default look that resembles an operating system that is a piece of...well, you get the idea.  For KDE4, they've gone to a main menu that resembles Vista's Start menu as well as a black panel, which is what Vista also has.  I've seen what a headache Vista has been for people who have it (thank God I don't have it, and I definitely do NOT want it!), and for me, KDE choosing to emulate a Vista look and feel in its default settings was a MAJOR turn-off.  I know, it's a piece of cake to change all that, but the screenshots of it just left me with a bad aftertaste.

I had tried Gnome once but didn't really care for it.  Well, I decided to give it another go, and I've found that Gnome is actually a MUCH smarter desktop than KDE!  My reason for that statement is because I found it MUCH easier to install my printer in Gnome than KDE.  In KDE, you have to jump through a few hoops to install it, whereas Gnome INSTANTLY recognized it without me having to even lift a finger.  Xfce is almost as easy as well, too.  Not quite, but it is easier than KDE.

Anyways, I wound up switching from PCLinuxOS to Ubuntu, and Ubuntu has done LOTS of neat things with Gnome.  I used Ubuntu until I ran into a problem of being unable to play a couple of streaming radio stations, no matter what I downloaded to play them, so I switched to Linux Mint, which didn't have this issue.

However, only a few days after installing Mint, I noticed that the "Quit" button in the menu refused to work anymore, so I switched to the Xfce version of Mint, and it doesn't have this problem.  Xfce may not do everything Gnome or KDE does, but really, that's a good thing.  After all, I got quite used to the look and feel of Windows 95 and 98 back in my pre-Linux days, and Mint Xfce edition tends to have that general look and feel, which makes for a desktop that is very predictable and familiar to most people, I dare say.

However, the inability to "lasso" multiple desktop icons is most definitely a real pain!  This is a piece of functionality the team might want to start implementing in the next version of Xfce.  Other than that, it's great, and yes, as you can probably tell, I'm a little old-fashioned in what I think a graphical desktop should be.  Oh, and since I can easily run KDE or Gnome, you can obviously tell I have a powerful computer, with 512 MB of RAM and a 2.26 GHz Intel CPU.

However, in all fairness to KDE, if you used Kubuntu to base your opinion of KDE on, you're not getting a good representation of KDE.  Even I have never cared for Kubuntu, even back in my KDE-using days.  To me, Kubuntu felt like little more than a hack job.  If you want to see KDE at its best, I recommend either Mandriva or PCLinuxOS.  Even then, it's a little garish, but at least they give KDE a more fair shake than Kubuntu does.
