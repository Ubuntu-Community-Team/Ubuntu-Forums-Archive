### Post by schurtek on 2006-08-11
The word you are looking for is aesthetics. The problem is that up until now, the Free Society that Richard M Stallman talks about in his book (Free Software, Free Society) has been dominated by hackers. No not, hackers that sit at a PC and steal your identity. The true sense of the word. People who spend all their spare time at the computer, and have the ability to code their own applications. 

Now since these guys are mostly hackers, they are not very artistic, hence while the programs may look crappy, they are very functional, yet frustrating to use if you don't think like a hacker. 

Now you can do your part in the revolution, but stop trying to jump in front of the bull. Start by getting the windows versions of open source apps that run nativly on linux, such as OpenOffice.org, FireFox, Eudora, etc. Then join their communities, and give feed back as to where you feel they can improve it. You don't have to be a hacker (programmer) to help in making a programme better. 

If you are graphically inclined, which I suspect you are, then offer to help with interface designs... it's matter of drawing them in Scribus, Gimp or Xara Xtreme, and giving it to them to rip to pieces and put in their software... 

You see, the beauty of OpenSource is if you don't like something, you can change it... and the best way to change it, is to join the developers website. Chat with the developers, and give them your feedback.

I know one guy, all he does is design icons for toolbars... and he offers his services to countless projects doing that.

Buy jumping in front of the bull (moving from Windows to Linux cold turkey...) is the number one cause of people turning against opensource. It needs to be gradual. 

You can't change the OS first, so change the APPS first... then the OS. Do them one at a time... and you will see that the transition is more comfortable.

Also most open source apps are a branch of another similar app, so if you don't like the one you are using... look at the original app... and so forth... 

Well I hope, I am giving you new confidence... 

Good luck... and keep the discerning outlook when choosing apps, just don't dump the app... send in the feed back first... then dump the app. You will make a good disciple of the revolution one day... :D

### Post by cogsprocket on 2006-08-13
The lack of the "Linux look" out of the box is one of the reasons Ubuntu intrigued me in the first place.  While it does have the gnome desktop that tends to throw some confusion into the mix for lifetime Windows users, Ubuntu has a very nice user interface out of the box.

You are right about some programs, though.  Even the synaptic package manager still suffers from the "Linux look" in that it has a GUI that's not matured yet.  There are work arounds to make these programs fall in line, but you have to do them every time you change the look of of you desktop.  The fact that you're a little put off by some of these things is really no surprise.  Linux really has a way to go before it becomes a complete Windows replacement.  It's a really great operating system, but these guys who expect that people will leave Windows in droves every time Microsoft does something silly are really fooling themselves.

On the lighter side, however, I'm glad to hear you found a solution to your problem.  I hope that you'll stick it out.  Many who have been using Linux at varying levels over the past decade have had to give up some things in order to use it, find other ways of doing the same things or had to wait through some of the bad.  The great thing about Linux is that if you stick with it long enough you [B]will[B] learn some very cool things not only about Linux but about how your computer actually works.  There are very few Linux super users who do not understand some basic things about programming and functionality.  

That being said, there is one great perk to being a Linux user; Linux is in and of itself a skill set.  If you become proficient with Linux, understand how the shell works and how to write shell scripts, those skills become valuable to people who are looking to create solutions to real-life computer problems.  The thing about Linux in the working world is that though Linux has not quite become the end-all for desktop systems it has great task-oriented capabilities.  Linux is an often used server solution and for embedded systems, well you can't go wrong with an operating system you can build to suit your needs.

I've said all of that to say this; I know it's a difficult row to hoe and most people give up quickly because they were looking for the holy grail of  free operating systems and the truth is that Linux isn't there yet.  It's getting there and it's closer than it has been in the past, but it still has a way to go, but if you are willing to stick with it the rewards will be great down the line.
