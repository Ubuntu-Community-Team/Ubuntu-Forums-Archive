### Post by smoshe on 2005-11-17
Hey guys,

I've been using Ubuntu now for a few months. Started with Hoary, and was very happy with it.  The I did an apt-get upgrade to Breezy, and my system fried. X wouldn't start, and I don't know enough about recovery mode to bring something like that back on-line. So I did a manual install of Breezy and it worked fine. I like it. It now runs on both of my home computers and it's great. 

Breezy has much better hardware support than Hoary. It runs without a hitch on my girl friend's Compaq Deskpro 800mhz box, which had issues with the last major release of Ubuntu being too slow and freezing. In it's stead I installed an old version of mandrake on her box and it was a nightmare to manage. Now I remember why I liked Ubuntu as apposed to Mandrake. No darn RPM's to manage. They're horrible. 

Speaking of my lovely gf. She's always been a social activist, but when it came to computers, she just never really knew enough to know to try something like Linux. But now, she's jumped on the bandwagon. I did not expect her to become the rabid Linux enthusiast she is today. Mind you, this is someone with no technical background. But she says she feels a lot better since we made the switch. She thinks Breezy is the best thing since jpegs, and she likes the fact that Microsoft is nowhere near her box.  I tend to agree.

Breezy is smooth, feature rich, and a great distro to work with.  It's even helped me get over my life long fear of compiling at a command line interface, and for this I thank you. 

I think the only real snag I hit with it is that the version of gcc that Ubuntu comes with, and the one the kernel is compiled in don't seem to be compatible for system modules because of the differing version numbers. I now have so much built around gcc that removing it would be very tough to do. If anyone on the dev team is reading this, it would be great if there was a fix for this problem available. At present, I can't run certain programs that require this functionality, and life without them is difficult.

Other than that, the terminal links are gone. They're still in the system, but need to be accessed through manual shortcuts.

Thanks again.
There's really no other system like Ubuntu out there, and I'm ever amazed at how easy it makes linux advocacy. Great job guys.

-Sam
