### Post by peabody on 2006-08-27
> 
1. will i be able to run ms office?


There's a commercial product you can purchase called crossover office that allows you to run Microsoft Office natively.  Others have suggested that you may wish to try OpenOffice.org which comes preinstalled with Ubuntu.  It can read and write office formats.  There are occasionally hangups with very complicated office documents, such as Embedded images, pdfs, etc. But I've used it for my classes and have only ever once run into a problem, and turned out to be due to a corrupt file.

> 
2. is ubuntu faster than windows?


This is the hardest question to answer.  Some people's experience is that it is faster than windows, other people's experience is that is slower.  A lot seems to depend on your hardware.  There's probably no for sure way to answer this.

> 
3. wireless internet with ubuntu?


This is a page that lists the status of many different wireless cards:

[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/WirelessCardsSupported?highlight=%28%28WifiDocs%7CDevice%7CNetgearWG111%29%29](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/WirelessCardsSupported?highlight=%28%28WifiDocs%7CDevice%7CNetgearWG111%29%29)

Some cards work very well out of the box.  Others require the actual windows drivers which Ubuntu doesn't come with.  Others just don't work.  If you're seriously about giving Linux a go I would research and see if my wireless card is supported, and if it isn't, buy a different one.

> 
4. ubuntu interface


This is a hard question to answer because it's very subjective.  Some people find Ubuntu easier and more intuitive.  Others feel it's difficult to use and get used to (where's "Program Files?" etc.).

> 
5. can you get virus or spyware when using ubuntu?


In theory any computer platfrom can.  In practice, I know of no one who has in the Linux world.

> 
6. programming on ubuntu,


MS programming languages?  That seems to limit things to C# and Visual Basic.  Both of those things are almost non-existant in the Linux world, so you may find that'll just have to stick with Windows.  However, I'm a computer science major too, and I'll tell you at my University, they prefer you to have an extensive Unix background and familiarity with the Unix environment.  The C programming language and Unix sprang up around each other and their design and philosophy is tied to each other.  Most Universities that I'm familiar with want Unix C/C++/Java, and all of those are Linux strong points.

It may be difficult for you to switch to Linux from the sounds of things, but I hope you at least give it a shot and if it doesn't turn out to be your thing, keep it mind for the future if you find yourself in an environment that doesn't require you to be married to Microsoft technology.
