### Post by RAOF on 2006-02-20
[QUOTE=ardchoille]"[The Digital Millennium Copyright Act] strengthens the protection of copyrighted materials in digital formats, such as motion pictures on DVDs, by outlawing the manufacture, importation or distribution of devices, programs or services that circumvent technical protection measures that restrict access to or prevent infringement [copying] of copyrighted works. Thus, it prohibits anyone from distributing a software utility designed to circumvent the CSS technology used to protect DVD software."
Hmm.. according to that, anyone who licenses software that decrypts an encrypted DVD (including the Microsoft Corp., anyone whom has ever built or distributed a set-top DVD player, Blockbuster, Netflix, any movie rental house, developers of DVD copying apps designed for the Windows OS, etc.) is guilty of licensing illegal software.

Interesting how everyone except Linux users are allowed to violate the law.[/QUOTE]
They're not **circumventing** the CSS technology.  They have (paid for) a legitimate CSS key which they can use in software to decrypt CSS encrypted DVDs.  Those that haven't (you may remember 123Studios - they made a DVD backup program based upon DeCSS, and were forced to withdraw it) are illegal.

DeCSS either uses an **unlicenced** key extracted from a piece of licensed software, or uses brute force to crack the key used to encrypt the DVD.  Either way, it's circumventing the CSS protection.

[QUOTE=Tichondrius]I...Therefore, the MPAA doesn't like that and may try to outlaw the DeCSS software, even though by itself this software is not illegal, and in fact it can be used to do personal backups which are allowed.[/QUOTE]
The DeCSS software **is** illegal (in the US) - the DCMA makes it so.  It is a program (library) specifically designed to circumvent the technical protection measures on DVDs.

(For the US) Fair use means it is legal to back up your DVDs.  However, there is no legal way of doing that.  Therefore, you can't (legally) back up your DVDs.  Sucks, eh?

### Post by Redcard on 2006-08-16
I think I need to point something out here.

Fair use does NOT entitle you to make a backup of a DVD or any other software.  The portions of copyright laws that did allow you to make copies for personal archival were written out for items like DVDs and other such licensable items.

You don't own the movie when you purchase it.  You own the right to watch it in your home on MPAA/retailer approved devices.  Therefore, you don't own the right to back it up.
