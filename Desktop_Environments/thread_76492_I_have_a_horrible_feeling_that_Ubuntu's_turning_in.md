### Post by TheValk on 2005-10-15
Just to give a different perspective.
As someone who has been using Windows since 1994, may I say thanks to the Ubuntu developers for providing the first Linux distro that has been able to ease my journey out of the Microsoft camp and across to the open source.
I have dabbled quite a few times over the years, first with Suse 6.4, then 9.1, Mandrake, FreeBSD and Knoppix.
I had to give up, the learning curve was too steep and my time too short. I would spend hours just getting the sound to work, searching for instructions on editing obscure scripts and then find I had to do the same for the network card. As for the display....it was like going back to the days of Windows 3.1 and 256 colours.
I didn't need the agro so I left it to the propeller heads and went back to the slick Windows world where everthing happened under the bonnet and life was easy as long as you paid for it.
Then two weeks ago I was recommended to try Ubuntu.
It installed, configured and ran straight off the CD. Everthing was discovered and everything.. well... just worked. Just like Windows XP.
I had a working PC and didn't need to tear my hair out before I could browse the forums, read a newsgroup or send an email.
Gradually, I have tuned it, loaded a few new apps, (something I never managed to do with any other distro), changed the nVidia driver from nv to nvidia-glx, edited a few scripts under the guidence of these excellent newsgroups and now I hardly even log into Windows at all.
Hardened Linux Gurus may find these helpful little bubbles that treat them like newbies patronising but, hey, you can turn them off. I needed them and when I don't, I will too.
I sometimes get the impression that people who have come through the Linux mill since before there was a GUI resent the fact that someone should be able to use the OS without having to struggle for years through the command line syntax, compiling a few kernels and configuring baffling and interminable scripts just to hear a beep from their soundcards.
If Linux does adopt a few of the windows virtues like usability, ease of upgrading, universal drivers without the security problems then I for one will applaud the developers. It still has someway to go though. As a friend remarked as he watched me using Ubuntu yesterday and saw the log out text scrolling up the screen, "if my mother saw that when the machine shut down she would have a fit".
I didn't like to tell him about the user friendly display when the OS has booted up 30 times and forces a disk check....:D

### Post by rplantz on 2005-10-16
I've been writing code since the early 1960s. Most of my experience has been at the assembly language level. My experience ranges from debuging special purpose device drivers at the hex dump level to designing gui-s.

The most challenging task is designing a user interface. One person's "intuitive" interface is unacceptable to another person.

In my view, computer system user interfaces are about where automobile user interfaces were in the early 1900s. In those days, one had to be a mechanic in order to use a car. Nowadays, one doesn't even need to know how to drive in order to use a car.  :rolleyes:

There is a discussion of how to drive a 1903 Cadillac delivery van in the Oct. 10, 2005 issue of Autoweek. ([http://www.autoweek.com/article.cms?articleId=103262](http://www.autoweek.com/article.cms?articleId=103262)) An example of the user interface:
> 
There is no throttle in the modern sense; instead a lever below the steering column slips into a series of notches, each representing more or less intake-valve lift. For each position the spark advance must be manually adjusted using the thin round lever that pokes up beside the seat cushion on the driver’s right. A pedal to the left of the steering column engages low gear; releasing the pedal while pushing the gear lever forward (also on the driver’s right) shifts the planetary transmission into high.

Change a few of the nouns and you are very close to many manuals we've all seen. :-)

My real complaint is with big companies that push their way onto the market. Drawing again from the automobile industry, GM introduced the wrap-around windshield in 1953. The marketing people convinced the public that it provided better vision. In fact, the pillars were closer to the driver, effectively **increasing** the size of the blindspot. And the windshield curve created distortion. If these drawbacks weren't enough, you almost always banged your knees when getting in or out of the car. In other words, GM, through its shear size, managed to sell us a **worse** feature. Again, change a couple of nouns and you have a description of several software products on the market today.

As several people have noted in this thread, Linux is largely about choice. One of the best things is that we have a chance to try new things and to critique them. Hopefully, **good** designs will win - instead of rich people getting their way.
