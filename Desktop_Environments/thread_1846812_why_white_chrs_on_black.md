### Post by mcduck on 2011-09-20
Actually it's a common design guideline that black text on white background is easier for eyes on a light-reflecting surface (like paper) while white text on black background is better on a light-emitting surface (like a display).

Of course how comfortable each person feels with each color scheme is a bit of a personal  question, but as a generic rule the white text/dark background really is supposed to be easier to read and less tasking for your eyes. This is especially true if the surroundings are not brightly lit when you are at your computer.

### Post by mcduck on 2011-09-20
> **ottosykora said:**
> hmmm, ok then , strange that the forum here writes black on white...

I don't see anything strange about not every possible thing following all the possible design guidelines. After all, not every person in the world is a designer... ;)

But for example pretty much every professional audio/video/graphics/studio application will default to dark backgrounds with white text. Makes sense knowing that users of such applications will spend really long times staring intensively at the screen, often in a dimly lit studio environment. And of course the black background/light text scheme of command-line interfaces isn't just a coincidence either.

(I forgot to add in the previous post that neither black on white or white on black is actually considered good, the contrast is way too high in both cases. So something like light gray on dark background or dark gray on light background should be the best combinations. But in general things really do work differently between screen medias and printed medias, for example serif fonts are known to be most readable on print media, while screen medias should use sans-serif fonts instead.)

### Post by mcduck on 2011-09-23
> **rg4w said:**

> 
In order to assess whether the direction of the brightness difference (i.e. bright text on dim background vs. dim text on bright background) is of any importance in determining readability ratings, brightness difference was calculated as the difference between background and text brightness. In the case where the background was brighter than the text, this difference was found to be positive. For those images in which the text was brighter than the background, this difference was negative. The brightness difference was then plotted against the median rating, and yielded a &#8216;U-shaped&#8217; plot, shown in Figure 6 (below). **This plot suggests that the direction of the brightness difference does not appear to be of importance in determining overall readability ratings.** Low readability ratings were observed to be associated with brightness differences close to zero, and ratings were found to increase as the distance from the brightness difference to zero increased. **This provides support to the theory that readability ratings are higher for images which contain a greater difference in the brightness of the text and background, regardless of the direction of this difference.**
[http://www.aprompt.ca/WebPageColors.html]("http://www.aprompt.ca/WebPageColors.html")

...and why the light text on dark background might be easier for eyes? Simply because you don't need to stare at a white lamp for hours that way. :) Of course the lightning of your environment makes a big difference here, in a brightly lit surroundings the white screen is fine, but in dark environment the contrast between ambient lightning and the brightness of the screen becomes too high.

Anyway, I've understood that astigmatism can make light text on dark background harder to read than it should otherwise be. That is of course something worth considering in designs, but so is the stress caused by staring a bright light. Design definitely isn't easy whatever you do somebody will have problems with it. :D Of course having themes available for both cases is always nice, and  luckily there's also the Negative plugin on Compiz in case some program or web site doesn't have color scheme options.
