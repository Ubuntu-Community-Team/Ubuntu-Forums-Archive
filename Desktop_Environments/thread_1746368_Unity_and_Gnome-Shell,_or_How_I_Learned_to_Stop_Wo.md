### Post by manzdagratiano on 2011-05-01
Here is an unbiased (well slightly biased, but equally towards both) perspective on both Unity and Gnome-Shell.

I am with Unity on Ubuntu, and Gnome-Shell on Arch, which hit the repos the day before.

**First impressions:**

On Ubuntu, I went with the Natty upgrade to Beta 1, and rebooted, and waited...
And Unity was before me...
And it was AWESOME!
I was blown away by the shiny new interface... Things are intuitive, and everything is crisp. I changed the side panel icon size to the smallest possible, and it looks extremely elegant now. I also changed the indicator applet settings using dconf-editor to show my wicd applet and dropbox - perfect!
I love being able to search for an app without bothering with its category.
For users obsessed with categories, pray note that when you click the applications icon and the windows displaying applications pops up, on the top right corner, there is a drop-down item that states 'All Applications'. **It can be clicked on and it will drop down to reveal the very same - and more - application categories from gnome 2**, and that should seal the deal!
I love being able to rotate between windows by using <Sup>+<Num>. I love how Alt + Tab still works. I love how you can reorder your launcher favorites by a simple drag-and-drop - intuitive! I love everything!
It ***is*** a tad bit unstable, but in a few weeks it shall be ready and hailed as the greatest phenomenon that happened to Ubuntu in quite some time!

On Arch, I logged into Blackbox, removed Compiz, went with the upgrade, and logged out... A pristine GDM unlike any I had seen before awaits me. I log in...
And Gnome-Shell was before me.
And it was AWESOME!
I love being able to search for an app without bothering with its category.
For users obsessed with categories, pray note that when you click... <The same drill>
I love being able to rotate between windows with Alt + Tab. I love how Alt + <Num> gives you sub-windows of a particular window. I love how you can reorder your launcher favorites by a simple darg-and-drop - intuitive! I love everything!
It did not seem unstable at all... but partly because it has gone through more extensive testing as compared to Unity. In some time, it shall be hailed as the greatest phenomenon that happened to Gnome since Miguel de Icaza.

**Comparisons:**

1) They are different in their looks - Unity has the Ubuntu `Human' feel, while Gnome-Shell is completely new.
2)However, they are **VERY** similar in their philosophy. They both operate pretty much the SAME way - at least from the user's perspective. Anybody who disagrees on this may save that opinion for themselves - this is my completely unbiased opinion.
3) I totally agree that this is a new desktop paradigm in which clustering of menus the old way has become unnecessary. The focus, in either one, is on speed and accessibility, both of which, in terms of getting to something, are greatly enhanced, again in either one.
4) Both will mature to be very excellent UI's.

This is EVOLUTION!

I am EXTRA pleased, since I was worried that three of my five installs had reached the same look and feel - Ubuntu, Debian Sid, and Arch. Now I have Unity in Ubuntu and Gnome-Shell in Arch, and old Gnome 2 in Debian Sid for the time being... very VERY pleased!

I just have to now figure out to be able to use Compiz as a standalone window manager as well, since I love the Desktop Cube - I don't want to change the default Desktop Wall setting in Unity, since I like that too. This should be easy to do.

To the future then! :guitar:

**Keyboard shortcuts** Sir! Once you learn a few keyboard shortcuts your life with Unity becomes excellent! Alt + Tab is still around, so all you do is press Alt + Tab to reveal a cycle of all the open windows, and while holding the Alt keep on pressing the Tab until you get the window you want. This is very fast, and the previews are
a) large enough, and
b) have the application icon at the bottom right
both of which will enable you to immediately pick out which window you want.

Also. if you press the <Sup> (Win) key (I should call it an Ubuntu key - I keep forgetting to get those free Ubuntu stickers for this key from System 76), the launcher appears, and it will show you numbers corresponding to each of the launcher icons. While holding the <Sup> key, just press that number, and you are there.
Therefore, if say your terminal is the first launcher, from any window, pressing <Sup> + <1> will either reveal an existing terminal window, or open a new one if there are none. If there are multiple windows open, this will show you a cycle of all windows open, which you can choose from with one click!

All this can be, and shall be, rather, done with you **left hand** while your right hand is free to stay with the touchpad/mouse.

Ergo, Unity indeed is awesome, once one has gotten the hang of it!

Well, Unity is not usable **only** with the keyboard, but the keyboard and mouse work in unison, allowing for fast access. There are many who find it irritating to use a mouse to click every single thing, and I am one of them. The principle is like a hard disk - the head moves linearly while the drive spins, allowing fast access!

Moreover, there are window managers which are **keyboard-only**, **ratpoison** being one of them, and I would say it is pretty awesome!

And there are window managers which are **mouse-only**, **blackbox** being one of them (at least by default), and I would say it is pretty awesome too!

And if you have **THIRTY** open windows, first of all, in the bottom bar, you would not even be able to see what each window was, and even if you did see the names by some miracle of resolution, you would still have to read a lot to find out which window was which - a complete waste of time.

I just opened **fifteen** windows, and the Alt + Tab seems to be able to handle that with complete ease! The previews are still too big to see what is what, and it appears it can easily handle about **fifty** I would say.

EDIT: I just discovered, when pressing Alt + Tab with the left hand, one can, instead of cycling with the TAB key, just click on the desired window with the right hand, and have instead switching. Voila!

### Post by Roasted on 2011-05-02
I like manzdagratiano's outlook here, as it is obvious that they took a mature and calculated approach to using both desktop interfaces and given them a fair chance, which is far more than what a lot of users posting on these forums spouting off negative uneducated comments have done.

I have to agree with manzdagratiano on a lot of accounts here. I think both interfaces are extremely intuitive and I love things about both of them. There is more I dislike about Gnome Shell than Unity, though, which ultimately landed me in using Unity for now. Gnome Shell has that honeymoon effect, I feel. I love using it and I love tinkering around with it. But once I began to use it in a production environment, it grew tired on my eyes, as I find myself at times after long hours of work using my mouse far more than I use keyboard shortcuts. That said, it was irritating when in my mouse-mode to have the constant zoom effect to simply switch windows. Don't get me wrong, Gnome Shell is beautiful, but they clearly have to offer *something* more in the usability department in this scenario. Because of that, I don't like using Gnome Shell full time as I don't feel as it is the golden ticket since I don't feel as though I can use it in *any* scenario.

Unity on the other hand I feel offers a more well rounded user experience. It's quick, even on netbook spec (typing from my CR-48 now) and does the job in a very similar manner. While being beautiful, I feel as though it offers me a greater multitasking feel to it, considering I can use my keyboard AND mouse efficiently and effectively without having the "I'm sea-sick" feeling after being in power-user mode for a few hours.

Don't get me wrong. I love both of them, and I plan to utilize both of them as much as I can. But I have my preferences, just like anybody else does, and for me Unity fits the bill a tiny bit more. I certainly wouldn't second guess anybody shooting Gnome Shell as their primary desktop environment, as it is a very fine choice. But I'm quite happy with my choice too. :guitar:
