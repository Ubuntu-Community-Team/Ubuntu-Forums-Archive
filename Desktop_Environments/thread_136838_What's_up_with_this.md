### Post by ardchoille on 2006-02-26
I have been using Linux for 5 years. I have used Fedora Core (several versions), debian, CentOS, Mandrakelinux/Mandriva (several versions), Slackware, MEPIS (several versions), knoppix, Ubuntu (2 versions), KUbuntu, Arch, Linspire, Xandros and a few others. I always install my apps from the repos available for the proper distro and I always follow the docs for all apps.

The reason I bounce around with distros is because of unreliability and inconsistency. Example: Xine works great on Fedora but yum is horrible. Alacarte/Smeg works great on Ubuntu but MPlayer and Xine are unreliable and inconsistent. I got games to work great on MEPIS but the package manager sucks. I'd rather not have to run 100 different distros, and bounce around between them, just to get everything running the way it is supposed to work.

The support communities are outstanding, and I applaud you, it's the apps that I have complaints about. I have 7 machines now, four of which are identical as far as hardware, and an app that works great on one of the four identical machines gives me nothing but problems on another of the four identical machines. Those four machines are **identical**, so the software should run the exact same on all of them. I always try to solve a problem and always follow through until I find a solution or I have exhausted all help and support - which usually leads me to uninstalling the app and finding a different app to perform the task and wasting my valuable time.

When I turn on the water faucet, water comes out. There has never been an instance when I turned on the faucet and water didn't come out. When I flip a light switch, the light comes on. There has never been an instance when I flipped the light switch and the light didn't come on. The point is, when you perform an action, a certain result is expected.

Software should be the same. When you write a software program, you know the code. Therefore, you should know where every bug/problem is and eliminate it. If you don't eliminate the bugs, then you have no business allowing others to have access to your code because you are just wasting their time. If you don't where all the bugs are, then you have no business writing code. People who write software and release it hoping that other people will find and report bugs should be shot. I even found one app that had no documentation. I had to waste several days in trial and error just to figure out how to run the app. Can you believe that?

One of the most important rules in life is "never cause another person to go out of their way", but too many people on this planet don't live by that rule.

From what I have seen in the last 5 years, the state of Linux software is horrible with inconsitency, unreliablility and developer laziness/stupidity running rampant. I had never had an app crash on Windows 98, when I used it years ago, but the security on Windows leaves much to be desired. The security in Linux is awesome but the software (always from the repos) often sucks. And we wonder why people still use a Windows operating system?

I'm sorry, but I just cannot recommend Linux as an alternative to Windows any longer. And I won't be supporting Linux apps until the developers pull their heads out of their asses and learn the definition of the terms "reliable", "consistent" and "tested". It's a shame too because Linux can be so much better. I am no longer going to be dumping my hard earned money and valuable time into Linux. When these computers stop working for whatever reason, they'll be placed in the trash and that will be that.

By the way, I have never seen a bad support forum and ubuntuforums is the best of the best, in my opinion. You all are awesome.. keep up the great work. I am hoping that someone builds a decent OS someday that puts support forums out of business.
