### Post by peabody on 2006-08-23
The fact that your system is hanging is probably either due to the network or video drivers.  Drivers are typically the only thing that makes Linux crash.  To my knowledge, Intel on board video is pretty well supported, so It probably has to do with the wireless usb adapter.  I'll try and look into that model for you in just a sec.

Again it's a shame, but Linux compatible hardware often shapes the Linux experience.

As for your other comments:

There are a few reasons you see so many commandline solutions.  One reason is that their is a Unix core to Linux that'll never go away and many solutions to problem can always be solved via this Unix command line when the GUI fails.

Another good reasons for the commandline solutions you see in this support forum is because commandline solutions are straightforward for technical support.  Just copy and paste, you don't have to understand them.

I remember in 1996 when I was on a telephone technical support call for my computer (an old Packard Bell branded 486) and the tech support rep had me quit to DOS and enter commands.  I didn't have to describe my desktop, or my menus, or my dialog boxes, I just entered literally what he told me and it fixed the problem.  Later, when I became better with computers I discovered I could have fixed the problem from within Windows.  The fact that he had me use DOS was more straightforward for him.

Having gotten into computers as a career and given hours of support over the phone to Windows users, I can't tell you how many times I wished I was able to direct them to a command line rather than say "click on that icon that looks like an orange globe, or bunny, or blackboard, etc. etc. Okay, now what does the window say?  Can you look at the part that says connections settings...no I'm sure its there just keep looking.  You see it?  Okay good, now..."

In fact, I can get away with it from time to time.  When I need the user to find out their IP address settings in windows, I tell them to click start, select run, type "cmd" then enter, then type "ipconfig" in the "black box" and hit enter.  I find I have much more success with people this way then telling them to go through the network settings window.

That said, there admittidely needs to be more features accessible from the GUI for users to help themselves, and that is slowly taking place.  I've been a Linux user since 1999, and I can tell you its amazing how much is doable from the GUI these days.  It's a slow process and it only gets better over time.
