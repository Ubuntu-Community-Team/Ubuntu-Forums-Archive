### Post by GTKpower on 2005-04-28
An EXCELLENT system-wide font to use in Ubuntu is **Frutiger**.  Shows up very clearly, and is stylish without drawing too much attention to itself. 

Myriad Pro (of current Apple fame), is based on it, but isn't as good as Frutiger.  They are nearly identical, however (see link.)

[http://jeff.cs.mcgill.ca/~luc/MyriadFrutiger.pdf](http://jeff.cs.mcgill.ca/~luc/MyriadFrutiger.pdf)

Alternatively, I can also recommend Myriad Pro, but at a 1280x1024 res, it's too small at 10 points, and too large at 11.  But that's really a matter of taste.

I recommend the Postscript format for these, but if you can't come by that, .ttf or .otf should suffice.

Well said, Shakin, well said.

It's really none of my business how a person obtains this font.  I just showcased it for people to see.  I'm glad you like it, and that you understand where I'm coming from.  

Considering the fact that quite a few of the otherwise good-looking sans-serif fonts render quite poorly in GNOME, this font might be more important to some users.

Is it worth the price?  IMHO, yes, for these reasons:

1.)  You don't need the whole set for system-wide use.  The regular and bold faces should be enough. 

2.)  If you do get this font, it's yours forever, and depending on the license, whichever company you purchase it from might offer future support when a different version of that font comes out.  Right now we have PostScript (my favourite, .pfb and .afm for linux), TrueType (.tff), and OpenType (.otf.)  Postscript has been around forever (since before 1990, I think?), and TrueType for nearly as long.  Support for these versions should continue for a long time (as in, years.)  

I have fonts that I bought years ago, and I still use them, from OS to OS.  If they need tweaking in some way, there are programs out there that can do that, and convert between formats.  As far as I know, PostScript and TrueType can be converted to OpenType and vice-versa, although I've never tried this.

So, you make a one-time investment, if you like, and you'll have a font that can meet your needs for years to come.  You can install and use it from OS to OS.  With the functionality and customizability of Linux, this can go on indefinitely, as far as I can tell.

When I saw it was from Agfa-Monotype, I smiled - not surprising.  They've been known to produce some interesting knockoffs of famous, industry-leading fonts - and walk a VERY thin legal line while doing so.  Book Antiqua is one example:  it's a copy of Linotype's Palatino (Herman Zapf's creation), and is nearly identical.  Zapf was up in arms over this and purportedly quit the font industry for a while because of this.  Microsoft of course, had this font as standard in Office for years, without giving a sh*t about Herman Zapf.  Finally,  Monotype came to an agreement with Zapf, and as far as I can tell, Book Antiqua is no longer part of Microsoft's products.

Nothing will happen to you if you use Book Antiqua, of course.  It's freely available anywhere, basically.  Linotype's Palatino is slightly higher quality.  Adobe's is also very nice (my fave), and comes in different weights, including a very attractive Light. 

As for Segoe, I've heard of it before.  It is regarded as a Frutiger clone, and a pretty close one.  It was commissioned by Microsoft, and there's controversy over it, no surprise.  Microsoft, Agfa-Montype and Linotype are discussing the issue.  Either way, if it's a free font, it's a free font.  You, the user, will be fine.  Microsoft, Agfa-Mono and Lino can resolve their issues themselves.  

In any case, it won't make headlines, and it is definitely not a matter of public interest.

Bitstream, incidentally, also makes plenty of legal clones, alot of them free.
