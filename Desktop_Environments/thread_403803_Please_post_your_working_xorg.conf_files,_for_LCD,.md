### Post by calraith on 2007-04-07
You might also try the following steps:

[LIST=1]
[*]sudo killall -9 gdm
[*]Log out of your session.
[*]When you get logged, out, hit Ctrl-Alt-Backspace.
[*]Log into the console.
[*]sudo X -configure
[*]edit the xorg.conf file you just generated (nano -w xorg.*), and comment out HorizSync and VertRefresh in Section "Monitor."  Also make sure Section "Device" has Driver "nvidia" instead of "nv."
[*]sudo mv /etc/X11/xorg.conf /etc/X11/xorg.conf.borked
[*]sudo mv xorg.conf* /etc/X11/xorg.conf
[*]startx
[/LIST]

Commenting out the HorizSync and VertRefresh should make X detect it on startup and adjust its timings / refresh accordingly.  Something else interesting about X -configure is it doesn't bother inserting resolutions into the new conf file.  Without resolutions statically defined in xorg.conf, X automatically chooses the highest resolution capable by the monitor and the video adapter, even widescreen if appropriate.

If the newly generated xorg.conf works, then merge the FontPaths, mouse customizations, and whatever else is appropriate from the borked xorg.conf into the new one.

### Post by bcrowell on 2007-04-09
OK, thanks, everybody, for your help -- I finally figured it out.

My LCD screen has some front-panel controls that mimic a CRT's analog controls. You can adjust the size of the screen image, and also its sharpness. Well, obviously these are kind of silly things for an LCD to have, but there's a button prominently marked "AUTO," which you would think would set it to the best default settings, where the monitor wouldn't be doing any interpolation or filtering to try to pretend it's an analog device. Well no, the "AUTO" button gives me settings that are horribly non-optimal. To get things to look right, I have to play with those controls, and tune in the best looking image. On a screen full of text, I can actually see Moire patterns as I fiddle with the size control, and eventually when I home in on the optimum value, the Moire pattern goes away. On the sharpness control, what happens if you set it too high is that it's apparently doing some image processing, and it's the same kind of thing you see in Gimp when you try to sharpen an image too much -- you get artifacts surrounding any sharp edge. Well, that was exactly why all my fonts were messed up.

Now I just have to go back to Fry's and return the new video card I bought, which didn't do any good at all :-) (It did have the effect of making the drivers set the appropriate refresh rate of 60-75 Hz, but that wasn't really the problem at all, and it's not worth it to me to add the extra cost and electricity consumption to my system.)

Just thought I would point out (for future reference, or something...) that you're not actually making use of the Modeline in your xorg.conf. The entries I've highlighted have to match, so your first entry in the _Modes_ line should be [COLOR=Black]"1440x900@60". Having said that, if you're happy with your display without the Modeline, then leave xorg.conf alone. :)

[/COLOR]```
 Section "Monitor"
    Identifier    "Generic Monitor"
    Option        "DPMS"
    HorizSync    30-82
    VertRefresh    56-75
    Modeline [COLOR=Red]"1440x900@60"[/COLOR] 106.4 1440 1520 1672 1904 900 903 909 934 -Hsync +Vsync
EndSection

``````
     SubSection "Display"
        Depth        24
        Modes        [COLOR=Red]"1440x900"[/COLOR] "1400x1050" "1280x1024" "1024x768" "800x600" "640x480"
    EndSubSection

```[COLOR=Black]
