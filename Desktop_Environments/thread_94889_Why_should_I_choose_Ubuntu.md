### Post by redactech on 2005-11-25
Hello,

Don't make the switch let's create you a conversion strategy (I'm a good at that )

1. On your MAC are you using OpenOffice or still using MS-office ?
2. On your MAC do you use Gimp or photoshop ?
3. do  your children play games specific to Windows XP such as Counter-strike, 
4. How old are your children ?
5. Do you have a third party using this computer ? Neigbor ? girlfriend, wife, boyfriend room mate ?
6. What your children do with this computer ? What you want them to do ?
7. Are you going to use it ?
8. What are the main application installed on this computer ? (office ware and such)
9. Hardware (any special hardware) if you can list it..., dvd, HD, Soundcard, Webcam etc
10. What do you expect from linux

That said Ubuntu is very good for : absolute computer newbie and Hardcore "open everything so I can see how it works" kind of person

### Post by hw-tph on 2005-11-25
[LIST]
[*]The features you are likely to need are already there and require minimal configuration, if any: Networking in Windows networks or mixed networks, sound and graphics usually work as expected out of the box, MS Exchange support in Evolution - and so on.
[*]Maintenance is incredibly easy. If there are any new updates when you log in, you will be prompted for your password and review the updates before clicking the Install button.
[*]Good community support :D
[*]It is a Debian distribution which means the entire system is free and very thoroughly tested and unlikely to do bad things to your personal data. And running Debian gives you that nice warm fuzzy feeling in your gut.
[*]Language support is excellent, which is nice if you live in a multi-language country/community/family.
[*]Up to date software and high paced release cycle that is matched to that of Gnome (the default desktop environment) - 2 releases a year, roughly. This is what sets it apart the most from Debian Stable.
[/LIST]

### Post by David Marrs on 2005-11-25
[QUOTE=hw-tph][LIST]
[*]Up to date software and high paced release cycle that is matched to that of Gnome (the default desktop environment) - 2 releases a year, roughly. This is what sets it apart the most from Debian Stable.
[/LIST]
Håkan[/QUOTE]
This can be a mixed blessing. For an end-user such as myself, I'd rather have a system that I don't have to upgrade for a couple of years rather than one that I'd have to upgrade every 6 months. This isn't really practical with Ubuntu because, although your system is supported for 18 months, backports are dropped with the beginning of the new release so you're basically stuck with the same applications. Of course, you can install from source, but then you don't get the benefits of automatic package management and are moving into the realms of geek/hacker.

Personally, I'm still waiting for the perfect Linux distro that has a longer release cycle, that's binary-compatible with debian, is based around Gnome, is marketted towards home users and (most importantly) that I can *buy* in a nice shiny box with a nice shiny warranty.

The nearest thing to this so far is Linspire, but I don't like its KDE-ness and I'm not comfortable with CNR being proprietary. Maybe SuSE can show the way?

But since such a distro doesn't exist, Ubuntu is the next best thing for my laptop. To be honest, though, I think all the major user-centric distros are very good these days. I use Fedora on my desktop and find it great. I'd give Ubuntu the edge (just), but only because it's geared towards the home desktop.
