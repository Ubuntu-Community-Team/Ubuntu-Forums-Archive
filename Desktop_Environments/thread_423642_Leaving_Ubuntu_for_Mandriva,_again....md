### Post by grenier on 2007-04-27
Ok, fair enough. Let's go for the distro first.
Today I read a review about the latest Mandriva that sums it up nicely. Distros such as Gentoo, slack and so on are used by people who like to completely control their system through config files, command line and so. They like to get their hands dirty.
On the other end of the spectrum are distros of the Ubuntu's kind, which made for either newbie, or for people who like the way things are set up in such 'user friendly' (very relative term) environments.
Mandriva is neither. It sits somewhere in the middle, mainly because it has:
- GUI tools whose advanced parameters let you tweak a lot of things - for example that ability when you create users during install to select the id number. I no of no other that do so.
- If you do want/must edit config files, these tools won't rewrite over your changes. Again, I think the Mandriva admin and config tools are overall second to none. Considering they're open source, I don't understand why no other distro is using them (except PCLinuxOS, of course).
Of course, Ubuntu's being based on Debian, it's package system is the best. But honestly, the Mandriva's urpmi package manager isn't bad. I really mainly regret it being slower.

I hear people have been having hardware problems with Mandriva's latest batch, but since I had none of these... In the end, I think that what I don't like in the *buntus is the gnomish approach of dumbing things down to somekind of common lowest denominator (and that's true with even Kubuntu).

Talking about the environement itself, I'd say that the keyboard shortcut way of doing things isn't always the fastest - depending on many parameters.
To begin with and make things clear, I do use shortcuts within apps, but not with my desktop. When I have the wheel switch available, I find it faster.

First, I have a 15.4" wide screen, which mean the default gnome with both upper and lower panel really eats too much screen real estate for my confort. Furthermore, I found no way (including in gconf, I may have missed it) to have these panels go behind the apps. Of course, I put both on one side of the screen, but then it was either still having a part of my screen permanently gone, or wait forever on them (or it, with only one) to come out of masking - and that was with a delay of zero.
On the other hand, my Windowmaker environment has pretty big icons (56x56 on a 1200x800), but they are set up to have no priority over apps' windows, and so can be behind.

Second, I rely heavily on virtual desktops, almost never having more than one app running on one desktop. that mean I always have a thin but large fringe of desktop either on the bottom or on a side of the app. It is really thin, but because of the principle that screen borders are infinite for all intent and purpose if you need to go there to click,I really don't have to think or pause to wheel-switch desktops that way.

Third, I have never found a way to duplicate the convenience the clipper gives me. If you're not familiar with it, I'll detail the WM desktop a bit. Basically, you first have the Dock, where icons for apps are. Initially, these icons are the launch buttons, but once lauched they become the app reference (like the bottom apps' panel in default Gnome). An interesting thing is that such reference icons appear for all launched application, at the bottom of the screen by default. These and the dock are on all desktops, and if you want to make a launcher for an app in the dock you just have to drag it from the bottom of the screen to the dock, and that's it.
The clipper is similar to the dock, except for one specific thing: apps lauchers attached to it only appear in the virtual desktop it was done in.
As far as I'm aware, only WM's clipper has this feature.

Since I've configured it to have a couple of desktop more than the number of applications I usually run, having to look for the desktop background to access the menu is never an issue.
Because of what I've just detailed, I honestly think no other environment has been written with such a multi-desktop in mind.

To get back on the distros side of things, the unfortunate truth is that developement on WM has mostly stopped, as far as I'm aware (cursed be Gnome and KDE...:p). BUT, a few years back, Mandriva (then Mandrake) had a survey with its users as to which environment they prefered. It turns out that, at the time, WM was in third or fourth position after KDE and Gnome. Consequently, they've always integrated it in the distro (it doesn't take much room anyway), and they've always made sure it really is a part of it (which, in WM case, is no big deal as it mostly mean having the menu application/daemon/whatever make necessary changes to the Windowmaker menu when needed).
