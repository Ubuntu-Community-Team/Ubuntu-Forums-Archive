### Post by kevinlyfellow on 2007-08-14
Before I started using linux, I had heard that KDE was better.  Then I tried KDE, and boy was I disappointed.  Part of it was that I thought it looked awkward, it was slower, and it was cluttered with largely useless options.  I also think qt simply does not look as nice as gtk.  Gnome offers just about everything I need so why use something I think is ugly?  I know, kde can be configured to look good, but why not just use gnome and have it look good from the start?  KDE kinda reminds me of Netscape Communicator for some reason.

Gnome has its downsides.  Many kde users like to say, "Gnome devs thinks that its users are stupid, therefore they don't include options."  That's simply not true, gnome devs actually are just ridiculously stubborn and expect its users to conform to their ideas.  This is largely ok, since they have some great interface designs that work out.  However, it can be infuriating when they get it wrong, because there's nothing you can do to change their minds.  Some examples of things wrong with gnome:

gnome-screensaver: a great idea, screensavers should follow themes.  So they made the change and expected users to swallow it.  But they forgot a very important feature, something to help make those themes.  What once was a simple process is now complicated, and I for one have stopped using screensavers because of this usability issue.  Devs won't fix this issue.

open/save dialogs:  they suck.  They are never the right sizes, they don't offer previews of pics when you need it to, you can't do simple file management in it, there is a nearly useless sidebar that accompanies it, user is forced to use bread crumbs and not a location bar with autocomplete..........  Devs won't fix this issue.

Configuration files:  They are a mess, and are ugly and are thrown about everywhere.  A graphical editor is almost needed for anything in gconf.

gnome-sessions-manager:  I can compile my own kernel, I know how to install my win modems, and I'm not scared of getting wireless setup.  But something about gnome-sessions-manager makes me scared and confused.  That's ok, I'll just open a terminal and use vim to edit some config files.  Hmmm where are those config files?  ~/.gnome2/sessions ?  Wrong!  Let's just try and grep for this line I know is in the config file...

nautilus:  I use thunar.  Why nautilus takes 20+ seconds to open up /usr/bin I don't know, I don't care.  Thunar has its faults, but not nearly as bad as that.  Devs are aware of this issue, and I expect that they will be working to fix it.
