### Post by mark2741 on 2006-09-12
About a year ago I got fed up with Windows and bought a Mac Mini. Although expensive (considering what you get, hardware wise), I liked Mac OS X a lot but it still had its share of issues and more discouraging to me was that the Mini was kind of slow (about similar to my p4 1.7ghz machine that I bought about 5 years ago, though slightly less responsive) and of course right after I bought my Mini the rumours started flying that a new Mini would be announced. So I ebayed it for more than I paid for it (amazing what apple stuff goes for). Anyways, the one app I couldn't find a suitable replacement for on the Mac was Money, and Quicken for the Mac is horrible. Quicken for the PC was pretty good, but not as good as Money.

I've been using Ubuntu now for about 7 months and the first 3 months I used VMWare to boot into an instance of Win XP just to run Money. But that got old after a while, since I liked to check my transactions nightly. I tried EVERY program available on linux. Probably the best option was MoneyDance but I never could get used to it's UI and it never handled my imported transactions correctly. I'm sure with some time I could get used to it.

In the end, I'm now using an openoffice spreadsheet and my bank's online bill paying service, and just using their website to check balances and history. Gnucash has the ofx/direct-connect feature now, which I always felt was very important, but I'm still not impressed. A simple, easy to use personal finance app that connects to my bank for transaction data and bill paying is just not available for anything other than Windows, unfortunately. 

I'm close to beginning designing and developing my own java-based personal finance app that will meet my simple needs - tracking bills and payments and some other simple stuff, in a clean, slick UI and none of that 'memorized transactions' crap. But for now, a spreadsheet and my bank's website is really the only option for me. YMMV.
