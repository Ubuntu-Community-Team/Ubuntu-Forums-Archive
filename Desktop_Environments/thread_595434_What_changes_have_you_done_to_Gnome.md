### Post by ofb on 2007-10-28
Well, obviously the first change is a single stripped-down toolbar (sorry, "panel") across the bottom. The great thing about Gnome panels is they are very easy to reconfigure to taste.

Re your experience, may I suggest trying Konqueror? A quick F4 to launch a terminal in the directory you're viewing, and a very handy image-thumbnail feature when you hover over an item.

Aside from default actions, Konqueror is highly configurable, and it's worth taking some time to test the range of that to make a fine fit for yourself.

For more than casual glancing at images, I tend to fire up GQview. 

Thunar is perhaps worth looking at too. They were doing a good job with default thumb view last time I checked it out.

### Post by ofb on 2007-10-28
> **dansku said:**
> how do i set to have a ingle stripped-down toolbar ?

Right-click a blank portion of a toolbar and select Help. That'll give you a full run-down of what can be configured.

Or you can just start experimenting with right-click. Keep in mind there is no 'revert' function, so try not to get lost while experimenting.

What you might do for caution, is create a new toolbar and configure it to taste before deleting the others.

### Post by ofb on 2007-10-29
Different method, same thing. If I was starting now, I'd be interested in the Deskbar Applet. But I started with card catalogs, and have the sort of memory that can flip open a book after thirty years and find the page I want. My setup is definitely all about keeping things at the fingertips, just like yours.

One thing you don't see is my local homepage, which is a personal rat nest of links and search forms. Slap ctrl-n, spacebar, (search_term), enter, and I'm in results. Insert a few 'tab' for the other 11 forms. Don't have to reach for the mouse - an almost trivial pause - until I'm pausing to read those results. There's a few dozen little things like that picked up since switching to the modern GUI in '95. The Deskbar Applet sort of puts those in one convenient spot, but I'm already doing those things unconciously as I go along, like assembling coffee in the morning.

And I mouse on the left. Other than being more comfortable and freeing up the right side for the wacom, having the mouse in the left hand while the right works the numpad, arrows, & the Home, Del, etc, is worth it. (Doug Englebart's chording keypad is intriguing though. Still mean to try that.) Change my physical half of the interface, like work from a laptop, and helpers like the Deskbar Applet become more interesting.

So that's a big part of speed, and my familiar hierarchical filesystem is the other.

It's sort of like people who didn't throw out the abacus when adding machines came along - ignoring the downtime to learn the new device, it wasn't actually faster for the already-skilled. But it's worth adding that slide-rules vanished overnight when we got calculators. Some improvements are a real jump, and those are the ones I switch to. Having Workspaces in Linux is that sort of thing.
