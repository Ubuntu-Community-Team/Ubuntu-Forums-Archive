### Post by ian-weisser on 2015-08-24
For my young children, I didn't add any software. I did change a few rules around the house. How you introduce kids to video games, computer activities, and internet entertainment should reflect how you as a parent gauge their needs.

I didn't teach my young kids how to type URLs. Instead, I set up browser bookmark-bar bookmarks for their game and puzzle sites.

As kids got older and learned how to type URLs and use search engines, the sharing of videos has been strongly encouraged. I find most of them juvenile and boring (well, of course!), but I *know* what they are seeing and doing, and they are happy that I seem to care. They watch some of my (boring) videos, too. The idea is that they learn internet use to be a group or social activity that includes the whole family, not solitary.

In my house, kids use computers and pads in the public living room.  Never in their closed rooms. I obey the same rule; it's fair that way, and my kids respond to fairness. My spouse is the only exception - nobody minds if Mom gets a perk.

Siblings are very good to help monitor time limits for shared pads and desktops. Again, they enforce fairness...and good practice reading clocks. If siblings aren't around, I monitor time.

Of course, that's how I raise *my* kids. My methods work for my kids. Your own kids and methods will surely differ. Whatever meets your kids needs makes you a good parent.

### Post by bigbadsubaru on 2015-08-25
I have a spare system with no hard drive that the kids my wife watches can use (with her supervision, of course), it just has a Live CD with Ubuntu on it that it boots from. If they need to save anything (The only one who uses it regularly is 11) they can save it to a flash drive, but if anything gets messed up it just gets rebooted. It doesn't have parental controls on it or anything (I could have set those up but chose not to) but it never gets used without someone there to monitor its use, and the router is set to block certain sites. 

Something else that I've considered is having the system installed normally, but have GRUB installed on a flash drive (with no boot sector on the hard drive), system won't boot without the USB key or a proper boot disc in the DVD drive, much easier to just take the USB stick when you don't want them to have computer time vs lugging the computer out of wherever it is, taking the power cord, etc... Microsoft's controls let you specify when a "child" user account is allowed to log in, but I am not sure if there is an equivalent control on Linux. As for Internet access, most routers (as well as dd-WRT, etc..) will let you restrict network access during certain times by the MAC address of the system.
