### Post by DougInKY on 2005-01-01
Hello georgetoon. I understand your fears of doing a install (as do most here on the list) as we all were pretty much there at least once in our lives. The best suggestion I can offer regarding learning to install Linux (any distro) is to contact a local LUG (Linux User Group).

Now, if you are like me and don't have one handy you can do it the hard way like I did and read read read. Then try it on a spare hard drive. They are pretty cheap nowdays especially if you can find a used 6-10 gig critter laying about. I have been given a couple of these drives for the asking.

As to Ubuntu vs Mandrake I can speak to that as I have been a Mandrake user since they forked from Red Hat. I have just made the move to Ubuntu and don't think I will move back although I still use Mandrake 9.2 on my laptop as (so far) no one has been able to offer me a way of getting sound on the newer 2.6 kernels on my old hardware.

I also thought I was a die-hard KDE user (loved the eye candy) but have found that the Gnome Human interface has really grown on me. I am getting to where it seems odd to use KDE on my laptop and I am even considering moving to the Gnome desktop on my laptop (Mandrake does make that easy).

In short, Mandrake makes a great starter system (or one for the person that just wants things to work with no or little hassle). Ubuntu (being Debian) has the wonderful apt (and Synaptic) systems for updating and they are better imho than Mandrakes urpm. 

I might suggest a good starter book as well, maybe something like O'Reilly's book Running Linux by Matt Welsh (and others). This book is good enough that I have bought the last three editions of it. It is not biased toward any single distro and will give you a solid grounding in the basics of Linux.

I have not discussed the politics (and philosophy) behind Ubuntu versus Mandrake. Free (as in freedom not as in beer) software appeals to the old hippy in me that still lurks not too deep beneath the surface. You will have to decide if using truly free software is a issue for you. Many here who are much more articulate than I can help guide you. All you have to do is ask.

Feel free to write me anytime. I am retired and you will find that my ideas are worth exactly what you are paying for them - not much. But I am generally of many opinions some of which may even be right. My son tells me I have gotten a lot smarter while he was in college.

### Post by HankB on 2005-01-01
I've tried Mandrake, Fedora Core and have been a Debian user for years. My recent foray into Ubuntu was mostly because of difficulties installing Debian on an AMD64 system.

Mandrake is a very good distro and if I were picking something for a first time user, it would be high on my list. Mandrakes weak spot in my experience was problems with updates and upgrades. I like to stay current so the first thing I did (after installing MDK 9.1) was to try to update using their update tool urpmi. I picked out a near by repository and ran the update command and it failed with unintelligible error messages. I went to their forum seeking help to resolve the problem and was told to keep trying other repositories until I found one that had the files I needed =8-O I did that and also found that apt had been ported to RPM so I installed that and used it to keep my system up to date. When 9.2 came out, I tried to update using apt and that simply didn't work. I reverted to urpmi and the update was done but with a lot of help solving non-obvious problems with help again from the forum. With MDK there is no real upgrade path.

Debian, on the other hand, provides a great upgrade path via apt. I installed Progeny (another Debiuan derivative) in a laptop some years ago and it still remains current without having to reinstall. There are benefits to a complete reinstall however, but Debian lets you choose when to do that. But I have to add that the inside joke is that debian *has* to provide a good upgrade facility because there install is so tough. they recognize that and working hard on the installer, though it is not as good as Mandrakes or even Ubuntu's.

As far as Ubunbtu pre-installed on a hard drive... How much would you be willing to pay? :) :) :) Seriously, there might be some issues with that because I don't know how much the install results depend on hardware sensing. Even a preinstalled OS will require some configuration. Linux has a strong community and if you ask for help, you will probably get it. If you're concerned about repartitioning your hard drive, try a commercial product like Partition Magic (and like they say, back up your data first!)

good luck,
hank
