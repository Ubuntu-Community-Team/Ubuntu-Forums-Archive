A webpage that you open may consist of elements from several domains. The main domain (e.g. yahoo.com) contains all the "useful" info that you read (e.g. news headlines) whereas the other domains are usually ad domains (like doubleclick.net). True, most people set firefox to "accept third party cookies", and that's how these cookies get installed in the system. These cookies store some information that one can utilize to analyze your browsing habits, thereby enlarging the users' interest database for the ad-companies. That's the main purpose of domains like doubleclick.net, mediaclick.net, fastclick.net, and the like.

A good way of blocking all these ad-servers and other stuff is to block them using your hosts file. A tutorial (for windows) goes [here]("http://www.mvps.org/winhelp2002/hosts.htm").You can download the same hosts file from there, and append its data (by copying and pasting in a text editor) to your /etc/hosts file. This will block most unwanted ads, spyware, and  whatever that comes with it. Newbies should read [this.]("http://www.ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=110440")

Conclusion. Linux is not Windows.

*I hesitate posting this. I don't feel comfortable being the bearer of "gloom and doom". Only as a cautionary am I impelled to share the following...*

_Hypothetically_: Yes. Spyware for Linux can be crafted. No OS can be completely immune from such maligned programming.

Solution: Download from reputable repositories that offer source code as well as binaries. Download programs authored by a **team** of programmers.

_Experientially_: Yes. I'm sad to report that in this past year I've caught my boxen engaged in network activity not initiated by me. OK, I admit, my definition of "spyware" may be a bit broad from my perspective.

Case 1: I installed IPCop as a software firewall. All appeared "cool" until I caught my firewall communicating with its originators. A little utility within the firewall package was playing "ET" and calling home regularly. To do what? To download an icon for its GUI. Is that all? Beats me. I didn't ask to download a graphic from **within the depths** of my firewall that could just have easily been resident on my local machine. Why call home? What else was "ET" talking about when connected to the big IPCop in the sky! Unsolicited traffic **from** my box constitutes "spyware".

Solution: Remove IPCop and use the CD as a coffee mug coaster.

Case 2: I installed Tor and Privoxy to experience what all the hoopla was about anonymous browsing. It worked as claimed... and then some. Lo and behold, I walked in to use my Ubuntu box (just last week) to find my machine chatting away with whomever. I captured the packets with Ethereal and discovered  where Tor had set up camp to offer me as a proxy to .... someone out there in web-land. Ironic when you come to think of it. When I killed the Tor daemon all activity ceased.

Solution: Tor was removed from my system and has become a four letter word in my vocabulary. That's a four letter word, T-O-R, as in "spyware". The official Tor and its brilliant and benevolent creators are to be commended for a fine program, but my Tor binary was corrupted with its default scripts as found out-of-the-box (through the Internet).

No, we are certainly not as vulnerable as the microsoft community, but please be on guard. All is possible. As noble and open as the Linux community is, there's infrequently a bad apple in the barrel. I have met with some.

Check yourself with a Nessus scan for ports **you** never opened, check your Snort or IDS logs, and run Clamscan regularly.

(A note to those who take time to read their IDS logs: Isn't it disconcerting to count the daily number of attempts of infiltration into your system. There's a lot of hacking going on out there! With as much of that kind of activity as I see, there's certainly a possibility of a stealthly-placed trojan or "spyware" walking right through an open door and calling your Linux box "home.")

(Additional note: I'm relatively new to the Linux community, a veteran of only 7 years and yes, I have been hacked once through my own sloppy setup of ntfs.)

Bottom line: Fellow Linux users. Don't be complacent about perceived "immunity" from network vulnerabilty.
