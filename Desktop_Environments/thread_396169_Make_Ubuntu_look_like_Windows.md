### Post by Neobuntu on 2007-03-29
I'm not trying to change your personal choice but KDE has been much easier(finer control)  for me to make it look any way one wants. 

Currently I like the Baghira KDE style but I do not set it exactly like OS-X as I now like a custom mixture. The Aquativo Gnome GTK engine controls/style match well with KDE's Baghira; set to "milk". Therefore all my programs work and are unified in appearance.

BUT I have (a while back) set KDE to look (99%)EXACTLY like XP. Perhaps close is good enough for you users but if you really want to blow minds, KDE set to XP is the deal.

Now this is NOT the "sincerest form of flattery". It just speaks volumes about the far greater flexibility of open software. I do not use an XP style and my OS-X like look does NOT mean I yern for Macs. I simply like some of the style elements and no one can dictate my choice.

Also, I do not believe that open software has to be everything different from Windows. Just the bad stuff, should be avoided. We are mainly talking about looks here, but that doesn't mean looks do not matter. 

The problem area is the task bar. I had used a found XP set for KDE. It included a XP task bar and it worked BUT you will find life more stable and more flexible with the KDE taskbar instead. Perhaps it would be better to use a custom background with the KDE taskbar.

Point being, one can get about 90% there just by quickly installing certain elements. Then a few more custom tweaks makes it very hard to tell the difference.

All this should clue you in, Kubuntu is the most flexible and can be made to not only look, but WORK in any way you see fit AND, it's easy to do. Therefore, one wouldn't choose a user interface only for it's default look (Kubuntu's is great as default, no time needed) but for what it can do.

### Post by Lary Grant on 2007-05-30
At the risk of going a bit off-topic...

I know a lot of people who use Linux for their own machine but the rest of the family uses 1 or more Windows machines, just because they don't want to change.  They feel they have the right to do that because it's "the spouse's machine" or the "the kids machine".

However, I disagree.  Unless the people who use that machine are prepared to fix all its problems, keep it updated, install whatever is needed, etc. all on their own, it isn't fair that they get to make the choice, based solely on unwillingness to change.

After one too many bailout jobs I had to do on the "wife and kids'" Windows ME machine, I just got fed up and unilaterally installed Ubuntu on it one night (I left the Windows partition just in case, but removed it from the boot menu).  They were not happy at first but they got used to it fairly quickly.

I know it seems harsh and domineering but I have use Windows at work all day, and I don't want to have to look at it at home.  At first they pleaded with me to put Windows back, promising they wouldn't ask for any help, but I know that's a red herring, because when they eventually mess it up, it will be more painful for me to tolerate a messed up machine than to just fix it, so I will end up fixing Windows.  I think a fair deal is... if they can manage to figure out how to boot to the Windows partition on their own, then that shows they are prepared to maintain the machine.  So far that hasn't happened.
