### Post by SeijiSensei on 2015-04-27
The reality is that the segment of the Linux market that worries about the issues that bother the FSF is pretty tiny.  How many people do you think refuse to watch DVDs on Ubuntu because installing ubuntu-restricted-extras violates their ideological concerns about "purity?"  How about people who choose to use proprietary drivers from NVIDIA or ATI because they want better graphics performance?  Becoming the "most popular Free/GNU Linux distribution" would hurt Ubuntu's acceptance among the much broader community of users who just want things to work as expected.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-04-28
Distributions do exist that comply with the strict guidelines of the FSF, and hardly anyone uses them. Why?  Because they are less usable.

Free software advocacy has been around since the 1980's.  The percentage of computer users who make their computing choices based on their adherence to that ideology has always been small and marginal, and, I suspect, pretty constant over the years. 

Much of the reason for its lack of success is, I think, the movement's poor understanding of what mainstream non-ideoligically motivated people -- the rest of the world -- want to do. For most of us, a computing device is a communication and entertainment device.  We want to watch videos, listen to music, talk with people, use Facebook, etc.  We do not want to run emacs, mutt, and a tiling window manager.
