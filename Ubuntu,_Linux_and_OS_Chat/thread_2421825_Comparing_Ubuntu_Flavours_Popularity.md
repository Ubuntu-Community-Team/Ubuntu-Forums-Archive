### Post by Autodave on 2019-06-27
First of all, you are comparing a short term release with a long term release (LTS).  Understand the the LTS releases (which come out every 2 years) are supported for at least 5 years.  The short term ones only for 9 months from release date.  That means the the short term ones need to be upgraded every 6 months.  Upgrading from one release to the next OFTEN has problems and I personally do not recommend it: stick with the LTS releases.

Next, there is the issue of what will work well on your machine.  Older o less powerful machines do much better with the lighter versions such as Mate, Xubuntu, Lubuntu.  Of course, lighter means simpler desktops.  If you just have to have a fancy desktop, then Ubuntu will most likely be your choice.  Personally, I prefer something a little lighter (Xubuntu) because I don't care to sit and look at my desktop all day: it is just a means of getting to the programs that I want to use.

### Post by QIII on 2019-06-27
The Distrowatch figures are utterly useless as a measure of distro popularity.  They count only clicks on the various distros' links *on Distrowatch*.

From [their own site]("https://distrowatch.com/dwres.php?resource=popularity"):

> The DistroWatch Page Hit Ranking statistics are a light-hearted way of measuring the popularity of Linux distributions and other free operating systems among the visitors of this website. They correlate neither to usage nor to quality and should not be used to measure the market share of distributions. They simply show the number of times a distribution page on DistroWatch.com was accessed each day, nothing more.
