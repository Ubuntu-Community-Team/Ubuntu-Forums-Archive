### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-12-10
Sometimes you have to go where the money is. I have no experience of Microsoft products to share with you. Only a general principle or two.

1) All computer operating systems have to do the same things.
2) Applications have to do similar things whatever OS they are written to run on.
3) We all know more words than we actually use in day to day conversation. Likewise, people only use a small set of the functions available in any application.

So, my advice would be to think about the day to day tasks that your clients do and learn how they do them. Try to imagine how the user could mess up and then workout how to correct it. 

Knowledge is valuable but the knowledge of how to learn is the most valuable of all.

Regards.

### Post by mystics on 2015-12-10
Here's some of Microsoft's resources regarding Microsoft Office: [https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/learning/office-training.aspx](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/learning/office-training.aspx)

And here's a decent rundown of what you can expect in Windows 10: [http://www.gcflearnfree.org/windows10](http://www.gcflearnfree.org/windows10)

It also might be possible that there are local classes that teach the basics of Windows and Microsoft Office. Just be warned that they'll likely be based on older versions of Windows (though most seem to use at least Windows 7) and Microsoft Office (no guarantees it will be newer than Office 2007). Also, their introduction to Windows sometimes assumes no knowledge of any OS. So if you do want to take a class, be sure to do your research on it beforehand to make sure you'll learn something useful.

Beyond that, it would be very hard to really go in-depth with advice. Windows is massive, and any advice we can give without having something specific to target would be a waste of time for you. My advice, beyond some helpful tutorials and the documentation in Windows itself would be to play around, and if you have questions, ask about them.

> **mikodo said:**

 To be honest, I am a little fearful of this as, I didn't have a very good experience with MS those few months.

That goes without saying. No one, not even the most die-hard Windows fanatic, liked Vista. If someone says they liked Vista, they're either lying or crazy. At best they didn't mind it as much as other people, and all of those people certainly came to the party late.

Comparatively, the three major version of Windows in use now (Windows 7, Windows 8.1, and Windows 10) are, from what I've heard from friends and read online, been very well-received (forums designed for people with problems notwithstanding). My own experience with Windows 7 has been mostly positive, and Windows 10 is arguably the strongest version I've used so far (relatively to the amount of time its been out).

In short, even if you don't like Windows, it will almost certainly be a better experience than what you had with Vista.

### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2015-12-10
Use of MS Windows at a company, govt, or similar is fortunately a lot better experience than a consumer only environment.    Business secures it better, removes the crapware, removes the ability of every Tom, **** & Harry advising of various updates to apps or devices.   Typically, this is because all software is installed by admins from centralized images and repositories (much like Linux).   And the security layer is much better, almost as good as consumer grade Linux.

The only downside I can think of is you'll still have to deal with MS's specific "blueprint" of what each app looks like and how it works  . . . (the "ribbon" in MS Office Word or Excel for instance).   So, some learning curve there, but tolerable.

So, on the "Job" - - MS OS's are managed a lot different than the average consumer schmuck is capable of (or even aware of).
