### Post by Perfect Storm on 2020-05-24
Hello,

Coming from elementary OS to Ubuntu Budgie have been an easy transaction. Both Os's have many similarities on the outside. One big plus and that turned me to Ubuntu Budgie is that it's very flexible, where elementary are very strict. I had to hack the panel in elementary to bend it to my will. But with Ubuntu Budgie it takes no more but than a click or two. Though I do miss the elementary OS specific made apps in their apps center. There only been two minor downside with Ubuntu Budgie, 1) You can't change the menu to grid/row in category view, 2) The persistent use of snap when you want to download something from the app center, I had to install synaptic to get from the repositories. But again it's minor things.
Ubuntu Budgie is snappy and fast and it do what you tell it to do, PLUS you can customize it big time.
One happy customer from Denmark.


regards

### Post by MScapee on 2020-10-09
Love Budgie. I'd been using Mint/MATE since version 17.2 but I couldn't continue with Mint after being exposed to Ubuntu Budgie. Following is a quick screencast of my desktop which I made a couple of days ago to upload to the Ubuntu Budgie forum for a "show your desktop" post (BTW, this video will self-destruct about mid January 2021 as I'm on the "free" plan). The video mostly just shows my populating the workspaces with the help of Shuffler. For what it's worth, I like to use background images that are somewhat desaturated in order to make all of the desktop elements kind of jump out at ya. I desaturated this particular image in Gimp but the left face of the tower was left fully saturated which enhances the appearance of sunrise (or sunset). The other thing I did was to choose an unconventional font ("Chilanka") to give the desktop a bit more personality. It displays almost everywhere except in the text editor, the terminal and the icons that reside on the desktop. I originally tried using "Purisa" for this but it takes up way too much vertical space... BTW, the hardware that I'm running on here is based on the low-end Celeron J1900 which was introduced about 2014. As you can see, it's quite snappy considering that the motherboard consumes around 10 watts.
