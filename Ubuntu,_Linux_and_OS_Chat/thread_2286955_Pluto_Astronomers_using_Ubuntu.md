### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2015-07-16
When I worked for NASA back in the 1970's many of the computers used various implementations of UNIX. It doesn't surprise me at all that they use various Linux distros today. 

As far as the satellites, probes, and spacecraft, NASA needs systems that are reliable, and don't have buggy new versions of an OS. Once they send something into space they can't retrieve it easily, and remote updates are risky. That is why some of the systems on the ISS still use Windows XP; although, they are getting rid of them as they can.

Exactly, reliability is a key word for NASA, it is usual to use components with an extensive base of behavior data, problems and solutions, to put in space equipment.
For example, the next-generation spacecraft Orion - which is expected to take one day human beings to Mars - is controlled by an IBM processor manufactured in 2002. The engineers essentially seek reliability and not other aspects that can be found in newer processors.
Anther example of this policy is the New Horizons probe that reached Pluto this week, which has a [MIPS R3000A CPU]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R3000"), the the same that was used in the Playstation PS1.
