### Post by Peripheral Visionary on 2013-07-05
I don't really think the desktop computer is really on it's way to being *totally* replaced by mobile devices.  For many applications, the mobile stuff will dominate, but people still produce literature, art, music, architechtural drawings, games, and all that on real honest-to-goodness computers.  You probably could write your next symphony or novel on an Android phone someday, but the desktop home computer still has a big role to play and is probably still expanding.

As for all the DEs and desktop eye candy, well that's just recreation for geeks and tinkerers and experimenters.  Thank God for those geenks and tinkerers though, for making the desktop more than just a tool, but keeping it interesting and providing options to fit every taste.  Doing my homework would be a lot less productive if I had to type it all on my Android phone, and a lot less fun if I couldn't stop once in awhile to open a chat or watch a video on the same device I'm doing my school work on.  

The home desktop is here to stay, but the interface will always be evolving!  Maybe keyboards and mice will go away and we'll all be doing voice commands and physical gestures and heads-up display, but home computers and work computers, I think, are likely to be merged in the cloud, and privacy will be available only to the completely disconnected, totally off-the-grid, living-off-the-land.

### Post by Peripheral Visionary on 2013-07-05
> **deri said:**
> Linux produces very often new versions of itself even the earlier versions have known bugs. Is this because of laziness of developers or why we are pushing new versions out even there are unfixed bugs?

No, not *Linux*, just certain Linux *distributions* that are all about experimentation and exploring the outer edges.  If you want a Linux that is stable rather than experimental, do what a lot of us do and either stick to the LTS releases of your favorite Ubuntu flavor (I love Xubuntu!), or choose a stable Linux distro like Debian Stable or CentOS (or one built on Debian Stable, like SimplyMepis).  Many of them have *very* long term support but still get security updates and later versions of great software.

There is just so much choice in Linux that you can probably find anything you want no matter what.
