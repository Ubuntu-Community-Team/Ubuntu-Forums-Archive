[SIZE=3][SIZE=5]What is Venux?[/SIZE]
[SIZE=2]
[/SIZE][SIZE=2]Venux is the name given to a new Ubuntu based Linux distribution.  I aim to convert Windows users who don't fully understand that there definitely can be a free, easy to use alternative to their current limited system.  One of the best things about Linux distributions is that you can configure every little aspect of them so that they can run how you want them to.
[/SIZE]
[SIZE=4][SIZE=5]What are our core values?
[/SIZE][/SIZE]
[SIZE=2]First: to spread the word of open-source further than its current reach.  I love open-source software because it gives you more control, and allows programs to greatly exceed the initial design.  By allowing other to make changes and redistribute your software, there is the potential for it to become truly amazing, whereas with proprietary software, that possibility is limited to its initial developers, and what they, themselves, can do.

Second: Computing doesn't need to be difficult.  An operating system should look and feel amazing right out of the box.  It should give you the most amazing software and experience, without you having to configure one thing.  We are pre-configuring a lot of software to make an amazing computing experience.  We're finding some of the best media software, document editing, image editing, you name it.  And Wine and Playonlinux are configured right from the get-go.
[/SIZE]
[SIZE=2]Third: Offer users a familiar layout.  Now this part was a little difficult because a lot of past Linux users like gnome2, yet a lot of people who may make the switch from Windows might not feel comfortable using that layout.  Because of this, I felt it would be difficult to build one single OS and expect that to work.  So, Venux OS is being built on the MATE desktop environment, while another version will be released using Cinnamon (modified, to make them even more comfortable).  The Cinnamon version is called Venux Starter Edition: "So easy my grandma can use it!", and internally referred to as Windex (Cleaning up windows).
[/SIZE]
[SIZE=5]What do we need?
[/SIZE]
[SIZE=2]We need people who are just as passionate as us about this.  People who want to make a difference and bring Linux to a whole new audience.  I am a web developer (check out my unfinished portfolio site at [http://nickgermaine.ca](http://nickgermaine.ca)) and also write in PHP/MySQL and along this journey I have been learning Python/GTK so that I can write certain things to include.  I am by no means a python programmer, but it is not the most difficult thing I have ever done.  I'm looking for people of all sorts who'd like to be a member of what very well can be a game changer in computing.
[/SIZE][SIZE=2]
You can come interact with us on the facebook page I've just created not too long ago: [http://facebook.com/venuxos](http://facebook.com/venuxos) and if you'd like to help out, just let me know.[/SIZE]

### Post by 1clue on 2013-09-17
FWIW I don't think people have trouble with Linux being a different look.

All through the years there has been at least one group who has made a theme to look like Windows of any flavor, Mac of any flavor, Amiga, whatever.

I don't recall anyone on any forum saying that they tried Linux because of these looks.  If anything, they complain about the things that are different in the slightest way at that point.

If Windows (or Mac or whatever) is the "golden standard" then what you've done is tell the world who you're trying to beat and declare your project to be second best.  At that point, you have to keep up and be as close as possible to that standard which is just plain impossible unless the golden standard is static, which it will never be.

People didn't have a hard time getting used to an iPhone or Android.  Those were significantly different from what was available on any computer at the time they came out.

People have a hard time figuring out any of these things:
[LIST=1]
[*]Something that is overly complicated.
[*]Something that is cryptic.  (Command line system for example)
[*]Something that they don't want to learn.  For example, trying to get my parents to use a computer when they had no desire or reason.
[*]Something that has no visible benefit for them.
[/LIST]

If you give an argument, then people are automatically going to take the other side:
[LIST=1]
[*]Free?  According to the average computer user, Windows is free.  Comes with the system.
[*]Easy to install?  Windows and Mac are already installed.  Nothing is easier than that.
[*]Free software?  Most of the software apps that people would recognize as apps are available on Windows too.
[/LIST]

My wife had a Vista laptop.  She got viruses on it from Facebook regularly.  Every 3 days or so I'd have to scrape them off, but since it's facebook and the viruses were there when I met her, I had no idea how to get them completely gone.  The laptop was a bit old and I didn't want to spend money on an antivirus.

I made an account for her on my Xubuntu box.  I used Linux for everything at home, happily and easily doing the things I do.  When her computer was incapacitated and I was at work, she would log into my Linux box and go do the things she does.  A few months later, she asked me to put Ubuntu on her laptop.

She's a native Spanish speaker, and Linux's internationalization is spotty, so she kept getting messages from the system that made no sense to her.  Another problem is Skype regularly lost its settings for the webcam and microphone.  Eventually we got her a Windows 8 laptop, which seems to be doing the job for her.  But it wasn't complexity that scared her away from Linux, it was the spotty internationalization and drivers.  I know several other people who use Linux and who use English as a second language.  They all use English on Linux, because everything in English is better than half in whatever their native language is.

I guess that's off topic.  The way to promote Linux/Ubuntu/whatever is to show it being used and working well.  Not in a "Look at me, I'm using UBUNTU!!!!" way, but just using it.  In my opinion it makes more sense to have it obviously NOT something they know.  People watch you do your thing, then eventually they ask questions.  Answer them without it taking an hour of their time.

IMO the default configuration should be:
[LIST=1]
[*]Easy access to configuration and basic apps.
[*]No unnecessary noise.  No crazy screen effects.  No irritating animations.
[*]Easy to figure out how to use.
[*]Enough apps apparent to be obviously able to get things done.
[*]Easy access to an application installer.
[/LIST]

Quick animations like half-second window zooming or window shade is fine, or screen flips from one workspace to another.  The cube is NOT OK.  Jello windows is NOT OK.  Weird sounds is not OK.

It's OK to have an account with all the bells and whistles, but people are more impressed by something that just works capably with no drama.  My wife was more interested in my setup because it WAS different and had no virus problems.

People aren't dummies.  Dummies would not be happy in any linux distro.
