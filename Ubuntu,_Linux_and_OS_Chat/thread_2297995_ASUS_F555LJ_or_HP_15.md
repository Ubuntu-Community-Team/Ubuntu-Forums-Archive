### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2015-10-09
Usually you just see a display of a PC and it says something like "Core I5 or I7 duo or quad" . . . meaning it's running the Intel 5 or 7 series of cpu (latest generation =gen. 6 "skylake").    The manufacturer of the PC is not Intel, it could be anybody (HP, Asus, Lenovo, Dell, etc.).   Then, you check the make and model and see if it ALSO has Intel integrated graphics (on mine it's Intel IrisPro 5200), and it's all intel if the wireless chipset is also Intel Wireless.

The advantage to this type of machine is no special drivers need to be loaded during or after the install.   Removes the possibility of install screwups and BSOD's (black screen of despair.).

Further, HP is not especially Linux friendly - - - they don't follow certain uefi build standards (which is odd, because HP Printers are the most Linux compatible).

Full reading of the Ubuntu official docs regarding UEFI installs is _**essential**_ if you're going to try to set up a dual boot yourself.    Really, even after 15 years of running Linux, I now just prefer to buy a PC with Linux already installed (some companies will also setup dual boots - with the added cost of whatever windows version).

### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2015-10-10
Hello Renee,

Yes, that is one of the right articles to read.   But youtube can be helpful also if you search for uefi related ubuntu installs.

About System76 . . my experience with them has been excellent . . much better than with most brand name PC purchases.   It's a small company, so you're not just a number.   They are proactive, and there is a simple way via their website to start a trouble ticket, or just get info or advice.   The setup/install of Ubuntu took maybe 10 minutes on my Galago laptop.  I don't believe they support Windows but Windows can always be installed "after" Ubuntu in Oracle's Virtual Box application (which is available free in the Ubuntu Software Center).   The PC should have at least 6 GiB's or ram (my GalagoPro has 16 - - it's worth the cost).  Plus, System76 uses a standard bios (no uefi), so it's easy to install any linux version.

I am running 15.04 now, and following System76 advice, the install went very smoothly.  No issues at all.   I expect the same for 15.10 upgrade.

Re HP . . . if you buy a consumer (retail) version of their PC's, they don't engineer or test the installs.    If you buy a business grade "workstation" desktop, you can actually get Linux pre-installed but you have to reach exactly the right person/department at HP.   (and good luck with that . . )

Dell is probably the most Linux friendly hardware outside of pre-installed systems.   They do uefi right, and if you order an all Intel system, it can be easy to install Ubuntu.

In post #4 above, I put a link in for pre-installed Linux vendors - I have checked out each one's website and done some comparing.   The prices and services vary greatly so be careful.   Besides System76, I like the products provided by Zareason also.    If more Linux users would take advantage of pre-installed systems, maybe that would at least have some (small) effect on the OEM's (computer makers, designers, etc.) and lessen Microsofts & Apples cartel.
