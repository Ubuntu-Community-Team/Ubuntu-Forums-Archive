### Post by DuckHook on 2018-02-10
In Windows, you download drivers from third party vendors to run your devices (like headphones and touchscreens). Makers of consumer devices make sure that their drivers work as flawlessly as possible with Windows.

In Linux, drivers are built into the kernel. However, most consumer device manufacturers don't give two hoots about Linux. The drivers are not built by those vendors, but instead, by Linux developers who reverse engineer the devices to try to figure out what makes them tick. Needless to say, this is a far more problematic and error-prone process.

So don't hold out high hopes that some other flavour will work for your devices if the main flavour does not. The likelihood is that every flavour will lack the needed driver.

The big upside to Linux and the different flavours of 'buntu is that you can easily try out whichever distro you want for the price of a download. I would recommend that you run whatever candidate distro you desire on a LiveUSB first.
