### Post by irv on 2014-11-08
What makes a person a Linux user?

	I thought about this question for sometime now, and came up with the fact that Linux users are in a class all by themselves. Not everyone makes a good Linux user. Let me explain what I mean.
	Linux users make up a small percent of computer users for many reasons but I believe the main reason is because they are more than just a user. Oh yes, they like using computers but it goes far beyond that. It’s like when you are a kid and you see an old alarm clock and you start to turn the knob in the back that makes the hands go around and around. It’s not too long and you start to take the screws out of the back to get to the inside to see what’s making the knob turn the hands. Before you know it you have the clock all apart. And then you start to put it all back together again to see if it still works. This kid will make a good Linux user.
	Windows and other OS’ offer some things to tinker with but they are limited. When you use Linux, it is like they are handing you the tools to take it all apart to get to the nuts and bolts. (Now I am not talking about programming, that would be like talking about manufacturing the clock), we are talking about taking it apart to see how it works. Not all Linux users are programmers but most are tinkers. (Maybe that is why we break our systems more than Window users.)
	Some use Linux for financial reasons but I don’t believe that is the main reason for using it, but it is like the frosting on the cake. I think we all like this fact.
	Now I think if we were to take a mic in hand and go out and interviewed Linux users we would find that many of the older users came from systems like Commodore or Atari; systems that you could tinker with. Also we liked DOS and TOS or whatever we called our command line interface. We liked the fact that we could control our computers with a command.
	Another thing we like is that we can make our systems different that the next guy. (Tinkering). We all have different likes and dislikes so that that makes us happy users. We like to show off our systems and we love to show off what our systems can do, and we do it “in your face” of them we call simple Windows users. Maybe that is why conky is so popular with Linux users. Also, all you have to do is go on youtube and watch all the Compiz effects. 
	Well, this is my take on a Linux user in contrast to a Windows or Mac user.

### Post by TheFu on 2014-11-09
Interesting story. It is completely different from the way I came to Linux and your end reason for running Linux is completely different from mine.  That's ok - and great!

First, I've never used an Atari or Commodore computer, though I did have an Atari 2600 game console. ;)

Second, I don't tweak my systems much - beyond not using any DE. The GUI that most people consider the OS is the least reason for my Ubuntu use. Couldn't care less about Compiz or Conky. The GUI is just a way to manage more xterms.

I started on a TRS80. Learned BASIC and Fortran66 on the Model3. In college, learned Fortran77 on an IBM 370 with punch cards, then CDC 750/170 and then on a Cray Y-MP. That was my first introduction to UNIX, but I didn't care or know it. I was given the 5 commands to type to make my extremely inefficient CFD code compile, then run on the cray.  Used a Mac for a sr engineering team project (diagrams and word processing) was completely impressed, but couldn't afford one. Got a job writing real-time GN&C code for AP101/S computers - these are like IBM 360s. - but we used MVS TSO/ISPF for the development environment. Learned DOS, Windows, OS/2 and started hearing "UNIX" more and more. 5 yrs later and my company needed people to work on a UNIX-based contract ... I knew almost nothing, but convinced the head guy that I'd learn. Ended up in a government lab where we had 1 of every sort of computer, writing cross-platform C/C++ for Mac, Windows, and about 12 different UNIXen. Linux was just starting out at this point and didn't have enough support to be really useful.  I pulled down a copy of SLS on my workstation (DEC Alpha running OSF/1) and tried it out on my home Intel PC. This was the age of 14.4K modems, so downloading at home was not an option.  So ... that's how I got to Linux.  Ran a single Linux server at home from '96 until '07 - not a desktop. It was SLS, Slackware, Redhat, Madrake, SUSE - tried debian and gentoo in the late 90s too.  At work, I was running Irix, AIX, Solaris, SunOS, HP-UX and Slackware machines.  Linux matured for normal server stuff, but any desktop stuff required manually loading source code, compiling dependencies, this was pre-package management.

From late '99, I switched to Redhat at home, but tried to run lots of GUI tools, which ended me in "rpm hell" with a system that couldn't be patched. Package managers only work if we don't do stupid things like going outside the core.

These days, I choose Linux many reasons. NONE have much to do with any GUI. Virtualization is rock solid and faster than the commercial options. Storage servers are fast. Media servers/players are best-of-breed, and thanks to a powerful firewall, we can block tracking on the internet for most things (but not all). Much less tracking is better than nothing, I suppose.

Used desktop Windows for daily work from 1996 - 2007, however where I worked was a reference site for Windows95 and had over 2000 desktops deployed on August 24th, 1995.  My job was 95% UNIX then and I was still rapidly learning "the UNIX way." Win95's GUI was a major leap and I loved it compared to other options. CDE was just starting in the UNIX world.  I was running OS/2 Warp at home and playing with Linux at the same time. OS/2 was awesome with the GNU tools, it just required 2x more RAM than most people had inside their systems at the time.

In 2012, I got a Mac and used it for 2 weeks as my main computer. It was extremely frustrating. Things were just too hard and I never got used to it. They don't have select/paste like Linux/UNIX has for 30+ years.  THAT drove me crazy. The CIFS file shares were broken at the time too. I gave the machine to a friend and he felt the same as me - he's Windows centric these days but worked with Stallman at MIT on The Gimp and core X/Windows. I can see where Mac users would love it compared to any other OS. All the keyboard stuff is smart, makes sense, and if I were raised on that, then I'd love it too.  To me it is just "different" to be different, not better.

Also, there are lots of Linux users who don't know anything about computers.  They just have someone like you or me who sets up the system so they can be pure end-users.  My Mom is like that - she doesn't know much about computers at all and doesn't want to.  Just wants to point-n-click to read email, surf and talk to grandkids. She doesn't need to know anymore than that.

Anyway - like I said initially, completely different than your uses for Linux. I expect that most people who come to Ubuntu didn't come from commercial UNIX like I did. Those folks seem to end up running CentOS/RHEL and Fedora.

OTOH, the idea that Linux people enjoy tinkering could be true. I tinkered with my systems for years before getting tired of all that. Now I just want a system that is stable, secure, and works. I want the GUI out of the way.   The same CLI/shell scripts I wrote in 1994-ish are still working today.  Screw all the GUI changes. If you learn the CLI, things hardly ever change.  Plus automation of CLI stuff becomes really easy - not-so-much for GUI things.

Can't wait to read how other people got here.

Interesting, but I agree that there are many many reasons people start using Linux. 

If you don't count a few TRS-80 classes, I started out in the PCXT era. Work has always been in a Windows shop, ever since 3.1.

My first try at Linux was with Red Hat in maybe 1997 -- I never did get it to do much that I was interested in. I installed OpenSUSE in 2006 or so, and used it for awhile, but Windows was still my main OS. Then, when I got a new desktop in 2012, I looked at the direction Microsoft was headed and decided to install Ubuntu. OK, so that's one big reason I'm staying with Linux: I always felt frustrated and angry when Windows broke, because it was the only OS I had. Now, if one Linux distro goofs up, I have other OS options -- and I can usually fix the one that broke anyway. And I don't much care if my Win 7 glitches.

My wife has a couple of Macs (and I have a really old one). So I can play with a lot of different systems, both at the GUI level and on the command line. Currently I haven't the time to set up a home server, but that's on the agenda eventually.

For me, there's something deeply satisfying about having many options.

### Post by SagaciousKJB on 2014-11-11
Well I got my first computer when I was like, less than ten years old I believe.  It still had Windows 3 on it, never figured out how to do more on it than use paint but I was always interested in it on a more technical level.  Later versions of Windows would come and go and by the time I was 12 I was using Windows registry editor and all sorts of stuff like that to tweak it under the hood thinking of myself as a "computer genius".  Back then they had a cable network called "Tech TV" and a show called "The Screen Savers" and they talked about Linux and programming, which by that point I had started to learn about.  I started with HTML and then jumped into C, both with "...for Dummies" books :P  Along the way I had heard how closely knit Linux and C were, and web servers and "Apache" and such, so hearing about Linux on TSS really got me interested.  Before I knew it I had ordered some RedHat discs...  I believe it was version 7, I didn't even have cable internet back then so I needed the discs.  Of course I didn't on all the stuff I'd have to download off the internet anyway, but I learned how to compile software by source or RPM and even setup XMMS and played my music and stuff, but I got kind of tired of it after a while--it was just too hands on to get stuff to work at that point in time.  Then again it could have been the distribution, but in any case that was back when the how-to-install-linux tutorials generally included a crash course on partitioning and maybe even kernel compilation.

I tried it off and on through the years, generally every time a new version of Windows would come out and I would think of making "the switch".  Most of my reasons were financial since I hated the way Windows licensing was going by the time XP came around, what with the limited activation limits.  Not really tinker-friendly

I don't remember when I got interested again, probably when Knoppix got popular.  I loved the whole LiveCD thing and before I knew it I heard about Ubuntu.  Tried that, saw more potential for a Linux-only desktop than I'd ever seen and tried Debian, eventually going back to Ubuntu because I like the overall package management and update cycles better than Debian.  I like Debian based systems in general though.  I think about 2007 is when I decided to finally drop Windows 100%.

Even having some background with Linux--however limited--there was still a real "learning curve" when it came to a lot of the things that I would learn how to do on Windows.  The thing is that those things when change with Windows releases too, so eventually figured that with Linux I could probably learn a way to do it that would be relevant for much longer.  I think the single biggest thing that will make users appreciate Linux and really open up their eyes to the usefulness of a free operating system is the terminal and text-based configuration files.  Things you can control and modify, edit and tweak and what not that in Windows you would need to go google obscure information for or download some kind of application to handle conveniently, you can configure in Linux over a wide array of distributions and system ages.  I don't think it really takes that long to use it either, and afterward I just can't see anyone wanting to go back.

But there's lots of Linux users who don't care about any of that and just want a hassle-free OS to do all their usual favorite tasks.  My friend David used my computers today and he didn't really have any problems with Linux, other than not being familiar with XFCE and how to access certain files over the other.  His only issue with Linux is it doesn't support the games he wants to play--alas I think having no DirextX support is the only thing that really keeps people shackled to Windows.  Even he said that Linux feels like "...more of a *real* OS where you can actually do stuff.  The things Linux does well it does extremely well, but the few things it sucks at it *really* sucks at.  Windows just does everything kind-of okay."
