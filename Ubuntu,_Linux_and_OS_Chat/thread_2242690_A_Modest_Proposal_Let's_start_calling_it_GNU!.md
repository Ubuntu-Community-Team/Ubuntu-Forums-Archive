### Post by oldrocker99 on 2014-09-03
OK, here's my thoughts in a nutshell.

The OS we love so much is **not** Linux; that's the kernel that enables the OS we **use**, which is GNU, which is what we should rationally, and correctly call it.

[Rant mode]
The Linux kernel, as completely awesome as it is, is **everywhere:** 82% of smartphones, 60% of tablets, in every cable set-top box, in every DVR, in every streaming Roku-like device, in most routers, and in each and every "smart device" to make up the "internet of things" that ubernerds are forecasting. As Linus Torvalds (hereafter referred to as LT) said recently that the only place the Linux kernel hasn't yet penetrated is the desktop. That's why we who use the best possible OS are in such a minority. People are of the opinion that Microsoft rules the PC world, and, if they put out a version of Windows that people hate (in other words, every other version of Windows: people liked Windows 3.11, they hated Windows 95, the liked Windows 98, they hated Windows Me, they really liked Windows XP, they hated Windows Vista, they liked Windows 7, they really, really hate Windows 8. See a pattern?), why, then Microsoft, in its benevolence, will put out a version of Windows that people like. And charge for it, as they always do, for a porous, vulnerable OS that has engendered a multi-billion dollar anti-malware industry, and the corresponding multi-billion dollar malware-**creation** industry.

We "Linux" users know what a load of crapola that attitude is, but after 20+ years, we have been notably unsuccessful at convincing other people of the superiority of our beloved OS. Oh, Apple fanboys, using a modified version of another free OS, OpenBSD, with its proprietary, closed-source interface and its expensive if nicely-designed hardware, think they have escaped the Microsoft hegemony, only to fall into Apple's hegemony. And since Apple allowed dual-booting, 98% of Apple users pay for, and install some version of Windows, not a superior OS. 

**BUT** that OS, the one we **use**, from the command line to the minimal desktops to the likes of Unity, GNOME 3.x and Unity, all run on GNU. The Linux kernel enables GNU to run. Since GNU was the first OS to be enabled by the Linux kernel, 20-odd years ago, a GNU system running on the Linux kernel became known generically, if inaccurately, as "Linux."

A personal hero of mine (and should be of yours), Richard M. Stallman (hereafter referred to as RMS), the founder of the GNU Project back in 1983, and the writer of the **essential** GNU License, rightly feels that our OS should be called "GNU/Linux," since, as he truthfully points out, in a "pure" (100% open source free software) "Linux" system, the kernel is about 5% of the whole system. I completely agree, but I wish, and argue, that we should take things a step further. 

Here's what I think. We don't call the OS that runs 82% of smartphones and 60% of tablets "Android/Linux," even though the Android OS is enabled, just like GNU, by the Linux kernel. We don't call ChromeOS "ChromeOS/Linux." (My wide, a complete computer illiterate, was startled when I told her that her Chromebook, which she loves, is a "Linux" system. It's, as I understand it, just enough of Ubuntu to run the browser.) We don't call, for example, the Roku OS "Roku/Linux." And so on.

Since, I feel, that we should think as highly of RMS as we do of LT. LT's work made running GNU possible, but RMS was the guy who started the wonderful ball rolling. Yes, RMS will not use any closed-source software at all, none. That's why the GNU site lists only a few 100% free software distros, because most distros (including Ubuntu, Mint, Fedora, etc. etc.) do use closed-source drivers, codecs, and so forth.

My proposal is simply this; Let's refer to our beloved OS as GNU, since that is what it plainly really is. It's that simple.

I, of course, welcome comments of any type.
[/Rant mode]

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-09-04
It's 'Linux.  If confronted with a Linux box populated with only a kernel and the GNU toolset, almost all of us would walk away.

Torvalds wanted a Unix, could not afford to buy it, so he jumped into the deep end and announced he was going to write his own. Other Unix-like systems were available then, some with source, documentation, and even, like Minix, textbooks on OS design, things like kernels and filesystems. He wasn't working in a vacuum.

Happily,  the GNU toolset was available at that time to provide versions of the traditional Unix character-based executables so Linus would have something to run once he got his kernel working. And, of course, emacs from RMS. At about the same time, other people were engaged in similar efforts to create "free" versions of BSD Unix, which also has its own version of the basic Unix tool set, plus vi.

So, in terms of software, the GNU contribution, like the BSD contribution, primarily consisted of the core character-based tools essential to running and administering Unix. These are the terminal tools that typical users of typical modern Linux distributions try to avoid.

The FOSS approach has allowed innumerable forks to emerge, as well as some new and innovative software. It's been vital. But, in terms of actual software from the GNU team, the contribution was essential, but relatively small measured against the amount of software in a typical Linux distribution these days.

It's worth remembering that nothing has ever prevented developers from releasing source for software they independently create. RMS and GNU relied on copyright to codify that right into licenses blocking creation of closed software that used GPL'd code.

(With hundreds of millions of people walking around with iPhones, iPad, iPods and carrying Mac Airs to and from the office, it's only natural the Jobs was, and is, much more of a celebrity than RMS, who comes across as something of an aging pot-bellied elf rather than a svelte, photogenic, appealing, California billionaire. Ritchie and the others in the core group that created Unix were obviously of fundamental importance, but there's no real reason for ordinary folks to know that.)
