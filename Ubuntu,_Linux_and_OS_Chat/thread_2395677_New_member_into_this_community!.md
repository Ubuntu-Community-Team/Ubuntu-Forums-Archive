### Post by TheFu on 2018-07-05
Welcome.

It depends.  It depends on your goals, aptitude, ability to learn and what background you already have.

Basically, you've just asked "how do I become a race driver" without saying what sort of vehicle you want to compete, whether you want to be a professional or hobbyist, or whether you've ever driven any vehicles before.  If you want to race trucks, there are different skills than racing F1 or slick track or desert buggies.  Perhaps you want to race snowmobiles in winter and NASCAR the rest of the time?

I wrote this article in 2011 [http://blog.jdpfu.com/2011/10/19/how-to-learn-to-program](http://blog.jdpfu.com/2011/10/19/how-to-learn-to-program) trying to answer the question you've asked.  It still applies, though Javascript has become much more popular today (for unknown reasons).

There are 25 other threads in these forums asking a similar question which might provide some insight you'd find useful too.

Regardless of what anyone else says, you can accomplish anything you want if you commit, follow through, and do the work.

Perhaps I'm over thinking the question?

How can you program?
1) pick a language.
2) open a text editor.
3) write a program.
4) Profit!

How long does it take?
It takes as long as it takes.  Some people will write 3 beginning programs their first day. Other people won't begin for 5 yrs, though they always intend to start.

There are thousands of books, thousands of youtube videos, thousands of learn-to-program websites.  You can use free resources or paid.  I don't think it matters.  You can use college course or actually go to a local collage and take their Programming 101 class.

Which works bests depends on you.  Some people need to pay $20,000 to feel it is "worth it."  Other people can follow a book from the library and learn.

There are books that teach the full SDLC - software development life cycle - methods. That would be design, code, testing, quality, version control and slight team programming.  Even with I'm programming on a solo-project, I do all those things. Version control has saved me more times than I care to admit.

Decide.
Commit.
Start.
