### Post by Billy_Martinez on 2015-01-10
Hi everyone I've been using Linux for a month now and I just wanted to give everyone my short review of Linux Mint Mate and give my reasons on why I left Linux Mint Mate for Ubuntu 14 LTS. Linux Mint is a great OS so please don't get me wrong this is not a Linux Mint bashing post. Also, please forgive me if my Linux terminology is a little off because I'm a newb to linux.  
 

 First, I'm going to give credit where credit is due. I thought that Mint had a better installer than Ubuntu. If your video card isn't compatible with the default video drivers that come with the kernel, then they had a preconfigured setting to load the Debian installer using the video compatibility mode. I know that Ubuntu has this option as well, but in Mint you don't have to edit any lines of the grub installer with “**nouveau.modeset=0****”** so it makes it easer for people that are not familiar with Linux. I was impressed with how stable and efficient Mint was on my computer. Mint only used around 700MB of my 8 GB of RAM on idle. Also, my PC has a 256 GB SSD and it booted up Mint in around 3 to 4 seconds. My system was very fast and never crashed and I was able to run a lot of heavy duty programs without it slowing down.  
 

 The major thing that pulled me away from Linux Mint was the look and feel of it. My mouse wasn't as smooth as it is in Ubuntu. I noticed that I was having a difficult time placing my pointer on different buttons in the GUI. I tried playing with the mouse settings, but nothing I did helped. I feel like the GUI of Ubuntu 14 LTS is better than Linux Mint Mate. I know that if you wanted a better GUI, then you could install Linux Mint Cinnamon, but I prefer to use LTS versions of distros since I'm a newb and want to avoid running into major problems. I found the Linux Mint Mate applications menu difficult to navigate and it was hard for me to find programs. The grouping of the applications wasn't as intuitive as I hoped it would be, applications just seemed out of place. The search feature wasn't as good as the search app in Ubuntu. I like how Ubuntu's search app organizes everything, it find it easier to read. Some people have complained that they're unhappy with the Canonical's decision to get rid of the application Menu in Unity, but if that really bothers you (which it bothered me), then you could just install the app “Classic Menu Indictator” and brings it back in the upper right corner of your screen. Mint's lock screen is very plain looking when compared to Ubuntu. I like how Ubuntu's lock screen fades into the desktop, it makes the OS look more sophisticated.  I really like the Unity Launcher which Linux Mint didn't have. Every major OS has their own version of a shortcut bar and that's because it makes it easier for the users to interact with their programs.  
 

 I find the Unity GUI of Ubuntu to be more modern, intuitive, and innovative than Linux Mint's GIU. I just feel like the overall design of the Linux Mint GUI looks like something that would've come out in the days of when Microsoft was releasing their NT based operating systems. The applications menu which is at the bottom left of the screen and their task bar reminded too much of Windows 2000/XP. I just built a really nice custom machine for 800 dollars and I didn't feel like I was using an a brand new computer. I feel like Ubuntu is the better option for someone that has a brand new computer and that wants a nice polished OS. Ubuntu does take couple of more seconds to load and uses up a little bit more resources, but you get the look and feel of a modern operating system. For anyone that wants to revive an old Win XP machine then I would recommend installing Linux Mint Mate on it.  
 

 Out of the two distros I find Mint to be a little bit more stable and faster of the two, but I find Ubuntu easier to use and Ubuntu has an amazing forum for those that need help. I feel like Mint is a solid OS, but it needs to mature more. Feel free to comment, I'm looking forward to hearing feedback from my post.

> **craig10x said:**
> It IS more modern then cinnamon desktop because (thanks to i phones and android phones) the "icon based" way is a very modern and up to date style that most people have now become accustomed to...

 The ''icon based'' UI of Android and iOS are actually meant for touchscreens and nothing else. I've tried Android Jelly Bean on my desktop with a mouse and it's not that great, really. There is no doubt that Unity is also designed with touchscreens in mind, one cannot deny that the side-bar launcher and the dash are meant for your finger to pressed on. Canonical's goal is convergence, so that users will be able to have the same OS on their laptop, tablet or smartphone. So that kind of make sens.

Personally, my criteria of a 'modern' DE is not based on smartphone's Oses. I don't want my desktop PC to be one big smartphone with big buttons, as such a plain desktop works very well for me. Linux Mint's Cinnamon desktop is elegant, modern, intuitive and convenient. It is designed for the majority of people who have the traditional and still very popular laptop and desktop, offering a plain, yet polished environment. Microsoft understood that as well, and that is why they are ditching the Metro screen for Windows 10. I really like their intergration of the Windows' store apps within the startup menu. They won't bother you with a touchscreen's UI unless you actually have one.

That being said, I do like Ubuntu, as long as the Launcher can be auto-hidden. I don't want it to take an inch of my screen. Unity is a very polished and stable DE but it's obvious downside is inflexibility. That's the main reason why I am not using on a consistant basis, I am drawn towards Lubuntu and Xubuntu instead which are way more customizable.
