Cinnamon's great tho. But heavy comparitive to other DEs. I turn off the desktop effects under Cinnamon. Improves the speed quite a bit.

Also don't bother installing Mint if you want to """"try a new OS"""". You'd get 99% of Mint just by installing the Mate/Cinnamon DE under Ubuntu.

Do a bit of research and look for distros that aren't based on Ubuntu. Some examples are:

[LIST]
[*]Fedora (based on Redhat, uses RPMs instead of DEB, friendly to newcomers, Fedora 21 will be released in December so I suggest switching then if you're interested)
[*]OpenSUSE (based on SUSE, another enterprise version Linux like Redhat). Recently just released 13.2, also uses RPM. Is beginner friendly as well and uses YAST, which has a lot more system control via UI then you'd find in the Ubuntu system settings app)
[*]ArchLinux (suggest trying this after you get your feed wet with a few other distros. In Arch you start with nothing and "build" the OS from a command line, installing stuff like your choice of DE and apps. In Arch the only stuff installed is whatever you choose. Nothing's preinstalled. Not very friendly to beginners but has a great Beginners Wiki that only requires reading and copy/pasteing commands)
[/LIST]

I'm generally unfamilier with Linux, but there are a few command line differences between Ubuntu and non-Ubuntu distros. sudo apt-get is an Ubuntu thing, and Xinput commands are different between Ubuntu and non-Ubuntu distros. Plus there's the whole DEB vs RPM. Anyways there you go.

Since I have a spare SSD I swapped it, put Mint 17.1 on it, and plugged in to the 2nd monitor. It, literally, made zero difference from using laptop only to using laptop + 2nd display. It was as crazy snappy with two monitors as it was one. This made me want to use Mint more, so I've been trucking along with it for a few days.

What I love about Mint/Cinnamon:

It's faster than Unity. I don't think Unity is "slow" but I should absolutely not be able to see any sort of slowdowns on an i5 Haswell chip with 8GB of RAM on a solid state drive, and I do, which is only further exacerbated by using the laptop in dual screen mode. Don't get me wrong, Unity is "acceptable", but the noticeable speed improvement with Cinnamon is hard to deny. Again, it's not that Unity is bad, it's just after having both setups on the exact same machine, the difference is hard to ignore. I can literally hit super, type Files, enter, in one fluid swing and it's instantly there. Unity also has some sort of weird issue at times where it won't actually scan in the dash when you type. If I type Files, it sits there as if it brought up zero results. I delete everything, type Files again, suddenly it understands me. I hope (and expect) that compiz being dropped in Unity 8 will make it faster and more comparable, but we'll just have to wait and see for *whenever* Unity 8 comes out.

One thing I miss from Unity is the actual launcher on the left side. I got really used to that and thoroughly enjoyed it. I think that was really the gleaming part of Unity that I liked, as the dash.... the dash is hard to like since it's not mouse centric whatsoever and even for the keyboard centric users, searching can be hit and miss as I described above. I'm getting used to Cinnamon's default interface. I like it a lot (especially with the Gnome theme), and it's far more fitting with the panel at the top of the screen for me, but I still find myself glancing over to the left side of my screen. That said, the Unity launcher had some issues too, such as it being difficult to tell if an application needs attention, as the icons look nearly identical to their idle state. But even still I like that darn launcher. 

The other irreplaceable thing I like (KDE fans will roll their eyes at this since they've had it for a while) is the quarter screen snap. Taking a terminal to the upper left, another to the lower left, and Firefox 1/2'd on the right, my gosh. Love it. I always loved that when I used KDE, I just never had KDE on any machine outside of a testing zone (personal preference). 

I think I'll be sticking to Mint for now. I like being able to get more out of my hardware with additional workspace-related functionality. Maybe I'll add plank to Mint or something for a dedicated spot off the panel for my open applications to reside, who knows. I'm enjoying it as-is in "flipped" mode right now. But hey, just my random brain dump of my experience while on topic. Use what works best for you.
