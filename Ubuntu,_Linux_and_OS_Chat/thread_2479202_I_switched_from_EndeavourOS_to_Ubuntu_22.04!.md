My first GNU/Linux was Debian... and one of the things I like about Ubuntu is I can just treat it as if it's a Debian box I think.

I've never been an Arch user [*as I see it*]; sure I made an install & kept it about ~9 months I think, but found it more work than Debian so returned to Debian for that install.

 I've got an OpenSuSE *tumbleweed* install here, and it's a couple of years old, but I find it *more work* too (than Debian/Ubuntu), and I suspect it's just the nature of *rolling* releases.

This box is Ubuntu *kinetic* or sits on the *development* release, as does what I consider my [*secondary*] Debian box (on *testing *or *bookworm*). Sure those boxes can have issues too on occasion (*testing or development exists for testing so problems can be found, reported & corrected* *where possible*), but I consider even with those '*problems*' they are less work than my little experience with *rolling* has been; but maybe it's the same too.

A *stable* system has its advantages; however the biggest difference is likely just **timing**, or when the problems occur.

On the *development/testing* cycle, even *rolling*, those problems are *drip fed* to you as they hit your box, ie. *smaller *but more often. On *stable* systems you get all those *dripped* issues combined when you perform the *release-upgrade* (ie. *move from one release to the next you're getting six months, or two years of changes hitting at once [using Ubuntu's release timeframes]*).

Myself, I think all GNU/Linux is pretty much the same, given all packages really come from the same upstream sources.. it's just packaged/delivered at different times & by different teams of people; sure some [*teams*] make mistakes now & again; but none are perfect with most differences being just the timing.
