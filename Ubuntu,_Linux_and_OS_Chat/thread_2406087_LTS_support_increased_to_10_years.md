### Post by TheFu on 2018-11-20
I have core servers running on 8-9 yr old hardware.  Performance is fine.  Server OS and service updates should only include security patches. No new features.  Servers need a stable system, without unnecessary changes.  In Feb, we moved many of our services from 14.04 to 16.04 after having almost 4 yrs of very stable performance.  On 2020, we'll decide which OS to migrate those 16.04 systems to next, if newer releases haven't produced features that we choose to migrate onto.

There is a danger in having systems unmodified for too long.  The people performing that task will have moved on and the new people may not know how to setup the same systems. This is more important for unpopular services, not widely deployed around the world.  There is a trade-off between having a system unchanged for many years.  Constantly changing the OS is bad for server uptime and service stability.

Desktops are a completely different issue. As external services are changed, outside our control, client programs need to be modified to retain access.

Is 10 yrs too long?  It certainly will make migrations even harder for businesses, but Canonical is just providing customers with what they are demanding. RHEL has 10 yrs of support and people run critical internal systems for much longer than that, unfortunately.  But business leaders seldom understand data security and just want computers doing what they've done forever, without change, like a washing machine.

Sadly, this announcement is for 18.04.  From what I've seen about that specific release, it will take 2 yrs for it to be stable, so perhaps making support longer lasting will be a good thing?

IMHO.

### Post by TheFu on 2018-11-30
Hoping for 11 decades, that's the money plan, but expect to get around 8.

I've come across RHEL 4 and 5 systems through work.  They weren't on an IP network and had been chugging away for a long time.  We raised the support issue to the client, made certain we could restore from the last backup and moved on.  Business decisions are left to the business people in charge.  We provide the information about risks, estimate the costs for changing and they decide.  I didn't do the what-it-would-take to moved to a supported release analysis, but sometimes the only answer is "that's impossible" for less than $250M.  Better just to let the systems die and explain to the clients they need a different solution when that happens.  Some times getting people/human to change is harder than anything else.  A dead system that cannot be brought back will limit the constant verbal complains as a new solution to solve almost, but not exactly, the same problem is deployed.  Management is often about human factors.
