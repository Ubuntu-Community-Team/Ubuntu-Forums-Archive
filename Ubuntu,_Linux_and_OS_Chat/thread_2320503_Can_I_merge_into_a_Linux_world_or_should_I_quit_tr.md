### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2016-04-14
To be honest . . . I don't see you leaving Windows.    You can dual-boot, or if you have up-to-date capable hardware, you can run Windows inside Ubuntu via "virtualization" . . . . using Oracle's Virtual Box or VMWare's "VMPlayer".    An install to a second hdd or a fast external SSD will provide an environment similar to installing on one HDD but in it's own "partition".   A usb-flash-stick or thumbdrive install is both slower, and much less manageable (imho).

Also, and maybe most important, how "open" are you to learning new or different methods of using a PC and/or other computing/mobile systems?  The current or former "Windows Power User" has the most difficult time making a transition to Linux.    Users with little to zero experience (younger kids) and technology professionals (any IT related degree including elec. engineering, etc.) have the easiest time.    

So, it's up to you . . . if you don't mind reading a fair bunch of material, and/or watching a few dozen instructional videos, then you're a good candidate.

EDIT:   just read your newest post below, and let me revise my assessment above - - I don't think you'd be a good candidate.   You've already made several inaccurate assumptions, and once down that road . . . . . it's not a good ending.   BTW, in IT terms something that really runs well and "smoothly" is called "elegant" . . . . dual-booting when set up right is both easy and elegant - - not messy in the least (but you've already made up your mind ahead of time, without having the actual experience.)

### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2016-04-14
VM's are used daily (millions of instances) on all platforms - - because it's far too time consuming and costly to do otherwise.   

Generally, what is the lowest cost and _most stable host for the guest OS_???    That certainly would **not be consumer grade Windows** by any means.   Corporate imaged and provisioned systems, managed via tools the consumer doesn't have (or generally knows how to use) is acceptable, but is also a more complex environment.   One of the draws of "Open Source" is not just excellent software but the much reduced red-tape involved when compared to prop. licensing, and reduced "vendor lock-in".    Consumers are much not aware of the last point, but are paying the price (in multiple ways).

### Post by O)9(yo&amp;# on 2016-04-15
Regarding which is better, to run Windows inside Linux, or the reverse: I think it makes sense to run Windows inside Linux. That way, you benefit from the fact that Linux is more secure, and your Windows is sandboxed inside Linux. Many people who do this don't even bother to update Windows anymore. You could run XP or Vista inside Linux without worrying about security. That way, you can do most of what you need to do on Linux, but have the virtual Windows for those things you need it for, all without having to update Windows, worry about malware, etc.

I am running Windows 10 in a VirtualBox machine on an Ubuntu Gnome host. It seems to work very well. The only problem I've run into so far was printing to a networked Samsung Multi-function. I could ping the printer but couldn't print to it. My solution was to create a second virtual network adapter and make it bridged (the default is NAT). Now I can print & scan from Windows running in a VM. I've never needed to do this but I know that I can if necessary. Yes, virtual machines are handy. The biggest requirement for running a Windows VM is to have enough RAM. 4 GB. is an absolute minimum IMO. Ubuntu Gnome & Windows 10-64 restricted to 1.6 GB. -  2 GB is recommended for 64 bit - takes around 3 GB without any apps running. Your processor must support virtualization but most recent desktop & laptops do AFAIK. I'm pretty sure gaming on a Windows guest wouldn't cut it - the most video RAM I've been able to dedicate to the Windows VM was 256 MB. and I doubt the VirtualBox video driver compares favorably to AMD or Nvidia. For someone wanting to dual boot an SSD makes a substantial difference but it's still quicker to switch between OSs in a VM. By dual booting each OS is running on 'bare metal' so there are no performance penalties or driver problems.
