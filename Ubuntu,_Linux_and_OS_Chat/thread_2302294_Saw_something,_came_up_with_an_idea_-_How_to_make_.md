### Post by GXdmWQH on 2015-11-09
Hi all,

To start out, I'm a fan. I have run Linux for the last 3 months as my "main desktop machine" at home. To be precise, I was using KDE on top of Ubuntu, and I was in heaven. I'm still running it as my corporate laptop (albeit with Gnome as opposed to KDE), and it's doing well there for reasons I can't be so sure of. Probably because all the code we write is Java, and Linux + Java actually works. I'm unhappy though. I had to revert my home machine to using Windows 10 so I could play certain games, use certain applications, and generally do certain things. There were many things I switched back for, but most notable ones were:


[LIST]
[*]Dolby Axon (voice chat application)
[*]World of Warcraft (MMORPG)
[*]GTAV (Online/offline game)
[/LIST]

Naturally, Axon has some alternatives, but none are quite so good as Axon. It's the best audio chat program for quality, consistency, reliability, and all that goes with, and if I weren't a linux fanboy I'd never use anything else. Anyway, the lack of availability of specific programs forced me to look for alternatives. I tried wine, it didn't really help. Stuff worked, but as with all things alcoholic, it quickly got messy.

I did some Googling, as we do, and found that there are a lot of people who agree with the sentiment (lots more stuff needs porting to Linux) and that the most common suggestion was to "put your money where your mouth is", since the only language developers understand is United States Dollars. Problem is, you can't put your money where your mouth is, if the place that your mouth is doesn't exist. I bought a metric ton of Linux games, and I've played them until they lose all meaning. It hasn't helped, and most likely won't help. 

[U][B]To get to the point...
[/B][/U]We need a way to tell developers that we want stuff compatible with our OS. We can write emails, we can send petitions, we can do all that crap, it doesn't help. We need, effectively, a crowdsourcing platform that will allow us to specify that we are willing to pay $X in order to get a game ported. If we get enough money, the companies will express an interest. If we really get enough money, we might even end up not having to ask for each new game that comes out. If we could set up such a system under the trusted name of Ubuntu, I believe we could easily convince more people to build for this great platform, and I could finally do everything I want to do on the OS I want to do it on, without having to get drunk in the process.

Does anyone know if this has been suggested before? Is there any reason we wouldn't want to do it? I understand that it puts a very "do or die" persona on the growth of Ubuntu (which has had to be stimulated by a simulated growth, to some extent), but I don't feel it would be particularly deadly if it were to backfire in this way.

### Post by mystics on 2015-11-09
The problem with AAA games in particular is that they are expensive. You're looking into the tens of millions in development and advertising costs, sometimes on the Xbox and Playsation versions alone. The Windows versions have been seen as an added, more risky cost for years, and that's with absolute dominance of the PC-gaming space. Likewise, porting to Linux wouldn't be cheap, and Linux is struggling to stay above 1% of Steam users. That's not a safe space to make a possibly multi-million dollar port to, and I really doubt that companies like Rockstar or Bethesda would ever consider it right now. Blizzard is even less likely, since it would take time away from cranking out the expansion packs they need to keep people coming back to pay for subscriptions.

I know Linux users like talking about all the games on Steam with Linux support now (I think it just passed 1600), but what are the biggest names among those? Now, of those big names, remove all that came out 5+ years ago and/or are from Valve (who clearly is going to push it). Now, are any of those anywhere near the same level of popularity as Call of Duty, Grand Theft Auto, Fallout and Elder Scrolls, Dragon Age, or Assasin's Creed? No, not even Shadows of Mordor is that popular. And there's even some series (e.g. Divinity) that are questioning ever getting another Linux game after the performance of the last one on Linux. And to make matters worse, plenty of Linux gamers will jump over to Windows to play any of the popular franchises, so the company is already getting money from these people who may not buy the Linux port when it comes out due to being burned out from the game by then.

All of this to say: If you want to see major games supporting Linux, it will take a lot more than asking for a Linux port and claiming you'll pay $X. The publisher can't guarantee you'll pay $X, and that's assuming enough pay $X to begin with. Support for Linux will need to grow (here's hoping Steam OS helps with that), and Linux users might even need to start boycotting Windows-only games. But let's face it, that latter one won't happen given [SARCASM]gamers' excellent history of standing by their threats of boycott[/SARCASM], so do what you can to support what Linux does have and hope it eventually catches the eye of a major developer. Who knows, maybe Valve will get things started with Half-Life 3...

I've been using computers since 1965.  Taught by the Navy to do so, and have been in the support game all of my career.  To me. the addition of graphics, sound, video, and joysticks have really just turned something seriously useful into an expensive toy.  Lots of people get hooked on it, but it's not the real thing.  Best advice for them is:  Get a life.

There was a bit of Dune, Wolfenstein and Mech yhrown in there, but  I got off them quick because I did not like the impact on my subconscious, as they showed up in my dreams at times.  Especially the image of blood all over the screen. 

Now it is usually just a quick game of Majong. which lets my mind muse on as I play it.  You know how getting away from something can suddenly bring an answer to mind?  That happens a lot to me.  I wake up with the answer in place.  I can be typing, and thoughts range into other areas, which might culminate in new concepts. but then I'm sharing them with others as I type, and the others may not appreciate my startling new revelations.  Majong gives me a bit of that fluidity because it only takes a small amount of focus and concentration to play, letting my mind work on at the same time.

I don't want a game to take my total attention because I've got other things to do.  I don't want to make my life about game playing, which is what I see others doing.  I can't put down one project and devote time to another just because the first stalls or takes extra time now and then.  I mean I would rather not do this, but it does and can happen.   I try to keep my mind locked to one project at a time.

I find Gnome and Ubuntu liberate me from the total preoccupation with graphics that Windows seeks to impose on you,  I hated Windows 8.1, but can tolerate Win7 and WinXP.  I gave up my claim for a free upgrade to Win10 with no hesitation and no regrets by ridding my laptop of Windows completely.  I added WinXP and Win7 back as clients under Virtualbox for a a convenient alternative if need be, but hardly ever bother with either.  Mostly I have them so that I can fall back to PowerBasic if I decide to use it, but I am adapting to PureBasic and Bash now.

You want the latest and greatest games?  Stick with Windows.  That's how you make that happen.   Don't argue that you can organize us all to follow your lead and make a big difference.  Linux users are a breed apart, independent enough to move off Windows in a search for a better alternative.  We only follow the herd when it suits us. and following the path back to high gains for some game developers is not the part of the world I want to be going towards.
