### Post by ian-weisser on 2023-02-19
> **ne29914 said:**
> 
But I think Canonical could improve communication.

Well, ALL of us could improve our communication. So there's a wider world there.

But there's also a big element of folks not paying attention. Phased Updates were introduced almost a decade ago. They were added to apt two full years ago, and extensively tested. There was a big, long thread on Discourse about it. OMG Ubuntu ran features "It's coming". It was in the 22.04 release notes. It was in the Ubuntu Weekly News. And then lots of folks whined and complained that nobody told them.

Pro is heading along a similar trajectory. What Pro does is actually fairly clearly explained on their website...but it's layered with marketing-speak (of course! It's a sales product), so requires a moment of decryption. Various support venues have figured out what Pro does, what Pro doesn't, and which Pro-related questions are appropriate to answer. But we're still getting the trickle of questions from folks who simply didn't think about what they read, or didn't bother to follow the tutorial, or other foolishness. 

Most information from Canonical comes from team/product leads who are experienced, professional software engineers. They are NOT professional copywriters nor professional marketers. And here's the good news: We get the information from them unfiltered on the Ubuntu Blog, Planet Ubuntu, the Ubuntu Weekly News, Ubuntu Discourse, the mailing lists, the IRC meetings, and other places. Real information, not marketing copy. Few other companies are as open.

Because those leaders are not professional marketers, they tend to be circumspect. They are paid to create and maintain code. That's what they are comfortable doing. They open the door to share with the community because they *believe* in Open Source and in Free Software. When we in the community let screamers and complainers and jerks control the conversation, those leaders shut the door. They're NOT paid to humor the whiners. They're not paid to entertain us. **We are not their customer. We are participants.**

So we --the community-- need to meet those Canonical folks halfway. They do tell us what they are doing...but they tell us in their own way. It's up to us in the community to share what we have learned and help each other to understand the meaning.

### Post by TheFu on 2023-02-23
If you don't want to see advertising at login, 
```
$ touch ~/.hushlogin
```
You could do that for each user, but I see it as a user's choice.  This has been around 30+ yrs.  It is standard Unix behavior.

According to the login manpage:
```
...
       Then the user’s shell is started. If no shell is specified for
       the user in /etc/passwd, then /bin/sh is used. If there is no
       home directory specified in /etc/passwd, then / is used, followed
       by .hushlogin check as described below.

       If the file .hushlogin exists, then a "quiet" login is performed.
       This disables the checking of mail and the printing of the last
       login time and message of the day. Otherwise, if /var/log/lastlog
       exists, the last login time is printed, and the current login is
       recorded.
...
```
