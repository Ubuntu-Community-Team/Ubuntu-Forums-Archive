### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2013-10-25
Rather than making assumptions, there's a pretty good explanation of what's sent to canonical, what's passed to third parties, what's logged, who has access, and so forth on this blog post from Jono Bacon:

[http://www.jonobacon.org/2012/09/25/more-information-about-online-dash-search-privacy/](http://www.jonobacon.org/2012/09/25/more-information-about-online-dash-search-privacy/)

For those who don't care to read it, main points are:

- Canonical receives your IP, what you typed into Dash, and what you clicked on.
- This is sent over http (maybe https now?), and the http logs are kept for an undisclosed amount of time.
- Only "a small group of people  who [...] are trained in respecting people's privacy in accordance to European law" have access to the httpd logs.
- The search data is made available to "a slightly larger group of people" with the ip addresses hashed.

I think it's reasonable for a person (or business) to feel that this is too invasive.  

I also think it's reasonable for a person to find this an acceptable trade-off for a useful service.  

I think it's a bit dodgy that such a service is opt-out instead of opt-in, and that there's no warning or explanation of what's going on.
