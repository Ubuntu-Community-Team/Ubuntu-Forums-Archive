### Post by Kurt_Alan on 2015-06-26
Playin' the Flavas

Good-bye to my Main Squeeze, Xubuntu (DE Xfce).  
I was a Gnome 2 diehard until the Gnome 2 devs hit a brick wall with Canonical and ceased Gnome 2 with my FAV, 10.04 Lucid Lynx, LTS.

Yeah, I “got” Shuttleworth's Unity vision but I ran screaming from that DE and, for the first time in around 10 years, I thought about leaving the Ubuntu family. I started with 6.06 Dapper Drake.

Xubuntu and DE Xfce to the rescue (from Unity and Gnome 2 in oblivion). At last, GUI precision and control.

Now it's good-bye to Xubuntu due to three factors.  The first two are Xubuntu deal-breakers.  First, there is the cursor deal-breaker. Every time I drag an object, I get a cursor grab of that object, with an image and a double cursor. Most annoying. Must log-off/on every time. A known bug with no solution.  The second deal-breaker (for me) is the failure of Dropbox—passive functionality, yes, but the lack of menu access to the powerful selective sync feature along with dropbox.com's admission (to me) from the second tier of support, that Xubuntu is not supported, drives the nail into the coffin.

And the third factor is the resurrection of my beloved Gnome 2. in the now Canonical recognized flavor, MATE. In MATE, like Gnome 2, you can move any program anywhere, to desktop and/or one-four panels. And if you want to move a panel program, simply unlock and drag. In Xfce you have to use the bizarre and overly complicated spacers. . . 

Soon I will be dual booting MATE and Unity and relegate Xubuntu to a VM. Why Unity? I use Unity for passive browsing and reading only, not for doing work. Unity is so “out of the way” that it engenders a sense of calm. And yet it is powerful. Need to use gparted? As soon as I type “gp” in the dock, there it is! (Of course without a traditional menu, how can you find what you don't know exists or you know it exists but can't remember the name? Most exasperating for new users and older folks like me losing memory). You can add a classical menu.  And the Launcher? There's Terminal, right at the top where I can grab it.

What's your “playin' the flavas” story?

### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-06-27
This is not correct

> the Gnome 2 devs hit a brick wall with Canonical

The decision to discontinue development of Gnome 2 and Gnome panel was nothing to do with Canonical and everything to do with Gnome organisation itself. Over the last few years we have had people coming on this forum complaining about changes in Ubuntu but the changes they complain about were changes made to Gnome applications by the Gnome developers. And they were blaming the wrong people.

If you want to know what Ubuntu would be like if Canonical developers had not written Unity then install Ubuntu Gnome or Fedora with Gnome 3 DE and Gnome 3 shell. 

It was Canonical that hit a brick wall of Gnome developer intransigence when Canonical asked if the Unity APIs could be merged into Gnome 3 shell. If the Gnome devs had not refused we might have had Ubuntu that came with a log in choice of Unity or Gnome 3 shell.

But as far as the Gnome developers were concerned Gnome 2 and Gnome 2 panel were "Going, Going, Gone." And that was nothing to do with Canonical. If you have a need to blame somebody then blame Redhat. It is the largest corporate contributor to Gnome.

> Before GNOME 3, GNOME used the traditional [desktop metaphor]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_metaphor"), but in GNOME 3 this was replaced with [GNOME Shell]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNOME_Shell"),  a more abstract metaphor where switching between different tasks and  virtual workspaces takes place in a separate area called Overview. Also,  since [Mutter]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutter_%28software%29")  replaced Metacity as the default window manager, the minimize and  maximize buttons no longer appear on the titlebar by default. [Adwaita]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adwaita_%28software%29") replaced [Clearlooks]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clearlooks") as the default theme. Many [GNOME Core Applications]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNOME_Core_Applications") also went through redesigns to provide a more consistent user experience.

 These major changes initially evoked widespread criticism. The [MATE desktop environment]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MATE_%28software%29") [forked]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fork_%28software_development%29")  from the GNOME 2 codebase with the intent of retaining the traditional  GNOME 2 interface while keeping it compatible with modern Linux  technology, such as [GTK+]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GTK%2B") 3. The [Linux Mint]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_Mint")  team addressed the issue in another way by developing the "Mint GNOME  Shell Extensions," extensions that ran on top of GNOME 3. These  extensions allowed GNOME 3 to be used via the traditional desktop  metaphor. This eventually led to the creation of the [Cinnamon user interface]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinnamon_%28user_interface%29"), which was forked from the GNOME 3 codebase.

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2015-06-27
Started with 7.04 and loved it. 8.04 LTS was even better. Then, eventually, along came Unity. I have never run Unity on anything. I only ever looked at the screenshots, heard about it and got the picture from people's experiences on here. I went straight from Gnome to Xubuntu/Xfce4, no stops in between. 

After a few years, I began fiddling with the minimal install. One of the reasons I tried Linux in the first place was that you could strip it back to just what you wanted and not what was shoved down your throat if you wanted to use an operating system. No bloat! I like sleek (possibly to OCD levels!). The mini.iso was my dream solution. I'd been around and knew which apps suited me best so away I went. 

Been using that thinking for four or five years; mini.iso and xfce4 and my checklist of standard apps for my workflow. Can't see myself doing a 'standard' off-the-shelf 'flava' install on any of my machines in the near or distant future, at least not to use as my main squeeze. I just need it solid, fast and familiar, and that it is, on several machines. :)

(PS: Not to say I didn't go on a VM binge a couple of years ago for a month or two and try everything I'd been curious about, so I've played some flavas in a minimal way. Have been meaning to drop to my 12.04 install with VBox and try Mate when I have the time, though. Heard plenty of good things about it and it looks good, no disco and not too fussy.)
