### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-01-29
> **Don_Stahl said:**
> 
I think Munich went over-schedule on their switch to Ubuntu / OO, but last I heard they had 13,000 workstations...

13,000 workstations to manage pretty much creates bureaucracy. I.e., a hierarchy of tasks, budget and schedule mapped against the organization chart.

Employers do not like to spend money on what they (usually correctly) believe will be non-productive upgrades. Software is plumbing.  Security fixes keep the pipes from breaking, but the rest very often seems to management as change for the sake of getting their money.

The cost-free aspect of FOSS sounds like it ought to ease management's worries about costs.  But, in my experience, purchase cost is far less an issue that deployment, retraining, and continuing maintenance. 

A hidden cost is employee morale.  I spent several years helping to shepherd the rollouts of more than a few multi-million dollar software revamps in a large organization. Employees *always* were vocal in opposition.  Sometimes management failed to adequately explain the reasons for the change.  Most often, the changes were in the interests of management, and did little ot nothing for employees.  We even killed one 7-figure effort within a month after its launch because employees were so hostile in their opposition to it.

Employees are not computer enthusiasts.  Most of them don't like their job.  Once they've figured out the workarounds they need to get their work done, few of them will be happy about having that stability yanked out from under.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-01-30
I never knew any employee to try to sabotage a project.  But, I knew many who complained about the transition and long, boring, training sessions that meant they'd need to work longer hours.

One of the big mistakes management can make is assuming they know what their employees are doing to get their jobs done.  Often, managers don't know about all the little workarounds, tricks, etc., workers have figured out and depend on.  So, when management talks to the software design people and says, "Here's what we need the new software to do...", they're speaking out of an ignorance of what their employees actually do.  The result can be new software that employees decide is unusable, and which may,in fact, be unusable.  People designing software for employees should  spend most of their time with those employees, not sitting in conference rooms listening to management.

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2014-01-30
Small but critical distinction to make here:

The article does *not* state that they are proposing to move to OpenOffice.  The proposal is to move to open document format (ODF), specifically for the purpose of allowing more competition for software vendors.  In other words, government offices could potentially use OpenOffice, LibreOffice, GoogleDocs, or even stick with MS Office since any of those support ODF.  

IMO this is a better move than mandating this or that FOSS office suite, since it removes any kind of lock-in and allows different segments of the government to determine which product best meets their needs.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-01-31
> **rewyllys said:**
> ...emphasized over and over again the importance of finding out in detail how the workers are actually doing their jobs...

When I was in a position to do it, I required developers to spend time, usually 3-5 full days, sitting with employees. The employees were selected based on their experience and skills, and their ability to articulate what they were doing, and why.  In most ways, their experience with a developer was a lot like giving a new employee a crash course in how to do the job.  Later, as development moved along, those same workers would be testers.

Some developers hated this and really preferred working in their cubicle from a design specifications document.  There was a negative correlation between those developers who were unwilling to learn from the people who would be using their product and the developers who moved along and up their career path.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-02-01
> **wamses2 said:**
> ...I bet any procurement for new hardware, desktop, laptop is still likely to be Windows based..

When I was in it, we would consider non-Windows only when we were not integrating something into our almost exclusively Windows environment. In practical terms, that meant we ran some special-purpose Linux and Solaris servers, and fitted out the graphics and multimedia folks with nice things from Apple. Everything else was Windows, and stayed that way because there was no advantage to moving to a new platform.

Linux has to offer something more than just being "as good as" Windows before organizations will bear the cost of playing "all change". That "better" doesn't have to come from spiffy new software, or long-term cost savings.  It can come in terms of meeting some specific purpose better than Windows.  In terms of this thread, from something  like switching to ODF.  But, if you go into a board room and try to convince people to move to Linux because it is "as good as Windows", they will very likely tell you, "Well, OK then.  We already have Windows.  If Linux is just as good as Windows, it's also no better.  Why switch?"
