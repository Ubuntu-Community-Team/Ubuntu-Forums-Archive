### Post by DuckHook on 2020-02-18
Can't help you on the technical front, especially since Windows and its WAD are an alien landscape to me, but since you've posted on the chat forum and presumably are welcoming peripheral commentary…

No matter how easy Canonical makes it to add Ubuntu machines to WAD, this won't appreciably increase Ubuntu on the desktop workplace. Office desktop IT dynamics are not driven by technical considerations, but by financial and psychological ones. CIOs stick with what they know and are used to, what has the most app support with the least bother, and what everybody else is using. Aside from sheer prejudice, there are valid real-world reasons for this: Windows expertise is easy to find whereas Linux expertise is rarer, mixed IT environments are harder to administer and maintain than monolithic ones and Linux remains a geeky OS that can quickly become highly technical (witness most of the threads these very forums).

Apple succeeds in carving out a role on the desktop workplace because it is, in many respects, the opposite of Linux: it has a large installed base, prides itself on being even more "intuitive" than Windows, requires the least technical know-how, and (because it ruthlessly and exclusively marries its OS to its own HW) is much easier to administer and maintain since it is the most monolithic architecture of all.

Simply said, Linux dynamics work against its adoption on the desktop. I'm more than happy with that. I like Linux as a geeky, highly technical, highly customizable and therefore ultra-powerful OS. Wouldn't want it any other way, and I certainly wouldn't want any of that power or flexibility to be sacrificed in a futile chase for more desktop "market share".
