### Post by Enigma12 on 2016-11-25
My not-very-experienced advice is to view this as a bit of an adventure. Like you, and many others, I began using Linux after beaucoup years of using Windows. The only thing I MIGHT use Windows for again is doing tax returns, unless someone creates a Fed + state Linux tax program. 

Unless you are in a big rush, you might want to explore a variety of Linux distros on YouTube or a huge bunch of websites. None of that is hard to find. Since many (most?) distros also allow you to try before loading, you may even discover the "perfect" distro for you. There are gobs (tech term!) of choices. Most, if not all, distros have many software choices available. Hopefully, that answers your first question well enough.

For your Q2, trying a distro will let you know whether or not it works on your system. Just realize that operating off of a CD/DVD or thumb drive will be [COLOR=#ff0000]much[/COLOR] slower than from an installation on a hard drive. 

Re: Q3 - You MAY have to seek and tweak drivers for your system or not. If you have any difficulty making that happen yourself, many people here have solved the same problems or know how and will advise you. 

I can't advise on your programming question. Expect that from others. 

As for stability, some distros MAY be more stable than others. I happen to use Lubuntu, and it has been rock solid for over 2 1/2 years. I have so far only used LTS (Long Term Support) versions. Other than going with a fresh install, rather than an update from 14.04 to 16.04, all updating has been semi-automatic, in that I have to accept the offered updates. 

Since you are likely to dual-boot, I suggest using what you are comfortable with as needed and get more proficient with whichever Linux distro you choose at your own pace. Linux is not a free Windows version, but it has kept many people satisfied around the planet. Live, learn, enjoy.

### Post by Bashing-om on 2016-11-25
eritromycin; Hello. Welcome to the forum .

Though linux is not for everybody, and does not suit everyone's needs - jump in ! The only way you will find out is to use the sytem(s) and see what works for your use cases, and what you like.
Be aware there are about 20 or so different desktop environments. The only difference in the linux releases are these desktops and the default installed applications; ubuntu and (k)ubuntu being heavies ( resource intensive), the lightest in our line is (l)ubuntu .
But keep in mind - This Ain't Windows . There is a learning curve that is as step as you want to make it . This learning curve is much like learning a new language - coming from Windows and a different way of thinking.

To answer your immediate questions:
1) I too messed around with differing linux distributions, For me hands down was ubuntu - solid open source operating system, good documentation, access to a huge variety of software ( free !) and last but not even the least is the community support at all levels . Installation of "most" softwares is as easy as selecting what you want and clicking . Last I was aware there were about 43,000 packages available in the software repository that both our parent and the community maintain .

2) You will have a better experience with 4 Gigs or more of ram in the heavier distributions; 2 Gigs is doable .

3) Depends on the bells and whistles - and the user interfaces you are accustomed to - as to the desktop that suits your wishes . By far (k)ubuntu has the more bells and the greatest eye candy . Next is ubuntu for usability and those bells and whistles - me, I like the lower end lubuntu . Has all the functionality of the high ends - but less on eye appeal and reduced GUI abilities . But, I am prejudiced and in favor of functionality and speed above all other considerations . I run xfce as my on-demand desktop generally running in terminal .

4) What operating system has the greater programmer's support .., Well, my take again is ubuntu . My experience is that ubuntu - with all the support structures - is a programmer's dream come true . In the ubuntu family there is no application that is not universal across all the distributions, Right out of the box at the click of a button .

5) That stable distribution for Long Term Support is ubuntu 16.04 . LTS == Long Term Support . That support period is 5 years from the time of release . We are talking full support for security and fixes until April of 2021 .

Keep in mind the downside . My use/experience : NONE
It is free, well supported with access to more applications and softwares than I will ever use - much less even have an occasion to look for.

This operating systems meets and exceeds everything I look for in a operating system . It is by far my operating system by choice.
