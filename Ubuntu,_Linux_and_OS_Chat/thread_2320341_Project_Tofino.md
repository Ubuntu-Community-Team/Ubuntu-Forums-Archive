### Post by grahammechanical on 2016-04-13
Disclaimer.

I have not read all of the first link. Just enough to see the direction it is going. And I have not (yet) read any of the second link. My response to the first link is: "That's democracy for you. And community power."

People criticise Ubuntu because it is it is backed by a commercial organisation & not a foundation type organisation. In a commercial organisation it is nice if there is a consensus over which direction to in but when push comes to shove there is always some there who can make the executive decision. And that settles disagreements. If we don't like our job, we can always look for another one. That is another form of freedom.

For example, At Debian there was a fractious discussion about switching from Upstart to systemd. But there was no contention in Canonical over Ubuntu making the switch because someone with authority to lead accepted the decision made by Debian and decided that Ubuntu should make the switch because it was based on Debian.

Another example is the direction Canonical is going with Snappy Ubuntu core and Snap packaging and not deb packaging. But someone has decided that it would be of benefit to all if it was possible to use deb packages and snap packages in Ubuntu 16.04 LTS. We might get to see that by the autumn of this year. Someone had to make that decision to pay for the work to be done.

Canonical has proved itself willing to pay people to innovate. Some of the projects come good. Some are put aside as useful experiments that may or may not have a use in the future.

Regards.

I'm not at all sure the browser needs to be "redefined".  Sometimes these kinds of grand schemes are driven by an organization's, or a batch of developers, need to justify their continued existence.
