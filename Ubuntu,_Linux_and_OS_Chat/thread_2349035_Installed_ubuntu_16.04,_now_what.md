> Installed my school programs, now what? i really love ubuntu but i dont know the full capabilities yet hehe

You have no idea the POWER of the Linux workstation.  So, to unleash most of that power requires learning to think in a Unix way and stop using GUIs.  Make the system do things for you, automatically.

If you intend to have a career related to computing, learning Unix is a huge help.  Every popular OS running everywhere in the world today, except MS-Windows is based on Unix.  That includes every iOS, Android, OSX, Linux, and UNIX OS.  Most people don't understand that pretty much everything on the internet is running Linux - well over 80% of those servers are Linux. It is only in small businesses and homes where Windows is considered "a server."  I've worked places with about 80K servers.  20K were Windows. 60K were Unix/Linux.  Best of all, by dealing with Unix/linux, I never had to deal with any end-users!  Talk about a bonus!

So - if you plan to do more than point-n-click your way with computers, getting a nice introduction would be helpful: [http://blog.jdpfu.com/2014/12/28/learning-linux](http://blog.jdpfu.com/2014/12/28/learning-linux)  The first few links should take only 45 min to read.  The PDF book will take you to the next level.
If you just want to stay at the end-user level, then look for the "Ubuntu Desktop Guide."  If you want to customize your desktop, get away from "Unity" and try some other DEs like Mate, KDE, LXDE, XFCE.  A search for "Linux Desktops" will show you some amazing desktops.  Some of my favorites with lots of transparency are here: [http://fvwm-crystal.sourceforge.net/screenshots.html](http://fvwm-crystal.sourceforge.net/screenshots.html)   That window-manager has been around since the mid-1990s, BTW.  It runs nice on low-end systems too.

The world of Linux is huge.  I've been using it over 20 yrs now and guess I know about 10%.

Just looked at some of your other posts.  See VNC in there.  VNC is slow and lacking security. Take a look at x2go. It is 2-3x faster.

### Post by TheFu on 2017-01-13

Most people don't.  It does much of the behind-the-scenes work at most companies with 200+ employees.  If you don't work there, you'd never know. Actually, the CSRs might not know either because they either use a fat client program that talks to a back end running on Unix or a web-app running on Unix/Linux.  Everywhere I've worked since the late 1980s had more Unix than Windows servers. 2 places we didn't have Windows desktops, we used Unix workstations instead.

At my last job, we had 1 Windows machine for the accountants. Everything else was Linux running F/LOSS servers. Each employee was allowed to pick their client workstation - it was 100% BYOD. As CIO, I didn't care and wasn't responsible for any end-user devices. I put up services that followed standards. It was up to each person to pick how they interfaced.  The guys running Windows had the most issues, but learned about standards quickly.

BTW, there are places still using mainframes, which run something like MVS (they renamed it to OS/390, I think).  Usually banks, insurance, and telcos still have 5-20 mainframes. If they only allow 8 character alphanumeric passwords, it is probably an old mainframe limitation.  BTW, mainframes aren't dying either. My brother-in-law has a thriving mainframe software company that he thought would die in the early 2000s. Sales are just as strong now as they ever were, he tells me. I was a mainframe programmer for about 5 yrs long ago.

Unix/Linux is huge. More than 50 lifetimes of learning.
