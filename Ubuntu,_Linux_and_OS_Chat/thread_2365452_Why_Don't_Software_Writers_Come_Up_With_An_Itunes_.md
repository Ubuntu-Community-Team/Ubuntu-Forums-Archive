### Post by QIII on 2017-07-06
Hello!

"Ubuntu" can't write software.  Ubuntu is a Linux-based operating system (a distribution or distro) developed and maintained by Canonical, LTD.  Canonical is a small company with very limited resources.  There are many, many Linux distros developed by many, many other entities, so Ubuntu is not "all there is" to Linux-based OSes.  Nor is it all there is to Linux.

Linux apps are generally created and maintained by independent developers.  OS developers will often package those apps to make them installable on their distros,  but they don't write the apps.

Your question then, has nothing to do with why "Ubuntu" hasn't developed such an app, but would be better expressed as "Why hasn't some independent developer developed it?"

The answer to that is "Who knows?"  Maybe there is no interest among developers to do so.  

That said, I can think of Exaile, Banshee, Rythymbox, Amarok and Clementine off the top of my head.

No Linux distro is a serious challenger to Windows on the desktop.  None.  There is too much inertia going for Microsoft there.  As for "mainstream", Linux already is.  It is part of a bigger stream than Windows.  By an order of magnitude, there are more devices running a Linux kernel than Windows.  The world is awash in Linux and most people are blissfully unaware.  To most PC users, though, computer == Windows.

### Post by exhile on 2017-07-07
iTunes is generating revenue by the intellectual property sales of books, movies, music and apps. Most Linux distributions generate sales from server support of their respective software. Linux on the desktop/laptop platforms represent a minor portion of the market share compared to Windows which has 90% and Mac OS X which has about 8%. So trying to develop an iTunes alternative on Linux for the desktop/laptop market would be futile.

Software businesses have to thrive through either profit or donations so you ought to guess where iTunes and Linux software stand in their business strategy.

### Post by TheFu on 2017-07-09
I haven't burned a CDROM in about 7 yrs.  I don't know many people who do (if any).  Most of us don't have optical drives on our main systems any more either.  This is especially true for software developers who like to look forward, not backwards.  If a software developer doesn't have a "personal itch" about some problem, then they probably won't work on it much, if ever.

Most software developers have a smartphone and keep their music organized on that device.  They are most interested in making music software that works with their home stereo system or their car stereo systems.

Lastly, as others have mentioned above, the funding model for Linux distros is mostly donations.  How much did you donate to Canonical/Ubuntu when you downloaded?  How many people donate anything?  

Now compare that to the Apple i-store - which takes 30% of all funds for applications and media purchases. 30%!!!!  1 Day of that amount of money for a single Linux distro would fund 3 people full-time for a year - perhaps 10 people.

Also, compare to Microsoft's "forced donation" of $120+ per retail OS license across 1B devices.  To be fair, the pre-installed Windows costs are probably around $10/seat.

Now, if Canonical mandated a $10 per download price (which they could do), lots of people would simply get the install from a different location (I get mine from GA Tech already) or switch to a free download Linux distro like Debian (which is what Ubuntu is based anyways).

If you'd like to understand more, some light reading about how this all works:
* [http://www.dedoimedo.com/computers/ultimate-linux-guide-for-windows-users.html](http://www.dedoimedo.com/computers/ultimate-linux-guide-for-windows-users.html)
* [http://linux.oneandoneis2.org/LNW.htm](http://linux.oneandoneis2.org/LNW.htm)
* and there is a book called *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* [http://www.catb.org/esr/writings/cathedral-bazaar/](http://www.catb.org/esr/writings/cathedral-bazaar/) which explains Free/Libre Open Source Software and why it is magical and why it truly sucks. Both are true. ;)

BTW, as much as you appear to like iTunes, I abhor it, for a number of reasons - some technical, others political and lastly terrible usability.  Don't feel bad if you disagree with me. That's absolutely fine. Many people do. ;)
