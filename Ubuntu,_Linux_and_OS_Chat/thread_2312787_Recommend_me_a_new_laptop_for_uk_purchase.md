### Post by TheFu on 2016-02-07
I like the Dell keyboards best out of all vendors.  Lenovo has a good rep in the Linux world too.  There's always Apple too, but used would be the only option at that price.

Unfortunately, every specific model often gets very different internal components, so support for those basically needs to be looked up on a chip-by-chip basis.  Wifi is a typical issue. I've been lucky with my laptops and wifi - they have "just worked" for the last 5 yrs. Plus, I don't really use wifi much.

You know, if working from home is really what this is for, perhaps a US$350 desktop would make more sense?  It will be much more capable than any laptop at any price and with the left over money, you could get a really nice chromebook that weighs under 3 lbs, has a 1080p screen and can also be wiped and Ubuntu installed.  For web development, having the powerful system on the physical machine you sit behind just isn't necessary.
Here's a web-app dev guy using an iPad [http://yieldthought.com/post/31857050698/ipad-linode-1-year-later](http://yieldthought.com/post/31857050698/ipad-linode-1-year-later)

I'm not saying that a huge laptop isn't the best idea, just that there are other, perhaps more flexible options available. Plus it is easy to hook up any laptop to a keyboard, monitor, mouse these days when at home to have those nice, big, human touch points without giving up portability.

A few other questions?
* Did you want wired ethernet?  GigE?
* WiFi - is G good enough or is 600+Mbps wifi needed?
* USB3? How many ports?
* VGA, HDMI, DisplayPort output?
* Battery life target? 2hrs - 13 hrs?
* Weight - is 8 lbs ok or would 3 lbs be heavy?
* SDHC/SHDX support needed?  CF, SONY Memory stick?

BTW, last month I picked up a Toshiba Chromebook 2 (4G + Core i3 + 1080p) - amazing, lite, machine for $420. Had to break the warranty to make it useful to me. I used to carry around a monster, heavy Thinkpad. Won't ever go back.

### Post by TheFu on 2016-02-12
Perhaps in some parts of the world, it may be illegal, but not in the USA. If you don't like their terms, take your business elsewhere.  For me, the Dell keyboards make it worth strong consideration.

Dell probably pays $20/system for Windows licenses, if that.  I used to work at a place where we paid $26/install for Windows-Pro.  Every desktop system got a license, even if it didn't run Windows as part of the enterprise agreement.  Inside the company of 100K+ employees, I'd guess fewer than 200 didn't run Windows and each of those people had to get an approved "variance" with a business reason to be allowed.  Most worked in data corporate security.  They couldn't access Sharepoint. ;)

Personally, I'd pull the factory HDD and replace it with a different drive. Then if anything happen except to the HDD, the solution is as simple as dropping the factory HDD back in and testing. Plus that way when Dell has you ship it back to be fixed, none of your personal data goes with it.  If they want to do remote support, you can boot the factory disk for those connections and let them screw around as much as you like.  Of course, the day after the warranty ends, I'd wipe that disk and use it for something useful.  I've never needed warranty service from Dell in 20+ yrs of personal purchases - OTOH, that's only 4 machines and 2 of those machines died after the warranty period was up (dropped them about 18 inches). The cost to fix the problem was almost what a new 2x faster machine would cost.  Once my credit card paid for the cost to fix the machine, so I got a new machine for $100 (core 2 duo --> Core i5). That i5 has been working for ... I'll look it up ... 8/13/2010.  5.5 yrs for a laptop - not bad.

There's nothing wrong with spinning disks.  They are well understood and have known characteristics.  SSDs tend to fail quickly when they fail, locking all that data where it cannot be retrieved.  The theory is that SSD storage should last 10 yrs, but in reality, some of them fail in 10 days, 60 days, 6, 12, 18 months just like HDDs.  Regardless of the storage, good backups are a must.  I hope SSDs really do last 10 yrs, but doubt that will be the case for me.  Any storage over 5 yrs old makes me nervous.

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2016-02-14
*Thread moved to **Ubuntu, Linux and OS Chat***.

I have a Toshiba Satellite L510. Works great. Wife has a Compaq. Fine. We have an Odroid. Lubuntu works faultlessly on that. I just built a desktop and Xubuntu-core runs great. I have a HP Stream 11. Ubuntu Minimal beautiful. All 14.04 LTS (except Xubuntu-core 15.10).

Don't get confused. The only thing that you really need to worry about is internal wireless and graphics. Hard drive, processor, RAM; pretty generic. Incompatible wireless is even easily fixable with a cheap USB dongle. Graphics, though ...

I'd focus my attention there and wireless secondly. I'd also sit down with a pen, paper and beverage of choice and figure out the hardware specs - RAM, processor, hard drive(s), gpu - are going to achieve that. If a machine matches your criteria, then look at graphics firstly, then wireless secondly.

Research the machine online and see what you can dig up. '<machine-name> ubuntu' is a good start. You'll find out the experiences of Ubuntu users with that machine, mostly their issues as you won't find a lot sometimes about stuff that works great, so take that into account. Then make a decision. Good luck. :)
