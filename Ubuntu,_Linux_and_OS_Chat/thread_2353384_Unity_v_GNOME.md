Quick version:

Mark and Canonical has nothing to do with Gnome or the Gnome Foundation etc. except that Gnome 2.x was chosen as the default installed Desktop Environment for the official Ubuntu release. ### Post by DuckHook on 2017-02-28
Just a bit of a different perspective for the OP:

I have no real DE allegiance. Have used at least half a dozen and they all have their strengths and their weaknesses. Though some users turn their likes or dislikes into holy crusades, I've never understood why.

Here's how my own stuff is set up:

Primary box runs Unity, but this is not really due to preference&#8212;I help a lot of general users on these forums and it's more convenient for reference purposes to have the most prevalent DE up and running.
Laptops run Xubuntu or Lubuntu depending on how ancient they are.
I have every official flavour up and running in VMs on the host. They're actually used too, to sandbox browsers and other apps that I don't want to expose to my host or my network. Also useful for test install of apps I am not sure about, either security-wise or just functionally.

So, I would suggest running Gnome in a VM to see if you really really like it. It may pale on you over time.

Though what follows may seem a bit PollyAnnish, I do truly feel this way: even though enthusiasts love to indulge in expressing their pet peeves on each DE, those irritations and drawbacks pale in comparison to the sheer wonder and gratitude I feel towards those who have given me so much for free. The Gnome 3 has developed a lot. It is now very customisable thanks to extensions. You can make it to be Windows 10. Still, I came back to Unity. It just fits me. I consider Unity so much more refined.
- Gnome shell always requires one more click. To change windows with the mouse, you click Activities, then your favourite on the dash. Or you throw your mouse to the hotcorner, then back down to your application. In unity, it takes a simple click the dash. Increase the volume? In gnome, click on the combined settings icon, then on volume, only then can you start adjusting your volume. In Unity, it takes one click on the sound icon.
- Shut your laptop down? In gnome, you can't. It means, you have to know that, in order to turn of your laptop, you need to press the Alt key before selecting the settings icon.
- Task switching with Alt+tab. In gnome, this still worked as it did in the early Unity versions. There, Alt+tab moves fully to the other application, bringing all its windows in front. Say you have an archive open and you want to switch to the most recent nautilus window? In gnome, all other nautilus windows will also come to the foreground, possibly hiding your archive window. In Unity, only the most recent window of the file manager will come to the front. Allegedly there are instances where the gnome model works better, for instance for a multi window app like the Gimp (which I use in single window mode). 
- Want to launch an application? Your whole desktop environment is fully swirled around (makes me dizzy by times), giving you all kinds of options, not only to launch programs, but also to move windows between workspaces etc. Unity, instead, leaves your desktop intact and provides you the option you want: an opportunity to launch a program or a file. Want to move windows to a different workspace? There is Super+s for that, or the Workspace switcher icon.
- What is this crazy "application menu" in gnome? Why this complication in a desktop environment that pretends to want to be simple and efficient? 
- Latest Gnome shell strangely enough still cannot find files (presumably if you do not have Gnome Documents installed, which is the case in Antergos by default). Yet tracker is churning and using full processor time for hours. I never hear the ventilator of my Dell XPS 13. That was until installing gnome shell. That issue was solved removing the tracker package. Yet either with or without tracker, gnome shell would not find files. Once I tested gnome shell in Ubuntu (with Documents installed). Documents were found then, but when launching them ... the Gnome documents app was launched instead! !#@?
- One can tell that Unity was tweaked. Tweaked towards efficiency. Most window management, launching, closing, is managed from the left corner. Switching with alt+tab moves you between applications. Waiting a little bit, you continue moving within windows of the same application. After that, Alt+tab switches you consistently between the last windows, whether these belong to the same application or not. Clicking an icon of the dash moves you to the most recent window of the application. If that is not what you want, a second click opens the windows of only that application in a spread.

Yes, also for me it is the best desktop. Its defaults fully suited me, from the first minute. Others people's mileage may vary. For one thing in linux, we are very happy: we can choose our desktop. Most desktops, moreover, can be tweaked to fit many workflows. Unity, however, is an exception here.  However, it is for me the only desktop that I do not need to teak for myself.
