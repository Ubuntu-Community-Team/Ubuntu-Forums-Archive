### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-06-24
A few things to remember:

1. All of Linux has made significant technical progress since the first release of Ubuntu.  The changes in Ubuntu over that time correspond with that progress. Being more professional is a good thing.

2. It is a mistake to project individual opinions (Unity "hate') on other people.  If Unity was as universally loathed as many people would like us to believe, no one would choose to install it. Ubuntu is available with a range of desktop environments, and no other distribution nurtures as many derivatives of all shapes and sizes. If you can't do it in Ubuntu, you probably can't do it in Linux.

3.  The fact that Ubuntu has survived all these years, and the breadth and depth of its software base, is down to the fact that Canonical (Shuttleworth) money makes that happen. Like Red Hat and Suse, Canonical fund paid staff to design and develop. To date, it appears Canonical has never turned a profit.  That cannot continue indefinitely. Not because Canonical is a greedy corporate evil.  But, because a business cannot survive unless it replenishes the resources it expends. (That's true even for warm and fuzzy non-profits.) The monetization efforts we've seen included in the most recent Ubuntu releases are, it seems to me, rather desparate efforts to find a way to derive some revenue from Ubuntu so it does not become a permanent Mark Shuttleworth charity.

4. Someone motivated  to use Linux primarily for ideological reasons, and not because of the nature and quality of the software, would be better advised to consider a distribution like Debian.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-06-24
I've never been sure just exactly what a "community" distribution is supposed to be. "Community" is just an internet buzzword used to describe a group of people whose single point of commonality is the use of a certain kind of software. It's as legitimate to talk about the Windows "community" as it is the Ubuntu "community". It's a word used to make us feel part of something and, hence, boost brand loyal.

If, though, by "community" we really mean "all volunteer and anti-corporate", then we really ought to say so.

In any case, decisions need to be made in the management of any software, and communities cannot make decisions.  Even distributions like Debian and Fedora, which make a big deal out of community, create small groups of individuals who have the responsibility for managing the product. Perhaps they make an effort to measure the consensus of opinion in the community, but that can really only be an educated guess.  I don't know of any distribution that hires professional pollsters to see what users are thinking.  

If some folks don't like the way Ubuntu is going these days, they can always fork it, in the grand tradition of Linux.

Regarding bugs, and how a distro works:
The operating system is the kernel.  Bugs there are fixed by the guys who work on the kernel.  Anything called Linux gets a fresh kernel every so often and so those changes trickle out to absolutely any Linux distro whose managers want to get them.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-07-18
The problem here is the requirement to create a .desktop file in order to add something to the Launcher. Since .desktop files are a Linux convention, this is not uniquely an Ubuntu issue.

The same problem shows up in Gnome Shell if you want to make an ad hoc entry to the Overview.  A .desktop file must be created and include an icon assignment.

As suggested earlier, the easiest way to make a .desktop file is to copy and edit an existing file.  A GUI tool to create a .desktop file ought not to be difficult to create, since it would amount to collecting data from the user.

The bits and pieces of a GUI have nothing to do with the OS.  They're just ways to collect input from a user and send it on its way to the code that  actually controls the hardware. 

Configurability is useful if you are a knowledgable and sophisticated user.  Most users don't meet that description. For them, configurability is often just another way to screw things up. Desktop developers and designers -- particularly in Linux given their limited resources -- target the largest audience. (The two can be combined in a single system.  See OS X, for example.  The GUI most folks use is quite capable and not terribly configurable. Beneath that, OS X is very flexible and configurable in a powerful way. And it's remained remarkably consistent and stable over its 10-year-plus lifetime.  But, no Linux project has the resources to pull that off.  So, choices are made.)

Rather than complain that DE's targeting these mainstream users don't deliver enough geeky tools, users who want those capabilities should work to ensure the DE's that do provide them have the resources needed to survive.

Whether a distribution or a desktop environment allows, encourages, or demands a lot of customization is a choice by the developers.  They write the code and users decide if they like it or not. That's the risk developers always take when they release code. I suspect for every user who demands more customization, there is another user who says, "Just get it right out of the box; don't make me spend hours fixing your mistakes." (I'm in the latter group.  If I don't like they way it looks, it better offer some real *substantive* capabilities to make it worth my time to noodle around fixing cosmetic issues.)

Two last points:

1. Noise on online forums cannot be taken as accurately reflecting user interest.  Users who make the effort to post online are, by definition, self-selected and biased. The herding instinct adds to their number because many people will suddenly decide they have an opinion merely to be seen aligning themselves with the people they'd like to be.

2. As for the "corporate" tag -- intended as a term of disparagement -- nothing is free.  Resources, usually money, need to flow into any effort, endlessly and without fail. In distributions like Debian, almost all of that comes from the spare time of developers.  In other words, Debian is funded by the resources its developers spend to support it. They work long and hard for Debain and do not get paid.  They could be spending those hours making money.  So, they are in a very real sense funding Debian. (And, that's the real reason Debian never works to a schedule.  They can't, because they can't tell people to "Stop doing that and get *this* done by Friday.  That's what's really behind the "not until it's ready" business.)

Ubuntu is not supported entirely by a band of volunteers. Either it finds a way to generate the resources to continue, or it goes away. If Ubuntu was supported entirely by a band of volunteers, it would be a considerably less compelling piece of work.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-08-11
Some posters here are over-emphasizing the role of "community" in Linux because they are suggesting that early distributions and "community" distributions today heed community wishes. That's not the case.

The first problem is that no way exists to know who is in one of these communities, much less accurately poll that community to know what it wants.

The second problem is that, as used in this context, "community" usually mean users. 

If there are communties in Linux, they are communities of developers, not users.  That's as it should be because Linux is software and software belongs to the people who write it. 

Regardless of how community minded someone might be, communities are incapable of making decisions.  Some one, some individual, has to make the calls. Even when someone honestly tries to pay attention to the wishes of users, decisions about the course of a distribution are made by one or a few people.  They are not based on asking the users what they want. (That can't be done because the developers have no idea who is or is not a user.)

For example, there are people who consider themselves part of the Slackware community.  But, it has always been a one-person show.  The code that's in a Slackware release is there because *one* person wants it there.

Some people think anything to do with money is evil and don't want to dirty their hands with any software with ties to a corporation.  That's fine.  

Meanwhile, some people are more interested in using whatever software they think is best for them, no matter who is behind it,  That's fine, too.

But, please, don't pretend that FOSS and Linux are some great crusade to purge the world of corporations and the universal desire for wealth that sustains them.
