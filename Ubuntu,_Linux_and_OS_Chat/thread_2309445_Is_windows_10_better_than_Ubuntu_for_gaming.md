### Post by mystics on 2016-01-13
Generally speaking, Windows is (and will remain for the near-future) the top OS for gaming. It's where the biggest names in the industry (EA, Ubisoft, Rockstar, Bethesda) release their games. It's where most of the work is put into on multi-OS releases, and it will inevitably get most (if not all) games before OS X and definitely before Linux, provided they all don't release at the same time. While Linux does have a friend in Valve, it still has a long way to go before it gets the same number of games as Windows and gets those games to run as well on Windows.

As for performance, there's not a lot of data about it right now. However, benchmarks have shown Windows 10 to consistently beat out Steam OS on Valve-made games, sometimes considerably so. No data, to my knowledge, exists on Windows 10 vs. Ubuntu, but considering the gaming-focused nature of Steam OS and the fact Valve made it theoretically means it would do better than Ubuntu.

That said, plenty of games can still run (and run well) on Linux. I'd imagine that, at the very least, you should have no issue with Counter-Strike: Source (not sure about Global Offensive). However, if gaming is important to you, then you really should stick with Windows for at least gaming purposes. You could always install Linux as a VM and use that when you're not gaming.

As of November, over 30 million: [https://www.yahoo.com/tech/s/over-30-million-playstation-4-units-sold-worldwide-114303745.html](https://www.yahoo.com/tech/s/over-30-million-playstation-4-units-sold-worldwide-114303745.html)

It also sold nearly 6 million over the holidays alone: [http://time.com/4167439/playstation-4-holiday-sales/](http://time.com/4167439/playstation-4-holiday-sales/)

Xbox One doesn't have a lot of data, but around 2014 it had shipped 10 million units. In the mean time, the Wii U, which is the weakest of the consoles, was around 10 million towards the end of last year. Furthermore, if these consoles (particularly the PS4) goes on to match last generation's consoles, it will reach approximately 100+ million within the next few years. The fact that it is the most successful Sony launch means that it could go on to top the PS2, which sold a little over 150 million. 

That is, by the way, at or more than the total number of active users on Steam (approximately 125 million). Approximately 1% of those gamers are on Linux, a usage share Windows 10 has already passed up.

Now, none of this is to downplay the tremendous advancements Linux has made in gaming over the last couple of years. It's got the backing of a major company (Valve), could benefit from new graphics API technologies like Vulkan (granted, I have my doubts that it will take over DirectX 12 any time soon), and has had a tremendous interest from the gaming community compared to when Valve introduced OS X gaming to Steam 5-6 years ago. And in terms of light gaming to do alongside the actual strengths of the OS, Linux looks like it could join OS X as a viable option. Heck, I would argue that it is already there.

However, core gamers are going to stick with consoles and Windows for the near future. Way too many big-name games release exclusively on consoles and/or Windows, and I don't see that changing any time soon.

So, to bring this back on topic: in terms of the simple market-share generating developer interest, Windows 10 is, and will likely remain for the near future, the vastly superior option for gaming. That isn't to say Ubuntu isn't good for gaming (depending on the needs), but comparing the two is rather unfair at this point. Maybe in 2020, when we start seeing users forced to choose a new OS, we can start pondering whether or not Linux may pull in enough market to start getting a better shot at the big-name games.
