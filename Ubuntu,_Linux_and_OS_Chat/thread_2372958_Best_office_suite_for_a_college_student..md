Having spent more years in undergrad than I care to admit... my meta-answer is "feed the College Machine what it wants - no more, no less." Practically, this means that for every class, you have to double-check with the professor what file formats are acceptable. For example, my degree was in applied math, but I had required literature classes. The acceptable file format list for the literature classes included the usual MS formats and RTF. For those classes, I wrote everything in a text editor with source control, and then submitted everything in RTF using Open Office - paying very close attention to the formatting requirements on the syllabus. If you're in a math/science class, and professor lists "TeX/LaTeX" or "PDF" on the syllabus, then using TeX/LaTex/LyX is OK - as long as you can meet *every* formatting requirement on the syllabus without wasting time. If the class only allows the latest Office format, test once with LibreOffice/Onlyoffice/etc. If you get dinged for formatting, buy an academic Office 365 subscription, and then pass your docs through online Word before you hand them in. In fact, every student at my university got an Office 365 account "for free" to handle their email, calendar, etc., so I didn't even need to buy it. Feed the machine. Also, more and more professors today are adjuncts that sometimes earn close to minimum wage after accounting for all their prep time. Make them happy - or at least don't be the tall blade of grass.
