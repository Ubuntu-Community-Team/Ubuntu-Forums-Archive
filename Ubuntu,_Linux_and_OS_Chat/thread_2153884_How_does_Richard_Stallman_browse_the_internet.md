### Post by llanitedave on 2013-06-12
I admire Stallman, especially the fact that he tries to practice what he preaches.  We need more people like him.  But we can't *all* be like him. ### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2013-06-13
RMS is an idealist, the leader of an ideologicial movement.  He has an obligation to stick to his ideals as much as possible.  It's easy to argue pragmatism over ideals, but that's not the point.  

The FSF fundamentally holds that proprietary *licensing* of software is morally untenable.  It's not the *software* that's bad, it's the *licensing*.  It's not trying to take away your web browser, flash player, or favorite games; it's working toward a world where you can have those things under terms that are (In its opinion) fair and reasonable.

If they view boycotting the software as the most expedient means of promoting this, they push that.  If writing a better alternative seems more effective, they do that (this is what the GNU project is all about).  I don't think they always get it right, and I think some of their campaigns have been silly and pointless, but I at least understand where they're coming from.

To me the takeaway message is this:  there is inherent value and advantage to the user in using open source, even if you aren't personally reading or modifying the source code.  Among other things, accountability and transparency.

### Post by llanitedave on 2013-06-13
> **buzzingrobot said:**

Stallman's notions work, after a fashion, in software development because the resources are intellectual and, hence, constantly replenishable.

True in large part.  Software development is an economy of abundance, where production is essentially free.  That's partly why software patenting is so pernicious, because it's an attempt to impose an artificial scarcity on something that is not scarce by its nature.

On the other hand, that IS one of the evils of capitalism, not that it manages scarce goods and services -- it does that well -- but it attempts to CREATE scarcity to maximize profit.  And that is unethical in my book.

> 
Ditto and double ditto.  I have little tolerance for anyone who wants my behavior to be controlled by his beliefs.

I wouldn't go quite that far.  As an individual, I agree in principle, but I don't think Stallman is trying to control your behavior via imposition.  He's trying to *convince* you to behave as he would prefer by your own free will.  Big difference.  And what he's doing: speaking, writing, and setting examples, is a perfectly legitimate form of advocacy for changing beliefs and behaviors.  It's even effective to a point.

On the other hand, there are some behaviors that really shouldn't be left to the individual's choice, and that's those that damage other people.  Unfortunately, the boundaries there are sometimes a bit fuzzy.

### Post by tech7 on 2013-06-13
I dig Stallman and what he has to say.  I'm glad there are people like him in this world.  But, I also think the current system works okay.  If people are fine with trading their privacy for convenience, let them.  If you don't want to use proprietary software and want to have unlimited use of what you use, there's an option for that, too.  Where there is a limited market for an application and nobody is developing for it, it makes sense that people charge some money in exchange for improving that field's resources - if that's what they need to do to be able to do it.  I charge money for some of the software that I write.  Some of it is also free.  Some of my software is closed-source, some of it is completely free and open source.  I had identified a small, but latent market for an application that I could never justify spending so much time working on.  So, It's a paid application.  Otherwise, I could not spend enough time working on it.  Of course, I don't actually make enough money from it to quit my full-time job, but it keeps my wife from getting upset over a couple hours here and there on my computer.  
I do use gnu/linux because of it's licensing.  Just like how I use php and java because I can't possibly pay for visual studio tools, adobe or using M$ servers.  It's only because there is FOSS that I'm able to develop anything.  So, to me, it seems like the system works.  I can use FOSS to make FOSS and use FOSS to make proprietary applications that pay me for the time I spend making anything.  Besides, I keep reading where people implicate that RMS is against proprietary software, that's not entirely the case, either.  He's against monopolies and abuse in software industry.
