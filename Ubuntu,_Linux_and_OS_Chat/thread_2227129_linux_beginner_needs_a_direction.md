### Post by s9032g@comcast.net on 2014-05-31
I am a casual, but interested, Ubuntu user like yourself. I started with 8.04 installed as the only OS in a small laptop. I bought a book like "Ubuntu for Dummies" and went through it. Deciding to be adventurous, I upgraded as each new release came out, until I reached 12.04 LTS. Then I had trouble going to 12.10 and decided to back off to 12.04 LTS and just wait for the next LTS release. I now have 14.04 LTS.

There has been a learning curve dealing mostly with problems that I created for myself. The biggest help in learning has been this wonderful forum with it's very helpful Gurus who stick with me until I solve the problem. As we go I keep notes for future reference. I also read the suggestions that are always out there for " 10 things to do after getting Ubuntu 14.04" etc. Essentially I recommend just starting to use Ubuntu, don't be afraid of reasonable experimentation, and believe that you can/will be made whole if you make a mistake.

The worst I have had to do was to wipe my SSD drive, but that worked out very well. I have learned to seek out the most simple advice first as some of the proposed solutions, particularly from well meaning helpers who have not actually had the problem, turn out to be overkill.

My experience is that clean installs work out better than upgrades, though I have done both with success. A clean install just requires backing up.

All the best!  Enjoy!

### Post by LastDino on 2014-05-31
Well, ''read the book first'' aproach has never worked for me with Linux. I love reading books by the way; I've around thousand books varying from story to psychology in my house alone.
BUT, with Linux, day to day average users like us need to learn from experience and need. I strongly advise you to keep a file around to note down few  useful commands you will find soon enough. Especially from guides like ''things to do after installing Ubuntu xyz version''.

As mentioned before, Ubuntu ( and most Linux distro's) these days are quite user-friendly, but water gets muddy when you run into some problem and people all of sudden start to throw around commands as solution. This is when whatever knowledge you gain will come in handy, so don't be shy to use **man xyz xommand** whenever you've time. You can start with **man man**
