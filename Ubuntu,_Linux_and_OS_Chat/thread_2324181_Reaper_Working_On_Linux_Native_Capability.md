### Post by Bucky Ball on 2016-05-13
I suppose the only way to get some global average number would be to see if there were surveys done per country then see if you can glean some mean average by combining them. This is unlikely to give anything close to an accurate number as, unless this is a co-ordinated survey using uniform survey methods in each country, survey methods would vary. Is it judged by how many students graduate from music degrees? That would not give a meaningful number as it omits 'lay-musicians' who have gained their reading skills through other avenues (sacred music practitioners for instance, e.g. choir members, may learn reading skills very early in their lives (childhood) via non-educational institutions/organisations).

If you consider that it is generally agreed that any survey result worth a bean requires at least 1000 participants, then we start to see the magnitude of the problem. In a country like Australia, for instance, where I am, we have 22,000,000+ people. How representative would a survey of a thousand people be of the actual number of readers in the country? Combine this number with surveys of 1000 people in, say, 120 other countries and we have fudge. Of course, we can go for a larger survey group, but then we face funding issues and organisational/co-ordination issues. Who wants to know these numbers and are they curious enough to pay for the information? 

So, the numbers are elusive and we may never have any that are close to accurate, so speculating on how many do and don't may equate to speculating on how many drops are in the ocean or how many holes are in a crumpet (although the latter you could actually count). :)

There is also the question 'what is a music reader?'. e.g. despite public misconception, there are VERY few musicians, professional, secular, sacred, convervatorium trained, or otherwise, that can sight-read. i.e. play it as they are reading it. This is not sight-reading, it is speed-reading. Most sight-reading musicians need to spend a little time at least with a piece before they can confidently launch into it (even if that is a minute or two). Therefore, do we survey speed-readers only, sight-readers only, both? If a person needs to spend a week with a simple piece but can then play it faultlessly by the end of the week, are they included in the survey?

In other words, what level of proficiency qualifies someone to participate? Do we present a participant with one note on a stave and if they can identify what it is, they can read music? Or do we present a participant with a page of a piece and if they don't get at least close to accurate first run through they are out? Although if we spend some time setting conditions for the survey, it is still a conundrum and how relevant will the numbers be at the end of it?

Anyway, this is radically off-topic, so lucky its a non-support area! As a musicologist (don't tell anyone!) I couldn't help contributing that to the conversation. ;) 

Looking forward to testing out Reaper. Bring it on.
