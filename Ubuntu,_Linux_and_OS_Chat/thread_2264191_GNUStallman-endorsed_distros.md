Nice list.

I've seen people using Trisquel and had for a few years a gNewSense machine myself, though now I have OpenBSD on that hardware instead.  If you are comfortable with Ubuntu, you would be comfortable with either of  gNewSense or Trisquel .  I have not seen the others yet.  They have browsers and you can watch HTML5 video and Ogg and such, which covers my needs 100%.  They also have several options for GUI, I use FVWM there.  Non-free programs can probably be installed but are not part of the repositories.  Wifi worked fine for me, but then I was careful about which card first. ### Post by ian-weisser on 2015-02-06
> **KayeNg said:**
> Would it be too inconvenient for a newbie?

I think so.

New users are busy discovering their new environment and unlearning old Windows/Mac/Android habits.
Like where to look for software and how to install it.
Or how to get e-mail and networking started.
Or how to get their printer working.
Or trying to remember their Google password.

Ubuntu includes a lot of work to make new-user transitions easier, to ensure stuff 'just works'. That is one reason Ubuntu is the most recommended distro for new users.

Using other distros for a new user is, of course, possible...but expect the new user to have lots of questions for that distro's forums.

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2015-02-06
I agree with a lot of the above, but be aware that whatever you use, as a newcomer (or even as an old-hand) you are going to need some support. Would you buy a car that didn't have after-sales service or a piece of hardware that had no after-sales support? One of the reasons Ubuntu is popular is because it has a great support community and there's lots of us. The more 'exotic' you get with your OS, the smaller the communities, the reduced chance of support. 

I'd advise using Ubuntu for awhile and learning your way around prior to branching out. By then you may have more to give to smaller communities. Having said that, the learning curve is up to you. You can make it steep by leaping in with a reasonably obscure OS and nutting out the issues without much support, or not-so-steep by going with a well-supported release with a large and active community.

Good luck, whichever way you fly. At the end of the day it's about what suits the way you work best, not someone else's opinion about what you should and shouldn't be using. I personally use a minimal Ubuntu install, which only installs the base kernel, and customise to suit exactly how I work. I'd call it a 'hybrid'. ;)
