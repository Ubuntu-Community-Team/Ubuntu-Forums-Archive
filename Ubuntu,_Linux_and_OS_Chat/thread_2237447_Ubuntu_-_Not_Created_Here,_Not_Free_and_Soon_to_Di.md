### Post by mooreted on 2014-08-01
[http://www.turnkeylinux.org/blog/ubuntu-not-invented-here-syndrome](http://www.turnkeylinux.org/blog/ubuntu-not-invented-here-syndrome)

"Debian is superior in every way". I don't see that as being true at all. Of course I see it through the lens of an end-user, but I have tried to make Debian work for me many times and it has always ended up being more difficult to configure and in the case of my HP remote control; I couldn't get it to work at all in Debian. I have also had to use Debian Unstable and Testing to get software and packages I need because Debian is often years behind everyone else.

The "Free software community" seems stuck in the past with neck beards like Richard Stallman to me. For Linux to be a commercial success, it has have the flexibility to be configured for business as well as home use and that means a mix of free and non-free software. Gnu/Hurd is never going to be a thing. As I look around on the web for 3rd-party applications, I see downloadable packages being built almost exclusively for Ubuntu. You rarely see Debian installers. It seems to me that Ubuntu has become the platform of choice for most commercial projects.

The age-old argument of "not invented here" and not doing what some in the community think should be done are, to me, red herrings. The whole point of open source is the ability to take the code, modify it and use it however you wish. If Mark wants to go his own direction, he's perfectly within his rights to do so. Those who don't like that don't have to use Ubuntu, but why rag on Ubuntu for doing it's own thing?

After that the article just starts taking cheap pot-shots at Canonical that are old, tiresome, worn out. And arguing against utility and convenience is not helping the case for me. I happen to like utility and convenience. The free software hippie culture wants to provide me with software that doesn't even work most of the time. I would rather have commercial, open-source software that's easy to use than "freedom code" that's old, esoteric and difficult to use. The whole point of having an operating system is so that I, a lowly human, can use my computer.

I understand that some Linux - oops, Gnu/Linux - people have a value system and some kind of loose philosophy that everything in the world should be free and open, but an ordinary person like myself just wants things to work. Ubuntu is the only Linux distro I have found that seems to "just work" as they say. I have no delusions of creating world peace, I just want my computer to work.

Upstart and systemd are good examples of how things are supposed to work. Both were created to solve a problem (in this case init scripts). Both took different approaches and had their own sets of problems, but eventually, what we generically call linux standardized on one and things are unified. 

Even Mir and Wayland are following the same path. Wayland wasn't a) moving fast enough for Canonical's needs and b) had extra stuff that Canonical didn't want/need, so they started their own project. It is too early to tell which will be the choice for "linux" down the road as neither is really quite ready yet. Would it have been nice if Canonical and Wayland developers could have come up with a single solution? Of course it would, but that didn't happen. The reason had nothing to do with NIH, but instead to different use cases. Wayland is being designed as a full fledged replacement for x.org while Mir is targeted towards Canonical's mobile device plans.  Eventually, this, too will be sorted out.

It's easy to pick on Canonical and claim NIH, but it happens with every distro.  The real difference, tends to be other major distros make such announcements at their annual conferences and after much of the details happen to have been worked out by their respective communities. Canonical tends to do it on the blog page of its benevolent dictator.  That's not to say that the details haven't been worked out, it's just a manner of the communication style that makes them seem that way.

So, just look at it this way, some of the backlash is because Ubuntu is one of, if not the most popular linux distributions, when you are at the top, people will try and knock you off. In addition, as good of a distribution Canonical puts out, they could improve their communications efforts so that things don't seem so haphazard. 

Canonical tends to have a vision/plan of where they want Ubuntu to go. Not everybody shares that vision and so it opens Canonical up to criticism. On the otherhand, if they weren't as successful at what they were doing, nobody would care.  Finally, one has to remember, that bloggers often make outlandish statements to create controversy to get people to view their page. The same thing happens on most news networks. Controversy brings viewership.

### Post by Joeb on 2014-08-19
> **Gyokuro said:**
>  Debian has a much better vision as most other distributions and it's work together with upstream is superb. However everybody should make his/her own conclusion and should use what ever it's the best tool for the job.

I am a big fan of Debian. It is an excellent distribution. However, I take exception to the "vision" you talk about.  What kind of distro is debian? Is it for desktops? Is it for servers? Is it for the enterprise? Is it for cloud?  The answer of course is "Yes," to all of these questions. Debian works however you want it to; and that is the problem.  Being all things to all people creates issues for specific use cases.  For instance, should Debian include sendmail in a default install. For a server, probably yes. For a desktop, probably no.  The problem with being a multi-purpose tool that can handle all situations means that there are usually specific tools for specific situations that will outperform you. Can debian be made to perform like these specific tools. Yes, of course it can, but it takes extra effort to make it do so.

By the way, this issue/problem is not unique to Debian. Fedora is also dealing with it and has created Fedora.Next as the long term solution.  Likewise, this probably explains the numerous distros based on debian. As I said, it is a great multipurpose distro, but if you want, for instance, a desktop centric distro, you will probably go with one of the *buntus or Mint (assuming you want to stay Debian). 

Again, this is by no means a criticism of Debian, only an observation. Now, in terms of the other things you mention, particularly their upstream work, it is spot on.
