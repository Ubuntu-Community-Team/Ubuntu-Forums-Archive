### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-06-03
Nobody is entitled to anything. We are obligated to comply with the license that the software is distributed under.

[http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/selling.html](http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/selling.html)

[http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/selling-exceptions.html](http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/selling-exceptions.html)

If you sell a DVD with Ubuntu on it for whatever price you can get you will not be able to stop the purchaser from making copies and giving the copies away or also selling the copies. You cannot change the licence. Not exactly a profitable business model.

With Microsoft you buy the software but do not own it. You just purchase a license to use the software. With Free and Open Source Software (FOSS), whether we download without charge or buy a DVD with it on, the software still belongs to the authors of the software and we have the freedom to comply with the license. And it is a great freedom.

And then there is the issue of breach of copyright regarding such things as logos and trademarks. There is a reason why some derivatives of Ubuntu have "buntu" as part of the name and other derivatives do not.

[http://www.ubuntu.com/legal](http://www.ubuntu.com/legal)

[http://www.ubuntu.com/legal/terms-and-policies/intellectual-property-policy](http://www.ubuntu.com/legal/terms-and-policies/intellectual-property-policy)

[http://www.ubuntu.com/about/about-ubuntu/licensing](http://www.ubuntu.com/about/about-ubuntu/licensing)

> 
[LIST]
[*]Must allow redistribution. Your right to sell or give away the  software alone, or as part of an aggregate software distribution, is  important because:
[LIST]
[*]You, the user, must be able to pass on any software you have received from Ubuntu in either source code or compiled form.
[*]While Ubuntu will not charge licence fees for this  distribution, you might  want to charge to print Ubuntu CDs, or create  your own customised versions of Ubuntu which you sell, and should have  the freedom to do so.
[/LIST]
 			
[*]Must not require royalty payments or any other fee for  redistribution or modification.It's important that you can exercise your  rights to this software without having to pay for the privilege, and  that you can pass these rights on to other people on exactly the same  basis.
[*]Must allow these rights to be passed on along with the software.  You should be able to have exactly the same rights to the software as we  do.
[/LIST]

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-06-04
> **grahammechanical said:**
> 
If you sell a DVD with Ubuntu on it for whatever price you can get you will not be able to stop the purchaser from making copies and giving the copies away or also selling the copies. You cannot change the licence. Not exactly a profitable business model.


Precisely.  In the early days of Linux, in the mid 1990's or so, before the blossoming of the internet allowed for easy distribution, some businesses did market retail Linux. You could buy it on shelves next to the Windows boxes. That's how Red Hat began, for example.  They got out of that business because the source was freely available, of course.  

Branding, etc., can be trademarked and that can't be copied or re-distributed. You can rebuild the source but you can't market or redistribute something that can be construed as the original product. CentOS, for example, is a straightforward rebuild of Red Hat source with all the Red Hat logos, artwork, etc., removed and replaced.  (Which is one reason why "Linux" is trademarked by Torvalds.)
