### Post by lysander6662 on 2017-03-28
I'm a fan of the glass look which was started in the mid 2000s [my first exposure to it was in Windows Vista and it was perfected in Windows 7]. Though these days it's clear that it's become outmoded. The flat look came into trend with the proliferation of the hipster movement - things that are simple, industrialised, functional but also visually appealing, retro and elegant [to use Budgie's terminology]. Windows 8 uses the flat look as does Windows 10. On the Linux side, many DEs use the flat look though Unity 7 still uses glass look, though it has been replaced with the flat look for Unity 8. 

The more I look at Unity 7 these days, the more dated it looks to me. I am, however, not a fan of the flat look mostly. It looks boring, childlike, almost patronising. However, the one DE that does it well is Budgie. The reason for this is to do with the fact that Budgie has a minimalist look and feel, which works well with flat. I don't like the look of Deepin or Manjaro that seem to make more of a maximised look of the flat style. 

So my question is, do you prefer the glass look or the flat look? Which DEs do you think do them best [or not?]

"Hipster" used to be a derogatory word, maybe, but it's now fully accepted as a contemporary subculture with its own styles, philosophies and locales [certainly in London anyway]. EDIT, I've been trawling the web and came across three interesting article that relate to flat design. There a very nice passage in this one which is relevant to start off with:

> What we consider &#8216;appropriate looking&#8217; today in the medial industry is affected by the products which are created by much faster moving industries such as consumer electronics. Whether you love it or hate it this new GUI style is here to stay (at least for a couple of years) and if it looks a bit odd now, we&#8217;ll soon get used to it.

[https://www.team-consulting.com/insights/shifting-trends-in-gui-design/](https://www.team-consulting.com/insights/shifting-trends-in-gui-design/)

So GUIs are influenced by consumer products. In this case, flat design was born out of a kind of necessity to make better use of mobile device screens, taking inspiration from minimalist print design from the mid 20th c [there's the hipster connection, since the original hipster movement came in the 1940s]:

> It is important to note too one of the reasons flat design has become so popular in recent years as well is the development of responsive design. As more devices are connecting to the Web, with various screen sizes and browser constraints, designers are finding that their tried-and-true design styles that relied heavily on textures, drop shadows, and fixed imagery don&#8217;t translate as well when you have to shrink those designs into smaller and smaller viewports.

Flat design allows for Web design to become more efficient. Without extra design elements in the way, websites can load much faster and are easier to resize and form around the content it holds.
This also goes hand in hand with our screens becoming more high-def and the need to display crisper imagery. It is much easier to display crisp boxes and typography than it is to make several different images to accommodate all the various devices and features out there.

[https://thenextweb.com/dd/2014/03/19/history-flat-design-efficiency-minimalism-made-digital-world-flat/3/https://thenextweb.com/dd/2014/03/19/history-flat-design-efficiency-minimalism-made-digital-world-flat/3/](https://thenextweb.com/dd/2014/03/19/history-flat-design-efficiency-minimalism-made-digital-world-flat/3/https://thenextweb.com/dd/2014/03/19/history-flat-design-efficiency-minimalism-made-digital-world-flat/3/)
