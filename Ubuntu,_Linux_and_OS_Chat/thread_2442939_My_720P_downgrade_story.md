### Post by TheFu on 2020-05-09
HD = 720p
Full HD = 1080i/p

Monitors and TVs are different. They have 2 different purposes.
In 2005, I bought a laptop that was 1440x900 resolution. 

And got "HD Ready"  50" rear projection TV. The "ready" part meant that the tuner didn't support HD signals, but those were 3+ yrs away from happening, so it didn't matter, except there were Component inputs (RGB over 3 wires) for analog 1080i video inputs. A few years later, the CATV company started deploying cable boxes that supported 1080i content.
A few years after that, I picked up a 720p 37inch TV.  According to all the reviews I've seen, the human eye can't tell the difference between 1080 and 720 until the TV screen gets over 40 inches. I find that easy to believe based on my experience. 

As both those TVs stopped working, I replace only 1 with a normal 1080p projector that does about 120 inch projection. Cannot imagine ever using a regular TV again for video stuff.

I've never tried to use a TV as a computer monitor.  For a very long time, I've had dual 24inch 1200p monitors in my home office. Had a few laptops with 768p and 600p resolutions, but those were always painful to use. Felt those systems were always missing 500+ pixels.  1080p is my minimal resolution for any laptop since 2015.

The people of the world have all been duped by the monitor marketing people with the HD and FullHD B.S.  We've gotten less than we had before purely by marketing.

### Post by DuckHook on 2020-05-09
HDTVs are so common these days that they can serve as very cost-effective monitors provided that one is careful and buys something with sufficient refresh and full&#8209;range chroma.

When my old monitor gave up the ghost a few months ago, I did some research into using 4K TVs for monitors. Everyone is going to have different ideas about picture/colour/aesthetics, so I won't comment on that, but I did find that TVs can be fine monitor replacements. My biggest challenge was finding TVs with full&#8209;range 4&#8209;4&#8209;4 chroma. This spec almost never appears on spec sheets or websites, so one has to trawl through specialist websites to find the info. Here's one: [https://www.rtings.com/tv/reviews/best/by-usage/pc-monitor](https://www.rtings.com/tv/reviews/best/by-usage/pc-monitor)

Most TVs come with 4&#8209;4&#8209;2 or even 4&#8209;2&#8209;2 chroma. These render unreadable type on the screen, so I think 4&#8209;4&#8209;4 chroma is an absolute must&#8209;have. Aside from that, make sure that your TV is at least 60 Hz P. Anything less could give you headaches and eyestrain. It's not worth physical discomfort and eyestrain just to save a few bucks. Most TVs are not fast enough for serious gaming. Be aware of this going in. Monitors are still more expensive than TVs partially because TVs sell in far higher volumes, but also because monitors have faster internal circuitry and less lag.

### Post by Shibblet on 2020-05-11
I used to work at privately owned electronics store.  We carried Samsung, LG, Sony, and a smattering of other Televisions.

When the 4K TV's first came in, we were excited to see how much better the picture was than the 1080p (or 2K) televisions.

Sony sent us this Hard Drive media player that output 4K content via HDMI and a specialized HDMI switch that would split the signal to multiple TV's.  So, we set this all up, and stood back and marveled at the new 4K content, and how amazing the picture was.  

After a couple of weeks, we started getting used to the 4K content, and instead of walking up to the TV, we were looking at them from what I would call "reasonable" distance.  Like 6 feet away from 55", or 8 feet away from a 65" and so on.  Then a customer who was waiting for something else, asked if her daughter could watch "Frozen" until she was ready to go.  I happily fired up the Blu-Ray (not an upscaler) and connected it to just one of the TV's.  The 65" Sony XBR.

I stood back about 6 feet, and COULD NOT tell the difference between the 4K content, and the 1080p Blu-Ray.  They both looked amazing!  So, I decided to compare the content on a 1080p television.

The 1080p TV didn't look as good as it's 4K counter part on the "Frozen" Blu-Ray.  I think that has to do with the 4K TV's pixel density.  But on the 4K TV's, at reasonable watching distance, you CAN NOT see any difference between 1080p content, and 4K.

So, then what, I wonder, is the point of an 8K TV?

### Post by SeijiSensei on 2020-05-11
> **Shibblet said:**
> So, then what, I wonder, is the point of an 8K TV?
To create demand for televisions.  For most of my life we had analog 4:3 NTSC sets because that was the standard for decades. They just kept getting bigger. At the end I had a 32" inch Sharp tube TV that weighed a ton.  Eventually, though, extracting profits just by making things bigger can't be sustained because it runs up against technological and practical limitations. Now that 55" 4K TVs [cost just $300-500 or so]("https://www.bestbuy.com/site/promo/tvs-4k-smart-tv-savings?qp=tvscreensizeplusrange_facet%3DTV%20Screen%20Size~55%22%20-%2064%22"), the margins are so slim that its just a volume business. Adding a new technology like 4/8K usually brings a shot to profits as some people upgrade and pay the high prices charged when the innovation is first released. Over time, though, the incredible economies now available in chip and screen production destroy those excess profits, so the manufacturers need a new innovation to provide another short-term profit burst.

I have a computer connected to my 55" inch TV.  I use 1280x720 all the time because I cannot read small 1920x1080 text from across the room. Video games with lots of detailed on-screen text (Witcher, WoW, etc.) are impossible for me to read at 1080p. (I'm seventy; it comes with the territory.)  My desktop display is 1440x900, but it's only two feet away.
