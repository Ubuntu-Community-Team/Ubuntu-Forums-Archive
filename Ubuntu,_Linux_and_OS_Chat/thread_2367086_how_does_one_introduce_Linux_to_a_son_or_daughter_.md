You cannot control what the teachers or the school do. By sending your kids to school you are implicitly trusting the school on teaching your kids the necessary skills they will need in their life, and you are also accepting that your kids will have to comply with the school's rules and everything they are requested. If you cannot trust the school then you would be homeschooling your kids yourself.

So, if in school they teach, use, and basically demand Microsoft products, you may as well have a computer at home with the same tools for them to complete their homework successfully. You can always have the Linux option available at home as well, but in my opinion it would be wrong to unnecessarily force them to use Linux products, if it would be much simpler for them to just use the same software as the school. As long as you continue using Linux, teaching by example, they will probably not abandon Linux, and will see the advantages of using both systems.

Office work environments are similar in this aspect. If the office practically requires a particular Microsoft product like an Exchange account, Outlook for email, calendar, and scheduling meetings, and particular programs for accounting, management, and what not, then you should use that, despite your personal preference in free software. It is troublesome to go against the establishment and find free alternatives, if the benefits are not significant. I started using Ubuntu about 10 years ago. I stopped 5 years ago because I got a job where I had to use Microsoft products all the time, so during this period I really had no space for Linux, although I did try to squeeze in free software where I could. Now, I'm in a different job, so again I am free to use Linux as much as I want. I don't need to share my documents with others, so I have liberty choosing my tools, such as LibreOffice, and others.

So, to answer more directly your question, I would provide the tools the kids require, Office, Visual Studio, whatever; I want them to be successful at school. But I would also give them alternatives at home. "Hey, look at this Python programming, you can do cool stuff as well." "Hey, you can play some cool games in Linux too." "Hey, you can create graphical applications with Qt too." "Hey, look at this drawing, 3D modeler program, pretty cool, eh?"

### Post by QIII on 2017-07-26
I have always (and still do) recommend the use of whatever tools are required by the institution's curriculum.  That is the path most likely to result in the academic success required to move forward to further academic endeavors or to gain the skills for gainful employment.  Making a statement about free software is not only unnecessary, it is a detrimental distraction.  A student's "job" is to do well in school.  It is not to be a Linux evangelist.

My opinion, then, is to provide BOTH environments.  Windows for school work to avoid problems, Linux for personal use and learning.  As a parent, I felt it was incumbent on me to provide for my children every resource for their success even at the cost of my interests - and finances.

Multi-booting or virtualization will provide the opportunity for perfect compliance with school work AND the opportunity to learn to use Linux, the latter being a skill also in demand in the workplace.  Use of OSes is not an either/or choice.  It is not a marriage requiring that a person cleave to one and abandon all others.  There is no moral victory in using Linux instead of "the evil OS".  

One man's opinion.
