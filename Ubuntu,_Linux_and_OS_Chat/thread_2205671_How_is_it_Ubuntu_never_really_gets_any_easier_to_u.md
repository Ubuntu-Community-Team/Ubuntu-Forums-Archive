### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2014-02-15
You know, it's an interesting thing that many of us have observed over the years; the people who have the hardest time figuring out Linux are those who are long-time "technical" users of Windows.  They tend to come into the experience with too many presuppositions, too many biases, and an unwillingness to be a "newbie" at anything computer-related.

Take a moment to reflect on the fact that millions of really very ordinary people use Linux on the desktop every day.  Over the years on various Linux communities I've met people from all walks of life, from teenagers to octagenerians, from countries all over the world who use and maintain Linux on their computers.  They are not all programmers or computer scientists; some were students, pastors, truck drivers, homemakers, school teachers, soldiers, writers, or musicians.  I bet if we did a roll-call of what people do with their lives, we'd get quite a diverse list here.

Ultimately, nobody really cares what OS you choose to run on your system; if you sincerely *want* to avail yourself of this excellent, free option called "Ubuntu" (or any of the many other high-quality Linux distributions out there), then you'll undoubtedly have to put forward some effort to learn some new things.  The only one holding you back is you.

### Post by Don_Stahl on 2014-02-16
I think it's as easy , and sometimes easier, to install Ubuntu -- or Manjaro, or Salix -- than to install Windows. Most of the distros I've tried recently use GUI installers, all of them recognize the hardware I'm using, and all of them install faster than Win 7. 

If you're installing Windows as a second OS on a machine already formatted to run Linux, you're going to have problems. That's because of Windows' inability to recognize other OS's partition formatting, right? Not Linux's or Ubuntu's problem -- Linux, BSD, and BeOS all recognize* Windows *partitions just fine. Ubuntu installs just fine as a second OS. It's just Windows that balks.

I don't get that Ubuntu is harder to install than Windows. I think it's not true. I suspect anyone who has trouble installing Linux would have trouble installing Windows. 

I think the salient point is that 89% of the PCs in the West are sold with Windows already installed, and 10% are sold with Mac OSX. Users think that their computer is what they see when they boot up. "What's an 'operating system'? I just point and click!" If somebody installed Zorin on a bunch of PCs then when the average user got his new PC running he'd say "Huh! They've made the menus cleaner, and it seems snappier. Technology sure is improving!" And he'd go on working on his ground-breaking novel, or his definitive list of orchids of Madagascar, or whatever other genius stuff he's doing.

But presented with a bare box, the average user would have no more trouble installing Ubuntu -- or Manjaro, or Salix -- than installing Windows. Which is to say, if he had a DVD and could follow simple instructions, he'd probably do OK. Probably. 

At least that's what I kind of think. Could be wrong. Was only wrong 3,209 times yesterday, so figure the odds.

(Edit: no, a user wouldn't think Zorin was Windows, just because most users know Windows software. Once they pulled up the menu and started clicking, they'd find out that the applications are different and so is the filesystem. So that part of my post is a bit of fantasy... )
