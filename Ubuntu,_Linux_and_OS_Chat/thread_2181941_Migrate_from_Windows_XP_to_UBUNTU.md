### Post by The Cog on 2013-10-19
I think what you want to do can for the most part be done. 
LibreOffice is quite good at handling word and excel documents, although some compatibility issues occur from time to time.
File sharing should not be a problem. 
I've never heard of ip messenger so I can't comment on that.
The switchover will not be seamless though - there is a new OS to learn and configuration to work out.
If you think switching to Ubuntu will be easy then you will be surprised how hard it is, but if you think it will be hard you may be surprised how easy it is. 

My advice is to get an Ubuntu machine and try using it for a few weeks - find out how well it fits into your environment. 
If you have to decide on the laptops right now, go for windows and work on Ubuntu for the next purchasing round.
It can do pretty-much everything you want but it is not just a drop-in replacement. It needs careful planning.

### Post by vandammes on 2013-10-19
I work in a 3-person small consulting firm. 3 years ago I put LinuxMint on a 5 year old XP machine (dual boot) and haven't looked back. Everyone else is stuck with Windows; they use Word, Powerpoint, and Excel which I have to collaborate on, using LibreOffice. Most of the time here are no incompatibilities, but (rarely) I use a remote desktop program over to a Win 7 machine to make sure that docs are "Microsoft compliant". I can also import PDFs and edit them (and export of course).  I also do desktop publishing, movie editing, and graphics, both photo editing and vector. And the occasional electronic circuit simulation and schematic drawing. I prefer Mint, but Zorin has several "windows lookalike" desktops, and there are plenty you can install on Ubuntu with some fiddling (Cinnamon, Gnome, KDE, E17, XFCE, LXDE...) that will suit your equipment performance and taste.

We use Google Docs which is browser based and we can share and edit our documents that way. I sometimes use Microsoft Office365 but it's pretty lame. I can see all shared files on XP machines over the network easily. Win7 machines are not so easy.

So, using your present machines, you can have better performance (less overhead), no virus worries, and a modern OS which comes with most of the apps you need. Or get new machines and blazing speed without the Microsoft drama and cost.

The downside is unfamiliarity. You should appoint an Uber-Geek to get familiar with Ubuntu and learn all the tricks, and install the systems, and give users their ten minute orientation on where their apps are. It's not as hard as learning "computers" from scratch. 

There are lots of desktop machines you can buy or build without OSes. There's also some really nice solid Linux laptops like System76, or Dell if you can find them. Or get Chromebooks (better units coming) and put a "real" Linux on them.

Go for it! You have nothing to lose but your chains.

### Post by QIII on 2013-10-20
*You are a business person with enough headaches.  Don't give yourself heartburn over a "one or the other" choice you don't have to make.*

Something not mentioned yet is this:

You can keep a Win XP Pro (or buy Win 7 Pro or Ultimate) machine on your LAN and load it up with one copy of Office.

Win 7 Pro and Ultimate allow two people to remotely access the desktop (RDP) concurrently.  So, if you absolutely *must *use a Microsoft product from time to time, Bill and Sam could RDP into the Windows machine to use Office (get a two-user license).  Jane would have to wait until one or the other of them is logged off to log in to do her work.

I have a Win 7 Pro machine on my home network, and I RDP to it using remmina with the RDP add-on.  No moving from computer to computer or using a KVM switch.  I just keep remmina running and connected to that machine.  When I need to work on Windows, I just spin the desktop cube (you'll find out all about that) and start writing C# in Visual Studio.  If you don't use multiple desktops, you can just maximize/minimize remmina like any other program.

By way of example, in the following image, I am using my Kubuntu machine, which has three monitors attached and the desktop spanning all three.  On the left monitor is a remote desktop connection to a different *physical machine* running Ubuntu (Unity) using NoMachine NX, the center is Firefox on my primary Kubuntu machine and on the right is a connection to a *third physical* machine running Win 7 using remmina.  I can just move my mouse from screen to screen and interact with all three computers without having to flip any switches or hot-keys.  Three completely separate physical machines, one keyboard, one mouse, no KVM switch and no need to use any hot-keys to rotate between them.

I guess what I am saying is that you don't have to make a total break.  You can have your cake and eat it, too.  You can give two people at a time access to Windows with the cost of just one machine.  The whole office can use that machine, but no more than two may be logged in to it at a time.  Nobody has to get up from their desk and move to a different location.  But you'd have to manage the resource just like anything else.  

I'm not a Linux evangelist if that means getting people to dump Windows and use only Linux.  I say to use what works, what your clients expect you to use, etc.  My evangel is that you can have both.  I do.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2013-10-20
> what we wish to know is can we work in the same environment as in microsoft office 'can earlier files created in ms office be imported and edited in ubuntu
Depends on the version of Office and the components in question.  Older Office versions (2007) create files that are better handled by LibreOffice than newer versions.  Plus, although Word and Excel files generally work well, the same can not be said of Powerpoint -- especially if you're using OLE to embed stuff in the ppt files.  Unfortunately, Libreoffice is not a plug-in replacement for MS Office.  For more info, see the following WineHQ website page: [http://appdb.winehq.org/objectManager.php?sClass=application&iId=31]("http://appdb.winehq.org/objectManager.php?sClass=application&iId=31")
> can we run ubuntu and windows in the same office on the same network on seperate computers and still use print sharing on one printer Once again, depends ... this time, on the make and model printer.  Some makes (e.g., HP) tend to work better than others.
> is there a interface between two os if used in the same office on the same network on seperate computers is interface still possibleYou can use Samba to exchange files between the different PCs.
> is file sharing possible.Depends ... on the direction.  Linux machines can access and read files from Windows PCs and can even write to them -- although I wouldn't advise that unless you are using shared "data" partitions.  Writing to Windows OS partitions risks filesystem corruption and boot loader damage.  Windows, however, can't read or understand Linux filesystems.
[QUOTEcan common internal messaging programmes like ip messenger be installed and used between 2 computers one using ubuntu and the other ms windows on the same network.[/QUOTE]Don't use it; don't know.
> can the switchover from ms windows to ubuntu be done seamlessly and without any pain or headaches or loss of productivity. NO switchover between different OSs, different desktops, different filesystems, and different apps is going to be "seamless", and every such change is going to have an initial loss of productivity.  There's stuff to learn when migrating between these two different worlds -- and there's no way around that.  It would be a similar situation if you decided to upgrade all the existing PCs to Windows 8 and force folks to use the Tiles desktop.  And, with any learning experience, there will be those people who handle it well, and those who do not.
> is the shorcuts in ms office and ubuntu the sameNo -- different apps, different desktops.

### Post by jmarkrobinson on 2013-10-20
I am also new to Linux, however, I have been doing IT work for 34 years and can speak to system migration.

To save you and your entire staff a lot of undue stress, use the "parallel" migration process.  What I mean is, you or whoever your key IT person is should start with a new Linux box and get to know it inside out, backwards and inside out.  They should also retain their Windows box and use both.  Why both?  If (s)he runs into issues with the Linux box, they can still fallback to the Windows box until they are satisfied that they can be as productive with Linux as Windows.

Then you move on to your office staff and do exactly the same thing.  Start with the staff that have the least "professional" skills as they will likely migrate far faster.  Again, two computers, they start working with Linux and retain the Windows box until they are proficient with the Linux box.

Office professionals will be the most challenging to work with, especially those involved in accounting.  You don't want to second guess anything in that department.  I STRONGLY advise that these persons and their immediate support staff operate dual computers for a minimum of 6 months, longer if it is a bigger accounting system.  Any "mission critical" application needs to be installed on a Linux PC by the IT people and they need to work with it until it appears functional.  AT this point is where it can be really challenging.  You hand off to accounting and they really SHOULD do all of their work TWICE, once on each system for at least 6 months or longer and then have one staff person responsible for Quality Control audits on ALL reports to make sure that they are both giving the identical data.  There is one exception to this rule:  If your mission critical apps and/or data is driven from a centralized server, then the Linux PC becomes no more than a "dumb terminal" at which point, the users only need become familiar with the desktop operation.

Do not interpret this as being "Linux" is expensive in terms of a migration.  It matters not what platforms are involved in an extensive migration of systems, it costs money to make sure that you have everything AFTER the migration that you had BEFORE.  I have been involved in migrations of multi million dollar legacy (ie mainframe) systems and the added time and expense of migration is part of the cost of doing business.   Oh, and if you think that a user's desk occupying two PC's is a challenge, try working in a large data center and figure out how to jam two mainframe systems into the space that was designed for one mainframe and run them both for 18 months.  I've done it. We practically had to crawl over and under equipment for more than a year!  We were never so happy as to get the all clear and then get to work an entire weekend just to remove the old mainframe hardware!
