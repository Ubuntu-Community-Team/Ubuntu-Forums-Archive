### Post by ian-weisser on 2015-02-08
The OEMs, not Ubuntu nor Canonical, decide what hardware is appropriate for the market they want to sell to. 
So you might ask BQ about extending the life of an older, midrange model by trying a new OS on it with a feel, style, and features unlike anything their competition has.
Seems like a fairly low-risk experiment that has already paid off in press coverage.

I must admit, my mother-in-law likes her Ubuntu laptop, and is comfortable with it. She has not been comfortable with her android phones or iphones (courtesy of well-intentioned children). She might be interested a phone with a comfortable, familiar look-and-feel.

### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-02-09
For all we know BQ might be using existing stock and not starting a new production run. And so the production costs have already been met and they run down the stock inventory for this device without selling it at a discount. It would be the smart thing to do. If the product gets a good reception then they may risk installing Ubuntu on other devices. And it is a risk for any supplier. They invest money in production and need to not only recover the costs but make profit.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-02-09
As an Ubuntu user, I hope the phone is a profitable success. I use Ubuntu, which Canonical gives away.  Giving away a product is, by definition, unsustainable unless resources -- money -- are plowed back into the effort.

The phone market here in the U.S. is different enough from much of the rest of the world -- a small corporate oligarchy controls the market, costs to users are higher than elsewhere, users are required to sign lengthy contracts that effectively eliminate our ability to choose, many phones lack SIM cards, etc. -- that I wonder why anyone would tailor a product to sell to 340 million Americans while forgetting about the other 6 billion+ people on the planet.

I own a Nexus 5 and I'm not looking to buy a new phone. But, I find myself increasingly using the "smart" features of the phone less and less. Voice calls, messaging and a bit of mail is what I do with it. So, I'm all for efforts by Canonical or anyone else to disrupt the market enough so the next time this American customer looks for a new phone I have more, cheaper, better choices all around.
