### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-09-21
Regardless of the virtues of Linux, it lacks the marketing clout to become a viable candidate to assume the role of Windows, even if Windows was to seriously stumble.   That seems unlikely, even given the reception of Windows 8. (Windows 7 is still alive, supported, and popular.)

Even if Windows stumbled often enough to threaten the financial viability of Microsoft, there is no reason to think Windows would vanish, or be replaced by Linux.  If faced with bankruptcy and collapse, Microsoft would simply sell Windows. 

I can't imagine a scenario that sees any Linux distribution assume Windows' role and become the dominant, monopolistic, OS. Significant and influential parts of the Linux community seem to think that any efforts to market Linux are heretical. Their road to Linux success depends on converting people to believe in the same ideology as they do.  That won't work in the future any more than it has in the past.

Apple?  Well, they're interested in revenue and profit, not simply market share and not simply conquering the universe. Looks like they're doing just fine at that.

Linux is a fine OS, in most of its many guises.  Just keep making it better and let things take care of themselves.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-09-21
Sorry, a world where people 'share things' without money won't happen until there is an endless free supply of anything and everything anyone could ever want.

We have money, and economies, and greed, because the things people want and need are in limited supply.  As they are consumed, they must be replaced.  Inevitably, some people desire A more than B, and commerce happens.

I.e., you can't share the food you just gave to your kids. And, their next meal depends on you competing to acquire more food. So, rather than share something others might want, you will sell it so you can buy food.

Even wild animals compete for territory because they need to ensure access to food and shelter.

### Post by anachronism2 on 2013-09-26
For all of my life until two weeks ago, I've been a Windows user, and I've decided to make the jump into the unknown. This is my opinion on the subject: 

Microsoft Windows 8 is going towards the ease-of-use direction. It's not my cup of tea; every time I see Windows 8 interface, I think, "Where's the terminal? The lists? The raw data?" Each to their own and different strokes for different folks. If ease of use, a picture-oriented GUI intensive interface (Does Windows 8 even have a power shell?), security, and guranteed support in exchange for cost and immutability float your boat, more power to you. Quite frankly I hate the difficulty of importing modules. I don't think I'll ever like coding in Windows unless I was on the dev team and didn't have so many restrictions.

What draws me to Linux is my desire to understand how a computer works, the hardware and the software, how it interacts to produce the interface I use. I would like to work towards a grasp of that and of programming core concepts, and Linux offers a mouth-watering array of development tools. I ever so enjoy the right to pull the tablecloth off of programs and study their inner workings. The latent ability to literally modify every last thing, even the kernel itself, if I had the ability, gives me the future possibility of  a backed-up testbed to fiddle with to my heart's content. The nature of Linux, to stave off most kinds of malware, is also a beautiful thing, that may eventually allow me to fiddle with running a basic server for a blog or something. All in all, I am loving this experience, and I am loving the challenge. I have no doubts that I'll continue to have wonderful and heartbreaking experiences with Linux distros. 

I say heartbreaking because, well, I'm an idiot. Watch me foolishly charge into this novelty without a care; I installed the version 12.04 of Ubuntu after testing version 10.04 for a while; poof, my laptop isn't compatible because of the drivers, and I can't adjust brightness or power, nor can I hibernate. But, plenty of other distros to try, I'm not stuck with Ubuntu if the drivers specs aren't available. I also pulled off the classic pratfall of re-installing Windows 7 stripped down to defaults. On the positive side, I now have a nice copy of Linux-secure-remix to add to my collection. I'm not too worried, though. With system backups and images via Clonezilla, I feel like learning, challenging ,and tampering with anything I want to, and the number one thing I appreciate is the free ability to do so. Thank you, everyone, for all of your hard work, and if I ever have the ability, and the hardware, I very much so want to go testing all the shiny new alpha and beta projects.

 I also honestly want to know if a novice has any place in this community. Linux OSesses(?) only function from the combined efforts of the thousands of people who use and contribute to it, based on the logic that those who use the system and like it would want to do their bit to improve it, whether that's testing the new stuff, supporting the LTS stuff, or forging the code. However I can understand that the trivial nothingness of new users like me may very well be unwelcome. Who in their right mind wants to go out of the way to help with things that should be obvious? As much as I would like to be a useful member of this forum and the Linux community, I am no expert, I'm a greenhorn, and right now I'm not sure, from the syntax of many people who do know what they're doing, if that's okay. If it's not okay to start out knowing close to nothing on a forum of experts, things could be more difficult for a while, but I can accept that and politely leave. I would like to learn the essentials, though.
