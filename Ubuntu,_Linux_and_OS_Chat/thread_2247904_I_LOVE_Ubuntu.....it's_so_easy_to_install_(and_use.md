### Post by Mike_Walsh on 2014-10-11
Morning, all.

Well, now; following hard on the heels of someone who hates Ubuntu, 

[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2247875](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2247875)

here's a success story...

I gotta confess, I love Ubuntu's ease of installation. I started off with Ubuntu back in May. After running Windoze XP for YEARS, I made the switch to Ubuntu 14.04, having become thoroughly disillusioned with MS. So I waved bye-bye to Redmond, and installed it on my elderly Compaq Presario desktop. Okay, it was still in the fairly early days of the release; there were a few teething problems (mainly the wireless connection), but by the time of the 3.13-0.32 kernel release, that was fixed.

I then installed Lubuntu 14.04 onto my even older Dell Inspiron 'brick' of a laptop (an 1100). It was in a terminal state of sluggishness with XP (the 'Netburst' Celeron didn't help matters, having a tiny cache), but.....it DID have SSE2s, so I carried on, and.....voila! Another success story; it flies now!

Last week, I installed Ubuntu to a mate's Dell Inspiron 530s desktop. He'd been running Vista (ack!!) for 5 or 6 years, and I'd been his unofficial 'go-to' guy for maintenance and stuff; he's the first to admit he is NOT technically-minded when it comes to 'puters. Now, with my years of experience with these things, I could have kicked myself for not noticing this, but..... I was attempting to install Service Pack 2, as it hadn't been receiving updates for a while. I then find out that because Service Pack 1 had never been installed (I don't know HOW I missed that, but anyway...), the system wouldn't allow Service Pack 2 to install itself, and.....guess what? Yep, SP1 wasn't available anymore.....

I explained to him that this meant his system was pretty insecure, and he said, "Well, what can we do about it?" So I said you've got 2 options, really; upgrade the O/S (which meant buying 8 or 8.1, and I wouldn't wish those on my worst enemy......they're problematical, to say the least), or he could go for a Linux distribution, like me. He knew I'd been using Ubuntu for a little while (I'd shown the Live-CD to him, back in June, and he quite liked it!), so he asked me what it would cost him. I said it wouldn't cost him a penny; just the data cost of downloading the updates; at which point he said, "Go for it!" So I did.....

Yet another flawless, beautifully easy install. 2 hours, tops, including tweaking, updating, and installing the software he wanted.....mainly Chromium (he's used to Chrome), Shotwell, and Abiword (which seems to work easier with his printer, for some strange reason; Libre Office was being 'awkward'); and he's chuffed to bits with how easy it is to use. Even his mobile broadband dongle worked 'out-of-the-box'....!!

And that brings me to last night. My Mum's got a Dell Inspiron N5110 laptop (approx 4 yrs old); Core-i5 2410M CPU, 6 GB of RAM, 500 GB Sata2 hard drive.....fairly modern, and up to date.She's seen me using Ubuntu, and has said on many occasions that she likes the look of it. So I said I could put it on her laptop, alongside Windows 7, and she could then decide which one to use... "OK", she said, and off I went again.....

That HAD to be the fastest install yet! Including resizing her Win 7 partition, creating extended, logical & swap partitions, installing Ubuntu, tweaking, updating and setting up the software she wanted (she's another one who likes Chrome, so in went Chromium again), and setting up Grub for Win 7 auto-starting.....an hour and three-quarters, tops. SO easy, it was untrue.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

I don't know whether it's anything to do with the fact that most of my family & friends all seem to prefer Dells, but every single install I've done has been totally straight-forward, and SO easy compared to Windows, with its multiple re-starts, and all the associated mucking about that seems to come with it.  It could ALSO have something to do with the fact that not ONE of these machines, so far, has had the dreaded UEFI/SecureBoot setup; just a plain old vanilla BIOS...

I guess 30-odd years of 'playing around' with these things must have helped a LITTLE bit, too...! :D

I've read countless tales of woe on here from various folk; whether it's a Win 7 / Ubuntu dual-boot, or replacing Windoze with Ubuntu, or dual-booting with Win 8/8.1 (*gag*); whatever the scenario, most of these seem to involve Asus, Acer, Lenovo, Toshiba, and similar makes. You just don't hear of that many problems with Dells! Whatever the reason, I'm becoming more & more of a convert as the months go by, and am more than happy to 'spread the word'.

More power to Ubuntu (and by association, to Canonical, for making everything so easy)..! \\:D/

Regards,

Mike.

True that! I've got a 4 year old Gateway NV59c that Ubuntu 14.04 installs and runs almost perfectly on (Intel graphics drivers kinda suck for it, but work well enough for everything but gaming - got Windows 8.1 for that). Never had any real issues setting up Ubuntu on it before.

My Macbook 4,1, on the other hand, was a bit of a pain to install and setup 14.04 on.

First, my wireless didn't work properly. Yes, it saw that there were WiFi access points available, but it wouldn't connect properly to them. I wound up having to use an ethernet connection to download a different set of Broadcom drivers and discarding the ones that came preinstalled. Works fine now.

Second, my iSight camera didn't work. I had to find the driver online and manually put the right file in the right directory and reboot. Now it works fine.

Third, I had a problem with my audio output that caused extreme irritation for me. It wound up requiring me to permanantly disable PulseAudio so that everything would use ALSA instead. As a result, I lost my sound indicator icon. Replaced it with QasMixer, and have it set to start automatically on startup. I also lost the functionality of my volume keys. So I wrote up some scripts and assigned them to keyboard shortcuts that would execute them to control my volume. Now my audio works almost flawlessly.

Again, as QIII pointed out, Apple hardware is not very typical for most Ubuntu users. As such, it must be assumed that there will be a few hicups in trying to make it work properly, and the knowlege and ability to workaround the problems, or admit that it may just not be worthwhile to use Ubuntu on that particular hardware. This, however, does not reflect on the ease of use of Ubuntu in general. It just reflects on its compatibility issues with some sets of hardware.

For my old Macbook, though, it was worthwhile to install and muck around with it until I sorted out the problems as Apple no longer supports it. The latest OS X release that will run on it is Lion, while 14.04 is supported for several years and works pretty darn well with the computer after implementing a few workarounds (that are quite quick and simple if you just follow some instructions and are willing to put some time in researching it).

Anywho, in general, Ubuntu is pretty darn easy to install and use in **MOST** cases.
