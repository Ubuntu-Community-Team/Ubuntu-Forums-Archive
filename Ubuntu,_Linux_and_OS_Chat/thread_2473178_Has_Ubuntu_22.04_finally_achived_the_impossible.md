I genuinely hope Ubuntu continues work on snaps and does not give in to other packaging options. I feel they can incrementally improve them over time, they have made progress since they were introduced and I would like to see them enjoy success with them. Windows has always been very difficult to compete with but Ubuntu is gaining a lot of ground now!

Most have given up on "the year of the Linux desktop" but Ubuntu might just be quietly achieving that. Currently I have Windows 11 and Ubuntu 22.04 development build installed on two computers and use both on a regular basis. As far as look and feel go, Ubuntu 22.04 is just as pleasurable to use as Windows 11. Both systems have been reliable, keeping in mind 22.04 is still in development. The workflow in 22.04 just seems natural and kind of fun actually. I preform the same tasks with both systems and they both work well. Windows has a lot of annoying ad crap that had to be turned off, Ubuntu does not have this problem!

I see the glass half full for Ubuntu rather than half empty! I can see all their hard work paying off! Ubuntu is not going to replace Windows overnight but I can picture more user's at least looking more seriously at it.

### Post by DuckHook on 2022-03-26
I admire your enthusiasm and am quite touched by your optimism.

But&#8212;you could probably see that "but" coming a mile away&#8212;I long ago stopped desiring Linux to rival MS on the desktop. Frankly, were Ubuntu to evolve into that, I would consider it a disaster. And I don't believe that I am overstating my feelings for dramatic effect: for me, it would really constitute a disaster.

I'll try to explain succinctly. I used to evangelize Linux to my friends and family. I used to believe that once the superiority of the OS was shown to them, the desire to migrate would naturally follow. I was wrong. People's preferences are not based on inherent quality. Their preferences are dictated by other considerations: popularity, clique, tribalism, superficialities, shiny bells and whistles, the network effect, being one with the crowd. Quality has little to do with it.

Had I given the matter more serious consideration, I would have realized this truth sooner. The most popular singers are not those with the best voices; it's those who are flashiest. The most popular actors are not those who act most skillfully; it's those who look the cutest. The most popular cars are not those that run the best; it's those that successfully tote a certain image. We are a society of superficialities. Why would it be any different in OSes?

Given that people respond not to quality but to superficialities, then it follows that, were Ubuntu ever to become a serious rival to Windows, it would have to sacrifice quality for those same superficialities. It would have to focus on bells and whistles instead of power and flexibility. It would have to pursue the network effect over freedom and independence. In other words, it would have to stop being the OS I love and become the OS that I ran screaming from.

Why would I want that?

I don't. So I stopped wanting Ubuntu to be on a par with anything else. Rather, I want it to continue getting more powerful, more flexible, more unique, more geeky. This necessarily entails that it also remain or even increasingly become more arcane, more obscure, more difficult for the average Jane or Joe to use. That's the price we pay for its power.

I wouldn't want it any other way.

### Post by DuckHook on 2022-03-26
> **1fallen said:**
> Once again>>>Drops mic and walks away. ;)

;) Well, I hope I'm not coming across as a mike&#8209;dropping know&#8209;it&#8209;all. ;)

I really meant it when I said that I found exploder's enthusiasm admirable. There is an innocent (naïve?) side of me that also wants Ubuntu/Linux to become more "popular", in the sense that people magically wake up one morning to spontaneously embrace quality over chintz. But I'm also aware that that's just a daydream. A magic carpet ride fantasy.

Since this is a chat thread, perhaps it would help if I give an example of what I am alluding to:

Like millions of others, I used to install an OS, then fill it with apps. I would grudgingly beaver away in this confined little environment hoping that nothing goes wrong, that my disk won't fail at a critical moment, and if it did, that I would have sufficiently robust backups to trudge through the ordeal of reinstalling and then restoring from them. In Windows, there is no other choice. This is the way the ecosystem is designed and its inmates are constrained to use it in only this way.

Recently though, and due in no small part to the expertise of my betters on these very forums, I've discovered the sheer awesome power, freedom and joy of LXD containerization. I now install a truly minimalist system on bare metal. Everything else—apps, services, even many utilities, are installed within containers. This has given me unparalleled flexibility, portability and power. For example, my whole user stack of apps, docs, games, files, history, everything, is routinely synced from my primary desktop machine to my secondary desktop machine with a ridiculously simple one&#8209;liner:```
lxc copy working_environment secondary&#8209;desktop:working_environment --refresh
```Everything goes over. And I mean everything. Apps, docs, games, VMs, bookmarks, history… I can just hop over to that second machine and it's like I never left my first machine. I do the same thing on my laptop and it's like I'm taking my whole desktop with me wherever I go. Come home, do the same thing in reverse, and instant synchronization. The entirety of my userspace has become ultra&#8209;portable but without having to rely on some untrustworthy "cloud". The redundancy alone is immensely reassuring. Combined with LXD's snapshotting and I am covered twelve ways to Sunday.

This cannot be done on Windows. Even if one could kludge together a technical solution, the licensing restrictions alone would make the whole thing impractical. In this respect as in so many others, Windows is designed to bind chains around your wrists and your ankles. Pretty chains, but still chains.

Yet, getting to this place has not been easy. LXD has a stupidly steep learning curve. It is arcane. It is obscure. It is poorly documented. It is, frankly, a royal pain in the neck to implement. The best usable implementation data consists of blog entries from its devs—blogs that are hard to find and, even when eventually found, are cryptic, incomplete, unofficial and filled with jargon.

This is what I meant by the price of power. The LXD functionality within Ubuntu is of a magnitude that is inconceivable to a Windows user: they do not even know what they are missing. But that power comes with a cost in complexity and sheer cussedness that puts it beyond the capability of general users. It is geek power designed for geeks. It is not accessible to the general public any more than quantum mechanics is accessible to the dilettante.

There's no way that LXD can be made "easy". There's just no way. One may as well ask quantum mechanics to be made "easy". There's a reason that physicists have to earn PhDs to really understand quantum mechanics. The same dynamic is at work with Linux. If we want the power, then the complexity, the obscurity, the arcanery, are unavoidable consequences.

Okay. Time to get off the soapbox. Sorry for the long&#8209;winded rant.

### Post by DuckHook on 2022-04-11
> **xeonbionic said:**

Mint is a fine distro. I have nothing against it. Likewise, I have nothing against PCLinuxOS or Zorin. All are solid and functional.

But in my opinion, the biggest problem that these three share with each other is: they've succumbed to the temptation of trying to look like Windows. This is part of the aforementioned mistake—this ongoing urge to "replace" Windows. Among other things, it leads to this copy&#8209;cat syndrome where the DE is made to *look* as close as possible to the "market leader" when, at heart, there's nothing in the OS that is remotely like the "market leader".

This creates a whole host of problems:

[LIST]
[*]Windows users install these distros expecting an experience that is identical to Windows. Once they start exploring beyond the most superficial level, it is anything but.
[*]It plays into MS's hands by reinforcing the idea that the Windows layout is some sort of magically superior "standard".
[*]If it looks so much like Windows, why can't it run Windows apps? The uninformed (which are the majority) will conclude that Linux is nothing more than an elaborate bait&#8209;and&#8209;switch.
[*]By copy&#8209;catting something else, they fail to carve out distinct identities for themselves and are forever chained to the whims and wiles of an entity they cannot control, cannot influence and cannot depend on.
[*]Windows sees Linux as a competitive product that has the potential to diminish their market share. The profit dynamic necessarily entails that they look upon Linux as "the enemy". It only takes one hostile party to set the stage for an adversarial relationship. Why would we want to validate such an implacable adversary?
[/LIST]
The problem with Mint, PCLinuxOS, Zorin and other "look&#8209;&&#8209;feels" is not their technical chops, but this misguided desire to be Windows wannabes. This doesn't diminish the user experience for people looking only for technical competency, but it will disappoint the less informed users who won't be getting the OS they were set up to believe that they would.

In the end, this approach does Linux no favours.
