### Post by grahammechanical on 2014-11-14
What I think you are missing about the Ubuntu development method is that it is not exclusive. Nobody is being locked out or locked in. But you need to understand that Canonical was set up with two basic goals.

1) Develop Ubuntu. 
2) Become self sustainable.

Some people speak as if Canonical engineers should be working on other people's software projects and to do that without any influence over the development of those projects. Or, Canonical should be paying the developers of other projects. I see those kind of people as being governed by self-interest.

If someone decides to fork Ubuntu and bring out a derivative, then they have the freedom to do that. But if at some point they find that it is all too much for them, then they and their users have no right to expect Ubuntu developers to solve the problem for them. Ubuntu developers, whether community or Canonical employees, are there to develop Ubuntu. And they are free to go elsewhere at any time.

For Canonical to be sustainable as a commercial organisation it needs to make profit. And at the moment it is not making profit. Some areas of the business are self sustainable. Other areas are not. You have to keep in mind that there is one Ubuntu for everyone, whether we are the single user (like myself) or a business user with many hundreds or even thousands of machines. And we get it free. We do not have a crippled home user version and a paid for enterprise version with applications that are not available in the free version. 

There has been much criticism of Ubuntu over the last three or four years. Those who complain of fragmentation forget or perhaps never bothered to find out, that the very existence of Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) is to due to fragmentation. Forking the code is a fundamental right in the FOSS universe. Richard Stallmann may well be wishing that we were all using Gnu Herd and not Linux but the principles of FOSS that he came up with allowed for the freedom that results in fragmentation. 

Now, I do not care what web sites you access. I do not care whose opinions you accept. I do think it wrong that you join this forum just to spread those opinions. Are you even using Ubuntu? I wonder. Earn credit as a member of this forum by helping Ubuntu users with their problems and your opinions might carry some weight with me. But expect me to disagree with you if I do disagree. When you give your opinion you also give me the right to express my opinion.

Earn credit by becoming a contributor to Ubuntu and your opinions may have weight among fellow contributors and you may share in deciding the direction of Ubuntu development.
