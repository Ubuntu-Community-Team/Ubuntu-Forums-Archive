### Post by aerhys on 2022-02-28
Hello,

I would like to express, on these forums, some lines. I have been using Ubuntu for more or less five years. I had tried when I was younger, about fifteen years ago, but without much success.

Then, there was the advent of Windows 10 and my growing political awareness, which made me switch to Ubuntu. At first with a dual boot, then with Wine when my knowledge slowly evolved, helped by growing graphical user interfaces within the Linuxverse. Recently, I wanted to change said interface. I wanted to have only one bar at the top of my screen. So I looked for and installed Dash to Panel, which was very easy although I had to understand at first that I shouldn't use the repository version. Once that was done I had an interesting experience : There are always pleasant surprises when you are curious. Ubuntu was no longer just an interface to run my programs, it was my interface, it was now my Ubuntu. I think this feeling is what best defines what "free software" is, beyond even its legal aspect, which is only a defense for its existence.

I would like to sincerely thank all the community and all those who participate, from users to developers, to Linux. Long live free software, long live software that belongs to the one who uses it. Let's now try together to get free hardware for everyone.

Respectfully.

> **username2758 said:**

I remember when Tim Berners&#8209;Lee first pioneered the WWW. He hoped that it would lead to a world that empowered us all: that it would change us from a society of passive consumers into one of active producers. It has done this&#8212;to a small extent&#8212;but any such good is being overwhelmed by the tsunami of bad foisted on us by the new tech giants. We are sadly still a society of largely passive consumers. We do have more avenues to produce&#8212;for example, we can contribute to community knowledge like we do on these forums&#8212;but such productive outlets are so many drops in the ocean of canned content that megacorps push on us in their campaign to keep us addicted to passive consumption. Even such creative output as has been produced has been co&#8209;opted by the tech giants to serve their own interests. Just look at Youtube. It started out as a communal arena for independent creative productions. These days, it's just more feedstock for the tech giant bloodsucking machine. IT infrastructure itself is stacked against us: upload speeds are asymmetrical&#8212;usually a small fraction of download speeds&#8212;and most people have gotten so used to this imbalance that they don't question it. In most jurisdictions, ISPs have succeeded in killing net neutrality. Proprietary megacorps have largely succeeded in normalizing the insanity of walled silos and fiefdoms such that we are now rapidly regressing into Internet feudalism and technological medievalism.

Against this sad landscape, Linux stands out as a beacon of progress and enlightenment. It empowers us instead of the corporations. It still proudly holds high the torch that TimBL passed onto the next generation. There are a lot of pragmatic reasons that make me thankful for Linux. But the biggest reasons are more abstract: I'm thankful to it for giving me back control of my information, my privacy and my life.
