### Post by jago25_98 on 2013-09-04
I love Ubuntu and Mark for providing leadership in opensource. I'm ok with some compromise on opensource values in exchange for that leadership. 

The edge is an interesting thing. It will be good to finally have something a potential to offer greater low level freedom that commercial offers. 

 But going head to head in competition with tech giants seems like hard work. 

It would be good to leverage the advantages of FOSS to greater effect. 

 So let's look at the advantages of Ubuntu. 

A big thing for everyone rather than just developers and enthusiats is security. There isn't a phone out there at the moment that can garantee a NSA free phone call. Even the GeeksPhone wasn't able to pressure for much more openness of modem code or firewall the closed nature of those drivers from the O/S. Every Bloomberg broker is using closed source solutions and so they don't really have security at all. The whole thing is open for FOSS to get in there and say "Blackberry could be snooping on your trades", "China is snooping on your agencies with it's phone modem drivers" etc. That is the market that only FOSS can properly address. This is a market that only FOSS can really nail and Ubuntu is well placed to market to this. 
If I was Mark I would remarket the edge into this niche straight away, assuring FOSS level security by design and firewalling any compromised closed source where compromise might be unavoidable. The next thing 

I would do is try to get into the Cyanogenmod crowd. There's a growing number of phone users getting frustrated by the inflexibility of some phones. There are people out there buying phone specifically with liberty in mind (myself for one). This is a growing market. At the end of the day the problem with companies is that they are there to make money. It's convienient that USB ports break. It's convienient that iphone screens smash. For them. 
Can Ubuntu become aligned with it's community so that it's not *just *a company? 

 Ubuntu has is an army of people working... for free! This advantage has to be maximised. Any ideas on how to make better use of this? You have to win the respect of leaders of the community. Leaders appreciate open, well documented hardware. Finally some clout in this underrepresented area is greatly appreciated. If a compromise on opensource has to be made it needs to be clearly firewalled and acknowedged as an imperfection. Just look at all the crowdsourced tech projects. The community is dying to get involved. How do you let people get involved in a way that helps rather than hinders? What if you could get involved with designing the Ubuntu Edge? What if ubuntu used it's position to help crowdsource other community projects. Look at the Raspberry Pi clones. 

I just read an article by a non open source knowledged newspaper article. They make some very good critisms, in particular that the Edge is trying to move into an area that is already pretty saturated. I agree. Ubuntu needs to differentiate itself and explain what it is for people who, at the moment, view it as just another company.
