### Post by cobalt2 on 2015-10-20
I find this Ubuntu user community to  be very helpful. Whenever I have a problem with my Ubuntu I write about  here, and I receive helpful responses from knowledgeable users who are  very responsive to my requests for help or advice. This is a community  of people who are motivated by the spirit of community, helping your neighbour, and user empowerment.

 The StackExchange community Ask Ubuntu, as with all other StackExchange sites, is much like proprietary software, in that the website controls the user. In  Ask Ubuntu, the admins seem to be obsessed with handcuffing the  users. Users need 50 reputation in order to comment on a thread and 15 reputation in order to give  feedback. The admins say that the 50 reputation  restriction is aimed at preventing spam, but there are ways to combat spam, that don't require draconian rules. 

For example, I found a bash function that I  wanted to use, but I didn't know how to implement it, so I wanted to ask  a question on an answer. When I tried to post a comment, I received a  message saying, "You must have 50 reputation in order to comment." So I  was forced to start a new thread that received 0 responses.

Anyone  here who used Ask Ubuntu, what is your experience there and how do  you contrast it with your experience on Ubuntu Forums?

### Post by sudodus on 2015-10-20
Yes. Askubuntu is made in order to provide questions and answers to standard problems. I think they try to avoid complication and discussion, so that Askubuntu can be used by many people without interaction.

The Ubuntu Forums are different. We try to look at the specific problems of each user, and also provide alternative solutions, discussions and a high degree of interaction. Maybe we can say that many standard problems are solved quickly by looking at Askubuntu. More complicated or unusual problems can be solved at the Ubuntu Forums. We can also help beginners, who need some help to reach the level necessary to use Askubuntu.

### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-10-20
As far as I can tell AskUbuntu was the idea of some Canonical engineers. Whereas, the Forums started out one person's project. and it then became a Ubuntu user community project. The two sites have two different purposes and two different methods of obtaining their goals.

I do not wish to get into a "them" and "us" type of discussion. We are all part of the same community, after all.

I like the forums because I can guess at a solution and you never know I might just get lucky. I feel that AskUbuntu requires the kind of answer that would be suitable for an encyclopedia, complete with illustrations. I do not have the time or resources for that kind of answer.

I also like the fact that we do not care if someone is asking the same question that someone's has already asked. In fact we prefer it if people start their own post for their own problems.

### Post by cobalt2 on 2015-10-21
Bashing has a destructive connotation, of "throwing under the bus." That was not my intention, although I see that initially I came down heavy handed on Ask Ubuntu and was very biased in my critique.

However this kind of mindset of needlessly restricting the user is something that people in the Ubuntu community should be opposing. It violates one of the tenets of the free software movement - that the user should be able to run a program as they wish. In a free community, people should be allowed to express themselves freely notwithstanding their rank or social status. A forum for users of free software should respect users' freedom and community.

From the FSF website:
> We campaign for these freedoms because everyone deserves them.  With these freedoms, the users (both individually and collectively) control the program and what it does for them.  When users don't control the program, we call it a &#8220;nonfree&#8221; or &#8220;proprietary&#8221; program.  The nonfree program controls the users, and the developer controls the program; this makes the program [ an instrument of unjust power]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-software-even-more-important.html").
