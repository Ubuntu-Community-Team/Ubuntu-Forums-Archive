### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-03-23
As someone who, once upon a time, had a role in hiring admins, I'd say give yourself broad, and deep, expertise in Linux and then layer on the specific product and certification you think most likely to be most rewarding, career-wise.  Expertise and experience in only one Linux flavor will limit your opportunities.  

The people who who hire admins, and the people who write the paychecks, just want things to get done and get fixed.  They think in terms like, "Customers are complaining that the site is way too slow... fix it." Or, "Why do we have to go offline for 4 hours just to do this @#$@# upgrade?  Do it faster!!   They don't think in terms of what version of Linux is running on what server.

And, we wanted people who could think, not just rely on rote memory to duplicate something they once learned in a class.

### Post by SeijiSensei on 2015-03-23
RedHat is the corporate standard for servers, so yes, I would go that route.  And I'm unconvinced that, "The people who hire admins, and the people who write the paychecks, just want things to get done and get fixed."  If you're applying for a job in a shop with lots of RH or CentOS servers, that may matter as much or more to those hiring.

Beyond that, I'd start browsing Linux job listings at places like Craigslist.  Find some jobs you might like to apply for and see what they require.  Just getting a certificate "in principle" seems like a waste of time and money to me unless you can see it affecting your employability.
