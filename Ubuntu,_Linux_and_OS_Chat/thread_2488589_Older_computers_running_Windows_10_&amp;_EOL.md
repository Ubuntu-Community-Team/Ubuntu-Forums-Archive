### Post by DuckHook on 2023-07-15
I used to collect any old HW for the purpose of resurrecting them into Linux.

However, in recent years, I've become far more selective. The main reason is power usage. Most of that old technology used power slurping components that can be shockingly hungry. About two years ago, I calculated that repositioning an old box as a server would eat up so much power as to pay for a new server in 14 months. After 14 months, I would be economically worse off every month. This is not even considering the carbon footprint nor the better performance from newer gear. Admittedly, against this is the fact that just throwing old HW away adds to our e&#8209;waste problem and is toxic to the environment.

There is old HW that is both cost effective and miserly with power. I've repositioned a couple of old Western Digital MyBookLive's by nuking their dead OSes and replacing them with OpenWRT. They just sip tiny amounts of power and have been running tickety&#8209;boo now for three years. I bought them for $40 on ebay and replaced their HDDs with big drives. But aside from specialized HW of that sort, I've found myself to be increasingly disillusioned by old HW.

Just another factor to consider.

the windows 11 ISO you need is the >>>"international"<<< ISO. then use rufus (which is windows only) to burn the ISO as a bootable disk. this can be done within the within the windows 10 VM. download rufus and the win 11 international ISO and burn the USB from there.

>>>refuse will give you options before the burn to disable several checks, TPM, cpu, ram etc before the burn with the international windows 11 ISO<<< 

once this is done with the VM of windows 10 open in gnome boxes (you must have USB pass through enabled or be able to enable) use windows 10 explorer to go into the USB drive and double click the windows install.exe file.

the upgrade window will open and you can do a full upgrade to windows 11 from windows 10. i have it running in gnome boxes already.
