### Post by tgalati4 on 2013-11-10
The issue is the Institution.  The Law requires that a 14-year-old go to school.  Why?  Has anyone questioned it?  There are many vested interests in keeping schools open.  There are many vendors that sell supplies to schools.  Your tax dollars go to pay for schools and salaries of teachers and administrators.

A 14-year-old goes to school because the law says that he has to.  So he gets an Institutional education using Institutional tools.  Microsoft Windows and its products are part of that Institution.  

If you were to advocate abolishing high schools there would be an outcry.  If you were to substitute Microsoft Windows in school computers there would be an outcry.  There are vested interests (id est Money) involved in the status quo.  So things remain the same.  

The fact that the OP is asking the question is a good sign.  It never occurred to him that he is caught up in a system that he can't change.  Yet, he sees inconsistencies and at his age, it will only get worse as he continues his studies.

The Los Angeles school district dumped a bunch of iPads on high school students.  Besides being broken, stolen, lost, several students quickly figured out how to remove the restrictions put on them by school officials that would not allow playing games or social networking on them.  Guess what most kids want to do with an iPad--play games and social networking, not read textbooks.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2013-11-14
And that is precisely my point.  And it only took 5 Whys.  Institutional schools are not prepared to use disruptive technology.  It upsets the balance.  Let kids write their own programs on an operating system that they have compiled themselves?  How would you control what they are learning?  How would you control their behavior?  

There are many stories of schools trying to adopt open source--small scale--and with mixed results.  Listen to the podcasts and read the blogs of these efforts.  There is often a showstopper that kills open source efforts in grade school.  No budget for training.  Proprietary grading or attendance software that the entire district must use.  Machines that require more IT maintanence to install and maintain linux vs Windows.  And on it goes.

Formal education is required for several professions.  This is true.  But for grade school, the level of institutionalization hampers the adoption of linux and open source software in general.

I think the OP's teachers are not aware of open source software or its benefits and why would a teacher want to risk their career promoting such software?

The good news is the OP only has 3 and 1/2 years of high school to go.
