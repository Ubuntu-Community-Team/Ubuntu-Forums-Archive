### Post by benwahwah2 on 2022-04-24
In about 2008/9 I tried Linux properly for the first time, and found that there was always something that didn’t work. It was usually the wi-fi, sound card, or the graphics card that I’d have problems with. No matter which distro I went with, there was always something. (Including Ubuntu) After months of fighting with Linux, I gave up and went back to Windows.


Roll on a few years, rinse and repeat, but this time I went to MacOS. I really liked Apple and I’ve ended up paying the Apple tax for the last decade.


This year my Mac broke, and I decided to get myself an Intel PC computer. I’ve grown weary of power twice as much for my hardware as PC stuff. I’ve also become somewhat disillusioned with ‘big tech’. The way that companies like Google and Facebook has turned the end user into commodities. FOSS has always interested me (hence my dabbling was with Linux previously).

For the last month I’ve been distro hopping. At first I went with Linux Mint, the graphics didn’t seem to work properly and it wouldn’t recognise my monitor, only displaying in 640x480! Great I thought, Linux is up to it’s old tricks again.

Then I decided to give PopOS a try as that seems to be the YouTubers distro of choice. (Or at least the ones I’ve been watching.) PopOS worked great to be fair. Everything worked, but I was on 21.10 and I knew it wasn’t a ‘long term’ option, and then Ubuntu 22.04 got released. 
 
So I got to installing Ubuntu 22.04 as a fresh install, and I was delighted to find that everything also worked out of the box! I can do almost everything I could with PopOS, (PopOS allowed me to make the dock icons bigger) and I can be safe in the knowledge that I am now on an LTS just in time for the new academic year. (I’m an English teacher in Thailand.) So safe and steady is exactly what I’m looking for in a distribution.


Not only does everything work, but now I can actually play more games because of Steam and Proton. I can now play 32-bit games that I previously couldn’t due to Apple dropping 32-bit like a hot potato a few years ago.
 
Plus, open source software is now up to spec as well. LibreOffice does a great job, and looks great as well. Switching over to Linux is actually saving me money as well! I’ve cancelled my Office 365 subscription (no longer needed), cancelled my Apple One subscription (no real access via Linux is a deal breaker). I’m going to get myself a lifetime pCloud account (unless any of you have an alternative you can recommend to me) in the coming weeks, and I’ve set up Spotify so that I can easily access music across all my devices. All in all, within a year 

In conclusion, Linux has come a hell of a long way since I last dabbled with it. It seems to be a lot more stable, and information is much more readily available than it used to be.



Here is to many years of Linux use!

### Post by DuckHook on 2022-04-27
The last thing I want to do is rain on your parade&#8212;goodness knows it's the very last thing I want to do&#8212;but it is important to approach Ubuntu (and Linux in general) with the right expectations.

Linux is not a newbie&#8209;friendly OS. In most cases and given mainstream, tested, Goldilocks HW (not too old, not too new, but "just right") it works out of the box and doesn't need tinkering. However, if you add a NIC that's too new, or a bleeding edge GPU, or an obscure Bluetooth module, you can get ornery cussed incompatibilities that can be really difficult to solve.

I think the proper way to approach Ubuntu is not to expect an effortless out&#8209;of&#8209;the&#8209;box experience, but rather, something opposite: expect a few irritations, then make use of the community to iron out the kinks. In my experience, once the initial kinks are flattened, it tends to run way more reliably than the proprietary OSes.

I counsel the above for a very important reason: Linux is not superior because it is easy to use; its real superiority is in its immense power. Your own allusion is a case in point: before springing big bucks for a lifetime membership on pCloud, consider freeing yourself from the tracking, data&#8209;slurping, privacy destroying world of proprietary cloud services altogether by running your own cloud. I'm talking about Nextcloud. And, yep, it is possible only because it is run on Linux and is entirely FOSS.

***That*** is an example of the real power of Linux. Windows? Apple? Nada. Made possible only because of Linux.
