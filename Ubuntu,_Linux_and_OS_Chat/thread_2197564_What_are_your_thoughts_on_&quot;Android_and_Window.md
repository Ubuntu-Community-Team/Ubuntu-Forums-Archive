### Post by Copper Bezel on 2014-01-04
I think that's not the problem. What *everyone *wants is Android, but with MS Office. What *no one *wants is to resort to dual-booting, and the fact that manufacturers would even present it as an available option is, as the article indicates, a rather pathetic grab for headlines. 

People don't hate Windows 8. All three flavors just have their own individual problems. There's resistance to every new Windows version on the desktop, usually for good reason (but clearly, given that some individuals even clung to Vista after the release of 7, not always.) The tablet and phone versions are inferior third contenders in already-saturated markets, like Linux on the desktop. Desktop Windows 8 will become the norm and people will continue to tolerate it as they have every other version of Windows, and it will never be exciting or fun. The mobile versions are failing to overcome their difficulties with limited applications and difficult or inconsistent interface elements, but so did Android at first. The real problem is one of device classes - non-ARM Windows 8 and Android target two different kinds of devices. Tablets should be running Windows RT, and notebooks should not be running Android. Both Microsoft and Google know this, which is why RT and Chromebook exist.

It's not a desperate move by anyone, because no one cares about Windows but Microsoft. It's not an attempt to sell Windows laptops to people, because they'd be buying Windows laptops anyway. The only oddity here is the option of booting into x86 Android instead, and the port is getting better thanks to efforts by Intel, but I think not sufficient, particularly when used like this. If this actually gets to market, it'll be a silly thing to put on the label to sway potential buyers, like every other ridiculous use of Android on a Windows PC. (My favorite so far was on an all-in-one. Yeah. I don't remember the brand and model.)
