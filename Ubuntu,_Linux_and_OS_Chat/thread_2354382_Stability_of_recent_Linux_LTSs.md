### Post by DuckHook on 2017-03-02
I suppose I'll throw my hat in the ring too.

> **lysander6662 said:**
> …a stable OS is one which is least likely to break/freeze/crash/lose ability to boot due to clashes between applications, bugs or user incompetence.

As good and relevant as your question is, perhaps you might like a different perspective:

Application conflicts and bugs affect all distros. I would be highly sceptical of any claim that one was superior to another. Most of the old-timers on this forum have likely used many different distros. In my case, they've ranged from all of the 'buntus to Debian, Fedora, openSuse, Mageia, Chakra, Bodhi, the recently deceased CrunchBang, the complete list is too long to post. To be frank, as much as I like the 'buntus, I haven't found them to be any "superior" in the stability or dependability department. I daresay that most of us could have settled on any alternative distro and considered them dependable and stable. And I further dare to say that this is because "stability" and "dependability" have little to do with the distro itself and are mainly a function of the user.

Now, before I get into trouble, let me clarify: I am *not* saying that problems are always the user's fault or that technical issues arising from initial installs or subsequent upgrades could be avoided. As these forums well attest, there are all sorts of technical problems that can bite us due to the almost countless interactions between different OS modules, drivers, apps, and varieties of hardware.

However, *once a system is stable,* knowledgeable users tend to avoid the sort of behaviour that get them into trouble. On the other hand, unknowledgeable users tend to engage in the kind of practices that vastly increase their chances of getting into trouble. They add PPAs willy-nilly. They install a hundred apps. They download .debs from who-knows-where.com and then install them without thought. They turn off challenge/response authentication and then bristle when advised not to. They try running everything using sudo. Most of all, they drag in tons of bad habits from their previous computing life and then insist that Linux be changed to accommodate them.

So—just like security—stability and dependability are maybe one-tenth distro-related and nine-tenths user-related. No distro can protect a user from him/her/self. No distro is more immune to user incompetence than another. So long as we value the power to change, improve and add to our OSes, we must live with the power to destroy them too, and there is no way for any OS to defend itself from that.

### Post by sgian on 2017-03-03
Linux Mint uses an Ubuntu LTS base, and makes it even more polished and stable.  However, there have been some security concerns in the past, especially with security updates.  The Mint philosophy (if it ain't broke, don't fix it) tends to be so conservative by default that until Linux Mint 18, kernels were not updated by default.  Even now with 18, the Mint update manager gives one the choice of updating the kernel and such, but warns against it.  They also don't recommend updating between 18 and 18.1 unless there is a bug to fix or similar, because of the risk of messing up the OS.  This is in contrast to Canonical (Ubuntu), which in the past has pushed security updates even though they knew it would break some installations.  With one such example a couple of years ago,which was a kernel update known to have issues with fglrx (no longer used in 16.04 btw), it got me so angry that I switched all my computers to Mint for a while because my family blamed me for updates crashing computers and losing data.

So short story, Mint emphasizes stability over security, and Ubuntu tries to balance stability and security in my opinion.  My kids and wife don't like change and really don't like crashes, so they get Linux Mint for their everyday use.  For the pc that I use, especially since I do finances on it, it gets Ubuntu.

Also consider the desktop environment, some are more polished or stable than others.  Something to do with resources, testing, and such.  However some environments favor certain things or have individual apps that are more useful for certain things.  For example, I have had difficulties with Gnome and Ubuntu 16.04 in printers and webcams working that I don't get in KDE.  So since I use some KDE apps more than Gnome, I use the KDE environment.  Other people will be happier with other environments.  Just look at the apps that work the best for you, and go with that environment.

In my opinion, if you need the OS to be absolutely stable and aren't going to run a server, do secret or sensitive stuff, or do foolhardy security practices like going to shady sites that might spread malware, then Linux Mint 18.x might be better for you.  If it is an older computer with less RAM, XFCE or MATE might be more suitable.  However, if you have more than 4 GB of RAM, Cinnamon or KDE should be fine.  If you use Dropbox, I have had trouble in the past getting it to work seamlessly with XFCE and MATE, although it can certainly be done.
