### Post by grahammechanical on 2016-03-03
IBM invented the Personal Computer (PC) from standard off the shelf parts. In those days IBM made its money from selling wall sized computers and the clever people at the top did not see much of a future for a machine that sat on a desktop. So, the PC was not patented.

This meant that anyone could make and sell IBM compatible PCs using the same off the shelf components. Yes, in those days we did not look for "Intel inside" or "Windows certified" but "IBM compatible." That label meant the machine would run the operating systems being developed for it. Those same off the shelf components make it possible for DIY desktops.

Laptops are not made from off the self components. Has anyone tried for a DIY laptop? I once did a little research. The components are not too available like they are with desktops.

As, someone has already pointed out there is a great variety in the selection of laptop & netbook hardware that open source developers have a great problem producing drivers. We cannot upgrade and repair laptops like we can desktops. More is the pity.

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2016-03-05
Because when I got into Ubuntu in 2007, Broadcom wireless was a complete nightmare on Linux. The dreaded NDiswrapper was the only way to go and it was a seriously problematic kludge. 

Now we have B43 and STA drivers for most Broadcom cards and they are one of the easiest to get going. Many 'just work'. The Broadcom 43** series of cards were an issue. Now they're generally not, but they, in tandem with ndiswrapper, created some nightmares in the day ... :D

Wireless cards are probably not a good example, though, as they can be problematic in Ubuntu on a laptop or desktop. Same with graphics.
