### Post by Unanimated on 2014-05-13
I used Ubuntu several years ago, on a really old PC with aging hardware. I was in sixth grade at the time, the computer had XP on it, and it had a really interesting method of getting on the internet (basically connecting to another computer that was sharing its internet connection in order for mine to get online) that took FOREVER to set up with XP. I heard about Ubuntu, started reading up on it, then bit the bullet and did an install. Almost everything worked perfectly, even my wonky internet setup - the only thing that didn't work was sound, and that was how I quickly learned of these forums and of the power of Google, and how I began to learn how Linux works as a whole. I also learned how to compile packages and what EXACTLY to search for when looking for drivers online. Long story short, I had an aging computer that could barely run XP, and Ubuntu took it and suddenly I had fancy desktop animations happening all over the place with smooth frame rates, I was playing games at 60fps with maxed out graphics settings, and everything was just fast and worked wonderfully. Rhythmbox even took my 22GB music library in stride, something no music player on Windows was able to do at the time, on that computer.

Then the dark ages came and the power supply failed on that computer, but honestly the thing was so old and my reliance on it had diminished less and less that I just stopped using it. I took an old (like, OLD) IBM ThinkPad from my mom and used that as my daily driver for a while. It was also running XP, but it was even worse off than my desktop - it took at least 20 minutes to get it booted, and at least another 5 to get a web browser open. I immediately downloaded Xubuntu and threw it on. Once again, I was amazed - even with an aftermarket Wi-Fi chip from an unknown brand, the laptop was running really well. Not very well, mind you, because this thing was a technological marvel at this point, but it turned on and got a web browser open in under 5 minutes, so I was happy.

But eventually, the cables running through the hinge died and I ended up having to borrow a computer from my mom's employer. (Don't worry, I actually have paid for a computer lately.) This was actually the best computer I had had so far - Intel Core 2 Duo, decent graphics card from ATI, 8GB RAM, the whole nine yards. It was running XP, and even with these decent specs it still took a solid 25 minutes to get into a useable state after booting up - there wasn't even that much stuff installed on it by default, but I also don't really know what was done to it before it fell into my hands. But I didn't mind because I wasn't using it that often; this was during my senior year of high school, and my school handed out MacBook Airs for all the students to use, and I had just gotten a tablet for college that satisfied my needs when the laptop wouldn't. I actually didn't even really touch the thing for about a year and a half. But, when college came around and I realized the tablet wasn't enough and I was practically living at the computer labs, I grabbed the desktop from home and threw Ubuntu 13.10 on there. I didn't even boot into XP, I used one of the computers in the computer lab to put Ubuntu on a flash drive and booted straight into that after turning the desktop on.

This was my first time using Ubuntu since Unity had been introduced, but I had seen a few people around campus with it installed on their laptops. I was blown away. Ubuntu had suddenly become gorgeous, and once I got Unity figured out it really grew on me. What surprised me the most was that I had literally **no** tinkering to do - I never minded tinkering with my computer in the past to get it working, but the fact that I didn't have any to do after a fresh install really got me. The computer was blazing fast, and really made me wonder what the hell had made XP so slow. Even my boyfriend, who has a 2013 MacBook Pro without Retina, was shocked at how fast the computer was given its age. The only thing that got to me was the lack of ATI support and the fact that I never really got the proprietary driver to work, but I wasn't too crushed because the interface ran well (even with all the fancy desktop effects I threw on) and I have an Xbox for gaming anyway.

That computer actually didn't die and still works, it's just been decomissioned - as a birthday present to myself, I finally bought myself an actual laptop. While I enjoyed having a desktop to use as a "home base," it really bugged me that I couldn't be productive at all if I was away from it. And when you're on a college campus and in a long distance relationship, there are a lot of times when you're away from your home computer. So anyway, I used my birthday money and bought myself a $300 no-name HP laptop from Walmart... Running Windows 8.1. The first thing I did on this thing was download uTorrent and get an Ubuntu 13.10 CD made right away. But, with the UEFI/SecureBoot disaster that I had not yet been exposed to suddenly rearing its ugly head, I was kind of at a loss for what to do. I read plenty of guides online that explained what to do, but no matter what happened, I couldn't get the damn thing to boot into the Live CD. So I gave up for a while because I wanted to actually use the $300 laptop I had just bought, and what the hell, maybe I won't end up hating Windows 8 as much as everybody else does. (It's terrible.)

I used Windows 8 for a while and eventually it started to grow on me, but it still wasn't my favorite thing in the world. Ubuntu 14.04 eventually came out, but by this point I was already used to Windows 8 and I was under a homework load from hell at this point in the semester and didn't have time to mess with Linux. Then, last week right as my classes were ending, I decided what the hell, I'll see if Ubuntu works now. And lo and behold, I turned off UEFI and it booted right up. Everything seemed to be working fine, so I said goodbye to Windows 8 forever and wiped my hard drive in favor of a fresh Ubuntu install. There was just one problem: my damn Realtek RTL8188EE. Finding a solution that worked for me proved to be an absolute nightmare, and I didn't really touch my laptop for a week because I just couldn't find the motivation to fix it. But after doing a kernel update on a fresh install (which I had done before to no avail), the thing just started working, and I rejoiced. Everything else is working perfectly - webcam, video card, everything. On a $300 HP piece of crap. Programs load faster than they did in Windows 8, and the only thing that required tweaking was Compiz - the graphics couldn't quite handle all the animations very well, but toning them down a bit was effective.

And now, it feels absolutely wonderful to be back in the world of Ubuntu, Linux, and this wonderfully supportive community. I couldn't be happier with my decision to come back, and my laptop seems much happier, as well - it runs cooler, quieter, things just happen faster, and the battery life is much better. I can only say I hope that nothing else deters me from putting Ubuntu on every computer I own from here on out. It's really amazed me to watch Ubuntu grow and evolve over the last 7 years, even if I have only been an occasional dabbler at best.

Just thought I would throw my two cents out there.
