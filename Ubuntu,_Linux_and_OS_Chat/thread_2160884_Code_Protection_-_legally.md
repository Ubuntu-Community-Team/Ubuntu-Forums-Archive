### Post by Cheesehead on 2013-07-08
Most of your concerns are addressed by the software's license.

> **vgezer said:**
> Lets think about a company who makes millions of euros by selling its software products or OS. Does open source software allow usage of itself by them?
This question is specifically addressed in most licenses.


> **vgezer said:**
> Let's think about SVN or GIT. How some could know if those companies are not using it for version control?
You cannot prove a negative.


> **vgezer said:**
> or how some could make sure if a company who makes office suite does not include any lines of an Open Source office software?
Again, cannot prove a negative.
If a company is discovered to be violating the license, they can be sued in court...with unpredictable -but likely expensive- consequences.


> **vgezer said:**
> The second thing is Ubuntu or common Linux distro derivatives. Ubuntu is very successful and probably all Linux users have heard about it. Many people work for hours to make it this popular and successful. Then one person uses a basic text editor, searches *ubuntu *and replaces by *asdfghjklLinux, *without any efforts*. *Only spreading the word better makes it more popular. Is there any protective license who prevents this? or does GNU do that? If not, I think there should be more strict license for those problems (yes i see these as problems).
You are mixing the concepts of copyright, software license, marketing brand, and ethics. Try to avoid that if you want a useful answer.
Forks happen all the time. For example, LibreOffice is a fork of OpenOffice. Forking is well covered in the license(s). Read them.
Many forks wither without any sort of 'enforcement' needed. Sometimes forks improve upon and overshadow the original, again without enforcement.
Reputable projects pay close attention to licenses, and do not include software with that violates the licenses of dependencies. That is your enforcement mechanism: Unethical projects get shunned.
