### Post by TheFu on 2016-12-31
It is a pure numbers thing for any company.  Profit vs costs.  **Run some numbers**. Assuming even 1% of the desktop users are gamers willing to purchase a specific game. They just don't work out.  Game companies that do support Linux do it because they've drank the koolaid, not because it will be more profitable.

In short, you'll need a much better arguments than > "I think it's time that we linux users get equal access to games made by all of these companies." That doesn't work inside a company or with the board of directors.

Run some numbers. They will speak for themselves.

In 2007, my boss's boss came to me to work out how to move 22K users inside our company from Windows to Linux. He knew I'm addicted to Linux and would find a way.  I couldn't.  The numbers just didn't make sense.  The cost to move all the drivers and port the 20 (or so) applications that those users needed wouldn't pay back the required costs for about 25 yrs.  These users all had specialized software and less than 500K people world-wide use. I think we were their largest, single customer.  It pained me to tell him this, but the facts are the facts.

I won't pretend to know the future and I think Linux **is** the future for all platforms, but it will take time - lots of time.  I've been a Linux user since 1993. Things are much easier now, but still a huge commitment in resources is necessary to support another platform.  I worked over a decade as a cross-platform software developer - most Unix, but Mac and Windows too. I've seen resources tied up for years trying to support platforms that so few people wanted. It was a waste of my team's time.  My personal preference for Linux doesn't matter. Neither does yours.  It is purely numbers. Profit and loss.

### Post by oldrocker99 on 2017-01-01
There are companies who support Linux (Paradox is head and shoulders above the others on that count), and companies which port games to Linux (Feral stands head and shoulders above the rest, but there's also Aspyr and Virtual Programming, who are also busy).

The Steam Linux percentage is ~1% of users, and, although Linux users do buy a lot of games\\:D/, it can cost a lot to create a native Linux game and it's not always in a company's best interests to port to a userbase which is tiny. Now, 1% of 125M users is still a boatload of people, but it's also *1%*. Linux gaming on Steam has been a rousing success (over 1000 games released in 2016 alone), but it will **never ever** approach the numbers for Windows (95+% of Steam users) or even (Mac ~3%).

This is still the best of times to be a Linux gamer, and I speak from the experience of there having been exactly ONE commercial game with a Linux client in 2008, when I started using Linux: Neverwinter Nights, whose Linux client still works. In comparison, these days are golden.

There will always be games for another OS or console that you won't be able to play; look at what you have available and count your blessings.
