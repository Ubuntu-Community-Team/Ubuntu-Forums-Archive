### Post by ian-weisser on 2014-03-28
Wow, how many buzzwords can they cram into a single web page?
I love their fantasy of drawing-board to funded, operational, FCC-licensed, in-space hardware constellation in 18 months.

I think the project has all the signs of 'vaporware' or some kind of scam. 

I'm leaning toward outright scam preying on people who didn't learn any basic physics in school. You can't cheat gravity - launching tiny satellites is *expensive*, even to low orbit. Satellites in low orbit don't live long. And you can't cheat the EM spectrum - a low-power transmitter and a low-power receiver across a great distance will have low bandwidth (not wi-fi) and require significant directional guides to work at all.

john_burns2 is right: A zombie apocalyse is more likely than a single outernet launch.

And grahammechinical is right: Connecting to networks is a long-solved problem.

### Post by PondPuppy on 2014-03-29
Of course, satellite internet *per se* isn't so far-fetched. Is now: [http://www.hughesnet.com/index.cfm?page=satellite-Internet-101](http://www.hughesnet.com/index.cfm?page=satellite-Internet-101)

This uses geosynchronous satellites. My guess is that satellite-for-internet tech will advance, just as DSL and cable internet advanced in the two decades. The hardware will get lighter, more reliable, and more powerful. No, that doesn't change the cost of putting kilos in orbit. It does make the system more likely to be profitable once it's up there, though.

But I guess what's being discussed in the "outernet" is not really what Hughesnet and other satellite-internet providers are doing. But IMHO the details are in the implementation -- low-Earth vs geosynchronous, many small semi-disposable units versus fewer but more sophisticated ones.
