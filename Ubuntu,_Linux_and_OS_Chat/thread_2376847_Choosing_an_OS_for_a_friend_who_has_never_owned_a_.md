### Post by ian-weisser on 2017-11-06
When I set up older folks with a computer (done it a few times), I start with the uses and applications. What have they used in the past, what will they use it for? The application choices and preferences will drive the rest.

 In one case it come down to Firefox vs Explorer...she preferred the look of Explorer, so she's happily using her Explorer for Gmail and browsing on Win 10.

In another case, it came down to he-was-left-handed, she-was-right-handed, so Ubuntu (Unity) and Chrome and a few settings tweaks...like disabling the trackpad for one of them.

In yet another case, she decided to stick with books and newspapers, and one of her grandchildren coming by every few days to read her the email over dinner, so no machine at all for her. Her dinners are fantastic, so nobody minds a bit.

You know, this is kind of funny.  I had to recommend a new laptop for a older handicapped friend of mine who I thought was somewhat computer literate. ### Post by alwayshacked on 2017-11-09
If your friend is likely to watch DRM (copyrighted media like films online), I'd stick with a mainstream OS, like Windows, Apple products or Ubuntu. Some forms of Linux dont handle DRM so your friend wont be able to stream films or listen to music bought online. 

Installing extra device's, Windows & Apple stuff support lots of devices like printers and scanners and other things quite easily, I know for example some of the Apple ipod stuff doesnt work on Ubuntu 14.04 when tried, so its a consideration to be aware of.

Saying that, theres also forms of security built into all the major OS's apparmour on Ubuntu, window's own form and Apple's own form on theirs, yet its possible for all of these to get hacked in one way or another and all will suffer from hardware hacking but thats something which is harder to detect.

I've no preference if its something new to your friend, but Ubuntu is popular as is Linux Mint, Windows is the most popular so can easily find answers online if your friend wants to self help themselves, but like any platform, forums for all OS's can also be populated with advice which gives out bad advice even if it was well meaning.

You may also like Raspbian for PC & Mac, [https://www.raspberrypi.org/downloads/raspberry-pi-desktop/](https://www.raspberrypi.org/downloads/raspberry-pi-desktop/) although more intended for educational purposes, this might also be useful if your friend is looking to learn more about computers in general.

### Post by Jason80513 on 2017-11-10
On the commercial side, ChromeOS is going to require the least hand-holding. Updates are automatic, and a lot of the complexity is hidden (and very hard to get at). This means living in the Google ecosystem, with the requisite loss of privacy. But most people don't seem to mind that.

If you want to go FOSS, take a look at Solus. Don't get me wrong - Ubuntu and Ubuntu Mate are my daily drivers - laptops and all the servers I work on. Solus has moved to rolling updates, so you never have to install it again, and updates are pretty automatic (HUGE selling point for a new user). The app store on Solus is going to have a lot more available than you would get on a ChromeBook, of course. Solus is not based on Debian or RedHat, so there is a lot of software out there that won't run on it yet - but that won't last much longer (it supports snaps and flatpak). Seems to run well on modest hardware. 

Ubuntu is also a good choice, though you may want to start with 17.10 - it's really super! And prepare to upgrade to the LTS in 5 months time. 

Good luck!
