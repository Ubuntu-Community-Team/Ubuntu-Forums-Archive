### Post by 1clue on 2013-07-07
Not Linux exactly, but it's Open Source and Android, which started out as Linux.

I don't know how many of you guys used to use HP calculators back in the day. I've been horribly disappointed in the quality and inanity of smart phone calculator apps ever since I got a smart phone. Can't carry a phone AND a calculator, right?  You'd need a pocket protector and some tape on your glasses over the bridge of your nose.

I was trying to do some basic trig, found out that none of the 6 or 7 calculators I had loaded knew how to do arc-cosine. Started swearing, and then I started reminiscing about the calculator from back in college.

So I just found Droid48, an HP48 calculator app. I haven't dug my real HP48 out yet, but it sure looks the same. Probably about a hundred times faster too, just guessing.

5 stars, Open Source, RPN and does anything up to and including symbolic calculus. Probably a few things since then that outshine it, but I can't really say I've seen any of those.

The crazy thing about it is, it asks for almost no permissions.

### Post by 1clue on 2013-07-09
I never cared for the TI products, but frankly I haven't looked at them recently.

I must be in the niche, because while a bunch of the smart phone calculators can find the square root of pi, not many of them can do symbolic calculus, algebra and trig -- not just finding values, but manipulating the equations.

The HP48 could do that and the equation would look like it does on the chalk board.  It was incredibly slow, but it would do it.  The teachers weren't really that worried about it because of speed.  It was extremely fast back then as far as a calculator went, but slow in terms of real-world stuff.

We used to go into the exam, look at the 4 questions, decide which one we were most likely to mess up and punch that one into the calculator.  Then we'd take the exam and by the time we finished the last question, the calculator would be finished and we could check the answer.

So while it does that stuff, the most valuable part is that it knew as much math as I had to take, and had formulas built in so you didn't have to remember them.  It also had the first unit converter -- miles to km, that sort of thing.  You could add units to all your numbers, and it would convert them for you automatically.  Linux has "units" for that, almost the same thing.
