### Post by DuckHook on 2020-04-12
Security matters are rarely binary decisions. It's not a light switch: "A" is "secure"; "B" is not. Most security concerns exist on a continuum with a few apps highly secure, far too many a sick joke and the rest scattered somewhere in between. Zoom is closer to the sick joke end.

Many of my friends and family use Zoom because they are forced to. If your employer requires you to use this app, then you use it. The choice between sticking to your principles versus being out of a job is not a real choice. What floors me though is the silent sliding acceptance from using such apps under duress to using them as a matter of course. Between doctors and patients, where there is presumably a choice, its use is irresponsible.

It's not merely the fact that Zoom is opaque and has a bad history of more security holes than Swiss cheese. It's also the fact that they *are entitled* to skim your data and sell it to "third parties", which means to anyone who comes along and waves a coin under their nose.

Most of us realize that FOSS apps are not magic wands. Even as basic an app as ssh was broadsided by heartbleed. But there's a vast gaping chasm of a difference between unintentional security holes and intentional harvesting of your most private and intimate data. I'm prepared to put up with the former as an unavoidable fact of computing life; I am not remotely okay with the latter.

### Post by DuckHook on 2020-04-13
@kansasnoob,

I must politely disagree. Because that's not how real-world discussions work. If they are to be useful, they must be permitted to be spontaneous. Business discussions must be allowed to roam into next year's projections and last year's financials: "How's that new project coming along?" "What's your ROI?" Health discussions naturally roam into sensitive personal territory: "The injury is healing doc, but with the stress of this lockdown, my mental depression is returning. Should I increase my meds?" Else there's no point in having a discussion in the first place.

I too learned long ago that anything exchanged via e-mail is like a broadcast to the world. But the lightspeed changes that have happened since have rendered that old advice obsolete. Everyone does their banking online these days. Protonmail, Tutanota and similar providers have made it possible to exchange e-mails with a high level of security/privacy assurance. Sysadmins routinely make high-level adjustments to mission-critical servers remotely. Whether the Internet was originally built for security or not, it has long since evolved into a medium in which security is necessary and indispensable.

If we continue to reward apps like Zoom with our patronage, almost in spite of their awful security/privacy, what incentive is there for any app developer to attend to these concerns? If they have limited development resources (and who doesn't?), are they going to devote them to hunting down security holes and locking down privacy or developing that next shiny "feature" that will double their revenue stream?

While nothing is guaranteed to be unbreakable, there are apps that are more secure and there are apps that are less secure. The same applies to privacy. My point is a simple one: use those that are more secure, more private.

For one-on-one chats, use Signal.
For group video conferencing, use Jitsi or Jami.

They may be rougher around the edges and not as easy to use, but they are transparent. Dozens of eyeballs belonging to people far more capable than I have gone over them to ensure they contain nothing that will siphon my personal info off to some server on the dark web.

### Post by grahammechanical on 2020-04-13
Whilst not solving all the security concerns about Zoom it might be useful to know that someone has made an unofficial Snap app of the Zoom client. It is in the 18.04 & the 20.4 software center/snap store.

Once installed the snap store offers a page to set permissions which is more extensive that that offered by the Zoom app. It should be noted that five additional snaps have to be installed through the command line to get the functionality needed.

As the Zoom code is proprietary there is no way the code can be audited or modified but I guess that making it a snap package is a means of keeping the beast in some kind of cage and preventing it from roaming through the OS.
