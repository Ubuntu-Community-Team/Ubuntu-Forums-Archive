### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2015-11-19
Good News and Bad News!

First the Bad News . . . . 

>   Ubuntu or any version (we call versions . . . "distros") . . . . has a learning curve that can range from about 6 hours, to Infinity and Beyond.     Lot's depend on both your computer/tech background, AND, your needs.    If just a regular user, doink things like email, saving-tweaking photos, buying/shopping on the web, basic Office stuff, instant messaging etc., well then, the learning curving can be very short   For me, back in 2003, it took about 6 weeks to learn 90% of the basics.   The other 10% learning curve is still "under-way" . . 
>  IF you don't like doing *some* (occasional) online research (like these forums or a couple 300 other websites including youtube), AND doing some (occasional) article reading, well . . . . might think twice about using.  Note the article reading is mostly about optimizing or tweaking your installs (not really required but helpful).   If you can read a newspaper, you can read a few basic articles if needed.

The Good News . . . . 

>   The whole Linux environment can be a LOT less costly than the Windows environment.
>   Linux normally takes a LOT less maintenance than Windows (no Antivirus overhead, no disk defragging, no constant crapware assaults on the OS itself (still have it on the browser)
>   Easier and more stable software management (installs, uninstalls, etc.) because of centralized, vetted software repositories versus installing programs from any old website

A really good and lighter distro for older Windows PS's is Xubuntu . . . very stable, very modifiable.

Oh, by the way, about programs in Linux . . . . well, there are well over _50,000 programs_ (referred to as packages in Linux).    Yes, Firefox and Thunderbird are on the list.  (they might have been available on Linux before being on Windows even).

Almost all common Windows or Mac programs are also found on Linux (photo mgmt, media players, office suites, email suites, and about 40 or 50 other categories of software).

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2015-11-20
> **ralph16 said:**
> Mebbe have one machine with UBUNTU for web, words and sheets and keep a Win machine for Pshop and cubase...?      :)

Pretty much exactly what I do, except for Premiere Pro and Cubase (and Protools and Sibelius). Gimp works fine as a Photoshop replacement in my case. I dual-boot XP with a Ubuntu minimal install. Some would advise to use Wine but I personally don't see the point and have never used it.

You might want to look into Ardour as a replacement for Protools/Cubase. There are others and there is LOTS of audio software (see Ubuntu Studio, another flavour to look into, but you can add ubuntustudio-audio to Ubuntu from the Software Centre and that will install all the UStudio audio stuff).

Ubuntu is very flexible and customisable. You can create quite the hybrid! :D For an older machine, though, as has been suggested, a lighter flavour might suit better. Lubuntu or Xubuntu. Same flexibility, though. Same repositories to install from as Ubuntu.
