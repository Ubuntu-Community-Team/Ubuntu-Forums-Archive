### Post by vince-vlara on 2015-05-22
I do.

Today I read the BrightTalk user agreement and privacy statement and decided that I didn't want to let them collect data about me. 
Who is BrightTalk? Canonical use them for hosting their web events. I guess I will just have to skip the 'Designing and Sizing OpenStack for Success' webinar next week. Especially after I made the extra effort to check that the invalid DKIM email I received promoting it was actually a genuine message from Canonical sent by a marketeer who hasn't got a clue how to send authentic email properly.

So here's my customer insights for any Canonical CX staff that may be reading.

1. If you have the technology and capability to host your own web events, do so. Don't outsource to a company that gets between you and your customers with their own terms and conditions. Will your outsourced supplier tell you how many potential members of your audience walked away before completing their sign-up form?

2. Please ensure marketing emails originate from your own domain and that they are properly authenticated with DMARC(SPF+DKIM). A technology company really shouldn't be making simple mistakes like this. Instead of sending marketing messages from em-sj-77.mktomail.com DKIM'd for canonical.com consider sending them from mktomail.canonical.com .

3. Never send an email from [EMAIL="noreply@canonical.com"]noreply@canonical.com[/EMAIL] . If you are a CX professional you will already know why you should not do this. If you don't know, then you are not a CX professional! It is much better to have a campaign specific email address that forwards email for a set period to Canonical staff and then is either deleted or rejects email a month after the event. E.g. Event promotion email sent from [EMAIL="DaSOpenStackfS.event@canonical.com"]DaSOpenStackfS.event@canonical.com[/EMAIL] with replies forwarding to [EMAIL="jane.doe@canonical.com"]jane.doe@canonical.com[/EMAIL] in Marketing until 26 June 2015.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-05-22
To answer the question, not if it's free.  In those circumstances, I think it's guaranteed that the business is trying to generate revenue from collecting data, one way or another, about my use of their freebie. How could it be otherwise unless they're financially suicidal?

If I pay for something, I do usually at least skim the T&C, if just to make sure it matches their hype and contains no surprises. Looking for reputable reviews helps here, because they often point to unusual language in a T&C.

OTOH, there's usually a clause about you agreeing that the T&C can be changed without notification, so...
