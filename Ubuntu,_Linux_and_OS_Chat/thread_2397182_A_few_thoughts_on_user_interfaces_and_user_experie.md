### Post by frankvw on 2018-07-26
In response to [the thread about installation experiences with Bionic]("https://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2390456"), I'd like to ventilate my opinions about user interfaces.

I'm an old, grumpy stick-in-the-mud which, according to the author of the Frippery Gnome extensions, means I want a good, old menu, system tray, task bar and workplace switcher. And I must be, because I do! At the top of the screen I have a narrow bar that gives access to my applications via a menu and a bunch of short cuts, gives me a heads-up about running apps on hot standby (Skype, Veracrypt, Dropbox, Knotes) which can be brought to the foreground with a single click, and controls/indicators for network, sound and battery. At the bottom I have a task bar with (on the right hand side) a workplace selector to choose between any of my 6 virtual deskops. And that works great! Everything is out of the way, yet immediately available any time I want it.

How different is the trend that started with Unity (and the Metro desktop from Microsoft's little shop of horrors). Some icons are on screen, others aren't. Things zoom in and out of focus or applications whizz about across the desktop in response to keyboard shortcuts that may or may not conflict with what goes on within applications. Some things live in a "dock" on the left hand side of the screen, of all places, and for the rest you need a search function. Wait. Let's stop here for a moment and consider this: the default go-to for finding an application is a search function??? If there ever was more damning proof of a completely failing user interface, I don't know what it is. The job of a user interface is to make applications easily and quickly accessible, not to prevent it to the point where you have to go around the UI and resort to a search feature!!

Like Microsoft with Metro, Ubuntu has made the obvious (but terrible) mistake of getting mixed up in the hysteria surrounding mobile devices, and treating everything like a mobile device. Both Metro and the UI on Ubuntu 18.04 make a certain amount of sense on a cellphone or small tablet where you have limited screen real estate and no mouse/keyboard. But on a desktop machine they are an impediment to productivity, not an aid.

I don't think I can say it more plainly than this: **There are reasons why people resort to gnome-flashback, Mate or the Frippery extensions! They want a UI that works for them.**

So. I would like to make a suggestion here. Rather than forcing people to resort to forks like Mate, reversals like Gnome 2 or hacks like the Frippery extensions (which do need a fair amount of surgery on config files with an editor) it would be so much better to offer a "classic" desktop UI as an alternative option to the default one that belongs on a mobile device but has not business on the desktop. If the Frippery extensions do the job, why not bundle the whole thing as an option? That way even grumpy old farts like myself will be happy and nobody has to muck around with obsolete versions of Gnome, cumbersome forks and geekish hacks.

And while I'm on the subject, integrating various different settings that are currently all over the place would also be good. Some things live in "settings", others in "tweaks". Except for startup applications, which are somewhere else. And the dconf editor, which is guaranteed to boggle the minds of the vast majority of users. I could continue here, but I'm sure you get the picture. Let's just integrate all that stuff or at least make a good wrapper or front-end for it so that one doesn't have to search in four or more different places for where and how to configure stuff!

And now that I have solved the world's problems single-handedly with this post, I think I'll have a beer. :)
