### Post by DuckHook on 2016-01-09
Hi heroquestelf

Reading between the lines here so correct me if I'm wrong. You appear to be a hacker in denial. I mean that in a good way. The "hacker" I'm talking about is in the original sense of the word: a guy or gal who wanted to take things apart so that s/he could tinker with it, break it, put it back together again, and just get a charge out of learning as much as s/he could about it.

So, here's my suggestion:

1. Go on eBay.
2. Pick up a used computer for a song.
3. Load the mini.iso or Ubuntu Server on the damn thing with *no GUI*.
4. Go nuts.

I guarantee you'll learn more about Linux than you ever wanted to know.

Or you could take the lazy bum's way out (which is what I did), and load up such an environment in a VM. No matter how much or how badly I break it, I've always been able to dig myself out by reverting to a good snapshot. But that's really weaseling my way out of a hole. The macho dude does things the painful way.

### Post by Kale_Freemon on 2016-01-10
I've pretty much learned what I know just from breaking things. The same way I learned how to use Windows is how I learned how to use Ubuntu. I kept trying different things to see what would happen. I would frequently break my installations doing this, but that taught be what NOT to do.

I don't generally break my installs very often anymore because I've learned how not to. 

I've also learned ways to make my life easier for when things do break on occasion. I keep my home folder (personal files and configurations) on a 500GB secondary HDD and keep the operating system and installed software (except Steam games) on my 120GB SSD. That way, if I need to reinstall, all my files are still there, and all I have to do is reinstall software and do some minor reconfiguring. Less than a couple hours to do that. Compare that to Windows, which takes for flipping ever to set back up - unless you keep a backup image around (I only backup important files, not entire installations) - and that's only installing the updates.

Google and this community have also been invaluable tools to me for learning.
