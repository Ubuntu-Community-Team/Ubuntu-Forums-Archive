### Post by lon2 on 2015-04-07
It's been a few years since I work with laptops exclusively - and I ought to be as I get to travel a lot. I've had half a dozen of those now and a bunch of very recurring issues on every of them with using Ubuntu. Here's a set:

- slower-than-ought-to-be systems
- draining batteries
- misworking fans (and mild-to-gross overheating)
- misfiring wireless connectivity (and general hanging)
- unrecognized touchpads (and the resulting usability nightmare)
- poor support for a variety of things hardware

And here's my issue: while it was fun as a student to blow a week or two on diving into the system, I don't have that kind of luxury anymore, and having yet a novel problem with new code/hardware feels a lot like going in circles overall. I realize these issues are bound to the OEM and them not providing support/not caring enough. But I admit that does it for me: having yet another laptop with half the issues killed it for me. While I still use and need Linux extensively for both dev and servers (and rever it in that regard), I'll stick to ssh'ing now instead of having to deal with all the desktop mess. 

No hard feelings Ubuntu. Can't say it's been such a fun journey, but a journey it was nonetheless.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-04-08
Issues the OP raises still exist on some hardware, primarily because vendors have little incentive to ensure they don't. 

If something isn't working right, and Googling for fixes doesn't help, then the only real alternative is avoid buying laptops with known Linux issues the next time. Searching for both fixes and reports of problems can be made more difficult because vendors sometimes alter a design without changing the name of the product or bumping the version number.

I've also found Lenovo hardware very Linux friendly.  Even there, though, I'd avoid the brand new stuff.
