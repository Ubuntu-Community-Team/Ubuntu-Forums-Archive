### Post by guiverc on 2020-09-28
If you're after a *fast* Linux, as Mint uses run-time *adjustments* so they can use foreign (Ubuntu) packages, you're not talking about the fastest GNU/Linux anyway.

Any OS has strengths when compared to another OS, but also weaknesses too.

There are differences in Arch and your example of Linux Mint (which isn't Ubuntu, Ubuntu doesn't use run-time adjustments are if changes are required, changes are made to the code, it's recompiled and new packages are propagated to Ubuntu systems, Mint uses the run-time *adjustments *so they can use packages built by upstream Ubuntu). 

The best will depend on how it's used, what it's used on, and a lot of other very specific circumstances, and not general statements make little real sense.  But your example of Mint is not a minimalist approach anyway; having **not** chosen a fully-independent distribution of GNU/Linux (but one which uses a *hack* to achieve it's end goal).

### Post by DuckHook on 2020-11-09
To be frank, the OP's question is like asking: "Which is better: a hammer or a screwdriver?"

The two are so different, and address such divergent needs, that plastering a moralistic term like "better" onto the comparison constitutes a category fallacy and risks degenerating into pointless argumentation.

If you want to drive a nail into a piece of wood, use a hammer. If you want to turn a screw into that same piece of wood, use a screwdriver.

Likewise, what is it that you want to do with your OS?

MAC provides a pretty silo with high impenetrable walls within which apps trade freedom and power for ease of use. Linux provides freedom and power at the cost of far greater complexity and geekiness.

Within Linux itself, the same dynamic exists. Ubuntu provides a more structured approach to freedom with a preset look and feel. Arch foregoes structure but offers almost infinite customization.

It is pointless to ask which of these OSes are "better" than the other. They have different strengths because they have different goals. If you want freedom, MAC is the wrong OS. If you want hand-holding and familiarity, any of the Linuxes are the wrong OS.

Figure out what your goals are first. Then the OS that best attains those goals is the "best" one.
