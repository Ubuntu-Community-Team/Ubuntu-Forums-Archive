### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-07-14
"REAL Linux" and "REAL UBUNTU" are whatever developers and designers want it to be.  Today's Ubuntu is as real as yesterday's.

What you are nostalgic for is the panel-and-menu interface used in those old versions of Ubuntu, Gnome 2. Gnome 2 was terminated 3 years ago -- its developers cancelled the project and moved on to the entirely different Gnome 3.  Canonical moved to an interface it controlled, Unity. 

The Gnome 2 code has been picked up by the Mate project. Its most common, modernized, use is in the Linux Mint disto, which is based on Ubuntu.  The brand new, very alpha, Ubuntu Mate Remix highlights themes mimicing the old look of Ubuntu.  Mate can also be installed on Ubuntu as an alternative interface.

As mentioned, the panel-and-menu interface is alive in well in many other places.

> **QIII said:**
> 

1.  Ask the GNOME developers why they dropped development of GNOME 2. 

They would say they felt it was bug-ridden, etc.  I suspect they just didn't want to go into permanent maintenance mode with an otherwise frozen batch of code. Too boring.

Some issues were very real. Fuduntu, for one, made a valiant effort to stay with Gnome 2 but gave it up when the hassle and complexity of dealing with GTK3 and everything else that's come along since 2011 made it more than their small team could handle. Mate's porting to GTK3 and, in the next release, wants to give distros a compile-time option between building for GTK2 or GTK3.

> 2.  Ask Canonical why they aren't sticking with the desktop when there is an explosion of other devices that present a great number of new opportunities for market penetration.

Canonical's wants to turn a profit, and there's no money in selling desktop Linux. Red Hat, among others, tried it years ago and got out.  The licensing requirements mean anyone can copy and give away what you're trying to sell.

Yes. 

We've managed to come up with only two basic desktop models:  Dock and panel, used by OS X, Gnome Shell and Unity, and menu and panel, used by everything else. 

They're all very, very similar:  Rows of icons representing frequently used, and currently executing, apps.  Horizontal row = panel.  Vertical row = dock. (You can move a Gnome 2/Mate panel, or a KDE panel, to the left or right side and it goes vertical:  instant dock.)
