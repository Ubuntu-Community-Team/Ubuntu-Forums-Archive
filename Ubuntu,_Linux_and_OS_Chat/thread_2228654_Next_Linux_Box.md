I'm sure I'm not the only one here to think, "If I had my druthers, my next Linux Box would be . . . "  And it is possible to think about this even if a purchase (or build) is not imminent.

I've been laying the groundwork for my next Linux Box for some months now; purchase six months out . . . 

I'm an old school desktop user who travels.  As such I need mobility as well as precise input devices, keyboard and mouse, and a large output device, LCD, because I am low vision.  

Practically speaking, I regard tablets and smartphones as "read only" devices.  (And even for my present netbook I use a USB keyboard and mouse and output to a monitor via my D-sub port).

I've come up with an apparent sweet-spot for my situation.  The purpose of this post is to provide information that might be useful to others as well as to hear your feedback re: (my) blindspots and "gotchas."

Why not take a sff (small form factor) computer of 8 x 8 x 2.5 inches (198 mm x 198 mm x 60 mm) that you can shove in your pocket or small bag, then at your country of destination (for me, nearly exclusively Third World, for up to a year at a time) and purchase used input and output devices?

The computer I'm talking about is the Penguin Wee 4th Gen. GNU/Linux Desktop found at thinkpenguin.com (Shop < Desktop Computers). 

After reviewing Linux vendors such as Puget Systems, System 76, and ZaReason (polished professional websites and high-quality hardware), I must say I was disappointed by the "fly by night" appearance of the thinkpenguin.com website.  So, initially, I dismissed this site.

But I changed my mind for two reasons.  The first is that Linux Format (U.K.), a magazine that I have read and respect, gave this machine a 9/10 rating in the March, 2014 issue (LFX181).

The second is that, when I went to the thinkpenguin.com website to do my first config., I had many questions.  For example, SSD's in the drop down menu were listed by size but manufacturers were unstated (in fact, LFX gave the site a "6" for documentation). And I wanted to see an image of the open case to check accessibility. Etc.

Over several days, I emailed tech support with my questions.  I received detailed and rapid responses.  I received a list of all the SSD's that they are shipping.  I also received images of the open case and I/O ports (attached).  So, for me, this counts for a lot in spite of the "roughness" of the site.

The case, white plastic, is ventilated.  Removing 4 Phillips screws opens the case.  The mb is mini-itx.  You can see, picture attached, that this machine is very easy to work on.

The I/O Port backward compatibility: Wow!  Where I am now, Dominican Republic and Haiti, it is very hard to find a USB keyboard/mouse, used.  But this machine has a PS/2 port!  And a D-Sub for graphics output in less developed countries and HDMI/DVI for Europe and the U.S.  Other I/O ports include: 2 x USB 2.0; 2 x USB 3.0; 1 x eSATA; 1 x optical; 5 x audio.  See attached image.

Other specs:

--North Bridge Chipset, Intel H87
--Graphics: Intel HD 4400
--up to 16 GB DDR3 RAM
--May select "No OS" or preinstall choice of nine distros, including, Ubuntu 13.10 and 14.04.

The company tests and guarantees all hardware for Linux compatibility. Also, according to tech support, I may choose UEFI or BIOS.  When I order, I'll probably write everything out and do so by phone as the drop down menus are lacking. 

Base price: $449.00

BTW, crucial.com has a new ssd, 256 GB for $109.00, Crucial MX100 (consumer division of Micron).  My next question to tech support is if they can give me a discount for no hdd (comes with base price) and I'll buy my own . . . 

Still have to find out if I can run Second Life on Intel HD 4400. Supports 1080P. On die or mb?  Maybe I could get 8 gigs of RAM and run it from a 4 gig RAM disk . . . 

Anyway, I'm pretty jazzed about this machine.

Thoughts?
