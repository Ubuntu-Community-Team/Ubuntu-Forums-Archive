### Post by QIII on 2022-07-02
Out of the box, Ubuntu installs with only packages vetted by Canonical.  If you wish thereafter to add new packages, you can confidently add from the Main repository, which are maintained by Canonical.  The Universe repository contains packages maintained by Masters Of The Universe (MOTUs), who are volunteers that package and test packages not in the Main repo.  Those people are vetted by Canonical and you can also be confident of those packages.

There are good maintainers and bad.  Steer clear of them until you have asked about them here and even then be very wary.

A statement like "Ubuntu rivals OS X", is rather broad and needs more context. I would observe that this statement is true in some areas but not others. In matters of power and flexibility, I would say that Ubuntu is even more powerful than OS X. For example, I can change my Ubuntu kernel to a whole range of other optional kernels of my choosing. OS X won't allow that. On the other hand, OS X will run iTunes natively. This is not possible on Ubuntu.

So, it isn't a matter of confidence that leads me to my conclusions; it's a matter of bowing to reality. Ubuntu is a geek's OS: it's raison d'etre is to permit practically limitless tinkering and augmentation. OS X is the opposite: it permits no changes because it doesn't want to be obligated to fixing the inevitable resulting breakage. A mass market OS *cannot* permit such breakage: it is too destructive to the bottom line. Ergo, an OS that is open for geeks to break cannot be a mass market OS.
> **jacksonguitars said:**
> So there is a version of Ubuntu with pre-installed appz with only safe audited code?
Your idea of "audited code" is a creature of the proprietary space.

It may help to underline the key difference between FOSS and proprietary code. The foundational principle of FOSS is that anyone can go through the code and ascertain for themselves whether it is good or bad. Theoretically, it is impossible to "hide" malicious code in FOSS because, by definition, Open Source means that the source code is always open for inspection and that this is its natural state. Open code is transparent. How can any code be more auditable than that?

Now, it matters little that the code is inaccessible to you or I (I can't code to save my life). What matters is that there are thousands of qualified eyeballs—or potential eyeballs—perusing that code. For example, I doubt that any of the proprietary kernels are as heavily examined as the Linux kernel. No matter how many resources Redmond throws at their secretive Windows, they can hardly match the hundreds of thousands (possibly millions) of eyeballs that scour Linux at any given time.

So the concept of "audited code" only has applicable meaning when the code is closed source. Such an environment is opaque, which creates the need for some trusted third party to review the code (since we cannot). That's the only point in auditing anything, whether accounting books or code: to ensure that opaque processes are accurately reflecting what the proprietor is claiming.

When that opacity is eliminated, the need for "auditing" also disappears.

### Post by DuckHook on 2022-07-04
> **jacksonguitars said:**
> I think "Ubuntu Software" should only have apps with safe audited code. Every other app. should be in its own subsection.
I've already addressed the misconception re: "audited code". It is not applicable to FOSS, from which Ubuntu is entirely built. Hence, your continuing insistence on applying the concept of "auditing" to Ubuntu is a non sequitur.

Now let's address "safe code". Safety is not a simplistic, absolutist concept. All software has bugs. All software has security weaknesses. There is no such thing as absolutely "safe" code. Even the most well reviewed app that has been examined by thousands of highly skilled experts will contain overlooked faults and exploits that bad actors can potentially target. The apps in the Main and Universe repositories have been vetted for anything intentionally malicious, but no one can guarantee that an app is perfect and free of defects. This is the reason we must keep all software updated and patched. The best we can do is to close off holes *as they are discovered* and maintain *a relative degree* of safety.

If we follow your prescription, there will be nothing in "Ubuntu Software" because nothing would qualify—not even the base operating system itself. Not a week goes by that we don't have to patch a security hole or a bug in some fundamental component of Ubuntu.

In my opinion, the Ubuntu repos are laid out now about as logically and sensibly as they can be. Further changes won't add anything.
> …Ubuntu has become so good it should be in the retail chains or at least pre-distributed with some laptops and desktops.
This was tried by ASUS as early as a decade ago. I may have been one of the few hundred or so people who actually bought one of their machines from Best Buy, a large NA retail chain.

It was quickly taken off the market for lack of sales. Fortunately, being Ubuntu&#8209;based, it has been easy for me to maintain it by myself without any help from ASUS, but the experience was instructive—there's just no mass retail market for Ubuntu machines. Ubuntu in particular and Linux in general is a specialty market for a specialty crowd. Certainly, some retailers have done well enough selling Ubuntu machines online, but they are minnows compared to the OEM whales selling Windows or OS X.

We may be posting at cross purposes: I too think that Ubuntu is good enough to rival the proprietary Goliaths. In fact, as my previous posts state, I think Ubuntu is actually superior to them. But survival in the retail space is not predicated on how technically superior Ubuntu/Linux is to the market leaders. It has far more to do with considerations like brand inertia, the network effect, eye&#8209;candy, apps and games, marketing clout and even the underhanded tactics that the proprietary cartel uses to spread fear, uncertainty and doubt about Linux.

The amount of lobbying they do is also ferocious and cannot be matched by the open source community. Some years ago, the municipality of Munich switched to Linux—not without some teething pains. Just as the civic staff were finally getting used to their new operating system, a new elected council came into power and pulled the plug on Linux, ordering the administration to re-implement Windows. This fiasco ended up costing Munich millions of dollars and was orchestrated behind the scenes by a fierce lobbying effort from Microsoft because they were scared to death that Munich would serve as a model for other municipalities to drop Windows.

Did you know that in 2020, Microsoft's marketing budget alone was $20 billion? Apple's was even more. There is just no way that open source projects like Ubuntu can come close to that sort of clout.

These factors are so powerful and so oppressive that we cannot change them just by hoping and wishing.

My own strategy is to show my friends and family quietly and by example. I started out loudly proselytizing Ubuntu, but I found that this was counterproductive. It raised false expectations and turned them off the OS when they inevitably discovered that it won't run their favourite games, or won't run Photoshop (or whatever). Nowadays, I just run the OS in front of them without comment. About 1 in 4 will show some natural curiosity. That's when I show them how lean it is, how it doesn't need AV, how I can do everything on it that they can do on their far more expensive proprietary cartel OSes, etc. A small number then go on to adopt Linux. Over the years, I've switched about half a dozen people to Ubuntu using this approach.

If every Linux enthusiast would successfully introduce Ubuntu to six of their friends and family, it would go a long way to countering the massive advantages that the proprietary cartel possesses. And it has the advantage of proven efficacy over the stream of "improvement suggestions" that are constantly floated by Ubuntu's well meaning but sometimes unrealistic proponents.
