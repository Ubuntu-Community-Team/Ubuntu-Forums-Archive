"We?" whose is "we?"

Canonical and Microsoft live in 2 completely different universes. Microsoft had all these kinds of issues until it set up a system where OEMs had to apply to Microsoft to have their products accredited as working with the Windows OS. Without an official label that the product would work with Windows people would not buy that hardware. And now we have Windows computers. Not, mind you, a computer with a choice of OS of which Windows is just one. OH, no.

You seem to forget that Ubuntu is a Linux distribution. Ubuntu is built on components that are the responsibility of other developers. Canonical cannot fix the lack of a Nvidia driver for Nvidia Optimus technology because that technology is proprietary and Nvidia is not going to release details of its products otherwise it will loose sales to its rivals. And yet without such a disclosure of information it is very difficult for the developers of open source drivers to solve these issues.

What Canonical is doing is working with hardware makers so that those hardware makers will bring out mobile devices with the Ubuntu platform pre-installed. Canonical is polishing the Ubuntu platform. 

And benefits will come to the desktop. And it will still be open source so other Linux distributions can make use of Ubuntu code. Thus benefiting their users.

I have already seen better usage of memory on 13.04 compared with 12.04 and it is due to optimization work done in preparation for the Ubuntu phone. I have seen a 15 - 20% drop in memory usage on my 1GB RAM machine.

Not at all. Canonical understands that when it comes to the Desktop Microsoft is like an "elephant in the room." And when it comes to mobiles devices there are two elephants in the room. That is why Canonical is taking the high risk, high reward gamble of convergence. At the moment Canonical makes money but it is not making profits. ### Post by Cheesehead on 2013-07-09
> **EdgyPowell said:**
> ... is Canonical trying to do too much too soon?
No. Look at the stated goals for each cycle vs. the delivered software. Canonical has delivered the great majority of promised software, and usually on time.


> **EdgyPowell said:**
> I thought it would have been more logical to develop one product, release it and then move onto the next but that doesn't seem to be happening
False assumption: The way one person works most effectively is not the way an organization works most effectively. Well-run organizations certainly can pursue multiple projects in pursuit of a single overall goal (like Ubuntu).


> **EdgyPowell said:**
> in fact why don't we polish of Ubuntu OS to fix common problems such as graphical and resolution problems
False choice: Each release of Ubuntu works on both fixing bugs and introducing new features. It's not an either/or tradeoff.
False assumption: Additional resources on a single software project does not necessarily create better software. For example, engineers troubleshooting and fixing X bugs are limited by the number of useful bugs to work from. 
False assertion: "Graphical and resolution" problems are much rarer than in the past.  A few years ago, the Xorg project made *huge* progress improving their software, improving compatibility and eliminating whole classes of bugs. Most "resolution" bugs that I see today are defective, dying, or unsupported hardware instead of Ubuntu or X.


> **EdgyPowell said:**
> It seems like Canonical is just focused on trying to out beat Microsoft and Apple and ignoring what the consumers and the community want.
False assertion: Microsoft, Apple, and Canonical have very different business models, and different measures of success. "beat out" according to which model?
False assertion: Ubuntu developers listen to and act upon huge amounts of consumer and community feedback. Including user testing by the Unity designers, open and public goal-setting discussion at Virtual UDS four times each year, public team discussions at ubuntuonair.com, public team meetings on IRC, public team mailing lists, open and transparent bug tracking and code hosting, and more.


You can believe what you want, naturally. But you're flat wrong.
