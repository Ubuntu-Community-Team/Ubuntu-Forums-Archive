It's not any harder *because it's a LTS release*; actually the exact opposite is the case: I've seen many freeze exceptions granted in the case of new upstream releases, especially in Universe, simply due to the fact that the software will be maintained for a longer duration and we don't want people to get stuck with an older version for 3 or 5 years if there's a very significant new feature in the newer version that has little risk of regressions. It's usually already hard to get things in after freezes, which are there for a reason.

I see what you mean. While it's inevitable that this will be the case at times, saying that it's *usually* the case would be an exaggeration, especially in the case of upstreams such as GNOME, X and Compiz that we track closely. It may be that you were simply late in encountering the bugs you reported so far. If a bug of moderate to high impact is known upstream, and is present in the code that's in the current development version of Ubuntu, it will almost certainly have been reported in LP too, and LP (or Apport, if it's a crasher) will most likely catch it and warn you that it's a duplicate if you've entered a good descriptive title. And the fact that a bug has been reported doesn't mean the function of the bug tracker has been fulfilled for that bug; it has yet to be triaged, and additional information coming from distribution bug trackers, or people using the software as it's shipped in distributions, can help triage it.

And even if the bug is known upstream, it may manifest itself in a different form in different distributions due to the ways it interacts with the different software in the distributions. This is just one more reason why it's not useless to have bugs reported and triaged on the distribution side too.

Not filing a report based on the assumption that "it must be known already" would be bad practice.

The trick here is to ensure that the bug really *is* an upstream bug.  Ubuntu will often have some patches, different libraries, different other components, different configs, etc.

Also, even if it is an upstream bug it's still useful to us to track it in Launchpad; this gives us a better idea of the cost/benefit of upgrading packages, for example.

In short, filing in Launchpad should be the first step; the bug is *definitely* in Ubuntu, and we definitely want to know about it.  It can be a good second step to see whether it's an upstream bug, and file it there, but it's not mandatory.
