### Post by 23meg on 2010-03-15
Unfortunately, I don't think that's a very useful question, at least on its own. Because:

[list][*] It's next to inevitable that people will at least *once* click the wrong thing when a layout they're used to changes suddenly. Slightly better questions would be how many times they clicked the wrong thing, and how long the misclicks continued until they adapted.


[*] Data on whether people clicked the wrong thing or not, or how often and how long it happened, are not to be used alone in determining the appropriateness of the positioning. Even if people don't misclick, they could be hesitating, looking for the buttons, shoving their cursor to the right, discovering that there are no buttons, and then going left, so on. The cognitive load that the change makes people suffer is also important, and cannot be quantified with binary choices.


[*] People will rationalize in their answers according to what they prefer, and since most people's comfort zone is the old setup with window controls on the right, the rationalization will be hugely skewed in that direction. *Observing* people's behavior in a usability testing setup would be hugely more useful.[/list]
I'll try to come up with such an ad-hoc usability test for this.

When you are not clicking on stuff which side of the screen do you move the mouse cursor to?

Getting off the topic of this specific change, and into the general discussion of options, each additional option has a cost, so there are good arguments to keep options to a minimum, but the thought process should also include the questions 'What fuels the desire for the option in question?', 'Is it because of a something that isn't done or could be done better?' and 'Is there a way to modify the existing stuff to handle things/fix things that would take care of the situation that fuels the desire for the option making the option obsolete?'

In the context of this change, having left and right themes would allow for the choice, but without some geeky utility or "option" that requires them to choose where to put the buttons.

This all assumes the buttons on the left thing was an arbitrary decision, which the earlier response to peoples reaction to the change made it seem, but since then there has been mention of "interesting things" that can be done on the right side of the title bar, if the buttons are moved to the left. Depending on what these "interesting things" are it may make perfect sense to move the buttons to the left, because the new functionality makes more sense on the right and outweighs the desire to have the buttons on the right, but until we actually see these things, we don't know, and the buttons on the left should be optional.

### Post by BFG on 2010-03-20
I've clicked on the wrong thing a few times.  I will get used to it, but I doubt it will ever stop feeling wrong.

Human Interfaces are not just created for aesthetic values.  Cognitive psychology plays a huge part.
From early days of interfaces this has been prevalent. The earliest common inteface are the controls of an automobile.  E.g. On the steering wheel of a car, the direction indicators can be on the left stalk, or the right stalk.  But regardless, they will operate in the direction of the steeringwheel.  The right signal WILL ALWAYS be operated by moving the stalk in the clockwise direction of the steering wheel and vice versa.  Humans could get used to it if it were opposite, but it would feel wrong.

Aesthetics are a small part of the picture. By all means move it, but only do so for valid UI reasons, not just to be different. For the majority of humans the brain associates the top right field of the visual cortex with the concept of "ending".  This is why corporate TV logos are on the top left.  It makes sense. 

[LIST]
[*]In most cultures we write left to right. The right is the end.
[*]In all cultures, mathematical graphs are shown left to right.
[*]On searches, forms etc.  The action button is on the right hand side.  The concept is clear; "I am done".
[*]On a control panel showing go / stop horizontally, Stop is never on the left. 
[*]On traffic lights stop is at the top.
[*]For a right-handed person, most hand gestures illustrating the concept of termination move from left to right.  E.g. Stop, get out, director saying "cut" using finger across throat, etc.
[*]Ask people to align the concept of go and stop, and the majority will place stop on the right hand side.

[IMG]http://www.weldedbliss.co.uk/images/uploads/stop_go_1_thumb.jpg[/IMG]
[/LIST]

THIS is why the earliest computer interfaces had it top right.  Someone didn't just make it up and then everyone else copied.

Ubuntu works well at present in that the button to shut down is top right.  The button to end a window is also top right.  Top and right are the place for it to be as it's where humans expect to find it, and at the very least they are both in the same direction.


Some assurance that Cognitive Psychology has at least been explored in this change would be most welcome.

### Post by cpmman on 2010-03-22
**Other**

Most (if not all) operating systems retain the keyboard conventions for most operations.  Since the introduction of gui from gem to date those operations have been replicated to a marked degree on the mouse. The convention to have pictographs for certain operations was introduced to enable new users to perform those operations with a simple click.  The simple click can also be performed by visiting the title bar and right clicking/hold/descend to target/let go - this action also keeps the action as one movement and avoids the need to locate and visit a small target area.  I have tried the left configuration and found it to be no different from the right configuration.

With a little effort one can also add various personal preference features to the mouse such as roll/shade and even compound instructions.  This is not beyond the average user when given a little encouragement and howto guidance.  For some people the abandonment of 'muscle memory' may help them to avoid RSI - the target area of the title bar being close to the next operation requirement which in my case may be any one of the 16 pictographs which are distributed there.

**Left-side window controls vs Gnome 3 plans**

> Sorry for (re?)opening this can of worms. I have searched and couldn't

seems to be the most comprehensive and up-to-date document about the
design goals of Gnome 3. On page 6, you can find the considerations I'm

[B][COLOR="Red"]Note in the second paragraph how the hot corner of the screen assumes
that the windows' top-left corner is free of other items: "Window

'Hot corner

The upper left corner of the Activities Item and the screen should
behave as a "hot corner." Moving the pointer into this area should
automatically activate the Activies Item. This offers a few advantages:
      * Allows for quickly accessing functionality
      * Eliminates need to aim and click on target button
      * May be perceived as more efficient by users
      * Obviates the need for "dwell click" for input devices without
        button click functionality (eg. head/eye tracking pointers)
      * May facilitate drag-and-drop into the Overview (if necessary)

Unlike many other forms of hot corners or edges this will likely not be
subject to inadvertent triggering for a few reasons:
      * The corner in which it resides is already "owned" by a control
        for identical functionality
      * Window caption controls are located on the opposite (right-hand)
        side

Once the Activities Overview has been triggered using the hot-corner,
the pointer should have to move at least 5 pixels before the corner can
be triggered again. Similarly, clicks on the Activities Item should be
ignored for a short time after the hot-corner is activated. These
are necessary in order to "de-bounce" activations.'


Otherwise, thanks for a job IMHO extremely well done with Lucid's
design.

Mario
