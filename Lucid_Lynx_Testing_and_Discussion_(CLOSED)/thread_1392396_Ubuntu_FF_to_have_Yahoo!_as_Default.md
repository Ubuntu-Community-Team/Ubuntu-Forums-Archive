### Post by 23meg on 2010-01-26
I thought I'd start a thread to clarify this in advance, since lots of people will probably be asking the same question in a short while.

The default search provider in Firefox 3.6 will soon be changed from Google to Yahoo. This is intentional, and is due to a deal between Canonical and Yahoo, which will generate revenue for Canonical. Mozilla [is aware of]("http://popey.com/blog/2010/01/26/yahoobuntu/#comment-1905") and has no problems with the change.

You will also note that the default home page will change according to your preference of search provider.

This is, of course, just a change to the defaults, which you can change with two clicks, the way you've always been able to, in which case no referrer revenue from your searches will go to Canonical.

[Here's a post with more information on the change]("https://lists.ubuntu.com/archives/ubuntu-devel/2010-January/030065.html") by Rick Spencer, Ubuntu Desktop Team manager at Canonical, to the ubuntu-devel mailing list.

I think when talking about the process it's important to make a distinction between what Canonical is legally obligated to do and what it is morally obligated to do. Legally, Canonical does not need to solicit any user input. Most Ubuntu users are not shareholders in Canonical (have they even gone public?). We do not have a direct financial stake in the company. Morally, I think user input should matter a little. It isn't a democracy. We don't all get one vote, but it is a matter of courtesy and good politics to acknowledge the voice of the community that supports you.

Where would Ubuntu be today without the excellent support, tutorials, and bug reporting that the Ubuntu community (users and forum members) provides? We don't all get paid to work for Ubuntu. We are not official employees. But we certainly help make Ubuntu happen and we have a sociological stake in the company if not a financial one.

Now, that said, I think if every decision relies on a poll of Ubuntu Forums members, nothing is ever going to change. People in general (not just Ubuntu users) tend to be conservative and want to stick to what kind of, sort of works instead of testing out what could work better. The other danger, of course, is that you switch to something worse and once you've switched it's too much trouble to go back to the old way, as many have gotten used to the new way.

It's not all cut and dried, folks. It's not as simple as "They can do whatever they want. Good on 'em." It's also not as simple as "They have to listen to us. We're the community. They're just like closed source!" It's a delicate balancing act here to be an autonomous company with its own goals and financial struggles that also relies very heavily on a volunteer support community that is largely made up of its user base.
