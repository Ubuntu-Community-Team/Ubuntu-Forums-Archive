The only thing "usable" of buttons being on the right was (when I used windows) I could close the window quickly, simply move the mouse to the top-most right possible and click. 'Kinda like an action-corner which involves a click.

I'm using emerald and while exploring it I got the sense of how much you can break the boundaries of "standards & habits".
I moved the buttons to be on the left side a while ago (actually it was before Canonical's announcement) thinking one thing: The buttons are actually closer to where you work the mouse.

If you think about it, the major "action items" on the screen are on the left.
In the big picture, the gnome menu is on the left so if you opened something by mistake closing it is closer.
If you look at the program's scope, the buttons are actually where all the critical operations of the program reside, which is on the left also (the File menu, back button in browsers, new message in mailers, other quick access icons, etc.)

I know it's a big change for most users, perhaps the biggest for some of them. There should be a "change to right" button IMO.
I personally like it, I even changed it to the same order Lucid will have (which makes more sense preventing closing a software accidentally).

--

One more thing about usability and habits. I came to realize that prior to internationalization of OSs, they were written for English speakers only (or should I say, supporting one keyboard layout).
As a side-kick to support other keyboard layouts, a combination of keys (alt+shift, both alts) was implemented.

After thinking about it for a while, it seems against human logic to use a combination just so you could write a different language.
Using a combination for switching for people typing in more then one language, requires them to use both of the hands (two alts) or a non-comfortable combo (alt+shift) which requires you to move the left/right hand entirely away from the keyboard.

I'm using the menu key, a single key which I use one finger for. Typing in two languages makes more sense this way (and is hell faster).
Although the option to change it exist, not many users do because of that habit of using a combination.

I think what I want to say is that habits and so called "standards" not following any guideline is a bad thing, and changing them is a good thing.

EdgeSwitch=0
WorkspaceNames="1", "2", "3", "4

```

### Post by aysiu on 2010-03-15
You can ask users for their input without having it be a straight democratic vote.

Here's how you do it:

**Step 1**: Announce that you are exploring new ways of changing the Ubuntu look and feel. Have users take a survey on various options *with an express* caveat *that the decision will be made not based directly on the user feedback but still taking the feedback into account.*

**Step 2**: Consult among the design team to figure out what the best way is to implement a change (What problem does it solve? What are the benefits? What are the drawbacks? How does it affect usability? What obstacles will there be to adapting to the new change?). Finally come to a decision.

**Step 3**: Announce (could be on a mailing list, a blog, a wiki entry... ANYTHING!) the change and _the rationale behind the change_. Explain both the explicit and specific benefits of the change and the drawbacks you have recognized.

Here's how not to do it:

**Step 1**: Consult amongst the design team.

**Step 2**: Make a seemingly arbitrary change that brings about no immediately obvious usability improvements

**Step 3**: Allow the Ubuntu communities and other non-design team employees to speculate as to what reasons (or not) there may be for the change. Just hope they get used to it.

See the difference? You don't have to be a slave to the opinions of the masses. You just have to involve the community and explain the reasons for your choices.
