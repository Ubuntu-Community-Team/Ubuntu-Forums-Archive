### Post by Mark Phelps on 2010-04-07
Seems like every day now, we're getting posts in the General forum from folks with singe-digit post counts who just installed one of the beta's and are now devastated that some stuff won't work!

And, of course, none of the "newbie"s doing this have a clue about doing some form of back first so they can restore their current 9.x Ubuntu install.

I've personally had a lot of experience doing beta testing of commercial and company software, and in all cases, there was a form of "vetting" done to ensure that I was able to actually DO beta testing before allowing me access to the product.

And, OK, I realize this is open-source stuff and, to many folks, ANY limitation at all amounts to heresy ...

But, I wanted to get feedback about changing the way the pre-release Ubuntu versions are made available as follows:
1) Host them at a site that requires approval to access
2) Approval involves filling out a basic questionnaire that will indicate the skills needed to do beta testing, the risks involved, and agreement to abide by a code of conduct.
3) Require, as a condition of receiving the pre-release versions, that issues and problems are ONLY posted to the proper testing subforum.

My goals in doing the above are the following:
1) Indicating to those unfamiliar with pre-release products the risks they're undertaking when installing such a product
2) Indicating the types of skills needed to do beta testing -- such that those unfamiliar with such testing get some idea of what is involved
3) Preventing the now commonplace problem of folks who can't even spell Ubuntu from posting in the general forums on a daily basis
4) Preventing the other beta testers from being bogged down by elementary "how do I do ..." questions in the testing forum what indicate general lack of Ubuntu knowledge, not actual problems with the beta software.

OK, so everyone is probably going to think these are BAD suggestions, but I'd be interested in hearing others for dealing with the current spate of newbie-based-beta problems.

### Post by tokyobadger on 2010-04-08
I think limiting access to development releases would pave the way to a Windows/OSX like situation where the ISOs would appear on unsanctioned torrents / mirrors, the inherent risk then being that there is no knowing that the code hasn't been tampered with.

I also think that limiting access would also (in some ways) counter the stated [Ubuntu Philosophy](http://www.ubuntu.com/community/ubuntustory/philosophy)

In reality, the dev teams are surely already testing before public release of alphas and betas, so that would be a form of limited access.

If you take 2 steps back and consider what Ubuntu has aimed for, it is a 'just works' distro for those who are less likely to tinker under the hood. With that demographic in mind, having the broadest cross-section of users try the development releases provides richer feedback and contributes to a smoother final release experience.

As others have pointed out, human nature (curiosity, allure of the new and shiny etc) + prominence on the top of the website are going to drive the number of people interested to try irrespective of their ability to fix things when they break.

How should this be countered?
1) bigger, scarier warnings on the download page? won't work, see human nature comment above

2) bigger, scarier warnings in the installer? won't work, if someone's gone as far as to download (and for some expend limited bandwidth) and burn the ISO (some still use CD-R) a couple of lines of text will not turn them back

3) make them RTFM / RTFS? might work for some, but will undermine the positioning Ubuntu has striven for

4) ignore their requests for help in the forums? same as #3

I'd advocate a couple of different approaches:

1) insert a 'beta tester's code of conduct agreement' in the installer outlining what they are embarking on, how to go about addressing problems in the forums / irc channels; make sure that until read and checked the installer does not move forward

2) at the end of the agreement in #1, give people the option to bail on the beta install and opt instead for a net-install of current stable from within the same installer

3) if they opt for the beta, along with user pwd etc, make the installer enter their forums ID or create one

4) set the default homepage of all browsers in the beta editions to the current testing Discussion thread (would certainly avoid meaningless yahoo vs google debates ;-) )

5) have community experts & forum mods create a sticky with common tricks for fixing well known problems (this could be largely recyclable per release after a one time effort) and encourage all beta testers to refer people to the sticky first (a gentler form of RTFS)

I'm sure there are a lot of other good ideas that would avoid repeating the RTFM-syndrome that plagued many distros' communities back in the late '90s when I first got involved, and still provide good feedback to the dev teams.

### Post by Mike_IronFist on 2010-04-08
> **Mark Phelps said:**
> Seems like every day now, we're getting posts in the General forum from folks with singe-digit post counts who just installed one of the beta's and are now devastated that some stuff won't work!

And, of course, none of the "newbie"s doing this have a clue about doing some form of back first so they can restore their current 9.x Ubuntu install.

I've personally had a lot of experience doing beta testing of commercial and company software, and in all cases, there was a form of "vetting" done to ensure that I was able to actually DO beta testing before allowing me access to the product.

And, OK, I realize this is open-source stuff and, to many folks, ANY limitation at all amounts to heresy ...

But, I wanted to get feedback about changing the way the pre-release Ubuntu versions are made available as follows:
1) Host them at a site that requires approval to access
2) Approval involves filling out a basic questionnaire that will indicate the skills needed to do beta testing, the risks involved, and agreement to abide by a code of conduct.
3) Require, as a condition of receiving the pre-release versions, that issues and problems are ONLY posted to the proper testing subforum.

My goals in doing the above are the following:
1) Indicating to those unfamiliar with pre-release products the risks they're undertaking when installing such a product
2) Indicating the types of skills needed to do beta testing -- such that those unfamiliar with such testing get some idea of what is involved
3) Preventing the now commonplace problem of folks who can't even spell Ubuntu from posting in the general forums on a daily basis
4) Preventing the other beta testers from being bogged down by elementary "how do I do ..." questions in the testing forum what indicate general lack of Ubuntu knowledge, not actual problems with the beta software.

OK, so everyone is probably going to think these are BAD suggestions, but I'd be interested in hearing others for dealing with the current spate of newbie-based-beta problems.

These are not just BAD suggestions, they're TERRIBLE suggestions. Ubuntu is not just Open Source, it follows a community development model - that means that the entire OS, warts and all, is available for testing no matter what state its currently-developing version is in. This is called the famous "Bazaar" model; people come on in, have a look around, and exchange bug reports, ideas/feedback, and code patches. Everyone's allowed to do it, and that's what makes Ubuntu so strong - the community gets involved and allows for a TON of revision to be done on things before the release date.

So it's not like some open source developers don't mind doing closed development and just releasing the source code with every release, but it's a bad development model - not enough eyes gawking at the code or hands using the software to make sure it works.

Lastly, the front page for Ubuntu's website during Beta and RC phases is extremely clear on what the beta is meant for:
[QUOTE= Ubuntu.com]Ubuntu 10.04 LTS (Long-Term Support) is just around the corner. If you want to help us, _download beta 1 now_. [B]We appreciate your feedback and support, but do not recommend using the beta version in working environments.
[/B]
**If you're unsure, play it safe...**
_**[Download Ubuntu 9.10]**_[/QUOTE]

> **geekwanabe said:**
> To have untrained people testing a complex system is just adding to the ubinntended clutter; no I don't advocate any banning or limits, just some simple qualification testing to validate user ability, interest and commitment to the project. This would then grant both elevated access/priority as well as a responsibility implied in their commitment.

Additionally, this could be a gateway to some official "training course" (that someone can develop with text and video) lessons - possibly concluding with something like an "open source MSCE".

[http://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/LPI_Linux_Certification](http://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/LPI_Linux_Certification)

[http://www.lpi.org/](http://www.lpi.org/)

You don't need any special qualifications to test Microsoft Software. It may make it more likely that you will get in on the ground level of the testing, but then they have their later "Public" betas, which anybody can get in on.

What you see with Ubuntu could be viewed as something parallel to this. It's open the entire way, but the people that are more serious about testing will jump in during the Alpha phase, the more casual testers will wait 'till some time during the Beta phase.

So you get more people asking 'newbie' questions as you get closer to release as the more casual testers start wanting to test it out, that's not necessarily a bad thing.

One of the reasons people may install the stuff that's in development is so they can learn, there is no better way to learn that to have to deal with problems and either figure out what is going on and take measures, find some guide or post somewhere laying out some steps for dealing with the issue or find some information to get educated on the factors surrounding the issue at hand to either figure out a solution or take things from multiple similar but different solutions and figure out how to use things from those to deal with your specific situation.

Some people may have pet peeves and install the stuff that's in development to try to do their part to see that it gets fixed, but outside of that not have any real interest in the minutia of testing the larger range of things that have changed during the development cycle.

Another issue is people asking if the version that's in development is stable and other people indicating 'it's stable for me'. 

Not crashing and being stable are two different things. 

If there are still changes going on that may cause things to break due to packaging issues, library incompatibilities, may cause scripts not work, etc... it's not stable, and you shouldn't tell people that ask that it is stable.

I don't think anybody has brought this up, by having people that are less capable of finding a solution on their own, you have some trial runs of what things are going to be like when the final release happens. Helping these people out is not only helping them collect information so maybe they can file a sane bug report or add their information to an existing one. It is also adding to the collective pool of knowledge that will be drawn on after the final release, to help the rest of the community in dealing with the things that changed or that didn't get fixed in the final release.

So, if you don't have 'newbies' and people willing to help deal with the relatively low number of 'newbie' questions during the beta cycle, what's it going to be like when the final release comes around.

Later, Seeker

In other words: what you're proposing already happens. However, there are layers of complexity unique to the development release (such as partial upgrades), that do not exist in the final release - and therefore do not need to be included in the final release notes.

> So, if you don't have 'newbies' and people willing to help deal with the relatively low number of 'newbie' questions during the beta cycle, what's it going to be like when the final release comes around.Relatively low? After the beta phase, I would describe it as relatively high - which isn't a problem, except for the fact that a large volume of these newbies do not read the supplied "pool of knowledge".

This is pure laziness, and at the end of the day I find that my time was wasted in trying to help.

I'm not against newbies using the development release - I'm against newbies who ignore advice and persist in asking the same questions, which they could have found out by themselves. On the forums, there are a lot of kind-hearted people who devote their time in helping others, but IMHO, sometimes this community spirit can be misguided.

This is what's happening now: newbies are being rewarded for laziness. As a prerequisite to testing, you should make yourself familiar with certain aspect of the development release. Thanks for your understanding."

Restricting availablity of the development release is a bad idea. What we really need is to find ways to encourage people (newbies and otherwise) to actually to read the testing documentation.
