**The reasons for removing the GIMP**

  The GIMP is an impressive software application and is arguably one of the most profound achievements of the Linux desktop ecosystem. In our past coverage of the GIMP here at Ars, we have highlighted its [increasingly viable status]("http://arstechnica.com/open-source/news/2008/10/gimp-2-6-released-one-step-closer-to-taking-on-photoshop.ars") as a [serious contender]("http://arstechnica.com/media/news/2009/01/gimp-2-6-review.ars") in the field of professional graphic editing.
  The GIMP's strengths are precisely what make it unsuitable as a default component of a mainstream operating system. Its expansive assortment of rich features necessitate a complicated user interface that is intimidating to inexperienced users. It is also saddled with a lengthy startup time due to its large collection of plugins. As one would expect of a high-end graphic editing tool, it also uses a lot of disk space. Its size footprint is especially problematic for Ubuntu, because the distribution ships as a CD image and has a very limited amount of space.
  Providing professional-grade editing capabilities is a goal that is mutually exclusive with Ubuntu's requirement of a lightweight and intuitive tool for simple editing tasks. There is no practical way for the GIMP to serve both uses cases. Trying to adapt it to make it serve as a mainstream tool would only impede its ability to advance towards its goal of providing a top-notch tool for professionals.
  The GIMP developers who have expressed views on the subject seem to agree with Ubuntu's plan. Sven Neumann, a respected GIMP developer and the author of the GIMP Pocket Reference, voiced approval on the GIMP developer mailing list.
  "That is pretty much in-line with our product vision. GIMP is a high-end application for professionals. It is not the tool that you would advise every user to use for their casual photo editing," he [wrote]("https://lists.xcf.berkeley.edu/lists/gimp-developer/2009-November/023779.html").
  Even though I personally run the GIMP on a very regular basis to create images for my articles, I only use and understand a slim fraction of its capabilities. I suspect that this is common among regular users. There is a clear need for a lighter editing program that will start faster and eschew features that are unnecessary for a majority of users.[/quote][arstechnica]("http://arstechnica.com/open-source/news/2009/11/giving-up-the-gimp-is-a-sign-of-ubuntus-mainstream-maturity.ars?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=rss")

I occasionally need to take an image and resize it or circle an element in it. That's about as far as my editing needs go. I'm not interested in learning more. I don't care. I don't take pictures with a digital camera, I probably couldn't draw a competent stick figure, I've made my peace with that. I just want to go in real quick, crop the image, and be done.

Using GIMP for this is really frustrating for me. I had to look up in a tutorial how to just draw on the image. I couldn't figure it out, I didn't understand the UI, things that I thought might be what I wanted weren't and I couldn't even tell what they were supposed to do. I only used it because I didn't know what else to use. I just wanted something like MSPaint, where doing things like that are obvious and right up there. I can't imagine that I'm alone in this, and I'll be happy if a simple, basic drawing program like GNU Paint gets some love as a result of removing the GIMP, which I never use. The Ubuntu Software Center also aims to make programs more "discoverable" to basic users, and from the conversation we're having it's clear to me that among people who use graphic design software GIMP is well-known, so I don't think that the people who might want to use it will be unable to find out it exists.

Part of the reason that Ubuntu restricts itself to CD is because it's used on older hardware and for people with dial-up. In many developing countries, it's not just that they have dial-up connections. They have computers we would consider ancient, with really low specs... and may not have DVD drives. I don't live in the developing world and until a year ago I didn't have a DVD drive. Moving to DVD releases would effectively bar these people from using Ubuntu entirely, because they can't use them. So space on a single install CD is a completely legitimate concern for Ubuntu.

As far as not having a "showcase" application on the CD goes... do you really know how much space the GIMP takes up? We could install GNU Paint or some equivalent and add another application that shows off open source capabilities and still have space left over. The GIMP is huge.

I can see why people who have slow/limited connections and also use GIMP would be sad about its removal from the default installation. However, there will always be an application that is not in the default install, is extremely large, and is desirable to SOMEONE on a slow connection, even if GIMP stays on the CD. A CD or collection of CDs with extra software, and perhaps a DVD, might be helpful, though I think it's important to have all of this accessible without a DVD drive - but the problem won't go away for these people just because we keep the GIMP and sacrifice something else.
