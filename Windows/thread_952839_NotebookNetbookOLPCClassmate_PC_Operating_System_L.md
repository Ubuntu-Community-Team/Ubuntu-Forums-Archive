### Post by Vryko Lakas on 2008-10-19
I believe one of the main hold-ups for Linux power management is the lack of support from the hardware side of things. Many companies are reluctant to release open source drivers for fear of the competition gleaning useful information from them. This lack of information means that power management (esp. for things like graphics chips) frequently isn't built into the reverse-engineered drivers or the Linux kernel. Getting drivers that work with Linux can sometimes be a challenge just by itself. Getting drivers with advanced power settings and optimization? Less likely. 
My laptop (already a year old, positively ancient!) gets nearly 3 hours of battery life from XP. Under Ubuntu, it's almost cut in half. If you're approaching things purely from a power consumption standpoint on reasonably modern laptop or desktop hardware, Windows will usually win. They can get their hands on driver code and find ways to cut power usage without worrying about usability. 

***HOWEVER***, Microsoft Windows Vista is not suitable for the "netbooks," especially those with Intel's new Atom line of processors. The demands of the OS are simply overwhelming, and CPU usage would be immense. Not only would this drive up power consumption, it'll also leave less headroom for the applications you want to run. Then you have the RAM requirements. A full desktop version of Ubuntu can squeeze somewhat comfortably onto 512MB of RAM, whereas Vista demands a full 1GB and prefers 2 or more to run smoothly. It's on "netbooks" and Mobile Internet Devices that Linux will most likely stave off a Vista invasion in market space. The serious competitor for Linux OSes on these devices will be some flavor of Windows XP (which MS is still licensing for the new market) on the netbooks, or embedded Windows on MIDs. 
Further hampering Vista on netbooks are Microsoft's guidelines for what a "netbook" is. Their original definition for the purposes of OEMs using XP included a screen size no bigger than 10.1 inches, and 80GB of storage space. MS recently eased those restrictions, doubling the amount of allowed space and upping the screen size by about 4 inches (and allows for touchscreens), but they still limit the processor to 1GHz single-core designs* and RAM to 1GB or less.


*Exceptions made for Intel's Atom processors
