ABI means [*Application Binary Interface*]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_binary_interface").  An ABI provides the low-level binary interfaces that third-parties can use to design a driver without releasing source.  The Linux API is well enough documented that a third party can develop drivers, but need consistent maintenance to remain compatible with new kernel versions.  Third parties that want to write one driver for all of a particular operating system's versions without releasing source code expect the kernel developers to break the kernel and handcuff themselves to just one paradigm in order to fit the mold of the device driver, instead of providing source and letting Linux driver teams maintain it for them.

### Post by k2t0f12d on 2008-03-04
[LIST]**1. I don't get why having a consistent API/ABI would in any way impede the on going development of the kernel. Surely there are certain system calls that remain the same between versions, otherwise it would be a maintenance nightmare for both application developers/maintainers and kernel developers.**[/LIST]
It is not enough that the system calls need to be the same at source level (and even they aren't 100%, just ask the Linux from Source guys about *futimens* from glibc and building something like coreutils before version 6.10.0), the resulting binary needs to be the same.  You immediately bloat the kernel the instant the development roadmap diverges from the expectations of the third party providing binary drivers and waste resources trying to maintain compatibility with software you cannot examine.  This would be like walking backwards, blindfolded, in a blizzard, through a minefield.


[LIST]**2. If there are bugs in the binary driver then the same bugs will exist in the source (as it follows that the binary is built from the source - the compilation process shouldn't introduce those bugs), so I don't understand how this could be a reason to oppose a consistent API.**[/LIST]
The kernel does maintain a fairly consistent API, but this is not a guarantee.  It wouldn't be good to deliberately break API without a good reason, since that is the primary interface to the kernel for the programmer.  The nature of an ABI is totally different.  Even if the API is identical, the ABI is ridiculously easy to change between compilations *even using the same source*.


[LIST]**3. Still on the theme of bugs, there are bugs in all pieces of software. This includes Ubuntu, and community maintained hardware drivers. Why is it OK to use software that has bugs in it if it is from one source but not from another?**[/LIST]
Any experienced programmer can fix bugs if they have the source.  Binary drivers deprive the programmer of every possible way to correct any bugs.  Even if the kernel developers tried to maintain a stable ABI there would be no possible way *at all* for them to determine what to do if a driver fails to operate.


[LIST]**4. Surely a piece of hardware not working at all is a far bigger bug than if it worked for most users most of the time.**[/LIST]
Don't buy hardware from vendors that refuse to support your platform.


[LIST]**5. Mac OSX is based on *nix. It has a consistent and well document API/ABI. How do they manage it?**[/LIST]
Mac OS is based on BSD.  There is no reason to think that the BSD API/ABI bears any resemblance at all to Linux.  Linux binary is neither native to BSD nor BSD to Linux, and neither of them to Apple.  What is being said here is not that it is technically impossible to provide a stable ABI, simply that there is no compelling reason to do so.  If a vendor wants to have their hardware work with the kernel, they may either write and maintain the drivers themselves, al la {nVidia,ATI,Brother,etc} or they have to provide specifications so a driver team can do the work for them.  If some vendors are bound by legal contract not to provide source, stop buying from them.  They do not care enough about the user, nor are they smart enough to avoid painting themselves into a corner.  The onus is on them, not the kernel developers, to prepare their hardware for service.

### Post by Yeti can't ski on 2008-03-07
Given that the question is more about commercial and economical issues than technical ones, I am glad to be able to finally make a contribution. 

Software – together with telephones, telex, faxes, ATM banking service spots, services like Facebook, etc. – belongs to a special class of products which are subject to “economics of networks”. This simply means that, unlike potatoes and shoes, the value of each individual product present in the market increases as other similar or identical products are sold. A single telephone has absolutely no use. Two telephones, on the other hand, allow for a conversation. Every new purchased and operational telephone makes existing telephones more useful to everyone (more people to talk, more places than can be reached).

A similar thing happens with OS’s and their applications. One single and isolated computer with, let’s say, Windows and MS Word is simply a sophisticated typewriter. But if everyone you work with uses them, you can easily exchange and modify files. You can also start using any computer you find around you without learning effort because you (and everyone else) are familiar with the software.

The problem with economics of networks is that if someone is able to become the owner and sole controller of the network created by the product, then there is a glitch (or a bug, if you wish) in the free market competition system. It is just like as if a dominant cell phone operator would refuse to allow its users to talk to the clients of a new competitor. With a small base of users, the phones of the new competitor (even if the service is technically superior!!) would always be less useful than that of the monopolist. The new guy would never get to consolidate a place in the market.  

In the presence of network economics, the historical advantage (the fact of being the first big guy in the market) may become much more important than the quality, innovation or price factors. That’s why in basically all countries the law forces telephone carriers/operators to allow their clients to interconnect with the clients of their competitors. 

MS faces no such explicit legal restriction and, thus, is constantly hiding source codes and creating updates, new releases, versions, etc. with the sole purpose of preventing or making it difficult for its applications to “talk” to other products. That’s precisely why the EU recently imposed another big fine on MS and that’s why it is so important to expand the base of users of Linux in general and of Ubuntu in particular. That is also why using illegal copies of MS software actually benefits MS itself. The company doesn’t get (directly) your money but the monopolist network is nonetheless strengthened.

Long live to Ubuntu!
