### Post by toupeiro on 2008-09-18
I'm going to take a different spin.

Here's a system admins take:

I support both Windows Server, Windows XP and vista of both 32-bit and 64-bit flavors.  

I support Linux Workstations and servers of both 32-bit and 64-bit flavors

I've clustered them both for failover, and for parallelism.


Say vendor X comes into my company with his product, that plans to revolutionize the way I do my core business, This app is going to require extreme visibility and very fine tuned resource management.

I've seen windows failover clustering break, and break badly, for no apparent reason, and no good explanation from Microsoft when they analyzed it.  I also know maintaining windows will simply not allow for the same uptime percentages than Linux, and I know Linux can put Windows server and workstation to shame with memory managemnt.  I can have a linux server with 128GB of memory and not even touch swap until well 80% of that physical space, and its INCREDIBLY small, like less than 8MB.  Windows will have swapped out, even in x64, in the gigabytes before I ever address 8GB of physical RAM.  It has HORRIBLE memory management.

When I need something to work efficiently and be bulletproof stable, and I have the choice between the two, I will Always, Always, ALWAYS default to linux.  The fact that its becoming a wonderful desktop alternative to windows is not a surprise to me, and I expect it to grow very, very strong.

I had a handful of production servers multi-tasking an average of 30-40 simultaneous user sessions per server, remain up for over 420 days without a single stitch of issues. (RHEL4U6)  I have never, ever, seen this happen with windows.

Windows desktop OS may play games well, and I will admit that their server OS gets better and better all the time, but I trust linux for reliable computing much moreso than Windows..

There are plenty of things that can tie up 12 or 128GB of ram that don't imply trade secrets.  Here are a few:

Maya

CAD/3d applications

My point there is there are a LOT of people in the world, be it for their profession or their hobby, that don't have to ask themselves "What can I do with 128GB of RAM", and where the questions are put back on the limitations in their software and Operating systems.  Linux can meet most peoples every day computing needs, and is dynamic enough to scale out to High-performance computing needs.  Windows struggles with this, much moreso on the desktop than in the datacenter, but in both areas you will not find it to be as scalable as Linux. 

 Linux's place in the datacenter is sealed IMO, and I don't think anyone in the industry would challenge that.  Linux at the desktop is growing at an impressive rate.  The amount of applications becoming available for it every day are a testament to that.  There are still some gaps, that aren't so much limitations of Linux as an OS, but in FOSS alternatives to prominent industry applications that do not currently run on linux yet.

Even without FOSS, I would pay for proprietary software to run on linux before windows if I had a choice between them, for the very reasons I mentioned before.  Linux's resource management is superior to Windows.  Linux's filesystems are faster than Windows.  NFS blows the doors off of SMB from an I/O standpoint, so your network performance is greatly improved.

In all cases, I will run Microsoft Windows only when I have to, and it still happens frequently.  I believe that is changing, however, more and more all the time. My reasons for saying that are not because Microsoft is evil, or filthy rich, or even because of their business ethics which I openly despise.  My reason is that because I believe Microsoft has lost the focus of how to combine and deliver efficiency, security, and productivity.  They still think of their customers as these monolithic entities that they can throw things at and it will suit all their needs, and when they do this, they don't leave themselves enough room to remold it as necessary.  Companies today are less and less like this, no matter what size they are.  They put far more focus into complex licensing structures than they do productive modulation, and when they do look at modulation, they are doing so from a monetary standpoint, not a productive one.  To me, that translates to "They don't get it."

Linux is not as great for gaming as Windows, but I personally believe now that Windows is not as great for gaming as current-generation consoles.  If you are a gamer, stick with windows.  I was a gamer, and I still game, I just do so primarily on devices designed with gaming in mind, and use my linux computer for everything else.

For me to consider windows by default in a dual offering, they wouldn't just have to match linux's performance and stability, they would have to surpass it.  As the old saying goes, why would I buy the cow if I can get the milk for free?   In some domains, Microsoft is interested in this competition, like with CCS, but in other areas, they put forth poor effort to better their products.  What windows 7 holds remains to be seen.  Server 2008 is a very impressive product, and in some ways an almost ironic return to the CLI for Microsoft.  If any company has the money to reinvent themselves and succeed, its Microsoft.  I don't have my hopes up, but you can never say never.
