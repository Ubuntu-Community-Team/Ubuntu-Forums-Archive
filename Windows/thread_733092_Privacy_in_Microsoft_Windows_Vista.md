### Post by Dr. C on 2008-03-26
There is a serious privacy risk to unique serial numbers on record able, media, photographs, printed output, Web cookies etc in that they can be traced back to a particular person but only if that person also provides personal information that correlates to the serial number.

For consumer items the simplest way to protect one privacy is to pay cash for the item, and do not provide any personal information that can be correlated to the item. (including information provided at the point of sale, rebates etc.). One also has to avoid to produce output that can be traced back back (very tricky with a printer) but if one is very careful and uses different printers for different purposes it can be done. 

Here is a link on embedded serial numbers all sorts of devices and what can be done in some cases

[http://www.privacydigest.com/2007/07/22/harry+potter+and+digital+fingerprints]("http://www.privacydigest.com/2007/07/22/harry+potter+and+digital+fingerprints")

The privacy risk of Web cookies cookies can be effectively eliminated with Firefox by setting cookies to accept and **keep until I close Firefox**. This creates a new cookie ID each time Firefox is restarted. The data miners still get the data but shredded into little pieces effectively eliminating the privacy risk. 

As for propriety software the biggest risk to privacy is more often that not DRM and Vista was built from the ground up to accommodate DRM. There is a very interesting study done by  the  Canadian Internet Policy & Public Interest Clinic (CIPPIC) of the Faculty of Law at the University of Ottawa, in Canada on how DRM in common Windows applications violates Canada's privacy laws. 

[http://www.cippic.ca/uploads/CIPPIC_Report_DRM_and_Privacy.pdf]("http://www.cippic.ca/uploads/CIPPIC_Report_DRM_and_Privacy.pdf")

They placed the propriety software on a Windows XP box behind a Kubuntu transparent proxy and then carefully monitored the communications when the propriety software calls home. The propriety software is effectively in a GNU / Linux "jail" with all communications carefully being monitored by its Free Software "guards". The communication were then analyzed for compliance with Canadian privacy law.

Their conclusion is that more often the not the "prisoners" may very well have been caught by the "guards" breaking the law by breaching the privacy of consumers.

To deal with file/ram slack you have a few options:

Type 1: No rebuild of computer
A - Run a defrag, then run a tool that writes 0s and 1s to free space. This will take care of a great deal of potential slack space, but not all as there will always be some on your computer.

Type 2: Rebuild of computer
A -  Back up all your files, wipe your drive with DD and a Linux live distro (most people use a Gentoo CD because it doesn't have a GUI) or you can use DBAN, then reinstall your OS
B -  use Ghost or another imaging software (ensure it only records active bits - some can record every thing) repeat all the steps in A but instead of reinstalling just use the image to restore your computer
C  - If you have Vista Ultimate you can make a complete backup of your system to a second or external drive. You then follow the steps in A except when you boot with the Vista DVD you select repair and then the option to restore your computer. It should find the backup image and restore the file.

Unless you are really, really paranoid I would almost never use a type 2 scenario.
