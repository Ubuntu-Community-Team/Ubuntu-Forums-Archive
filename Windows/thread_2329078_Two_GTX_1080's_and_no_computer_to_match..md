Nvidia 1080?  Leaping lemmings! If that's the case, what kind of budget do you have for this project?  

Assuming those are Nvidia 1080 cards and someone hasn't scammed you, those are at least $500 to $600 each.  If you have that kind of money to spend on a gaming machine, you should probably get advice from someone other than me.  I'm just a humble geek who games on a normal budget.

If I might say so though, you have a serious case of overkill.  Linux gaming is limited to very few AAA titles presently, although that is changing.  Even if it had every game Windows does, even the games on Windows are not designed or optimized for those cards.  They are just too new.  I doubt you will get much more out of your investment than you would have with a less expensive card.  The average discrete video card has about 5-10 times the processing power versus what is actually used, which is one of the reasons for Vulkan and DirectX 12, although only a smattering of software uses either presently.  No matter what happens, you are serious ready for whatever comes at you. =)
 
I could see why you might want them for 3D rendering, although if you are a professional artist, or working for an advertising studio, I'd assume you would be using their software.  So I can't make a comment on that.

I need to know what type of RAM I should look at and how much of it I will be needing. All of these things depend on the motherboard you chose.  Intel and AMD processor boards vary in what kind of RAM is used.  I have no favorite, but as I said I game on a budget, so it is usually AMD.  Intel is faster and more expensive.  If you can afford dual 1080s, I doubt an Intel chip is much of a budget obstacle for you.  I'd look at the Intel processors, and find one you want. Then find a board that fits the processor and probably has Nvdia SLI  Once you find a board, you will know what RAM, cooling system and case you need.

Be sure that your PSU (power supply) has extra watts for those cards, they are going to draw a respectable bit of power under load, and that the case and board are large enough to accommodate those monsters.  

Other than that very general advice, I would consider using standard HDD or hybrid rather than using all SSDs.  There is nothing wrong with SSDs naturally, but if you are going to be doing a lot of high end rendering, swapping many GBs of data day in and day out, you may find that SSDs - although a lot faster - have a shorter lifespan.  It all depends on what you use them for.

Neither Skyrim nor Dark Souls are available on Linux.  Even on Windows, games like them probably will be capped at a top speed, because the games will probably be unable to fully use the card.  You may even have problems because the cards are so new.

### Post by bcschmerker on 2016-06-27
**That's a lot of video capacity.**  Dual GTX 1080's in SLI really demand an Intel® 5th-Generation Core Processor&#8482; i7-5900- or 6th-Gen Core i7-6900-Series MPU (FCLGA 2025) and an X99 'board due to the need for no fewer than 32 PCIe lanes for full speed from both video cards; or, alternately, an Intel® Xeon Processor® E5-2600v3 Series MPU (FCLGA 2025) and either an X99 or C630 workstation 'board for systems needing twenty-plus threads at moderate to slightly high speeds.  The E5's will force DDR4-2133 registered ECC main memory, while the i7-5900/6900's use DDR4-3200 or faster unbuffered.
