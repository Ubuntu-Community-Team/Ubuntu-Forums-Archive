While it is true that the human eye cannot detect an FPS of over 60, there can be benefit of having it higher(although the advantage decreases rapidly over about 130Hz.).

this is due to both the frame rate of the monitor and the rate the graphics card can calculate the next frame of a game. if these 2 values are close it can lead to jitter as the same picture would be displayed in two refreshes of the monitor as the next one isn't ready yet, effectively halfing the frame rate.

it is very similar to the effect called 'aliasing' seen in computer graphics when it tries to display sub pixel patterns., infact it even has the same name and occurs in sound as well.

### Post by TheKid965 on 2008-09-05
I must post in this thread about my own experiences with Vista, since I've been running it now for a week.  (I was gifted an OEM copy of Home Premium from Micro Center and curiosity got the better of me.  SP1 came standard with this install.)

For reference's sake, here's my system:  Self-built, AMD64 @ 2.08 GHz (the Vista I have, however, is the 32-bit version - my Ubuntu is the same, for maximum compatibility), 2GB RAM (DDR), 250GB SATA HD as a primary drive, 400GB SATA as a secondary, two LightScribe DVD burners (one LG that's SATA, the other an IDE Samsung).

On this hardware, Vista was pretty sluggish (though not actually *painfully* slow) out of the gate with the default Aero interface, but once I turned off most of the pocketfluff and eye candy it was much kinder to my hardware.  In fact, after I got done tweaking things it's probably faster than XP (SP2) was on this same box.  Compatibility with older games (which I primarily play) was an issue until some Googling turned up solutions, including how to set programs to run with Administrator privileges.  Stability-wise, after almost a week I have yet to encounter any crashes severe enough to require a reboot.  And I'll give credit where it's due; even though it's an annoyance to a self-professed "power user" like myself, the hyper-paranoid Windows Defender is probably a good idea for the Joe and Jane Endusers that MS primarily targets... better this than running with all ports open like previous Windows versions have done.

Although I still have some ethical concerns about Vista due to DRM issues, WGA, and everything else that tends to treat the end user as a guilty criminal in advance of any crime being committed, I'm forced to concede that Vista, taken strictly as an OS, probably *isn't* as bad as all that.  If it wasn't for the fact it came from a company that represents the total antithesis of the Ubuntu/DSFG/FSF philosophy I'd have no hesitation in declaring my appreciation for it.  As it is, however, the best I feel comfortable in admitting is "It's not nearly as bad as I thought it was going to be."

Moral qualms aside, I think I'll keep Vista as my primary OS for a while, at least until the Ibex hits RC or at least beta status.  That would at least give me about eight weeks of solid experience with Vista, enough to speak with something approximating authority on both its high *and* its low points when discussing it.
