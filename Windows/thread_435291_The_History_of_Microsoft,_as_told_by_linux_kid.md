### Post by linux_kid on 2007-05-06
Thirty two short years ago, a lonesome man named William and his friend Paul started their own company.  In that time, most programs were *open source*.  Applications were shared openly among users.  This lonesome William **thought** he could line his pockets a little by selling software.  He also figured this software he was about to sell _mustn't_ be open source.  

     William then** became the father** of propitiatory software.  All of the other programmers at the time saw William as a **criminal**, selling software.  So then William made some patents and copyrights, sold some stocks, and held some market share.  He used his power to make copyright bombs, **more powerful than the atomic**.  The other programmers started to *disappear* into the Microsoft black hole.  Linus came and made a great attempt to stop William, but was not able to save the masses.  

     William's company became so large, he was the** richest man** on the face of our planet.  Then he *named one of his programs after himself*, ME.  It made people wake up from the Windows Dream, but most asked for five more minutes.  The few that listened to the wake up call either bought Steve's software, or they attempted to build up Linus's stuff. 

     William made some other pieces, and then revived ME, calling it Vista.  Even more people then turned to Steve and Linus, and **William got a temper tantrum**.  *The rest is yet to be told, but for us to decide*.

### Post by bsell on 2007-05-06
> **linux_kid said:**
> Thirty two short years ago, a lonesome man named William and his friend Paul started their own company.  In that time, most programs were *open source*.  Applications were shared openly among users.  No, most programs were proprietary. UNIX has always been proprietary and wasn't available for the PC. Part of the belief that programs were open source in the Golden Era was the fact that AT&T couldn't sell software due to antitrust regulations so they made it available to universities and later to independent developers who turned UNIX into a *commercial *product. 

 > **linux_kid said:**
> This lonesome William **thought** he could line his pockets a little by selling software.  He also figured this software he was about to sell _mustn't_ be open source.  Microsoft bought an excellent product in 86-DOS, which they acquired from Seattle Computer Products and renamed MS-DOS and licensed it to IBM for PC's. Digital Research offered the CP/M-86 and Softech offered their P-System. All of these were proprietary O/S's designed for PC's.  IBM helped make MS-DOS popular on PC's when it chose it over the others. 

 > **linux_kid said:**
> William then** became the father** of propitiatory software.  All of the other programmers at the time saw William as a **criminal**, selling software.  ... The other programmers started to *disappear* into the Microsoft black hole.  Linus came and made a great attempt to stop William, but was not able to save the masses.   Torvalds wrote a kernel for an x86 PC as a hobby and gave it away. 

Much of the work being done in open source software is being done by paid developers...just like at Microsoft. Programmers have to eat too.
