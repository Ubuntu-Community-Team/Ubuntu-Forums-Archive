Basically MS is in control over your computer and not yourself.

You can study and improve the code, you get the best support in the world from the community, it is safer, runs on slower machines and it is free of charge. For every Windows programm there is at least one replacement for GNU/Linux. And most of them are equally good or sometimes even better than that Windows counterparts. YOU decide what your desktop looks like, YOU decide what you wanna do with your computer and YOU are in control of everything that runs on your OS. Windows does not even allow another OS to be installed next to it on the same computer.

This is just a short intro. You can find a much better list of reasons contra Vista on [www.badvista.org](www.badvista.org). Also check out [www.fsf.org](www.fsf.org) for more information about free software.

### Post by cunawarit on 2007-07-12
> **PrivateVoid said:**
> Is the design of the F-22 free?

No, and it isn't for sale either. It is a closely guarded secret, much like a lot of software that isn't for sale. That's OK, if you were an investment bank you wouldn't want your competitors to know how your software predicts the market. 

> Is the F-22 itself free?

No, but you can't copy an F-22 either, so it isn't really much of an issue. Also, the F-22 is not like most software because it is a weapon of war, and thusly you expect some restrictions to exist and not anyone can get hold of one. Some software can be a weapon of war too and you'd expect similar restrictions to exist. 

> If you were a software programmer and worked for two years to produce a product that everyone wanted for free... how would you pay your bills, buy food, etc?

Easily, I'd publish it as a web service, never release the source code, and charge a subscription for it. ;) 

More seriously, I am a software developer, and the software I produce is closely guarded by the company I work for. We don't sell it, only we use it and don't let any of our competitors know what it does. 

Nearly all large scale software development is driven by profit, even free software is driven by profit. There are other business models than just selling licenses for closed source software.

I by no means meant that a switch to 100% free software should happen right away. FWIW, I think the FSF is far too militant about it and the way the software industry works right now quite often non-free software works best, but as I said the world is far from ideal... Perhaps in a couple of generations.
