### Post by TheKid965 on 2007-01-03
*Öö, activation means typing in the licence key?*

Activation, in this sense, refers to the following process.

1.  Begin the WinXP installation process.
2.  When prompted, enter the 25-digit CD key.
3.  Wait for the install to finish.
4.  After the installation, you will be prompted to "activate" XP, either over the Internet or the phone.
5a.  If you choose "Activate over the Internet," XP will "phone home" to see if the CD key you used has already been used.  If it can't find the key in its database, it will allow unrestricted use of XP.  If it can, you will be flagged and asked to purchase a legal XP license.
5b.  If you choose "Activate over the phone," you will be given a (thankfully toll-free) number to call and a 60-digit number to read to the automated system (or a human operator if you're lucky).  The system/operator will read back another 60-digit verification number for you to enter, which will activate Windows.
6.  You have until 30 days after the XP install to activate Windows.  If you do not, your computer will be "deactivated" and you will be unable to perform all but the most trivial of tasks with it until you activate it.

They say this is done as a method of curtailing software piracy and enforcing the (IMO, arbitrary) "One computer, one license, one copy" restriction in their EULA.  *Legally*, nothing has changed; MS has never allowed you to install one copy of the OS on multiple PCs, even though most of us always did that anyway.  Product Activation is their first major attempt to enforce that rule on Joe Enduser - and, as always with such measures, the only ones who are *really* hurt by it are the ones who *do* use XP legally.  Pirates can circumvent *anything* given the chance, but the typical home user is more or less forced to appease the Microsoft Gods before they're allowed to use the software they legally acquired and paid for.

### Post by TheKid965 on 2007-01-03
Most of the pirated XP copies that are out there in circulation do skip the activation steps, as they are rips of the Corporate Edition (*not* the same as Professional), which bypasses it for the sake of convenience in corporate environments where activating each individual PC on a network would be logistically nightmarish.  The only catch is that the Corporate Edition is only (legally) available to those who pay massive amounts of cash for, say, a volume license for 500 potential users.

The shrinkwrapped copies of XP you see in stores (Home Edition, Professional Edition, Media Center Edition) do force you to go through the activation process, one way or another.  Or, at least, they're *supposed* to.
