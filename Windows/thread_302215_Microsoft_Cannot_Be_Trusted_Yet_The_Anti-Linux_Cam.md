### Post by GaryDaGeek on 2007-11-02
[B]The Real State Of Affairs
[/B] 
Things are not really as bad as you think. They are far worse.

First I would like to say that I believe the Linux concept of free and open source code is the absolute best possible model bar none. Openness is best in business and government.

I would prefer that there would be no such thing as proprietary code. This is particularly true in high security environments, mission critical environments and in situations where a persons lively hood depends on its functionality for their economic well being. It should be illegal to hide anything a  computer does in operating systems source code.

That being said, hardware manufacturers do have legitimate reasons for keeping their source code private. They have trade secrets that they are do not wish to have divulged. If Nvidia doesn't want ATI to know how they implement certain protocols or vice versa that is fine with me. They pay for their development and they should hardly be expected to allow their competition to simply steal it.

However that does not mean there should not be oversight to ensure that nothing surreptitious is being done in the background which is not related to the normal function or operation of the device and without the users knowledge or consent.

The same goes for secretive back room inter-corporate agreements and a multitude of corporate kick back deals that are designed to restrict competition and limit freedom of choice.

One such example of that is a stipulation in private contracts that penalizes computer makers if they release a dual boot computer which would allow a user to choose an alternate software operating system at start up. If they do so they lose their “volume discount” on the licensed software. The loss of the “discount” would make the system so costly as to make the “feature” a competitive disadvantage. against their competition. Additionally the terms of the contract do not allow the manufacture to disclose the contents thus preventing them from seeking any form of  legal redress once they drink the Kool Aid. 

This has the effect of charging the company, and its potential customers, for using free software. It is reminiscent of the type of anti-competitive behavior that Microsoft has been found guilty of under the United States Anti-trust laws in the past. Anyone who is reasonably aware of this type of corporate behavior should also be aware of the failure of The United States Department Of Justice to provide effective remedies or enforcement of the laws which are designed to protect our rights as consumers.

Unfortunately these things have been tolerated by our society, and we have done so mainly because we believe that we have choices. If you believe the software that came on your machine is buggy and bloated mess, you simply don't like it and you don't want to use it, you don't have too. There are generally other and better ways to get things done on the same equipment that allow a greater degree of freedom at less cost. Anyone who uses any type of FOSS will know what I am talking about. 

As long as there are  reasonable alternatives I don't have a particular problem with allowing others to do something that I do not personally have to participate in. It's a little bit like tolerating personal pornography or recreational drug use. If your actions do not harm me, or others, and do not infringe on my personal freedoms and liberties I am willing to tolerate your right to be stupid.

However, when your actions do harm me, when they are intended to eliminate and restrict my choices and they do threaten my freedom of choice it is time for you to be dismembered and fed to the dogs. I believe Microsoft is making a concerted effort to do exactly that.     

** The Vista Threat to FOSS**

Microsoft's DRM, as implemented in Vista, further limits the divulgence of standards and specifications for devices designed to work on that system. In order to work on Vista the device drivers must be Vista certified. In order to become Vista certified they must be approved by at least three  members of a Hollywood consortium as well as Microsoft. This requires hardware vendors to submit their drivers and hardware specifications to a closed review board controlled by Microsoft and their partners.

Manufacturers are further required to agree not to publish or release information that would allow other programs (Linux is a program) to access or control the devices as the information, once disclosed, could then be used to circumvent DRM. Microsoft further reserves the right to revoke driver certification rendering any device inoperable on the Vista system if it fails the terms of compliance (Say it works on Linux). These revocation mechanisms are implemented through Windows Update which is now a mandatory feature implemented by their operating system. With their operating systems on over 95% of the computers sold through retail chains that is a significant piece of business for a hardware manufacturer to ignore.

I find it particularly noteworthy that of all the Microsoft Vista variants there is not a single one that is DRM free. There is no offering that does not invoke these draconian hardware requirements even if I do not want it. If I do not want to play High Definition or Blue Ray disks, or listen to DRM protected music I can not even get a Version of Vista that does not require it. I have to pay for it whether I want it or not. It is bundled and embedded in the OS. DRM is mandatory in Vista. There is absolutely no  way around it. There is no choice.

This is a crippling threat to any device manufacturer who might wish to have their devices work across multiple platforms. It effectively makes their hardware slave to a single master. I call these newly implemented requirements “Strangle Hold Information Technology”, or just plain S.H.I.T. for short.

Realistically hardware manufacturers should have the right to choose to build computers that will only work with Vista. The key word here being “choose”. Apple computers are designed to run Apple software. They choose to control the hardware and the software and it is optimized to work on their stuff. They have made a real choice. When you buy a Mac you know what you are getting (A damn good computer). It is not designed to not run other operating systems, in fact it will run Linux or Windows as well. 

With Vista choice is eliminated. The only choice for the hardware manufacturer  is surrender “We are the Borg. Your unique technology will be added to our own. You will become part of the collective. Comply or Die.”. 
 
This is yet another of their surreptitious attempts to extend their empire. What they are doing serves the interest of no one except the Microsoft Monopoly and a handful of media Mongols. At the same time  on a standard off the shelf computer it poses yet another threat to everyone and anyone who may wish to use any operating system that is not approved for use on the device by the Monopolist.

In this case we are talking about a monopolist, a convicted repeat offender, whose past practices have proven repeatedly that it can not be trusted. Microsoft is being allowed to exercise this kind of power and control and there is barely a whimper of protest. 

As you are reading this you may be saying “that's not true, I run Vista and Linux on the same machine.”. That may be so, but these features do not become fully implemented until we move to 64 bit systems and then only as Microsoft choses to invoke them. Certainly they wont strictly enforce these policies until they have thoroughly infected enough systems to make it damn near politically impossible for anyone to do anything about it. 

Microsoft claims these controls are necessary in order to enforce DRM and comply with the DMCA. While the DMCA is in itself a draconian act, it does not in any way require any such implementation and it certainly does not designate Microsoft as the worlds compliance officer. We should also be aware that while Microsoft uses the DMCA as an excuse for its actions they were lobbying heavily for it's  enactment into law. Now they say “it ties our hands”. What a bunch of two faced hypocrites.

There is no requirement for any operating system to work with DRM protected media at all. I for one would be more than happy to fore go High Definition Television on my computer if that is the cost of having my computer act like a computer instead of a video player.

If I had wanted to buy a Video Player I would have bought a Video Player. If someone else wants to use their computer that way I do not care, but I do not want my hardware and operating system to be crippled to allow them to do something I don't give a rats a** about. 

We should not be so naive as to believe Microsoft is just playing the “good cop”. It appears to have far more sinister motives, not the least of which is preventing other operating systems from operating on the same hardware Vista uses, effectively making Vista a poison pill. 

Fortunately it is lousy enough system that it is not being widely sought after. Unfortunately it is being pushed out by the millions on new machines with no significant competitive alternatives. [B] The FOSS disadvantage
[/B] 
Drivers have always been the Achilles Heel for Linux. While the situation is much better today than it has been in the past a lot of the development has had to be done without vendor support or cooperation.  Much of it has had to be done the hard way by raw experimentation and by probing the device inputs and outputs and interpreting the data.

This approach is being attacked by Microsoft's new hardware requirements in a couple of ways. One is by severely restricting accessible access points. The other is by introducing encryption keys into the hardware devices themselves which require authorization from the operating system itself in order to function. Of course these are private keys and only Microsoft will have them. 

Proprietary drivers, when they are provided, are often provided for only a very few Linux distributions  and Kernel versions.  They are often shoddy and do not provide the full functionality of the device.  In addition users are made dependent on the vendors to reveal the system library requirements and specific system requirements. 

This often results in system conflicts with no clear cut resolution involving dependency conflicts which may arise between different applications due to system calls which are unknown. This is already a problem and it will in all likelihood only grow worse.

** Where the Power Lies**

As it stands today the only company that really has complete access is Microsoft. They have access to all of the Linux kernel code released under the GPL,  access to all of the code required by all of the hardware which interacts with their operating systems and access to all of the open source drivers that presently work with the Linux system. 

Knowledge is Power. Microsoft is in a great position to screw things up for FOSS through wielding their economic and political influence and control. They are now extending their reach deeply into the heart of all of the computing hardware we will be able to buy and use in the future. That is exactly what they are doing. Their driver signing requirements have not been all for naught.

Their deals with Novell and other Linux distributors are not for nothing.  All of the GPL code can be free and open but if Microsoft can be the “safe house” for admission to proprietary Linux device   drivers they will rapidly gain control over which Linux distributions (if any) will work on all future hardware that must be compatible with Vista. This is yet another of their “extend”, “embrace” and “extinguish” tactics.

There may well be “work-a rounds” for many of the problems that are encountered (for awhile) but they will not address the real issue. The distros outside of the approved Microsoft fold will eventually wind up with an uncoordinated disjointed mess.

** A Possible Solution**

The need for some form of transparency and openness is very real. The biggest roadblock to that is the lack of an international agency which can act as an impartial hardware and software standards consortium. In an ideal world this situation would not exist but in the real world it does.

Hardware manufacturers view volunteer contributors as “smoke in the mist” and they will not place their trust in them. We need full time paid regular employees. People who have names, offices and real work stations. People who are accountable for their actions.  Such an entity will cost money and someone will have to pay for it.

A responsible neutral authority that can really be trusted by the industry is an absolute necessity if the industry is to remain competitive and achieve its full potential. Such an agency should be granted full access to all  proprietary code by the force of law, under the stipulation that it will itself maintain proprietary non-disclosure confidentiality except in cases where the code violates international law. 

This would be an excellent way to intercept and prevent embedded spy ware, mail ware and viruses. Simply offering the service free to program developers yet requiring standards compliance and certification would solve a lot of problems. That would not mean unauthorized code couldn't run, but it would mean that it would not be certified and should not be trusted. The user could be made aware of the risks and responsibilities running uncertified code entails regardless of the OS being used.

Such an agency would be responsible for freely disseminating the dependency requirements, program calls and specifications for proprietary code to developers in order to allow hardware devices to operate and inter operate across various OS platforms without conflict. It could have divisions within it dedicated to ensuring that each operating system is given the same access to all of the information. This would ensure  a healthy competitive environment among hardware and software developers.  I believe hardware vendors would eagerly support such an agency as they have no interest in being chained to a technology stifling and crippling monopoly.

I would like to know that my WIFI device is not reporting to the NSA, the FBI the CIA the EIEIO or anyone else without my permission. I want to know that my hardware is certified as safe to use on my system, whatever it may be, and I want the hardware to be cross platform compatible. Today I don't know that this is the case when proprietary drivers or software are being used. I want my devices to be certified by an entity I can trust, one that is looking out for my interests rather than the interests of a convicted monopolist and a government that has its nose poked far too deeply up my butt.
