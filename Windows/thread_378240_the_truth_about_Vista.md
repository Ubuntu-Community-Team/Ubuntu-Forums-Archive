### Post by billdotson on 2007-03-07
OK.  Enough is enough.  There are so many rumors floating around about how terrible Vista is and we should have a list of Vista does do wrong and what it doesn't.

Vista's minimum recommended requirements are as follows: for Home Basic a 1GHz x86 or x64 processor, 512MB RAM, 20GB HDD space w/ 15GB available (that seems a bit bloated but come on HDD space is cheap these days) DVD-ROM drive, compatible DX9 hardware w/ 32MB video RAM.  

Home Premium, Business and Ultimate editions have the same hardware specifications as Home Basic with the following being different: instead of 512MB RAM, it is recommended that you use 1GB.  Graphics hardware must support DX9 graphics w/ 128MB video memory, a WDDM driver (not really sure what that is), 32 bits per pixel (again not entirely sure the meaning), and pixel shader 2.0

For 64 bit systems it is recommended t have 2GB RAM, and you can only use the BitLocker drive encryption if you have a TPM 1.2 chip on your motherboard.

these requirements are directly from Microsoft's website so gather what you will from that.

*[btw what is so bad about trusted platform computing and the TPM chips.. is it that they constrict the rights of the consumer so that they can only do certain things with their hardware.. or what?  I know that these sort of things are generally looked down upon but I do not know specifically why.]*

You CAN run Windows Vista in a virtual machine.. depending on the version.  Home Basic does NOT allow you to do this, but Home Premium and Ultimate do.  I am not sure of Business although I would assume that you can. 

the prices are as follows:
Ultimate: full = $379 OEM = $199 Upgrade = $249
Home Premium: full = $228 OEM = $119 Upgrade = $154
Business: full = $285 OEM = $150 Upgrade = $192
Home Basic: full = $192 OEM = $95 Upgrade = $105

[these are from newegg.com and other places could vary up or down.. although with newegg you are probably getting the best deal :) ]

There have been many questions about the EULA for Vista and there have been several concerns with things such as DRM and less-than-specific clauses.  As I do not know all of the details of those issues I will let someone else lay them out rather than possibly get some of them wrong.

Features in Vista that are new:
-Supposed improved security (I have not yet seen any reports of any tests that show either way)
the Aero interface is a bit more pleasing (although more hardware intensive) than XP's desktop interface Luna
-Searching for folders is now easier and is similar to how Mac OS X's spotlight works
-ReadyBoost which is the ability to draw RAM from a USB flash drive

- if you are into PC games then the new Direct3D10 API will make games perform better, look better, etc. and games are supposed to be easier to manage
- better built in media features like a media center and upgraded Windows movie maker software

I do not personally know how well these new features work or how useful they are but I just created a simple list of some of the ones I could think of.  I might have left some out and I am sure I have left out some more technical ones such as stuff like PNRP.

### Post by gaten on 2007-03-07
I'm currently running Vista Ultimate in a VirtualBox session, and am quite impressed with the speed (obviously, being in a virtual session curtails any real "assessments", but it runs faster than my OS X session, so I'd say I'm impressed). I'm currently giving 512MB  to the session, and I'm not using the Aero interface or anything, but for the most part it seems that they just made everything a little more visually appealing.

The protection mechanisms are in full swing, and that darn pop up window always comes up. I see this in two lights, one good and one bad.

Good- It shows marked improvement with security. Windows is taking an active approach to security, and I'm not going to bash them for it.

Bad- The frequency. This of course depends on what you are doing, but that little window gets annoying. This will eventually bite windows in the butt, because as it stands now very few "average windows users" actually READ any sort of pop ups. They stare at it blankly for around 5 seconds, and then they click on whatever button their mouse is closest to. This "habitual" clicking is going to be the cause of security circumvention, mark my words.

One marked improvement I'd like to point out is Internet Explorer Protected Mode. This is kinda-sorta like a *nix jail, but I honestly don't know enough about either to give a REAL comparison. But in the end, it accomplishes the same thing. IE cannot access the core components of your OS, like in previous versions. I think this is a GREAT step forward by M$, even if they get it wrong the first couple of times (its bound to happen). 

Overall, I think its  an OK OS, nothing I'm particularly impressed with though. The DRM issues are a major concern for me, not b/c I play many movies or listen to anything but internet radio, but because of the potential doors it opens up. Privacy no more is my worry.

I'm probably going to get flamed for this post, as I don't state my overwhelming hate of M$, but some people need to love Linux for Linux, and not because it's NOT M$.

Oh, and in case you are wondering, nowadays I only use Windows when I'm getting paid too, but I still think its a good OS. I just hate the company that runs it (there, I stated my overwhelming hate for M$, happy now?)

### Post by iTrendsNET on 2007-03-08
I've been heavy into Linux for 5 years now.  Why?  Mainly because I often need to be at my desk 12+ hours a day and need a setup that works well and efficiently.  I build and repair computers, plus work on networks as a side job.  All of this is with Windows and I am considered by local companies to be very good at this work.  

I bought a new Intel Duo Core 2 laptop in October with 2GB memory and a 160 G SATA drive.  Unfortunately, I moved into a new apartment before discovering cable and DSL Internet were not available.  I quickly grabbed a Spring broadband wireless card and reformatted the laptop hard drive back to 2005 Media Edition while I waited for the free Vista upgrade.  I did this to test and make sure the wireless broadband would work correctly.  

I decided to bite the bullet and purchase Vista Business to run on my workstation, which is an AMD X2 64 4200+ with a 256 PCI express nVidia graphics card, 2GB memory and several SATA drives.  I REALLY wanted to love Vista.  I was willing to give up Linux "if" Windows could stop me from spending so much time having fun with Linux (tweaking, trying new distros, etc.).  I hoped in the end I would gain with Windows, especially with the new features offered in Vista.    

I ended up putting Vista on both above computers.  I consider them to be higher end and certainly not budget systems.  I did fresh installs and used all of my Windows experience to fine tune my systems.  

Vista = Good
1. The installs were painless.  The workstation install told me the sound driver was outdated and asked me to update.  No problem.  The laptop did the same.  While at the laptop manufacturer's website, I noticed they had released many Vista specific drivers so I installed them as well.  
2. Bottom line is I was happy that I could finally do a fresh Windows install and not have to search for driver downloads or CDs before I could have the network card working, proper graphics resolution, etc., etc.  
3. I liked the theory behind the new built in desktop search.
4. Being a Linux guy, I appreciated the user controls and did not mind getting asked to provide permission before certain things could happen.  
5. I liked the fresh new look.  The other tweaks like flipping Windows did not impress me.    
6. Scanning was very impressive.  

Vista - Bad
1. At this moment, I feel their desktop search is a joke.  There was an iFilter (or whatever) for my OpenOffice files.  Ditto for my PDF files.  No way to search my 5 years of Thunderbird (mbox) mail files and I do not care for the Thunderbird built in search functions.   

 In my short time with Windows Media Center Edition on the laptop, I fell in love with Copernic Desktop Search.  Fortunately, CDS did install and run efficiently as always.  I would do comparison searches with Windows Live Search and Copernic.  Results with Windows search were maybe 10 to 15% of the volume of correct hits I got with Copernic.  

2. Since it was still Windows, I found myself buying Diskkeeper to auto defrag and purchasing several Antivirus/Security suites to try out, including McAfee Total Protection, Norton, Bitdefender and Trend Micro.  I settled on a 90 day free trial of Windows One Care as I felt the systems were most responsive and that I would have less problems with networking.  So, now I am spending more money in a few months than I have spent on Linux in years, even considering that I have purchased software and/or donated money to Ubuntu, Red Hat, SUSE, Ximian and Lindows.  

3. So fresh install, good systems, set up correctly and no crap to slow things down.  Both of my computers offered a user experience as if I was running an old style Celeron processor with 256 MB of memory and a slow hard drive.  Trust me!  I know how to tune.  My installs were not messed up!  This irritated the heck out of me.  

People often talk about how Windows boots faster than Linux.  I did not run any tests, but feel Vista probably boots quicker to a visible desktop.  Then, you sit and wait for what seems like 5 minutes while Vista "continues to load in the background."  Don't let it sit and quickly open MS Explorer or an MS Office program and you have a good chance the program will stall and if you click again, crash if you keep being persistent.  This "crash" does not bring the system down, but you do get the box asking if you want to report an unresponsive program.  

Note that some of my tests were off line and without Copernic or  security software running, so it is not like I had heavy hitting programs killing resources.  The bottom line is you get a USABLE desktop quicker with Linux than with Vista.    

4. Networking was pure hell.  Be it file sharing or printer sharing.  At first, I thought maybe it was just that the Vista computer did not like taking to the Media Center computer.  Then, with Vista on both computers, it was much more of a hassle.  Using Internet Connection Sharing on the laptop to the workstation through MCE was no problem.  Vista to Vista, forget it.  

Maybe there are easy fixes to this.  I played around for a few days.  I decided if I was going to go through that much trouble, that I might as well be doing it in Linux.  

5. Yes, Internet Explorer is tightened up a bit.  But, you will soon find there are many things you can do longer do through your usual surfing habits and that the work around is to install Firefox or Opera.  Even Windows Media was a pain as Explorer most of the time could not auto play videos as this is "restricted."  It certainly can be done, but then you are disabling stuff.

CONCLUSION
I finally got fed up with the slow performance with Vista.  I took a chance and installed SUSE SLED 10 on my laptop to see if I could get the Sprint Pantech broadband card to work.  I prepared the 2 config files posted everywhere on the Internet and it worked the first try!  

I decided GNOME 2.12 was a bit behind what I wanted and decided I wanted to try Feisty.  I had to add pci=nomsi to get the hard drive detected with the Live CD on the laptop.  Installed and the Sprint card again set up with zero problems.  So, I backed up the rest of my data and installed Feisty on the workstation.  

Both computers fly again and I am so happy that I am free again.  Keep in mind that in addition to the other software purchases, I now have $450 worth of Vista DVDs that will collect dust on a shelf.  Even with my past experience, I do not feel I fall into the Linux fan boy category.  I paid the money and was willing to give up my fun and go with Vista.  Like I said above, that is NOT going to happen when Vista makes my performance machines run like $299 blue light specials.      

At this time, I cannot recommend Vista to anyone.  I will, however, start marketing Linux solutions to corporate clients.  

One last comment.  I went to the Crossover Office website to see if how much it would cost to get the latest version so I could run QuickBooks Pro 2002 with Feisty.  I entered my user name and discovered that although my support had expired, 6.0 was released and I was entitled to a free download.  QuickBooks works MUCH BETTER with Crossover Linux 6.0.  What a deal!  Also, if you have tried Vista, you know that QuickBooks versions prior to 2007 do NOT work correctly with Vista.  Not a big deal with M$ offers their free accounting software download.  But, if you like QB, that is another deal breaker unless you want to add another $200 to your future software and hardware purchases.
