### Post by cunawarit on 2007-08-31
Initially I was bullish about Vista, I was expecting great things. As I type this on my Vista work machine I am not quite a bear, but I am feeling decidedly less bullish about it. 

In terms of software compatibility I haven't come across anything show stopping. However, in terms of stability Vista is not quite there to XP SP2 levels. I've had the machine blue screen once, not your average blue screen with text. It just froze and the screen turned blue and I had to unplug it to turn it off. I've also had it restart itself at night twice, as far as I can tell dwm.exe crashed, and the OS reacted by initiating a shutting down.

This instability whilst not critical is bothering me quite a bit, as it is now I&#8217;ll be recommending that new developers in our company use Vista for homogeneity as most machines in this office are now on Vista. But at the same time, if someone is running XP successfully I wouldn&#8217;t recommend the upgrade to Vista unless they have a particular reason for it. 

As far as Aero goes, my ATI Radeon Xpress 1100 is OK to handle Aero for most people. But this machine is often running 3 instances of Visual Studio, 3 ASP.NET Development Servers, SQL Server with some pretty large databases, and a Debian or Ubuntu virtual machine. It may be merely psychological, but I do think the machine got quicker when I turned off Aero, particularly ASP.NET Development Server. However overall Vista&#8217;s speed seems great on a brand new machine, I haven&#8217;t carried out any formal tests, but I do think software loads faster on Vista than XP. 

As for the aesthetics of Aero, I can&#8217;t complain, I think it is great! Very Microsofty, but great nonetheless. The little task bar previews are very nice, the window transparencies look pretty neat, and as useless as flip 3D thing is I do tend to use it simply because it looks so cool. 

Your experiences? What are the biggest Vista hiccups you have experienced? Trying to stay away from baseless it blue screens every 5 seconds, and got infected by 2037 viruses before I plugged it in comments...

### Post by marx2k on 2007-09-05
They CAN do better, but have no real incentive to do so. Users of Windows are not demanding lighter hardware requirements.   And hardware manufacturers are rushing out faster and faster processors, larger hard drives, larger memory but the issue is that people buying those new specs are doing the exact same stuff they've been doing with last generation's hardware.  

So really, unless the consumer is a hardcore gamer (very low percentage) or a graphics/music artist (very low percentage), that hardware is being used to check EMail and chat on AIM for the most part.

Same goes with larger hard drives.  Okay, so you got Best Buy bringing out 500, 750GB, 1TB hard drives for relatively low $$, but aside from people that do a lot of P2P, who is filling those hard drives? Grandma doesn't get that much saved EMail and students (again, unless they're doing tons of P2P), will never need that much space.

So, the point is that I agree.  Windows is larger/heavier than it really needs to be, but without consumer/industry pressure, there's no reason for the programmers to be conservative.

Consider console programming.  You have to design software within certain constraints (the XBOX has 64MB of memory and runs @ 700Mhz).  At that point, programmers have to know their sh*t and be conservative.  But when MS programmers develop for the PC, it's like they're given carte blanch and that's why you're seeing requirements that seem disproportionate with the task at hand (just getting to a Vista desktop)

But that's also why I find Linux has its' advantages. The kernel is tiny, and the apps running on top of it, the programmers seem to keep  constraints and don't bloat the software.
