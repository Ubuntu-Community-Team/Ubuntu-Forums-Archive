### Post by 3rdalbum on 2006-12-16
Okay, so a proprietry nvidia driver isn't currently letting the feds into your machine or hide your music from you.

But there is a lot of proprietry software that has done that.

On a more practical note, binary drivers do destabilise your machine to an extent, especially if you're running a kernel that was released after the driver. Binary drivers also hamper development of software that will have to interface with the driver (i.e. Xorg); developing is less efficient because they have to work around bugs and limitations in the driver. When there are security flaws, as there were recently with the Nvidia driver, the open-source community has to wait for the company to get around to fixing the vulnerability - they can't fix it themselves.

Proprietry software often goes hand-in-hand with closed standards; if you decide not to use Adobe Photoshop anymore, you immediately lose access to your own work.

It's your choice, and it's my choice, and I've chosen the same as you. But I try to keep proprietry software on my machine to a minimum, to preserve stability, security and interoperability.
