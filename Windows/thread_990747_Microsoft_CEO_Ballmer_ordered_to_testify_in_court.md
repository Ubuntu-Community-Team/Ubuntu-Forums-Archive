It's a little more complicated. Since it is a class-action, more than just HP is in on this. The problem is this is causing more EXPENSIVE machines to be built. A Vista machine doesn't just cost more to the end user, but it does cost the manufacturer a great deal as well. They have to pay for each part, every last nut, bolt, capacitor, transistor, and piece of software on that machine (Usually.).

When you buy a windows machine up to 1/3rd the cost of the machine is actually Windows, and you'll be more than compensating the manufacturer for the cost of the OEM hardware put in the machine.

Put simply, the cost ladder is like this:

Hardware manufacturer (Pays the least to make their products do to the inexpensive nature of components. Most of the budget goes into design.) -> Oroiginal Equipment Manufacturer (Pays not only more than the cost it took to produce the hardware for the hardware manufacturers, but must now pay bulk licensing costs to Microsoft and other software houses doing OEM services. -> Customer (Pays more than it cost to produce the machine. Essentially compensates the OEM for the licensing of the software.)

End users pay the absolute worst, and with the Vista machines driving up the costs of hardware, the End User pays even more.

The class-action is essentially about the fact that 1. Vista Capable Machines were really Vista Barely Capable Machines. 2. Vista "Capable" Machines became disgracefully expensive machines to become useful under Vista.

I wonder if that was what prompted Asus and Dell to release Linux machines.
