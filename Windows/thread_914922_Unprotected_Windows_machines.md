### Post by Darrell Lawrence on 2008-09-09
I recently made a trip to Ohio to visit family (I live in Missouri). Inquiring about the desktop systems of two of my sisters, I ask them what antivirus software they were using - they didn't know. So I checked out their machines. Neither of them were running antivirus software, firewalls or spam filters. Their computers were hooked up to DSL modems and left on most of the time. They also did not have Windows update activiated. Both machines were running Windows XP.

I set both their machines up with AVG antivirus (free edition), ran Windows Update, and activiated the Windows firewall. These machines had been running for well over a year completely wide open. Neither of my sisters are computer literate, and they use their machines for web surfing, e-mails and chats.

It made me think just how many Windows machines there are out there that are in the same situation, being run by owners that are almost completely computer illiterate, and how much this situation contributesed to the spread of malware?

Advertising places most of the emphasis on internet browsing, photos, games, e-mail, chat, and so on. Maybe along with this they need to start emphasing protecting your computer. I'm not knocking Microsoft Windows, but it ships with everything wide open by default. Trial versions of antivirus software, firewalls, etc., may be included, but they expire after a time, and the computer owner, if computer illiterate, may not even know their machines are unprotected.

In short, I educated my sisters on the need to keep their computers updated and protected. But again, just how much of this  same situation is out there?

Darrell

### Post by aysiu on 2008-09-17
Actually, I don't think antivirus is better than nothing.

Antivirus is bad in several ways: [list][*]**It gives many users a false sense of security.** People think "Oh, nothing bad will happen to me. I'm running antivirus." And, of course, every single machine I've ever seen compromised has always been running antivirus. [*]**Ironically, it also makes people ultra-paranoid.** Since anti-virus works not only through definition lists but also through pattern recognition, it will sometimes identify various files as "infected" or malicious even if they are benign. So people start freaking out and deleting or "cleaning" files that are perfectly fine.[*]**It focuses people in the wrong direction**. You don't protect yourself from disease by injecting yourself with every vaccine known to mankind and then sleeping around without condoms, not washing your hands before eating, and sharing needles with people. The proper protections against malware are education (to avoid social engineering) and the principle of least-privileged user account (which [Microsoft itself presumes to espouse](http://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/library/bb456992.aspx)). The analogy breaks down, of course, in that some medical vaccines are useful even if you are practicing basic hygiene, whereas antivirus is completely useless and even harmful if you have a proper permissions system set up and you can't be tricked by social engineering.[/list] The best way to secure Windows is to install and use SuRun.

Make your main account an SuRunner account (which is basically set up like *sudo* in Ubuntu and Mac OS X), educate yourself on avoiding social engineering attacks, and uninstall all that antivirus and antispyware crap.
