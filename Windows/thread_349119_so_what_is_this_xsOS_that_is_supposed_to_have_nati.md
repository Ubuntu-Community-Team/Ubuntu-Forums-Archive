We frown on insults on the Ubuntuforums.  However, below, I present my  "legitimate reasons."

***LEGITIMATE REASON 1:* Microsoft's EULA forbids it**.  Microsoft's [EULA for Windows XP](http://www.microsoft.com/windowsxp/home/eula.mspx) states, in relevant part:


> 4. LIMITATIONS ON REVERSE ENGINEERING, DECOMPILATION, AND DISASSEMBLY. You may not reverse engineer, decompile, or disassemble the Software, except and only to the extent that such activity is expressly permitted by applicable law notwithstanding this limitation.

The sort of modification contemplated by nn would seem to have been forbidden by Microsoft in very unambiguous terms.  From this, we can conclude one of two things:

**(A) nn is proceeding on this project legally**.  If this is so, then nn would have had to have negotiated a license on free enough terms to permit his contemplated modification and improvement of the operating system.  While this is possible, it is also highly improbable: Microsoft has an enormous vested interest in protecting its highly proprietary code. 

The last time (that I, personally, can remember) Microsoft collaborated with any outside entity to improve its operating systems was during the development of OS/2--and the other party in that venture was none other than the venerable International Business Machines Corporation of Armonk, New York.  nn may be many things, but he is certainly not IBM.  

Moreover, the business logic of this move is questionable, at best:  Why would Microsoft wish to dilute the sales of Vista, in which it is deeply invested, with this supposed 'XOS,'?  

**(B) nn is proceeding on this project illegally**.  Given the strictures of Microsoft's licensing, nn may be disassembling and modifying the Windows kernel and userspace in direct violation of Microsoft's licensing terms.  Once this reaches the attention of Redmond, this enterprise is (legally) dead in the water.  nn won't have a legal leg to stand on.

***LEGITIMATE REASON 2:* nn's credibility is doubtful, at best.**  Even disregarding the questionable legality of the whole enterprise--which perhaps accounts for its secrecy-- the project leader's claims have not been shown to be credible.

You note that Intel's new processor architectures do not undergo public beta testing.  This may be factually correct.  A lack of public beta testing might undermine Intel's credibility, except that there are other, more tangible indicators that Intel might be willing and able to deliver on its promises.  It is easy to track the construction and improvement of microprocessor fabrication facilities.  New factories and new tooling do a lot to convince the buying public that new end-products may be on the way.  Moreover, Intel has an established record.  The average consumer will expect Intel to be substantially true to its word, given the fact that Intel has delivered on its claims (to a greater or lesser extent) in the past. 

The XOS project, on the other hand, has no outside indicia of progress or success.  It has no track record of releases.  We have no means of comparing it to anything else.  In the absence of actual facts, we can only rely on rumor and speculation, which are weak reeds on which to support any realistic expectations.
