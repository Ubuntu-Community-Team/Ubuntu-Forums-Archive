### Post by amingv on 2008-01-31
From [this]("http://www.microsoft.com/resources/howtotell/content.aspx?pg=coa&displaylang=en") webpage:

> What is a Certificate of Authenticity (COA)?

A Certificate of Authenticity (COA) is a label that helps you identify genuine Microsoft software. A COA is not a software license – it is a visual identifier that assists in determining whether or not the Microsoft software you are running is genuine. However, without it, you will not have a legal license to run Microsoft software. A COA should never be purchased by itself without the software it authenticates.

### Post by digital_exhaust on 2008-01-31
It really depends on the "version" that you have. In your case (I'm assuming) it is an OEM license, so *technically* the license is tied to your motherboard. With an OEM copy of Vista, installed by an OEM (Dell etc..) you *techincally* can not "move" that license to another machine. In the case of a store bought OEM copy, Microsoft has been making exceptions to this rule. In situations where "catastrophic hardware failure" has been the cause of a re-install, they have been rather compliant regarding the issuance of new keys. I have seen many reports of minor hardware upgrades triggering invalidations, but in my personal experiences, these reports appear to be false. I have re-installed my store bought OEM Vista HP three times without incident, not to mention several video card upgrades, memory swaps and sound card changes. No call to Microsoft, nothing more than a simple click to activate. 

Probably went a bit off topic here, bit to answer the OP, no... you don't need to keep your case, your install is not tied to the case, maybe the motherboard, but as I said...."catastrophic hardware failure" seems to be the exception to the rule. Keep a copy of the key, or butcher the COA off the side and your fine. 

Hope that answers your question...

### Post by rune0077 on 2008-02-01
I recently removed Vista completely from my harddrive, then installed it on VirtualBox. This is an OEM version, and it caused problems because it was tied to my motherboard and complained about already being registered to another motherboard (since it didn't realize that it was being installed on a virtual board on the same machine).

So my registration key was useless. However, you can, at least here in Denmark, also register by phone, which uses a completely different key. I did this, and had to enter an insane amount of numbers into my phone, each series of numbers followed by a robotic voice that said "thank you, please press square to proceed" or some such. At the end of the registration I had to press 3 to promise that I didn't already have this software installed on another computer. I did (because I hadn't) and I got an activation code over the phone. 

The moral of the story being, that you don't need the key on the box, because you always have the telephone option instead.
