### Post by Prospero2006 on 2007-07-04
Hello All, 

I've been using Ubuntu full time now for several years. In fact I teach at a middle school in Texas where I've converted something like 150 computers so that they all dual-boot (K)ubuntu-Windows, but I haven't had the opportunity to test drive Vista until this week. This is my story:

My first child was born last week on June 22nd. As my wife and I were setting up the baby room I figured it would be nice to configure a server with 5.1 sound so we could do classical-jazz playlists in there at night. I found a Compaq Presario with a Celeron Processor, 120 gig hard drive, 512 memory, DVD burner, keyboard-mouse, and Vista Home Basic for 200 bucks + tax and shipping. (All said and done about $250.) It had everything I needed for a network sound server so I purchased it, and one week later it arrived at my door. 

Honestly, I was excited about getting to try out Vista.  The latest and greatest O/S from Microsoft would surely suit my basic audio/networking needs, right?  Plus, now I could brag to all my friends about my new Vista setup. "Vista, ah yes I'm familiar with that!"

As I fired it up and got started, it began by railroading me out of as much personal information as it possibly could. The Presario introductory screens presented themselves one after another threatening me with agreements and forcing me to fill out forms asking for everything short of my social security number.
I lied on every screen and it felt good, but I was growing impatient.

Finally, the windows boot splash appeared enticingly in front of me. This was it! I was close. My first Vista machine was about to take shape, and would soon be happily humming along in the corner of my son's room soothing him to sleep in the wee hours of the morning. The boot splash disappeared. I could feel my palms begin to sweat in anticipation. What would the control panel look like? Would the aero-glass work with this model? What about power-shell, the much heralded ability to search the entire hard drive in mere nanoseconds? This is gonna be neat!

The guys at Microsoft, as usual, had other plans for my time. More questions. More hoops. (Would you like to sign up for AOL, sir? We can't promise optimum functionality unless you read the following disclaimer statement and select one of the following options, sir.) This simple installation was quickly becoming a full-fledged interrogation. (For that reason, I initially named this new Presario Gestapo 1.) 

The array of software that came prepackaged on Gestapo 1's desktop was dizzying to say the least.
It had trial software galore: Office, Norton, AOL, and God only knows what else. I could only imagine what this felt like to the average user. The initial icon set looked like something straight out of hell.
What's necessary? What's not? (99 percent of it was designed to up-sell me in one way or another.) 

The first thing I needed to do was install Tight VNC so that I could access the system remotely. I installed the service, ran the client on my primary desktop, and...... Nothing! After checking the firewall and deleting Norton Internet Security, which was a 30-day trial cleverly disguised as the real thing, still it just wouldn't work.

(When running a program like TightVNC, Vista constantly asks you if you want to execute it as an administrator or regular user.  Frankly I didn't give a damn about user privileges. I just wanted it to work. It wouldn't. That seemed to be all there was to it. Something wasn't compatible.)

I proceeded to power it off, plug in a wireless D-Link adapter and connect a  Creative Labs 5.1 sound card. I turned it back on, and neither one was recognized. Installing the drivers was simple enough for the Creative Labs sound card, but the D-Link driver took a little more effort. 

Once it was time to select the network configuration applet in the control panel, it stopped to ask me if I was 'sure' I wanted to do this. (Yes, I was sure. I double clicked it didn't I?)

You see, that was pretty much the end of the road for me. The most frustrating thing about Windows Vista is it constantly treats the user like a mindless idiot. It seemed like every time I tried to install a program or make a configuration change, it would trigger a pop-up warning me that I was about to 'do something.'  (For the same price as the premium edition I could hire a munchkin full time to pop out and shout 'Follow the yellow brick road!' or something along those lines. Granted, for 300 bucks it wouldn't be a very hot munchkin or anything, but a munchkin nonetheless. 

It was frustrating, and my VNC still didn't work. 

My new Vista machine had a life span of about 3 hours, give or take the time it took me to pull it out of the box. Kubuntu correctly configured the sound-blaster and all relevant hardware. 
(The only real challenge was configuring the firmware for the wireless card. I find that wireless on Ubuntu can still be extremely hard to navigate and requires expertise to get right.) That said, the wired connection auto-configured and I was ready to run apt-get immediately. The 5.1 sound worked like a charm, and I can remotely run programs and access the thing from my office. 

Anyone interested in buying a slightly used copy of Vista? I'll start the bidding at 25 cents. 
I'll even pay the shipping.

Pros

Well, I've had a nearly identical experience.  But I DO have a topper.

I saw one of these little HP 7700 PCs in a Staples store going for cheap with an AMD64 dual core and 1GB of ram and I think a 250GB drive for about $500.  The case is so small, it almost looks like a fat laptop turned up on end.  I thought it was very cute.

I brought it home and put the box in my office and never opened it for three months.  Apparently I didn't need it too badly.

Today, I hauled it out and fired it up.  Lo - it had VISTA preinstalled.  I really didn't have to answer a lot of questions, though several to get it up and running.  I DID have to fight off the Norton anti-virus Virus which I think is where Bill goes to get tips on creating nasty aggressive software.  And HP is getting into to it as well, they have a health/help or something console that it is quite difficult to make go away.

I did find a nice tutorial here on the forums about dual booting Vista and was pleasantly surprised to learn that Vista had its own partitioner now, and it worked rather well to shrink the disk to about half leaving some 115 GB for Ubuntu, which I threw on there and in a remarkably short period of time had a dual boot up and running.

Vista was reasonably pretty, and desperately slow.  I wrestled a bit with the networking, though I don't know why.  It was pretty straight forward but screens would just disappear and so forth so it was a bit of a fist fight but it worked.

I have been playing with Linux for about a year and have Ubuntut on most of my machines at this point.  In fact, I've recently thrown in the towel and dumped Fedora Core 6 on a pretty massive machine with a couple of TB of disk raid, two Opteron dual cores, and 16 GB of RAM that I built, and put Ubuntu on it.  I far and away think Ubuntu is the best of the distros.

But frankly, in spite of the religious spitting and hacking and shrieking about open source software and the open source community, frankly application software on Linux sux heroically.  I have battled for months to do 
reasonably simple things and the tools just aren't there.  Hardware drivers are a constant battle, albeit less so in Ubuntu.  But I've played for months with things like GIMP as a replacement for Photoshop.  Sorry guys, it just isn't.  If you think it is, that's a sure sign you just don't do much or know much about photo images.

Recently, I became enamored of webcams.  I've found a couple of hundred webcams around the world that have stunning views of whatever, usually a skyline or some scenic tourist spot.  I care nothing for video frame rate, just resolution.  I can put together a slideshow of these things and people are just mesmerized by it on a big screen.  I did a little Ruby program to go fetch these sequentially to update them.  I've gotten pretty good, through some knowledge of the cameras themselves, at cleaving the webcam image, in highest resolution form, from all the surrounding HTML advertising and self aggrandizement where I just wind up with their image.  

The problem with webcams, and I have several that run over a couple of thousand dollars each, is they really don't do a very good job of it.  They have all gone for video speed and compression and so forth at the expense of resolution.

So I found that some of the digital still cameras out there are really quite good as to exposure, resolution, color, etc. and instead of several thousand dollars, for a couple of hundred bucks I can get a camera that does significantly higher resolution than HD (1920x1080).  In fact, I've used a little Canon S500 that I think is supposed to be 3 MP and I have to cut it DOWN to 1920 x 1080.  And if I remote capture it every 30 seconds, this little **** brick will crank out 2880 shots a day without a complaint.  Will it last a lifetime under this duty?  I'm 52 next week.  A lfietime isnt' that long anymore.

In fact, I can take these snapshots, and roll em together into a H.264 movie and basically I've created an HD time lapse film.  

But x264 ain't really.  And GPHOTO2 ain't really.  They both ALMOST work, but not really.  They kludge along throwing packets at things hoping for good things to happen.  GPHOTO certainly supports my latest camera, a Canon Digital Rebel XT.  Well, the docs say it does.  It actually doesn't.  And after compiling from subversion this little GPHOTO2/LIBGPHOTO2 nightmare, it kind of sort of works better, but not actually.

So I dug out an old laptop with XP SP2 on it, and loaded the Canon software.  Guess what?  It works perfectly and has time lapse built in.  So does DSLR Remote Pro from Breezy.  They can do it.  GPHOTO can clumsily clobber it but it doesn't actually work, despite 10,000 source files and 30 minutes to compile the very latest version on a fast machine.  

Bottom line is that over and over, I find the advertised specs on Linux applications to be very nearly fantasy.  If it does work you have to learn to compile the latest version yourself (it kinda works NOW) and the configuration arcana just never ends.  And mostly, it is broken software written by unemployed kids who lose interest before it actually works.

So today I was back in the world of Windows, and little applications like remote controlling a digital camera were suddenly easy.  In fact, I wanted to try Apples' Quicktime Pro to do the movie part I had been struggling with with FFMPEG and X264 (the AWARD WINNING codec that doesn't actually work).  So I downloaded it from Apple, and paid the $29 to activate the Pro features.

It does an amazing job, with almost no user effort at taking 2500 jpegs and making an H.264 movie out of them.

But all that is hard work.  It does take it awhile to do it.  So I copied one directory of snapshots to the new machine from my desktop.  This was over a GB LAN hardwired so it took just a few minutes.  I got Quicktime to load the JPEGS almost by looking at them and started it rendering a movie.  Another set of snapshots from the new camera was on the laptop.  It was connected by wireless so this was going to be slow.  But it can do it while the Quicktime is rendering.  Actually, it took it a couple of HOURS to copy 800 images over.  And the Quicktime took quite a bit of time.....

So I'm sitting herre drinking tea and smoking cigarettes and browsing about the web, and SUDDENLY......

Windows VISTA SHUTS DOWN Quicktime, SHUTS DOWN the file transfers, and starts to logoff of itself....
THen it announces that it has located upgrades, and is going to configure them, and it shuts down and restarts itself....

The file transfer was aborted.  I don't know what Quicktime ever did with its 70  minutes of processing, but I almost threw the little brick through the Window.

But along the way....  I did some browsing on the Apple site.  They have a new operating system coming out titled LEOPARD in October and the previews were just amazing.   So I ordered a brand new MacBook Pro with a dual core and a big 1920x1080 screen, along with Final Cut Pro and Aperture.  

After YEARS of sneering at Apple computers and the Apple geeks, I think the Apple is about to fall on my head.  I know VISTA is the beginning of the downhill curve for Microsoft.  They scrapped it twice and started over.  It has taken years.  It sucks HEROICALLY.

I've had three recent conversations with VETERAN Linux heads who have been working in it for years.  EACH of them mentioned they were kinda sorta going over to the OSX side.  I really didn't know what they were talking about.  They were very separate conversations.  Although all of them in the last three weeks.  Hmm.....

I think Linux is doing great.  I like the concept of open software.  But I think Open Source is probably the DOWNFALL of Linux more than its strength.  I need software that works.  That appears to be part and parcel with the concept of someone actually putting full time effort into writing it, with the expectation that someone somewhere is going to mail him a few sheckels for his efforts.  

Yes, yes, I know.  I can write a whole operating system and all necessary applications myself (standard free software retort/whine).  Actually, I probably could.  But I would be 75 then and I also make a little homemade whiskey in the garage which takes some time too....

So I'm going to try the Apple thing.  I have several thousand shares of their stock, but I guess I didn't really get that either.  I don't have an IPOD.  I don't tune into Itunes.   And I don't have an IPHONE.  I was just speculating.  Today, I think I kind of see the future and understand the sizzle behind the rise in the stock price.  It isn't just IPHONE or IPOD sales.  Steve Jobs is back and I think he's going for Bill Gates throat.  It's taken him awhile to turn PepsiAPple back into his Apple, but he's done it.

Meanwhile, I also read about the most innovative application I've heard of in a long time.  It's called LoJack.  It is hidden in Apple laptops.  It doesn't have any screens.  You don't configure it.  You don't use it.  You don't watch it.   But whenever you plug into the network it calls a central server and reports its IP location and I guess GEO data as well from the IP/GEO stuff.  

Now why would I want a program that tells somebody where I'm at any time I'm connected to the Internet?  Isn't that a little spooky?  Well actually, when I think about it really hard, I don't give a flying f who knows where I'm at anyway.  But if I call this service and tell them my laptop has been stolen, they call the local police and tell them where to go pick it up.  They claim a 90% recovery rate on stolen notebooks....
