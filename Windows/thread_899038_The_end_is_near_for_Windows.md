### Post by purplepaint on 2008-08-27
I think what's more likely to spell the end for Windows (if anything) is the cost associated with each new version.

In my experience, Windows XP was the latest version at a time when PCs were becoming affordable to more people than ever.  There are a lot of unadventurous computer users (young and old) who had Windows XP as their first operating system.  However, the fact that it came pre-installed on nearly everything meant that many of these new home PC users remained unaware of what exactly an operating system was, let alone that Windows was anything but *part* of their computer.  Many of these users will congratulate Microsoft for making what they feel is the first operating system they are confident to use, when in fact Microsoft rely on misinformation to ensure that users are unaware of any alternatives to their products  (Microsoft have never cared about the tiny "geek" market, or - to a lesser extent - even the business market, since all they need is for the majority of the world to have been brought up using their products oblivious to anything else).  When Vista was released, XP had been adopted by many new businesses and home users who were reluctant to upgrade, and Microsoft are having just as hard a time convincing them to do so as many Linux (over)enthusiasts have trying (in good faith) to force Linux on the same unadventurous/uninterested users!

If everyone was aware that something like Ubuntu could do the "word processing and web browsing that is all they need a PC for and therefore have no need for anything other than Windows" for free, the end for Windows would have been near and then gone.
