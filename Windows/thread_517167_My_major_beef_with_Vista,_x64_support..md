### Post by izanbardprince on 2007-08-04
I've been running 64-bit Linux for a while now, Ubuntu's 64-bit version pretty much has all the kinks in the 32-bit compatibility layer smoothed out, and 64-bit applications are becoming much more prevalent in Linux, especially since 32-bit processors aren't even sold anymore.

So with Linux embracing the X86-64 architecture, 4 years ago iD Software, Valve, and EPIC decided that they would "support" 64-bit Linux by tweaking their game engines a little and recompiling them as 64-bit binaries, this allows them a number of the advantages of x64, but not all of them, since the engine was designed primarily with 32-bit processors in mind, this is why in gaming, 64-bit has been rather ho-hum so far, being virtually identical to x86 in performance.

But Microsoft assured everyone that with Vista being the "OS of the future" that x64 would take front row, and 32-bit would basically be the throwback to people running ancient PC's that didn't support the x64 instruction set, this was great news and so the major video game companies told everyone that instead of just supporting x64, their new engines would practically REQUIRE it, but since then, Microsoft has managed to make a big enough mess out of Vista that it's essentially a 32-bit operating system, with the 64-bit version being nothing more than a novelty, what's worse is that PC makers are still shipping the 32-bit version of Vista on 64-bit hardware because they're worried about compatibility problems, and this means that since Linux is already an after thought by major developers, the best we can hope for is more engines with x64 support tacked on after the fact, that still have all kinds of problems that the developers don't really care about fixing.

With the next Windows release probably being around 2011 or so, at this rate we'll all have 128-bit processors before Microsoft even manages to get a functional 64-bit OS out the door.

If Microsoft dedicated the time and effort towards improving Vista technologically that they spent making sure they could implement all the Treacherous Computing garbage and Damnable Restrictions Malware, they could have made Vista a viable 64-bit OS.

And lastly, if you even want to tinker with Vista x64, you have to buy the 32-bit version, mail your discs back to Microsoft with a replacement form, pay S&H back to your address, and wait 4-6 weeks for them to ship it to you.

### Post by insane_alien on 2007-08-05
the advantages of using 64-bit:

- you can use all of your processors power
- you can access lots more RAM without the slow big memory model
- processing large datasets with data segments more than 32-bits long speeds up 100fold if not more.
there is more than this but this is what i have come across in my usage.

disadvantages:
-some 3rd party apps don't run so well in 64-bit. most notablty, flash.
-some drivers can be a pain.

on the brightside, the disadvantages are disappearing fast and the advantages are becoming even more important as base machines sold in shops have around 2GB of RAM now. probably by this time next year they will be sold with 4GB or more. if they do not use a 64-bit OS by then then there will be an outcry from the more tech savvy public soon followed by the rest when they find out that they bought something that doesn't actually give them what they bought.

go 64-bit. its worth the few little tweaks you need to start off.
