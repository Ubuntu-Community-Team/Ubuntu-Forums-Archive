### Post by MicahCarrick on 2007-11-27
> I have read many posts in these forums and peoples opinions on web development tools available under Linux, and some say Linux have THE BEST tools for web development. If that is the case, how is it that Dreamweaver is the best selling web IDE in the world? and why have Macromedia (now owned my Adobe), been seeling them in their millions? I just reconcile these two positions.

When people say Linux development tools are THE BEST, whether they realize it or not, they are stating a preference. People who choose to develop websites in Linux (such as myself) are often a different breed than those working with Dream weaver in Windows. We expect different things out of the tools we use.

Dreamweaver is the standard. Why? Because it's the big dog commercial application. Web development has almost become as common as word processing. The good folks behind Dreamweaver have marketing departments, share holders, HR department, etc. The point is, they're developing a "product" and they're marketing it. And it's a great program--now. But it wasn't too long ago that Dreamweaver generated messy, invalid, and very ugly HTML. I made good money as a free lance developer fixing websites that had broken due to Dreamweaver.

However, the more recent versions of Dreamweaver I see my colleges using is much nicer. I can even use it at the office (though I just use the text editor portion). Although the developers I work with work in Dreamweaver, they are not without knowledge of the underlying CSS and HTML. In fact, they spend just as much time in the text-editing view--if not more--than the WYSIWYG view.

I work with a web development team and I'm the only Linux user. I have my preference, they have theirs, and that's okay. As a developer working in Linux, I bring some important elements to the table though.

1. I'm the first to know when one of the web designers or developers write code that only works in IE or Windows. I can do more thorough cross-browser testing.

2. I have a much more intimate understanding of the functionality of our website. I know where every piece is coming from and how--PHP, Javascript, Apache and it's modules, MySQL, HTML, CSS, etc. When there's a bug, I know the exact place to look. I don't see the website as it's final appearance, but as code. As a result, I can typically do anything the Dreamweaver guys can do faster in Gedit (short of the initial layout design) than they can in Dreamweaver--and my code will be valid and pretty.

3. My system works like a web server. I can SSH into the server on a command line and quickly and effectively troubleshoot, parse/process/view logs, track down memmory/cpu bottlenecks, etc. 

I could go on for days about the benefits of open-source software for web development, however, the bottom line is that it's my preference. The things I want out of my development environment, the things I see as benefits, might sound like a nightmare to somebody else.

### Post by MicahCarrick on 2007-11-27
Regarding LaRoza's response...

Exactly. Very few sites are just static HTML/CSS anymore.

As I mentioned before, Dreamweaver has come a long way. Like any other tool--you have to know how to use it. A user inexperiences in HTML and CSS will code an ugly, invalid page in Notepad just as they'll get an ugly, invalid page using Dreamweaver. They'll just do it faster in Dreamweaver and it will take them longer to know why it's bad.

The company I work for is a large online e-commerce site (over 18000 products, over 2500 pages). We also use PHP, MySQL, etc. 

The WYSIWYG portion is used in laying out a template. We (not me but the designer) will create a single xhtml and css file in dreamweaver until it looks the way we want. When working with complex designs, this is where dreamweaver can come in handy (or Kompozer or Quanta in Linux). Once the template is designed, the web designer goes into the text editor portion of Dreamweaver--which is a good text editor.  Remember, Dreamweaver isn't just a WYSIWYG code generator, but an IDE. At this point, Dreamweaver is functioning like any other text editor (when I do this phase I do it in Gedit). We tweak the code and CSS to make sure it's all up to both web standards and our own internal standards. And finally, it's passed off to a guy like me--a programmer--to make it will work with our template engine and deploy (we use Smarty, a custom PHP-based CMS,  and a custom cache system).

The point I'm making, is that web development is a process, and different tools can be used for different phases and different user preferences. Ultimately, there are as many opinions to this topic as there are web development objectives. "Web Development" is such a broad topic. If you want to be a professional web developer--you'd better know what's going on behind Dreamweaver (but should understand Dreamweaver if you're going to be working with a team who uses Dreamweaver). If you're going to post some photos on a website for Grandma... go ahead and use Frontpage *shudder*. Or just open up a photobucket account for that matter.

### Post by Samhain13 on 2007-11-28
I wouldn't say "wild" but truly, it's all very speculative. And that is why I have been proposing to look into to the final products themselves and not just on the tools used to creating them; because then, we can have a better perspective on what users or companies can actually do with their tools.

Using commercial products has its advantages in terms of marketing. When a company says in their website or brochure that they use a certain brand of products, they can invoke a certain sense of affinity from their potential customers who know about those products. And for potential customers who do not know about those products, because the products are branded, it is easier to do research on them and to find ratings and reviews on those products.

But the problem with this is, there is a strong possibility that customers who do the research stop short of reviewing what a company actually produces when the customer sees very good reviews of the products that the company uses.

Think about it, say you didn't know any better: if you had a business making and selling handicraft and you think your company could use the services of a web development firm to make an e-commerce site for you. You do a search for firms and pick out the ones that offer the best prices. From your shortlist, would you rather pick a firm who throws technical jargon at you to describe what they do or a firm who simply says that they use the latest Creative Suite from Adobe?

Branding helps. And companies will take advantage of co-branding themselves with better-known companies in order to sell their products and services. Does this mean they offer better or worse products and services than the competition? No. But it does help them to sell-- and that's what business is  about.
