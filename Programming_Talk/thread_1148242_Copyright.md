### Post by Flimm on 2009-05-04
(I'm not a lawyer, and this is not legal advice)
If you've written the program, you automatically own the copyright to it, and no one is allowed to copy it without your permission.
Most people put a disclaimer in their source code at the beginning of every file, in the documentation for the program, and in its about dialog. For example:
```
Copyright © 1998-2000 Evan Lawrence, Alex Robert
```
This isn't actually necessary, but its useful reminder for all users of the program, and it tells them who they need to contact for more information about their rights.
A license explicitly tells you what you are allowed to do with the program. For example, the [GNU General Public License](http://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-3.0.html) gives you permission to copy, change and share the program, as long as the source is available, in a nutshell. For proprietary software, licenses would only be granted to those who pay a fee, and usually do not allow you to access the source code or to share your copy of the software.
A lot of proprietary programs also make you sign a contract or agreement by clicking "I agree", in order to make you give up certain rights. Spyware is famous for this, they get you to agree to giving up your privacy to use their software.
I hope that helps.

very good reply

I am going to add that general copyright transfer or cancellation is very hard to occur. It usually happens when you work as an employee to a company or an individual, the mental work that is created in these circumstances is transfered to the employer as copyright. 

So you can sleep in peace that none can touch your copyright. Just put a license txt file like the GPL and you will be just fine. 

If you plan to sell and believe that you work will be of very important value (money ) then go to a lawyer and enter in a contract with other party. 

The terms of the contract will make absolutely clear the TERMS OF USE for your software. Generally a signed contract (physically signed not electronically ) is the best way to ensure that your copyright is protected even under the strangest of circumstances. 

Of course lawyers and contracts costs, but it may cost you even more to defend your copyright in court. 

If you believe that you work wont be of important value then yes the above solution as suggested by the previous poster will suffice.
