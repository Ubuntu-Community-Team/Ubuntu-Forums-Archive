### Post by PaulM1985 on 2010-05-11
I have found that it is all about experience and dabbling with writing programs.  If you aren't practising you don't get better.  

Clearly you are practising while doing you course, but what I mean is additional work you do to supplement your study.  Have you got anything that you look at part time?  I am not saying that you should take on projects which could conflict with the time you are supposed to be studying, but just having an active outside interest in development, rather than the stuff you are "forced" to do.

Also, I am not sure it is right to compare yourself to openoffice.  That was not written by one person who was "ready to be a developer".  Those projects were completed (I imagine) by a team, rather than one lone developer.

I have also found that this forum is really useful.  Looking at things other people are doing, thinking how I would solve the problem and then looking at other people's suggestions also helps with learning.

I guess I haven't really answered your question by giving a set timescale and criteria which makes you ready, because I don't think this is possible.

Just keep learning and keep looking at different bits of code.

Hopefully this has been of some use.

### Post by trent.josephsen on 2010-05-11
That depends heavily on what realm of development you are interested in.  With a LAMP server and a little understanding of PHP, Python, or Perl (pick one), you can host other people's pages or collect information about open source projects or programming in general, which would be a contribution probably more significant than anything you can give to OpenOffice.org.  On the other hand, it takes quite a bit of C experience to be able to usefully contribute to the kernel -- there might be counterexamples but I imagine most major kernel contributors have more than just a few years.

You can contribute at any skill level by filing bug reports, writing documentation, and helping users newer than yourself.  Skills to develop in the meantime include at least a scripting language such as Python or Perl, one or both of C and C++, probably Java, and a Lisp dialect.  I refer you to [How To Become a Hacker]("http://catb.org/~esr/faqs/hacker-howto.html") as my primary source.  You would probably do well to read *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* (if you haven't already) for a better understanding of the open source development model and variations on it.

Certainly. From what I've seen, most "large" application (Desktop App) projects, have been written in derivative of C (C, C++). Its worth learning C++, as once you have learn that, you will be able to move very very easily to other languages, and you will also be exposed to important coding aspects such as memory management (manual, as opposed to Java that uses a Garbage Collector and handles memory management for you).

Now, though I would recommend this action, I would also like to stress that, it is a mistake to believe, "I can code so I can become a coder for open source project X". Coding is, of course, a very important skill, but an even more important skill is **communication**. Find an open source project you're interested in, and enter their IRC Channel (or forums etc). Get used to talking with people you don't know, and witnessing the "Developer Community". After that, you can use whatever interests and skills you have to your advantage.

Good luck :)
