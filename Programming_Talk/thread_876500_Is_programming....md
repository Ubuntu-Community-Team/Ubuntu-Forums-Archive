### Post by 3370318 on 2008-07-31
If you don't enjoy programming, maybe you shouldn't really pursue it.  I personally don't think that lacking such knowledge will detract from your Linux experience; especially on a distribution like Ubuntu.  That said, possessing some level of understanding will enrich your experience not only on Linux, but in Windows too (or OSX if you're into that sort of thing :-P).

Speaking from a hardware bias, I have a preference toward lower level languages like C (dont worry about assembly or HDLs :)).  I'm sure many will disagree, but C exposes you to a fairly regular syntax which, in my opinion, is usually fairly easy to read.  Moreover, once you become comfortable with the basics of control flow and understand how some simple algorithms work (ex. a bubble sort) C offers you the opportunity to take a peek behind the scenes at many of your system's inner workings.  Unfortunately, C also requires an understanding of makefiles and the learning curve involved would likely sour your opinion of programming even further.  Thus, it would probably be best to avoid C as your first language, but keep it in the back of your mind as an option if you ever feel like taking the plunge into a deeper level of control.

Given the likelihood of a disdainful experience with C, I would begin with scripting languages such as Perl, Python, or Tcl (Tcl/Tk has a special place in my heart) as they share much of the same structure as C, yet offer a more accommodating experience.  However, I would not recommend trying to learn these languages based solely on tutorials as many tutorials don't offer enough information in the way of examples or explanations to fully convey the concepts presented.  If you're looking for some useful texts, I've found books like "Programming Perl" and "Practical programming in Tcl and Tk" to be very beneficial.  You don't even need to purchase them as much of the information is available on Google books:
[Programming Perl]("http://books.google.com/books?id=ezqe1hh91q4C&dq=programming+perl&pg=PP1&ots=juAEzyeRR2&sig=2pGkA-2km9t1h3foCvjbLEYEkEo&hl=en&sa=X&oi=book_result&resnum=1&ct=result")
[Practical programming in Tcl and Tk]("http://books.google.com/books?id=0EB9OIFBnFgC&dq=welch+practical+tcl/tk&pg=PP1&ots=ZblHbFl8Go&sig=ywDekfbbWekSPs8BexpBuxkmyGQ&hl=en&sa=X&oi=book_result&resnum=1&ct=result#PPP1,M1")
The Tcl/Tk book even offers many of its chapters as downloadable pdfs at:
[http://www.beedub.com/book/]("http://www.beedub.com/book/")

I hope that you'll take the plunge but, like I said, if you don't find programming to be enjoyable, I really don't think you'll miss out on much of what Linux (and OSS in general) offers.  Also, don't get discouraged when those that sneer from their Ivory Keyboards insist that your lack of programming knowledge relegates you to a world outside of Linux.  You'll undoubtedly run into people bent on feeding insatiable egos after posting enough questions; dangling their supposed intelectual superiority over those they "help" seems to be a highly frequented avenue for this purpose.
