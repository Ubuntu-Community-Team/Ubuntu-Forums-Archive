### Post by KdotJ on 2012-09-16
I'll answer these with my own opinions:

1) No. A lot of people in my course hadn't done a lot of (if any) real programming before. However, many of those people found it all very hard and some didn't even progress to semester two.

2) Practise is key here I think. The more experience you get, the better. BUT I think you should try to keep researching as well and have a look at standard ways to do things. They say that practise makes perfect... but I disagree and say that practise makes permanence. If you practise doing something wrong, you'll always do it wrong.

3) I'd say that python is a pretty popular choice for a first language. But try to keep your mind open to non-python ideas too.

4) Personally I would say its okay to do that, but keep one language as your "main language". You will probably find that your university will use one particular language for most things (most likely Java). 


As a side note, I personally think that one of the most important things to come to realise is that at the end of the day it really doesn't matter what language you use. What's important is tat you learn the fundamental ideas of programming, data structures and techniques. Try not to get "locked" in a specified language.

 Try to see that the language is just a tool... If I want to put a nail in a wall, there are plenty of different types of hammers that I could use... but they all do the same job really.

Focus on your abstraction and reasoning skills, not the languages.      Somebody who's brilliant at problem solving can usually quickly learn the mere mechanical details of a language, while expertise about arbitrary APIs and language details is pointless if you haven't a clue about how to properly use them.

### Post by kaytrance on 2012-09-17
1. not at all, although you will feel much better there if you have knowledge of some programming. It very depends on the university. For ex., when I was studying at bachelor degree, the programming in the first semester was very hard for people, that were unfamiliar with C.
