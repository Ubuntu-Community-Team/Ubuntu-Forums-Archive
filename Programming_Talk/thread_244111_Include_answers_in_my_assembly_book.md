### Post by rplantz on 2006-08-25
I have written an introductory assembly language book and wonder what you think about including solutions to my exercises in an appendix.

I taught from it at my university for 5 years. I retired 2 years ago, and they continue to use it. The book is intended for students who have taken an introduction to programming class in C or C++. I am quite sure Java would be okay.

It is quite different from other books. Mine is not intended to teach how to program in assembly language, but to show what is going on at the instruction set level when programming in C/C++. For example, I show a while loop in C, then the assembly language created by gcc (the -S option), then how an assembly language programmer might write it. I only present a very small subset of the instructions, because I want to concentrate on the concepts.

After a little more work, I'm planning to publish the book, probably through [www.lulu.com](www.lulu.com).

There are quite a few exercises, and I have prepared a solutions manual. Originally, I was only going to make it available only to instructors but am now thinking of including it as an appendix in the book because:
1. I think the best way to learn is to try for a while, then peek at a solution if you're getting stuck.
2. Solutions quickly find their way onto the internet anyway.
3. Many students copy homework solutions from each other, and this would level the playing field.
4. Better students will try more exercises.
5. Some people may wish to use the book on their own.
6. Many instructors make up their own assignments anyway.

It seems like most programming books do not include exercise solutions. But I studied electrical engineering, and I recall most of my math and engineering textbooks had solutions to at least half the exercises.

I would appreciate hearing opinions about this. My number one goal is to make this a useful book for students. (Since I'm retired, I don't need to publish to get tenure. And I don't think I will get rich from an assembly language book.  :) )

### Post by rplantz on 2006-08-26
> **cantormath said:**
> Including the answers in the book, good answers, not short sarcastic hints, can only help a student when learning to program..  

Good point. My answers to math problems, e.g., convert decimal to binary, are simply numbers. All my programming solutions are complete, including good (in my opinion) comments.

I realize that people have different programming styles. Another reason for including my programming solutions is that an instructor could compare his/her style with mine for the students.

My overall impression from teaching programming for over twenty years is that we don't give students enough code to read. It's weird. We explain the concepts, give them a couple of very short examples, then expect them to develop creative designs. I believe that we still have a lot to learn about teaching computer science.
