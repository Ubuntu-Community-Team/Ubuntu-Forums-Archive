### Post by TheFu on 2014-05-07
[http://blog.jdpfu.com/2011/10/19/how-to-learn-to-program](http://blog.jdpfu.com/2011/10/19/how-to-learn-to-program) explains it.
Game programming has many, many, many different sorts of skills depending on the type of game. Chess games use different knowledge than flight sims than real-time strategy that FPS.  PC games with heavy graphics will require C/C++.  A friend of mine works for a company that does game optimization about 6-12 months before the big-name games are released. He's heavy on C++.  He spent 8 yrs working for a big game house for suck pay before finally getting an offer from the boutique where he works now. He was a NASA rocket scientist before that.  Just sayin'.

Android is COMPLETELY different - Java - Android's version of Java.

So - pick one that you will follow through on and get started. Depending on where you live, Android development is not likely to pay the bills. Most Android apps don't make $10K/yr ... can't live on that were I am. OTOH, if you build Android software for other companies, you can be paid extremely well, after experience is proven.  Looked last week and average Sr Android Developers salaries are $110K+.

Books are almost always out-of-date before they are published.

The overhead for Android development is significant. I'm a programmer with 20+ yrs of experience, but Android is just too specialized to bother.  Most of my programming these days is scripting - perl, ruby stuff. The productivity compared to compiled languages is probably 400%.  Android is different enough from all other platforms that it takes specialized skill.  

The MIT lecture links were part of the original article, I'm certain.

Why do we don't programmers?
a) In HS, I took a programming class. Made friends.
b) In college, my engineering classes assumed I knew how to program already (they didn't bother teaching any languages). Made friends.
c) My 1st real job was programming. Made friends.
d) I took more programming classes from the local community college while working - which provided me with skills for my follow jobs. Made friends.
e) From '89 - 2000, I was basically a full-time programmer. Making friends happens at work. 

I'm still friends with those people and most are still programming today. Most have left the positions and moved on, so my friends work all over the world now - Spain, France, Austria, Korea, Japan, Nepal, India, Argentina, Brazil, and the USA (of course). They work in many companies you know - google, microsoft, Dell, Oracle, EMC, VMware,  Boeing, and aviation companies, plus lots of mid-sized ISPs and smaller companies we don't think usually have programmers.  Many do boring things, but a few are doing very interesting stuff indeed.  A relative works at the company behind WordPress. I haven't looked at my LinkedIn connections recently to see where everyone is.  I don't use facebook or tweeter - call me paranoid.

A friend or two is very high up in the MGM gambling corporation, for example.  AT&T, BT - I know folks in both ... most are NOT programming anymore. They are architects.

So most of my connections do not hit in Europe all that much, but a few do. It can be a small world, if you connect to lots of folks.  I've talked/presented in multiple countries and organize a local LUG with about 20 members showing up to monthly meetings. I'm active in a different LUG with weekly meetings - geared towards helping noobs to Linux. We have a good mix of completely "what is Linux" people and folks with 20+ yrs experience helping out. It really is amazing to watch. Last week, I saw a transplant from central Africa to ATL get help loading Ubuntu on his laptop - a crappy 5 yr old POSC.  At the same time, I was helping a retired guy learn about firefox plugins to make life easy and about 4 others chatted about their professional experiences over the last 15 yrs with Linux.

Tonight I'm speaking on how to "Get Paid with Linux Skilz" at the monthly LUG meeting. Should be fun, if any students actually show up. Finals happened here last week, so I'm not expecting a large crowd.

If you aren't around programmers all the time, it is hard to learn properly.  It is like becoming a priest when you don't go to church or seminary school. Very hard.

Join a local Linux group, meetup group for languages, take a programming class, do something YOU LOVE.  The last 2 yrs, I took a free Ruby on Rails class taught by the local Ruby group.  The Perl group is still going too, plus there are Java, Python, and cough ... .Net groups in my metro area.  If you live in a rural area ... learn using the internet - videos, youtube, reading, join a F/LOSS team and do documentation, testing, and simple bug fixes.  

YOU have to take the imitative.  That is the secret to life, BTW.  Asking a question here is fine, but actually DOING SOMETHING YOURSELF matters 99% more. The struggle and failures will teach more than any other method of learning.  Find a group, ask to help out, volunteer and you will be amazed at what you will learn.
