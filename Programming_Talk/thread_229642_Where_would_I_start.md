### Post by eXisor on 2006-08-04
I agree with stormblue, but we all had to start somewhere so don't let us put you off. 
Game programming is mostly done in c++, but since that's quite a difficult language to start out on, I'd suggest you learn javascript. Look at one of the many Ajax packages and step through the code until you get the hang of it. (Ajax will also introduce you to xml).

Further, you could buy a cheap game engine and play around with that. One of many is [www.garagegames.com](www.garagegames.com). They have a downloadable demo of their FPS oriented Torque game engine. It runs on linux, mac, xbox and windows. If you like it you can bye the source code for $100. But even an engine like Torque, although very scriptable, is going to require c++ coding to get a basic game concept going.

Another way to go is to make mods for existing games. Look at the modding forums for your favourite games. I know Fear, Oblivion, Quake, Half-Life and X3 are all very moddable.

First off, an FPS is not a good first project unless you're already pretty familiar with the language and most of the libraries you'll be using, and it sounds like you're not. Keep it really simple (e.g., Pong, Tetris, etc.) until you know your way around then try to make the big stuff.

If you want to get into games, you'll probably want to learn C++, OpenGL, and [SDL](http://www.libsdl.org/). I also highly recommend the following books:

[list]
[*][The C Programming Language, 2nd Edition](http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0131103628), by Kernighan and Ritchie. You probably don't want to stick with C, but it's the best programming tutorial I've seen to date, and it will give you a solid base for learning C++.
[*]The [red](http://www.opengl.org/documentation/red_book/) and [blue](http://www.opengl.org/documentation/blue_book/) books. These are the official OpenGL programming guide and reference manual, respectively. Both are available for free online, but I highly recommend a paper copy of at least the red book.
[/list]
And some websites:
[list]
[*][GameDev.net](http://www.gamedev.net/). They have some great articles and forums, all geared toward game development.
[*][NeHe Productions](http://nehe.gamedev.net/). Some of the best OpenGL tutorials I've seen on the net, and lots of them.
[/list]

### Post by themusicwave on 2006-08-08
Just a few thoughts/tips for you.

An FPS is not the place to start nor is something like an MMPORG or anything huge like that.  Start small and be patient.  Start with something 2D before trying 3D, build up to things.

I've been programming for a few years now, I'm currently in college for software engineering.  I've been working up to building a 3D game for awhile now(few years).

I started out with simple stuff like tic-tac-toe.  Then I made a pretty big old school window based RPG with a completely flat 2D world.  Then I made a 2D side scroller with basic AI, tile based maps, music, powerups and such.  Now I am learning some OpenGL. I did most of that along with all my assignments for class, so I worked on my personal projects very little.  

It's good to dream big, but you also need patientce.  You will not be making an MMPORG or FPS overnight.  

Some tips for you:
-If games are your goal you might want to learn C++.You can buy some books off amazon or half.com for pretty cheap usually.
-To start pick one language and get good with it.  Learning too many languages at once is confusing.  Especially if you are starting out.
-Start with small games and build up.
-Learn OpenGl once you have a good grasp of your language of choice.
-Study up on good game design, AI coding, pathfinding, all that kinda stuff.
-Now tackle that big project.

When I started out I thought I'd be making awesome games real soon, I was wrong.  But, I am gettign there slowly and you can as well.

You're confusing game design with software engineering. An RPG may be complex, but the underlying engine is usually fairly simple. A FPS, on the other hand, usually involves a physics engine and network synchronization. Also, making a game and playing a game are very different things; I, for example, like playing fast-paced action games, but I find in-depth strategy/tactics games more fun to create.

> But!! I do see your point fluffington. Keep it simple at first till I can move on. But stuff like pong or whatever won't keep me with that project. Know what I mean? I'm not saying to keep making Pong forever. Just make one, and don't rush; make a *good* Pong clone. Once you've done that, look over it and write down what you did wrong and what you did right. Then, pick something a little bigger, and apply what you learned to that. Repeat indefinitely; after enough iterations, the slightly more complex next project will be a shooter or RPG or whatever it is you want to end up making.

Also, I highly recommend you attempt a multiplayer Asteroids clone as a second or third project; it's a decent challenge, and you'll have fun playing it when you're done.

And one more entry for books: [The Art of Computer Programming](http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0201485419/), by Donald E. Knuth. I had one of the books in the series as a textbook in college, but I hated that class so much that I never put much effort into reading the book. I went out and bought a copy recently, though, and have discovered that they're actually really good. And a word of warning: this is not a light read, and an understanding of calculus is recommended.
