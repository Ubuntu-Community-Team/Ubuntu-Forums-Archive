This statement sounds way too oversimplified. Machine learning is HARD and COMPLEX. Nobody has really gotten it right yet. As a matter of fact, computers are still pretty bad at simply recognizing patterns (modern image recognition for instance is still rather limited and too slow) even with a targeted, predefined goal.

They're especially limited when it comes to context. Humans rely heavily on relational context that computers simply don't posses yet. For instance "he threw the ball at the batter". They aren't ready to fill in the gaps when presented information that wasn't formatted for them.

### Post by evymetal on 2007-11-11
For the OP:
You sound like a mathematician, the problem is that when you come to face the problem in the real world it becomes much harder.

You can actually do a lot with pure mathematical logic equations. (Here I mean the axioms of first order languages, and the standard rules for variable substitution, etc.)

Actually, the problem is exactly what you said, but it's certainly not simple:

There is a lot of work going on proving mathematical theorems like this - which works really well (see cambridge university computer science - UK), but the problem in the real world is this:

Logically you can induce a fact F from a premise P that is sufficient.
i.e.

P => F

iff P is sufficient for F

If you are working in a system which is suitable for mathematical analysis then this is ok, since you begin with your axioms for that system (A), and then you can prove any provable statement from them, combined with the axioms for logic. 

BUT, the type of questions that are asked in the real world are not governed by some set axioms (and even very few scientific philosophers would think it is. Practically no scientists would say it is due to how well Quantum Mechanics fits in.)

In fact, all that you have are the axioms for logic, and lots of imprecise data. Artificial Intelligence is largely a problem of Machine Learning, which is finding methods of statistically analysing the data to try to find facts that are probably correct. This means that instead of having simple logical statements, you quickly end up with huge statistical models.

People try to find ways around this, by defining different axioms for logic (i.e. Fuzzy Logic), or by making first order approximations of the truth of a statement, which introduces errors. Neither is perfect, and both of them introduce a huge level of complexity into the problem, as a simple yes/no decision turns into a huge statistical analysis.

Hope that helps explain it

### Post by CptPicard on 2007-11-12
In the field of philosophy of AI and as that relates to the idea that you could have a set of some kind of logic axioms you could construct an AI from, the most fundamental issue is, of course, the sad fact of the [incompleteness]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems") of symbolic logic systems.

There was great hope for constructing a general proof-finding algorithm of the kind the OP perhaps has in mind, but as most knowledgeable people know, that program ran into serious trouble and was essentially sank in the first half of last century. The most fundamental results in CS are closely related to this.

There certainly is no proof that the human mind isn't limited in the same fashion, and some strong AI proponents say that Gödel's result shouldn't be taken as so forbidding, but I'm not so sure... as soon as you can express natural numbers, your logic system is already fundamentally limited in a way whose full implications we haven't quite yet grasped. There always seems to be some kind of outside semantics that just "are there", but are beyond our system!

A [great paper]("http://members.aol.com/NeoNoetics/MindsBrainsPrograms.html") about this was written by John Searle, everyone interested in this topic should read it... it's very readable even for laypeople.
