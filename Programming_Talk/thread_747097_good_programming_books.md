### Post by elithrar on 2008-04-06
If you haven't touched programming before, I'd second the Head First recommendation from Chayak. However, once you've moved through that, I'd have a look at these (Python) books:

[Learning Python](http://www.oreilly.com/catalog/9780596513986/index.html) - very good starting point for the language. Can't recommend this highly enough; even with a bit of knowledge under my belt I found the majority of the content useful.
[Python Cookbook](http://www.oreilly.com/catalog/pythoncook2/index.html) - a bit outdated (2.3 & 2.4), but still pretty handy if you ever need a good hard copy of code snippets and small scripts/applications. Meshes well with online snippet repositories.
[Programming Python](http://www.oreilly.com/catalog/python3/index.html) - This is a fair bit more advanced, and is a veritable tome of knowledge. Worthwhile buying once you've got a few applications/large scripts under your belt as a solid reference on more advanced functions and libraries - not so great to start with but something to consider later on. I haven't used this a whole lot due to my focus being more on Django over advanced Python apps.

There's also an Apress book called "[Beginning Python](http://www.amazon.com/Beginning-Python-Novice-Professional/dp/159059519X/ref=pd_bbs_sr_1?ie=UTF8&s=books&qid=1207530523&sr=8-1)" that isn't half bad, but the reviews are more mixed than they are for Learning Python.
