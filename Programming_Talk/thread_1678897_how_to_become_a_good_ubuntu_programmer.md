### Post by Ferrat on 2011-01-31
First of, you can become a good pianist or tennis player by practice, doing it over and over, every day, true that some people will have a more natural aptitude towards the piano or tennis or programming but natural talent isn't everything. 
For ex. in Tennis all of the people playing at a top level are good, really good and they got there by practice and hard work, some however have a "natural" advantage but that only shows when it comes to the finals, Bjorn Borg for ex. is probably one of the greatest tennis players that ever have or ever will live but he spent hours and hours playing ball against his garage door when he was young and trained a few extra years before switching from the old tree racket to the new metal once that came along. 

Pure talent will help you, it will give you and edge in the start and in the end but it will only do that, you still need to work, I used to be fairly good at drawing when I was younger but due to lack of interest I didn''t do it that much and there for my skill never increased, if anything it decreased. 
While someone that really really wants to learn will get better and better every day someone even if he/she wasn't born with a "god given talent" for that particular skill.

To learn how to program you just need to read books and I recommend watching Standfords programming classes on youtube (they are free and goes from no understanding to pretty high levels of knowledge) and all other sources you can, I'n not that good of a programmer I admit, but I know the very basics like the back of my hand, having read about 10 "starting programming books, watching tutorials and reading other online sources" when you understand the basic and start understanding how it all fits together then you will have a much easier time understanding the code you are looking at to learn.

I agree. Too many fine details to discuss in here. But I agree.

Programming, just like any other skill, can be learnt by practice. Now, it is very helpful if you actually enrol in a course at a university, because they supposedly follow a best way to teach something.

Nevertheless, I do suspect that in order to learn something, you really, *really* need to want it. I mean, you need to make a conscious effort to learn. If after years of studying you just don't "get it", perhaps it's time to realize you just don't have what it takes to do that, and it's best to move to something else, like business school.

My single advice about programming is that it's all about "layers". There are different layers to program, system level (kernel), scripting utilities (bash, perl, python), graphical user interfaces (GTK, Qt), etc.

So define what you want to program and focus on that. Don't try to master the entire programming world, focus!
