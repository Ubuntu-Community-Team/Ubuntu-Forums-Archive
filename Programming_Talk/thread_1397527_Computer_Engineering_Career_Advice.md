### Post by era86 on 2010-02-03
It really comes down to who you know and what people know what your abilities are.  I did an internship at Boeing in my senior year at a uni, but found that I didn't really want to work there based on the type of software I was writing.

Employers will already assume, based on education, what your basic abilities are.  Programming, problem solving, etc.  Those are given.  You  need experience in real-life situations using the tools you've learned (thus, internships).

Once you've got internship experience, you need to find people you know in the industry or connections you have through advisors, teachers, alumni, etc.  Those who are able to randomly find a job after graduating are exceptions to the rule.  The rule being, "it isn't what you know, it's who you know".

### Post by CptPicard on 2010-02-04
> **smdawson said:**
> See, I don't want to be seen as having an "A&C" degree, and I feel like that is pinned on Computer Science degrees.

Oh, come on... I know that anything got called "Computer Science" during the dotcom boom 10 years ago, but "real" CS degree programs do exist. You just need to avoid the universities that turned it into a vocational program.

CS is a strangely unknown major for some weird reason. At its purest, it is applied mathematics in the context of computable functions. It studies the properties of both theoretical models of computation devices, and the properties of problems in order to discover computable solutions to said problems using those (theoretical or real) machines.

At my (and Linus Torvalds') alma mater, we had just one really brief mandatory programming class at the beginning. After that it was expected that you learn to program on your own, so we could get on with the task of hitting the theory.

> If I get a Computer Science degree will I just be a programmer, or some other one trick pony?

The more time passes from graduation, the more I value a CS degree that is not a "programming degree" -- I am most certainly not a one trick pony, as the math, and the understanding of a really wide class of problems one is likely to meet in life, and the ability to automatize their solution, is very, very valuable indeed.

That said, I do suspect that EE/CE is probably the more demanding major on the basic curriculum level -- but one has to understand that it is *engineering* which is different from CS (I do not, for example, really have any sort of understanding of electronics). However, despite the undergrad stuff in CS being probably easier, it can be pushed as far as you like in the applications domains (AI, computer vision, computational biology, robotics, statistical knowledge formation from data...).

Even if you do continue with CE, I would suggest you take some CS on the side. You will be surprised how detached from the machine the algorithmics part is, for example. You will want to be able to actually program something on those machines you build...
