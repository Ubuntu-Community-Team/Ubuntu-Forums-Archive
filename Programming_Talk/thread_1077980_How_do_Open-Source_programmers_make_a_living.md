Not many developers program open source software full time. The general idea of it is that **many** developers spend **a small amount of time** on whatever part of the project interests them the most.

Contrast this to the propietory development model: **few** developers spending a **large portion of their time** on whatever part of the project has been assigned to them.

Basically, open-source is non-committal. This is better for the developers, but not so great for anybody who becomes reliant on the software. However, the beauty is that even if the developers lose interest in a project and let it die, its users can take the code and hire somebody to keep it maintained, or do it themselves.

### Post by nvteighen on 2009-02-23
I don't know how this works. But looking at the Red Hat case, which sells its products to give you official technical support, I guess the idea is to move from the "goods trade" ("I give you an apple for 0.5 * [currency unit]") to the "services trade" ("I give you the right to x for 0.5 * [currency unit]"). What I mean is that in modern markets, you sometimes just trade fictional objects, "services"... for example, when you sign an insurance, you don't get anything but a bunch of rights (and duties)... you never actually see "what" insurance is... This based on the issue of titular propierty ("what I have a right on") opposed to material propierty ("what I hold").

OSS seems to live as a services market. The software is usually free as in "free beer", but if you want to get "services", you have to pay. And some people need specific services... Think about servers or people that need to rely on a professional technical support. 

But also, nothing prevents to sell Open Source Software as in "goods trade". I can write my FreeTruco project and ask for US$560 for downloading it... of course, it will be a really stupid move from me, as the GPL allows anybody to redistribute it for free and therefore, ruining my "strategy" (but, in the other hand, how does the redistributor regain the investment?).

This is a fascinating topic, as it defies the "traditional" ways of marketing with real success.

### Post by eye208 on 2009-02-25
> **Xavieran said:**
> My Dad has been asking me a few questions about how open source programmers make a living, and for once, I can't actually think of the answer.
Obviously they don't live off of sponsorships by major companies
Actually most of them do.

Why do they get paid for producing "free stuff"? Because of the GPL and something called "Game Theory".

Imagine an open source project that is under the BSD license or even in the public domain. If you pay someone to work on that software and improve it, someone else can take the code, include it in a closed-source project and sell it. You paid for it, someone else makes money from it. Would you put more money into such a project? Probably not.

Now imagine an open source project under the GPL. The GPL ensures that any changes and extensions to the project remain open for everyone. The software may not yet fit your specific needs, but it's very close. Now you are facing two alternatives:
[list=1][*]Put some money into the project to make it meet your requirements.
[*]Start your own closed-source project from scratch and reinvent the wheel.[/list]
Which option is less expensive? Option #1 of course. So you invest money in the project, i.e. you employ developers to extend it. Maybe you see other corporates do the same thing and speed up development even more. This is where Game Theory comes in. As long as the payoffs exceed the investments, players are encouraged to increase their investments. That's why e.g. most Linux kernel developers are on corporate payrolls. Very few of them are hobbyists.

IBM, Sun, Novell, Red Hat and other major OSS contributors are not in the business of selling software. They sell hardware and support.

Uh, pardon my logic, but your example above just counts using an existing GPL project, not one from scratch (in which case the closed-source equivalent will garner the same investment and give more return, due to full ownership of the revenue stream).

Also, game theory relies on probability - so an OSS developer *may or may not* get employed by a large corporate.

It's best to look at any examples of full OSS developers that make a living purely from the software they write, which - understanding the nature of OSS - is not possible; they get paid via the service and/or maintenance of the software, if someone chooses to do so apart from themselves.
