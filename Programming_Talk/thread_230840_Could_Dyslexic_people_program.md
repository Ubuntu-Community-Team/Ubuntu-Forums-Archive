### Post by Tomosaur on 2006-08-07
I'm not dyslexic and I'm constantly spelling stuff wrong. Something which will definately help you is a syntax highlighting editor, since you'll be able to tell more easily when you're spelling something the right way. I can imagine that some of the structuring of programs may throw you off if you're dyslexic. If it's a real problem for you to put words into the correct order, then it's quite possible that the way structures are implemented could lead to confusion. I'm not dyslexic, but I do a lot of writing anyway, so I have trouble recognising why what I'm doing is wrong. You have to get out of the 'writing' mindset. It's quite tempting to write  programs like you would a story, and in many cases this works just fine, but when you're getting into more complex programs, you'll frequently find that what you assumed would work fine just leads to hang ups, errors, whatever. I find it very helpful if I stop thinking about my programs in terms of words, but think of it more as a machine. I find myself sitting back from the computer a lot and working things like this through in my head:

'X equals 2 here, and it passes through this function, which multiplies it by a random integer, and then passes it on to this function, which...etc etc etc'. It may sound stupid, but it works a lot of the time, and it helps me out when I'm stuck on a problem. My real hangup is that I HATE documenting anything. I rarely plan anything at all (in life aswell as working), and so when I begin to tackle a problem I usually just have an idea of what I want to accomplish. The actual method of solving the problem will be worked out on-the-fly. I usually write a few implementations of the same thing and see which one works best, then scrap the rest. You seriously don't want to see the mess a lot of my work (not just programming, mind you) ends up in before the final product, which I then modify and tweak until it's clean.

People have this misconception that math plays a huge part in programming. While it would be entirely incorrect of me to say that you don't need math skills, it really is wrong to assume that if you can't do maths, you can't program. Unless your problem is very math specific, such as physics or whatever, then simple algebra should suffice.

And yes, you do need a creative streak. While programming is seen by non-programmers as dull, boring, and geeky, it's more accurate to think of it as an unacknowledged art form. You're **creating** a final product which can, by anyone's standards, appear beautiful, yet underlying what the end user actually sees, is a whole load of code which can't be interpreted by the non-programmer, and is, basically, just reams of text and numbers. In the same way, if you hold a magnifying glass up to a painting, you'll see brush strokes, scratches, pencil marks, all the things the artist has worked through to produce the final piece of work. 

Don't forget that there are tons of programming languages out there, so just find one that's right for you. It may seem a little irritating getting things to work how you want them to, but computers are **really goddamn stupid**, and once you realise that you have to hold their hand through every little thing, working out how to fix a problem is a lot easier.

### Post by Tomosaur on 2006-08-08
I know I'm no expert, but I did do a fairly detailed study of Dyslexia as part of a course I was taking. There are several different degrees/variations, the most _common_ being characterised by the confusion of the ordering of letters and whole words. People with dyslexia are very often misdiagnosed as having attention deficit disorder, due in large part to their inability to process written information 'as it comes'. It is also often confused with certain types of autism, since in many cases the symptoms are similar (inability to focus, difficulty reading/writing, strong tendency towards VK methods in order to learn).

I would also just like to point out the following line from the wikipedia entry you linked to:
> 
Dyslexia is not limited to reversing the order of letters in reading or writing.


_Not limited_. It's just that this happens to be the most common dyslexic trait.
