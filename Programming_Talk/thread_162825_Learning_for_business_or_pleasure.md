### Post by meeki on 2006-04-19
Hi all,

You'll notice I don't post much - I'm more the strong, silent type :-k .

There are always numerous posts in the forum by folk asking which language to use, how to learn programming etc. My question is; are people interested to learn for the sake of learning, a hobby for personal gratification or is it more aimed at learning to put into practice in employment?

The reason I ask is that I come from the (brace yourselves)...Mainframe world; COBOL, CICS, DB2, SAS, JCL........and so on. Over the past 18 months or so, I have learned C#, VB and C++ and introduced myself to HTML, XML and Java; this on top of getting to grips with the joys of Linux (Dapper is now my only OS).

At the beginning, this learning was purely intended as a hobby, but as I learned more, I thought of earning a living in the world of instant gratification that is Gui development (as opposed to the grind of the Mainframe). All to no avail however; no commercial experience means no job; no job means no commercial experience - five years of Mainframe development means nothing.

Now, the point of this hitherto meaningless ramble. If you are planning to learn programming for a living, get yourself on a proper formal course, at college or university; ideally one where you have the option of a commercial placement. Failing that, befriend the owner of a software house. If on the other hand, you are looking simply to enjoy a hobby, dive in, get involved in community projects and enjoy yourselves.

Either way - happy learning.

Ramble over,
Ian.

### Post by rplantz on 2006-04-21
I taught computer science at a university for 21 years. As a previous poster noted, I saw many students who majored in CS because of the money, especially in the 90s. They didn't like it and often weren't very good. Many of them got high-paying jobs in the late 90s, but later lost them.

I've also seen many students who like to use computers and are good at it, but simply do not have the talent or work ethic to learn programming. Unfortunately, their parents are in awe of the kid's ability to use the computer, so they think the kid would do well in computer science. Sort of like advising a good cook to major in chemistry.

On the other side, I worked with minicomputers in the 70s and 80s, before I started teaching. I wanted to move back into industry in the late 90s -- before the down turn. I have all the degrees -- BS, MS, and PhD in EE -- and lots of industry programming experience. I recall applying for one job where they were trying to build a device for recording a special type of electrical brain activity. I had worked with the man who had discovered the technique for six years. Excuse my lack of modesty, but I'm sure that I was one of the most qualified persons for the job. They would not even interview me because I was "over-qualified." That's code for "too old."

Another story. After working at one place for a while, the VP of marketing told me that he originally opposed hiring me because I have a PhD. He thought that I would not be practical enough.

I don't think there is a magic formula that applies to everyone. Do what you like. Be open to opportunities. The best job I ever had resulted from an off-hand comment by a co-worker. I followed up.

presentt, my specialty in grad school was modeling biological systems. During the software design phase of my career, I worked on a CT scanner, a gas chromatograph/mass spectrometer, a device to map out peripheral vision, a frequency analyzer, and a shipping container handling system. (These jobs were in the late 70s - early 80s.) In other words, I did a wide variety of projects.

I think the most important thing is that I was eager to learn about each of the fields I worked in. I seldom sat in a cubicle. I was usually in the lab. The "lab" for the container handling system several times took me onto a ship and up on overhead cranes. I had to write the software to specify the locations where containers could be located on the ship. Nobody was able to answer my questions, even from the blueprints. So I flew to where the ship was docked and climbed around on it until I figured it out.

One suggestion. You might try to locate a geophysics group near you and meet with them. Ask them which they would prefer: CS with a geophysics minor, or the other way.

Bottom line, though, is to do what you like the most. And keep an open mind about learning new things. It's great fun, and the person in the other field will be flattered.
