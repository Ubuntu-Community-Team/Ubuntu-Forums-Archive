### Post by wmcbrine on 2010-03-06
I have to go off on a little rant here. This is not aimed at anyone in particular, since I've seen it used over and over again by different posters. Curiously, I've only ever seen it here in this forum.

What I'm talking about is the use of the word "doubt", mainly in subject lines, to mean "question". Please stop doing this. It's wrong.

I realize that "doubt" could be considered a synonym for "question" in certain contexts, but English can be a subtle thing, and trust me, this is not good English. I also realize that the people doing this are not native English speakers, so I have to ask: Where are you learning this?

Furthermore, even if you were using a more appropriate word, it would still be a mistake. Subject lines have limited space; you want to make the best use of it. The words "question" or "doubt" don't convey any information that isn't conveyed by the question mark ("?"). Plus, just the fact that you're starting a thread at all makes it more likely than not that you're going to ask a question. So don't use the subject line to tell us that you have a question; use it to tell us what the question *is*. The more descriptive you are, the better chance you have of attracting readers who can give you the answer you need.

### Post by CptPicard on 2010-03-07
It comes from India, where apparently this is some sort of usage that gets learned in school. Imagine a kid in a classroom.. "Just one doubt, ma'am..."

I've noticed this phenomenon too... to the guys on the subcontinent I would like to point out that using "doubt" in the fashion that gets used here conveys a degree of *distrust*... and it somehow rubs people the wrong way. If one is "doubting" something, then one should have some underlying reason to assume things are wrong, and to offer criticism to remedy that or to be able to ask for rather specific clarifications. But this is of course not what they actually mean -- they just come across as weirdly passive-aggressive.

### Post by CptPicard on 2010-03-07
Yeah, but these Indian guys don't really doubt the veracity of things based on their experience and perception, they just need help because they don't know something.

"Doubting" is, at least here in a technical realm, slightly aggressive -- you're suggesting someone, something is wrong. That puts the onus on you to elucidate... if someone comes tell me about my code that they have "doubts" about my solution, I'm interested in hearing what they are: there is implied criticism.

In short, it annoys people when someone they don't perceive as being in a position to "doubt" things, starts doubting... and IMO telling those who use this is just for their benefit, as I find myself going into a certain kind of "mode" about a poster when I see this. Yes, I know it's really weird but still...
