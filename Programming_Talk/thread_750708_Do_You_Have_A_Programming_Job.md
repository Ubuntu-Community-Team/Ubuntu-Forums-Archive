### Post by pmasiar on 2008-04-09
I am professional programmer but I do not dress up - in the sense that jeans, t-shirt and sandals is perfect fit.

Programming job is perfect. There are many kinds of programming job:
- in a **big shop making something**, where work of someone else is earning profit, and jor job is expenses, just a standardized replacable cog in a machinery. Bad - stay away if you can.
- in a **small shop making something** else: someone else brings money in, but you are not as easy to replace because you handle many projects and there might be no standards. Little better (at least more fun) but you are not in control what will happen
- **Big company creating software for sale**. This is good, because you are profit center, not expense center. But low risk/low reward scenario.
- **Small company creating software for sale**, or even better - a **startup.** Your daily performance reflects directly in profitability, and you have stake. High risk/high reward, especially if you are in startup.
- **Academia.** It is kinda mixed, because you are like in a big company working for others, but **everyone** is expenses, and big chunk of work is interesting, lots of learning.

While you are young, you should try for a startup. After year you can see if it has a chance to take off or not. If you are lucky, you solve monetary problems early, and later can work for fun. Learn the basics in big company if you have to, but keep eye on startup. It is lots of work (3 years working 80 hour weeks for 20hr/week salary), but reward is worth the risk. Remember, only 1% of startups succeeds - don't get broke while trying to get rich :-)

### Post by dchurch24 on 2008-04-10
dwblas is absolutely spot on.

At one place I used to work (that shall remain nameless!), I would receive a 'spec' (which was often not more than a sheet of paper with more signatures on it than information about what was needed).

One such requests was: "Make a Credit Control system" (honestly, that was it. It was signed off by about 5 people too!)

I would write the code, knowing that I was really writing a prototype.

Give them the *finished* product for 'testing' and then sit back and wait for the whines.

They would moan that it didn't do this or that (things that were never asked for), which I would note down.

By this point I would have a fair idea of what they wanted, and would second guess some of the future feature requests, and start again from scratch.

That way, not only did they get what they wanted, but I would avoid any programming or design faults that I may have made in the first version, resulting in a solid, reliable application.  

It took a little longer, but everyone was happy most of the time with this approach.

I agree that you do have to try to extract from people what it is they want, when they often don't really know.  That's not a critism of them, it's just an observation.

### Post by bfhicks on 2008-04-10
I have been working for a large engineering firm for ~1.5 years. I graduated with a CS degree with a minor in math. 

For my company, I've been working strictly on Reasearch & Development programs. We have many, many, many programs, both small and large.

For the one post about graphics programming, I have done a lot of programming involving image processing. Matlab is my greatest friend as far as implementing an algorithm (we use many PDE's - partial differential equations). As far as my actual job goes, it involves a good bit of reading white papers on various algorithms and methods to solve problems, and then applying them later. All the code is first done (most of the time) in matlab, and then implemented in C++. We deal with image processing and registration of them to the earth, and to do this we use a local API that's around ~4 million lines of code that was all developed within the company.

Like I mentioned before, C++ is the primary language, although we we make use of perl & python as a supplement. One of the biggest obstacles for developing all this software is maintaining cross-platform compatibility. My primary development area consists of a sun machine and windows box, and we have linux box's (32 & 64bit) to allow us to compile and build on all the machines.

The biggest thing I think that helps with my job is feeling comfortable with unix-based systems and C++. We make judicious use of Makefiles to build our baselines which on a sun, take 7hrs, 64bit multi-core linux only about 1hr, though. Also, a knowledge of version control systems and code-management can help greatly also, which is why I encourage everyone to at least know about open-source projects, as this is a great intro into version control and multiple contributors to a common baseline (I was completely new to this once I started and it didn't take too long to figure everything out but prior knowledge will always help).

Finally (sheesh a long post I hate those), the thing you have to decide is if you like being a code junky, or if you want to move up into design or requirements analysis. I have friends that are very very happy just writing code and developing the software, and playing a minor role in the design, but want no part of the business development part of it. I enjoy coding, and working on state of the art technology.

Advice: if you are going to attend college, try to work and get a co-op or internship as this proves to be useful when it comes time to find a job -- if you choose not to attend college, I would suggest improving your knowledge of programming and various languages. Study design patterns and refactoring. There are many books that can help.

python, perl, c++

### Post by skeeterbug on 2008-04-10
**What is it like to have a job programming?**
Like others have said, it depends where you work. My first job was through an internship at a manufacturing company. We had a little cubicle farm for the interns, classic office space stuff. My current job is with a small software startup. Be prepared to fill many roles if you go the startup route (I do sys admin, network admin, support, along with my real title, which is lead dev).

** Do you enjoy it?**
For the most part yes. I am valued where I work and not just a number. My boss is German and loves beer, so every once in a while we have a beer & movie Friday! However multi-tasking all day long really stresses me out, to the point where I almost never want to work on side projects when I get home (something I used to enjoy doing).

**Do you want out?**
No

**What's the basic layout of your day at work? **
We are really laid back here. I get my *** out of bed around 8:30, sometimes closer to 9. I get in to work anywhere between 9 and 9:30. The dress for the development team is casual, so I am always in jeans and a t-shirt or polo shirt. I check my email, get myself a Dr. Pepper, open my rss reader, log in to Trac to see what people have committed, and open my IM's to shoot the **** with the other devs. If the customer has encountered any errors, I will get an email about it from our system before they can even contact support. I almost always have issues resolved when the customer contacts us. Code can/will break, so we need to provide the best support possible. Thats how you can stand out against the bigger companies.

**Do you do freelance programming on the side?**
Not really. I did write guildreport.com as my own side project. I wouldn't mind doing some more projects, however.

Some additional information. When you first start out you might be overwhelmed by an 8 hour day. Don't worry, you will generally never have to sit down and program for 8 hours straight. I try to push for several hours of real coding, the rest can be research and communication.

*EDIT* 
I have been programming professionally for 5 years now.

I've been a professional programmer now for just over 11 years. Almost all of the time working for a company (3 different ones over the course of my career), but I have also done some freelance work.

I'll attempt to answer each of your questions (from my point of view at least):

"What is it like to have a job programming?" The main difference between programming for your own interest and programming for an employer is the sense of freedom in what you do. You can't decide "today I'll rewrite that chunk of code six months ago and see if I can improve it", or "today I'm not in the mood to work on project A so I'll spend some time on B instead" (not strictly true, if you have two pieces of work checked out to you both with the same deadline then you can juggle). The plus side is that you are generally surrounded by other programmers with experience in other areas, and you can bounce ideas off each other.

Working as part of a team also brings its own challenges. You need to keep a vague awareness of what your colleagues on the same project are doing, and where they are at. Sometimes tension arises when one accidentally does something that breaks something you've done, etc, but in a well managed team such things are fairly rare.

"Do you enjoy it?" - I'm lucky to have a human (as opposed to a beaurocratic jobsworth) for a boss, so I enjoy my work. I have the freedom to use the Internet to research solutions etc, and we are all trusted to get on with our work so we are allowed a lot of liberties that some don't get. Fridays are generally more banter than work, unless of course there is a deadline looming in which case we have to just get on with it.

"Do you want out?" - Hmm, I think regardless of your career choice there will be times when you get bored, wish you'd chosen a different path etc. I'm no exception, and I bet you won't be either. I did once work for a company that I didn't like, so I quit and got a job elsewhere. It didn't make me leave the industry though.

"What's the basic layout of your day at work?" - Boot my machine, start Visual Studio, open my project, going and get a cup of tea while waiting for my machine to start responding again (MS stuff is so bloated), get on with my work for a while. Interspersed with visits to the tea dispenser and my lunch break fits in somewhere. The occassional meeting with end-users to discuss requirements, a daily complaint to my boss that I need a new PC because mine is too slow, the odd debate with colleagues about such topics as the origin of the Internet, the latest act of grand incompetence by our government, the early Christian persecution of Eurpoean Pagans and other such nonsense.

"Do you do freelance programming on the side?" - Not any more but have done in the past. It creates too much of a headache. Your client expects you to press a magic button and its all done, they generally think its OK to change the specification AFTER you've implemented the original spec and tested it, and they often try to avoid you when it is time to pay up, so you don't get your money without an argument. At least when you work for an employer on a fixed salary you know what you will get paid each month, and if the spec changes part way through a project, annoying though it is, you know it makes no difference to how much time you have to spend and how much money you'll get paid.

The only thing I would add is this: Use any job as a foot in the door, even if it is not exactly what you want. BUT, don't lose sight of what you want, if you spend too long in an area close to what you want but not exactly, you'll end up just settling there.

Good luck.

### Post by DrMega on 2008-04-12
> **raul_ said:**
> There is a lot more behind software than just coding. Maybe the word programmer is being misused here.

I would definitely agree with you there. I've always worked at companies where the software is developed for in-house use, where I think the approach is somewhat different to that of software houses. I would never go to a software house for one simple reason - I like to do more than just write the code.

Here's the differences as I see them:

IN-HOUSE PROGRAMMING:
* You actually meet the end-users in the flesh. You see how they work, what their requirements are, what is holding them back etc.
* Because of the above point, you can actually understand the importance of deadlines and priorities - they are not just targets that your boss has said you have to meet "or else".
* You are more likely to be involved in the full development life-cycle, from initial analysis, through design, implementation, testing, release, rework.

SOFTWARE HOUSE:
* There will be someone that decides priorities, someone else that does the initial analysis, someone else that does the design, someone else that implements that design, someone else that tests your implementation against the criteria set down in the spec (which comes out of the design stage), and someone else that tells you that you're code has a bug in it, so fix it.
* In this situation, a programmer is just a "coder", no freedom to design the solution, never meets the end-user, might not even see their finished piece of work in production. You might literally be given a UML class diagram, and get told to implement that class, so you never even see it running for real (only in a test setup).
