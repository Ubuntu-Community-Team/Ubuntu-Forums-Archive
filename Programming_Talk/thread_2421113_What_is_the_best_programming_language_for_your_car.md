### Post by TheFu on 2019-06-17
There isn't 1 answer.  It depends on the industry AND greatly depends on your level of skill with the languages.  No programmer gets paid for knowing 1 language in the absence of knowing others.  We all have a toolbox to carry around.  1 language is like a hammer.  Another is like a screw driver. Another, a crescent wrench.  The more tools you have in your toolbox, the more likely you are to be hired to help build a house.

Popular languages aren't always the smartest to become proficient in. After all, if there are 1B Indians learning the same language, if you live it a 1st-world country, you cannot hope to compete where the living expenses are 1/3rd what you need.  Actually, India is having problems competing as their living expenses go up - there's Kenya which is still relatively cheap - about 1/10th the normal living expenses.

THE language you need to know depends on the local employment situation and how capable/willing you are to relocate to somewhere else that needs programmers at any price.

Java has always been heavy, but it definitely hasn't always been popular.  Just depends on the industry.  How many Java games do you play on Ubuntu/Linux/Windows?  I'd bet ZERO is the number.  So if you want to write games, then Java isn't exactly a good choice of language.  But if you want to write Android games, some of the Android-java-variant is required.  High performance gaming on all platforms is a mix of C, C++ and assembly language.  Period.

Being "popular" means lots of programmers chasing lots of jobs, usually for less pay.  Languages like Haskell or R have fewer practitioners, so finding smart programmers in those is harder, so they have to pay more.  But there are fewer jobs. What about Erlang?  Golang?  Perl?

Or, cough, cough, Rust.  Ewwww. I feel dirty now.

If you want an answer - go the the job site for your location and do some research.  10 minutes will answer the question.  Obviously, beginners are paid much less than intermediates. Most programmers are beginners-intermediates, IME.  About 10% make it to expert level over their careers.  Very few programmers actually need to become expert level for most programming work.
