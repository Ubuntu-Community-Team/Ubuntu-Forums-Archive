### Post by soltanis on 2009-08-16
Obviously you can do whatever you want, if you teach yourself the code part of it then more power to you. As I understand it, it's nice to stand out from the crowd by having a math degree as opposed to a CS degree -- especially if you're doing stuff like cryptography or algorithm design.

On the other hand, which one do you really like? I have a friend who is pretty into math and does some computer science on the side -- he actually hates coding, he likes the mathematical theory of computer sciences. There's nothing wrong with this -- but it does have a pretty important effect on your end-goals as to whether you are doing Math over CS because you are more interested in the Math *in* CS.

### Post by lisati on 2009-08-16
It can depend on the kind of coding you're likely to be doing. 

I didn't get beyond first-year statistics/algebra/calculus at university, and what I retained was usually sufficient for the kinds of programming I did when working (mostly basic reports from things like system accounting records showing things like who used the system and which programs crashed, with the occasional maintenance of already-exisiting system tools)

On the other hand, if the kind of coding you'll be doing is of more abstract nature, e.g. the previously mentioned cryptography & algorithm design then there's the possibility that some of the more advanced ideas will be of use.

### Post by Dill on 2009-08-16
You are not your major, and I imagine with enough effort you'd be able to succeed in whatever field you'd like.
