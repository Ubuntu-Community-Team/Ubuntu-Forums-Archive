### Post by CptPicard on 2008-08-14
Now, talking purely from the philosophical point of view, while the old-fashioned concept of copyright has probably served society well, it has taken some odd turns in recent years in the sense of software patents and such.

Newton didn't "trade" his theory of gravity in order to get food on the table, and if his descendants still held some legal rights over the theory, I am not sure if science could have advanced as much as it has since his time. And I also think that Newton would not have become any better a scientist if he had had a stronger financial "motive" -- you just don't make people more creative and/or smarter by pouring money on them. And frankly, a lot of the great insights have come from standing on the shoulders of giants... I am not completely sure that some huge reward strategy is even really justified. You just end up being "blessed" by the ideas that come to you.

Interestingly, in the realm of art, a published work such as a novel is clearly copyrightable. Now... a scientific theory, *as a published work* (think a "representation" of the theory in physical book form), is copyrightable, but the theory itself is not.

The difference seems to be that the scientific theory is informative on a more abstract level than its copyrightable textual explanation. You can't make this kind of a distinction on a novel or a poem. It's an interesting difference that has not given me much peace in mind when considering the morality of immaterial rights :)

Patents are opposite of copyright.

With patent, you have exclusive right to prevent others to create own solution to the same problem, regardless if they had any previous knowledge about your patent or not. ### Post by hod139 on 2008-08-14
With respect to academic books, it's my understanding that the author's don't actually get that much money from them; most of the money goes to the publishers.  After being in college for many years now, I'm of the belief that publishers are very greedy.  For example, is there a need to release updated editions of calculus books every year?  Has calculus changed that much in a year that a new edition is needed.  No (it hasn't changed much in the last 100 years), but many publishers have deals with school's book sellers that they can only sell new editions of books, so students are forced to by a new book instead of using an older edition from previous years. 

The same is true for academic journals.  The cost of a journal subscription is outrageous, and the authors make no money on those publications.  Some journals even prohibit the authors from putting the paper online for free, you must sign a copyright transfer to the journal to have it published in the journal.  Most authors get around this problem by simply putting a previous draft online, that is very close to the final published work.

Lastly, more and more authors are putting their books online for free, and there is also the wikibooks project ([http://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Main_Page](http://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Main_Page)).  The hard part now is determining the good stuff from the crappy stuff.  But that's the chicken and egg problem, since a novice can't tell the good stuff from the bad stuff.

I'll end with a recent quote I recently [read]("http://chronicle.com/wiredcampus/article/3233/professor-proposes-taking-open-education-beyond-posting-course-materials"), "The knowledge is free, the degree will cost you money".
