### Post by schauerlich on 2012-05-02
Why pay a tutor? There are plenty of resources available online to learn Python. If you're really self-taught and a quick learner, you shouldn't have any issues. And besides, a critical part of learning to program is learning how to solve your own problems, and knowing when to ask for help. If you have a personal tutor they may become little more than a crutch. Add to that, most experienced programmers aren't exactly looking to tutor someone... the people who want to do that become CS professors. :)

I recommend using an online tutorial, and using resources like this forum or [Stack Overflow](http://stackoverflow.com/) when you get stuck. I recommend [Learn Python the Hard Way](http://learnpythonthehardway.org/) if you're new to programming. I've heard [Dive Into Python](http://getpython3.com/diveintopython3/) is good for intermediate to advanced programmers who just want to pick up Python itself.

Out of curiosity, why do you want to learn Python?

I don't agree with this part of your quote, but the rest gets a +1.

While programming is certainly a central tool for computer scientists, a Computer Science degree is most definitely not a degree in programming.  CS faculty study the science of computers, they don't tutor students with programming skills.  Logic, algorithms, graph theory, parts of electrical engineering, parts of physics can all be considered to lie under the realm of computer science, just to name a few.

Now on point, I say python is an excellent language for learning general programming and CS ideas.  It's somewhat difficult to dive into solving some graph theory problems in C, but since python has so many built in types, I think it's an excellent language to learn early on.  If you need to learn assembly or C later on, then do it.

There are plenty of reasons to hire a tutor.  There are plenty of resources for learning guitar chords, but you can certainly learn `faster', and hopefully, in the long run, will have a more solid foundation from which to build on if you work with a tutor.

With that being said, most programmers are self taught.

Also, as to your question, "why python?"  See my previous post.  One argument for learning a high level language is to get started with solving more complicated problems at an earlier state.  While writing linked lists in C is challenging the first time you do it, it's not necessarily all that interesting of a problem nowadays.  Of course, the downside is that you may be too far removed from the machine.  At some point, you need to learn about fundamental data types, and python certainly doesn't ask you to do this from the getgo.
