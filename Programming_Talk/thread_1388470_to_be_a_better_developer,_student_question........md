Agreed with everything said in the thread, but being the Lisper I am, I will also point out that this works the other way around too. As the great, late computer scientist E. Dijkstra said, "tools influence our thinking". I probably didn't quite believe it some 5 years ago when I was still mostly a pure pencil and paper CS guy who actually didn't bother to program much ("as soon as I know how it's solved, programming is just work"), but after graduation I actually had more time to actually hack, and it opened up my mind on the programming side...

The differences in languages are not only syntactical candy where you're free to choose whatever language after problem is solved... different languages behave differently enough that they insist on a different view on your problem. This is particularly noticeable on larger programs where you're working on the ill-defined program structure problem as you hack on the code.

Of course it's good to have some prior understanding of how you'd go about the given problem in the possible languages, so you can choose beforehand...

This is why Lisp is good -- it can demonstrate pretty much all other solution strategies in a form where it is easy to see how they relate to other things, and also the very very basic abstractions Lisp is built on... and the rest of languages just seem like corner-cases of Lisp where some set of features is just fixed in place. Messing with it broadens your mind about writing programmatic solutions in general.
