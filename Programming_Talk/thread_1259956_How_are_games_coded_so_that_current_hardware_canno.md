### Post by sp00f3r on 2009-09-07
I wonder if PC game developers have shares in graphics card companies? :-k

PC games have always been stimulating the sales of new computer hardware in general, and the graphics card specifically. And imho that is the second biggest draw-back in PC gaming (the need for windoze being the first biggest). If you want to play the game the way it is advertised / reviewed online or on TV, than you need to invest in new hardware every 4-6 months. You will not have that problem with a console like XBox360 / PS3, but new games on those platforms _will_ improve graphically, because developers learn to squeeze the maximum out of the hardware.

I don't know why games like Crysis (or Farcry a few years ago) are made so that current hardware cannot max them. Maybe Crytek likes to show off their coding abilities with the MS API DirectX? On the other hand, if reviewers scream that a new game features "the best graphics they have ever, ever seen", than that is great advertisement too. A third option might be that the longevity of the game is increased. When I got my X1900XTX with a E6600 CPU, the first thing I did was install Farcry, set the configuration to max and replay half the game again.

Mostly I feel that game developers don't really care if you are able to run the game at max settings. Why should they? They are stimulating the whole PC hardware market with their bleeding edge game, and this will eventually come back to them as increased sales. It's like an upward spiral.

### Post by dribeas on 2009-09-07
Do anyone here remember GeoWorks? Do anyone even heard of it before? They are the live, well dead, proof that tons of developer work dedicated to optimizing code so that it is as fast as it can does not pay off.

GeoWorks was a software suite similar to office, but had its own window management system (did not run on top of windows, but rather plain msdos), multitasking... and all that implemented manually in assembly language (C compilers were not perfect optimizers). The fact is that they build a wonderful piece of software that could run on most not-too-old hardware rather fine. At the same time this other company built an OS and on top of that an office suite. It was slow and heavy in comparison, but they could implement more things in less time. You needed the best hardware money could buy to take even use the applications... nonetheless, MS Office is still the best selling office suite and GeoWorks is a lost tale of failure.

At one point, time to market is more important than the program itself. You can have the best tool that exists in the world and shrink a company. Game developers have just enough time to develop a game. If they try to optimize a little more and another company makes a better looking game that  has stronger hardware requirements, but that can be fulfilled by the time they get to market, what game do you think will get the highest share? Developers must work on the assumption that newer hardware will be more powerful than current hardware is, and get the best user experience they can afford (where afford is with a yet-unknown-hardware platform) praying that when they hit the market, a big share of the people will be able to pay for hardware powerful enough to play the game.

Game market is also special in that sense. An office suite will not make you buy new hardware, but probe around and ask how many people has bought a new computer just to play X game (I did, Call of Duty 4 is the culprit). All companies follow market trends, if people were not to buy newer, more powerful hardware, they would invest more in optimizing the algorithms so that their game can hit a big share of the people, but the fact is that they do not need to adapt to you, YOU will adapt to their requirements.

As a side note, when they hit the market, games must be playable in sufficient graphic quality with current hardware, the fact that they can run better on more expensive hardware is just an add on. Telling the graphics card to work in a 1920x1200 video mode is easy, and if most people can't,... well, just too bad, as far as they can at least play in 1024x786 everything will be fine as it will hit the mass market.
