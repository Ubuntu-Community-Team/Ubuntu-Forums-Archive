### Post by Bodsda on 2011-09-07
My only guideline is that you should try to forget how long it will take you. All of those 'Learn <language> in 24hours' books are a joke. They may be a decent reference, or give you a basic understanding but they lack depth and experience. 
 
Being a programmer, and being a good programmer are two completely different things. The former can be achieved in a few hours (anyone can follow a howto to write a bash script) but the latter _will_ take years, not because it is extremely difficult, but because good programmers learn from their many mistakes, which takes time.
 
Bodsda

### Post by nvteighen on 2011-09-07
People are (as usual) suggesting programming languages. I will do differently, actually closer to what the OP's intention appears to to me to be.

I started programming in the late 90s, using an early 80s computer :P It was a ZX-Spectrum my late grandfather had at his house and that was left there, completely forgotten, after he died. So, one day I found it, in a box, full of old computer magazines, several manuals, tapes with programs, etc. I was quickly amazed by it... I had experience on computers already, quite vast for a 12 years old, having used MS-DOS 5.x, Windows 3.1 and 95 and also Macintosh System 6 and Mac OS 7. So, I set it up and turned it on.

I followed a basic BASIC tutorial (pun intended) there was on my grandad's books. I loved it and created a couple of things on the ZX-Spectrum. Of course, nothing fancy, but I remember I enjoyed it a lot, possibly without knowing very much about what I was doing.

Ok, my family and I moved to another country and I couldn't bring that ZX-Spectrum with me. It's still at my grandmom's house (I will recover it one day; I love that machine). But I wanted to code, I didn't want to use the computer "passively" any longer: I wanted it to do things I told it, as I could with the ZX-Spectrum. I think it was per coincidence that I found QBASIC in Win95's CD... and I loved it too... Again, I did nothing more than stupid games and calculators.

But then Win98 came in and QBASIC was no longer to be found on the system. I remember I regreted it a lot. A year later, something weird happened: we had to install Word 97 or something and for some reason we couldn't buy it, so we resorted to a friend of my father who pirated a whole CD with lots of software. And guess what was there? MS Visual Basic 4.

That was a major point in my "carreer", as it led me to my very first programming project: a Notepad-like text editor. It was quite good for my age: it supported both an ASCII and an RTF mode. Of course, it was written in the usual interface-driven programming style VB-beginners tend to write.. with lots of repetitive code, etc.

A friend of mine had VB 6 and I pirated it too :P VB 6 was great because it had MSDN online documentation, which sorta made me learn how to read API docs (even though the API was crap). Then came that very same friend of mine (who now is an engineer) and told me he found in Visual Studio about another programming language that wasn't Basic and that an uncle of him used professionally... I was intrigued and the language in question was C++.

Another major point in my "carreer", more or less when I was 15 years old... My exposure to C++ only knowing BASIC-derivatives made me lose all interest in programming. For instance, I couldn't install Visual C++ and had to resort to Bloodshed Dev-C++. I essentially learned basic C, with iostream I/O and no pointers... why? Well, because I got easily frustrated by the whole thing. Being used to VB and the whole Windows approach to things, I wanted to write GUIs. And because of all the stories of this uncle of my friend about VB not being professional and C++ being the best thing in the world, instead of returning to where I was comfortable, I just decided to quit programming.

And I was quite disciplined: I didn't program again until I switched to GNU/Linux some four years ago. Well, except for one thing: I learned HTML 4.0, some very basic Javascript and learned about SVG. But essentially I gave up about programming.

As I said, I "came back" aprox. four years ago. I had switched to GNU/Linux mostly because of interest on the platform. For some reason I don't quite remember, perhaps it was the fact that using the command line is a very rudimentary way of programming, I saw myself gaining back interest on programming. Part of this was also that I started college, Philology, and that I had discovered my love for linguistics... so I somehow wanted to understand linguistic properties of programming languages. Short story: I learned about GCC and thought in learning C++ again, but my previous experience with it refrained me to do that: I went for C, instead. Looking in retrospect, it's quite funny, because what I thought I knew about C++ was purely C using cout instead of printf :P

I liked C. I discovered low-level programming with it, having only used high-level languages in the past. Ok, BASIC is not Python nor Lisp, but it is high-level. But paradoxically, and I'm sure slavik will take this as an argument for learning C before anything else, it was through C that I learned about abstraction, at least in a practical sense. The GNU C Tutorial is quite fond of teaching about data structures and splitting up things into small functions and I followed its advice, without knowing too much about why that was important. Slowly, I was realizing that what I did in the past was just baby-steps and several years had to pass in order to me to finally get the fundamental blocks of programming.

I remember quite well how I got into high-level languages. It was on the #ubuntu-programming IRC and it was all CptPicard's fault :P Maybe, pmasiar was also responsible. They helped me realize that higher-level languages reflected much better what I started to see in C regarding the relationship between abstraction, lexical meaning, syntax, etc. I was not the typical C diehard fan who thought that low-level gives power and that everything else is lame... I could understand what he was meaning because I actually had some little previous programming experience in high-level languages. So, Python followed and I loved it, because I could relate to it very well: those who say Python is the new BASIC are right in the sense that it makes teaching easier.

The rest is history: after Python, I learned Scheme, some Perl, C++ (yes, I learned it), Objective-C, some Forth, Common Lisp, some ASM, some Haskell (I should learn more), etc. Not only languages, also APIs: ncurses, GTK+, BSD sockets, CUPS, etc. I watched the SICP videos, read about programming design. Have had projects like the (deceased) FreeTruco, LaRoza's SysRes, PycTacToe, Common Chess, these forums' programming challenges, etc.

I think the point of my story is clear. Do things, code, test them out, learn from your failures and don't frustrate yourself. If you persist, things will make sense sooner or later, but you've got to persist. If you like to program, you'll get the patience to do it, even if you "quit" angered and frustrated for a while like I did.

Yea, I think it really takes about a decade or so to reach that point.

Anyway, I really enjoyed your post.

My story is somewhat similar, in that I actually tried to program a computer when I was a pre-teen (also in the late 90s - you and I are probably in the same age group), but I started out with a standard PC, which ran Windows 98.

So, think of your frustration when you were beginning C++ on windows, and then imagine what it would feel like if *that* was your first programming experience.

My initial frustrations with Dev-C++ on windows left such a bitter taste, that I basically cursed all programming, until, almost six years later, I experienced the blue screen of death (yet again), and thought that maybe I should try ubuntu.

And so, I did, and I learned to play in the shell, and about languages like Python, and later C/++. I wish I started out with C a little earlier, not because it's "the best" language - Haskell holds this prestigious title, clearly - but because C requires a certain level of knowledge about how the machine works - you can go through life as a Python programmer, and never know about the stack, the heap, or that memory is byte ordered (some people will say that this is a good thing, but it kinda bothers me).

In any case: I rediscovered programming as something that was fun, and truly empowering, but I regret that it took so long.

... You were lucky to start with a late 80's machine that ran BASIC.

[quote=ofnuts]I don't think you become a programmer. You are one or not. In 30 years dealing with computers, I've met people with plenty of computer-related academic credentials who were not good programmers, and some very good self-taught ones.[/quote]

There is just no substitute for self-directed experimentation, true.

However, to extrapolate that into "programmers are born" .... I'm not really sure about that one.

I think your experience hints that people who don't experiment beyond the classroom are generally stuck in a rut, where as those who are truly passionate will hack at home, on their own personal projects, and therefore reach a higher level of competence.

In my opinion, you don't have to be "destined to code" in order to became a relatively good programmer. It's really just the 3 p's: Passion, Patience, and Persistence.
