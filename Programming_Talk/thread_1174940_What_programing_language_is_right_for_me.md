### Post by CptPicard on 2009-05-31
> **penguin-of-doom said:**
> that's true. But when you can one language like C++, the other languages are really easy to learn. It's really easy.

Which would mean programming in general is really easy? :) I really do not share the view that C++ is a suitable language for someone just starting out. I have 15 years of hacking behind me, and I *still* find C++ to be somewhat messy and annoying to use a lot of the time (for which I have received my fair share of hate ;) ) .. in particular, the relatively beginner C++ proponents turn out to actually not have used the language for anything substantial, frankly.

Now, I do respect the competence of the known C++ wizards around here, but generally the "w00t C++ is the best language" is a naive myth. The question of "best language" is a very complex topic... please read this thread for example to see what kind of thinking has gone here before on this:

[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=851794](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=851794)

In short, C++ is a language that forces a lot of low-level detail on the programmer in a higher-level wrapper that does not still abstract a lot of stuff away. You need to learn both OOP design and resource management etc at the same time, which can make the learning curve very steep for the beginner. A learner is much better off (IMO) not hitting it all at once but learning most of his programming in a higher level language and then adding the required technical detail on top of that, when it is time.

And if you consider C++ teaching you everything you need to know about programming, I would suggest you look into the concepts of functional programming, Lisp, Prolog, the ideas behind dynamic typing and the genericity it introduces... there are lots of big "programming ideas" outside of static-typed OOP languages such as Java, C# or C++ (which are essentially very similar as core concepts go).
