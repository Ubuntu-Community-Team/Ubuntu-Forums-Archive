### Post by pmdkh on 2008-01-27
What are some good books for learning algorithms?

I know that a lot of people consider Donald Knuth's "The Art of Computer Programming" series to be classic, but I am wary about it because it uses an assembly language (and a fictional one at that), and assembly isn't high on my list of languages to learn (though I plan to one day).

The same applies for Cormen, Rivest,  & Lieserson's "Introduction to Algorithms."

### Post by a9bejo on 2008-01-27
[_The Algorithm Design Manual_](http://www2.toki.or.id/book/AlgDesignManual/) by Skiena is a very good book about algorithms, and there is a free version online.

Many people consider Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs (also known as SICP) as one of the best books of all time in this category ([_free online version_](http://mitpress.mit.edu/sicp/full-text/book/book.html), [_at amazon_](http://www.amazon.com/Structure-Interpretation-Computer-Programs-Engineering/dp/0262011530)).  

Another very good book is [_Artificial Intelligence Programming - Case Studies in Common Lisp_](http://www.amazon.com/Paradigms-Artificial-Intelligence-Programming-Studies/dp/1558601910) from [Peter Norvig](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Norvig).  In this book, Norvig explains Algorithm design by using real world examples from artificial intelligence research (such as a general problem solver or Eliza, natural language processing, expert systems and so on).

### Post by amo-ej1 on 2008-01-27
Knuth's books are highly mathematical and can be out of scope for you. I own two volumes of knuth's work, but I must admit that I haven't had the time to work my way through them. The use of MIX (the assembly language) being the most blocking factor for me. I like to see code (mainly to explain the maths ;-) ), but when you're used to a high level language assembly, or in this case fictional assembly doesn't read that easily.

Sedgwick series of books are indeed classics as well and I would advise you to read those. They have flavours of algorithms in both java, c and  c++ (others as well ?). 

Don't worry about 'not having answers to exercises', you will learn most from reading the text and attempting to solve the exercises than from knowing that the solution is correct ;).

### Post by CptPicard on 2008-01-27
I'd have to vote for the Cormen, et al "Introduction". It's the only Bible on the topic you really need to get started, and frankly, I feel it is a bit on the verbose and even easy side. Works well for both learning book and reference.

I know this is heresy when I say this, but I did not find Knuth's tomes to be as great as they're said to be. It's incredible how he can spend so many pages on something seemingly simple such as linked lists and sorting algorithms...

Also, avoid language-specific "Data Structures in X" style books. You want  to learn in the abstract; I know that for a beginner it might seem comforting to read "real" code, but trust me, it's not the way to go.
