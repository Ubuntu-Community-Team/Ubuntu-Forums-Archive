### Post by pmasiar on 2008-12-30
Go to college but use your time wisely. Ie you might consider to get a financial or business degree with a minor in information technology, or some hard science degree with minor in CompSci. Best one (with most bright future) is IMHO biology/genetics, where CompSci is huge, and will continue for some time - and one reason is that many bio people don't "get" computers, so it gives you competitive advantage. 

Computers are tools used to solve problems in some problem area. To succeed, you need not only to master the tool, but understand the problem area. I would not recommend to focus on computers exclusively, most people solve problems. Yes I know some people write OSes, databases, and compilers, but most people write applications - you you need to understand area.

As a result, you have both understanding the area, BS degree, and learned comp sci - and yes, most comp sci you learn best by yourself. Any BS/BA degree is fine, if you know programming and can prove it by portfolio of projects you participated.

### Post by ankursethi on 2008-12-31
Phew. UbuntuForums is sane, rational and intelligent. The tab next to the one showing this page is a Reddit page full of comments on how a degree is useless. Just because you can learn programming on your own or are "good with computers" doesn't mean you're absolutely the best and don't need to go to college.

College is a chance to -
1. Learn subjects such as computer architecture, discrete math etc. which you'd probably shy away from if you were teaching yourself.
2. Meet new people, go to conferences and build contacts.
3. Learn to teach yourself things that you *know* you should be learning but which your college doesn't cover ;)
4. Beer!
5. Participate in programs like Google's Summer of Code, and get internships. These jobs will allow you to mess up with little or no penalty. Mess up in the real world and you lose your job.

I don't know where you're living, but you might want to consider your financial situation before applying for a degree. Since I live in India, I don't even have to think of finances. My total fee amounts to a measly USD1400 an year (about $5600 for 4 years), but things might be different in your country.
