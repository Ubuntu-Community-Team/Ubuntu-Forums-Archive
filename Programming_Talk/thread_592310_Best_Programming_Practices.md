### Post by rplantz on 2007-10-26
I think the best way is to work with a group of people who are willing to critique your code. In 1984 I wrote some assembly language code for an HP spectrum analyzer as part of my summer job. (I already had over 10 years experience writing assembly language and was teaching it at the university level. So I wasn't a newbie.) During the critique of my code, their only remarks were about comments they did not fully understand. Of course, I fixed them before leaving.

If that is not available to you, pmasiar's advice to work on another project is very good. My first industry programming job was porting an assembly language application from a Data General Nova to Eclipse (a superset of the Nova) in 1977. It was for a CAT scanner. The original code had almost no comments. That's when I learned the value of comments!

BTW, I already had a PhD in EE and had been programming in academia for 15 years. Even with my "credentials" I still had to dig in and get my hands dirty working on other people's code. It paid off. People have often complemented me on my code style. I think I learned it from having to struggle and seeing how not to do it.
