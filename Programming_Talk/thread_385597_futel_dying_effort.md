### Post by Tomosaur on 2007-03-16
I know you probably don't want to hear this, but I'm going to say it anyway (and I mean you no disrespect, I just think the topic has a blindingly obvious answer).

You say this:
> 
i have relized a severe need in this comunity to have users sync thier windows pda to thier linux pc.


when the reality is such that linux users are probably the least likely group of people to use anything from Microsoft. The inability to get Microsoft products working on Linux is a Microsoft problem - they pretty much engineer their products to make inter-operability a totally impossible feat.

Now, I'm sure that many people DO want their Windws PDA to work on Linux, and I wish you all good luck in accomplishing this, but you need to understand that if you're going to switch to a completely different operating system, you will need to make some sacrifices in order to reap the benefits. An inevitable outcome of this is that you should look for hardware which is known to work with your OS. Microsoft / Linux is not one such combination. While Linux does recognise and work perfectly harmoniously with tons of hardware - Microsoft-specific products are a problem, not least because of the outrageous legal issues surrouning them. In all seriousness, there could well be legal notice in the packaging of your PDA, or even on the PDA itself, which says that using the PDA with any operating system other than Windows is a criminal offence, or that it simply won't work, period. Yes, many people simply ignore these issues and create 'illegal' drivers anyway, but Microsoft hates Linux enough as it is, and already likes to threaten Linux developers and users with legal proceedings for 'infringing' their intellectual property. This is a bigger debate which I will not go into here, but I'm sure you can understand.

I'm sure you can appreciate my point: you want Linux to be Windows. If that is the case, then just use Windows. If your PDA requires Windows to work, then you should use Windows. Having Linux support each and every piece of hardware you can throw at it is an amiable goal, but it is just not feasible, and Microsoft will only change their hardware specs again later on, forcing developers to start all over again. If Microsoft starts playing nice, rather than taking their ball and going home, then I'm sure your drivers will appear pretty quickly. However - Microsoft doesn't look like it's going to change its ways one iota (for the time being, at least). In fact, it looks like it wants to make it's products even less interoperable than they already are. MS supports DRM, whereas Linux developers generally find it a greedy, anti-consumer tactic. The WGA (Windows Genuine Advantage) software is a case in point. Microsoft treats its customers with suspicion and derision - I personally can think of nothing worse. Not only do they take your money, they reserve the right to cripple your machine if and when they deem you unworthy. There have been hundreds of cases of WGA reporting 'false positives' - so people with perfectly legal copies of Windows have been accused of piracy. It's only been due to shoddy MS programming that people have found ways around this. It will certainly be a bigger problem when/if Vista is widely adopted.

I don't want you to come off feeling that Linux programmers hate Microsoft or whatever - it's just not the case. Most people use Linux because it suits them better, not because Microsoft drove them to it. I don't LIKE Microsoft, but I don't particularly care whether or not they are destroyed by competition, or whether Linux becomes the more popular OS. It would be nice if that were the case, but either way - I will continue using Linux until a new, better OS comes along and takes my interest. The point I am trying (pretty long-windedly, I know) to make, is that if you are going to buy Microsoft hardware, you better be willing to give them all your loyalty, because it's pretty damn unlikely that that hardware will work on anything other than Windows. If you care about your own freedom to use your hardware in whichever way you please, then the answer is simple: don't buy Microsoft products. You will **not** be in control of what you, or your hardware, does. I know that many people are required to use these PDA devices for work, and that yes, since most businesses currently use Windows - the PDAs used could well be MS PDAs. However, there ARE alternatives, and I would urge anyone in such a position to seek them out. Tell your bosses that you can't perform your work because MS makes horrible products, and that you use Linux at home, anyway. At the very least, they may give you a cheap, or even free, copy of Windows so you can use your PDA. The best outcome would be that they scrap the PDAs in favour of something more friendly. Perhaps you could give him/her a list of PDAs which are known to work on Linux (most hardware comes with Windows drivers anyway, so cross-platform compatible hardware is always a bonus). If these are simply not options, then you have a few existing potential solutions:

1) Try [Wine](http://winehq.org). Wine allows you to run many Windows programs on Linux. It may be possible to install your PDA software/drivers into Wine, and then you can go about happily, problem solved.

2) Try Virtualization. If you have a copy of Windows lying around, you can install it into a virtualization software and run it as you would a normal program. This MAY allow you to use your PDA, but then again, it may not. Virtualization relies on the host OS (in this case, Linux), to allow it to use hardware. If Linux doesn't recognise the PDA at all, you may find it wont work even in virtualization. However - the chances are that Linux is seeing the hardware - it just doesn't know what to do with it. If this is the case, then it may well pass the hardware on to the virtualized Windows anyway. You won't really know until you try it.

3) Use Windows. Many people dual boot precisely because of probems like this. They find that they need to keep Windows around for some things. There's nothing wrong with that, it can just be a little annoying to reboot and launch Windows instead of Linux.

4) Cross your fingers. I'm not against people developing drivers for MS devices - I just don't think it's a realistic expectation, for the reasons outlined above. It's always great to have Linux please a new user, and, of course, to have your hardware work out of the box, but MS products are particularly nasty to get working. I do hope you find a solution, preferably a Linux based one, but the simplest, and less time consuming solution would be to drop the MS PDA and invest in one that is known to work already. Even if it's a Linux PDA, chances are it will work with Windows, because Linux isn't designed to inhibit your enjoyment of it. Maybe someone WILL take up this project, particularly if he/she is experiencing the same problem. I wouldn't hold my breath though.
