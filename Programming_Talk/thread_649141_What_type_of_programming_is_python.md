### Post by CptPicard on 2007-12-24
A declarative language makes you describe the "end result" of your computation. An imperative language explicitly makes you tell *how* to arrive at that result.

My own feeling is that declarative languages may work ok in a specialized context when you can reasonably have a description of your problem domain, but in general purpose languages like Prolog it just ends up being a contrived way to drive whatever solution-seeking mechanism underlies the language's semantics... depth-first search to satisfy formulas in Prolog's case. Programming in Prolog never made me feel like I'm describing my problem ... it's always more like "how do I force this thing to actually do this considering it insists on doing that and that under the hood.."
