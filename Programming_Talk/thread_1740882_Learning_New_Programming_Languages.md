### Post by aphatak on 2011-04-27
Someone once asked P. G. Wodehouse, one of the best humorists the world has seen, "How do you write all these funny stories?"  He is reputed to have replied, "Well, I sit at the typewriter and curse."

The best way to learn a programming language is to load a good IDE, stare at the screen and curse!  On a more serious note, I found the best way was to pick something you want to do and then try and do it in that language - that makes an excellent game.  If you find a feature that you need but do not know how to use, dig the details out of the documentation.  To me, this has a double advantage - one: you learn the features in order of importance to *you*, not the instructor, and two: your research into the feature has an immediate relevance, so it is likely to be more rewarding.  Anyhow, that was how I learnt to write Assembler, and it has worked beautifully for me.

### Post by aphatak on 2011-04-28
I agree wholeheartedly with Java being a good general-purpose language to start with, and to start with a plain text editor.  However, once you start doing something useful, you'll probably find that your programs grow large, they use a lot of classes you (or other people) have defined elsewhere, and you tend to overlook tiny little typing errors that make you want to tear your hair out.

Once you reach that point, you would want to go with a good IDE:  I found NetBeans to be pretty good.

As to the purpose of languages, I think most of us could spend their productive lives with java as their staple diet.  You could always try your hand at C# - you would cross over to the Windows world to do it.

If you grow into a speed demon, or want to write software that has no choice but to run quickly, you might need C - an ancient language, and almost dead like Sanskrit, but it is more like a sports car than a family car - gearshift is manual, so it is not as easy to drive, but you can tear round corners and do cool things like double-declutching and four-wheel drifts in it.

Finally, if you really want to break the sound-barrier, you want to go into assembly language.  It gives you total control, but you are strictly on your own.  You can screw up big time - you can bring your PC to its knees, and you can do it really *fast*.  Assembly language is a lot like a Formula-1 racing car.  It is hellishly difficult to drive, and killingly expensive to maintain, and you have to have the skills of Michael Schumacher if you are not going to kill yourself.  But there is nothing else in the world that will let you go faster than 200 miles per hour on a 2500 cc engine!

### Post by llanitedave on 2011-04-29
One of the things I like about Python is the error messages.  The "Traceback" does exactly what it says, and helps me understand just where my code went wrong.

For those who claim learning a programming language is NOT fun, I have to disagree.  I'm enjoying getting to know Python better, and learning how to create a full GUI application using wxPython.  Yes it's frustrating, yes I curse a lot, but when a wall comes down and I see the solution to a problem, it's pretty exhilarating.

I think the difference is not just to blindly slog through tutorials and examples, but to actually have a project you want to accomplish.  I had a particular application in mind when I decided to learn Python, and I'm developing my application and learning the language at the same time.

I'm sure the resulting code will not be the most professional-looking or well-optimized, but the progress is tangible, and I really enjoy watching it all come together while my skills grow.

At the moment I'm trying to figure out threads in wxPython, and there are a few things I haven't wrapped my mind about yet, but I'm getting there.

Don't let the gloom-and-doomers discourage you.  Yes -- it's fun.

But, maybe I'm doing it wrong.  ;-)
