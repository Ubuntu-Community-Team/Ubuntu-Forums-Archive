### Post by Some Penguin on 2010-03-08
Perhaps Pascal.  It was designed as a teaching language, after all... and is more general-purpose than Logo.  And it's related to the Algol -> C -> etc imperative languages... not a hard shift.

Pascal -> C -> Java is a reasonable progression.  C makes a nice stopover because it encourages you to think about the underlying architecture of a system and what's actually happening, which in turn helps you behave intelligently when programs don't act as you initially expect them to, or the like.  Java is better for when you would prefer not to focus on those details and when you would prefer to have expansive standard libraries, but it doesn't absolve you of having some awareness because those details will still have potentially quite significant effects on performance and capabilities. 

Personally, I find Perl *very* easy to use, and it's one of the ones I have used fairly regularly after learning... but it lets you get away with atrocious programming practices (somewhat, anyway), so you should learn something more rigorous before you go there.

I count at least ten general-purpose high-level languages I've written non-trivial code in -- but I've only used four in the last ten years (C, C++, Perl and Java).

### Post by CptPicard on 2010-03-10
This philosophical difference that we're going to encounter (again) here is a fascinating one. I never managed to see things from this perspective, but I guess people who subscribe to it have their reasons... :)

> **azagaros said:**
> 
I don't know of one that didn't challenge the logic thoughts in you brain.

A programming language is supposed to be a machine-evaluatable description of the thoughts in your brain. Interestingly, all (sufficiently strong) programming languages are theoretically equivalent, so it remains that what the machine *can't* do is upon the programmer to produce *in any case*, and the better the programming language fits that task, the more preferable I would say the programming language is.

> 
  To me programming in c++ is pretty easy.

C++ is regardless, objectively, a "big" language with a lot of detail. Add to that that static-typed OOP tends to result in elaborate "design pattern"-based programming, so there is quite a learning curve in particular for someone who may be a beginner.

> 
I feel most programmers should learn a crude form assembly and envision the code in any of the languages in assembly.  It would help boost performance of most of the code out there.

They should, at some point, but for the life of me I can't help envisioning anything I've ever written primarily in assembly and that being conceptually helpful -- that is, suggesting something I would not have otherwise thought about -- the abstractions are just totally different and much more ... abstract ... than a sequence of small operations on a small amount of variables plus memory. It's actually a remarkably obscure model that, because of its nature, serves as a good compiler target, though.

I would like to turn this on its head -- check out Lisp and see how it gives you a "general programming language" that, in terms of which, you can actually see pretty much any other programming language (yes, even assembly). It is remarkably simple too; the core set of concepts is very minimal, yet abstract. 

When it comes to performance, algorithmic complexity should always be the primary concern. Again, when it comes to assembly, compilers do a much more consistent job at finding the best arrangement of register allocation and so on. Always trying to find efficiency from the metal-level seems like a serious case of premature optimization.

> 
I don't consider Java, Python, or other reference based languages real languages.

They're Turing-complete just fine. I can easily write you a CPU emulator in Python :)

> They loose some of the abstraction the other languages have

I would say they add to the abstraction... they do things more in terms of language-defined abstractions rather than a description of the underlying machine. Some of those abstractions are genuinely completely different from what you have on the hardware level, and are pretty important and strong on the theory-side: consider closures, which seem to be a completely alien thing to most C-style programmers, but are remarkably versatile in a language that has them.

> 
 and are the easy road for new programmers who don't want to learn pointer management.  Pointers and the abstraction layer of most of the programming languages.

I don't really understand. I'm a pretty experienced programmer, and I consider memory management to be mostly tedious so I do it when I have to, but there's nothing of *interest* in it. When it comes to the pointer itself, it's just a reference type... a value that refers to another value, or alternatively, can be seen as an index into an underlying implicit array (the RAM). Once those two views are mastered -- just as well from a higher-level language that operates in terms of symbol-bindings -- the pointer in itself is no big deal. The other way around, it doesn't really bring much to the table.
