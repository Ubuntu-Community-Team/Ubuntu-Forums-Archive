Well, normally in a good economy IT/SE businesses can do pretty well if you have a good product. With the recent downturn people have been investing less in IT and SE, and some people will probably be less inclined to purchase a product. And don't forget that no matter what field you are in, failure is very common.

[http://smallbiztrends.com/2008/05/startup-failure-rates-vary-across-economy.html](http://smallbiztrends.com/2008/05/startup-failure-rates-vary-across-economy.html)

According to that site, which admittedly is a little out of date, suggests that more than half all startups fail.

That being said, remember that on the other end of the spectrum some of the greatest computing firms began in people's garages. Microsoft, apple, google, youtube, facebook, etc. 

I'm no expert, being a college student myself, but this is what I think helps one to succeed at something like this:

1) It helps to try to deliver a small or moderate number of features very well, rather than a whole bunch of features only moderately well. Unless you're the the son of a millionaire you probably won't have the resources to develop super-complex software. With google, it was just a search engine, and that's it. Only after several years were they able to add a whole bunch of features. Get people's attention with a simple but reliable product and then expand.

2) Don't expect to charge people outright for your work, especially as an unknown. Run an open beta with limited features, or provide a limited demo to gain exposure. iShoot, the most popular downloaded app on the iPhone app store, barely registered on the radar until a demo version was released, which caused sales to skyrocket.

For a recent example of startup that is doing very well, look at the Digsby IM client. The application was developed by college students and is now is one of the most popular IM clients.

Just a thought.

I work for a startup of 5 people - but we have a parent company who can help us out from time to time with the admin side of things. We develop a mix of bespoke software and software which is entirely our own idea for the communications and travel industry in the UK. However, we also provide the hardware that the software runs on (mostly stripped down Windows boxes which can be screwed into tiny spaces :P ).

In my experience, freelance websites are not all that great an idea. The reason for this is that the customer is almost always going to go for the lowest bidder (you WILL be undercut, often), and free-lancing in general is a tricky business to be in when you have little or no experience in the industry as a whole. I would suggest you set-up a company which does bespoke software - meaning customers come to you with ideas and you implement them, rather than you hunting customers down. This would allow you to focus on building up a real identity for your business, allow you to work out contracts on your terms, and give you far more control over the design and specifications of the software (generally speaking: customers do not understand that what they want is not what they need - freelancer websites generally tell you to 'do this, don't ask questions', and so you will spend a lot of time creating something which is convoluted and over-complicated, for the minimum amount of money).

### Post by Mirge on 2009-06-03
Having been a start-up in the past (I make decent money, but I'm certainly not far beyond "start-up" status)... running a web development business for about 5-6 years now... been through 2 partners.. yadda.

My #1 piece of advice to you, in addition to the (in my opinion) excellent advice already provided in this thread (I realize it's semi-old now).... is DO YOUR HOMEWORK. Never step foot into a new business venture without having done every last bit of homework. Know exactly what to expect (to the best of your ability anyway). It's the little "GOTCHAS" that can sometimes be the most irritating and difficult to deal with. Plan as much as you can, and be realistic with your goals. Don't expect to be a superstar overnight. Plan your next 3 months, plan your next 6 months, plan your next 3 years... set realistic goals, do your homework, and work extremely hard.
