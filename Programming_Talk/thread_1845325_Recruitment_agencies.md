### Post by JDShu on 2011-09-16
I wanted to get some opinions from the developer community here. Today I got a linkedin message from a person from a recruitment agency that apparently specialized in "software technology markets" saying that they were looking for java and python developers and wanted to talk to me. My profile just says that I am a student studying computer science at a university, and that I know python and java.

I understand that many software development firms don't like recruitment agencies, and that the market for devs is currently pretty open (especially in Boston). So my question is, is it worth responding? In addition, is it bad if I don't respond? Or is this a case of online spamming?

### Post by Senesence on 2011-09-16
> **JDShu said:**
> Or is this a case of online spamming?

Probably.

Although, even if they, as a recruiting agency, seem genuinely interested in you, it's usually not to help you, but to expand their database.

You see, for them, people like you are a commodity that basically represents their value to potential candidates (crappy companies that try to cut everything down to the bone - which explains why they don't have their own HR department, and therefore have to rely on even crappier recruiting agencies).

Generally, these are the people you want to avoid, because they represent companies that you wouldn't want to work for in the first place.

The whole thing seems designed to satisfy corporate interests, while simultaneously marginalizing the individual, which is why I never bothered to get a Linkedin account.

If you're going to network, do it face to face - There are computer groups in your area, I'm sure, be it for programming specifically, or just technology in general. I think it's very interesting that you grouped the Pharma/Insurance companies in that mix, but for slightly different reasons. ;)

Now, yes, there is a layer of highly experienced users for whom this networked arrangement is equally beneficial (as highly skilled workers, they can outsource their job search with a certain level of comfort - benefits for the employer are clearly positive), but the OP is not one of them (as he admits). So, for the OP, it's hard to see the benefits - the benefit for the network is more users, which raises the perceived value of the network.

This network tends to attract other networks, each aiming to raise their perceived value, but by and large, they don't offer anything of substance.

Hence the spam.
