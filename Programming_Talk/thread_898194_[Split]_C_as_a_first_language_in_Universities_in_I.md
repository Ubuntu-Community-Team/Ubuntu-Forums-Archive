### Post by samjh on 2008-08-23
> **LaRoza said:**
> No, I am not questioning their use of C, just wondering why it is so uniform. Perhaps their programs are more unified in their curriculum.

I learnt C at university, but not as part of a basic programming course (it was part of the Intro to Computer Systems, and System Programming subjects, which were in the second year).

Our foundational programming and software engineering courses all used Java (my class was the first to use Java, after the faculty phased out Ada as the introductory teaching language).  Assembly, C, C++, VB, Haskell, Prolog, etc. came later, but most of the time we were required to teach ourselves (lecturers usually gave one or two-lecture crash courses only, and left the rest to students and class tutors).

Interestingly, I learnt Pascal in high school - the last of my generation to do so.

There are a few reasons why C is so suitable:
[LIST=1]
[*]It is a small and relatively simple language.  For anyone with a basic knowledge of computer architecture, C can be learned relatively quickly to high proficiency compared to the likes of C++, Java, and the main .NET languages.
[*]It is reasonably low-level, and forces students to learn how programming languages interact with a computer system, if they are to be proficient at using C.  As CS/IT/SE graduates should all have at least foundational knowledge about computer architecture, teaching C helps to "hit two birds with one stone".
[*]It is widely used in computer and electronics industries, has a well-established standard, many free compilers and other development tools, and many free learning resources.
[*]C forces students to use defensive programming techniques to construct effective software, as it does not "hold hands".  Obviously this is heavily dependent on the course objectives and quality of teaching, but students who are sloppy in C get burnt more severely than those who get sloppy with higher-level languages.
[*]Many network engineering, security, operating systems, and computer systems engineering courses require at least basic knowledge of C or C++.  So teaching C earlier in a degree program helps to remove one stumbling block when students move into those areas of study.
[/LIST]

Having said all that, I disagree with using C to teach **introductory** programming courses.  I think introductory courses should use more abstract languages.  However, I think that teaching C is a very good idea immediately after the introductory programming course is completed.  That way a student will be proficient at abstract problem-solving in their introductory course, and then obtain an understanding of programming from under-the-hood in the follow-up C course.

[quote=LaRoza]One word: textbook.

The professor has one too and should have experience.[/quote]You need more than a teacher with a textbook.  Textbooks can teach you theoretical usage of a programming language, but will not teach you how to use that language in an industrial project efficiently.

The worst teachers of a programming language are not only have bad teaching methods, but demonstrate the subject programming language non-idiomatically.  Those who teach C++ as a dialect of C, is a common example of such rubbish.  If a teacher is in a business of teaching a programming language, they need to be able to teach it idiomatically and in accordance with best industry practice, and you won't often get that while sitting in a university office.

Of all the lecturers I've been taught by, the most knowledgeable lecturers were usually working as consultants in their industry.  They didn't need textbooks, because they wrote their own.
