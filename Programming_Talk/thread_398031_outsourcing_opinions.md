### Post by pmasiar on 2007-03-31
I live in USA and see outsourcing as very controversial: [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outsourcing](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outsourcing) - BTW you want to talk also about  offshoring?

1) company has to care about profits for stockholders first, so they are tempted to outsource to lower cost, even if quality suffers (if decrease in quality is acceptable). So companies *have* to do it. 

2) in terms of free market, protectionism (of other countries) is wrong - unless senator is protecting interest of farmers/manufacturers etc of her own state. Like import tariffs protecting special interest of farmers in important/battleground states: on sugar, orange juice (protecting growers in Florida), ethanol (Iowa), etc. So it is a lot of hypocrisy to go around. 

3) USA has biggest salary gap between average worker and top-level executive, and gap is increasing.

4) companies may underestimate complexities of offshoring: time difference (video-meetings at strange times), security problems, etc (see link).

5) Cultural differences might be big, and training is needed (increasing cost): ie. in China it is not acceptable to disagree with boss, even if boos is obviously wrong. In USA, boss expects worker to warn about the known problems.

3) Little discussed problem is career limits: if tasks requiring junior level of skills are offshored, there is no career path for next generation of senior-level experts - they are getting trained offshore too. Until recently, it was possible to move up one level, making more complex tasks, but soon many of the jobs left which cannot be outsourced will be stuffing Walmart shelves in night shifts and making burgers. And education system in USA is not helping - it is rather bad up to hight school, and after that is very good, but very expensive, and good college is crazy expensive: one year (2007) in Yale costs student $45K - and tuition increases 4-6% a year.

So usual advice for people in IT is make sure your position has plenty of face time with customers, and you understand business - otherwise you are easily replaced by offshore facility. Or go into law and medicine, or into management.

Different kind of outsourcing (inshore) is in federal government and military: outsourced work costs *more* than insourced, but for political reasons it is outsourced anyway, ie. to lower number of federal employees, even if outsourcers' services cost more. Private security contractors in Iraq are making 5-10 times salary of military personnel. Same for contractors working in federal agencies.

Yet another tricky kind of outsourcing/big government trying to influence free market: Federal government spends millions to build irrigation projects in traditionaly "red" states, like potato growers in Idaho, so they can compete with potato growers from "blue" states like Maine, which basically pay federals to help their competitors. I have no problem helping them, but why then those guys talk so loudly about "bad big government" which is helping them? 

Another example is healthcare: private HMS are more expensive than singe-payer system. Drugs in USA are 2-3 times more expensive than same drugs in Canada, even if like 30% of USA employees cannot afford health insurance. So far no riots about that.

So outsourcing/offshoring includes many issues, and most government (except of USA) invest heavily in education of their own people.

Fortunately, with balooning federal deficit, value of dollar will inevitably decrease and outsourcing will make less sense. :-)

IMHO, reasonable people disagree about those issues. Mentioned issues may not be my opinion, just POV worth discussion. Maybe, maybe not - or not here :-)

I don't think many programmers drive technology. Most are just developing rather boring solutions to a problem someone else created.

If outsourcing brings cheaper software then it might be better to have cheaper software than programming jobs.
As something gets cheaper consumption usually increases. Certain business ideas might become viable as software costs are reduced. Existing businesses will be able to use software to improve efficiency where it was previously not viable due to software costs.
Plus there will be jobs created helping companies to consume this increased amount of software.

The manufacturing industry was in a similar position a while ago so might provide some insight into what will happen.
One think it did show is that if there is cheap labor available somewhere else someone will work out how to use it. There where all sorts of horror stories back then, everything from poor quality or completely incorrect parts to finding containers full of scrap and rubble instead of product. Today you don't have to be much of an expert to have something manufactured correctly overseas.
Programming is likely to follow a similar path.

I'm still employed in the manufacturing industry. You need to be good at what you do and also very adaptable. One year you might be working on something the size of a train and a couple of years later on hearing aids small enough to fit in the ear canal. I expect programming jobs may go the same way. People who can quickly produce robust, maintainable code with few bugs will always be in demand. Mediocre programmers that can only write fairly simple business logic in applications architectured by someone else are going to have a much harder time.

### Post by cunawarit on 2007-04-02
I’ve worked on a project that was outsourced; cultural differences can be a problem. I was acting as the user interface expert, and it took me a while to realize than when the programmers said yes it could mean no, perhaps, maybe, or yes. And I had to ask explicitly if they envisaged any problems, otherwise they would just do what they could and not what I expected. 

It is not something that cannot be overcome, but it is something that should be kept in mind every time the work moves to a new country. You need to go out there, and speak to people who have worked with people from that culture so you know a bit about how they think... Otherwise you could suffer bad communication problems.
