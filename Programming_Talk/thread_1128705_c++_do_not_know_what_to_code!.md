### Post by Alterax on 2009-04-18
I think I know where you're coming from.  I used to program quite a bit in C++ a couple of years ago, but I got a job about two years ago programming in PHP (their requirement). As a result, my skills atrophied.

However, being unemployed now, I've had a lot of time on my hands, and have as a result felt the need to sharpen my coding skills.  Here's what I've been doing:

1.  GUI development:  I use the terminal a lot to do simple tasks, but at the same time not everyone understands the terminal.  So I installed Anjuta and the GLADE libraries on my station.  What I do is find a fairly obscure task that I normally do in a shell, and write a GUI application to serve as a frontend for these functions.  One example is decoding video files from my digital video recorder and re-encoding them in ogg formats.  Another is setting up a remote control panel for my server environment.

The standard in the Unix programming mindset is usually never to solve the same problem twice, but what the heck, these are practice runs to me, much like lifting weights or riding around the block.  

2.  Projects Need You:  A substantial portion of Linux utilities are derived from the Free Software Foundation's GNU project.  These are almost without exception volunteer efforts.  [http://savannah.gnu.org](http://savannah.gnu.org) has a listing of these.  And let's not forget the Ubuntu or Debian dev teams--if you can find a bug, track it down yourself, hack a fix, and hand it off to them, you'd not only have their gratitude but that of the rest of us as well.  And to your benefit, volunteer work like this does look good on a resume!

Bottom line is that coding is like any other art.  It's hard for me as a writer to get started or find a subject, especially if I am out of practice.  But if you make an effort to do *something* a couple of times a week, you start recognizing places where your skills could really be useful.

--Alterax

P.S.  Another idea is to check out sites that broker freelance programmers to businesses and individuals in need of custom programs.  I'm not ashamed to say that I've done this for a buck or two in my time, but just looking over the listings are bound to give you some ideas.  And if you can talk the businesses into releasing the code under GPLv3, so much the better!
