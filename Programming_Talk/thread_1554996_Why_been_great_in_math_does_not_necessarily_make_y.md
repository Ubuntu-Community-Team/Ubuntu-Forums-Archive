### Post by GenBattle on 2010-08-17
I think a sound mathematical knowledge can contribute to strong programming knowledge, but it isn't the primary factor. As other people have said, mathematics is a different field to programming. Programming is more about algorithmic development and logic. Maths still involves algorithms and algorithmic development (depending on the type of maths you study) plus logic (mathematical assertions, etc.).

Knowing how computers work, and knowing how to phrase problems in a format that a computer can accept and process is an art in itself. Mathematics does not convey a knowledge of debugging, relational database design, GUI design, library APIs, etc. Likewise, knowing how to program doesn't necessarily convey a knowledge of how to solve for x (but your programming skill is certainly increased by being able to do so).

Knowing maths can contribute to a strong programming knowledge, and knowing programming can help you be a more successful mathematician. Being a good mathematician does not automatically grant you excellent programming ability, and being a good programmer does not guarantee that you are good at maths.

### Post by Zorgoth on 2010-08-17
There is a lot of crossover, but it is just not going to make or break you as a programmer.  Also there are two different kinds of "math" you might be referring to - one is essentially computational skill, not just arithmetic, but algebra and even basic calculus one could learn to do quite adeptly without having a mastery of what a mathematician means by mathematics, which is proving things and thinking in a particular logical way.  The former kind of math is useful in certain areas of programming (e.g. graphics), but will not particularly help you learn to program.  My guess is that if you are not a mathy person yourself (like the kind that does "Theorem (lemma,lemma,lemma.lemma,lemma) Proof"), 90% of the people you would think of as good at math are good at the computational/rote skills that I am talking about here, which are essentially unrelated to programming imo.

The latter, the more logic-based mathematics, is a comparable skill to programming, because particularly in certain fields of mathematics, writing a proof or a complicated derivation requires similar thought processes to writing a program (although the proof is usually much harder to get from each step to the next, and does not have so many loops).  

However, they are not exactly the same thing and even with this, you could be a mathematician/mathematician-like thinker, and still not be good at programming, because they *aren't* exactly the same.  Being a good programmer involves thinking a lot about efficiency, legibility, making sure your code is easy to modify/debug, etc., which unless you are a numerical analyst (in which case you hopefully are a good programmer), are often not essential skills for a purely mathematical problem.  

I definitely know mathematicians who are not exceptional programmers.
