### Post by scooper on 2007-09-14
There is an issue that gets pushed under the table, whether or not free-as-in-beer Open Source software devalues the programming profession.  There has definitely been a downward pressure to salaries in the industry, and after outsourcing, I think freely available high quality software contributes to the problem.  I'm guilty of contributing myself, but have experienced the other side of the coin too.  Just because we enjoy doing something doesn't mean we should not demand proper compensation for it.

I'm not suggesting that sharing software code and effort isn't a good thing.  I just wish more minds were trying to solve the problem of how to keep it as a viable profession and business.  I have a feeling most here will disagree.

### Post by DMills on 2007-09-15
Sure, at least one project I work on competes with a range of commercial offerings in a very limited market (as well as competing with another open source project (well several, but one that matters)....). 

At least in this particular market (Radio Broadcast automation systems), the money is very definitely in the support and customisation rather then in the software itself, and it would be a pretty poor show if the commercial players couldn't beat us to a bloody pulp in that game (The fact that some of them seem to be failing to do so is, if anything, depressing).  

Now I don't program for a living any more, I did, for a while, but suffered a bad case of burn out and needed to go do something else,  and at least in the market I worked in, open source was **NOWHERE** as far as market penetration was concerned (We used the tools, but as far as competing product was concerned, it did not exist), hard realtime safety critical systems (SIL 3 & 4) are almost all custom written for a particular client and in many cases end up having large parts in 'odd' languages (One springs to mind was a mix of 68K assembler, HC12 assembler, C, Simulink Cmex, RTW, with some awk and perl thrown in for the test harnesses).

I suspect that open source will very much take the commodity end of the market for a wide range of systems, but there will always be the interesting niche markets that need skilled people, so I don't really see a looming employment disaster as long as you can adapt to whatever needs writing.  Some of those nice markets are very big indeed (Think Banking and financial services), and employ by at least one estimate, more programmers then work in the entire shrinkwrap software industry.

I don't see a problem.

Regards, Dan.

### Post by scooper on 2007-09-16
I like open source, I use open source, I create and distribute open source programs.  But I don't see how it can be argued that replacing products that cost money with products that are done voluntarily and are free doesn't lower the value of software and its producers.  There's a lot of people out there that just see this stuff as a free candy store, and they don't contribute back. Many of those "people" are large companies.  We are giving away our talents.

I'm probably pushing the point harder than I intend to.  There's an element of frustration that you now have to come up with ingenious business models to make money on simple software products.  I'm trying to build things to sell, but unless I cleverly package it in a web site that can ask for money (rare), or build a gadget, there are very few viable ways to sell PC software for anything but Windows and Mac.

If value is received economics expects value to be paid.  I don't see this happening in a balanced way in open source.  There are very few Linus Torvalds and others who can build a career on reputation alone.  Why should the software market have different expectations for human behavior than the kitchen appliance market?  You build something that someone can't build themselves, it saves them time or effort or increases productivity, and they willingly pay you.

EDIT
I have OSS projects on my resume, and it's only helped a little (so far).
