### Post by lavinog on 2009-06-02
I have been noticing complaints about interfaces for programs being redesigned to be compliant with gnome human interface guidelines.
After spending some time reading them, I got kind of discouraged about gnome development.
I understand the reasoning for many of the guidelines, but I feel like some of them really prevent designers from thinking outside of the box, and really limit a users choice between a bunch of programs that all look and act the same.

A good example is the transmission colored progress bars being removed: [http://forum.transmissionbt.com/viewtopic.php?p=22144#p22144](http://forum.transmissionbt.com/viewtopic.php?p=22144#p22144)

Another limitation is that buttons can't change functions (play, pause, and stop have to be three separate buttons, even if only two should be needed at any given moment)

Is there room for exceptions?  Can a photo viewer ignore guidelines for the visual impaired?  Do audio players have to be accessible for the hearing impaired...etc.
I am not trying to be mean. I don't understand every aspect of accessibility.  It may have been George Carlin that asked: "Why do they put Braille on the drive-through ATM machines?"

What prevents developers from making two different form layouts?
Is there some sort of waiver that permits breaking certain guidelines, while remaining compliant?
How important is being compliant?
If a user redesigns a program to break the guidelines, would this prevent the package from being included in the repository as a alternative package?

Also: Is ubiquity's partitioner considered compliant with the color coded partitions?
