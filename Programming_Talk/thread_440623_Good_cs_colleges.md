### Post by pmasiar on 2007-05-11
> **bluewagon said:**
> I was wondering if anyone had a link/list of known CS colleges, as its its that time for me to find my college choices, yet i have been unable to discover any colleges

it depends on your budget. Can you afford to pay like 40K a year for top college like MIT? Even if they most likely will *not* admit you - rate is about 10% or so.

Go to your city public library and ask friendly librarian about book with list of colleges in your region. Makes sense to go to your state's university - as tax payer you have discount. Don't worry too much (and don't pend too much money) on your BS degree - but during studies, try to impress by good results, publication, to get scholarship for MS/PhD - and then the big name is more important than your BS degree.

Also, think about double major in something: computer science is oxymoron, computer is just a tool like anything else. There is no "microscope science". Some scientists I know are extremely good programmers. It is important to know the problem area with tasks you are solving. Unless you hack kernel, X, GCC or MySQL, there is not much pure CS to work - you are solving problems from some non-CS area, and you have better chances for career if you know that area. Biology and life sciences look like next internet. Lots of interesting problems and lots of money ready to change hands for real solution.

Many private colleges offset the outrageous costs with scholarships.  Many scholarships are merit based, but others are not.  If you are a minority student, there are a many scholarships available for minorities entering the sciences.

> 
Go to your city public library and ask friendly librarian about book with list of colleges in your region. 
If you don't mind spending the money, you can also pay for the US News rankings: [http://www.usnews.com/usnews/edu/college/rankings/rankindex_brief.php](http://www.usnews.com/usnews/edu/college/rankings/rankindex_brief.php)

> 
Makes sense to go to your state's university - as tax payer you have discount. Don't worry too much (and don't pend too much money) on your BS degree
There is some truth to this, but don't think you shouldn't apply to some big name private schools hoping for scholarships.

> 
 but during studies, try to impress by good results, publication, to get scholarship for MS/PhD - and then the big name is more important than your BS degree.
I cannot emphasize the first part enough.  Your junior and senior years (sophomore if you are lucky) you should do your best to convince your professors you are good enough for an undergraduate research program.  Doing a good job and getting strong letters of recommendation go much further when applying to grad school than your GPA or GRE scores.  I have two undergrads working with me this summer, and if they do a good job they will get strong letters from myself and my advisor.  

If you enter a Ph.D program after graduating you do not have to worry about scholarships, your tuition will be covered and you will get a stipend either as a teaching assistant or as a research assistant.  Masters students are typically covered by companies.

>

Computation biology and the "nano-sciences" are up and coming fields, so having a dual major in biology or chemistry could give you a leg up there.  Physics and Math are two other majors the complement the CS degree well (Math complements any degree well I suppose).
