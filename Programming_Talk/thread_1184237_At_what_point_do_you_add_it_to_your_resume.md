### Post by rplantz on 2009-06-14
> **slavik said:**
> if you can talk about it, put it on.

This is really good advice.

Keep in mind that many (perhaps most) interviewers are not good at interviewing. If an interview does not go well, it may not be your fault.

I'm 70 and have been through lots of job interviews. (Retired now.) Few interviewers ask good questions. I recall one good interview. It was for a contracting job (in 1979) doing assembly language programming. There were two interviewers. They asked if I knew assembly language for the machine they were using (IBM Series 1). I said, "No, but I can learn it very quickly." One of the guys said, "Okay, we'll believe you. Now, think of the best project you have done. Tell us how you approached the solution." About half way through my discussion they stopped me and asked if I could start tomorrow.

The problem with most interviewers is that they are too focused on the details of their own project. They ask the candidate questions about that. Not many know how to ask if you can solve problems.

Another important point. Remember that *you* are also interviewing *them*. What are you looking for in a job? Do you want to expand your experience? How will they support that? Etc.... It's a two-way street. You'll spend lots of time there, so it should be something that is positive for you.

### Post by jpkotta on 2009-06-14
Try to include "soft skills", stuff that applies to any job anywhere.  Like "learned to work in a team on project such and such by doing this and that".  

I hate resumes because they're so subjective.  When I was in school, I took mine to a resume critique service offered by the school.  The guy gave sensible advice, so I made the changes and brought it back, to a different guy.  He proceeded to tell me completely different things, most of which contradicted the advice of the first guy.  It really depends on the person reading it, and there's no way to know what they consider a good resume.

### Post by n4mgr on 2009-06-14
In my own personal opinion, if you have working knowledge of a subject such as programing, networking, Ubuntu installation and/or admin tasks then it would only benefit you to include that information on a resume. To withhold that, whether you have a piece of paper to prove it or not is only doing a disservice to you and possibly the people you will be working for.
