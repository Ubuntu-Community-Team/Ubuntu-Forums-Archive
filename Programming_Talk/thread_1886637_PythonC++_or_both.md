### Post by CptPicard on 2011-11-26
Oh, cool, a direct claim of programming language difficulty/programmer capability as a hierarchy where lower-level is more complex, hence better. I haven't seen those stated so explicitly for years here ;) 

I do understand that if people have worked in something like embedded devices, they will likely think the hardware-level is something to particularly "aspire to"; it's only natural I suppose. If I am allowed to be blunt as well (as that is such a nice turn of phrase to assume superiority), subscribing to the idea that assembly is inherently most demanding and therefore represents the pinnacle of general programming just gives me the feeling the person making these kinds of claims does not think of programming on a particularly abstract level (abstract being a good "understanding the overall big picture" kind of abstract, not the "hiding details into libraries" kind of abstract). 

It's the [Blub Paradox ]("http://www.paulgraham.com/avg.html") -- you don't know what you don't know, therefore you can't appreciate its effect on your thinking. Mind you, I'm not "against" learning lower-level languages and sometimes they even contribute something to my understanding of some machine-specific property of an implementation, but it seems to me the LLL guys are just way more prone to this error in reasoning.

When someone is just starting out, it should definitely be Python first, then C++ (or something else) if not for any other reason but the beneficial learning curve. Of course, Python serves as a nice, fast introduction to all kinds of programming conceptuals that you'll find hard to get to or don't really quite exist in C++. Being able to program in Python therefore makes you think "programmatically" very fast and gives you a good springboard to other languages in multiple paradigms. Even to C++, where, after Python, you'll just have to learn the many quirks that would have slowed you down if you had started the other way around, plus maybe some static-typed OOP-design, a good hint of which you will have gained already by implicitly constructing your interfaces using dynamic typing in Python. In C++ you'll just have to spell them out.

The issue with hardware-level stuff is that it's comparable to J2EE in its kind of complexity -- you may be an expert in the libraries and the architecture and the details, but you're dealing with the problems with that particular domain... 

And btw, the best programmers with the greatest brainpower I've ever met have mostly been Lisp enthusiasts. The language's minimalism yet abstraction liberates from needless garbage that they've grown tired with, yet they get to very freely express their ideas in a language that is remarkably flexible.

### Post by gardnan on 2011-11-26
To the OP:

I would think about your proficiency with computing and the basic concepts of programming. 

If you have very little experience with computing overall, then I would recommend Python to start out, to get you started in thinking like a programmer.

 However, if you are more computer proficient and maybe have done a little programming already like you said, then I would want to ask you what you want to program.  

If you want to go into something like web development, I would choose Python, as many web based languages have similar features and paradigms. If you want to go into something more like general application development, then I would go with C++.

To the people arguing over Low-Level Languages:

Alright, there are really two sides of programming. There is the technical low-level side, and the high-level abstract side. Now, I would argue that they are both equally important, and that every programmer should aspire to each. 

I think that the reason that the abstract side gets a bad rap by many programmers is that, due to languages that make programming 'easy' like Python, there are many inexperienced programmers that are learning the higher-level language. 

However, I would argue that the abstract stuff is just as difficult to comprehend as the low level stuff. Take something like Lisp for example. Lisp is probably the most 'academic' language ever made, developed by mathematicians at Harvard and MIT (Richard Stallman took part in its development, and is one of Lisp's greatest fans), and yet it is very abstract and can be *very *hard to understand. 

You see, programming has a great overlap with both math and engineering.  Math is very abstract, while engineering is very practical.  

Just think of it this way. There may be someone who has totally mastered x86 assembly and memorized every detail of the 386 architecture, but can't implement a binary search, quicksort, or Dijkstra's algorithm to save their life. That person would make a terrible programmer. On the other side of the spectrum, there could be a mathematician that could design a brilliant algorithm in the abstract, but could become completely lost talking about conditional jumps and cache misses. That person would also be a terrible programmer.

Programming is a mixture of the two fields, and I think it is a mistake to think that one side is better than the other. The two can coexist peacefully, and I think that I can be an example, as my favorite languages are C, Lisp, and Javascript.

Level 4: C, C++, database design, good shell scripts
Level 5: Assembler, advanced C/C++ (systems programming, compilers...), multithreading
Level 6: Kernel programming, file systems
Level 7: You're Linus Torvalds, Richard Stallman or the ghost of Dennis Ritchie
 
Peter's principle doesn't apply, unlike management, people rarely go  higher than their competence level :) but I find that most people reach  their competence level rather quickly.

People who master anything at level N will usually master other stuff at the same level  without much difficulty, not speaking of anything at the lower levels. For instance, even in Java project, if there is multi-threading stuff to do, I'll try to find some "Level 5" and look for C/C++ in resumes.

So, my net answer: evaluate your level and aim for it.

(*) yes, your pet language (Lisp, Haskell, Lua, Prolog, APL, Rexx..) isn't in the list but I bet something else you can do is.

People think in different ways, some people will find low-level programming much simper if they expect to have to tell the machine precisely what to do, rather than telling the machine what you want to achieve and letting it decide how to compute the problem. Just because you may find it simpler to learn or use high level languages does not make them 'easy'.

That requires a completely different mindset to achieve when compared to writing device drivers for example.
