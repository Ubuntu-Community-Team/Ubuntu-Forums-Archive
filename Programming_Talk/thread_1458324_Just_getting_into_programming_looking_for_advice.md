### Post by Cracauer on 2010-04-19
Uhh, don't choose a MMORPG as your first project :)

There's no "C/C++". C is a small fast language lacking features that needs a lot of crafting to be used in large projects (such as the Linux kernel). C++ is a massive language that requires full-time learning and constantly updating and swapping in. Neither of them is a great first language if your goal is higher level application programming.

If you are seriously interesting in game programming then don't pick a language. What you want to do is pick libraries and reusable engines that seem to suit your needs - and then use whatever language they use.

### Post by 1clue on 2010-04-20
I'm a professional programmer.

Any time somebody says, 'what language?' I ask, 'what project?'  Every language has both strengths and weaknesses.  Every project has a language or languages that best suit that project.

When you say you want to do gaming, I want to ask how much math you have?  If you're not comfortable with calculus and physics and multi-dimensional vector math, then you're not up to 3d gaming.  Gaming is some of the most challenging programming you can do, and the industry is so competitive nobody gets any money for it.

Nothing will prepare you like a formal education.  There are good programmers out there who don't have college for it, but they tend to be disorganized and difficult to work with IMO.

If you can't or won't do that, then here's how I'd go about it:

Find a project you're interested in, one that already exists.  Download the source and join (or at least lurk on) their developer forum.  When people ask questions, go to that part of the code and look at it, and make sure you know what's going on.  If you don't like the people, then go find another project.  If you don't like the application, you probably won't fit well either.

This is a huge thing.  If the community is not good, then their product sucks.  Every time.  It's true for Open Source, and it's true for any commercial venture you might find.  If you don't like the project, then don't join the community.  Any decent software project uses a philosophy in designing their software, and if you don't like the product you probably will get in a lot of arguments.  Human nature and politics is a huge part of any project even if its members don't think so.  Why torture yourself?

The community defines the project.  The project defines the product.  If the people can't collaborate effectively, and/or if they're not well organized, then the product can't be good.  The end.

Start learning whatever languages they use.  There are free tutorials for just about any language you might want, but I strongly recommend you get a good book on it.  Ask the members of your project's forum about the books they use.  Work through the tutorial, and keep looking at your chosen project as an example.

The people on the forum might not have time to walk you through things you would be able to find in a book, and they certainly won't teach you how to write software.  However they will probably help you understand what's wrong with your code, and if you're obviously working and making best use of info someone will probably mentor you.
