### Post by ju2wheels on 2011-12-03
I can tell you being graduated already and also 2 years working post grad, that you should really do whichever you think will make you happiest in the long run. Dont put yourself in a position where you will end up doing work you dont like because it will just make you unmotivated and leave you hating your job.

Contrary to what has been said, if your job is your career, you should be able to expect intellectual enjoyment, thats why you spent those years paying for good education (thats the difference between learning to be a McDonalds cashier and learning to be a systems admin or developer).

I would do option 1 while also doing option 2 on the side so you can have both skill sets, still be doing something you like better than being an analyst, and have the choice to change later and take those skills with you. Things like COBOL arent in high demand, but the few that do need will sure be paying top dollar for it because of it.

### Post by ofnuts on 2011-12-03
> (1) Company is willing to train me as a mainframe programmer (COBOL, CICS, Assembler, JCL, etc.).This would be a very long term investment for you. You are going to have to deal with very boring mainframe stuff, but in 20 years or so, you'll be the only one left to take care of all these machines and will be able to ask for top money. But will there still be mainframes?

> (2) Train myself in JAVA/J2EE framework by taking series of courses on my own time in night classes at some University.If you have a MSc in CS you don't need formal courses. Get a book about each, and read it. This isn't rocket science. These frameworks are used because Joe Averageprog can understand them. And the fashionable frameworks change every couple of years, so there are really two kinds of people, those that can follow by learning new stuff on the job, and those that are stuck to what they learned when they got their degrees, and morph within 5 years into business analysts or project managers (there is a small intermediate category of people who can get up to date with some formal retraining, but they don't always get it). In large projects, no-one masters the whole thing, and there are multiple technical roles: architect, product specialist, DB specialist/admin, developer, build & deployment experts... You don't need to be good at everything, and with an MSc you can become good at anything thrown at you.

> (3) Stick with current role and train as a business analyst (with ITIL v3 framework) and then jump to a business analyst role.If you are in Canada, this is the last job in the chain to be contracted offshore, because it requires a lot of local contact. On the other hand, my personal experience is that this job is a relic of the past when a programmer would spend most of his time coding (you had to write you own sorted sets, linked lists, etc...). With modern languages, concepts have direct code equivalents, so the hard part is understanding the actual requirements and actual coding is at most one third of the job. So as a business analyst you end up writing specs that could almost compile, without the intellectual satisfaction of creating something that works, and with the potential frustration of seeing your brain child butchered overseas. 

And remember, a fourth possibility is to try to get out of the business-related programming...
