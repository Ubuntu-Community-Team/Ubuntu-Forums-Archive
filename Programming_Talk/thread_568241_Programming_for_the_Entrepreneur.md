### Post by CptPicard on 2007-10-05
It really depends. I probably wouldn't encourage anyone to take up an independent consultancy without any forward knowledge of what exactly you are going to be doing. Software is for the most part a team effort. There is just so much plain raw work in it that you want to be working with others, unless you're remarkably good in *everything* that needs to be done.

I, on the other hand, am a bit of an exception...I am "employed" in a really weird situation -- I am a CS "theorist" who would probably get his *** kicked by kids five years his younger in the real world industry. Then again, I wouldn't bother coding just "anything" just for the money... it would bore me to tears and would perhaps even force me to switch fields.

My situation is such that while I was being bogged down with my Master's thesis, I met this guy who later became a good friend of mine. He has a business he is remarkably good at, but he needs a number-cruncher and someone who can automate his processes to expand it. So I was essentially a gift from heaven for him.

So... I am now the only other person in the world who actually knows his business secrets, and I have been able to code stuff for him that has expanded his business about 10 times of what it used to be by automating things he used to do by Excel and email(!). I am completely irreplaceable, and can actually even afford to be a bit sloppy in my coding... after all, the only person who is ever going to modify the code is me. ;) He is happy with command-line tools and is even starting to see the wisdom in them, which is great...

Of course, I don't want to be completely tied up with him, so I have my own dotcom project going, but it's more of a hobby project in case my work for him stops... but I am not seeing that anytime soon, unless the "boss" decides to retire to Spain for good and enjoy his wealth.. 

But, the moral of the story really is that you're probably better of working for some company unless you really know what you're doing. The downside is that most of the stuff that is being done in companies is so boring nobody with half an active brain would want to get involved...

### Post by dwhitney67 on 2007-10-06
Anybody who can learning English or any other spoken language should have no difficulty in learning a simple programming language which has only very few key-words.  In fact, all programming languages are very simplistic and easy to learn.  Thus it is safe to say that programmers are "a dime a dozen".

However, many companies do not just want programmers.  They want folks who can also engineer (i.e. design) new software applications.  Not everybody is cut out to be a designer.

If you have the experience with designing/architecting systems and know a popular language (C, C++, Java), then there are numerous opportunities in the US that will pay in excess of "6-figures".  Many of these opportunities are hourly positions that may last from 1 to 6 months.

If you are willing to travel to locations far from "home", then you would have no trouble whatsoever getting a job.  You can represent yourself, set your pay rate, hours, etc.

For those who lack real-world experience, regardless of their education credentials, may find it hard to land a job, much less a good job, that pays well.  Until you have the experience and the track record to prove it, being an entrepreneur might as well be a pipe dream.  (Someone who is incompetent can be detected very easily!).

P.S.  I am not a programmer.  I am a software engineer with a degree in Computer Engineering.  Programmers get their education from home, a book, or from an "underwater basket weaving" program at the university level.  An engineer has a different level of education that focuses on more than just the programming aspects of computers.  The understanding of OSes, hardware, system performance, project requirements, etc. all play into this education.
