### Post by ICEcoffee on 2007-11-28
I have years of experience dealing with small business startups (here in the UK), and the best advice I can give you is:

Ask the prospective client to detail exactly what he is after. This is important, because most startups have NO IDEA, they just want a website. You need to sit down with him/her and establish exactly what he/she wants.

Once this is established, you should have a fairly good idea what will be involved and how much time it will take you to do this (don't under estimate the time), and charge accordingly, either £'s per page or £'s per hour. Most startups feel more comfortable with the £'s per page because it is a known quantity for the get-go.  

If for example he want 8 pages of basic html, you might charge say £30 per page, which is very cheap, but you can't charge experienced professional rates, and he should be happy with that.

Always define a limit to waht you wil do for x amount of pounds, so you don't run over on the expectations of your client.

### Post by smartbei on 2007-11-28
I would like to second ICEcoffee with his advice: It is truly extremely important to hammer out all the details with the client, otherwise you would be giving a price quote to something ambiguous. Then, (since you say you have some experiance building websites), estimate the amount of time it will take you. Estimate how much an hour of your work is worth. Now, multiply hours*worth*[1.5 or so]. Also, think about hosting charges, etc. If they are on you for a certain period of time, add that as well. Lastly, I suggest you get a portion of the payment ahead of time, or at some previously defined point in the middle of the project, so as to garauntee that your work will be paid promptly. This should happen even if this person is a friend of yours.

Still, taking all of the above into account, this is your first time with it and it may be better to go a little cheaper, since for both you and the client it is the first time working with someone on a project like this, and it is important that both sides view it in the end as a profitable arrangement.

Good job on getting your first technology-related job!

### Post by ThinkBuntu on 2007-11-28
I run a web design business and freelance for a living. How much you charge depends on a few things: How good you are, how quick you are, and market rates. I charge $35 per hour which is fairly reasonable. It pays the bills, I'm a quick worker, but I'm not going to blow anyone away with my designs.

One piece of advice: In this industry there is such thing as being nice (good customer service, solid advice, etc.) but never, ever be nice with how you bill your client or what work you do. Always report your hours accurately and only go over the top if you're worried you're about to lose a valuable client, or if you're very new and you need a foot in the door.

One thing you should never do is charge by the page. If you want to go on a per-project basis, itemize expenses and make sure it's weighted towards where the real work is done: Planning, design, and templates. Then charge hourly for building the pages, revisions, etc.

Can you show me examples of you work? I'll try to help you out with how much you should be charging. PM me if interested.

### Post by KCPokes on 2007-11-29
That always seems to be the catch.  People assume its just a website, how hard could it be and how much could it really cost?  The trick I've ran into, dealing with other design companies, is that some firms are really good at design but not so good at anything more then presentation.  They can deliver the "flash" but it lacks actual substance.  Anything that requires backend processing, database work, etc..., they are over their heads and subcontract it out.  People want a replica of a huge site without understanding what is involved.

As was stated before, define the requirements thoroughly first.  Define what will be involved in each and estimate the number of hours it would take you to accomplish each.  Remember that there is a limit to how much the client should have to pay for you to learn, so it does become a judgement call on the hours.  You should be able to provide a total cost figure to the client before any work starts.  

Something I learned quickly, the hard way, is that regardless of how well you know them, secure funds up front (even if its just half).  I have a site design completed that I've yet to collect any money for due to the fact that the company ran into issues (of the natural sort) and is now struggling to rebuild.  Just be sure to cover yourself before you get too deep.
