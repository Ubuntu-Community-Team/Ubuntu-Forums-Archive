### Post by uOpt on 2010-05-27
I usually think in data structures. I decide which structures and I need and how they are connected.

And I "code" from minute one, as in building something that does something with those data structures. Usually getting the data from outside into those structures. Build the whole layout that I will then later do the actual work on.

Most of that mixed advanced planning is useless. You discover too many things not compatible with your original "out of thin air" plan when you actually hack things in. Not only is the planning time wasted at that point, there is a mental problem in that the investment made into your plan might make you stick with a plan longer than it is useful. That drift might be conscious or not conscious. 

Of course that accelerates - the more you follow along with a sub-optimal or outright bogus plan, the more investment you have in there and the stronger the urge to stick with it, getting you deeper into the bog. That's bad enough, but if you have stated your plan to anybody else there's not only your own perceived investment, there is also the aspect of losing face by admitting that it was bogus. This is one of the major reasons why managers shouldn't ask too detailed questions about ongoing projects, and why daily meetings about how everybody's own code developed yesterday are stupid.

Code speaks.
