### Post by pmasiar on 2006-12-10
This discussion started as my off-topic comment #5 in [this thread about OpenGL]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=316074"). Summary: we agreed that wikipedia is a safe start in most cases, but not perfect, because you can have bad luck reading article with bogus information, seconds before it was fixed. 

Obviously risk is higher with more obscure articles (nobody cares to double-check and fix it), or more emotionally loaded, like best IDE, editor, language, or operating system. :-) Wikipedians have rules of conduct for handling edit wars, but all humans are fallible.

I decided to re-post it as separate thread, maybe some people want to comment who missed original thread, or may learn by reading it. Before commenting, please read previous discussion in above thread, and maybe follow links to original wiki (C2) - you may be in for a surprise.

### Post by pmasiar on 2006-12-12
> **hod139 said:**
>  Where did you get this statistic, wikipedia :rolleyes:?  Seriously though, I really doubt 1 in 20 is bogus.

Absolutely! 1 out of 20 is as bogus as claims that wikipedia articles are unreliable. I read article about comparing errors in wikipedia and Britannica (linked from wikipedia of course :-) ), about the same number of errors - but wikipedia is much younger, and evolves faster (and errors are fixed faster).

1 out of 20 (5%) is based on term [confidence interval]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_interval"). Standard science is done with 95% CI. Are 5% of the results flawed? Possibly. Is 100% correct? We *know* all 100% cannot be true. How we check flaws? Repeat experiment, and if results are not repeatable, its not science. How do you fix wikipedia? Many people will read, check, and fix.

BTW for really funny bogus facts, visit [uncyclopedia]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncyclopedia")

The big problem is that many of these authoritative sources  (i.e. peer-reviewed journals) are not free, and unless you are at an academic institution that pays the large subscription costs this information is out of your reach.  I've always felt that this information should be free, but the journal publishers claim that it takes money to have this peer review process and having these journals/proceeding freely available would be detrimental to quality.  Some publishers won't allow me to post an accepted paper on my website. (You'll notice that one of my papers is a link to the book containing the paper, not the actual paper).  If anyone asked me by email for the paper I would send it to them, but the publishers make it difficult.  

I congratulate wikipedia for filling in this gap, but I think the ultimate solution is convincing the publishers to release these journals for free to the public.
