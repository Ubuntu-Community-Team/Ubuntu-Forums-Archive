### Post by hod139 on 2006-12-20
I'm of the opinion the more math the better.  Vector calculus (also known as multi-variate calculus) is used in graphics, robotics, dynamics, etc.  Until you start hitting some of the _very_ theoretical math (e.g. abstract algebra, variational inequalities, homology), you will find your math knowledge very useful.   Even some of the more theoretical studies are being applied; variational inequalities are starting to get applied in operations research, game theory, and even robotics.  Homology is also showing up in robotics for solving the motion planning problem.   Plus, having a math degree always helps on your resume, as it shows you can think abstractly and solve problems.

### Post by pmasiar on 2006-12-20
> **YourFriendlyGopher said:**
> The first one looks as if it has to do with the internet, designing web sites and learning Javascript etc...maybe I could just learn Javascript, XML etc on my own? 

It helps to have solid foundations in algorithms and data structures. Languages are not important - they change, and they are easy to pick up. (3rd and 4th are easier than 2nd).

First class is introduction to application development. Next step would be to learn databases. This would be my choice (because I develop apps for living). Applications are exciting, but you also need to learn industry for which you will write applications. It is very different to write applications for travel agency (hotel booking), loan management, financial accounting, and/or biomedical research. In every area, you need to learn what they do, how they call things, what problems they have and how they solve them. 

There are many other kinds of programmers: embedded systems. Drivers. Someone has to program air traffic controller system and nuclear powerplant.

>  For those who have a degree, did your class choices limit your job opportunities? 

Not classes - more it was jobs I worked in, to learn some industry you need time. Not sure where most opportunities are - and which ones would be outsourced to india/china/russia :-) That's why is important to be expert in application area and talk directly to customers, understand what they need - if you are head-down coder with no knowledge of the problem customer wants to solve, your job is easy to be moved to cheaper area.

> is knowledge in math as high as say, vector calculus, really required? From what I've heard stuff like that's pretty useless out in the real world. 

High math was just mental gymnastics for me, but it is important: you need to learn new industry and talk in their terms, same as math. Some people even use it - not many. Statistics might be useful - especially in biomedical research.

For me: sorting, modeling, cellular automata, combinatorics was more relevant than calculus.

At basic levels of competency, yes, you can learn them on your own.  But if you're going to be implementing complex web services for enterprises using RMI, CORBA, etc., you will probably need formal training.

On the other hand, I have only taken introductory studies in computer systems engineering, and was able to learn basic network programming in the space of a few days.

> For those who have a degree, did your class choices limit your job opportunities?Yes.  I came out of uni with a computer science degree with heavy software development background (I started out with dual Engineering/Business).  Some employers have rejected me on the basis that my studies did not involve enough information systems or web services work.

Most of my subjects have been related to formal software engineering, some computer systems engineering, and human-computer interfaces (ie. useability, cognitive science stuff).  Good basis for an application or system programmer, but not very employable in mainstream business roles, when compared to most IT graduates whose studies cover more business-centric stuff.

Even in the most maths-intensive computing fields, you probably will not need any more maths than your 1st-year maths subjects at university.
