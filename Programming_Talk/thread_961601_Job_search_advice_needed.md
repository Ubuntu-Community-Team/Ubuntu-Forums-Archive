### Post by themusicwave on 2008-10-28
Be careful making the switch in this economic market.

Earlier this month I tried to switch employers, I aced the interview and was all set to start my new job at the smallish(70 people) software company, when they suddenly had to make cuts.

Basically, after I had resigned my current gig, they called to tell me they just fired half the company and might not be able to pay me anymore.

I was then in the fun filled and awkward position of unresigning.

So if you do decide to switch right now watch out.  Definitely look into your possible employer and see how they are doing, my new one was privately held so no such luck for me.

As for getting a job in general, perhaps look for a smaller shop.  My company basically has 6 programmers.  I do desktop PC development, the rest do embedded PLC programming.  Most of them don't have programming degrees.

Almost every big firm wants a BS in Computer Science or equivalent.  So I think a smaller firm is the best bet.

### Post by hod139 on 2008-10-28
As others have said, this is not the best time to be looking for jobs.  Some places to start looking would be career fairs and job listing sites, e.g. [http://jobs.arstechnica.com/](http://jobs.arstechnica.com/), [http://www.monster.com/](http://www.monster.com/), craigslist, etc.  If you have any friends/family members working at a company, I would also use them for connections as much as possible.

If you are interested in a IT/programming job, you should emphasize those skills more than your education and other work history.  For example, your internship at Sandia is your most relevant work experience, yet it is the last thing on the resume.  Do you have any programming projects that you can add?  You should emphasize that you graduated summa cum laude (shows you are smart and capable of learning on the job).  Did you receive any other honors, those should be added as well.

Good luck with the job hunting.

If they find one for you they call you up and give you a pretty thorough rundown of the company and what they're looking for, and call both you and the company afterwards to see how it went.  They only get paid (by the company they place you with, not by you) after you get hired, so they go nuts trying to find you a job.

### Post by pmasiar on 2008-10-29
> **mssever said:**
> Any particular certifications that I could earn reasonably quickly that would help me?

Depends on what you want to get certified in. There are various network certifications, Linux, MSFT, etc. 

O'Reilly's certification are done with University of Illinois in Urbana-Champlain, one of the top public universities in USA, so my gut feeling it would be better than average. And they have multiple career paths. Another option (harder, so more valuable) would be to get MSFT certification, if you are into .NET. Java I am sure has some, too.

But all those certifications are hardly replacement to plain BS in CompSci degree. Pick decent state university where you reside, enroll (you still have time) and invest 4 years into it. Maybe 3 or even years, if you manage to transfer some credits, and work hard. You can work as PC support and earn room and board, and in-state tuition is about 6-8K and you can get grant or loan to cover it. You would have substantially better chance to get a job with lower GPA, but relevant degree. This current market is exactly time where hiring managers will not take any chances. 

> Any ideas on how to find one? 

Try non-profit organizations, they may be more open to your background - but again, current market squeezes then the worst. Volunteer to get your leg in. Another option might be your own alma mater, or other schools of that specific type on other parts of the country: do they need PC support people? They may like your unusual (for IT person) but relevant for them, backround. 

You may want even to write to some schools like that, explaining your situation, and looking for IT-related jobs.

> I have a few people I know who might have some connections. 

With your background and current market, connections are more important. Talk to recruiters, many will not bother but maybe some will design a strategy. You are tricky case you know that: such steep career change in such bad market! And you know you should never pay recruiter or sign exclusivity, right?

> how do you recommend that I add emphasis to the fact that I graduated *summa cum laude* with departmental recognition? 

Sorry to tell you, but nobody cares, really. All they care is, do you have recent and first-hand experience with tools and technology they use. Placing focus on GPA makes you look like fresh student with no relevant field experience at all. IMHO it belongs way below fold, in "Personal" section. Nice but irrelevant. More important are your open-sorce projects. Put source online, link it, maybe someone will look and recognize "stoke of the master" :-)

> my performance in the two programming classes I took. 

Don't. Just two classes - red flag: reminder that you do not have real CompSci degree.

Consider getting recommendation letters from teachers instead.

Thinking about it, I would hit alumni organization of your school, and work your contacts, looking for IT support job in school like yours: most jobs are never advertised, and IT support is there even if no development.
