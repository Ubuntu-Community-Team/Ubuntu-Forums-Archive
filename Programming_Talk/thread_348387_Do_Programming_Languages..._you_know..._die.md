### Post by pmasiar on 2007-01-29
Very good question. I had a lot of fun researching for this answer.

> **Vox754 said:**
> I'm sure we all have heard stories about the first analog and then digital computers, how they were large as houses and how they were programmed with bulbs or whatever.

we can even read these stories at [wikipedia]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_computing_hardware") :-)

"Bulbs" (vacuum tubes, diodes and triodes) were used before invention of transistors.  [Some]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Williams_tube") "bulbs" were also used as memory: CRT = monitor to store data - how cool or obsolete is that?. Computers were programmed by paper tape or punch cards.

> I have some old books, from the 80's, so they are not really that old, 
Most, but not all books that old (and older) are obsolete. [The Mythical Man-Month]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mythical_man-month") is as true now as it was 30 years ago - it was re-issued again, 25 years later. Highly recommended. Another great book from '70 is [Etudes for Programmers]("http://www.amazon.com/Etudes-Programmers-Charles-Wetherell/dp/0132918072") with excellent programming tasks.

> For the unexperienced one, like myself, in essence all languages do the same: variables, functions, procedures, *if-then-else*, *for*, *while*, etc.

Try to look into very different languages: [Prolog]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolog") has only one "looping" concept: recursion. You describe facts and rules, and ask questions, but it's task of prolog system to find a way to "figure out" answer (using facts and rules). BTW Prolog is part of Ubuntu, too.

> So, how can you tell a language has become old, or more importantly *obsolete*?

Obviously, new languages are born because they implement new things, but what is the defining moment that can be considered their downfall?
Do people simply stop using them? Do they present lots of bugs? Are they not scalable enough? Are they simply too *old*?

Excellent essay of how languages are born, live, and die, is Paul Greenspun's [The Hundred Year Language]("http://www.paulgraham.com/hundred.html")

Languages are designed to fulfill a role. When another language fills it better, or role is not needed anymore, language dies. Ie. Java replaced Cobol as "enterprise" language.

When (sometime during next 100 years from now) enterprise will become less obsessed in static type checking and static exception handling, will decide to use increased computer power to let more checks happen at runtime and increase programmer's productivity instead, Java will be abandoned for new language. "Generics" is step in that direction, IMHO.

> Can you think of *really dead* programming languages?

Yes, and I even programmed in them: :-) Algol60 and Algol68 were "theoretical" languages to try out describing syntax: [Backus–Naur form]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form") and [Van Wijngaarden grammar]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Wijngaarden_grammar"). BNF is still used, but let me tell you VWG is *way* too complicated. :-) But really cool: rules to generate grammars.

[B and BCPL]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_programming_language") were replaced by C.

> it wouldn't surprise me to hear people programming stuff in languages that were developed in the 1920's or so.
And if you search enough you'll probably find that Leonardo DaVinci had actually invented programming computers from old arabian-greek manuscripts or something strange like that. 

Sorry to disappoint you, but greek and arab (and up to industrial era) top computing technology was [abacus]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abacus") :-)  and it is not programmable, even now :-) In 1920 they had better (mechanical with gears) calculators - also not programmable. "Computer" was title for calculator users who computed calculations for nuclear bomb, ballistics tables etc.

> Do you think Python, Perl and PHP will still be around 30 years in the future?

Let's try *not* start another flamewar on this issue, OK? :-)

According to Greenspun's article above (makes sense to me but you are free to disagree):  as CPU productivity increases according to Moore's law, we need to burn the new cheap CPU cycles on something, so new programming languages will be more interested in programmer's productivity (which is increasing *substantially* slower than CPU speed).

IMHO Perl and PHP are steps in right direction, but they both are less than optimal for reading programs. So 30 years from now we still could possibly have PHP and Perl around, but as obscure and obsolete code, what only couple of greybeards possibly can understand and fix. Future language (for applications) will be something Python-like, optimized for reading and "doing the right stuff". Possibly instead of PHP for web pages we will have something like "Python server pages" - as simple way to create web pages using simple OO language of next generation. Or maybe something even simpler, but still readable - we will see. 

Of course we will have many languages from present crop. Java, Ada, Fortran, Lisp, C and C++ will still be around 30 years from now, just maybe not used as often as they are now - as Cobol and PL/1 are still used today, but are really obscure. And we will have many new languages, designed to solve problems in some special areas in very visual ways. 

Something like [Game Maker]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_maker") GUI language for creating computer games: GUI, almost no code needed, special high-level constructs for often used tasks, like manipulate score, create object moving in random direction and acceleration, etc. It is useless for general programming tasks, but great for the niche it was designed.

As they say: "think twice before you start coding. Code is like tattoo: it will stick on you for a very long time" :-)

IMHO, YMMV. Please no flames, of course your favorite language will survive too, it is entirely my mistake I forgot to mention it. :-)

I don't wish to start a flame war here, but I'm a retired CS professor. During my 21 years of teaching I met some professors who match this description, but most of my colleagues spent an enormous amount of time trying to keep up with this rapidly changing field.

I worked as a programmer for 10 years before going into teaching. I can tell you that teaching is a much more challenging and takes a hell of a lot more time. Furthermore, it pays a LOT less than industry.

I taught at a state university that emphasizes teaching over research. Research universities present another challenge. The professors are under pressure to bring in research grants. They often do not have the time to redesign their classes.

Don't forget that changing the primary teaching language ripples throughout the entire curriculum for several years. When teaching a higher level class, I have to make assumptions about what the students already know. Almost all lectures, assignments, and exams have to be changed in almost all other classes.

I know first-hand how difficult it is to change the primary teaching language in a CS department. When I started (in 1983) the language was Pascal. I was the first person to teach programming 1 in C a few years later. Then I was the professor who changed the department to C++ a few years after that. By the way, the language choices were made by the department, not by me alone. I just happened to be the professor who was scheduled to teach programming 1 at those times.

Perhaps I'm being a bit too defensive here, but very few professors kick back and do nothing when they get tenure. I've done both -- work in industry and teach -- so I know what the relative workloads are like. As for being "teachers, not learners." I assert that nothing causes (forces?) you to learn more about a subject than teaching it.

To be honest, I'm sorry that I spent two decades of my life teaching. Few students appreciate the amount of work it requires, and the pay is lousy. On the plus side, I did have a few students (very few) who enjoyed the learning experience. They were fun to work with. And one of the things I did like about teaching is that I learned so much.
