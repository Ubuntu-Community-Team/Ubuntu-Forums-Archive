### Post by CptPicard on 2010-02-26
It's a meritocracy, and you just have to make your own way... nobody is going to "come get you", because people who actually contribute to projects are busy doing that. That's the way it just is, and the only way to fix your situation is to work on your competence, find a project you want to contribute to, and then actually write something worthwhile for it.

For example, if you want to contribute bugfixes, the net is full of FOSS projects with bug trackers. Pick one, hack away, submit a patch, and see if it gets accepted. Mind you, you'll have to already be pretty good at that point, because project leaders don't want to introduce more issues because of your patch.

### Post by Simian Man on 2010-02-26
I'd really recommend working on something of your own first.  That way you can learn to program and make progress.  If you just grab the code of some popular project, you're only going to be overwhelmed.  Also most project leaders aren't going to appreciate an inexperienced user committing bad code to their projects.

Start small and learn on your own first.  Then you'll be in a position where you can work with others.

### Post by TurnOfTide on 2010-02-26
That is your problem, you aren't writing anything. You can read all you want about how to play a piano but you won't ever know how to play it unless you actually sit down and practice......


Start by actually doing some sample programs, really simple stuff, get it to compile (might be a headache, think of this an initation process).

After those basics are down, find a very simple sample application's source code, then go for larger *sample* apps with 2k lines of code.** Read some, write some, repeat.**
