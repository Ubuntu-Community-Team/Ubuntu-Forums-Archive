### Post by aysiu on 2007-10-24
> **Depressed Man said:**
> I agree with you that value and price are not interchangable terms. But I fail to see your value arguement regarding laptops. (though I'm really tired right now). So rephrase please?
The cheapest Dell notebook is US$499.
The cheapest HP notebook is US$599.
The cheapest Mac notebook is US$1099.

In terms of price alone, the Mac is more expensive. If you amp up the specs on the Dell and HP notebook in order to match the specs of the Mac notebook, you may find that Dell/HP are a comparable, better, or worse value.

You cannot "amp *down*" the Mac notebook any low than $1099 in order to match the cost of the cheapest Dell notebook, just as you cannot carve out a small bottle's worth of soy sauce from the huge soy sauce tub at Costco.

Expensive refers to cost. A car is an expensive purchase (even a "cheap" car is costly). A pack of bubble gum is an inexpensive purchase (even if it's an "expensive" pack of bubble gum). Likewise, the Macbook is an expensive purchase--more than double the price of the cheapest Dell notebook--but it is not necessarily a worse value.

What I keep hearing Mac fans say is "It's not expensive." Well, it *is* expensive. If you want to say it's a good value for the price, that's a different story entirely. If someone can rent a 4-bedroom apartment in San Francisco for $2000 a month, that's a good value. But it's still expensive. I don't have $2000 a month to spend on rent, so I don't care how good a value it is.

Likewise, a home consumer may want a laptop for basic functions (email, web browsing, light word processing) and may consider the Macbook too expensive, even if it is a good value (like the 4-bedroom apartment). If that home consumer has only $600 to spend on a computer, do you think she's going to care whether the Macbook is a good value or not? All she'll care about is that she has $600 to spend, and the cheapest Mac is $400 more than that.

### Post by AusIV4 on 2007-11-10
From my comparisons, if you're going to buy all of the hardware you get on a Mac, the Mac is as good an option as any. The thing is, I don't need the even bare minimum available on a Mac.

Options on Macs are fairly limited. IMHO, the perfect size for a laptop screen is 14.1" - not an option on a Mac, it's either 13.3" (for which you generally pay a premium for the small footprint), or if you want a bigger screen, you have to pay for nicer specs on every other aspect of the laptop.
deem
I don't need a DVI port, I need a VGA port. DVI ports tend to be a bit more expensive than VGA, plus I then have to buy a $19 adapter to use with my VGA monitors.

The minimum amount of ram available is 1 GB. I guess this is becoming the standard these days, but I still think 512 should be plenty to run basic applications (word processing, web browsing, etc.). I don't want to pay the premium for more ram than I'm going to use.

The minimum processor is a Core 2 Duo. I like core 2 duos, but there are certainly less expensive processors available, and a lot of people don't need a 2 ghz core 2 duo for every day tasks.

Magnetic power adapters are nice, but again they're a premium feature that I feel are unnecessary. I'm either at my desk or running on battery, so I don't want to pay a premium for an AC adapter that easily comes unplugged.

I guess combo drives (DVD-R / CD-RW) are standard these days, but if you want to be able to write DVDs, you have to upgrade to a dual layer burner **and** upgrade your processor. Likewise, if you want to upgrade your processor, you **have** to upgrade your DVD drive **and** your hard drive.

Bluetooth is another addition. I like having bluetooth, so I won't complain too much, but you can still get by without having bluetooth, so it seems like another unnecessary expenditure.

Also, I believe every Mac apple offers has an IR port and a remote. If the remote is necessary, it seems like it would be cheaper to use the already-mandatory bluetooth, but it would be nice if they offered you the option of not buying the remote.

Lastly, I'm quite fond of Ubuntu. I bought a System76 because I didn't want to pay for an OS I wasn't going to use, so there's an extra expense I find unnecessary.

In short, I'd like more options. You can buy a respectable laptop for $600 or so. The cheapest Mac is $1,100. Admittedly, you get a lot more with the Mac, but I think it would be nice if Apple offered a lower end Mac so that they could reach people who can't justify $1,100.
