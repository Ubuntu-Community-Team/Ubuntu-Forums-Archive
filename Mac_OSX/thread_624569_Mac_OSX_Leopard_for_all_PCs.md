### Post by 3rdalbum on 2007-12-09
> **hanzomon4 said:**
> Can't Apple take advantage of the hardware support of Unix?

It depends what you mean. Darwin (the supposedly Unix core of Mac OS X) has almost no hardware support in it. It only supports hardware that is in Macs, basically!

As far as using device drivers from the BSDs, that's difficult. Darwin's internal architecture is too different from the BSDs to be able to directly use modules from them, so Apple would need to make some heavy modifications to the existing drivers from the BSDs in order to get them working on Darwin.

It's a huge task, and then remember that Unix and Linux don't support all the hardware that can be made to work on Windows. Linux has better hardware support than the Unixes, yet Apple can't touch the drivers in Linux without having to relicense Darwin under the GPL - that's something Apple won't do. Windows users have enough gripes about Linux's hardware support; imagine the chest-beating that would happen if they bought a $129 operating system that wouldn't work with their hardware.

### Post by AlphaMack on 2007-12-24
iLife aside, if you're already running platform-agnostic apps and have your data saved in open and/or platform-agnostic apps, what is the point of running a closed OS on a closed platform?  This is coming from an OS X -> Ubuntu user.

Think about what you get with OS X:

- Mediocre X support at best.

- Outdated ports of your favorite open source programs (e.g. Fink and MacPorts).

- An inadequate firewall that circumvents ipfw - change your ipfw ruleset and watch FirewallTool go haywire.  I spent a great deal of time getting my ipfw ruleset and FirewallTool's implementation saved at /Library/Preferences/com.apple.sharing.firewall.plist to play nicely without breaking Bonjour or iApp sharing support.

- Format lock-in.  Sure you can import open formats but then you'll be locked into Apple's proprietary formats (e.g. .emlx, iPhoto Library and iTunes data files and xml "backups," iCal data files, etc.).  Exporting back into open formats is a royal PITA (you'll need a third party utility to convert your post-Panther Mail.app mailboxes back into .mbox and Address Book's vCard format can't parse URLs correctly).

- If you're on Intel, you still can't buy a native version of Photoshop CS or MS Office.  You're paying $$$ to emulate at the speed of a PPC G3!  The GIMP and NeoOffice already have native releases out.

- Many third party apps require you to pay for what you can already do on Windows or Linux for free.  Some are nothing more than simple GUI front-ends utilizing AppleScript which in turn call simple UNIX commands.  There are open-source equivalents out there, but it may require somewhat of an adventure to find the wheat from the chaff, especially if you're a newcomer to the platform.

- Apple's ignorance and censorship of important bugs or security issues.

- Apple's insistence that you cough up another $129 for other bug fixes that still remain unresolved in regressed versions of OS X.  And if the new upgrade doesn't support your current hardware?  Well, open your wallet...

- The hysterical fanboys.  Enough said.


Granted that there are numerous issues with Leopard that still remain unresolved and that Apple refuse to release 10.5.2 until Macworld (because 76 major fixes are so earth-shattering as to require an official SteveNote), it is not a viable option coming from Tiger.  Yet Leopard still remains an alternative to the laughingstock that is Vista for those dying to get off of Windows.

Of course, if what I said at the beginning about apps and data are still true, why bother with Vista?

IMO the only reason to stick around with OS X is if **you are locked into non-cross platform proprietary formats or programs** or have no functional alternatives in the open source world.
