### Post by igknighted on 2007-11-08
If linux vs. windows is apples to oranges, linux vs. leopard is apples to kites.  They diverge from windows (while similar in underneath structure) with nearly opposite goals.  Leopard (and OS X in general) take the tact that windows offers too much complication to the user.  It tries to figure out the best way to do something, and make the user do it that way.  Linux, on the other hand, says windows locks you into doing things its way too much, and presents the user with myriad choices.

What am I saying?  There are types of users for which both types of systems are best.  Power users will likely feel restricted by the Apple platform.  Users who don't care about the OS, just what you can do after it is installed may find linux to be too much work.  But they both work very well for their respective target audiences.  Does apple work better for the crowd they target better than linux does for its own target crowd?  Impossible to tell.

My personal opinion is that Leopard is a flop.  The graphical tricks are annoying, and the only features that were added are of little consequence to 99% of users (things like time machine aren't really that valuable on the desktop, IMHO).  I think that once you consider the requirement of having [new] Apple hardware to run OSX Leopard, while linux can run on your 10 or 15 year old PC, then Linux is the superior OS.  But with that said, if I happened to acquire an apple computer (note, I would never buy one new) then it would run OSX and not linux (Linux never seems to run properly on Apple hardware for some reason), and probably Leopard because I am a whore for new software.

### Post by p_quarles on 2007-11-08
I don't have extensive experience with Macs, but I can offer a couple of amateur comparisons. 

iTunes vs. Amarok/Exaile

In terms of basic functionality as music players, they seem pretty much equal (unless iTunes has gapless playback, which I don't think it does). Amarok uses fewer system resources, and Exaile uses fewer still. I also find iTunes to be fairly intrusive -- frequently demanding that you update it, and basically reinstalling itself entirely every time you do. For me, that's kind of a showstopper.

What I will say about iTunes, though, is that it does a great job integrating a variety of media applications: playing music *and *video, ripping CDs and DVDs, as well as burning CDs, and functioning as a media feed aggregator. 

I don't care about the CD/DVD thing, since K3b is much better for that. However, having my audio and video podcasts in one place is nice, and I haven't found a way to do that in Linux. I use Miro for video feeds, but it's unacceptable for audio: it has a resource-hungry "visualization" thingy that you can't turn off. Plus, it's just far less functional than Amarok, which I use with audio podcasts. 

Konqueror

No "vs." here, because OS X has nothing to compare it with. I realize that Konqi doesn't get a lot of love, but it is one of the fastest, most customizable browsers out there, combined with a superior file manager. (note to those interested in DE flamewars: I'm not comparing it to other X11 web browsers/file managers: just to Safari, Finder, etc.)

OS X dock vs. AWN/Simdock/Kooldock/Ksmoothdock/Kibadock

I'll have to go with Apple on this one, since it's the only one I've never seen crash, or flicker, or behave erratically, or superimpose a piece of the wallpaper over an application window. 

Time Machine vs. rsync

This depends entirely on who's using it. As far I understand it, they both have the same basic functionality, but they work for completely different types of users. Time Machine is great for anyone who wants easy, reliable backups. rsync works for people who know how to write shell scripts and turn them into cronjobs. I prefer rsync, because I can't imagine a backup application with a greater number of options. But, I'm a geek. 

Apart from that, I understand there are a few high-end video production applications available for OS X, and Linux has fielded no real challengers. Not my specialty, just what I've heard.

### Post by ablaze on 2007-11-10
I'm a Mac user since 1998. I switched over from a combination of SuSE and my good old C64. Nevertheless, I followed what happened in the Linux community and contributed from time to time.

Here's what I think about comparing Ubuntu to Leopard:

- Mac OS X is much less complex than all forms of Linux. You just have a few folders visible for users. Most programs come with all the libraries they need. Installation is just drag and drop to the only Applications folder. Ubuntu, being Linux, is much more complicated. Why, oh why would an end user ever have to have a look at /etc, /pub, or /user ?

- Leopard has so much polish on its surface. Everything works like expected. The apps shipped with the OS are all fully integrated. Try dragging and dropping. Everything works like expected. Apple tries to think how you could do a process intuitively and invests time that it works that way. One shiny example for me is spring loaded folders that work about the same in Exposé.

- System Administration is complete. Everything you really want to change is there in System prefs. Nothing more but that what's needed. I guess the majority of users would not want to set the amount of *bling* on their OS (like the new Compiz prefs in Ubuntu). If you don't want something (e.g. the standard dock in Mac OS X), use the command line. 

- Apple makes smart choices about the software they include as standard. One example that is really strange in Ubuntu is the choice of the combination of File Juicer, Rhythmbox, and Serpentine as the standard for music apps. iTunes is the one stop shop. And, moreover it's a one stop shop that tightly integrates in the system (like the media browser in all the other iApps). Amarok is KDE. It's not integrated at all. There are alternatives for Gnome, too, like Banshee. Of yourse you can say, that one can install whatever jukebox app he wants, but that adds too much complexity to the enduser. Ship one great solution and concentrate on integrating it.

- Mac OS X has much much better artwork. One small example that's really bad in Ubuntu is the standard turn off button in the upper right corner. Something like this would never ship from Apple.

- Software Updates are shipped in packages for the system. (like from 10.5 to 10.5.1. including bug fixes for many system components that Ubuntu ships seperately.) This (Apple's solution) reduces complexity for the end user. Moreover, Apple seperates Security Updates from functionality and stability updates. A great thing too. Why not bundle updates in Ubuntu, too (for functionality)? And: get rid of the cryptic names for different system components in the update manager. The enduser normally doesn't get in touch even with the kernel. If the kernel is updated, why not call it Ubuntu 7.10.x. (as an aside: Get rid of the kernel information in the grub menu. People want to simply start ubuntu). To sum it up: Ubuntu needs to reduce complexity much much more than it has in the past.

- The only thing that's much better in Ubuntu is the openness of the system. Free software and the fact that software will be open and free in the future is great! Keep up your good work!
