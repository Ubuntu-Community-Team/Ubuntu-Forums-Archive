> **Apple pushes anti-virus for Macs**

Apple has urged Mac owners to use anti-virus software.

In a note posted on its support site in late November, Apple said it wanted to "encourage" people to use anti-virus to stay safe online.

The move is widely seen as a response to the growing trend among cyber criminals of booby-trapping webpages that can catch out Mac users.

Before now Mac users have been largely free of the security problems that plague Microsoft's Windows.

Fresh threat

The support note recommends that Mac owners install one or more of three anti-virus products.

Advice on the site said: "Apple encourages the widespread use of multiple antivirus utilities so that virus programmers have more than one application to circumvent, thus making the whole virus writing process more difficult."

Apple recommended users try McAfee VirusScan, Symantec Norton Anti-Virus 11, or Intego VirusBarrier X5.

The vast majority of malicious programs circulating online are aimed at Microsoft's Windows, largely because the software is used by so many people.

A handful of viruses have been written that targets Mac's OSX, but most have been demonstration versions only and few have had any significant impact on Apple users.

One virus, known as AppleScript.THT, could take control of a Mac and grab screenshots or keystrokes.

However, in recent months, hi-tech criminals have signalled a change in tactics away from e-mail borne viruses. Instead, many are infiltrating popular webpages in a bid to infect the machine of any and every visitor.

Many seek to steal valuable information such as login names, passwords or game accounts instead of trying to install themselves on a machine.

### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2008-12-02
I think drive-by downloads are largely fueled by the "advertisements" that appear on most web sites. What we need is web advertisement hosts to be held accountable for their content. In Canada, we have an Advertising Standards group that makes sure any conventional advertising (television, radio, print) is truthful, safe and not discriminatory. They are *effective*.

However, the Internet is something of an unexplained realm to this point. The advertising standards groups have no real effect on it all since the whole technology is a constant import / export. It's something like a purée. (Compose key FTW!).

As a result, web advertisers continually export their advertisements - ads which would be banned immediately in any other medium - upon a group of people who are completely unused to such things. A lot of the advertisement services strike me as something like mercenaries; they don't really care what the ads contain just so long as they get paid for serving them. They do get paid, and the advertisements they spew over the web continue to trick unsuspecting users into doing things like download the Antivirus 2008 malware, sign up to nasty pyramid schemes and rarely ever actually legitimately buy / look at useful things.

Then it comes down to the web developers. They link to these advertisements because they need money. Right now, many don't care at all whether they are basically giving away their users to thieves and liars. They need to so their sites can keep running!

So what happens? Some users (the clever ones) use things like adblock to stop those intrusions. Web developers apply more ads, advertisers get more obtrusive, advertisements themselves get more annoying.
Legitimate advertisers like Google, which do have standards, are filtered - almost as collateral damage - by the same tools those clever users use. Most users, who do not have adblock and still run Internet Explorer 6, are hit head-on by the rougher advertisements and are completely traumatized by them.

So what needs to happen, in my opinion, is *we* need to support good advertising on the web. We shouldn't discriminate against the "good" advertisers with tools like Adblock - at least not by default - because so long as we do that nothing will change. It would be really nice if website developers could recognize the option of using more ethical advertising services and earning more positive income as a result. It really would be no different from our existing advertising standards in print or TV, except that the regulation is in the hands of individual users.

The correct term for such malefactory behavior as we are discussing here, vis a vis writing viruses, trojans, exploits, ad-ware, spy-ware, drive-by web-evilness, etc., are all properly classified as cracks and cracking, and those engaging in these behaviors are crackers. ### Post by Erunno on 2008-12-03
> **MikeTheC said:**
> The F/OSS community writes based out of personal interest.

[http://www.redhat.com/]("http://www.redhat.com/"). Proven wrong by counter-example.

Anyway, one of Windows' major problems for years was the abyssal default configuration and less on the coding side (although there are enough problems as it is). Windows had an incredibly fine-grained group- and role-based access control for years now while only recent developments like PolicyKit and SELinux slowly started to replace the primitive read/write/execute scheme for users, groups and the rest of the world on the Linux side of the pond. And while this Windows power quickly came to good use in cooperate environments (at least when competent administrators were present) Microsoft decided to ship a configuration where every user is an Administrator by default and therefore every process a user starts has elevated rights. Ugh. No wonder that malware writers had such an easy time targeting the casual user's desktop. Only recently with Vista Microsoft has started to tackle this issue that they themselves created and now find themselves in a hell where a lot of third-party products presume administrative rights and users which where never sensitized about this problem (e.g. the distinction between using and administrating).

Apropos virus scanners: There's also another angle on why Apple might recommend them. Apple machines are increasingly common and are usually used in an environment where a lot of Windows machines are present and accessible via network. While not all viruses might affect OS X itself a virus scanner will prevent that infected files are spread from the Apple computer.

Plus, to a certain degree virus scanners actually can help with zero day exploits (i.e. exploits for which there are no signatures yet). Most virus scanners today use heuristic scanners to look for patterns which resemble known viruses. This is no exact science by any means and the success rate varies from scanner to scanner but there is actually a certain degree of protection against unknown threats from this problem domain.
