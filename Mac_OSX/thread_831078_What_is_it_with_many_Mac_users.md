### Post by aysiu on 2008-06-16
I think this attitude is prevalent in the Linux community as well, and it certainly doesn't apply to *all* Mac users, but I think it's quite obvious where it comes from - Steve Jobs.

From [his speech at the Stanford commencement in 2005](http://news-service.stanford.edu/news/2005/june15/jobs-061505.html): > None of this had even a hope of any practical application in my life. But ten years later, when we were designing the first Macintosh computer, it all came back to me. And we designed it all into the Mac. It was the first computer with beautiful typography. If I had never dropped in on that single course in college, the Mac would have never had multiple typefaces or proportionally spaced fonts. *And since Windows just copied the Mac, its likely that no personal computer would have them.* (Emphasis added)

And don't forget the "I'm a Mac, I'm a PC" ads, all of which, rather than giving specific and relevant criticisms of Windows resort to FUD and vague allusions, usually either criticizing Windows for problems that went away with the advent of the NT kernel (seven years ago) or claiming that Windows can't do something it clearly can (and insinuating that only Mac can do said task).

Even my wife has turned into an anti-Windows snob about using a Mac. I was an anti-Windows snob for probably about the first three months of using Linux, but after that, I got over it. I know (besides my wife) a handful of other Mac users who look down on Windows users and every time a problem comes up with Windows will say smugly "That's what you get for using a PC" ("PC" here translates to "Windows PC," since Linux doesn't exist, and Macs aren't PCs).

It's kind of the same attitude a lot of frustrated new Linux users exhibit at the first sign of trouble with Linux ("Oh, I'd never have this problem with Windows...").

I'll be the first to say Macs look slick and have very smooth animation for all their desktop effects (yes, I haven't seen the likes of it in Compiz). I'll also say that a modified version of Apple's approach to hardware/software integration is the best consumer approach to operating system experiences. Instead of locking people into their hardware, though, they should just not guarantee OS X will work on non-Apple hardware. That said, I don't really see what Mac has over Windows or Linux, and it is ultimately a matter of preference.

What I've learned over time, using my wife's Mac, using my own Linux computers, and using Windows at work, is that operating systems all have their problems and advantages, and people should just find what's right for them. No need to be snobby about what you use. Just use it.
