### Post by Sorivenul on 2009-02-12
OS X and *BSD are two completely separate systems, though they have some similarities. 

OS X's networking stack, VFS layer, POSIX system calls, and high-level abstraction (with the exception of threads) are loosely based on FreeBSD, though anything still directly related to FreeBSD is in userland. 

OS X is built on a [Mach kernel]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mach_(kernel)"), [XNU]("http://www.kernelthread.com/mac/osx/arch_xnu.html"). Mach itself forked from 4.2BSD I believe, and when Steve Jobs went to NeXT they used it along with the 4.3BSD userland for NeXTSTEP. Then Apple aquired NeXT, forked the OS again (basically making [Raphsody]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhapsody_(operating_system)")). They then proceeded to Darwin/OS X (server). The desktop incarnations of OS X started with Mach 4, I believe, and FreeBSD 3.1; 10.2 took code from FreeBSD 4.5, and 10.3 took code from 4.8. Tiger borrowed code from the FreeBSD 5.x series. I'm not sure what Leopard is using... 

To further your education (and to have a defining "geek" moment), go print the [UNIX History]("http://www.levenez.com/unix/") and put it on your wall or in your cubicle.

So, the OS X kernel, as well as init system, are quite different from any BSD.

Once you get past those differences, you get into the [Single Unix Specification]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_UNIX_Specification") (SUS), which sets for the a list of qualifications to be called "UNIX" under the trademark. I follow NetBSD's philosophy, and ["call it a duck"]("http://www.netbsd.org/about/call-it-a-duck.html").

Linux based distributions (such as ubuntu, arch, fedora ect) do not aim at the same market as apple users, the problem with them is that they are 'geek centric' and are not designed to be a complete system yes you can have alternatives to most of the OSX apps but can you just simply download them and drag them to the apps folder?
