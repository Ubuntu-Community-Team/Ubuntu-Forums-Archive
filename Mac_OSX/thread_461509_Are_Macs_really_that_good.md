### Post by GFC2 on 2007-07-02
Frankly, no.  Migration to Mac a couple of years back (iMac G5, 1.2 GB RAM) was extremely disappointing for me.  OS X has a wonderful interface and control features, but in my experience it was less stable than XP or Vista.  I lost count of the times the thing had complete freezes that were not resolved by the Force Quit command and forced me to hit the power switch.  And that was under not particularly demanding use.  But the killer for me was hardware reliability.  The HDD quickly went bad, then the logic board went bad in about a year.  The replacement logic board went bad (or was originally defective) and the thing started racing the fan and shutting down.  That could be triggered by something as simple as a flash video in Safari.  Similar experiences show up on the Apple support forums.  Apple provides essentially no protection on parts replaced under warranty.  

OS10 and Vista are both resource hogs.  Vista is a RAM hog.  OS X is a CPU hog.  The Mach kernel of OS10 requires more CPU executions for a given task than does a monolithic kernel, so a faster CPU is required to get the same work done in OS X.  More CPU use generates more heat and presents challenges for power management that seem to be unresolved with OS10.  The last really reliable Macs were the G4 desktops, but they were slow.  Since Mac's been going for speed, power management is balanced on a knife edge and it doesn't take much of a glitch to bring the whole thing down.  The first generation Intel Macs were infamous for heating up and shutting down.  The problems apparently have not gone away with the later C2D Intel Macs.   

Then there are the issues with melting magsafes, flickering screens, bad slot drives...........

### Post by Backharlow on 2007-07-05
I'd just like to clarify a few tidbits.
> I like to be able to plug new hard disks and customize my hardware. MAC can't do that.
Just because something isn't for sale at the Apple Store, or costs a fortune there doesn't mean it does exist elsewhere.
You really have to start with a real Mac, i.e. motherboard, but after that a surprising amount of stuff can be mix-and-matched with PC hardware and customized without having to buy anything mac only.
Mac's DO NOT require special: keyboard, mouse, monitor, hard drives, cd/dvd drives, other peripherals i.e. printers, cameras, jump drives, etc. Storage devices must be formatted for file systems that Mac understands, i.e. you have to reformat PC disks, but any physical harddrive will work including all externals (usb, firewire, etc.).
Often, standard pc hardware will work automatically. Sometimes you must have the mac drivers. I have found that a lot of sound cards, network cards, firewire cards, A/V controllers, are like this. In this case just ask the vendor if they offer mac drivers before you buy.
Plus 3rd party mac only hardware such as: CPU, ram, graphics cards, sounds cards, pci cards, etc.. all can be acquired without really spending any/much more money than you would for the PC version. You just have to understand that it does exist and verify before purchasing. You might spend a little more for a decent graphics card from Nvidia or Radeon to get the mac model.
Also, in answer to the original topic, it doesn't seem that Mac hardware is particularly special, but it does facilitate some software that has yet to be matched in other environments. I love Ubuntu but I still trust nothing but apple pro applications with my music and film work. The so called iBrainChip is not always the result of the latest marketing propaganda. Apple is not a new company and so some seemingly bind-faith mac advocacy does come from years of industry experience in a world where mac was literally the only way to get certain tasks accomplished.
