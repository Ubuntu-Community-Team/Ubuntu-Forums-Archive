So it's no good anyone's getting some beat-up box and then hoping he can run, for example, the next version of Delicious Monster, written in Objective-C 2.0 on it.

I suppose it's convenient from that POV -- that lots of people use it.  But really for day-to-day use I can't see that there's any particular advantage to basing your temperature scale on putting the freezing point of water at nought and its boiling point at 100.  It all comes down to base ten -- which only fixates people because we happen to have ten fingers. (Computers don't, of course, work on base ten -- although [Babage's Analytical Engine](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_engine) (which was never built in his lifetime) apparently does.)

Most traditional measuring systems are based around what are convenient measures for whatever you happen to be doing -- and frequently (not always, of course) work by doubling and halving.  That way you end up with small round numbers.  It makes more sense to me to say someone is 6ft than that he is 182cm.  (And come to think of it, I don't think the centimetre is a valid SI unit -- you can advance by 10 or 1000 but not 100.)  Similarly, say, 4 oz. makes more sense to a cook than measures in the *hundreds* of grammes.

But convenience doesn't come into it when the ideologues, who like nothing better than tidy rationalistic (as opposed to rational) solutions are on the march.

But it useful to meet the same units everywhere, as you say.
