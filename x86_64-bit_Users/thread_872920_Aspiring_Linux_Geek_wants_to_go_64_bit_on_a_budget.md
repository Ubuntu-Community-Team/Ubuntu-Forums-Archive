Ati right now really is a work in progress. You won't be able to watch movies, or play games through wine with compiz fusion turned on. The good thing about ATI are cost effective cards and they've been releasing alot of documentation to the open source community to develop drivers that are open source. Intel (usually onboard)
Open source but undeveloped drivers. Not good for gaming, lots of issues on wine board. Not sure how video playback and compiz works.

### Post by Yellow Pasque on 2008-12-18
> Does this imply if I want to use Nvidia as an onboard video chipset, I might be better off going with Intel?
No, Nvidia and Intel have a love/hate relationship too**: Besides, I don't think it works like that. The CPU manufacturer specifies the pinout of the CPU in a technical document and the implementation of the north bridge is up to the chipset designer. Nvidia isn't going to intentionally sabotage their own products. If AMD or Intel gave bad specs, it would also piss off their motherboard partners, who are generally "agnostic" with chipset branding.

IMHO, there's no clear winner for graphics right now, unless you can get by on Intel integrated graphics, which still isn't perfect. Nvidia's binary driver is generally better than ATI's, but Nvidia shows no sign of opening up their GPU driver/specs, and this could limit some of the upcoming Linux features (e.g. kernel mode-setting). ATI has good open-source drivers (I personally love the stability of the "radeonhd" driver), but they still haven't released documentation for 3D/video acceleration on any modern GPU's (they cite "legal" reasons). Intel has the best Linux support but their GPU's are underpowered.

Personally, I would build with a cheap Athlon X2 and a socket AM2+ mobo (AMD 780G or Nvidia 8x00); I have this mobo and it works real well in Linux: [http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16813138105](http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16813138105)
