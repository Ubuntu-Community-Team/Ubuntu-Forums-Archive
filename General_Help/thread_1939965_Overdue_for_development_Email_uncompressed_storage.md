### Post by scruffyeagle on 2012-03-12
I've been playing with Linux for about a year now, trying out various OS's, variants, & email programs. One of the primary difficulties I've encounted, is that it's almost impossible to continuously take your previous stash of emails with you when you migrate from one program to the next. The basic cause of this problem, is that EVERY email program I've found compresses the stash of emails - and, NONE of the methods used for compression are universal for all email programs. (I know, you're thinking "DUH!" - tell me something new!)

The practice of compressing the stored emails was primarily the result of the need to conserve storage space on disks. It was a good solution for the time when disk storage space was limited. BUT - the storage space limitation is no longer the norm. In this time when 30GB hard drives are commonplace, a normal user's email would only take up a tiny fraction of the available space, even with every bit of it being left uncompressed.

A secondary consideration when considering compression of email, is data security. In the case of a government, or a business, data security is very important, and email compression enhances data security. But what percentage of users are governments or businesses? My guess, is that it's less than 10%. Which leaves the ordinary home user. Ordinary home users might have needs for enhancing data security - but, not always. Some people do very little email involving financial matters; i.e., it's mainly letters to friends & relatives, or forwarding some cute thing they came across. Those people have no real need for hiding the contents of their emails. Except in cases like communicating with the bank, or a service provider, etc.

The point of all this, is that the continuous mandatory compression of ALL email is no longer necessary or even justified. It would be sufficient to provide an option to compress or encrypt the current email on demand. Or, all emails from a specified sender. Standard compression &/or encryption methods work well - why insist on a proprietary method unique to just the current program?

On top of all this, there's there are a couple of more facts. Linux is, in general, a very secure OS. It's difficult to hack, and not friendly to unauthorized data requests from online. It's become commonplace for computers & HD manufacturers to install password authorization routines into the devices, so even if a drive is removed from the machine, it can't be accessed without the correct password being provided. (If the user specifies one during setup.)

In my playing around with various types of Linux, and various email programs it would have been incredibly advantageous for me if program designers had shifted their paradigm to fit modern conditions. I really DON'T WANT all of my email compressed! I really DON'T WANT all of my email encrypted in some program-specific format! I want to be able to relocate my stash of email as I please, whenever & wherever I please, without losing any content. I want plain text stored as plain text. Period. I want HTML stored as HTML. Period. I want pictures stored as pictures. Period.

This idea is way overdue. Hopefully, some designer may start to think it's worth developing.
