Yes, that's true. That's the type of user that never installs any software ;-)

Most people fail to see that there is not "the desktop". There are dozens of different desktops and Linux is indeed ready for three or four of them. And that's quite an accomplishment. It was ready for just one even two years ago :-) I'm pretty sure it will get ready for more and more desktops in the near future. Maybe it's ready for yours in a year or two? I think it'll be ready for mine within the next year. I'd tried Ubuntu 6.06 and that was NOT ready for my desktop... 7.04 looks promising but has some problems to solve... I *think* it's ready for my (business) notebook (!), but not quite yet for my home PC.

Now does that sound like I'm a Windows fan? No, I'm really not or I wouldn't be here. I'm fed up with Windows and currently evaluating alternatives. And that's the most important point for me: In the "Linux world", there are dozens of very different distributions. In the "Windows world", there's pretty much just one Windows and you can do absolutely NOTHING about it if the development is headed in a direction you don't like. What if Microsoft decides to switch to a subscription model for the next Windows version and starts charging you... let's say 10 dollars a month?

However, I'm more concerned about all the built-in restrictions and TCPA... (What if they say that you have to be connected to the Internet at all times and transmit "usage statistics"?)
I personally regret that "Linux is free" (as in beer) is such a common argument for Linux. I totally agree with you: for anybody but "poor students", it just *doesn't matter*. Let's face it: Windows is cheap. My Win XP cost 120 euros and I've been using it for 5 years. That's 2 euros per month. Big deal. How much time am I willing to spend for 2 euros? Honestly not really more than 10 minutes... 

There are (at least) two things you shouldn't forget:

1) Windows seems "easier" because you've probably had 10+ years of experience with it. Think about that. 10 years! And you're still "less comfortable with Ubuntu after two weeks". Big surprise. So am I. But I really like some of the concepts behind Linux a lot better than those behind Windows and I WANT to switch. That's why I start investing some time and I know that it'll take a while. I have managed to get a dual boot system where I can access all my data simultaneously from both operating systems (shared browser profile, shared Skype profile, shared Java projects...). Now I can spend just as much time as I want in Linux but am not forced to stay there when I get frustrated.
I think you do need to be curious and a little interested in Linux in order to do that. 

2) You say that there's nothing that Linux can do that Windows can't do (better). That's just not true. It's just that your current needs and expectations are strongly defined by what you've experienced and what was possible and "normal" during the last several years. I've only just started using Linux and I already like Konqueror MUCH better than Windows explorer. If I were really used to Konqueror, I would just HATE Windows explorer. You're now missing certain things you had in Windows but you would also miss things in Windows if you knew more things in Linux.

Switching to Linux is (imho) NOT a thing that you can do in a couple of days so it's worth starting now and taking your time... but you can only do it if you WANT to do it and are willing to invest even a fraction of the time you've already invested into Windows. If you need something that "just works", you're probably better off with Windows in the near future ... just keep doing what you've always been doing and don't mind the fact that that's what your (grand-?)parents said/did about the VCR, the digital camera and the cell phone ;-)  *eg* Oh, and the computer *g*

Have fun, keep taking a look at Linux, Live-CDs are just such a great thing!

ok, so although I've "said it all" :-), I still have something to add.

#1) Windows-bashing doesn't help. There are some bad things to say about Windows (see below *g*) and there are some bad things to say about Linux (see above *ggg*), but so what? That's just more likely to get people curious (and willing to prove that they can handle Linux). It's a martial arts thing about the direction of energy *g*
This difference becomes even more important AFTER they try it: If they look at Linux because somebody almost pressured them to do so, they will hate every minor glitch and not only go back to Windows, they will also stay there for years and tell everybody... If they do it because they're genuinely (pun intended *g*) curious about Linux, they'll put up with minor glitches and if they leave, they leave without hard feelings and might come back sooner rather than later. Did I mention that Live-CDs are such a great thing? *g*

#2) @blackspyder and others:
There it is again: The money argument - just in a different form. Hard disk space? Come one.... Even for a notebook, a shiny new harddisk costs 35 bucks (20 GB) or 50 bucks (60 GB). When I had to reinstall my notebook (see below) last week, I didn't want to erase or otherwise damage my huge installation and bought a 160 GB disk for 85 euros. Now YMMV, but how much time are you willing to spend to save 40 dollars? 2 days? I don't. Even 200 dollars are NOT a good reason to switch to Linux and just don't provide enough motivation to handle all the problems you will encounter in Linux that you wouldn't have encountered in the Windows you've known for years. Yes, Linux is better for old hardware that cannot be upgraded, but we're talking about "modern desktops" here, aren't we? Not some router with a crappy UI (if any).

But let's get back to the "technical side" of this question: What does Linux offer that Windows doesn't? I have a good one to offer: Flexibility in combining programs and a total lack of "vendor lock in" (related subjects, but still different things)
a) modularity vs. "bloatware": (most) Unix programs are designed to fulfil a specific task and accept input from standard interfaces and write output to standard interfaces. When a program does everything it was designed to do, it can be considered "complete" and the author starts a new project for his hobby... In Windows, that is very different. Commercial programs are supposed to generate revenue. The company is forced to always add new compelling reasons for an update/upgrade that they can sell for a lot of money. As soon as all useful features are complete, they try hard to add others *g*. Almost every piece of "nice und useful and almost perfect" commercial software for Windows I've seen was almost unusable three versions / five years later. My mother used to have a little tool for screen captures (free form and certain areas). She really loved it and it was about 1.x MB. But she'd lost the installation disk and just bought the new version. It's now 20 MB, does lots and lots of new things and is complex and frustrating (to my mother). And I don't just mean "new features", I mean completely new functionality. There's this one program everybody used to burn CDs a few years ago... then the product managers thought "Hey, if we burn CDs, let's add some functions for burning music CDs. From your jukebox software. Hey, let's create our own jukebox software. And burn DVDs. With a DVD menu editor. And of course some way of adding and converting video. Oh, let's add our own video editing 'solution'". And so on and so on. The latest version of this CD writer software has turned into a "software suite" that is 700 MB (!!!) if fully installed... This is just one example and it happens to almost every commercial piece of software. I see and understand WHY it happens, but I don't like it. It's really hard to stop this kind of software from annoying you when you're not using it and it constantly wants to remind you of its existence...

b) vendor lock-in
On Windows, most programs are meant to be used "stand alone", i.e. you can install and use them on a fresh copy of Windows with no need to add other software first. They don't rely on other software and that makes a lot of sense considering that this other software would also cost money and you cannot tell your customers: "Yes, you can only use use our 49$ software after buying this other 69$ software and that other 39$ software and ...". For this reason, companies *don't have to* worry about interoperability and standard file formats (for example). They just do their thing and don't worry about anybody else. This is for small programs / companies. For big companies, this is even more drastic: They choose to use a secret and proprietary format so people cannot switch to other software. That's one big difference: commercial software vendors want to keep even the unhappy customers as long as they buy new versions.
On Linux, there are many small projects and it just makes sense to use a standard format to make it easy for people to *start* using your software. If it's free, why shouldn't people just give it a try and only keep using it if they like it... for commercial software: If your competitor keeps his customers from using your software, it just makes more sense to keep your customers from using his software. Right?

I know the above is a somewhat simplified and "idealistic" view of things, but for me, that's the one fundamental difference between Linux and Windows and that's what makes me want to switch, willing to invest some time into learning Linux and willing to put up with some glitches and problems it undoubtedly has... and if the driver problem is "not Linux' fault", I agree with what sb else said: "I don't care! It's a problem I have in Linux but not in Windows, so for me, it is a Linux problem". I just hope that it'll get better and better and hope that the Linux desktop picks up momentum... one more reason to use it ("part time") and add some momentum ;-)

ok, let's see.. have I said it all this time? *g* I don't know, but it's a good start :-D
