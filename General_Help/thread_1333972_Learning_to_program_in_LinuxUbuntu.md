### Post by QIII on 2009-11-22
"Open source" is a cool phrase.  You have to ask yourself what it means.  Is it just a cool catch phrase?

Most good undergraduate programs at State colleges and universities (read:  not enticing you with their geek-coolness and sticking you with a tuition bill three times what it ought to be) are taught in platforms outside of the Microsoft fold.  That is, they use Linux, Solaris, Unix, etc, as their OS platforms.

Many, in fact, expressly do not allow you to use anything Microsoft-based.

An undergraduate BSCS degree taught in that environment will give you every bit as much mastery of the field as a "school for geeks".  How do you think those geeks got to be geeks?  How did I do it?

When you move on to Graduate work, I think you would have to shop around a bit to find a program that even mentioned the "other" OS.  I certainly didn't find that OS in the curriculum.

Don't be fooled by a catch phrase like "Open Source degree".  Find a good State school with a reasonable tuition rate that uses OSs other than Microsoft.  See that they use common languages used to teach you "open source" -- a phrase that I suggest you research a bit.

I think you are being wowed by gimmick advertising from a gimmick "school".

### Post by QIII on 2009-11-22
I'd be willing to bet that the classes you listed from the program at the University of Arizona are taught in C or C++ (probably the latter).  But you would need a good foundation in each.  That means ... (drumroll)... that you are going to have to take some courses in C/C++, among others -- before you can even attempt some of the other courses listed.  If you don't know how to program in C++ (or whatever the language used is), you will NOT be able to "pick it up" during those courses.  You will have to come into them with your feet on the ground and running.
  
Those classes will also likely have prerequisites for Math courses.  One of my Bachelor's degrees is in Applied Mathematics.  An understanding of Mathematics, as well as things like data structures and algorithms, is essential to being able to complete those courses.
 
 Think of it like this:  You can go to the coolest avant-garde art school full of "art geeks"; or you can learn art classically, keep in touch with the more avant-garde community and use the classical basis to move in an an avant-garde direction entirely of your own design.
 
 A flashy avant-garde artist without a sound foundation in (I will capitalize it) Art has nowhere to go when the winds of artistic popularity change.
 
 A developer working on C++ elements of projects has to be first a good C++ developer with a deep understanding of the language and best practices, regardless of whether the project is open source or not.  He has to know not only the fundamentals of the language, but also have an understanding of how the machine works and how his creations will interact with the OS he is targeting.
 
 "Open source technology", as described in the program you cited, is a red herring.  "Open source" is simply a particular approach to the design, development and distribution of software, with the key ingredient that the source code is accessible and available to others.

It is fluid and takes different shapes in different situations in different projects in different development communities.

Sorry.  I am suspicious of a program that offers a degree in "Open source technology".  Open source projects are developed by people who are good developers.

Edit:  After re-reading this, I don't want you to take away a "scolding" tone.  I absolutely encourage you to get as much education and knowledge as you can.  Our profession is always in need of good, well-trained people.  I applaud you for your dedication during what seems like a very circuitous route that has been necessitated by life's insistence on not making things easy on anyone!  I just want to caution you to beware of "cheap imitations."
