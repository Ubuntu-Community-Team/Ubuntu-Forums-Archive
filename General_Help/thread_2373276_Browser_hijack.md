### Post by QIII on 2017-10-04
Maybe, sorta.

It would be hard to say what sort of payload may have been deposited on your machine, what that might do and whether or not it will affect Linux.  You've been "socially engineered" into a compromise of unknown extent.  One should track USPS packages at the USPS website, not some random link.  Someone counted on you to let your guard down for just a moment and be careless.  It happens to the best of us.

At the very, very least I would go to your privacy settings and clear out all of your history items, including cookies, etc.  You might want to create a new Firefox profile.  Also check your default search engine and be sure that it is what you want.

I do frequent system backups and use VMs for random web searches and such.  (I use my physical machine here and other trusted places.)  You can dump a VM if you think it is compromised and go back to an earlier snapshot.  With your primary system, you can restore from a known point from backups.  You might consider such an arrangement.

### Post by efflandt on 2017-10-04
It is best to check were links go before clicking on them. Fortunately Linux itself is not subject to Windows exploits, but certain multiple OS applications like Firefox might be somewhat. If you want to track a UPS package go to ups.com, or fedex.com for a FedEx package or usps.com for tracked mail in the US.

When I got a Chromecast device I was able to configure it with my Android phone (which is does directly without going through WiFi router), but casting did not seem to work in Android or Linux Chrome, so I booted Win7 on a laptop, did a quick web search for chrome, downloaded a file with chrome in its name and installed it. Turned out it was a bogus Binkiland browser that installed adware/malware. I quickly uninstalled the browser, but the adware/malware had gotten into IE and Firefox. Didn't realize that I should have booted to safe mode to run Malwarebytes (didn't work in normal mode), ended up Malwarebytes scanning the drive on my work PC (AVG also quarantined a file). Then booted the drive in safe mode back in the laptop and reinstalled Malwarebytes to clean up the registry. I would not have had those problems if I had gone right to google.com to download Chrome in Windows (which is where I originally got it for Linux). In the end it turned out that I just needed to use the included HDMI extension cable to get a better WiFi signal to the Chromecast.
