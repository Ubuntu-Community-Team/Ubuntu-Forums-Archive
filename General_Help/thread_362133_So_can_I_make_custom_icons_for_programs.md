### Post by WW on 2007-02-15
Disclaimer: I am not a lawyer; my comments are just my (possibly incorrect) impressions of the law.

Whether or not you are going to make money off of it is irrelevant.  The copyright on the artwork belongs to the creator.  If you take some else's art work and use it on your web page without the permission of the creator, you are probably violating the copyright, even if you give credit to the creator.

Consider this analogous scenario involving print instead of the web.  Suppose I publish a science fiction newsletter, which I distribute for free.  I read a cool short story that I want to share with my readers, so I photocopy the story and put it in my newsletter.  It is a pretty safe bet that if the publisher of the story found out, they would insist that I stop publication, and might even sue me for copyright violation.

To be safe, you could stick to using images for which the owner has explicitly stated the conditions of use of the image. There are many icons and pictures out there that have been made available  under something like the GPL (or the Creative Commons license, or some other "free" license).  For example, at [www.iconbazaar.com](www.iconbazaar.com) you will find a "Conditions of Use" page that lets you use up to 20 images at no cost.   At iconfactory.org, they say their icons are available for "personal, desktop use".  You would have to check with them if that includes using one of their icons on your personal web page.
