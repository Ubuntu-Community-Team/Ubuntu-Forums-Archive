### Post by mb_webguy on 2008-09-13
Well, you have to consider that the page I linked to is only one small message in a long and often heated discussion.

But personally I see his point.  DRM is bad, but saying that it should use GPLv3 because that license puts restrictions on DRM is like saying that an automobile engine manufacturer should add restrictions on the use of their engines to cars with organic rubber tires.  The engine is completely separate from the tires, and putting some kind of silly limitations on the use of the engine based on the tires would only limit the desirability of that engine for automobile manufacturers and therefore the usefulness of the engine to the car-driving public.  Even in that post, Torvalds states that he's personally against DRM, but feels that it's a separate issue and has nothing to do with the kernel.

Also, Linux contains the work of innumerable people, who would all have to agree on a change in the license of their work, which isn't likely to happen.  Changing the Linux license simply isn't very feasible.

Also, as Torvalds mentions in that post, there's a big difference between Open Source and Free Software (in reference to the Free Software Foundation).  Open Source is an approach to development.  Free Software is a political and ideological position.  Torvalds wants to take Linux out of that particular ideo-political argument and focus on making it the best open-source OS available.  

I mean, come one.  People have been begging for a Linux version of iTunes since Apple first released it (though personally I could do without).  Do you think we'd *ever* see iTunes for Linux if Linux adopted GPLv3?  Of course not.
