### Post by pmooney78 on 2009-10-09
Ouch. Ouch ouch ouch. I am *so* sorry.

I worked for a cell phone company in customer service for several years. Not Verizon, but I still understand the general mentality and workings of cell phone companies, and can give you some general advice on how to deal with the human culture at Verizon.

First: Chances are that, when dealing with a billing problem of this magnitude, Verizon is going to investigate before deciding to give you any billing credit on your bill. Moreover, chances are that all of their calls are recorded. This means that it's incredibly important that you (a) have a plausible story, and (b) are consistent about that story throughout your interactions with the company. They'll quite likely listen to all of your recorded calls and go over the notes about your interactions with them, possibly all the way back through your customer relationship with them. This is a big issue; they're going to check into it.

Next: Turn on your cell phone. I know, I know ... but it's hard to imagine how you could know about this situation without having received the text messages. Chances are, Verizon bills you for the text message when their system receives it, not when it's delivered to your handset. If you have a way of finding that out without talking to them, do so ... but don't call them to ask (because their calls are recorded, and chances are their operators leave notes on your account about every interaction with you, anyway). Moreover, your friends and family need to be able to reach you if there's an emergency, and chances are, they'll try to use your cell phone for that.

Then, call Verizon. Tell them what happened in terms that you would be able to figure out if you hadn't caused the problem yourself, emphasizing that you're concerned about your bill. Keep it simple. ("I got hundreds of thousands of messages on my cell phone. I'm not sure know how it happened, exactly. I'm terrified to get my bill.") DON'T volunteer any more information than that. Let them ask questions.

Being rude, abusive, or angry is counterproductive. You want to make allies. Remember that your interactions are being recorded and may influence top-level people subconsciously if they listen to the recordings as part of their research. Being polite wins you allies. Being freaked out and terrified elicits sympathy in many employees. Carefully control how they perceive you ... it's more important than you probably appreciate.

From their perspective, you ****** up, even if they believe that you didn't cause the problem yourself (intentionally or otherwise). Being able to receive text messages without having an unlimited plan is rolling the dice, in their eyes. Remember that you're going to get further asking for sympathy and mercy than by demanding something and being belligerent.

Ask them at this point if you can go onto an unlimited text messaging program retroactively. They may very well let you do so ... if so, your problems are mostly solved. Get them to be very specific about how your billing will work. Make them say explicitly that you will not be billed for the messages, since you're going onto the unlimited plan retroactively, so that if your bill is wrong later, they can hear that that particular promise was made on the recording. Moreover, ask the employee to whom you're speaking to put a note on your account explicitly documenting what promises that they made you. Ask them to read the note describing your call back to you verbatim so you can be ABSOLUTELY SURE that it does, in fact, document whatever they told you. You're dealing with a six-figure billing problem ... it's important to make sure that what someone who gets paid $9/hour told you is actually recorded in their system.

If the person you're talking to asks you *any* questions about what happened that require you to provide more information, assume that you're not talking to a complete ******* idiot. At that point, provide the kind of information you would have if you hadn't caused the problem yourself. ("Well, they're the kind of messages I get in relation to my website ... but I've *never* had *anywhere* near this many before! It's thousands and thousands of times more than I normally get! I can't imagine ... maybe a hacker, or ... I don't know ...")

Remember that they're quite likely to assess you not only based on the technical information they can see, but also on how convincing your performance is. If you're too eager to volunteer a complete, packaged explanation of what must have gone wrong, it'll look suspicious. Be somewhat confused. Think hard ahead of time about what kind of information you'd be likely to actually have. It may be helpful to role-play the interaction with someone first. DON'T LIE about what kind of text messages you received, or claim that they're anything other than what they were. It's quite likely that they're able to look back at them, and your scrupulous honesty about verifiable facts will impress them.

If the person you're initially talking to says that there's nothing they can do for you, and that you're just screwed, thank them politely for their time and ask to talk to their supervisor. Remember that someone at the lowest possible level in the company hierarchy is probably not authorized to give you $100,000 in credit, anyway. The farther you move up the supervisor chain, the more likely you are to get someone who can actually help you. You don't want to just ask for a supervisor immediately, though, partly because you might be able to activate unlimited text-messaging retroactively, and partly because you don't want to look like you're trying to work the system. Again, be polite but freaked-out ... you're trying to make allies.

If the supervisor can't help you, ask to talk to THEIR supervisor. Repeat until you've been kicked up the supervisor chain as far as you can. Eventually, you'll get someone whose supervisor doesn't take calls (realistically, there's a supervisor at some level whose boss is an administrator, not a customer-service person) ... the highest-level person you can reach on the phone. If THAT person can't help you, ask what they'd recommend you do. Do that.

If you get all the way up the supervisor chain and they can't help you, start over from scratch. Call in, talk to the lowest-level person, give them your convincing, carefully prepared story, be polite, ask for their help, and then get kicked up the supervisor chain several times. If you get to the top and they can't help you, ask them what they'd recommend you do. Repeat this entire process, say, four or five times before you give up. What you're hoping is that you eventually get someone who is able and willing to help you ... you want to make sure that you talk to enough people that you actually are running up against company policy, not just belligerent, underpaid employees.

If all else fails, write a polite letter to the company president at corporate headquarters, keeping the guidelines above in mind. A helpful resource is planetfeedback.com, which will probably give you the address to use and name of the president. Chances that it will be read by the CEO himself/herself are slim, but it will be read by someone who can make actual decisions.

Good luck. Hope this helps.
