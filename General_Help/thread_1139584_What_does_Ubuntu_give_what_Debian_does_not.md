### Post by CatKiller on 2009-05-03
> **teel said:**
> The question is: what is Ubuntu better with than Debian nowadays?

As far as I'm aware, this forum is the big selling point for Ubuntu.

Canonical is a tiny company compared to the likes of Novell or Red Hat. They aren't in a position to start huge projects; they have simply been able to attract a community of people that like the package that they've put together, and this community is what drives the direction of Ubuntu. This community is largely made up of users who are new to the whole idea of GNU/Linux, and so the direction that Ubuntu has taken has largely been to help this class of user.

Debian's Free policy, while laudable, hasn't seemed to chime with the masses in quite the same way that Ubuntu's try-to-make-things-friendly policy has. But Debian and Ubuntu are still extremely closely related, and improvements still go both ways.

If you've moved on enough that this community isn't as relevant to you as it might be for other users, and you prefer Debian, then using Debian seems like the obvious choice.

### Post by adamfaulkner1 on 2009-05-03
Debian has a stronger ideological stance against proprietary software. Debian has a greater emphasis on stable software, hence the "stable" version that is sometimes lagging 5 years behind the current technology (like etch, which was stable until a few months ago, had a 2.4 kernel).

My experience with Debian vs Ubuntu has been negetive. Ubuntu is geared toward new users, so stuff tends to "just work". For instance, under debian I had to download a weird version of wireless driver, compile, disable network manager, and create startup scripts to call iwconfig commands, where as Ubuntu's network manager handles everything out of the box.
