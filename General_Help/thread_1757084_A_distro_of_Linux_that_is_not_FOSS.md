### Post by srs5694 on 2011-05-13
I think you misunderstand what "free" means in the context of "free software." The word refers to freedom, not to price. Anybody can charge whatever they want for software that's released under the GPL, or most other open source licenses. Want a copy of the Linux kernel source code? I'll send one to you (or anybody in the continental US) on CD-R by overnight mail for $1000. That's a legal offer, and I'll honor it if anybody takes me up on it, but I don't expect anybody to do so because it can be downloaded at no charge (other than Internet fees from your ISP) from [http://www.kernel.org](http://www.kernel.org). A Linux distribution that contains nothing but open source software can also be sold by third parties. That's what outfits like [LinuxCD](http://linuxcd.org/) and [CheapBytes](http://www.cheapbytes.com/) do.

It's also possible to bundle a combination of open source and proprietary software. Such a distribution may not be legally redistributed except under whatever terms the proprietary software's copyright holder dictates. There have been a number of such distributions over the years. Most have fared badly and/or been very specialized things. (TiVos, for instance, run on a combination of Linux and proprietary software.) It's entirely possible that this SMART Linux is such a beast. I've only skimmed the site you've referenced, but it sounds like it's a Linux distribution that includes proprietary forensic data analysis tools.

The open source licenses used by most Linux software (the GPL, the BSD license, etc.) permit such bundling, and even sale of the bundle. Some licenses, such as the GPL, impose the requirement that the distributor of such a bundle make the source code to the licensed (GPLed) software available, as well as any changes to that source code; but adding a user-space application, even a proprietary one, does not necessitate changing the source code to the kernel or other open source programs. Just because the Linux kernel (as one example) is under the GPL does not give the authors of that software any right to dictate terms on the distribution of another program in the bundle.

Thus, chances are this is perfectly legitimate. As an open source author myself, I have no problem with it -- at least, unless they don't comply with the license terms of the software they didn't write. For instance, if they were to modify my gdisk program and not release their changes, I'd want them to make good on gdisk's GPL terms. I see no evidence that they're violating the GPL or other open source licenses, though.

### Post by srs5694 on 2011-05-13
Some random additional comments:

I first started using Linux in late 1994, at a time when my Internet access was limited to dial-up modems. I downloaded Slackware Linux once or twice, but even with the much smaller size of Linux distributions at that time, it was a major hassle. Then I started buying CD-ROM sets. I've still got a couple of InfoMagic sets (from 1994 and 1997). The 1994 set has such gems as kernel source "up to 1.1.72" and four distributions: Slackware 2.1, SLS 1.06, Debian 0.91 beta, and TAMU 1.0-D.

Some licenses, such as the BSD license, do permit using the code in non-OSS products. IIRC, much of the BSD TCP/IP stack made its way into Windows. The GPL is designed to prevent this from happening. Which is better depends on your philosophy about such things.

I, too, have donated hardware and money to open source projects. I recall once shipping a video card to Germany so that an XFree86 developer could improve X's support for that chipset. Since I've released gdisk, I've been the recipient of donations, too, and they can be helpful -- I recently bought a new broadband router from NewEgg using my PayPal balance from donations over the past two or three months, for instance. I'm not exactly getting rich off of gdisk, but that's OK -- it's one way of giving back for all the open source software I've used over the years.
