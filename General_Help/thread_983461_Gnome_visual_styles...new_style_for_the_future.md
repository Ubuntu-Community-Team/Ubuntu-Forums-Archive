### Post by haran_elessar on 2008-11-15
Hi everyone,

I was talking with a friend the other day about the visual style of ubuntu.  He was telling me that Ubuntu needed a change from the usual brown and orange look.  He sent me examples of possible different looks for ubuntu.  The Dust theme was one of those examples.  I'm actually using Dust since then although I do like the brown and orange theme.

I know that the visual style of ubuntu brings a lot of heated discussion.  There are camps that want a drastic change and there are others who want to retain the traditional brown and orange human theme.  Talking to him about those discussions I realized we are forgetting about something...Ubuntu is all about the user.

I for one think we don't need to focus that much in the visual style.  As it is right now Ubuntu can look as you wish it to look. It can even look better than competing OSs.  It is a highly customizable system and every user can tweak it to his personal preference...with that in mind I came up with an alternative to the visual style debate.

Check out the following idea:
When you do a fresh install of ubuntu it would be nice to have a mini introduction to ubuntu.  A pop up window with 10 slides or so where the developers can better explain the idea and benefits of Ubuntu to its new users.  One of those slides should be about the possibility of tweaking the visual style to your liking.

I'm thinking in something like saying: "ubuntu is all about you...it can look as you want it to look...click here for an example"  The user then clicks a button and the theme instantly changes from human to dust for example...new wallpaper and everything.  That way the user would have a nice example of how easily you could drastically change the look of your desktop.  It will also motivate him to look for other alternatives.  This is because as I said before Ubuntu can look better than OSX as it is right now by tweaking it...and for some people it looks better than OSX with the human theme.  Instead of "forcing" a visual style to the masses we should encourage them to come up with their visual style.  Contrary to OSX and Windows it is fairly easy to completely change the look of your desktop in Ubuntu...that's a strength that we have that we are under exploiting.

With that in mind new, easier tools could be created for people to build new themes (even newbs).  A more comprehensive database of the available themes should also be built.  It would be awesome if you could browse all available themes in the add/remove application.  In summary make it easier for people to change the look of gnome.

The way I see it we shouldn't focus so much on making ubuntu look flashier.  I think we should focus on making it more compatible and easier for everyone to make the change from their old OS to ubuntu.  That way Ubuntu will be able to catch more of that mainstream market it wants to attract.  People don't have fear of ubuntu because it's ugly...they are afraid because it's something new for them.  Because of that we have to keep building ways to make that transition easier and friendlier.  As it is today Gnome has the capability of looking very flashy...we just need to let users know how to if that is what they want.  We should let them know that contrary to other OSs in Ubuntu thinks can look as they want.  Instead of concentrating efforts on the visuals most resources should be concentrated on making the transition easier.  Great strides have been done in that respect over the last few years and we should continue focusing on that to make even more progress.

I also think ccms should be completely pre-installed in the next release.  It should also come with some preset settings from the new user can choose.  For example, there could be a preset for burning windows, beaming up when opening, sidekick when minimizing and maximizing and another one completely different.  As I explain with the theme before the user would be able to choose one when going through that mini tutorial.  We can exhort them to try one of the pre sets and remind them they can come up with new pre sets by playing inside ccsm.

That mini-tutorial/introduction I think is necessary whether it is used with examples mentioned above or not.  I think Ubuntu has many distinguishing features that many people are not familiar with.   The add/ remove application should be a highlight of that introduction.  We can advertise how easy it is to find and install programs....no need to open a browser and no need to restart!! It's just a two clicks affair...windows users will be amazed by that.

well those are my two cents...I'm eager to see what you think of those ideas.

(just in case I hate mini tutorials to new things...but it's because they are cheesy, boring and useless...we can break that trend and make a very cool and enjoyable one)
