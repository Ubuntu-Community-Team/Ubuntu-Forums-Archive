### Post by Taxman415a on 2008-08-04
To fully solve the audio interference it's best to get your audio processing outside of the electrically noisy inside of the computer. Here's a listing of USB audio devices of which you should be able to find a compatible USB sound card. [http://www.qbik.ch/usb/devices/showdevcat.php?id=7](http://www.qbik.ch/usb/devices/showdevcat.php?id=7)
I found it from the links at the bottom of [https://wiki.ubuntu.com/HardwareSupport/](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/HardwareSupport/)
That could allow you to save the old motherboard if you want and if you like classical music, you may want to do that anyway.

Another thing to consider is a motherboard that supports coreboot. [http://www.coreboot.org](http://www.coreboot.org)
That allows some pretty cool features but it doesn't seem to be many new motherboards.

Im going to disagree with the USB sound card statement.  while there is electrical noise inside the case it can be canceled out by placing the card in a metal box.  This is what a lot of the high end audio card manufacturers do.  The reason why it is better to put it inside the case with a metal box as opposed to outside with USB is that the USB bus is pretty sluggish and it is much better to use something like pcie 1x or even an old PCI slot.

I guess it depends on how much money you have to spend on a sound card. More to the point though, what audio applications require more throughput than USB, particularly USB 2.0? Even DVD Audio at 8.5 GB for one hour of audio (I'm not sure the bit rates even get that high, this is a back of the envelope calculation) is 2.3 MB per second, well within what USB 2.0 can do.
