### Post by Phil_McCrackin on 2006-05-06
I think it has it's place. It's a subset of C++. It codes very similar (and the logic is identical) it just does a lot for you. Which of course gives you less control, but it's a trade off. It will do a lot of the same as C++ but more limited (ex: you may only inherit from one base class whereas in C++ you may inherit from as many as you can keep track of). Plus it just handles a lot of other coding behind the scenes that in C/C++ you have to do yourself. There again, this means you are not in control of those details any longer and it is not quite as efficient as if you coded it specifically for YOUR application.

Java was created for cross-platform web applications and it is very good for that.

That's one of the things I really like about Linux... I have so much control over my system. I love to have control over my PC. It means I can stream line it to do only what I need it to in the most efficient way possible.

### Post by ZylGadis on 2006-05-06
Hey windows wiz ;)
I'm glad you like linux and this community. For some yet unknown reason the community is much better than your average geeky one or, on the reverse, your average user one. I guess the mix of knowledgeable people and newbies helps.
I can't give advice for c/cpp, as I don't use them much (when I do, I go through vi or nano or smth similar). As for Java, though, definitely try NetBeans. Eclipse was all the hype three years ago, and actually started as an answer to the laziness of NetBeans developers and the correspongind lack of features of NetBeans. Competition is a good thing, though, and enormous strides ahead by the NetBeans team have left Eclipse eating dirt over the past year. Eclipse is still being driven by the hype and the large number of people who simply have not tried the new NetBeans yet.
And with Mustang looming on the horizon, which is supposed to make Swing about twice as good as before, you definitely don't want that abomination called SWT :) 
Another point - Java as a language and a platform has also taken enormous strides over the past two or three years. Currently Java's speed of execution is on par with C++, which speaks volumes for a VM-based language. Java's main resource hog remains memory consumption, which is the price for the fact it is impossible to leak memory in Java.
