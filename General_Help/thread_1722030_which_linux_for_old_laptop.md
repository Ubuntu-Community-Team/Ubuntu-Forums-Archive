### Post by 3Miro on 2011-04-05
Ubuntu family: Xubuntu and if that doesn't work, go for Lubuntu. in general I try XFCE first, then go to LXDE. LXDE is lighter, but XFCE has features I would rather not part with.

If you have the time and nerves to spend, you will get best performance with Gentoo + XFCE or LXDE.

Then comes Arch (assuming the CPU supports i686). Arch is much easier to install (although it is still advanced).

Other than that: Salix (XFCE) or CentOS (if you can set it up with XFCE or LXDE).

People tend to recommend Puppy, but I have not tried it myself.

I suggest UbuntuI agree 200%! For very old laptops, with less than 512MB of RAM (and less than 1GHz of speed maybe also, but I haven't tested this) you may have to make adjustments. On laptops with PII and PIII and 256MB of RAM I have found that:

- Ubuntu works, but is slow.
- Xubuntu works better, but is still too slow for comfort.
- Any LXDE based Linux works best.
- Chromium may be a better browser than Firefox.

I run Ubuntu on the following:

- 1.2 GHz Pentium M.
- 1,276 MB of RAM (maximum allowed).
- 4200 RPM hard drive (yikes!).

It runs very well, with boot taking about 50-55 seconds, and shut down taking all of five seconds. There are no issues whatsoever. This is twice as fast as a fresh installation of Win XP, and about ten times faster than a six month old, updated Win XP.

I did try Lubuntu, Peppermint, Puppy, Xubuntu with XFCE or LXDE, and found the improvements to be very slight (less than ten seconds faster to boot, for example).

With your machine, I suspect that you will notice the same. There's no good reason to deprive yourself of a full version of Ubuntu for such minor improvements in performance that will not be noticed during normal usage. If your hard drive is faster than mine, you'll get big improvements over my performance (mine is a ultra-portable, and used the slow hard drive to reduce heat).

One thing I do tend to do with older machines though, is use LTS (Long Term Support) releases only. The current is 10.04 Lucid Lynx (not 10.10 Maverick Meerkat), and that will remain current until 12.04 in 2012. Why? Because the hardware is older, there's no point in taking the risk of breaking a working installation on an older machine since it probably isn't going to be able to make use of the latest and greatest features of intermediate releases anyway.

Best of luck, and try that Live CD first to check if your wireless is working etc. etc. I've done this on four or five old laptops in the past year, and have yet to have a major problem (Dell, Toshiba, Compaq/HP).
