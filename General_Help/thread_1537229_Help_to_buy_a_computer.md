That means that you are getting 2 sticks, both 1GB in size. The motherboard can probably use up to 4GB sticks in each slot, but without know the exact model chipset that is just an educated guess.

Unless you live somewhere that has very expensive DDR3, I'd get a 'real' AM3 motherboard using DDR3 over a AM2+ DDR2 motherboard. 

Gigabyte is a decent brand, and has decent manuals which does make things easier. (ha, I can tell is a gigabyte from the info you posted ;)  ) 

> **BSG Fan said:**
> 
Okay, another question: 
I saw my old 'motherboard' and saw it has lot of cables... 
How the heck I know what cable go to what other component (Hard disk, CD/DVD rom, Floppy, on/off button, Desktop Processor, etc? 


As for how hard it is to do the actual assembly, its not that bad. It can be fiddly, the switches and power/HDD LEDs are probably the hardest bit. They are the only things you connect to the motherboard that can be installed 2 ways (one of which is wrong).  

HDDs/DVD drives the cables can only be inserted one way (doesnt matter if its a SATA or PATA connector) and the same goes for the power connectors for the HDD, DVD, motherboard, etc.. 

CPUs can only be inserted one way as well. 

BTW, unless you are planning on using the CD/DVD drive from your current computer, you are missing  a CD/DVD drive. I wouldnt use a floppy these days, it just takes up power, space in the case, and is pretty much useless for me. You might have a use for a floppy though, I dont know.
