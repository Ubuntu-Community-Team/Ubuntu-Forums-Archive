### Post by DrSturgeon on 2006-06-17
No matter how much aysiu hates discussing it, there are some distinct ideological differences between Gnome and KDE that will often make or break a user's experience.  As a rule of thumb, former OSX users tend to feel more at home in Gnome, and former Windows users tend to feel more at home in KDE.  No, this is not always true.

Here are some distinct differences:

**1. Icon Style Ideology**

You could create a spectrum of operating systems that orders them from "matte" to "glossy," or "least shiny" to "most shiny."  That spectrum would look like this:

--Windows Classic
--Windows XP
--Gnome
--Mac OS X
--Windows Vista
--KDE

In my opinion, KDE takes the shininess a little too far.  If you take a look at the [Tango Icon Theme Guidelines](http://tango.freedesktop.org/Tango_Icon_Theme_Guidelines) (scroll all the way to the bottom of that page), their example of an "overdone shiny, reflective highlight [that] obscures the actual content" reminds me of a few too many KDE icons.

Worth pointing out, right next to that example Tango shows another frequent mistake: icons with indistinguishable shapes.  The back button they use as an example is, ignoring color, almost identical to the one in Konqueror.  The default theme at least has clearly drawn icons, and the Konqueror toolbar is by far the worst example, but there are some very popular alternative icon themes for KDE (**cough** NuoveXT **cough**) that almost make you memorize the position of toolbar buttons, because telling them apart is next to impossible.

While we're at it, this can be our first demonstration of why people think KDE is more "Windows-Like."  Next time you have to use IE, take a look at its toolbar.  Why is every button shaped like that stupid Document icon with a clipped corner?  With OSX, Apple focused on logical usability, which includes distinguishable and modest icons.  Gnome has done the same.


**2. Themes**

Everyone will tell you that installing themes is easier in Gnome.  This is more or less uncontestable.  In Gnome, you literally drag the link straight from your web browser into the theme preferences, and it is installed.  KDE often requires you to use configure scripts and make.

To KDE's credit, themes themselves are more customizable.  In Gnome, well, they just aren't.  In Gnome, you better like the color of your favorite style, or hope someone has put together a different version, because there's no changing it.

For the record, and not necessarily to Gnome's credit, here is more evidence of the KDE/Windows vs. Gnome/OSX dichotomy.  KDE handles theme customization in a very similar way to Windows.  Neither Gnome or OSX *allow* you to customize themes.


**3. Applications**

Day to day use of KDE is centered around a number of super-applications, and many of them are fantastic programs.  Of course, the obvious example is Konqueror: the application that does everything.  But there are more:  K3b is the "everything you would ever want to do with a CD burner" application.  Amarok is the "everything you would ever want to do with music" application.

K3B (despite the fact that, at least in Kubuntu, cd burning permissions seem to be a mess) and Amarok are both powerful programs.  Konqueror will soon be cooking your dinner.  As far as funcionality goes, KDE applications are generally unparalleled by anything in the Gnome world.

In Gnome, we find the opposite organization:  there is no super-application to control your CD burner; instead, CD burning functionality is found throughout Nautilus or handled by a task-specific program.  (Like that one for burning audio CDs that I forget the name of; I'm not actually on a Linux box right now.)

And of course, Gnome has no Konqueror.  File browsing is Nautilus; web browsing is Epiphany or Firefox.  The rest of Konqueror's functionality is split across smaller, more task-specific applications.

The Gnome way is considered more Unix-like, and personally I think it is more usable and more intuitive.

BUT, for someone who is used to Windows, the KDE way may feel more at home.  Explorer, in some form, is both a Web Browser and a file manager.  K3b feels like a weird merging of Nero and EZ CD Creator.

Off-topic for a moment:  does anybody actually know what the relationship between Internet Explorer and Windows Explorer is?  You can browse the web from a Windows Explorer window, and you can perform file operations from an Internet Explorer window, and yet they group seperately in the taskbar, and each window will stay grouped with the type of windows it was originally opened as.  What's the logic behind this one?  I suppose it's Microsoft's way of trying to differentiate between a file browsing experience and a web browsing experience, something that Konqueror doesn't do very well.

Oh, but for the record, Konqueror is the awesomest name ever for a web browser; at least in a Navigator vs. Explorer world.

And also for the record, I already mentioned how the KDE application model matches the Windows application model, so, using the same CD burning example, it might be worth making the comparison between Gnome and OSX, yet again.  OSX spreads CD burning functionality across several programs (iTunes, Finder, Disk Utility) instead of providing a super-application for it.


**4. User Interface Behavior**

I always find this one hard to describe.

KDE seems a little snappier.  Things also seem to happen more smoothly.  I think most people will tell you that KWin (by the way, is it pronounced "K-Win" or "Quinn"?) is a better designed window manager than Metacity.  Personally I think the Metacity zoom rectangles on minimize are ugly, and the KWin middle-click to vertical maximize is brilliant, so I suppose I'd agree.

As far as organization goes (settings windows and stuff), Gnome seems to tend toward more windows that do fewer things each, KDE tends to us a bunch of tabs.  There are arguments for either, I don't think it matters.

I've always thought Gnome did a better job with text in dialog boxes and on buttons.  Seems more friendly, more descriptive, and more intuitive.

KDE settings tends to use apply buttons to apply settings.  Gnome settings tend to apply instantly.  I think this makes Gnome feel more advanced, or something, but arguably the KDE way is safer, and less annoying.

Oh, and apply buttons are a very *Windows-like* control.  All OSX settings apply instantly.


**5. Programmability**

I'm not a software developer, but I've heard great things about developing in Qt (KDE).  It's probably also the best way to write cross-platform software; Qt runs comfortably on all three major platforms.

The Mono project (open source .net) has been doing some really cool stuff on Gnome.  Although, Qt applications are much faster and less resource intensive than Mono, so I tend to think that KDE wins on this front.


**6. Stability**

KDE crashes more, and it is more severe when it does.  This is just personal experience.

(Hey, Windows crashes more too!)


**7. Conclusion**

You really have to try both of them.  I keep going back to Gnome.  Not sure why, maybe it's because I'm a former Mac user.

Which brings me to...tallying up why KDE is "Windows-like."

1. Shiny like Windows Vista
2. Indistinguishable icons
3. Theme Customization
4. Super-applications
5. Combined File and Web Browser
6. Apply Buttons
7. Less stable

They also *try* to copy Windows.  I'm not sure if it's still there, but for a long time, right clicking on a control in KDE would yield the "What's This?" menu item.  Straight out of Windows 98.


Whew.  Okay, them's my thoughts.

### Post by wyth on 2006-06-17
I think Dr. Sturgeon gave the better run-down so far, but I use both, so maybe this will help some.

I like Gnome and KDE each for specific things.  As stated, Gnome is just a bit more sanely oraganized on the desktop.  The applications menu is drop-dead easy.  The KDE kicker can be as simple as the Gnome menu, but you'll have to set that up yourself.  There are some scripts floating around on kde-look that will separate the Gnome apps in the kicker into their own places, and you can use Alacarte in Gnome to do the same.

I need to access remote storage where I host my web page and other work for school.  Here I think KDE wins.  I could set up a connection directly to my home directory fast and set it as a bookmark, and it integrates as seamlessly as any other folder in my OS.  Gnome was a little trickier to set up and doesn't integrate as nicely.  Is it a big deal?  Not at all.  But KDE is just a little easier (and faster) on this aspect.

I love how Konqueror handles something like my mp3 player.  It's flash-based, and all I need to do is plug it in, and I can drag and drop and change my mp3 list to my heart's content. I can't do it that simply in Gnome or Windows, where I need some other software to manage the drive.  Gnome identifies the player as a player and wants to treat it like an iPod (which it isn't).  It's much simpler to just open a folder, see a list of mp3's, and drag/drop/delete at will.  

I'm not a huge fan of using the file manager for my browser, so I rarely use Konqueror for browsing.  On occasion, though, it comes in handing, especially for testing purposes, and I'll admit, it's pretty snappy.  And some multimedia integrates more fluidly into Konqueror than Firefox.  

On the glossy/fisher-pricey/kiddy feel of KDE/Gnome/whatever, that's what themes are for.  As soon as I aptitude install kubuntu-desktop, I get nuoveXT as my icon theme and make that the default.  I have a pre-made color scheme that's more subtle, and with nuoveXT KDE looks as nice and professional as anything.  And with the speed bump you get with Kubuntu, it makes for an overall nice experience.

However, I've always had problems with a few things in Kubuntu that work better in Gnome.  Amarok never seems to play more than 3 songs for me before it starts spinning its wheels.  Banshee looks like a great alternative in Gnome, but it's still a bit immature. But all this may be a bit of a moot discussion in a year or two; I was checking out Linux Format yesterday, and there was an article about something that was announced earlier last year, that the KDE and Gnome developers were going to start working a little more closely together and sharing some code.  They (Linux Format) called it Knome.  It may not be for everyone, but I could see where I'd be interested.  In the forums, [poofyhairguy has already messed with a version of this, stealing some of KDE's window management functionality and zazz for gnome]("http://www.ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=115974").

I gotta agree with the general snappiness issues.  One of the reasons I keep going into Gnome is because I like hunting for ways to make it snappier --it's just a puzzle.  KDE already has those tweaks as standard.  I can't quite explain why, but I seem to always go one more about KDE and outwardly praise it a bit more, but it's Gnome that I keep as my standard; this may be just due to the stability issues mentioned above, but we'll see what happens when KDE 4 comes out, or if something like a Knome project ever gets off the ground.
