### Post by mikewhatever on 2008-09-12
If anyone's wondering what the OP is talking about, read below.

> [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brasso](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brasso)
It is used to polish CDs, DVDs, screens, and pools in order to repair scratches. It is a mild solvent and a mild abrasive, so when applied to the reflective surface of the disc and rubbed radially (in straight lines between the edge and center), it can smooth scratches and reduce their effect. Although the effect is invisible to the naked eye (and often makes the surface look more opaque), it can tremendously improve the ability of many disc drives to read the disc.

### Post by niteshifter on 2008-09-12
Hi,

The Brasso trick is handy. But not very effective for really deep (> 0.1 mm) scratches. For those, get thee to a automotive supplies store and pick a can of Rubbing Compound, Extra Fine (1500 grit). Apply and work radially with elbow grease / electric toothbrush, finish with the Brasso trick.

I came up with this after a cheap portable dvd player ate several discs by not mounting the disc level on the spindle and ground deep circumferential grooves in the outer few mm's of the disc surface. 

The cloudiness that Brasso leaves can give some players fits. A cheap, easily obtained source of extremely fine abrasive is find someone who smokes - cigarette ash and water worked with gauze (cheesecloth) will put a nice shiny finish back on the surface.

### Post by Taxman415a on 2008-09-12
The electric toothbrush is not a bad idea. While on the topic I thought I'd add mine.

I checkout enough discs from the library that are in unplayable condition that I made a set up to avoid the elbow grease and get it done faster. I bought some rubbing compound and a buffing wheel that I mount onto a drill. Then I cut a thin (3/8" or so) board a bit larger than a CD/DVD and use a wing nut and bolt and washer to attach the disc to it. I came up with the idea to use the board to keep the disc from bending and the bolt makes a nice handle. A few passes on low speed (radially out from the center as niteshifter mentioned) can take out any scratch that hasn't made it to the data layer. You do have to be careful of course. I've never had a problem with heating the plastic, probably because I use a low speed and don't stay in one spot too long, but some people warn about melting the plastic. As mentioned above rubbing compound varies from a bit to a lot more aggressive than brasso, but every player I've used plays discs with the rubbing comound haze on it perfectly. If you really wanted to do it right, you could get a 3 part system such as 3M sells of different grades of rubbing/buffing compound so that the last makes a perfectly mirror finish.
