### Post by mivo on 2007-11-05
I agree with the advice to try them out and see how they work for you. I use Ubuntu on three different machines, and while 7.10 required some special care on my new desktop (which is mainly the fault of the motherboard maker), I am quite happy with the distro.

I tried out Fedora 8 last week and was quite impressed with it; definitely polished. Ubuntu has the largest community and the "level of knowledge" that is required to ask questions and get help is relatively low, meaning that people here will help you even if you ask a question that you are worried about asking. Fedora users tend to be more technical, so you will find fewer answers to trivial questions. Ubuntu makes a good beginner distro if your hardware is fully supported, yet still is a "real" Linux and suited for everyone, including experts.

But really, they are both good distros, and they both have large communities. I prefer a Debian-based distro like Ubuntu because I'm a bit more familiar with the tools and packages, and because Ubuntu feels like home -- it is the distro that made me ditch Windows completely. But it is all Linux! They are all good, just slightly different. :)

redhat vs fedora.Fedora core *is* Redhat to all intents and purposes except you don't pay for it. Ubuntu and Fedora are quite different:
[LIST]
[*]Fedora uses RPM which, IMHO is not as good a packaging system.
[*]Fedora has a better installer (Anaconda - sorry folks but its true).
[*]Fedora has a nicer grub screen (Like that matters)
[*]Ubuntu is much better on laptops the Fedora
[*]Fedora uses SELinux for security
[*]Fedora has OK support but people tend to stay with much older releases for much longer than with Ubuntu.
[*]Ubuntu has excellent support and outstanding system help.[/LIST] Like the previous poster says - with the exception of RHEL they are free so try them out. If RH really flicks your switch there is another free version of it - CentOS of which I have heard good things all though I've not tried it.

### Post by gnomeuser on 2007-11-06
I'd say Fedora but I like my distributions to be beautiful, work with upstream, spend time, money and efforts on contributing to bring Linux forwards. Leading new solutions like PackageKit, PolicyKit, ConsoleKit come out of the Fedora community as well as the fact that Fedora works as the case for massive projects as well as the base on which much of the desktop you use on any machine is maintained by Fedora personel.  Innovation, investment and quality are things I value.

It all comes down to what you value, if you want a desktop that has years support with paid tech support to call at all out of the day, none of the community projects really offer this. This is a good venue to make a business which is why Red Hat, Novell and Canonical to some degree use this model to solve cost issues and turn a profit.

I'd look at my needs and my passions to determine this. Instead of asking which is better, say "I need so and so - I don't mind upgrading every 6-12 months". When wondering why someone else doesn't comply to the result you got, try to figure out how their needs and passions might differ.

### Post by toupeiro on 2007-11-07
There most certainly IS a root user in ubuntu, they just have sudo configured in a way that anyone can sudo execute anything. (which is the first thing I change in a business environment)

Large companies will continue to use RHEL because 1) exposure 2) vendor certification programs and standards 3) 3rd party vendor support.  Motorola, Halliburton, Paradigm, DGI, and EarthDecision Sciences and many other very large vendors that release UNIX and Linux based applications do not have standards in place yet for ubuntu, but they do for RHEL.  Naturally, a company will go with what is known to work and be supported if their base business will depend on it.  (Precisely why Canonical needs to get with these vendors and promote ubuntu the way Redhat has promoted RHEL to software companies.)
