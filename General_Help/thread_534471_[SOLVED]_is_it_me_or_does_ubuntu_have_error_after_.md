### Post by Neobuntu on 2007-08-25
Perhaps he is saying that Ubuntu release quality is slipping. Perhaps he is wondering why negligible new benefits also come with lost benefits, from the last release.

Perhaps we should look at how we are doing things. Can we turn this into a positive?

True, specifics would help but what if the general Ubuntu users/supporters/promoters stop seeing a major Ubuntu advantage? What defines Ubuntu?

Look, if you're a Windows using newbie, there is no question to me, open software and it's many custom fitting distributions are indeed overall better. You should NOT also just run away from an open system, when just any problem arises. It's like your ship has come in but your afraid of unloading it. It is change denial.

The fact is, (if you want to be a bit pessimistic) all total software operating systems stink; to some user unfriendly degree. Open software is doing the best at not stinking up the planet. Yet, most Windows users never get to see enough of open goodness and long enough to understand what they are missing. The problem is, if you stop using Windows (please do not buy Windows or pre-installed systems with Windows, use your old; paid for copy) and use only open software as a newbie, you MAY forget all thel Windows (time wasting) troubles and mistakenly conclude, any open imperfection is a not ready for prime time development failing. Many issues have nothing to do with (GNU)"Linux" or open software but you can't convince a newbie of this. Unless you "unload" (use/try a while) the wealth of free open software you may never understand the monopolistic (and other) issues (cheap hardware, closed standards, FUD) hidden there. 

Fortunately, we can run multiple systems and not just one. So we can test open software freely. Don't take your information freedom lightly.

If you are overprotective of Ubuntu, if feel your pain. I was and still am somewhat protective of Ubuntu. Yet, we should be able to voice our concerns; in order to promote fixes and improvements. This is not necessarily anti-Linux.

Right now today, users are asking why they shouldn't just use Debian? At different points in time, different distributions may have an edge that fits your needs best. What we see is the desire for open software to fill in (the few left) gaps in modern software development. We forget what something like Win98 (for example) did NOT have. We forget how far we have really come. We need both, no benefit left behind AND new benefits (over features). Both, not one or the other.

Still the desire to beat the real competition (Don't be deluded, we need to continue to do this and more) and have zero modern dis-advantages, pushes us toward what is really a less than stable test release, no matter what you call it. Look, it is philologically difficult for newbies (and all of us) to be patient. It is difficult enough to keep so called "stable" tiers mostly stable. Please govern yourself accordingly. Of course that is FAR more stable than Windows.

1) Promote SEPARATE testing and automation to help it. 

2) Let no "official" release out before it has been widely tested.

3) Releases and therefore distributions (and therefore open software, "Linux" and free software) are judged by the unusually static CD(iso)/Installer. (see # 2)

4) Promoting clean installs and better clean install automation help, is much better than inter release dist-upgrades, managers and that automation. That is, (dist) upgrade managers can't be stabilized (widely enough among ALL users) until the DYNAMIC release being (dist)upgraded is largely stabilized. AKA some significant time after an official release. It's more of a moving target. Thus (inter-release) "dist" upgrading is better for older, more stabilized server use, not the new cutting edge desktop. Even then, you would wonder if a servers clean install, might be better still. Clean installing also simplifies processes and makes the above points easier to accomplish; with greater superlative excellence. AKA a better mouse trap.

Note: Upgrading within your release, not dist-upgrades (addressed above) are one of open softwares  greatest strong points.
