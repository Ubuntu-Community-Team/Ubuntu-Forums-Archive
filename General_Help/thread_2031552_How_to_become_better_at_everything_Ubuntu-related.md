I've only been using Ubuntu since Warty :KS ,and Linux since RedHat 5 in 1998, but I'm still learning all the time. I don't know of any formal structured course of study, but as the previous poster said, there are no doubt courses-for-pay. 

Personally, I never bought any books on Linux - everything changes too fast, and it can be very confusing trying to work with out-of-date information. I have amassed a collection of how-to's over the years. I pretty much learned as I went - when I had an issue, I googled around, asked here, experimented on my own, etc. And I never gave up until the bugger was solved!

It seems to me there are tons of resources on the web, and you will discover the ones that work for you - you'll find one or two "go to" sites. For example, this forum. I have amassed a collection of how-to's over the years that are useful once in a while. 

The more you learn, the better your trouble-shooting skills will become - you'll be able to zero in on things much more quickly. You'll also become able to ask more targeted and effective questions on forums such as this one.

You might even want to set up a second installation for messing around in without fear of destroying your "production" system. Sometimes there's nothing like twiddling the knobs and seeing what happens to get a deep understanding of how things works - but breakage sometimes happens.

Anyway have fun and enjoy the ride!

### Post by oslub on 2012-07-22
I could not agree more. I also got my first knowledge about Ubuntu and Linux in general reading several tutorials, tips, reviews and videos, driven by the errors, bugs and needs I faced when moving to Ubuntu. The amount of information available is huge, specially for Ubuntu, but then we lose ourselves in so much reading scattered by the whole internet and we are limited at following other people's recommendations step by step, without learning much about what we are really doing.

The documentation and help pages from Ubuntu are quite useful and are worth a read.

About books, for absolute beginners I would recommend [this guide]("http://orgulhogeek.net/ubuntu-guia-do-iniciante/"), written by [a brazilian]("http://orgulhogeek.net/about/"), it is a free complete guide introducing people to Ubuntu most important features and functionality. The first version of this guide is no longer free because its rights were acquired by a publishing company, so you may want to download this second version before that happens again. It is also available a special fascicle about Virtualization. It is written in Portuguese, not a problem to me but anyway I strongly recommend.

Also there are a [lot of online tools]("http://askubuntu.com/questions/151488/what-is-the-best-way-to-learn-how-to-use-ubuntu-with-terminal") to help anyone know about using the terminal, I also am trying to understand the commands and apply them myself, given that before, all I used to do was to search on google for the right command, follow all instructions and get things done rather than trying to understand what I was doing to the system.

As you are introduced to the basics, like someone already stated, get a specific installation of Ubuntu or any of its derivatives just to play and mess around with it, without fear of damaging the system and if needed, take some notes and make a record of what you do and what is the outcome of such actions.

Follow some technology websites with good cover of Linux world, they may have useful tips, tutorials and reviews to Linux software. What really matters is that you never lose the thirst of knowledge and the will to learn more.
