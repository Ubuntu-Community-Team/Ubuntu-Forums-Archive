### Post by Delkster on 2007-01-11
> **presbp said:**
> If I own software that legally plays DVD movies and it is installed on Windows is it legal for me to download them?  I already have them on Windows don't I?

I take it that you mean whether libdvdcss is legal if you already have a licensed DVD player on Windows? I don't suppose it matters for the legal status of libdvdcss whether you also have a licensed player or not. That might matter if the legal issues were with the copyright of the decrypting software, but it's not -- the libdvdcss implementation is independent of whatever implementation you have on Windows. As far as I know, the legal problem is that libdvdcss works around the copy protection and could thus be considered a 'circumvention device' or whatever.

I'm not a lawyer, though, and probably know even less about your legislation than ours, so I'd ask somewhere else if I were you.
         
> 2) I use Windows to share files over wireless with the other computers in the house and I don't know how to do it in Ubuntu

Have you tried? I don't know exactly how to do it either because I don't have a LAN where I'd share files, but in Unix/Linux compatibility with Windows file sharing is achieved with the Samba software, and I've got the impression that it wouldn't be overly difficult to configure in Ubuntu. In Nautilus (the file manager) there's an option for sharing a given folder, and I think it'll even install Samba for you if needed. I can't say exactly how it works, though, or whether it's easy to do.

> 3) Because all of the software is free I often doubt how good the software is.

There's a lot of it, and some of it is very good, some isn't. If you take a completely random piece of software it may not be of great quality but for most tasks you'll be able to find at least one good alternative -- and what comes with Ubuntu by default usually works quite nicely.

Actually that also applies to proprietary and commercial software: most of it isn't of very high quality. While it may be that the possibly more organized way of doing things in the commercial world is sometimes good for the quality, most free/open source developers do what they do at least partially out of interest and love for the art, and that often makes them more pedantic. In commercial projects you generally only fix an issue if it provides more benefit economically than it costs to fix it, but when the motives for developing software are non-commercial, people are often inclined to fix problems just because it's the task itself that motivates them, not the economical benefit.

Also remember that a lot of the free/open source software has actually been developed at least partially with commercial interests in view; the development of Linux and related software is in the indirect interest of many large companies even if the software itself is distributed for free.
