### Post by Chethan S on 2010-10-08
Today I came across this site: [http://webapps.ubuntu.com/certification/](http://webapps.ubuntu.com/certification/) which lists hardware certified for use with ubuntu by Canonical Engineers. I have been using lucid lynx in IBM ThinkCentre at home and HP Compaq Desktop(using proprietary Nvidia drivers) at my college without any issues so far. I read about people facing issues with sound, video and so on when switching into Linux.

So my question is, is it really essential to buy only certified hardware(which as of now are only a few models from a handful of manufacturers and even many may not be available in all countries) or will it be possible to solve any kind of hardware compatibility issues with community help. I agree in some cases it may not be possible, when there are only proprietary drivers for a particular hardware. But still I am interested to know.

### Post by avacado on 2010-10-27
Actually the Ubuntu Certified Harware list includes a lot of antique computers and  inaccurate in at least two cases.
The only computer available in Australia with ubuntu pre-loaded is the Dell 2110 which is not listed. The Acer aspire one series of netbooks is listed as certified but in fact they represent a range of hardware that includes some which does not work - see [https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/linux/+bug/530277](https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/linux/+bug/530277)
I suggest checking community wiki and retailers who pride themselves in supplying hardware - there is one in australia who publishes the results of hardware testing online.

### Post by inameiname on 2010-10-27
No, it's not really necessary. The good thing about Ubuntu, really any Linux distro, it's is ability to run on just about anything. There are of course times when certain problems arise with hardware, such as wireless adapters/cards, graphic cards, etc, but as mentioned already, there are plenty of wonderful people in our little community here to help you out. Also, the newer the machine the more likely it'll run just find. Your odds of having issues tends to go up the older the machine is. In general, not always, of course.

### Post by Topsiho on 2010-10-27
I think this depends on what you want to do with your computer.

If it is Ubuntu certified, then I think you may be more certain the computer works well with (the tested version of) Ubuntu, but I fear that avacado has a point.

I also think that the chance that Ubuntu works well on an older computer is greater than on a very new one, and on a generic computer greater than on one of the well known brands, as in these their own  hardware may be used.

I have a USB wifi-adapter that uses a new chip, that until Maverick was useless in Ubuntu, but now works fine: the (open source) driver had first to be created, by some very clever and nice folks, to whom I am very grateful. This kind of thing applies to all new hardware.

My opinion :)

Topsiho
