### Post by phantom3113 on 2009-04-06
You can also try a liveCD as well. When you put the CD into your computer (make sure it's set to boot from CD) it will give you the option to install or try Ubuntu with no change to your computer (among other things). If you select "try with no change", you'll be able to explore Ubuntu without changing your computer in anyway (it will take a few minutes to load though since it's all coming from the CD. It is possible to make a Ubuntu USB startup disk, although I'm not sure if you can do that from a LiveCD or not.

### Post by pbpersson on 2009-04-07
I am not quite sure how to answer the question in this thread because I was not a "Windows user" when I found Ubuntu.

Down through the years I was intrigued by stories of Linux - an OS that ran on your desktop computer, was totally free, and could perhaps be used in place of Windows.

The first time I ventured into the Linux world was 2000.  I tried two versions - Corel Linux and Red Hat.  Corel was wonderful!  It seemed so friendly and I loved the graphics....but other than surfing the web and playing some games I could not figure out how to get anything to work and there was no documentation on the web.

Red Hat had lots of documentation but I could not understand any of it.  I think it said that to get the sound working with the sound card I had I would need to recompile part of the kernel.  I could not get Netscape mail to work.  I posted a question on a forum, someone replied and said, "Oh yes, well you JKL the XYS and then you command /l *&#//%$%# &&  !! and you are all set and don't forget to re-align your Heisenburg Compensators.  :confused:

So I said forget Linux

Years later I tried Mandrake and used that for several months.  I used to get onto forums and rant about how everything was so difficult and nothing made any sense.  The forums were of little help.

So I said forget Linux

Then in the late summer of 2007 I was interested in Linux again and a number of people said, "You have to try Ubuntu, you will love it!"

Now.....at this point I already KNEW a bunch of stuff about Linux.  I knew about the directory structure, permissions, packages....I had even used many of the applications and knew the names.

Between my knowledge and how easy Ubuntu was and this newbie-friendly forum it was a piece of cake.

However, my road in getting here was a long one that spanned several years.
