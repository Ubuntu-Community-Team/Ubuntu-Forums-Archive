### Post by gloken on 2009-07-21
Hey. This isn't strictly ubuntu related, so I hope that it's an appropriate forum for this question. Apologies in advance if it seems off topic.

I've been working on an MA thesis, and corresponding by email with my advisor who lives in another province. I've been writing my thesis in Open Office, and saving it in a .doc Word format in order to ease her access to it.

...I'm beginning to seriously regret this decision.

There seem to be a lot of translation issues between the two programs. Between me saving the file and her opening it, the file seems to randomly indent (or unindent) footnotes and paragraphs. It also seems to take footnotes out of superscript format.

Has anyone else experienced this, and do you have any solution other than ditching Open Office?

And if ditching Open Office is the answer, what do I replace it with? I tried out LaTeX but it seems much more geared toward home publishing, and not very suitable to my needs, especially at this late stage in the project.

Solutions? Ideas? Any help would be much appreciated. Stress levels rising.

### Post by DJonsson2008 on 2009-07-23
The above suggestions for giving RTF and PDF formats a try 
are very good ones. As well with MS Word have the recipient
try and change their default printer to a Generic Text File
line printer driver before opening the file in MSWord, sounds strange
but I've seen it make a big difference. As well remember 
between *.DOC MSWord documents versions there are variants
in file format. I'm on a Xbuntu machine at the moment but
you may try one of the older *.DOC formats, although RTF seems
a better solution.

Lastly will say as a freelance writer delivering items to numerous
offices across numerous platforms and versions, I send my copy
out either as plain text or if more formating is needed as RTF,
as have found the various flavours of *.Doc not always predictable,
or as standardized as advertised even between Windows and Mac 
machines.

As well make sure your fonts match and stay with common fonts
I usually work only Times Roman, Ariel and Courier when passing documents around.

I hope that helps.

### Post by fragos on 2009-07-24
Newspapers and magazines usually want to use their own layout and fonts. They also may need to edit to adjust fit. I send them the text in an email message only using CR as an end of paragraph. Images if required will be sent as attachments. If my document is the deliverable I use PDF format to avoid any issues of conversion. Sticking with the basic MS fonts is a good idea although I'd warn you that these have been changed or at least renamed by Vista. It's not clear to me what Windows 7 will do.
