### Post by mb_webguy on 2009-02-18
A CD+/-RW or DVD+/-RW can be reformatted, but once a CD+/-R or DVD+/-R is written, it's permanent.  Breaking it is a pretty good way to keep other people from reading it.  You should be aware, however, that discs generally don't break cleanly, but rather tend to explode into confetti.  Make sure you're breaking it into a trash can, or you'll have quite a mess.

Rewritable discs have a layer of semi-reflective gel (actually a phase-change alloy).  When writing to one, the laser heats up sections of the gel, causing it to become either more reflective or less reflective.  This is a reversible process, so the disc can be written and rewritten over and over.  Some older drives and players can't read rewritable discs because even when it's more transparent, this layer is still there, and still reflects a small amount of the reading laser.

Writable discs have a layer of (usually metallic) dye that is broken down by the writing laser, causing clear spots in the dye layer.  Since the dye can't be reconstituted, this is not a reversible process.  While it's possible to blank a writable disc by running the laser over the whole thing, I don't know of any application that will do this.

Even if such an app exists, it's conceivable that such a disc could still be read by a very sensitive drive (since if the second pass doesn't completely break down the dye, the original pits will still be more transparent than the newly burned groove).  Physical destruction -- such as by breaking, burning, or microwaving (which isn't something I'd suggest, btw) -- is the best and most successful means of destroying the data.
