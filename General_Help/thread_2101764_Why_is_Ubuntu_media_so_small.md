### Post by 1clue on 2013-01-05
It's really pretty simple.

The big disks contain a lot of stuff on the disk.  The small ones get a basic system up and running, then use the Internet to download the rest.

Considering that a good share of the preloaded stuff on the big disk image is going to be reloaded again because of an update, IMO the small disk is preferable.

### Post by MoebusNet on 2013-01-05
Up until 12.10, Ubuntu made a concerted effort to fit an operating Ubuntu system with all of the essentials onto a CD-sized .iso image. It got to be so much work, that starting with 12.10 they've pretty much standardized on a DVD .iso image. I think there are 1 or 2 exceptions (Lubuntu springs to mind), but I think CD-sized .iso images will be a thing of the past for Ubuntu.

### Post by 1clue on 2013-01-05
Let me try again:

Big disk, you can do an offline install and get most of the stuff you might want.

Little disk, you probably need the network for any more than a bare-bones install.

IMO it's a matter of how much you want to download twice. Once for the iso, once for updates.

For any distro, the average age of an image is half the time between image builds, so say some distro builds an iso every month, the average age is 2 weeks.  Probably pretty current there, but if they build every year then chances are your image is half a year old, and will have tons of updates, probably almost everything on the image is updated after install.
