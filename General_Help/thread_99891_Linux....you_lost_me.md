### Post by aysiu on 2005-12-06
Just about every new Linux user has to install her own operating system, whereas most Windows users do not (they buy a computer with Windows preinstalled).

So if you're going to the trouble of installing Linux, it's a good idea to ask yourself why.

For me, it's ease of customization, pretty themes for free, and a lack of worry over spyware, remembering where I kept activation keys, and weird licensing restrictions.

For you, it might be something else. Or.. it might be nothing at all. If there's no compelling reason you can think of, don't use it. Find what works best for you.

Someone who lives in Manhattan may not understand why people get cars and put up with the maintenance, the gas prices, the insurance, etc. Someone who lives in suburbia may not understand why anyone would ride a bicycle to work. You have to examine your needs, your desires, and what each operating system can offer you.

Username
Dell
Documents and Settings
HP-LJ Drivers-XP
I386
OfficeXP
Program Files
TEMP
WINDOWS

While the names themselves are descriptive of what's inside, they're also a bit misleading in terms of the file hierarchy.

A user would naturally assume that TEMP would hold all the temporary files. As a matter of fact, temporary files are scattered throughout, and most of the temporary files a user uses are actually in C:\Documents and Settings\username\Local Settings\Temp (which is a hidden folder).

Likewise, you would think (seeing your username), that your files would live there, but then why is there another folder called "Documents and Settings" (where they really live)?

It would make sense that OfficeXP would be in the OfficeXP folder, but to launch it you really have to go to C:\Program Files\Microsoft Office\.

WINDOWS is a confusing folder name, too, because you could think that's how you get into using Windows.

My Documents, My Pictures, and all that actually live in C:\Documents and Settings\username.

I'm not saying the Linux filesystem naming convention makes sense, but the hierarchy is certainly more consistent. The names are surely confusing to new users (why would program files be in a /bin or a /lib? why isn't /usr called /user?), but the fact that /tmp actually has temporary files and /home has all the user data (files and settings) in it makes a lot of sense.

At the end of the day, I guess you just get used to what you're used to.

OS X has a folder called *Applications* but the configuration and preferences for any application are still stored in the user's *Library* and *Application Data* folders.
