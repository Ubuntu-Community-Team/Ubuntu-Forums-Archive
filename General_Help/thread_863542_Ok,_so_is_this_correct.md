### Post by Potatoj316 on 2008-07-18
You have your distro (short for distrubution) that packages a bunch of things together.  The kernel (most basic part of the operating system, it does most of the hard work), the window manager (gnome, kde, etc... that is what makes everything look nice and not just a bunch of text), and the applications which you can add or remove later.  The distro also sets a bunch of defaults, again which you can change later.  You can even change the window manager later.

### Post by mocoloco on 2008-07-18
You're on the right track, but there's more to it than that.  The distro includes the GUI (DE, or Desktop Environment), and everything else that is "distributed" to make a complete OS.  Most different distributions may have a few things in common (using Linux for the kernel, many of the underlying libraries, modules, etc) but take a different approach to package management, default setup, etc.
What defines a distro is fairly loose, thus it can be built from scratch (relatively speaking), based on some other distro, or sometimes even pretty much identical to to the parent distro except the default theme.
You can't assume one distro is going to come with one DE (or any DE!), for example Suse will ask on install if you want KDE or Gnome, but it's still considered one distro (again, the loose definition).

One thing I point out to new users is the misuse of terms like "The Linux Operating System", which implies you can download one thing called "Linux" and run anything made for "Linux".  A better way to think of it is that Linux is a basis for an OS, on which many (many many) different operating systems are built, Ubuntu being one of those.  
It's like cookies, there are many kinds, some taste similar, some are extremely different, and because people share their recipes there are new kinds of cookies all the time, but you have a general idea of what to expect if someone offers you one.

I hope I haven't made things more confusing for you, the world of Linux definitely offers more "flavors" (pun intended) than users of other OSes are used to.
