### Post by Carborundum on 2011-10-17
> **pbpersson said:**
> I think you have to be a serious gamer to notice the  difference in performance that Intel chips will provide based on the  reviews I have read on this.

Games are, as a rule, not very CPU intensive. Even the heaviest gamer on the planet isn't going to notice any difference between Intel and AMD in a majority of cases.

> **psrdotcom said:**
> Because the Quality matters in my point of view. Intel provides better quality products with less damages in a large amount of volumes.

No. Intel and AMD both use TSMC as their primary manufacturer. There is no difference in quality what so ever.

The primary difference between Intel and AMD today is that Intel CPUs have higher IPC (Instructions Per Cycle). This means that an Intel CPU running at 4 GHz will be significantly faster than an AMD CPU running at the same frequency. This in turn means that Intel are more power efficient than AMD in general, i.e. you get more performance per Watt from an Intel CPU.

Intel also has the edge when it comes to really CPU intensive applications, such as video encoding. The only exception to this rule is AMD's new Bulldozer architecture, which excels at this task due to having 8 cores. Unfortunately, Bulldozer sucks at pretty much everything else.

AMD also has an interesting line-up of something they call "APUs" (Advances Processing Units), which are chips containing both a traditional CPU and a fairly powerful GPU. These processors are hard to beat when it comes to low-power applications that still require a good amount of graphics capability, such as HTPCs. Unfortunately, these APUs are not yet well supported under Linux.

In the higher price segments (>$300) Intel rules supreme. Unfortunately for consumers, AMD has not been able to produce anything that can stand up to Intel's most powerful processors.

### Post by mcduck on 2011-10-17
The question is kind of like asking what's the difference between Honda and Toyota.

Different manufacturer, different designs.

Which one is a better deal depends on what you do, although at the moment Intel does better with all generic computing tasks, while AMD is pretty much the better choice with highly threaded tasks as they give you more cores for the same price.

So unless you know otherwise, go for Intel right now. If AMD processors would be better option, you probably wouldn't need to as such question. ;)

Edit: AMD's Fusion APU's might be a good option for really compact/low power devices, though, as AMD has significantly better integrated GPU's than what Intel is able to produce... Definitely a good choice for home theater use, for example.
