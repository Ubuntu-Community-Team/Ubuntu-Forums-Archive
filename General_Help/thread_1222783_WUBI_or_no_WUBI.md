### Post by lindsay7 on 2009-07-25
I tried Wubi just to get familiar with Ubuntu and that was fine. You can do just about everything with the wubi install, but you have to remember that you are running a system within a system. It is a matter of what you are going to use Ubuntu for, if you are just playing around the wubi is fine, but if you are going to use Ubuntu equal to or more that windows then you should do the real dual install. I found very quickly that once I got a little familiar with Ubuntu that I do not use windows much at all except for Quicken, TurboTax, and a few odds and ends.

Dual booting will let you set up a separte partitions if you want with a "/" for the system and a "/home" to keep everything else. This will let you be able to upgrade to new verisions by installing to the "/" partition and will leave everything else on your drive. Dual booting will be faster and it is just a better way to go.

### Post by oldos2er on 2009-07-25
Wubi is not meant to be permanent in any way; it's main purpose is to "demo" Ubuntu for Windows users without altering their hard drive partitions. Of course you can keep it running for as long as you want, but it's always going to be at the mercy of Windows.

"The two times I tried it without the WUBI, there were two other options on the boot menu. I don;t remember what they were."

 Sounds like a normal grub menu to me. After installing Ubuntu on its own partition, grub will show memtest, any other OSes you have installed, and a couple different kernels plus their recovery modes. You can configure grub's menu list any way you prefer.
