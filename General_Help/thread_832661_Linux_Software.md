For example, if I were to say to myself, "I would like to have a look at what CD Ripping software is available", and I had Windows, I would just go to download.com and type in "cd rip" into the search field, and I'm good to go.  I have a comprehensive list, with download numbers to give me a good idea of which are the best apps, full software descriptions, editor & user reviews & ratings, I can sort by several fields, and I can search by lots of different parameters.  In no time at all, I have a pretty good idea I'm about to install exactly the software I wanted.

I have no choice but to install every single app that seems even remotely related, and basically do my own review and test of each of them.  This makes every single Linux user reinvent the wheel whenever they want software.

### Post by Eladon on 2008-06-18
To steveneddy, thanks for the suggestion.  I do have that link, but it doesn't address the issues that I brought up.

To the others, also thanks for the Google suggestion.  I do use this, but I have to confess I find it a much inferior solution.  Here's why:

1) There are only sometimes software reviews available
2) When there are software reviews, half the time the web pages don't have a date on them so you have no idea how old they are.  Anything older than a year these days is generally useless.
3) You don't necessarily get a comprehensive list, and you have to filter through a lot of garbage to try.
4) Even when I find a decent review, I'm still depending on a single person to be the know-all of the software category.  I have done this, and taken the reviewer's recommendation, only to find out later on he missed out on including a major app that I ended up preferring.
5) Frequently reviewers, in their desire to be equal, break things down strictly to academic things like features.  But there's so much more to software than that, i.e. user friendlyness, compatibility, reliability, development cycle, etc.  Rarely a good reviewer might include these subjects, but the bottom line is that ALL of these subjects are easily wrapped up into one summary statistic: What is everyone else using?  That is one piece of information that I find difficult to get.
