### Post by sabre2th on 2009-09-30
Everybody has different opinions about this.  For the most part, Ubuntu should work with any hardware.  However, there are some things that cause more trouble than others.  My short list:

[LIST]
[*]I would stick with nvidia graphics if I were you.  Generally less headaches.
[*]If you use wireless, check into the adapter before buying.  Problems are less common now than in the past, but they do occasionally pop up.
[*]Fingerprint scanners aren't going to work (software's not there yet)
[/LIST]
Actually, that's all that comes to mind at the moment.  I haven't really had THAT much trouble with anything in my time with Linux.

My only general advice as far as building a PC is to take it slow and understand the directions before doing anything.  It really isn't hard, but can be nerve-wracking the first time.  If you have an experienced friend willing to help out with your first build, that can help a lot.

Oh, and make sure you're always grounded (skin touching metal) when handling the internal workings of a computer.  Many people recommend a grounding strap; I just make sure some part of my body is touching the case.

### Post by Tanker Bob on 2009-09-30
I've used Gigabyte and MSI motherboards in Linux machines with no problems. You can see my current system components in my signature.

Definitely stick with nVidia video cards. ATI has been a mixed bag over the years, including under Jaunty, but nVidia has consistently supported Linux.

Don't go with cheap memory. A fast system with slow memory is a slow system and a waste of money. Same goes with CPU coolers. The stock ones are usually junk, including Intel's.

Avoid bleeding edge hardware. Everything new is expensive and drivers for them usually lag by months or more. One notch down from leading-edge components is usually a good price and performance target, and almost always well-supported by software drivers. With carefully planned overclocking, you can usually come close to matching top performance anyway.

Another vote here for Newegg. I have bought almost everything from them for many years, and always read pages of customer comments before I finalize any decisions. I've never had a bad experience with them.

[I have a series of articles on a system build here](http://www.mobiletechreview.com/ubbthreads/showflat.php?Cat=0&Number=27052&an=0&page=0#Post27052). Reading through it may give you some ideas and confidence. It really isn't hard, but it does take some planning and research to get what you need at a good price.
