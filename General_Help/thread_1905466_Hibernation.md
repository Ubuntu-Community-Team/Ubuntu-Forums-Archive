### Post by killermist on 2012-01-06
Well.  A full boot probably takes a little longer than a fast boot and restore from hibernation.  Though depending on how much (little) you're running on the server, a full boot may be faster than a restore from hibernation.

Personally I've not ever trusted hibernation since having issues with it in... Windows 2000.
Granted the concept of hibernation is probably far more advanced than in Windows 2000, but I still don't trust it as a concept.  I strongly prefer full poweroff or restarts than fuzzy-off hibernation.
Sufficiently low power-consumption machines, I just leave on and be done with it.

But, that's just me.  Hopefully this gives you some thoughts/ideas.

### Post by topsites on 2012-01-06
An interesting conversation we had today, is that if your computer runs off certain Uninterruptable Power Supplies (UPS) that the power consumption unfortunately remains awful similar whether the PC is off or on as it isn't the PC that's consuming the power per se... The UPS consumes power regardless so long it is plugged into the wall, whether anything is running off it or not.
Pretty much, there is little if any difference on the power bill.

Further it isn't running that ruins the components, nor is it heat in and of itself (although we did agree that too much heat wasn't good).
Rather, it's just like with old-fashioned incandescent light bulbs, when these units are first turned on they are cold and as they gear up, heat builds up inside of them, sometimes to a considerable point... The reverse happens when they are turned off, they cool... What happens then is with all this heating and cooling is the components themselves shrink and expand, and it is this shrinkage and expansion that causes it to break and ultimately fail.
I have had more incandescent light bulbs fail when I first hit the light switch, why, because the sudden heat.
I don't think I've ever had them fail when I turned one off because it cools slower, but nevertheless it is the cooling and heating of the element that is causing it to shrink and expand that is killing it.
And I believe the same holds true of computer components and circuitry.

Is it good to leave the PC on 24/7, probably not, but where do we draw the line?
I'd say for someone who uses the pc a few hours a day, turn it off when it's not in use.

And for power users which are best described as folks who use the PC for 8 or more hours a day, it is likely best to leave it running while allowing it to shut down the screen and certain other things go in stand by...

For myself, I only turn off the PC when I foresee being gone for more than 1-2 hours, sometimes not even then but usually more in summer as the PC contributes to heating the house, hence I lose little when leaving it running in winter...

I realize this doesn't quite answer the question, but it's insight I've gained.
