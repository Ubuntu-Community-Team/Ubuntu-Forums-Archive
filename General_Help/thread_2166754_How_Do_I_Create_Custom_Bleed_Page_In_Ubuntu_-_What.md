Hello, I was wondering what would be the easiest way in Ubuntu to create a bleed page? Here's what I've got:

JPG Image that needs to be created into a PDF document. Here's the instructions from the print company that I will be using:

*"In order to print a Bleed your files must not have a white margin and must be 1/2" larger than the finish size. If no bleed is chosen, please add 1/4" white margin on all sides. Otherwise, we will add a 1/4" for you."*


And here is the example/steps the company uses for an 8.5 X 11 document (what I want my end "bleed" page result to be):


**STEP 1: Adjust Your Document Size**

When you are creating a bleed document to print with us your document size must be .5 inches larger than the finished size. This means that an 8.5" x 11" bleed document must be submitted to us as 9" x 11.5".

Many programs have either a custom page size option or allow you to adjust the size of the page through a settings menu. We cannot bleed a file whose document size is the same as the finish size.



**STEP 2: Create Your Margins**

Once you have created the larger document size you will want to adjust your margins. In the program you are using you will either need to set specific margins or create guides at .25" and .5" around the document. The first guide at .25" around is where your document will be cut to create the bleed. The second guide at .5" will ensure that any important content you have, such as text, is not cut off when we go to cut your document.



[B]STEP 3: Extend Your Background
[/B]
Now that you have setup your document and margins you can begin designing your file. The most important part of the bleed design is to ensure that anything you want to bleed is extended all the way to the edge of the document. For an 8.5" x 11" document this means that any background colors or images should go all the way to the edge of the 9" x 11.5" file.

### Post by carl4926 on 2013-08-11
Gimp can create a custom image
BUT, some things to consider.
Gimp uses RGB, your print company will not! Though they will likely accept RGB, with the proviso that you understand that colours may differ slightly.
When you create an image in Gimp, be sure to up the quality from the default 72 to 300 or 600  see Eg: [http://paste.opensuse.org/59358337](http://paste.opensuse.org/59358337)
