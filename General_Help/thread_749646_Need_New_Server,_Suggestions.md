### Post by Rocket2DMn on 2008-04-08
By ordering a pre-built system, you can't really get one without an OS + software preinstalled.  The only time you ever really see this is on business computers (more specifically, business servers).  If you build your own computer, you need to deal with making sure everything is compatible, and you also don't get a warranty, but the advantage is you can shop around for the best deals and tailor the system to your needs more easily.
You can order a prebuilt system, then just wipe it clean when you get it.  You should be able to specify NOT to install most software, or the cheapest/default software it comes with and just trash it.  You may consider calling Dell (or whoever else you order from) and asking them over the phone to drop the software and subtract the relevant price from the order.
An advantage to ordering a prebuilt computer is you can keep the OS (Vista or XP), then use that to either dual boot or legally setup a VM on the machine (like with Virtual Box for seemless integration).

### Post by Rocket2DMn on 2008-04-08
Building your own computer is up to you - again, you don't get full coverage warranties, though some parts may have warranties on them for varying periods of time.  When companies build computers for you, they get good prices on large quantities of the components, but you can still get hardware decently cheap if you hunt around, it's really a tradeoff of convenience and pricing.  Basically, you motherboard has to fit in the tower you get, and the processor, RAM, and video card have to compatible with your motherboard.  If you don't really know what you're doing, it can be an excellent learning experience, or a pain in the butt.  It's not as difficult as it sounds, though, shop around and bookmark some links to components you want.  Then you can post the parts here and we can reassure you of their compatibility.

You could collect junk computers, but then you get stuck with some older hardware that will slow your system down - a lot of it may not be compatible, either.  I would not suggest taking this path unless you are trying to build a cheap computer to use as a file server, or something to just play around with.  This can be fun, but you won't get a very powerful computer out of it.

Again, if you want to build your own, do some research on certain pieces of hardware, check compatibility, then post here or in a new thread and people can tell you if you are likely to have problems.  

The most important things to check: the processor needs to fit in the socket that the motherboard has (often these 2 are sold as a pair anyway).  The RAM needs to be of the right type (I'm not even sure what is standard nowadays).  The peripherals like hard drive and CD/DVD drive need to connect to the motherboard, probably through SATA connections since IDE is old and slower.  Also, I think most boards don't have ps/2 ports on them so your keyboard and mouse will probably need to be USB.
