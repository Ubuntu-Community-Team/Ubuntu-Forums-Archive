### Post by fubarbundy on 2005-12-29
Nope.. because of where your ears are, you are only natively able to determine the left or right position of sounds (relative volume in left and right ear. Your brain figures out the front or back location from the acoustics of the sound - a sound from behind you will have had to travel through your earlobe, and so will have a different tone from something from the front (I think it is the earlobe that gives you this ability, but someone is welcome to correct me on this).

If you've got some decent headphones, you can experience some of the effects of this by downloading some binaural sound effects from somewhere ;-) and listening through headphones. Binaural recordings are made by recording sounds using two microphones placed where the ear canals would be on a synthetic head. The idea is that you record at the only point where you can catch everything as it is heard by the person, and then reproduce it at the same point (I really need a diagram for this). In theory, if you actually use your own head instead of a synthetic head, two very high quality microphones that sit almost inside the ear, and very high quality in-ear earphones (I'm talking electrostatics here like Stax, or at the very least Shure or Etymotics), then when you go away and replay the sound, it will sound freakily real. In practice, even when you listen to the downloaded sounds on good headphones, where the synthetic head (and earlobes etc) aren't exactly like yours, it sounds pretty freaky.

But I digress.

So, if you think about it, it is possible to mathematically do the same. You can create an acoustic computer model of a room and a person's ears, and figure out what happens to a sound from any point in the room before it gets to your earhole (echo, delay, muffling etc) and modify it accordingly. You do that for all the sounds, mix them together into two channels and play it back into your earholes et voila, true surround sound from two channels.

Apart from not knowing the acoustics of the outside of each individual's head, the only other problem I can think of is any sound that might be transmitted through bone, or not directly through the outside of the earhole (through the surrounding flesh for instance), but this won't be too significant.

That's the theory. In practice, the computer models are less than perfect. But a similar process is used on DSP features on amplifiers for 'Concert Hall', 'Live' and other effects.
