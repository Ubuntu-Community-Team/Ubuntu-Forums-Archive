### Post by Jeff Eklund on 2006-02-17
[QUOTE=Rumpty]That's an interesting article, some of which I barely understand. There is obviously a lot of functionality in KDE which many of us are not aware of? I guess learning about it is a matter of playing and trying things, or is there a tutorial somewhere?[/QUOTE]
I haven't seen any tutorial around, but the general thumbrule is: if you ever want to do something but can't think of how to do it, search the mailingslists or a forum, beacuse there is a way to do it in KDE. Many of the features are not very intuitive, like middle-mouse-clicking on a tab moves it around, like in firefox 1.5 (Konqueror's had that functionality för over three years) but it's not mentioned anywhere in te documentation. As far as I know... I have been using KDE, full time, for about one and a half year and I still learn new things all the time, beacuse people have had my problems before me and implemented some clever way to solve it. KDEs biggest problem is making all that functionality available to the user. Try just clicking around and tinker with things. The control-center, Kcontrol, is a mess, beacuse it's been packed with features as they went along and no one really stopped and thought about how to organise it all. That's where Gnome does a better job than KDE. But then again, reinventing the same feature fifteen times, beacuse it could me more intuvitive and easier to use, as Gnome does, bugs me enormously. If you read the developerblogs on KDE Planet, you'll notice that alot of thought is going into useability and such when planning for KDE4, whch will rock horrendously. 
So, to really answer your questions: many, many features are hidden in KDE. That's KDEs biggest disadvantage, but keep looking for yourself and be curious and ask on theese forums or any other place. The features are there.

P.S. Come to think of it, KDE can be like the Monkey Island games or the Broken Sword-games sometime. The strangest combinations achives really unexpected, but good, results. :D
