### Post by Silent Warrior on 2008-04-29
I though long and hard on where I should post this, and this was what I could deduce. I will not gripe if this is moved.

In sunday's GP ([www.gp.se](www.gp.se)) I spotted a small review of 'the latest version of Ubuntu'. The experiences depicted here are nothing like what I've experienced, so I thought I'd provide you lot with a quick translation and see if you make something useful out of it.
-------------------------------------------------------
[SIZE="5"]Sorry Linux, I don't wanna[/SIZE]
**If Mac is a cool guy, and Windows an old office-fox, what's Linux, then? A clever girl from the alternative-movement? No, afraid not. Beneath an exciting beautiful surface is a disturbed nerd*.**
This thursday there was a new release of Ubuntu, the Linux-variant many think is the best. I had just tested Linux in Asus little internet-computer and was hoping for something a little prettier for a bigger computer. And the first impression is good, really good.
The desktop is beautiful. A frightening bird in orange, brown and red**. The menus are populated by programs and settings, what I need, no more.
But even though Ubuntu is an african word for [not sure how to translate this word, equally unsure if it's necessary], this is a system that seems to lack empathy. It starts instantly upon boot with having to set a password. The field can't be left blank.
Then I want to set the time. To change settings I have to provide username and password***. Hello! I don't want to format my harddrive. Just set the clock correctly!
Then I want to surf. I can't enter the address, because I get apostrophies instead of colons. I manage to figure out how to change to swedish keyboard-layout. Hello again! I downloaded a swedish version. How hard can it be to understand that I'm presumably using a swedish keyboard?
I get the webbrowser running but it can't find the net even though I'm sitting in an open WLAN-network. I open the network-settings. Ubuntu asks for name and password every time something is changed.
I download Google Earth. I need to open a terminal-window and write some code to install. I need to go to a blog to get help. I never figure out how to make the program-icon appear in the menu.
Ubuntu, you're beautiful, but I don't want to live with you. You want everything done your way, as you've decided. You don't even try to understand how I think, you don't meet me half-way. You don't want me to have my own applications. You control everything I do and you don't trust me****. You keep asking for my name even if we've been together for two days now. You want me to learn all about you, but, honestly, I don't feel like hanging around your nerdy forums for neat tips on how to tweak you.
But your bird is nice. I'll save it as a souvenir.
--------------------------------------

Now, my impression is that this reviewer doesn't really know what he/she's doing. It's further reinforced when I read that this review is based on two days' usage. But, I had some comments first.

* Sort of pun-like, in Swedish: "disturbed nerd" = "störd nörd"
** Where'd she get that bird? I though the Ubuntu-logo was a pack of stylised humans holding hands?
*** I've never seen this. Of course, I've never needed to set the clock manually, either.
**** This reviewer CAN NOT have noticed the, what, 17 million lines of Linux-code geared towards security and functionality where Windows Vista touts some 30-odd million lines of copy-protection...

Now, to business. This article is translated straight off, as-is, no lingo-corrections on my part (I'd NEVER write WLAN-net - the N stands for Network, after all), so if something doesn't check out, quite likely it isn't my fault. Of course, I'm not perfect.
With the reviewer's issues with keyboard-layout and clock, I smell an improper install. I also noticed in the installs I've been through that every localisation is made available, not just swedish. How about declaring 'rushed and improper install'?
Also note that... there is no mention of hardware incompatibility or driver-unavailability, which is IMO Linux's greatest problem! Even in Windows, drivers can provide a bit of a hassle - videocard, keyboard, mouse, soundcard, even monitors - and the only problem reported was regarding WLAN. My parents have a WLAN, operated through WinXP, and even then they have sketchy reliability.
Obviously there was also no attempt at downloading source-code and compiling it user-side.

Well, spare a comment, friend?
Sure, Linux-life isn't smooth sailing, but attacking the policy of password-protected administration instead of the lack of drivers seems a bit misguided. With the extended use of the terminal, I can sympathise with the nerd-branding (now, who ever said that's automatically a bad thing?) - the user sort of gets closer to the actual operating of the system, instead of being stuck in a GUI-abstraction.
And, for Pete's sake, don't ask me why anyone would want their OS-login password blank... O' course, [insert name here] apparently didn't monkey around with the configuration enough to find the 'Automatic log-in'-setting. Can I just take the time to say I quite approve of it? :KS
Also, how much Windows-usage can you actually get within two days including OS-installation? Driver-hunt, anyone?
