### Post by prshah on 2009-06-26
Hi:

I have deli-linux installed on a (ex-windows98 ) Pentium MMX / 16MB RAM / 550MB HDD (or is it 200MB?)  / 1MB PCI VGA . It's GUI-able.

I'd done it just for kicks, it was... usable... but not enjoyable. Command line mode is just fine. I still have the machine lying around, somewhere.

If you use this with 30MB I guess you have nothing to worry about, but I wouldn't call it my primary PC.

My other options were:
a) Ubuntu minimal - Needs 48 MB to even boot
b) Minicore linux (10 MB) - Needs 32 MB minimum to boot, but then works very well (comparatively).
c) Microcore linux - Command line only. 7.5MB for the entire disto.
d) Puppy - 64MB min, otherwise takes too long (over 7 mins!!) to start
e) DSL - On 64MB, installs and works fine, but takes too long to start

### Post by eternal0void on 2009-06-26
> **wpshooter said:**
> If your time is worth anything to you, then get yourself a new computer !!!

Some people would rather not throw out something that still works.

Having said that, there's a computer recycling center (read: they would rather not throw out something that works either) several miles away which currently has no-OS P4-2.66Ghz systems (40GB hard drive, 512MB RAM) for $65, and 3.0Ghz systems for $100.

While I'm going to find useful things to do with old equipment, for most people there's no need to use a 30MB RAM computer as their primary PC.

Now, on a sadder and reality-check moment, there are people in the U.S.A. who need laptops of any type, including 30MB RAM laptops.  I do some volunteer work with a local food pantry and thrift store (not a chain), testing their donated computer parts.  As the primary purpose of the pantry and thrift store is to help senior citizens, I find out about a lot of the problems facing older people in the U.S.A.

For example, when someone, usually a senior citizen, sees my laptop on the workbench, they always ask me if it is for sale.  I asked the folks who run the thrift store about this, and they tell me that senior citizens are always asking about laptops.

The reason?  They are being taken care of by several of their kids, living at one grown-up child's house at a time, for about three to six months at a time.  Then they move to the next child's house for another three to six months.  A desktop computer is heavy and doesn't move well, and these senior citizens are moving two to four times a year.  Grown-up children often "suggest" that the older parent leave desktops behind.

Laptop computers offer senior citizens an easily-moved computer, but more importantly they provide a greater level of privacy.  This is because they don't have an account on "John Junior's" computer which can be explored by their progeny while they're living at "Daughter Jeannie's" house (I realize that you don't want to think about it, but your widowed parent is likely dating and having sex, and doesn't want you to know all the sordid details).
