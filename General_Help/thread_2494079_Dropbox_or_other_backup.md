### Post by TheFu on 2024-01-04
All of those options place your data on someone else's storage, connected to someone else's computer, on someone else's network, inside someone else's building.  Is that really acceptable for the sensitivity of the files involved?

Of course, if you use well-known, solid encryption before you push anything to these "cloudy" services, then the risks are lessened, but not 100% mitigated.

I'd only place files in any of those services if 
a) they were completely unimportant
b) they could be shared with the entire world - ENTIRE WORLD - like I'd post them here, now, today without any issues at all.

I don't trust cloudy services.  It is extremely common for tiny mistakes to allow other people access to the wrong accounts.  Just this week, it happened again from a respectable network security company. Full access to accounts, including all settings, were made available to the wrong people.  This happens far too often.

### Post by TheFu on 2024-01-09
> **Tom_Carr said:**
> I have not looked at my dropbox online for a year or more.  The format has changed and I was confused about my files being lost.  Nothing has been lost.

I appreciate the comments about security.  Nothing in my shared folders will be anything I don't want the whole world to see.  

I am 73 and probably have less than 5 more years to live.  I don't want to go into the health details.   I am creating a place online where friends and family can read certain things after I die.

It will contain my unfinished auto biography,  and a file called "Final wishes" about what I would like done after I die, and another called "Where things are" telling where my will and a paper list of my passwords can be found in my house.

I want something simple to use, and simple for friends and family to use after I die.  Would you suggest dropbox, google drive, one drive, or something else?

I've have a number of family deaths in the last decade. Fortunately, we all planned ahead and have wills, trusts, and ways for the official executor of our estates to know what they need to know in a secure way.  Having a final wishes document is helpful, but only if you clearly assign an executor for your estate and give that person/lawyer/group full decision-making authority.  With health issues, you need to assign a health power of attorney too and ensure they know your wishes, clearly for the end.  Families will fight over keeping you alive if that isn't clear.  

Had a brother-in-law fall off a roof and was basically a vegetable from the fall.  His sisters wanted the entire family to go bankrupt keeping him alive on machines forever. Thankfully, he'd specifically assigned my sister to handle all his health and money decisions. They'd talked about it and he had accidental death insurance that made it possible for her to learn a trade to support herself.  Their 2 kids agreed with pulling the plug when 3 doctors all said he would never, ever, come out of the coma.  

His accident got all of us thinking about what our final wishes were.  I decided that if I wouldn't come out of a coma in 90 days, they should remove all life support, even stop feeding me.  The family needs closure and sitting on a machine in hospital forever isn't closure.  I also wouldn't want my better half to be stuck indefinitely.  Mom always said, life is for the living.

Anyway, we share important data using a KeePass password manager/database.  This isn't anything with legal documents.  The will and access to it are controlled by access to each of our safety deposit boxes.  Nothing great inside those boxes, except the will and insurance paperwork and lists of accounts. No cash, jewels, or anything on value.  If you know you are going to die soon (nobody gets out of here alive), ensure your executors know which bank, and how to gain access to the box.  My mother had a "on-death" insurance payment of $10K to cover expenses in the initial month after her death.  If your executor can't "front" the money for about a month post-death as the govt paperwork catches up, or pay your rent or mortgage until they got your estate out of those things, it would be nice to have something like that setup.

Death certificates provide access to all your other financial accounts.  Ending utiliities, magazine subscriptions, newspapers, cableTV, etc is pretty easy without a death cert - well - perhaps not the utilities. Utility companies need to be stricter since there are some nasty exes who will screw around with water, electricity, garbage collection, and telephone services sometimes.  Some will require a death certificate which can take 2-4 weeks to get some places.

I'd never place any of these things online.  The legality of someone else trying to access your online accounts is questionable.  For example, it is illegal to touch any bank or investment accounts until the bank turns them over to the estate. Doing anything BEFORE then will cause issues, so it really isn't useful to ensure anyone has access before death.  They just need to know the company and account numbers and if you have a personal advisor at these places, the name and contact information for that person.

Anyways, KeePass databases are cross platform and can be used by a number of different programs on every platform.  To prevent early access, if you feel that's needed, there are some ways - like giving half of the passphrase for access to 2 different, unconnected, people.  Or placing the file on a memory card/usb stick somewhere in your home and placing the location of that inside the safe deposit box.  There are lots of ways to limit access until your death.  BTW, be certain to verify the USB storage every 6 months.

If you live alone, I suppose things can be more complex. Will someone have to break into your home to gain access. May want to arrange access to someone trusted.  Key in a hidden rock, garage door code, or just a shared key.

Of course, you may need to plan for a contingency should you and your better half die in an accident. If just you die, that person gets control of everything, I hope and any living expenses are covered for the rest of that life too.
