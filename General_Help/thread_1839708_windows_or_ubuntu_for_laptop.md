### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-09-06
Your Milage May Vary. Some people get better battery life on Linux, some get better life on Windows.

At the moment, **some** laptops use more power than they should on Linux, due to a bug. The effects of this have been overstated - some people are under the impression that ALL laptops get 30% less battery life, or something like that. It's not true - some laptops are affected, with 10% less battery life.

It's also important to note that battery life regressions have probably happened in the past too, but it's only now that we actually have a benchmark suite that tests battery life, and the company that makes it is making a big noise about the power regression.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2011-09-06
The term "better" is so subjective -- it's all but useless.  In general, my own evaluation of a PC depends on how well it does what I need it to do.  IF it already has Windows on it and that works well for you, changing to something else will not automatically make it "better".

Power usage varies from PC to PC, depending a lot on the specific hardware in the box, and how well the OS running there handles CPU scaling.  If you buy a laptop with an OS already installed, the vendor nearly always makes sure that CPU scaling works.  That will result in generally lower power usage -- with resulting longer battery life.

While it's true that the current excessive battery drain is a "bug" -- that's not much help if you HAVE to run on battery and your laptop dies too soon.

One thing you can try is running from the Ubuntu CD.  That will tell you how well Ubuntu detects your laptop hardware and installs the proper drivers.  Also, if it then is running HOT with the fan going nearly all the time -- that's telling you that it is NOT doing CPU scaling and it will use up the batter power faster.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2011-09-06

Unless you get a response from someone here who has used the same laptop with both Ubuntu and Win7, you're NOT going to get a definitive answer regarding battery life.

As I already tried to tell you, a key determining factor is the ability to scale back the CPU usage when the machine is not being heavily used.  Greater CPU usage translates to hotter CPU, which translates to faster fan speeds -- which translates to faster battery drain.

You have to TRY the Ubuntu distro you want to run on your laptop to see how well it works -- and whether or not CPU scaling is supported properly.

You could also try Xubuntu and Lubuntu -- to see how well they work and to get a feel for battery drain.

Basically it  is where you set your computer to "on demand" as opposed to "performance" or maybe even minimum or "powersave". It is possible to do that at boot so that your computer uses less cpu and the battery lasts longer. I guess a question to ask yourself is how much are you willing to sacrifice in terms of performance and features to achieve a longer battery life. For example you can use less ram and cpu by using something like abiword as opposed to libreoffice but you will not have as much features. Also, you will probably use less cpu if you use a lighter desktop like Lubuntu or Openbox as opposed to using Compiz or Unity. I suppose the extreme would be using a command line only interface when your on battery power. That would probably save the most juice. Personally, I like a gui for my web browser and email at least though.
