### Post by Prospero2006 on 2008-06-01
If you are going to buy a graphics card, be sure it is compatible with your motherboard. There are all kinds of specifications out there. If you are not interested in gaming, and you don't care about compiz, for 20-40 bucks you can get a NVIDIA card that will plug into a PCI slot. (It will have maybe 64-128 megabytes of onboard ram.)

That's your safest bet for cheap compatibility. 

I would suggest you stay away from ATI. ATI is very tricky to get working sometimes, and even at its best, it's in my opinion buggy. NVIDIA works very well.

### Post by Prospero2006 on 2008-06-01
Yes, I'm a technology teacher in San Antonio, Texas. 
I installed Ubuntu in two of our school computer labs and 
the entire curriculum is pretty much based around free software
in my class and one other. (6th and 7th grade.) --It's a magnet technology
school. 

I also own [http://linuxclassroom.com](http://linuxclassroom.com) 

I am using it to archive the video tutorials I put together for my students, but I'm finding that a lot of other people find them to be helpful.

As for math software, the goal is to get the kids invested in what they are doing while at the same time making them think quantitatively. The blender is pretty good for getting kids to think like this. It's an easy sell, too. It takes a long time and the instructor has to be familiar with the program, but building 3-d models and making games is all about working with very specific mathematical concepts.

It's very easy to get kids to buy into using the blender. My students can't get enough of it. Once that happens, you can start assigning challenges that deal with geometry and math. 

You could also try Celestia or Stellarium, both science programs. There is a lot of measurement that can be done there in terms of mass, distance, speed, etc.. The slick 3-D interface those two programs both offer is also an easy sell with the kids.

In all three of those cases though, as an instructor, you have to really do the homework yourself and have access to a computer lab where you can install the software. 

Actually teaching the stuff while the kids are in front of the computers is also an extreme art form I've found, but there are tools that can help there too. (iTALC, for example allows you to take over all the monitors in the class so you can demonstrate concepts.)

Anyway, that's probably a longer answer than you wanted, but I'd be happy to help you out. I'm all about getting Linux into public educational settings.
