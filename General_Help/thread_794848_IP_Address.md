### Post by igfud on 2008-05-15
IP addresses are assigned in blocks to Internet Service Providers (ISPs).  These addresses are then assigned to your computer/router.  They can also be used to trace the rough geographic area of your ISP, through sites like [http://www.ip-adress.com/](http://www.ip-adress.com/)

Every time you access a website or use any web service, your IP address is being used to route the data to your computer.  Wikipedia article: [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address)

An IP address can either be static or dynamic.  Static addresses remain the same even after turning off your comptuer or resetting your router (your ISP assigns the same address each time you connect).  Dynamic IP addresses are assigned by the ISP as clients connect, so they will change when your router is reset or your computer is shut down, depending on your network configuration.

An IP address in itself does not specifically relate to illegal hacking.  Granted, it may be used in the hacking process, but just because it is a number that can be used to transfer data with your network.

> **supertails said:**
> What is a SSH sever and what does this mean? "Be careful, too; the GNUs (and a lot of the non GNUs) will stomp you if you use the term "hacker" to name such lowlife"

A SSH server (Secure SHell) is a service that allows you to use the shell of your computer remotely and securely through data encryption (you can also tunnel many other services through it).
When you connect to an SSH server you need to login as you'd do on your own computer, so if you have a blank password or one that can be easily guessed then it's very easy for anyone to login to your PC and do virtually anything.
However, this service is not activated by default, so you needn't worry about it, it was just an example.

The last part was half joking. By "the GNUs" I mean the people from the [Free Software Foundation]("http://www.fsf.org/") and many others of the Open Source Community.
Contrary to what the media and movies have popularized hackers __are NOT__ network criminals, they will not log in into your PC and steal your data or create havoc, a hacker is someone who enjoys learning about computers (or anything else) and has been able to polish her skills into something that helps the community.
Crackers, on the other hand, are people who enjoy breaking systems and damaging data.

### Post by Winawer on 2008-05-15
Giving out your IP address is (very) roughly analogous to giving out your postal address:  with it, people can find a route to your house, and they know where *you* live.  


But with regards to cracking, the same analogy holds:  once they arrive at your house, whether they can get inside and steal things depends upon the security that you have in place.  Good locks on the doors, well-lit pathways, maybe bars on ground-level windows, an alarm system that works?  Then it's going to be hard to get in.  Did you instead leave the door wide open with a sign that says "Gone to Hawaii for two weeks?"  Well, then, expect to get robbed.  


Computer security is similar.  A basic firewall, a good password on your account, simple network security (if you don't know what an SSH server is, don't have one installed on your system.  Chances are, if you don't know, you probably don't) will stop most casual attacks.  Just like securing your home, though, there's limits.  A dedicated attacker with a lot of resources can break into your home, and he can probably break into your computer too;  nothing is foolproof.  But taking basic precautions will keep you safe in *most* cases.
