### Post by CraigBray on 2008-12-17
The truth is, even though people are capable of creating unix based viruses, there wouldn't be a use for it, in both quantity and duration. Windows viruses (like the nastiest one I've seen at my job which is AntiVirus 2008 (which goes by a load of other names by the way)) spread around through ads and other internet links/cookies/temp files. They make them for Windows only because over 90% of the Windows userbase doesn't understand how to remove a virus, and around 60 to 70 percent of that 90, do not notice a virus because everyone is so used to blaming Microsoft for all problems. Take a lot of things with Vista, for example, because a lot of the problems were programs, not Windows. I will state this, however stable Windows is (and for the most part, it is) it's a scapegoat. Back onto my point here, is that using those examples, a virus for Linux would therein be ineffective, because if someone can get a Linux machine running with all drivers, they can  most certainly notice a problem with a virus/spyware/malware. If someone makes a virus, it's because they know they can get a result, in AntiVirus2008's position they can get a subscription that takes credit card info through a bank account in the Cayman Islands. Linux's userbase is so small, and so many of them can fix it, that it has no valid point to make a virus on it, if it wrecks anything, 9 times out of ten the user A) doesn't care about data or B) has a backup. (These are my observations over my few years with computers. I could be wrong, but that's my opinion)
