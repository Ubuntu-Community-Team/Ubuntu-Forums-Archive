### Post by prizrak on 2007-05-30
Kamaboko,
This has been mentioned already. Dell gets kick backs for the trial software they install into Windows machines. They get special deals from MS on the software (do you honestly think Dell pays what they charge you?) like Office and A/V. 

Another thing you are not understanding is that Dell has been selling Windows machines for years. They have the talent and infrastructure needed in place for testing and system integration, All their manufacturers ship the drivers for them and Dell really just puts that crap together. 

On the Linux side they had to invest into those machines, if you looked at the vlog the person responsible for Ubuntu systems said that while Linux is Linux there was a learning curve from RHEL to Ubuntu. There are testing costs that are higher than that on Windows. They had to pick out the components that will work together and not screw up on suspend/resume. 

Ever wonder why it's often times cheaper to build your own system instead of going with a prebuilt one? (not much difference at low end but the higher end you go the more difference it makes) It's because system integration/testing costs money. There is also the cost of marketing to be considered. They had to create a new website, which means diverting resources from their main website. 

You might also want to consider economies of scale. Dell ships thousands of Windows systems to consumers making price per unit smaller. 

Right now Dell took a risk, they are the FIRST [major] OEM to offer preloaded Linux for the home user market. They have invested time, money and energy into this venture and the pay off is very uncertain. So yes it's quite possible that even with all those things in mind Ubuntu systems cost Dell less than Windows systems. Even if that happens to be the case, what is the problem? So they make an extra $20 on the Ubuntu system, seriously....

P.S. A MacBook Pro is $500 more than a ThinkPad with the SAME EXACT specs (and believe me quality wise they are the same). Apple also doesn't pay for the OS, they write it in house but you don't seem to be complaining about that. 

P.P.S. To use your car analogy, if you have a car with an engine that is free for the OEM and one that is pay for. However the free engine runs quiter, uses less oil and gas and only needs to be maintained every 100K miles. Would you say that it's a bad deal if both cars cost the same?
