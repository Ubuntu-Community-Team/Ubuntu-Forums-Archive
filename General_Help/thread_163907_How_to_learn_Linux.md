### Post by Mustard on 2006-04-21
A lot of stuff in linux is passed on by word of mouth, as documentation is sometimes lacking.  It really helps to be part of a community either via a forum, IRC channel, local linux user group, mailing list and other assorted mediums for communication.  The Ubuntu community is doing a good job of filling the documentation void, as are other communities like Gentoo (I hear they have very good community resources too).  It's for the most part volunteer work, which makes progress quite patchy and undirected at times.  To really get involved in linux, it's pretty much a necessity to get involved in a community of some kind.  A community can offer you access to a common pool of knowledge or knowledge base, and save you having to recreate your own knowledge base.  People in the community have made the same mistakes, had the same errors/problems/issues and can inform you of the methods they used to overcome them.  Doing it alone, you have to do all the work over again.

### Post by presentt on 2006-04-21
I'm by no means "expert," or even "intermediate."  I'm a Linux beginner, plain and true.  But the way I've learned what I have is by diving right into Linux.

I installed Hoary Hedgehog on my desktop machine, saw that Linux was right for me, and then proceeded to modify and tinker with everything I possibly could.  Don't let the terminal intimidate you.  Read tutorials and forums; if something sounds interesting, give it a try.

Then I downloaded Breezy Badger, and reformatted my ext3 partition so it would be completely clean to install the new version.  And I had to start over, reconfiguring everything again.  This time, however, I didn't need to read as many tutorials.  Even for things I forgot how to do, I could look around in different .conf files and trigger my memory.

I also attempted to install and configure an FTP server on my home network using an old laptop I have.  I first tried with Hoary, to ultimate success, even though I was using the base-server installation.  I think this really refined what I was learning.  Without a GUI, I needed to gain a firmer understanding of how Ubuntu (for you, Linux in general) was working.

Of course, then I updated to Breezy on the server, and I'm stuck on a major network problem.  But that's another thread, and another learning experience altogether.

My advice: just try anything.  What do you have to lose?  You should be backing up important data anyways.

### Post by elamericano on 2006-04-22
A good suggestion I got once was to learn all the commands in the /bin directory by reading their man pages. This is the foundation of Linux, and it's important for any future Linux expert to know all of these (including their options). If you ever try to build an LFS system ([http://www.linuxfromscratch.org/](http://www.linuxfromscratch.org/)) you will see that this is true.
