### Post by SamNSane on 2008-09-09
Well, "best" is best defined by you.

You might try analyzing your needs and being very specific about what you need in a task manager. Ability to create links among various tasks and events? Ability to see different types of functional views of tasks in their relationship to projects overall? Links to data / files outside the manager's own data stores?

I can tell you that I have suffered long to find a task manager that would really fit my needs -- combination systems analyst / system admin / researcher / writer. I need my data to be really free-form but still highly searchable and with lots of extensibility in the way it gets tied together and with other objects. 

GanttProject is a really nice full-fledged project manager, almost useless to me. But it's so good I almost want to use it anyway. If you need classical project management features and reporting it could be an excellent choice.

I just started switching over from using a combination of the Microsoft applications OneNote and Outlook (2007 versions, earlier ones being much less appealing to me) to using cross-platform Open Source software. Those two MS applications, when teamed together and used judiciously, have given me just about what I needed in terms of flexibility and features.

But using closed source software and somewhat proprietary data storage formats (MS isn't nearly as bad about that as it used to be.) have always driven me nuts.

I'm moving my e-mail to Thunderbird, calendar functions to Sunbird (mostly same as Lightning), and OneNote databases to WikidPad.

I have to say that Thunderbird meets my deliberately-kept-simple e-mail needs just fine. Sunbird / Lighting are still really rudimentary in capability and can be pretty flakey, but usable -- as long as they are used with care to protect the data.

WikidPad is, so far in my estimation, an absolutely awesome notes / tasks manager. The learning curve is not steep, despite the huge assortment of features. It does take time to learn it simply because of the complexity, but it really seems especially appropriate in a Linux environment. It's a bit early to be sure, but it may do pretty well for me as a replacement for OneNote. If you have experience using Wikis, it may seem like a natural fit.

Good luck!

It occurs to me that trying to use WikidPad under Ubuntu may be a bit of a trial. You might want to look for Zim in the Synaptic package manager.
