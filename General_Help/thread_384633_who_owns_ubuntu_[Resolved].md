### Post by mac.ryan on 2007-03-14
Far from being an expert on this, but I think the question in itself would need some reformulation. I think there are different levels of ownership involved and I can see at least three of them:[LIST=1]
[*]the rights given by having the intellectual property of the code of a programme
[*]"ubuntu" as trademark
[*]the ubuntu CD[/LIST]not necessarily the three owners might coincide (as it is for a movie on a DVD: you own the DVD itself, but the copyright is hold by the distribution company, while the title of the movie itself might have been registered by the producer).

In the case of ubuntu my guess (but _I could be wrong on this_!) is:[LIST=1]
[*]**the programmers**, who - being linux and GNU stuff free - chose to pass the most of their rights on to anybody who would use their production (this is done through the license).
[*]**canonical** might have registered "ubuntu" as a trademark.
[*]**you** are the owner of your ubuntu-CD, whether this has been delivered to you by ship-it or home-produced.[/LIST]If anybody knows for sure, I would be interested to know too! :)

### Post by 23meg on 2007-03-14
Canonical owns the Ubuntu trademark, but not Ubuntu as a product in the sense that Apple owns the iPod; Canonical and the Ubuntu Foundation sponsor Ubuntu by providing funding for its development and distribution. Since the Ubuntu base install consists entirely of Free software (except the Restricted component), you own it as much as Canonical, the Ubuntu core developers and the respective developers of those individual pieces of sotware do.

### Post by mac.ryan on 2007-03-15
> **23meg said:**
> Since the Ubuntu base install consists entirely of Free software (except the Restricted component), you own it as much as Canonical, the Ubuntu core developers and the respective developers of those individual pieces of sotware do.

Again, I am not an expert and I might be wrong, but i have doubts this is the case. **Linux is not  in the public domain **(i.e. owned by the collectivity), linux creators licensed it in a particular way that - for as much as paradoxical it might sound - limits your freedom in order to keep up the collective freedom.

In other words: because linux developers have the intellectual property over their code, they could be entitled to sell it, keep it secret, using for distributing only the binaries... **If you would own linux the same way they do, you would have the same entitlements**, which is not the case, as the GNU GPL license obliges you not to do a certain number of these things.

AFAIK, only if linux would be in the public domains, it would be owned by users as much as by programmers. This is for example the case for old books, that in fact can be printed and put for sale, edited, modified, released at the newsagent quarterly, added to the [gutenberg project]("http://www.gutenberg.org/wiki/Main_Page"), etc...

It has to be noitced, however, that once you licensed something in some form, you can't get "regress" the license anymore. This is for example important when answering those who say "When Linus Torvalds will need money, he will copyright linux and you will all have to pay hundreds of $$ to him"...
