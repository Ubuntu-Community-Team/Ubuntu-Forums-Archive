### Post by snowpine on 2011-04-12
Most Ubuntu releases are supported 18 months, so 10.10 will be supported through April 2012. 

Long Term Support (LTS) releases are supported 3 years; 10.04 is the current LTS with support through April 2013.

"A good alternative to eventually switch to" depends entirely on what you liked about the older Ubuntus (and/or what you don't like about the new release). A visit to distrowatch.com may be informative. Mint, Debian, Fedora, and OpenSUSE are a few very popular alternatives.

I think you and I are glass-half-empty/glass-half-full on this topic. I think it's a tremendous benefit that we have a wide range of Linux distros, from conservative-slow-changing-everything-must-work-or-else to bleeding-edge-give-me-the-latest-NOW! I like to think of a beautiful rainforest with a huge biodiversity of life forms, yes this rainforest can be a dangerous and overwhelming place, but I'd choose it over a 1,000-acre Iowa cornfield any day!

As an example, look at the Fedora/Red Hat relationship. As you may know, they are basically different stages of the same product; new features are introduced and tested in Fedora and eventually end up in Red Hat Enterprise Linux. A person who is skilled with one can easily transition to the other. You can choose the "Rawhide" branch of Fedora and be so-bleeding-edge-your-eyes-will-water, or you can choose RHEL and have supported Gnome 2.x until 2018! Guess which OS the United States Navy nuclear submarine fleet and the New York Stock Exchange use, Fedora or Red Hat? :)

Yes it can be frustrating and time consuming to find the perfect distro (and even then there are some people who don't find one--and who end up designing their own new "perfect" distro!) but doing so is a fun rite of passage that I've personally enjoyed immensely.
