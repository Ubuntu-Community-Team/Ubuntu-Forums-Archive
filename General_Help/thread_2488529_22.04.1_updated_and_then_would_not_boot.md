### Post by DuckHook on 2023-07-07
Are your HDDs healthy? By your own report, it's old equipment that you are trying to re&#8209;purpose, so my first concern would be: what shape is everything in? Is the PSU putting out clean power? Is the RAM all good and tested? After you have ascertained that the HW is all good, only then would I look at the following:

I take it that you are using mdadm?

Linux (and, by extension, Ubuntu) is a highly complex system, so avoid complicating it further if at all possible. In that vein and especially given that your use case is not mission critical (photo storage), why use RAID?

RAID is designed to preserve up&#8209;time in case of single HDD point failure. It actually increases system fragility because it must add points of failure to the OS to trade off for disk redundancy. It is also a bloated older technology. Though I don't use it myself, I've noticed quite a few people wrestling with its problems on these forums over the years.

LVM is a simpler technology that is tried, true and tested. It's what I use for simple multi&#8209;disk consolidations. It has most of the advantages of RAID but doesn't seem to generate as many problems, at least, insofar as I can tell by way of anecdotal forum help requests.

Another technology is the integrated file system/volume manager solution, ZFS, that I've had good results with so far. Others speak highly of BTRFS. These and other similar solutions are newer technology that integrate disk management into the file system itself for a more holistic approach to volume management. Those who use it attest that, with the right equipment, it is wickedly fast, responsive and very reliable. My ZFS server configured as RAID-Z1 (what you might know as RAID 5) has worked flawlessly for two years with no downtime except for user&#8209;initiated upgrades. It is superior to mdadm for data integrity, error handling, corruption resistance and recovery, but I combine ZFS with ECC RAM which further enhances its capabilities.

Windows doesn't offer very compelling integrated VM/FS solutions, hence the ongoing reliance on NTFS horked on top of RAID. This seems to be a Microsoft theme: building rickety houses of cards because they won't abandon the obsolete but would rather slather kludges on top of kludges to keep everything stumbling along.

You don't have to put up with such mediocrity in Linux, so try out the better tools. You can always go back to RAID if you don't like them.

### Post by MAFoElffen on 2023-07-07
I am also a past Windows and Linux Administrator.

Windows Server, when it came up with Storage Spaces, I feel like they tried to replicate LVM2... But LVM2 is decades ahead.

As for traditional hardware and software RAID strategies... Some technologies are just hanging onto "old places and things" for the sentimental values of. Newer technology and methodologies prevail. 

RE: [https://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2488301&p=14148830#post14148830](https://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2488301&p=14148830#post14148830)

Further:
> **MAFoElffen said:**
> RAID is NOT a substitute for Backups or a  Disaster Recovery Plan. Reiterating what TheFu said, because something  is possible, does not make it a good idea.

The disadvantages of both traditional hardware and software RAID is that  is does not give warnings before it fails and goes into a degraded or  failed state. Reassembling an Array on huge data may take days, and  sometimes, because of the extra stress during a reassembly process, you  will lose a second drive and lose everything.

RAID1 does not get capacity of performance benefits. It is a write once  redundant method, so it risks failure from bit rot. It will not guaranty  that when one disk fails that the other will come up without errors. A  RAID1 risks a second disk failure twice that of RAID5 on reassembly.

I myself, used to use LVM2 on mdadm RAID... Over a decade ago. Many  things have changed, grown and or changed in the last 15 years. I  switched from doing that, to LVM2 RAID, then back to using ZFS with  RAIDz. 

LVM2 uses MD to implement it's internal raid devices, and adds more to  that. If using LVM2 RAID, LVM2 at least has a 'raid_fault_policy' that  can warn or automatically bring in a spare when there are too many  errors. It will also report strings similar to "read failed after 0 of  4096 at 0: Input/output error" when there is a problem. So adds warnings  before a failure.

Since 'how' you worded and brought up the questions and discussion...  TheFu brought up the idea of using self-healing CoW Volume Managers such  as ZFS, BTRFS or ReFS... These try to detect and protect against bit  rot, warn when there are errors,  and are meant to be self healing. But  that is another discussion.

Many current NAS systems now use ZFS.

I've been doing ZFS since 2005, in OpenSolaris... I have come full circle in trying "other things". For me, ZFS still, just fulfills all my needs.
