### Post by jparshall on 2010-11-01
First off, thanks to the folks that have talked up CodeWeavers; much appreciated.  I'll try to temporarily throttle my twisted inner Marketing soul and answer as candidly as I can here.  :)

1) First off, in terms of the technical underpinnings, CodeWeavers' flavor of Wine is very similar to free Wine.  We do some things to make sure that our core supported apps run under CrossOver, though.  Free Wine doesn't necessarily have an incentive to keep, say, Office97 running on a day-to-day basis.  So, if a new patch that fixes a bug in Foozleware happens to break Office97 in free Wine, well, that's not such a big deal to the Wine community.  But it *is* a big deal to us.  So, we tend to be a bit more stable and tested than free Wine.

2) As some folks have mentioned, we do give all of our work on Wine back to the Wine Project. So, we play by the rules, and do a lot of good work for Wine as a whole.  And, of course, we get the benefit of the larger community's patches as well.  We do have children to feed, and your $40 goes to make that happen, but in the process it also provides a direct conduit for further funding of development in Wine.

3) To be honest, the difference between "supported" and "unsupported" apps, in terms of the tech support we try to deliver, isn't as black and white as you'd think.  If you're a customer of ours, our ninjas honestly do try to answer questions about unsupported apps as well.  Technically speaking, they don't have to care about unsupported apps, but in practice they actually *do* try to care.  They may not always have an answer, of course.  But customers are always allowed to ask anyway.

Anyway, hope that helps.

Best Wishes,

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2010-11-01
CodeWeavers is a company -- which produces, among other products Wine.  Asking the question the way you did is like asking Win7 Upgrade Advisor vs Microsoft!

From what I understand, since CodeWeavers various CrossOver products come in commercial form, THEY get the code improvements first.  Later, these are ported into Wine.

There are also other products line winetricks that can help simplify the difficult process of tuning Wine for specic games.
