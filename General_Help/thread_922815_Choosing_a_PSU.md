Meaning, will the PSU come standard with the appropriate PATA connectors also? All of them come with some old (pata) connectors. Yes, you can "just coil up" the sata ones. You should read the specs of the psu (say in newegg) to see how many connectors of each kind the psu comes with. Some newer psus come with connectors which you can plug in only the ones you want.

### Post by isecore on 2008-09-19
> **skynux said:**
> OK, well I guess the noob question of the hour is, Do dual rail PSUs hook up to the MoBo in two places, or do both rails connect to the same connector? I mean, does it just have twice as many wires hooked up to the same connector, or what? 

If that is the case, then I guess it would work fine. I find it hard to believe that they would have changed things so much in the last couple of years that it would be nearly impossible to find a PSU for a perfectly good motherboard.

Dual rails will not mean any practical difference as far as connection goes. The ATX 2.0 standard specifies two power-rails for PSUs, the original 1.1 specification does not. This is simply evolution since computers have come a long way since the original 1.1 was written.

Two power-rails is simply so that there will be some kind of redundancy. How this is achieved depends on the PSU in question, but most PSUs put drives/etc on one rail, and PCIe on the other. The CPU always has it's own dedicated rail AFAIK.

But to answer your question - there will be no real difference for you. You'll plug it in just the same anyway.
