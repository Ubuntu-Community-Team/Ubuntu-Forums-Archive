### Post by deathshadow on 2006-01-30
Ah, the old debate. Here's my thoughts.

First off, let's start with all the various gecko kine, as ALL of them I have tried (firefox, mozilla suite, epiphany, etc, etc, etc) on ALL the operating systems I've tried (linux, BeOs, Solaris, Windows, OS X)...

**_LEAK MEMORY LIKE A STEEL SIEVE_** *(best kind of sieve)* to the point I can consistantly lock it up in under 20 minutes use to the point you have to kill it's process... and usually around the ten minute mark it gets so annoyingly slow you are tempted to kill it anyways - EVEN on machines where people say there's nothing wrong with firefox (usually to their great surprise)... I know I have pretty aggressive browsing habits, but sheesh.

That there's been multiple bugzilla entries on it as 'unconfirmed' for over two years now, even with EXPLICIT directions on how to recreate the problem... and when I called them on it the best response they could come up with was to attempt to take me to task for using the term 'crash' instead of 'hung', a distinction I've not heard in over 30 years of computer use... {censored} that {censored} and {censored} them too. *(sorry folks, but I've not been THAT pissed off about anything in a long time and the constant media darling praises are wearing my nerves thin... I actually WOULDN'T be ranting but the minute you badmouth firefox everyone assumes you are trolling, so if you are going to be damned for a crime, commit it.)*

[I was at least relieved to find out I wasn't the only one encountering the same issues.]("http://www.informationweek.com/news/showArticle.jhtml?articleID=175007152") Nothing like watching a browser occupy 512 megs of RAM and 99% CPU when the only page open is Google.

That it routes image saves through it's slow *** download manager *(which chews an amount of cpu WAY out of proportion with what it's doing)* which seems to be the core of the memory leaks and only excasterbates it's buggy slow 'internal tasking' model doesn't help. 

So basically, {censored} Firefox... {censored} Gecko, and {censored} any of it's kin and kine. I have been so soured by the experience that it damn near broke my faith in Open Source solutions. I wouldn't even have it installed if I didn't 'have' to have it for testing browser compatability on the websites I maintain... and have been known to deinstall it under linux distributions (where possible)


So, what are the alternatives to the alternative. Well, there's Konqueror (and it's KHTML brethren), which despite a valiant effort and some (limited) backwards support from Apple definately falls short on Javascript, compliance to standards and functionality. It has snatched the title of 'also ran' away from our one remaining alternative:

Opera. They have been in the browser business longer than ANY of the current players - Including Microsoft and Netscape - but never quite managed to shake the title of "King of the Also Ran". Despite their status most of the 'innovations' that are expected on new browsers or added via plugins not only originated on Opera, they are built in. I don't NEED Extensions under Opera because 99% of that stuff is already built in. Tabbed Browsing? *Built in, did it first.*RSS? *Built in, did it first.* User Javascripts like Seamonkey? *Built in, did it first.* Dictionary or Encyclopedia lookups? *Built in, did it first.* I'm often amazed at how often I'll hear firefox or IE users badmouth opera because they cannot add their favorite plug-in/extension/whatever you want to call it today, when in fact the feature they are looking for is ALREADY BUILT IN.

The latest stable release (8.5) is tiny, coming in at a slim 3.7 megs for Windows and 4.0 megs for Ubuntu - and that's not only with the functionality of most of the 'plugins' people add to firefox, but also with a built in e-mail client that blows thunderbird out of the water. Let's see, Firefox + Thunderbird totals what, 6.5 megs for Windows and something like 18.3 megs for linux?

Then there's the stuff they do nobody else has caught onto: like the ability to drag and drop a favicon onto the toolbar as a button linking to that url. (I have buttons for google, OSNews, IMDB, NewEgg and even these forums!).. or that you can make your own buttons using plain english "open page in background" and even put them in a html file so people can drag and drop the buttons from a website into their browser. [The Nontroppo Opera wiki has a whole bunch of these.](http://nontroppo.org/wiki/CustomButtons). Just the consistancy on things like ctrl-click or ctrl-shift-click (open in new tab, open in new tab in background respectively) work on ALL of the buttons and links across the whole program, be they a hyperlink on a website, a button on the toolbar (including forward/back!) or even in your bookmarks.

Not to mention they are the heavyweight in the mobile market, have been for years... a Market in which Gecko isn't even a choice for being 'too bloated'. If you are running Symbian OS on your phone or PDA, there's only one weapon of choice, and there are even versions for some Windows Mobile devices. Their small screen rendering technology combined with the new "Opera Mini" ups the ante so high it is going to be Microsofts turn to play catch-up.

So with all these innovations, what's been holding them back? Rendering quality, Cost, unwillingness of people to switch from what comes with their OS.

Opera has had the stigma of 'always being a generation behind on site rendering' almost since it's inception. They were in a constant state of catch-up with Netscape and IE until the death of Netscape development and restart as mozilla. Combine that with the perpetual standstill of IE and they finally had the chance to even the field... They most certainly have done so.

Opera as of version 8 is without a doubt the most standards compliant browser out there. This is NOT necessarily a good thing as standards compliance isn't enough, since even when sites 'validate' that doesn't mean they are designed to display how the standard says, but how either IE or Firefox think the 'standard' should be implemented... regrettably not only do the two rarely ever see eye to eye on anything.

Web coders can be broken into two camps - IE and Gecko. The IE community codes for IE only, and if you are lucky MIGHT toss in some hacks to make the site at least render in other browsers *(and then usually only for firefox)*. The Gecko community attempts to code for standards, realize the standards aren't implemented right in gecko and hack it to display in firefox the way they want, *(or don't realize it and just code the workarounds not knowing they are workarounds)*, validate it, then load hack upon hack upon hack atop it to make it work in IE. What makes that way of doing it humorous is I've seen people use 29 to 60k in javascripts to make CSS layout work how they want in IE, but have the nerve to call using tables for layout a 'hack'. RIGHT. *(Still better than coding a website for fixed width display - the surest indicator of a inexperienced site coder working on a job bigger than they should be... nothing like that narrow 800 pixel wide strip down the screen with 240 to 400 pixel borders at the sides)*

What this means for Opera (and other browsers not in the big two) is that not only do they have to be prepared to handle standards, they also have to be prepared to deal with the various hacks used by both sides of the coding fence. It's a big task for someone in the #3 slot, and I have to say they've done a excellent job of it. Yes, there are some pages that still don't display right, but those seem to be decreasing in number daily as more and more people become aware of opera and adjust their code appropriately - just like when Mozilla was new.

As to cost, Opera for most of it's life costed money and when they did offer a free version, it was supported by ad banners. When all the alternatives are free, I find it very surprising they stayed afloat... This is probably the REASON for all their innovations and features. Thankfully back in September they went free without ad banners, so all is good. I know some of the open source zealots out there will still get thier panties in a twist over it not beeing 'truly free' as they aren't sharing source, but to be brutally honest: {censored} them too. If it doesn't cost me money and they still support/update it as needed, I could really care less about the underlying politics.

Basically, those first two problems are now non-issues... That leaves convincing users to switch, which is always a uphill battle. Given the features, size and speed Opera has become my weapon of choice. I suggest to anyone out there give version 8.5 a chance, or if you are feeling particularly daring, 9.0 Beta... Beta, RIGHT... 9.0 Beta seems to be more stable than ANY Firefox 'stable' release I've tried. If this is their idea of Beta, 9.0 final is going to truly kick some serious red furry tail.

If features, security, stability and speed aren't enough to get most people to switch... Well, let's just say there's a reason IE still has the lions share, and it has nothing to do with reason - no it stems from ignorance, lazyness, and just plain wishful thinking.
