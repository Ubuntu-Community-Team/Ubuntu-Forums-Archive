### Post by lordofduct on 2009-10-13
I agree with kareeser

it's very similar to just about EVERYTHING in my house that creates heat. I live in Florida where our weathermen will have written in the weekly forcast:

friday - humid
saturday - hot and humid
sunday - STICKY

sticky? really? it's going to be "sticky"... that's such technical word. 

Anyways... I digress

so yeah we're humid as all hell. And condensation forms a lot... that's actually why the ceiling in my bathroom fell out (the AC condensor is just above the bathroom and the combined variable temperatures of the bathroom and the attic temperature cause for majour condensation).


Now solutions.

Well the video card gets hot, you can't stop this... it's all about directing that heat. A better heatsink or fan won't change this because that's how the heat gets to the case anyways... heatsinks take the heat from the processor and move it to the interior of the case, and the fans take the heat out of the case.

You could always get a card that runs at a lower temp... but if you have 2 cards right now I'm suspecting you want power. And a lower temp card usually/almost always means less power.

Of course a new video card that is just as good as the 2 older cards you may be using right now can fix this. 1 card means less heat then 2 cards. I upgraded from the Nvido 7950 gx2 (a dual gpu card) to something in the 9000 series (don't remember what) which was faster and better and cooler.


You can also go with better venting and heat redirection. For instance water cooling not only is effective at disipating heat quickly, but it also absorbs the heat into the water lines as opposed to letting it sit in the case... this redirection of the heat would lower the risk. BUT it means an expensive water cooling system... and further more introducing water into the case which you're trying to stop.

Other things are better fans and those arc vents and the sort (like you can get that vent tubing that goes over the processor heatsink and fan that directs the air directly out of the case).



Lastly, reduce humidity. Here in Florida AC isn't ran just because it's hot. Actually it isn't that hot here. With the exception of August it really stays in the 80's and gets down the 60's and 70's in the winter. August it hits the 90's, we rarely see 100+ temperatures. What we DO have is the humidity. And 85 in humidity feels like 105 in dry Los Angeles (I know this because I moved there and people complained about the heat one day when it hit 100 and I found it GORGEOUS out I hadn't been so comfortable in years).

But yeah we run our AC because of the humidity, not because of the heat perse... some ignorant people will say "because it's hot"... but really it's because it's humid. The humidity feels like heat, but it's just water in the air. I keep my AC on all year round at about 80 or 81 just to keep the humidity down. Some houses also come with actual humidity controllers in the house (my house had one, but it was removed because it was old and inefficient).

Another cool little substance is you can buy these pellets that sit in a dish and absorb water from the air. I use them in my safe to protect the paper documents in it from falling apart. It's also very effective.

Another thing, normalize the temperatures of the exterior of the case to the interior of the case. For instance say when you drive your car on a hot humid day (like every day here in florida) and you turn on the AC full blast... you'll note the windows get all wet and damp. Vice versa this occurs when you turn the heat on full blast in the winter seasons up north.

Both defrost the window, but to much water starts to condense. When defrosting your window what you want to do is normalize the temperatures. Get the temperatures as relative to each other as possible (will out getting cold or hot yourself). Same goes for your house and computer. If you crank your AC down to like 60 degrees (I don't get how anyone finds that comfortable, but I know lots of people that do) you're going to have issues with condensation. The case temp is probably much warmer then that. So why not try bring the house temp up so it is closer to that of the case... of course barring in mind that you don't want to be uncomfortable.

With that said though, if you think 72 is uncomfortable... well take a freakin' shirt off or something. You might save some money on your electric bill anyways. Most electric companies suggest setting the thermostat at room temp (77 degrees fahrenheit)... a few degrees warmer in the summer and a few degrees cooler in the winter to conserve energy. Well the same suggestion goes for the health of your computer.

I doubt it's water; and as you pointed out the picture is not very clear.

However, if you feel it is related to humidity, I'd suggest placing a couple of "silica gel" desiccant packets inside the case.

Desiccants soak up humidity from the surrounding air.

A couple of packets should comfortably last you from 6 months to a year, after which you can either replace them or "recharge" them by heating them up to a very high temperature.
