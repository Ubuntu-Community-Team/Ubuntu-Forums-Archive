### Post by grahammechanical on 2014-06-23
Ice melts and the air coming off of the ice will be carrying water vapour which I would not want getting inside my machine. Keeping the air temperature of the room low is a better solution in my opinion. Do not open the windows. If you do that you may get a nice breeze but over time the temperature inside the room will rise as the hot air outside moves into the cooler room. You could try setting up a fan to blow cool air across the intake vents of the casing. Make sure the vents and grills are free of fluff.

Regards.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2014-06-24
90 Fahrenheit is only 32 Celcius. That's not so bad.

Your computer's CPU fan is probably spinning fast, but if it is keeping the CPU's temperature stable then it is doing its job just fine, and nothing is at risk.

If you can't stand the noise, there are plenty of aftermarket CPU coolers you can buy that are quieter or more effective. Or both. I have a CPU cooler made by Noctua; it's a big heatsink with one of their ultra-quiet fans on top. It does the job nice and quietly, and I can even overclock.

Unfortunately the GPU has its own fan that can get pretty noisy when gaming, especially during summer. If I was to spend more money on this computer, I would buy a passively-cooled graphics card and another couple of Noctua fans for the case.

You can still have a fairly quiet machine with air-cooling. I don't see a need for you to go liquid-cooled, unless you like overclocking your CPU.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2014-06-24
If you don't have a fan or air conditioning, then an ice pack is not a bad idea.  Just use a small towel under it to capture the condensation.  You could also prop the ice pack in front of the intake--typically in front and at the bottom of a tower.  Because of the internal fan and constantly moving air, the vapor won't be a problem.

A small, $8 fan from Walmart will move the air around the room to help.  That should only be a couple of hours of begging at the on-ramp.
