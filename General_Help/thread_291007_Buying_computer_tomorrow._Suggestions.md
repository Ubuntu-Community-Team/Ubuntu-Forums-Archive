### Post by stylishpants on 2006-11-02
I've had exactly the opposite experience with emachines.  Every one I've used has been crap, especially for linux.  They've had a lot of low-quality and non-standard parts in them that are hard to get linux drivers for, and are so cheap they have frequently failed anyway.

Personally I think the best approach is to go to a reputable, small local system builder in your town and tell them what you want.  You get personal attention, and a system built for your needs.  I am NOT talking about going to Best Buy, or -- even worse -- one of the other big stores where the employees are paid on commission.

You pay a little bit extra for getting personalized service.  You get it back again because: 
1. you get a system that has features that make everything more reliable,  like a quality power supply and good ram
2. you get an experienced system builder telling you where best to put your money, so it goes to features you need, instead of getting an off-the-shelf,  one-size-fits-all solution that is in some ways better than you need, and in some ways comes up short.
3. if you ever need service or have a warranty issue, you are dealing with the guy who built your system, not a faceless mail-order company or a know-nothing salesman at a big box store who just wants to get rid of you.

Just my two cents.  I've certainly found a huge difference in my personal experience between the two.

### Post by podunk on 2006-11-02
I'm typing this on a refurb emachine that I got for 299 from Tiger Direct. 1 gig of ram, AMD 64 4200 processor, dual layer DVD, on-board *Nvidia* graphics. It came with an empty drive bay and 2 open PCIe slot (can anyone say SLI?).

It's a nice responsive little machine.

The trouble is - all the good PCIe cards require more power than the power supply has - and it's the wrong amp power to boot. So in order to upgrade my video card I'll need to get a new power supply first. Or of course buy a entry level card.

Just about all the mass produced computers are like that power supply wise. They come with enough juice to run what's on board and not much else.

I can testify that ATI cards are a real PITA to set up. If you read the forums the vast majority of the video problems involve ATI - with on-board ATI being the worst. 

I'm sure Ubuntu will work for the emachines right out of the box, and eventually you'll find a way to get the on-board graphics to work with 3D.

Something to think on though. The emachines have 512 of ram, you plan on buying a card to take the place of the on board ATI, adding a larger harddrive - I'll bet you'll want more RAM. By the time you finish upgrading you'll have spent more money than a more powerful computer would have cost.

### Post by Redeyes_Gambit on 2006-11-02
}{i!

Looking to buy a new computer eh? That's always an exciting business :) . I have been building, repairing and upgrading PCs for some time now and have a few tips gathered from first hand experience if you'll have them :) .

1. Stay away from Compaq (read as Comp-ACK!) and HP. This is just my personal position but it comes from years of tortured existence. Just last night I had to format/re-install windows (and managed to pop in Ubuntu EE) on my sister's computer (guess what, a Compaq.) My family's first "real" computer was a Compaq and we probably paid more than twice what it cost to buy it, just for repairs. Many repairs. Over and over and over again *shudders*. In addition, I fix other people's computers and I have seen Compaqs all too frequently. Hp ranks right next to Compaq in my mind as far as quality or lack there of.

2. If possible try not to buy a mini case. Small cases have less potential for heat dissipation and can wind up cooking your computer. This is what our first Compaq died from besides just a lack of quality.

3. I have found Dell to be of a very high quality, technical support to be very helpful (despite the fact it's in India,) and just an all-around good brand. I personally own three ancient Dells that are still going strong. One is a file server running 24/7 and another is my little brother's PC. The final Dell I own sits in my room, unused only because of its antiquity. If a computer SURVIVES long enough to be incapable of performing the most basic tasks (like surfing the net) it shows something of its caliber and good quality.

4. As for your questions about additional HDDs (hard drives) and graphics cards: Yes, most computers come with room for an additional HDD, if they have only one to begin with. If you are looking for a HDD later, the better brands are Seagate and Western Digital. Don't buy Maxtor. Most of the drives I have had fail were Maxtor. As for Graphics cards Nvidia is recommendable. Better drivers are the main reason Nvidia stands out. I find Nvidia cards are better performers for the dollar too.

And that concludes my suggestions. Hope they helped you!
