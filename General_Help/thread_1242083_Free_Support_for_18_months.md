yup, and that is the idea.

all they do is shut down the update servers for 9.04 18 months after the _4_th month of 200_9_, which is when 9.04 was released. (9.04, 4th month of 2009, get it? :D ) 18 months after April 2009, you will no longer be able to update or install new software without jumping through hoops. this is so that the company doesn't have to spend money to maintain 500 versions of Ubuntu as time progresses, not because anyone is out to get you.

assuming the established pattern continues,
in the 10th month of 2009, 9.10 will come out with 18 months of free updates, support, applications, etc.
in the 4th month of 2010, 10.04 will come out.
then 10.10, 11.04, etc.

you will remain more than welcome and, in fact, highly encouraged, to use a later version of Ubuntu at that point.

If you don't want to deal with upgrading every 1.5 years, look into the latest Long Term Support release.

### Post by slakkie on 2009-08-16
You can see how long is release is supported here:
[http://www.ubuntu.com/products/ubuntu/release-cycle](http://www.ubuntu.com/products/ubuntu/release-cycle)
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Ubuntu_releases](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Ubuntu_releases)

And you can upgrade really easy to a newer release:
[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/UpgradeNotes](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/UpgradeNotes)

And upgrading from an unsupported release is also possible:
[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/EOLUpgrades](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/EOLUpgrades)

So to answer your question, after 18 months _official_ support of your Ubuntu release gets cancelled and you are expected to upgrade. If you don't, there is no man overboard. You can continue to run your OS, however, no security updates will reach you. You can still remove/install packages, as old releases still have their repo's available at [http://old-releases.ubuntu.com](http://old-releases.ubuntu.com).

### Post by CatKiller on 2009-08-17
> **bugsie said:**
> What happens after 18 months? The updates are chargeable?

Of course not. As the Ubuntu website where you found that quote clearly says, Ubuntu will always be free. It's not the freeness that stops after 18 months, it's the support. The Ubuntu devs can be supporting up to four different releases at once, as well as working on the next one. Back-porting security patches isn't trivial, so there's only so much that they can do. Normal releases are supported for 18 months and LTS releases are supported for three years.
