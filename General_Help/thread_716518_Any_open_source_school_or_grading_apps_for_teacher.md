### Post by randomnumber on 2008-03-06
Our college uses a commercial program called elearning. I disliked it and it seemed to take a year before it worked somewhat well. It was a commercial product. ([http://blackboard.com/us/index](http://blackboard.com/us/index))

There were people pushing to us the opensource equivalent which I believe to be .LRN ([http://dotlrn.org/](http://dotlrn.org/)). It seems that people in charge can not understand how free opensource software can be better than software you have to spend money on. 

Basically with the first you have little control. Others set the thing up for you at great cost or the second which is something you control and set up. The cost for the second is that you need people to set it up who are more knowledgeable then the first. It is my belief that the second is the better choice in the long run. You get controll and you do not have ever increasing cost as you become more reliant on the commercial support to fix everything.

Please keep in mind that these are merely my observations, investigate for your self.

### Post by randomnumber on 2008-03-07
I also only used blackboard as a student.

I asked my professors why the university choose to pay for the blackboard software instead of the stuff that was freely available. I mostly got a response that the professors suggested the open source approach after a committee was put together and the committee suggested open source but the administration decided against the committee's suggestion. Apparently it was a sore subject, because the professors seemed angry and did not want to talk about it. I would be too if I was put on the committee to researched the options only be ignored afterwards. 

Good luck getting people to try Linux. I would suggest getting them use to using Software in Linux first and then trying to move them to the OS if you have time. For example, get them to try OpenOffice and Mozilla Apps. first and as they see value in them suggest that they might look into crossing over entirely or dual booting etc.

I suspect unless the people you are trying to get to go open source are really interested and on board you will, at best, get them to support a trying the open source way while installing and running the commercial way. It takes time for people to adjust and to understand new things. 

Again good luck.
