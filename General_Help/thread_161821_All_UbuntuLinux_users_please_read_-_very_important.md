### Post by jlhughes on 2006-04-17
In the early 1990s I was all gungho for OS/2 2.0 and then OS/2 Warp.  OS/2 was miles ahead of Windows at the time. (Microsoft and IBM developed OS/2 but Microsoft split off and used its code to develop NT.)

In any event, the problem then (and I assume still today) was that OS/2's selection of native software was so dismal that you couldn't sell anyone on why they should switch. OS/2 offered Windows emulators, but that's no substitue. You might as well run Windows.

I bring this all up because the Ubuntu (Kubuntu, Edbuntu) concept -- a free desktop package of Linux and Open-Source productivity software that all fits on a single CD-ROM -- has everything necessary to make excellent in-roads into the mass market.  Not only is the operating system robust and naturally safe from the most annoying of viruses and malware, but the Open-Source software goes toe-to-toe with any commercial package. Why pay when you can get the work done for free?

I'm new to Ubuntu. I went out and purchased a low-end XP machine (less than $300) and created a dual-boot Ubuntu box in less than an hour.  You can search the forums to see the minor problems I've encountered.

This is fantastic.

But -- and this is a big but -- I'm not your average Joe.  I have six computers in my home network, counting the XP-Ubuntu box as one. I have a fiber-optic Internet connection to my home.  I can download software at speeds that max out the servers. 

Now, imagine that you are not me. You are someone who doesn't have broadband at home. Imagine downloading a 620MB Ubunutu Live CD over a dial-up modem.  Yes, you can get the CDs free -- assuming you know about the offer.  But what about upgrades from Breezy to Dapper or even incremental fixes?

My point is that the world is changing, and quickly. What we can do today, we didn't even imagine 10 years ago. (The OS/2 2.1 operating system came on something like 32 floppy disks. It wasn't until OS/2 Warp that you could get it on a CD-ROM.) 

What's needed is imagination fortified with persistence -- and a sense of community.

It's not that Microsoft is evil or Apple is greedy, but their business models by necessity create walls that keep people out. 

Ubuntu can be an answer. It can be the people's software. In time.

Patience, grasshopper.
