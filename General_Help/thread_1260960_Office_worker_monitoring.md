### Post by Grenage on 2009-09-08
Oh it's quite legal in France, especially with cause:

> France has yet to enact specific legislation on employee monitoring, so general labour, civil, and criminal provisions, as well as the French DP Act, apply.

In October 2005, the CNIL adopted guidance to help employers solve some practical issues related to the detection of employees&#8217; activities at the workplace.  Any employee monitoring in France must take into due consideration the transparency and proportionality of the monitoring and must be performed in compliance with collective bargaining agreements.  Moreover, consultation with the Works Council is an indispensable condition for employee monitoring.  According to Article L. 432-2-1 of the Labour Code, &#8220;the Works Committee must be informed and consulted prior to any significant introduction of new technologies, when the technologies are likely to affect &#8230; the employees&#8217; working conditions&#8212;especially when the decisions concern means and technology allowing the control of the employees&#8217; activities.&#8221;  To safeguard appropriately its legitimacy, monitoring should be mentioned in technology use policies, or internal rules (règlement d&#8217;ordre intérieur).  The establishment of these rules is subject to consultation with Works Councils and employees.

In accordance with the DP Act, employers must also register their monitoring of employee Internet use with the CNIL.  There is an exception to the obligation to register where the employer appoints a &#8220;correspondant informatique et libertés,&#8221; an internal data protection officer (&#8220;DPO&#8221;).  However, transfers of data outside the EU and, thus, any monitoring involving transfers to the U.S., are subject to authorisation and must always be registered with the CNIL, irrespective of the appointment of a DPO.  Additionally, the stored data generally cannot be retained for more than six months.

In the leading Cour de Cassation (the French Supreme Court) case, Nikon, the Court stated that &#8220;the employee has the right, even during working hours and at his workplace to the respect of his privacy; this includes in particular the confidentiality of his correspondence; the employer cannot, without infringing this fundamental liberty, examine the personal messages sent or received by the employee on a computer tool placed at his disposal for work, and this even in the case of the employer having prohibited a non-professional use of the computer.&#8221;

In summary, employee e-mail monitoring is generally lawful in France if it fulfils the following conditions:

    * Reasonableness.  Monitoring must be &#8220;reasonable,&#8221; meaning that the employee&#8217;s fundamental rights and freedoms must be balanced against the need to protect the employer&#8217;s interests;
    * Personal Communications.  Particular caution should be exercised with respect to an e-mail that is marked &#8220;private&#8221; or &#8220;personal&#8221;;
    * Legal Basis.  There must be a legal basis;
    * Notice.  The employer must inform the employee about the fact that it stores communications on its servers, retention periods, etc., and the conditions under which the employee may access the stored content;
    * Consultation with the Works Council.  Employers must consult with the Works Council prior to monitoring;
    * Registration with the CNIL.  If the employer monitors communications on a global basis (without identifying individual users), no specific notification of the monitoring is required.  However, if the monitoring identifies users, it has to be registered with the CNIL;

Unlawful monitoring may subject employers to civil and criminal sanctions.  In particular, unlawful interception of employee communications may constitute &#8220;breach of the confidentiality of personal correspondence,&#8221; and may result in imprisonment of up to one year and fines of &#8364;35,000 (approximately US$51,000).  Also, if a court decides that the monitoring were indeed unlawful, the employer cannot base any action, such as dismissal, on evidence obtained unlawfully.

The company I used to work at monitored all the employees, or I should say at least "could" monitor any employee when/if they wanted. It was windows XP and they installed a remote desktop viewer that automatically started up and you could not shut down. At any time, the boss or anyone in MIS could view your screen and you would never know. If you had a problem and called MIS, your mouse would suddenly start moving and the MIS guy would click the buttons and you could watch him. (obviously he could see your screen).

Set it up like this:
- Allow other users to view your desktop
- do *not* require confirm each access to this machine
- Never display an icon

Then you can just sit in your office and watch what s/he does all day long.... That's what my old boss probably did.

The only thing would be to lock those settings so the secretary could not change them. Maybe figure out where those settings get stored and then change their owner to root or something would do the trick.
