### Post by LordIshamael on 2009-12-07
1) Not all people believe that. Some people believe that information and resources, especially software, should always be free. This extends to not only using software for free but also modifying it to suit our own needs. 
See [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_source](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_source)
While not exactly the same thing, open source philosophy shares much with keeping information free.

2) No personal information like credit card or bank account information is given at any point if you are downloading Linux without buying it, especially Ubuntu. If you are buying a CD or something like that, then it is a different matter. I have no experience in that, but I have every confidence that it will be as safe and respectful of privacy as any online purchasing is.

3) It is certainly true that Linux, and Unix in general, has a more stable and virus-resistant structure than Windows. Linux is also used in many non-PC computers, such as in Apache, in supercomputers, or in many smaller computers used in everyday electronics. It is true that if Linux had more of the PC market, there would be more people trying to hack it, and by logical extension, some of them would be successful. While it is debatable exactly how virus-proof it is, Linux certainly does not have as many vulnerabilities - not just viruses but openings for viruses - as Windows.

4) It is certainly quite stable. I have never experienced a case where I lost actual data to Linux. Even in the case that the operating system had a problem - extremely rare, for me at least - the data is still secure, and in fact easily accessible through a live CD.

5) Like Dark Stang said, it is mostly donations and corporate backing. Canonical is the main sponsor for Ubuntu.

In short, you would be amazed at what a community of dedicated, selfless people looking out for quality instead of financial gain can achieve.

### Post by 3Miro on 2009-12-07
> **freedom_p said:**
> Today is big day for me, after doing lot of research I came to know that Ubuntu is the best Operating System and its FREE!!!!!!!!!!!!!.....

I have couple of questions in my mind before switching, I am sorry if I am asking any wrong questions.

1.) Nothing is free on this Earth, but how Ubuntu such a great OS is for free? What's the catch?
2.) By giving free OS, Are they collecting personal information like bank accounts, credit cards..etc?? Worried about privacy...
3.) By saying Virus free, does it mean Ubuntu cannot be hacked or people don't use like Windows that's the reason hackers don't pay attention to Ubuntu?
4.) Is it a stable OS, Data is safe on Ubuntu?
5.) How Ubuntu is surviving by giving it free?

1-2) Those are related to the Free Software philosophy (Google: GNU project and Free Software). The business model is well ... different. THEY are not doing anything and have nothing to do with Linux or Ubuntu, WE own the system. There are several corporations and foundations that deal with Linux, however, none of them "owns" it so that they can "give it for free". People own the thing, that is all the people that contribute to the project. When people make a program for Linux or any of the many different versions of Linux, then people make a program so that they themselves can use. Then people give the program for free because: a) it doesn't cost them, they did not do this for money in the first place, so they are not losing anything; b) if someone likes the program, then they can improve it and give the improved version for free, thus everyone will have a better program. Also, it is virtually impossible to put "spy-ware" or any kind of "mal-ware" into an open source program. Linux tells you exactly how it works, there is nothing "hidden" in the system, so there is no way it can "steal your credit card". More on the subject is available on the GNU project web-page.

NOTE: there are non-free things in Ubuntu such as the Adobe Flash player, nothing knows what is in it. When you install Ubuntu, you get only "free" and "safe" stuff at first. Then you can chose to install some of the non-free things if you want.

3) Linux can be hacked, nothing is 100% secure, however, when someone find a security hole in the system, they fix the system as opposed to install a program that watches if a "virus" gets in. If you have a hole in the wall of your house, you will fix the hole and not hire a guard to sit and watch someone gets through. Actually, most of the world servers work on Linux, if you hack into someone's windows machine, you can get their credit card, if you hack into the server of a bank, you can get 100 credit cards. There is more intensive to hack Linux than there is to hack Windows, people hack windows only because it is so easy.

4) As I mentioned before, Linux is extremely stable. Most of the large servers work with Linux including the YouTube servers. Ubuntu is just the version of Linux that is most geared towards desktop computers. Also, 90% of the top 500 supercomputers in the world use Linux, and those are machines that see extreme torture 24/7. I have a Linux desktop box that I forced to do scientific computations for me 24/7 for the past 2 weeks, it never crashed or even flinched. 

5) The operating system is free, people that make it donate their work so it is really very low cost. Then companies make money by selling support, i.e. you pay a price and then you can call people on the phone and yell at them if something isn't working. Otherwise, if you do not pay anything, you get many people on the forum willing to help, but no one will put up with a "yeller".
