### Post by Twitster on 2008-09-01
The best security book I've ever read was "Hacking: The Art Of Exploitation", the second edition. I'd really recommend looking up the basics of C and Intel Linux assembly and how to use GDB. The rest teaches itself. Obviously, the better the grounding you have in C, ASM, etc, before you read the book the faster you will understand it, but I can say that although I had a very basic knowledge of each, now I can write shellcode quite competantly and have made some vulnerable programs dance :). 
This edition of the book covers programming, networking, exploitation, shellcode, cryptography, and a few other large-scope sections. It's a must!

### Post by mb_webguy on 2008-09-12
The best and safest way to start hacking is to try hacking your own system, and then securing that system against any vulnerabilities you discover.  Then try it again.  Then set up a different system (e.g. Windows instead of Linux, or an ftp server instead of a web server, etc.) and try hacking that one.  But BEFORE you start hacking other systems, you need to educate yourself..  

You of course need to have a good working knowledge of programming (and not just the syntax of the language, but how to construct and trace an algorithm) and networks (and not just security, but protocols, hardware, and the common client and server applications).  

But the most important things to know are the legal and ethical issues involved in hacking, particularly those pertaining to privacy.  Even if you have permission to attempt to hack into someone's system, you can still get into trouble by accessing certain information, divulging certain information, or allowing others to do so.

I used to do a bit of white hat hacking.  When I first started, I was... well, a bit stupid about it.  I hacked systems without permission with the intention of informing the administrators of the systems of any vulnerabilities I found, and thought that as long as I did so with no malicious intent and didn't do anything to harm those systems that there was no problem.  I rather quickly discovered that I was wrong.  System administrators tend to react rather harshly when they find an intruder in their systems.  Fortunately, I avoided getting into serious trouble, but only because the head systems analyst for a particular system knew me well enough to know that I hadn't meant any harm, and convinced the sysadmin not to take legal action against me.  Needless to say, I decided that I'd better educate myself a bit more about the ethical and legal aspects of hacking before I tried poking around any other systems.
