### Post by DannyBiker on 2010-11-11
Hey,

For the past two years, I've been switching between Seven and Ubuntu every 3-4 months for various reasons. I've been using 7 for the last 6 months as I needed some pro tools unavailable on Linux. Now that I only use my old desktop computer to browse the net, type texts, watch videos and listen to music, I decided to switch permanently to Ubuntu. Until I remembered about my ATI Xpress 200...

Since I've been using Ubuntu (around 7.04 or 7.10), things have been going downhill with that integrated graphic card. At first, with ATI support, everything went fine. Then, Ati dropped the card's support and I was stuck with the open drivers. I was fine with it; performance were the same as I never used Ubuntu for more than what I mentioned before. I don't even play with Compiz that much : that cube is funny for a while but you get bored of it.

The problem is that since 2009, things get worse every release. I get major glitches sometimes forcing me to reboot. There is nothing wrong with the computer, the session is still fully operational but how am I suppose to use my desktop if the top navigation bars of all the programs just disappear ? Yeah, I know : console, etc. But seriously, it is embarrassing to have to open a console to resolve an issue that occurred when trying to open Firefox or Shotwell. I mean, I'm not working on a multi-layered Gimp project of a 7600x5897 resolution ! I'm just asking the browser to open...

Bug reports have been filled and are still pending. For a year and a half at least.

So my question is : is Ubuntu still worth my download ? I'm starting to doubt about it. It's like Ubuntu is trying to build a Ferrari not wondering if all the wheels are actually round. It's the basics. 
One of the main Linux's arguments against Windows/Apple is to offer an OS that works from the get go. No need for drivers, programs, codecs, etc. You turn it on, it's there. Well, not for me anymore...
I know my computer is old and I know my problem is linked to Ati dropping the support of my card. But having to reboot after trying to open Shotwell is indecent in (almost) 2011.

### Post by The Cog on 2010-11-11
I know what you mean. It's not entirely the fault of Ubuntu / Linux though. ATI have chosen to remove support for old cards in their recent drivers. That's ATI that chose to do that, not Linux.

The problem is that there doesn't seem to be an easy way to retain old outdated drivers from the manufacturers - the recent releases always use recent drivers. In windows, you can generally download old outdated drivers for old outdated hardware.

The proper solution is to have proper open-source drivers 
of course. That way, the drivers will always be available. But ATI choose not to allow that to happen.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2010-11-11
What makes the difference in Win7 is two things:
1) AMD/ATI did continue to provide some support for Legacy chipsets into the Catalyst 10.x timeframe
2) MS has seriously enhanced the capability to install Vista-era and XP-era drivers in Compatibility Mode.  

I have a really old ATI 9800 chipset machine and it STILL works OK under Win7 using the compatibility mode.

Unfortunately, Ubuntu has no such mode for using the older fglrx drivers with the newer X-servers in the current Ubuntu distros.  So, you're stuck using the open-source drivers.
