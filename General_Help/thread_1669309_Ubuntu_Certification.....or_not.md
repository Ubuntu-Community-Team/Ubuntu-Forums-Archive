### Post by srs5694 on 2011-01-17
My understanding is that the Ubuntu certification is just the LPIC-1 certification with one additional exam tacked on. I'm the author of [LPIC-1 Study Guide](http://www.amazon.com/LPIC-1-Linux-Professional-Institute-Certification/dp/0470404833/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&ie=UTF8&qid=1295294630&sr=1-1) (and of course I've taken the LPIC-1 exams), but I've not taken the Ubuntu add-on exam, so I don't know what it contains. The LPIC-1 certification is theoretically distribution-agnostic and it's definitely centered on the command line rather than GUI administration tools.

Don't get hung up on one distribution, particularly for a career path. Ubuntu may seem big and important today, but in five or ten years, it could be a faded memory. Learn at least enough to get by in Red Hat/Fedora, OpenSUSE, Debian, and perhaps others, and promote yourself as a Linux expert, not just an Ubuntu expert. (Saying you know Ubuntu, of course, will probably do no harm; just don't emphasize that exclusively.) That will better prepare you to deal with the inevitable shifts as distributions change. In fact, similar comments could apply to other Unix-like OSes -- you should expose yourself to FreeBSD, OpenSolaris (while it's still around), and anything else you can get your hands on. (Some schools may have commercial Unix installations, too, but these are often too expensive for individuals to use at home, with the exception of Apple's OS X.) Even non-Unix OSes can be worth knowing. I hear a company in Redmond has something that's pretty popular. ;) Computer technology changes rapidly, so you want to have a foot in the door in case something takes off rapidly.

### Post by srs5694 on 2011-01-18
Slackware is a much more "old-school" distribution than most others -- it has little in the way of flashy GUI configuration tools, it uses tarballs for binary package distribution, and it's less likely to adopt complex new tools in general. In some ways, you can get a similar experience from Debian or Gentoo, which also favor command-line configuration, although they're more modern than Slackware in some areas. Even flashier GUI-oriented distributions like Ubuntu provide the same basic command-line features, so you can learn how to do things like add users or edit partition tables with text-mode tools with any distribution -- it's just that with Ubuntu (or Fedora or OpenSUSE or whatever), you're likely to find the GUI tools in the menus, use them, and not think to look any further. To some extent this is true even of Slackware if you run KDE, GNOME, Xfce, or another major desktop environment, since they tend to provide links to the GUI tools on any distribution -- but of course if they don't exist, you can't find links to them!

If nothing else, it's worth installing Slackware and fiddling with it so that you can see where Linux came from. Slackware is the oldest of the major distributions, and it still does a lot of things the way they were commonly done fifteen years ago.
