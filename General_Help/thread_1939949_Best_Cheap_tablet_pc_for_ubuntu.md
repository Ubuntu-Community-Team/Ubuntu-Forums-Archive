### Post by Mark Phelps on 2012-03-13
You need to tell us what you mean by "Tablet PC" -- that's a rather vague term.

For example, the traditional Tablet PCs came with rotatable screens that could be used as writing surfaces, using a stylus.  Most of those were made by Fujitsu (I have one of them), but changed to the Linux kernel since Ubuntu 8.10 made getting special keys recognized a LOT harder -- to the degree I gave up using Ubuntu on it.

Current Tablet PCs from Fujitsu are quite expensive -- $1200 USD and UP.

Tablets, in contrast, are typically Android OS based, have no local hard drives, and use the "cloud" for all their storage needs.

You really need to provide us a list of specifications, if you want folks to make any useful recommendations.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2012-03-13
> I was wondering If anyone knows which tablet P.C. Works best with ubuntu and not so pricey as 200$.

Simple answer -- NONE.

I had the fan go out in my six-year-old, 1st gen, Fujitsu Tablet PC so I looked around for a replacement.  Cheapest price I could find was $800 USD -- and that's for a six-year old box (Fujitsu only made them for one year), that came with XP on it!

Bit the bullet, ordered a replacement fan for $30 (USD), completely disassembled the Tablet PC, replaced the fan, reassembled it twice (would not boot the first time), and now I have a quiet, working Tablet PC.

Tablet PCs come in a variety of styles, some have touch screens (which don't support a stylus), some have stylus-only touch screens (like mine), some have BOTH.  ALL are expensive.  The entry-level ones are at least $1000. The ones that have both screen features are nearly $2000.  Used ones are very expensive because they were not made in huge quantities.

You can get used, low-end Netbooks for around what you indicate, but personally, I wouldn't have one.  They're underpowered, come with a crippled version of Windows, and are NOT expandable.

The other factor, which is really important, is that Tablet PCs, given their unique features, are even worse than generic laptops -- in that they have VERY specialized hardware, which require very specialized drivers -- which are often NOT available in Linux.

So, even if you multiplied your price by 10-fold, you could still end up with a very expensive Tablet PC, for which key hardware components simply would not work!
