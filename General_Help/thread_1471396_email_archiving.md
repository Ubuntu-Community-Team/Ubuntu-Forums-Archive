### Post by MechaMechanism on 2010-05-03
What I did for my mother was to sign her up for Gmail and enable POP3.  In Gmail I set the settings to archive the email when they were downloaded.  So my mom opens Thunderbird and downloads her email and in Google the email is archived into the all mail folder.  This way you have an archive locally and a backup in Gmail.

Don't like using Gmail?  Then if you are willing to use Evolution, there is a setting in file menu called backup.  Another method is to simply make a copy of the Thunderbird profile.

### Post by frappe1 on 2010-05-03
I also use Thunderbird, and what I do is fairly simple. Any mail that is destined for the archive gets moved to any one of several subfolders of an archive folder. This gets it out of the most used folders so it isn't wasting screen space or (with several thousand others) slowing down reindexing, search, and such.

The archived mail is immediately accessible and searchable, and always gets backed up with the rest of my mail. I am a heavy user, and do a lot more email file transfer than most people. Over the past few years, despite my vast overuse of email, my ability to store it, compress it, back it up, and sync it over my WLAN to my laptop has been growing faster than the underlying mail those operations are performed upon.

Disk space is cheap. I can't see any tangible benefit to permanently removing the archived mail from the client itself. Also, remote archives can be lost, compromised, or used for aggressive marketing (if you use a third-party webmail).

Forget IMAP for archiving. If IMAP is convenient for some tasks, then use it for those, however any mail worthy of archiving really needs to be in your possession, on your system, and backed up on your media. If you have to use remote backup, use a content-neutral storage service. That doesn't cause privacy problems, nor do such services get too smart for their own good, purge old mail, and call it a feature.
