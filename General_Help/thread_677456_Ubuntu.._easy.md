### Post by Zeroangel on 2008-01-24
Linux, any distro of linux is far from perfect thanks to lack of support from the people who actually make the hardware. In about 50% of the cases, Ubuntu will have no problems with your hardware and things will just work, in that other 50% of the times something will not work perfectly, be it a troublesome graphics driver, touchpad or wireless support for certain types of 802.11g cards.

Linux was never meant to be used for non-geeks, with the latest releases of beginner friendly distros like SUSE, Mandriva, Ubuntu, PClinuxOS, Mepis, etc they are *starting* to become non-geek friendly. But if the OS doesnt like your hardware you are gonna have problems with editing system files and the like. And in lots of cases it is the sad state of affairs that the hardware makers do not give Linux compatability a thought and it is up to the open-source community to cobble together drivers (which do not always work perfectly).

I would *never* recommend that a non-geek install Ubuntu on their own. I will do Ubuntu installs for people I know, because I know how to get things working in most cases. It is the install and initial making things work that is the hard part about Linux, once that is all done its usually easy going from there.

### Post by mcduck on 2008-01-25
You have to remember that easy can be defined in many ways. "Easy to use" isn't always the same as "easy to learn".

Also, your problems seem to be hardware-related. People have countless amount of different configurations, and for those with hardware that is well supported in Linux Ubuntu really does "just work". I haven't needed to configure a single thing on either my desktop or laptop machines.

Finally, you don't really need to use the terminal for most of the things. It's just easier to give instructions in the forums using terminal commands than writing a step-by-step guide through graphical dialogs.

### Post by Zeroangel on 2008-01-25
Exactly! And good answer!

My analogy is that if wading through menus is like walking somewhere, then the terminal is like muttering a magic phrase to teleport there. For linux wizards, its simply easier and faster to use the terminal. And a lot of people disregard GUI ways of doing things since the GUI is so slow and clumsy for them.

The thing about certain hardware configurations not working well for ubuntu can certainly be applied to ANY linux distro. I can recall using Mandrake 10.1 and while it was rock solid on my home machine, my work machine HATED mandriva and would act flakily because the hardware config didnt mesh well with the distro itself. Same problem with lots of hardware -- broadcom, ati, sis, synaptic. All of these companies are terrible at writing up-to-date linux drivers for their hardware (but props to ATI for actually trying!) and that is why many people *have* to resort to hacking their machines to get them working perfectly. Linux isnt for everyone, nor is it 'desktop ready' in the sense that its as easy to set up as Windows (and even windows has hardware problems!). Try getting your grandparents to set up Windows+drivers+antivirus. That is not easy for them either. Using it once its set up is a different story.
