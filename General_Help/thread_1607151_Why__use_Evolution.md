### Post by gradysghost on 2010-10-27
Yes, you could put them all together using an email client.  Evolution is great for checking your work email if it's on an Exchange server, but Thunderbird works just as well for non-Exchange-based email accounts.

I suggest at least trying these.  When you set up POP3 accounts, make sure to check whatever options exist to leave a copy of the email on the server when you get new email, that way you can switch between programs without losing any email data.  If you try them and don't find it useful (I haven't), you can just stop using them.

One other note, though: Gmail uses some off-the-beaten organization techniques.  Gmail's "labels" are really folders, and when you use IMAP to sync your account, you kinda lose the "Archive" feature, which I make heavy use of.  To use the "Archive" feature from a client like these, you have to manually move the email to the "All Mail" folder.  Gmail can be kind of confusing in a standard POP/IMAP client (and I do suggest using IMAP where possible).  Their web interface is great to match their server's feature set.

### Post by sgosnell on 2010-10-27
There are several ways to solve your problem.  You can have a gmail account go and get mail from all you other accounts, or have the other accounts forwarded to the gmail account.  I sort of use belt and suspenders, by having everything consolidated on one gmail account, plus I use Evolution.  I don't really depend on Evolution, but I use it for the contacts and calendar, so it's no extra work to use the email.  I have Evolution display my google calendar, and I have a separate addressbook in Evolution for holding my Google contacts, in addition to my regular contacts.  For me, Evolution's value is in keeping track of my contacts and calendar, more than for email, because I still use the web interface for that more than I use Evolution.  Every now and then, though, I need access to an email when I can't get online, and having them stored on the computer allows that.
