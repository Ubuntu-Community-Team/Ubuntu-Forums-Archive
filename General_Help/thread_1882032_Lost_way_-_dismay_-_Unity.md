### Post by markling on 2011-11-16
I have high hopes for this command for restoring the old desktop environment in Ubuntu, which I just found in a sticky thread in General Help:

sudo apt-get install gnome-session-fallback

Has anyone at ever considered that backwards compatibility should concern not only applications but users?

I had Ubuntu running perfectly without having to concern myself with technical matters beyond my ken. I was able to work efficiently. Everything had its place. The O/S was stable. I could rely on it. I recommended it to other people. I helped them install it (though hardware compatibility was more often than not a problem).

But the 11.10 upgrade has been a massive disruption. I can't find applications using the search mechanism. I can't find features and menus that used to be easy to hand. Gedit, my preferred word processing device, has become unstable. My graphics corrupt after intense use of simple email/multiple-tab browsing/WP editing. Fonts corrupt after switching between users. My machine seems to be running much less efficiently. The Unity bar in the left margin is erratic and frustrating. Menus are, unintuitively and unhelpfully, no longer attributes of individual windows. This is a careless disruption of the aeons-old working principle that a single window is a unit of work or function, and that the desktop is a space upon which numerous functions can be orchestrated at once: the Ubuntu desktop now assumes the identity of whichever function you pick up, as one might expect things to look when working on a woodworking bench while wearing binoculars for glasses. For a conductor to lead an orchestra, he has to see all the parts. Ubuntu used to give excellent control over my workspace. Now open windows persistently maximise, despite being repeatedly minimised. It's hard to switch between open windows now the options for doing so are limited. It is also impossible to have a current and ongoing awareness of a work-session since it is all hidden: I no longer feel in control of my work-station. Ubuntu is trying to turn me into a simpleton. I don't need the encouragement, thank you. It's like being streamed in maths at school and being permitted to learn only as much as you would need to reach grade C. Its like being asked to work with mittens and a safety hat and goggles in case you fall off your office chair. Ubuntu's excellent handling of multiple workspaces has been broken. I used to be able to open an instance of Gedit or Nautilus in one workspace and other instances in other workspaces - and that is where they would remain. It was truly like having four workbenches with four distinct tasks occurring simultaneously. Ubuntu now treats it as a single space. If I want to create a new instance of nautilus, say, in another workspace, I have to i) go back to the workspace where I was last using Nautilus ii) open another instance of Nautilus in that workspace iii) then move that instance to the workspace where I wanted it to be in the first place iv) try and overcome my frustration and get back to work. When I try to get back to work I find that Ubuntu has maximised one of the windows again. So all I can see, either in menus or on the screen in front of me, is that one window Ubuntu has decided to maximise while I was in the other workspace. So I minimise the window, and then its onto step vi) Ah yes, now where was I? Yes, that's step six: trying to hold it all together because Unity has unified nothing - it has scattered it all. The errors and omissions are numerous and a constant source of infuriatingmentness. That's a new word to describe the level of frustration caused by such a complex array of hindrances, irritations and disappointments. Gedit also now treats docs opened from its own 'recent docs' shortcut differently from those opened in Nautilus. I can no longer find a general purpose 'recent docs' menu. Imagine spreading your working papers all over your desk to see the breadth of your current task and then finding that every time you turn away and look back they have all been put away bar one that hovers instantly right up under your nose? And that someone's taken your drawers and stuffed your desk tidy somewhere you can't find it.

I'm going to try restoring the old desktop. It was some relief to come across this option. I was about to wipe everything and reinstall the last version. I was facing the undesirable prospect of switching to another Linux distribution because I figured that Canonical would eventually ditch 10.10.

After all this, if anyone has stuck with me, I would like to propose that Canonical might, from the perspective of a mere plebeian user like myself, best concentrate its resources for the next release on bug-fixing, on making things hang together well, on filling in the gaps, on hardware incompatibility. By focusing, in other words, on the sort of unity that really matters in a system: harmony. And by focusing on one of its great influences: stability. And by restraining this apparent tendency to promote monotheism by incarnating it as a desktop golem. By only introducing ill-thought through and poorly implemented environments as <options>. By thinking of the backwards compatibility of users.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2011-11-16
The "new" Unity desktop, especially since they removed capabilities that were there in 11.04, is proving to be frustrating for a lot of folks.  Add this to the continued lack of any capability to do a version roll-back in Ubuntu, and you end up with lots of folks as unhappy as you.

Now, before all the Unity "fans" jump in and start proclaiming how wonderful it is ... this is NOT a Unity-bashing response.  So, all of you can calm down.

To better prepare yourself for this kind of change next time, consider installing Clonezilla and doing an image backup of your Ubuntu install to an external drive. That will give you the ability to do a "roll-back" in only  a few minutes by restoring your previously WORKING version of Ubuntu.
