### Post by 1clue on 2011-03-23
I've only ever bought one PC-based computer fully assembled.  It had pre-installed Linux.

I've bought Macs, and one Sun box.

There's no way I will buy a pre-built PC anytime soon.  The junk they put in for peripherals sucks.  Here's what I recommend:
[LIST=1]
[*]Decide on a CPU that works for you.
[*]Find a motherboard that you like.  Include as many adequate features as you can find, but don't sweat it if some things aren't good enough.  If you can find a board which just doesn't have any of the bad stuff (video, sound) then choose that.
[*]For every thing that is missing or inadequate on the motherboard, find something that works for you with slots that match on the motherboard.  (graphics, sound, network, disk interface, etc)
[*]Find enough of the proper RAM.  Prefer only fill half the available slots so you can expand later.
[*]Find disk(s) to meet your needs.
[*]For every additional piece of equipment you want now or later, make sure there is enough room for it on the card bus, in the case and in power supply size.  Keep in mind that some cards like video may take up two slots.
[*]Add up power consumption for everything, multiply by 2 and get a power supply that size.  Make sure it has the proper connector for your motherboard and your video card.
[*]Find a case you like, this could be your existing case.  Don't use the old power supply.  Make sure the board's form factor is the same as the case, and if you have a hotter running processor or you mean to overclock then make sure there are lots of fans.
[*]Get a container of screws, offsets, etc that you can find in any computer store.  Make sure that EVERY screw connecting the motherboard to the case has a spacer on it or you'll short the board against the case.
[*]Get a surge suppressor or battery backup which costs roughly 10% of the cost of everything you will attach to it.  Figure in any expansion you intend to do later.  Don't use the one from your old computer.
[/LIST]

### Post by 1clue on 2011-03-23
@cascade9,

6 - Power consumption *2:  The only component I've ever actually had go bad spontaneously (without power line events or dropped-down-the-steps events) is a power supply.  I will gladly sacrifice some performance to get a reliable supply that doesn't melt when I hit high loads.

7 - Fans:  Up to you, but I prefer low-noise boxes.  Better to have localized fans inside for the things that need it, and route air to and from those fans.  Not everyone has the patience for that, and again we're looking at a very modest computer here.  A tiny fan spinning extra fast is going to be either really noisy or really expensive.  At one point I had a box with a tiny high speed fan that actually whistled.  Never again.  My current box has 5 fans in it, a washable air filter and you can barely hear it if there's no other noise in the room.  Any noise source, you can't hear it at all.  That's the one in my signature.

8 - screws and standoffs:  Agreed.  If you need extra standoffs then replace everything that came with the board.

9 - Cost-based surge/batteries:  If you know what you're doing then you aren't the person who started this thread and you can use some other scheme.  The 10% rule makes a lot of sense and hopefully gets people to actually buy something.  If they've already bought something then they have experience and can adjust accordingly.

FWIW if you intend to use a high performance filesystem then you pretty much need a UPS or stand to risk severe data loss if your power goes out.

Re:  Batteries.  Lead-acid batteries are extremely green in real world practice, far more recyclable than any LiPo or other new chemistry.  If a battery actually makes it to the **battery** recycling bin then 100% of the battery is recycled and used to make new batteries.  Unlike many recycling modes, a new battery can be made of 100% recycled material with no loss of performance or lifespan.  I'm talking plastic, metals, acid, the works.  Maybe not the stickers with print on them.  Recycling lead-acid batteries is far more cost-effective and far more environmentally friendly than making new materials, and the pipeline for recycling them is very well established in the USA.

The only place where batteries are not recycled is when it's hidden in some other device and thrown in the landfill, or if somebody drops it and acid splatters all over, or if it gets lost some other way.

I have been AMD only since around 2002.  It is common knowledge on tech discussion boards that AMD is still the price/performance leader.  Plus, from a FOSS perspective, supporting the "underdog" CPU maker is a Good Thing.  You don't want an OS monopoly (Windows/MS), so why would you want to support a CPU monopoly?  If AMD doesn't continue to be supported, we will have no real CPU choice.

Plus, Intel's history with CPU serial numbers (user tracking), and now integrated DRM in Sandy Bridge, gives even more reasons to stick with the alternative AMD.

Technically, AMD's handling of CPU socket architecture has been more PC builder/consumer friendly, too.  With a wide berth of backward/forward compatibility among mobo's, sockets and CPU's, AMD is a no brainer.  Intel has changed sockets more often than Michigan weather ;).  This means you're stuck with no upgrade path with Intel hardware the past several years.

### Post by newbuntuxx on 2011-03-26
First rule about buying computers: You don't buy a PC from walmart!
Second rule about buying computers: YOU DON'T BUY A PC FROM WALMART!
(That was such a great movie. I'll have to watch it again :) )

Either build your own system, or go to someone who can build you a system, or drop by your local computer shop and tell them the specs that you want and they will recommend the parts and build the system for you.

The best time to learn about the new available hardware is when you are upgrading or buying a new system. And every computer user should know what their hardware is running.

Things that most users ignore when buying computers:
- Does the motherboard support future upgrades to the CPU/Memory/Graphics card?
- 32 bit or 64 bit CPU (go with 64 bit)
- Size of L2 CPU cache (the bigger the better)
- Motherboard FSB frequency (667 Mhz, 800 Mhz, 1066 Mhz, 1333 Mhz, the faster the better)
- RAM Frequency (667 Mhz, 800 Mhz, 1066 Mhz, 1333 Mhz the faster the better)
- HDD: rotating disc, or Solid state? (Go with solid state). Don't buy the huge solid state drives. 32 GB is enough. Use the SSD to install root on it and swap, and use the regular HDD for home directory and backup.

My two cents.

I don't even know how many systems I've put together.

But don't include "cost effective" in the mix of reasons to build.  It's been over 10 years since I could claim that my built boxes were cheaper than what was commonly available.  Here's my benefit list:
[LIST=1]
[*]You get EXACTLY the hardware you want.
[*]You don't have to take junk out of the box that isn't worth having.
[*]You don't pay for a Windows license that you don't want or need and would be crippled anyway.
[/LIST]

My biggest pet peeve about buying complete systems online is that whatever they advertise on the web page, you can pretty much guarantee that's not what you'll get when it ships.  It might have the same CPU and the same amount of RAM and the case may look pretty much the same but that's it.  If they advertise a brand of DVD or network card or video card, you Will Not Get that card.

Or at least that's how it was when I swore off online system purchases.  It's better with enterprise gear, but I kept ordering stuff that was compatible with Linux and getting stuff that wasn't.

If you order a specific piece of component hardware though, you get exactly that.  Do your research online, find out what works with Linux, what works best for your situation and then order what you need.
