### Post by Lars Noodén on 2009-10-04
The should answer is: you shouldn't, at least not directly.  

To get decent sound quality, you'll need to encode from a lossless format like FLAC or some other. 

[http://true-audio.com/Lossy_data_compression](http://true-audio.com/Lossy_data_compression)

[http://www.bobulous.org.uk/misc/lossless_audio_2006.html](http://www.bobulous.org.uk/misc/lossless_audio_2006.html)
[http://www.bobulous.org.uk/misc/lossy_audio_2006.html](http://www.bobulous.org.uk/misc/lossy_audio_2006.html)

MP3, is lossy, think of it as the JPEG of sound.  To make the small file, whole frequency ranges are tossed out.  Then of those that are left, much of those are thrown away.  To me, MP3 always sound like through a garden hose.  You can really hear it if you play MP3 over hi-fi speakers.  

Ogg is also lossy, but it throws out different data.  

So going (transcoding) from lossy codec to lossy codec, will result in a net reduction of sound quality.  That is regardless of the direction, Ogg to MP3 or vice versa. 

Re-rip from CD or FLAC.
