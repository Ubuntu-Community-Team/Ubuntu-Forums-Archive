### Post by TeXtonyx on 2008-12-14
When to reinstall depends upon the problem and the skill level of
the person with the problem. The judgment required to make such an
assessment is a professional and to some degree, objective decision.

One thing is that the decision shouldn't be colored by is fanatical
political OS obsessions which are as ignorant and uninformed as the
practice it attempts to chastise. IOW, kiddies should not rule,
rather balance should rule.

What do I mean by this practically. Decisions should be governed
by practicality and not by ideology. 60% of Ubuntu users dual boot.
That means that the influx of new users come mostly from Windows
backgrounds; they usually have no command line experience. Advice
to them should take this into account. Such users don't have a
lot of work put into their Ubuntu settings when they have just
installed Ubuntu a few days ago. They want to and the Ubuntu
community should want them, to enjoy their first experiences. 
That's the goal, not to get them to wave a penguin flag. 

Solving computer problems stresses efficiency. So reinstalling
is often the best option for these new Windows users because it
is the most efficient and professional solution and it is some
kind of logical fallacy to mislabel that choice as Microsoft
ineptitude. Time after time I've seen new to Ubuntu users spend
days and 30 to 50 posts to resolve some technical issue which
could be fixed within 45 minutes by reinstalling. For the older
Ubuntu users, fixing the problem by other means than reinstalling
is often the best solution. For one thing they are better prepared
to make the best of a specific solution because they don't have
to ask, 'how do you open a terminal' or they complain that the
command didn't work because they don't know enough to hit <enter>

The single biggest problem for new dual booting users is grub
errors. They lose Windows or sometimes Ubuntu. And it is so
preventable by emphasizing it on the install dialogue like Vector
Linux, rather than hiding it behind Step 7, Advanced, don't put
grub in the MBR.

It would be much better to have a Sticky that helps most of the
new Ubuntu community rather than have a Sticky which delivers 
a sermon which amounts to 'teach the new users the geek way of
fixing things' for that is the Ubuntu Way.

"Telling people to reinstall for every issue is not acceptable"
is true enough but the body of the post was a fanatical rant.

They say Question Authority. I question the authority of a kiddie
who introduces MS vs. Linux animosity under the guise of giving
useful advice. I think it is wiser to question the authority of
a "ubuntu forums staff" member who has made nearly 3,700 posts 
but only received 22 thank yous before he posted that last piece
of trash. That pretty much means he has been wasting a lot of
users time, appointing himself as a moral authority and not
providing useful answers at the level the (new) user requires.

Nice post TeXtonyx.

I am fairly new to a STABLE version of Linux. I have tried Linux off and on for a few years (Mandrake,OpenSuse, Red Hat, etc.) and have finally decided to give it an earnest shot.
My background goes back to MS-DOS. I am very well-versed in that command line environment. The hardest thing I am having to grasp is the different conventions, arguments, switches, etc., at the terminal box. (Could be my age :) ) I have re-installed Ubuntu five or six times in the last couple of weeks. I tried Kubuntu until instability led to issues booting (I really like KDE). Ubuntu seems to be best for me RIGHT NOW. I may re-try OpenSuse or another Linux distro in the future.
Having issues for me is minor, because I am learning how to quickly re-install and saving all of my programs for re-installation. I try command line fixes, but get lost in the conventions a lot and usually am unable to fix the issue. I have a few Linux books, but do not have the time to actually ingest the info right now.
I see some of the snide comments to Windows users and cringe, because this could easily turn off a lot of users that may become contributors if given enough time to learn. The learning curve is longer because of the foundations of Linux. I still dual-boot to Windows XP, but I am finding myself spending less and less time using that particular OS as I become more comfortable with Linux.
So, for those that want to "look" down on new users that cannot grasp what you may know as second nature, be careul, it's a two edge sword.
For new users, hang in there you will become more familiar. Learn the basics and build from there. If you are serious, go buy a book that will outline basics and keep it handy for reference. And as more people with programming capabilities come on board, there will be more reasonable alternatives to the command line. It is the eventual progression of a GUI for more and more things to happen in the background.
It's up to the "Linux" community to be able to adapt and keep tight distros that are able to welcome newer users and still have the ability for more "under the hood" users to customize if they have the desire and ability to do so.
Just my $.02
