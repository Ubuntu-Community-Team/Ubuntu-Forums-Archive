### Post by bodhi.zazen on 2008-12-24
Assuming this is a private business :

1. First, does Ubuntu indeed offer everything they need to perform their duties ? I ask this because sometimes there is specialized software that will not run on Linux.

2. Assuming it does, then I suggest you offer them some choice. For example they may prefer KDE to gnome.

3. You then need to transition them. Don't forget that many people have had "training" on how to use Windows and although to geeks it may seem easy, most businesses that introduce any new software will have some training that goes with it, so I suggest you set up 2-3 1-2 hour training sessions to answer FAQ.

### Post by ajcham on 2008-12-24
There is a script somewhere that installs a very comprehensive XP-like skin for Gnome - I'm not sure where but it's been posted in these forums a few times so you should be able to find a link by searching.

However, I would advise caution - it is an inescapable fact that using Linux is different from using Windows. You can make the desktop look very much like XP, but to what end?  It will still function as Gnome, so to my mind it wouldn't help much. It may even have the opposite effect by increasing the user's expectation that it should work exactly like Windows, thereby causing further frustration when it doesn't.

### Post by PierreDeKat on 2008-12-24
First, spend some time setting up their Ubuntu desktop to something they approve of. 

Like, I second the idea of moving the top panel down to the bottom. Old habits die hard. 

A lot of us are used to reaching for the lower left hand corner to get to a menu and the upper right hand corner to close a window. And we're not inclined to change unless somebody points a gun at our heads.

Then give your fellow employees some options for themes, give them some nice [desktop wallpapers]("http://interfacelift.com/wallpaper_beta/downloads/downloads/any/") to choose from, and watch how quickly they warm up to Ubuntu when they see how Ubuntu warms up to them.

Second -- and this depends on your work situation, of course -- but show them some applications or features that they can get into.

Like my daughter, I showed her Rhythmbox, VLC, Audacity, and easy plug-and-go support for her Ipod, and she never looked back.
