From what I understand with bidirectional communication it makes it easy to block using HDCP the TV , cable box ,recording device or computer that use bidirectional communication knows when you are using it and either one will know when the other is using HDCP.
 
That means HDMI, DVI and some Y/PB/PR jacks, unlike early RCA connections, has bidirectional communication and using it and either one will know when the other is using HDCP.
 
The movie studios have lobbied to get built-in HDCP to DVD players,DVD recorders ,set top box , recorders , TV etc.
 
That is why no blu ray recorder is out and why Mac OS X or any Linux can watch TV or record it even if you buy a TV tuner card or capture card and the right software for the card and OS the HDCP will block it.
 
That is why **cable providers are giving out HD PVR/DVR to **allow copying permitted by fair use laws but the fair use laws do not allow you to pull the recorded content .PVR/DVR units are extremely difficult to pull the recorded content off of, The reason that they don't allow you to record from HDMI, DVI and some Y/PB/PR jacks onto other devices is to protect that content from becoming available on a widespread scale without paying for it. 
 
 
Digging up some more info .
 
> High-bandwidth Digital Content Protection
High-bandwidth Digital Content Protection (HDCP) is a form of digital copy protection developed by Intel Corporation [1] to prevent copying of digital audio and video content as it travels across connections. These connections include popular ones like DisplayPort (DP), Digital Visual Interface (DVI), and High-Definition Multimedia Interface (HDMI), as well as non-popular or now defunct connections like Gigabit Video Interface (GVIF), and Unified Display Interface (UDI).
 
HDCP does not allow copying permitted by fair use laws
