### Post by stomakmonkee on 2010-01-10
+1 the gentleman above me. But another thing is controlled repositories.
The amount of programs that you "click here to install" on Linux is very small, and by most accounts should be avoided.  If you have a close handle on what you allow in and out of your OS, then you can't really install anything that could harm you.
As for exploits, because Linux is community driven and not corporately driven, any bugs that are noticed by the community can also be repaired by the community, instead of waiting for a team of programmers on a payroll to take care of the problem (after submitting the bug, and enough people submit the bug, and it is deemed fiscally prudent to fix the bug because it will effect enough people that they might complain).  There is less money involved here, and more people who are willing to devote their time (at times at no cost) to fixing the issue.

### Post by ibuclaw on 2010-01-10
> **NoaHall said:**
> Because Windows based applications can not run natively on GNU/Linux without a emulator or a layer. As about 100% of malware/viruses are written for Windows, we are safe.

You mean 98% ;)

But yes, other than that - the only thing that can really affect you is Malware - or more specifically, Trojans - in Linux.

Trojans are malicious software/scripts that trick you into thinking they are something else. ie: You think you are installing Enemy Territory, but something else is really happening instead. You can't really protect any OS from Trojans, as the malicious cannot **force** it's way into your system. Instead, you invite it willingly (not necessarily knowingly). I must also add that once in, trojans tend not to do much else in terms of trying to spread / conceal itself.

So long as you stick to software from the Ubuntu Software Centre, and don't add any third party / untrusted repositories, you will be pretty much safe at all times.

Have a look at our [Ubuntu Security Thread]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=510812").
