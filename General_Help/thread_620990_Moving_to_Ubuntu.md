### Post by Harpoon on 2007-11-23
For starters, let me issue a disclaimer:  I am not a graphics professional, nor have I ever even played one on TV.  What will do the job for you depends entirely on what you want to get done, and your impressions of the alternate software's handling characteristics - - e.g., interface, toos layut, available plugins, etc.

Gimp (I am not a fan) is **not** photoshop, but some people like it more, find it entirely capable, while others truly dislike it in the extreme.  I like inkscape, but I don't know how you would judge it.  And on, and on.

The best course of action is to set up ubuntu as a dual bootttttwih Vista.   You did, after all, already pay for it, Ubuntu plays well with other OSs, and you have the option of booting into it if you get frustrated with a learning curve and need to get something accomplished immediately.

After that, you can try the alternates  at your leisure.  If you find (as I do) that your need for windows has dropped to a minimum level, you have the option to run windows inside a virtual machine inside ubuntu to give you access to those apps while still doing other things in ubuntu at the same time.  Examples are virtualbox and vmware.  I have also been seeing people reporting that more Adobe apps are running well inside "wine" which is easy to install and use.

In the end, the judgement is yours, but things on this side of the aisle have progressed far enough to give you the option of trying something diffferent.

I wish you the  best,regardless of which side of the fence you eventually  land.

### Post by daengbo on 2007-11-23
I am a ten-year Linux user and use it exclusively on four different machines at home, but I think you're setting yourself up for disappointment with the expectation of switching cold turkey to Ubuntu.

Yes, Ubuntu is easy for some things. Virtually nothing is impossible.

However, scrapping your whole workflow and expecting to stay productive while learning a new OS and new apps is not realistic.

Start by booting the live disk. Play with it for a while. Try to do the work you usually do on Windows. Get a good idea about how much you'll need to change your workflow. Join a few mailing lists from graphic designers using e.g. The Gimp and Inkscape.

Don't jump into the deep end. You'll just end up drowning.
