### Post by Serpher on 2010-04-18
When you build a car for people to use, you have to build a new car every time you sell one for that person. Of course some of that cost of the car goes into Engineering and the thought that went into the car, but a lot of the cost is the actual physical making of the car. When you build software for people, you build it once time then distribute that program to as many people who buy it. So when you compare it to a car, you both have to engineer it, then you have to build the program once (coding it). This is why really, programs should be a lot cheaper than they actually are.

### Post by jkxx on 2010-04-18
All good responses above!

Good starting place for why open source is such a big deal: [http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/philosophy.html]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/philosophy.html").

A must-read for understanding how open-source works: [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cathedral_and_the_Bazaar]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cathedral_and_the_Bazaar")

And the related [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_open_source_software]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_open_source_software") - scroll down to the table at the bottom of the page and look at the topics next to "Challenges"


Anyway the above will give you plenty of quotes and ideas from some of the gurus behind the FLOSS movement.

The main reason in my opinion is because of what happens when a company establishes a monopoly and locks everything up the way Microsoft has. You get an overpriced product, your privacy can be compromised, the vendor doesn't really care about having a good product with a monopoly, and so on. With that model the vendor eventually gets all the control and power and the user is left with essentially nothing. With open-source it's exactly the opposite where the user always retains control.

I suggest perusing some of the above links though instead of just taking my statement or anyone else's at face value.
