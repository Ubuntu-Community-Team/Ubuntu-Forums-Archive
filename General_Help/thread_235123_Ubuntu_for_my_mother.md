### Post by aysiu on 2006-08-12
You said she "tried to use Firefox"? What happened when she did?

If your mother doesn't trust you, I'd say it's a very bad idea to install something new on her computer.

There are only a few viable solutions:

1. She doesn't trust you anyway, so just go ahead and install it, but use a Windows theme and Windows icons... and don't *tell* her she's using Linux.

2. Just give her some tough love, "I won't troubleshoot any of your computer problems if you're using Windows. Sorry."

3. Keep supporting her on Windows and try to protect her computer a bit: give her a limited account (no administrative privileges). Make her use Firefox and install some extensions that may help her (anti-phishing tools, NoScript, etc.).

### Post by paulyche on 2006-08-14
I got fed up of cleaning up my parent's computers all the time. So one day I gave them a old hard drive, but it in their computer and set it to be master.

Installed Dapper on it, locked down all the desktop and told them that if they wanted to run Windows all they had to do was choose to at boot up. (I configured grub to wait for a response rather than do a timed auto-boot in an attempt to not force the issue)

Nowadays they boot Ubuntu much more than Windows and seem to like it. My parents are the kind of people that this solution is good for - entirely computer illiterate. I'd never recommend Ubuntu to my 'XP power user' brothers, but ubuntu allows my parents to listen to music, send emails, look on the internet and make documents(formatted by spaces as if they were using a typewriter!). And I don't have to come visit and remind them AGAIN to run adaware etc..

As for Ubuntu/Kubuntu. I think Ubuntu is simpler and better for that reason. KDE is probably more windows like, but I think it's also a much more complicated and potentially intimidating desktop.

As for programs, the default set is pretty good for novice users. For example I know my dad wouldn't be able to figure out the power of amarok but he can cope rhythmbox's UI.
