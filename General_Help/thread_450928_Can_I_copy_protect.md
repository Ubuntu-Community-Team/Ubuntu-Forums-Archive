### Post by rocket777 on 2007-05-22
You raise an interesting (non technical) point. How, in a free market, with no IP protection at the point of a government gun (aka lawyer) make a profit for your labor when it is nearly costless for others to use your creation w/o paying you.

Technically, this is difficult to do w/o restricting your paying customers. There are simply too many easy ways for your pictures to be shared once you give anyone (who does not honor an agreement not to share) the access to all the pictures.

I feel for you but I also think in a free society one must find a compatible business model when technology does not provide a solution.

If I were in your place, in the future, I might consider selling the pictures, before they were already shot.

 I would contract with those at the event ahead of time with the proviso that if not enough paying customers come forward (cash in hand) that you would refund their money.  That's the best I can offer, as I believe the problem you pose is unsolved in current law. And it's always unfriendly to have to threaten your customers (like MS, RIAA, etc. do).

### Post by em007a on 2007-05-22
Yes, there are ways to protect the content of a DVD from a basic one to one copy. It won't stop the average 13 year old who already makes copies from Netflix. Do a little research on google.

Every year I make a band video for the 200 plus band students, which I play at the annual band banquet. I make available a DVD for $1 each. I put in a lot of time and effort to produce a professional video. (considerably more than a measly 15 hours), but all I ask in return is to recoup my actual costs. To start begging parents/students not to copy it is out of the question for me for a couple of reasons. 

1) I volunteer to do it, I wasn't contracted by the school. Since I was already doing it for myself and my own kids, why not share it with everyone?

2) Parents are overburdened with school cost already. $1 covers physical media and labeling. Why would I want to profit from a school function? In thinking this way, I could care less if they copy it or not.

3) I bring about 20 copies with me and then have parents sign a sheet to request copies. Since they just watched the video, they already know whats on it and whether or not they even want a copy. This helps reduce waste by bringing too any copies. Most pay right there and those who don't, usually send the money with the child the following day when the DVD's are delivered. What I find is that even months later a few parents will contact me and ask if I still have copies available. This tells me that there is not a huge conspiracy to copy or sell the DVD on a school black market.

If for some reason you don't have the equipment at home, maybe there is another parent that does have it and you could ask them to help you with the project. This would reduce your workload and you wouldn't have to rely on using a studio for the process. Maybe another parent would volunteer to do the entire thing for costs if someone would just ask. 

The kids and parents all volunteer time and money. They don't have the opportunity to profit from the performance. Why should you? With that logic, soon the school will double the price of admission and prevent parents from bringing in cameras or video equipment. 

If this were a wedding or something like that I could sympathize with you, but since it is a school function I do not. If you donated any profit back to the school,  I might understand. Even then, I would leave it up to the individual to do the right thing. 

To each his own i guess.
