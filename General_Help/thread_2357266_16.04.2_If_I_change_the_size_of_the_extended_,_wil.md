### Post by oldfred on 2017-03-31
If you have working Ubuntu live installer you can always reinstall grub.
Normally partitions should not get renumbered.

But in general I do not like moving the start of a partition. It then is a copy which can be very slow depending on size & speed of system. And any interruption during copy leaves part of data not moved and broken system. So have good backups.

You could use the space as /home or just a /mnt/data partition and have some or all of your data in it.
       To move /home uses rsync- Be sure to use parameters to preserve ownership & permissions 
[URL="https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Partitioning/Home/Moving"]https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Partitioning/Home/Moving

[/URL]If this is your Z97 system, then it is UEFI, but you have BIOS/MBR install. There are some advantages to UEFI with gpt partitioning, but that would require full reinstall with backup & restore of all data. May not be worth effort, but another option.

Also you show NTFS partitions, but no Windows. You cannot do most repairs of NTFS from Linux like chkdsk or defrag. So you either need Windows or at least a Windows repair disk to properly maintain your NTFS partitions. If you convert that data to Linux then a total backup & reinstall may make sense.

### Post by oldfred on 2017-03-31
Back in 2011, I converted my first drive to gpt with a new install of Ubuntu. I still had XP in another MBR(msdos) drive.
Since then all new drives including some larger flash drives are gpt.

When UEFI started becoming popular, I started adding both the ESP - efi system partition (FAT32) as first partition and bios_grub (for BIOS boot) to every new or reformatted drive. Then I could convert drive to a new UEFI system and not have to move all the data around to make room for the ESP at beginning of drive.

I just like that gpt has a backup partition table and does a few more internal checks. Not had any issues with gpt which then is a good thing.
