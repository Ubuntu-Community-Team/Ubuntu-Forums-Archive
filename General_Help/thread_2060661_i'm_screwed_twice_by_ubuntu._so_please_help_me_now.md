### Post by QIII on 2012-09-26
Perhaps the answer by Vaphell was a little terse, but it was factual.  The root cause of the loss of your data was an action taken on your part and not the result of the OS operating improperly.  But don't let anyone try to tell you that none of us has ever done the same, or similar, sort of thing.

How about if we try this just in a matter-of-fact manner?

1.  If you were having problems with your graphics, it might have been better to get a satisfactory answer on that.  If you were told "Just reinstall", then you were led astray.  The "Blast off and nuke 'em from orbit" approach is lazy advice and I hate it when that is the first thing someone tells a poster to do.

2.  As with Windows, if you simply install over a previous install you are bound to lose data.  Sometimes it can be recovered, sometimes it cannot.  At this point, if you have used Ubuntu much at all, the likelihood that you can recover data has diminished substantially.  This phenomenon is not unique to Ubuntu nor is it a failing on the part of Ubuntu.

3.  One should always back up important data before making major changes.  In fact, one should do backups regularly because things do go wrong in normal use of any OS.

It is generally not a good idea to immediately alienate the people you come to ask for help from, particularly when they are all volunteers.  We are here to help, but hostile posts often make us simply move on.

As for recovering you data, I would suggest not using your computer AT ALL when it is running from your hard drive.  This will mitigate against further data loss.  You may run a Live session from CD and install any of a number of tools, such as TestDisk, which has a utility called PhotoRec which will recover a wide variety of file types.  You will need some sort of external medium to copy the data to; perhaps an external drive or another internal drive.  Take care that the medium is of sufficient size that it can store the amount of data you are trying to recover.
