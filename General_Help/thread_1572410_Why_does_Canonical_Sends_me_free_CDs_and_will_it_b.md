### Post by coffeecat on 2010-09-11
Canonical's business model is based on making profit from technical support, but the software is free. It's a very similar business model to that of RedHat and Novell who also support (other) Linux distributions. It is quite unlike Microsoft's business model which partly relies on income from licences for their non-free software.

Put simply, Linux companies spend money on distributing their distros, but gain from bugfixes and developments from the wider open source community. Microsoft has to do everything in house. In the long term, the open source business model is probably more viable. I believe some economists predict a steady decline in Microsoft's fortunes.

The free CDs are a slightly separate issue. Consider them a marketing exercise - somewhat like advertising. Also, there is an altruistic side to shipping free CDs to people in poorer countries, which is where I believe Canonical would like to focus them. If your economist friend doesn't understand this, get him/her to look up the phrase 'pro bono publico'.

Of course, we the ordinary home users benefit from all this. We get a free as in 'free beer' (or 'free rice' if you prefer) operating system and all apps to use.
