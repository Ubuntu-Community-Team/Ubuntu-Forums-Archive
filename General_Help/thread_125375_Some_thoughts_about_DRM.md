### Post by ZylGadis on 2006-02-03
What you suggest is actually called watermarking, and can be done in much more clever ways than simply adding a line to a file. It is the logical thing to do under the circumstances, although I would still prefer to not be bound in any way by somebody who thinks I am a criminal.

Unfortunately company CEOs are not being paid to think logically, and I doubt most of them have the required intelligence level. Watermarking has continuously failed to enter the industry for a very simple reason: it does not prevent the spreading of information, it simply tells you who is responsible for it. Sue them all you like afterwards, it will not bring you back your (perceived) profits.

That leaves us with the current situation: industry officials implementing all kinds of crazy protection schemes, actually exacerbating their customers' life and doing nothing to prevent piracy (just look at [http://thepiratebay.org/legal.php](http://thepiratebay.org/legal.php) , there is some very funny stuff there related to the matter). ### Post by stoffe on 2006-02-04
DRM is practically worthless. If only ever hurts the legitimate users - the ones who buy in good faith is going to have problems when they can't play it in their devices, or something else goes wrong. It also tells your customer that you don't trust him/her, and that he/she aren't allowed to use their purchased item as they wish. Pretty condescending and partonizing, yes?

Pirated copies has the restrictions removed by someone with the right knowledge before it is copied and spread. So, if you use DRM, you are giving the users two choices:

1. To be honest: to pay **and** have problems.
2. To be dishonest: no pay, no problems.

By not using DRM, you are giving consumers a new kind of choice:

1. To be honest: pay with no problems
2. To be dishonest: not pay with no problems

Still look like #2 is the best deal that everyone will take? I think not. People are basically honest and understand that producing content costs money. Keep a reasonable price that is easily affordable (or at least actually mirrors the worth of the content), make it easy to pay, and above all, *trust your customer*. It think it will work. 

Some will make copies, but those would have anyways - because pirates will always find a way to copy the content. I do think that if you treat customers with some dignity, a majority will be treat you back in the same way.

From an extra business perspective: I think that it soon will be a real advantage to be able to state "DRM-free content", or rather "No restrictions". Given two equal offerings otherwise, I think that many will react very positively to that. Think about it. It may actually make you money...
