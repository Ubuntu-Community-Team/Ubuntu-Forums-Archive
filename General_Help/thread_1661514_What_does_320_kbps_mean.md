### Post by micael3000 on 2011-01-06
It's the audio bitrate.

Basically: The amount of data 'played' in 1 second.

Your original file has 120kbps so when you converted it to 320kbps the software just 'filled' it with 'blank space', cause you are converting something with a small quality to something with a high quality.

Its the same converting an image from 100x100 to 1000x1000, it will be made of small squares, the 'quality' will be the same, just displaying in a big way.

### Post by MaxIBoy on 2011-01-06
That represents the bitrate of the sound (how many kilobytes per second of sound data.) MP3 is a "lossy" format, meaning it throws away some of the detail to make the files smaller. The higher the bitrate, the less data must be thrown away, so the quality is better. 320 kbps is usually considered the minimum sound quality for MP3 for it to qualify as "good quality;" lower than that and everything sounds a little bit muffled.

However, since you started out with a 120 kbps file, that detail has already been thrown away, and it's gone forever. You can't get it back by re-encoding it with a higher bitrate.

### Post by Vaphell on 2011-01-06
the only way to achieve higher quality sound with lossy formats is to get files with higher native bitrate made from the lossless source, ie rip them again from cd with higher quality settings or purchase high bitrate files in online music stores.

it's the same dynamic that causes .jpg files to be a lousy format to work with. If you do some graphics manipulation you should always use a lossless format to be a master copy and then produce lower quality jpg. Working with jpg would kill quality fast in just few open/edit/save cycles.
