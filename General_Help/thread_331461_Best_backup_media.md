### Post by pseudonym on 2007-01-04
They say DVD is supposed to last 50 years. But claims like this are most probably meaningless, even if it were technically true. Because the issue is how long *the format* will be around, as well as players/software which support it. And the rate things change these days, it will be long before 50 years when DVD will have died.

Having said that, if you have 3 copies of all your data at any given time, stored on different media, you are well placed. I have one copy of my stuff on the installation hard drive, one on an external hard drive, and on DVDs. Re-copying every 3-5 years is also advisable, especially if there has been a major format change in that time (eg. CD to DVD in the last few years).

### Post by kolinab on 2007-01-04
I'm in a similar situation. 

I'm a little overwhelmed by all the information on sites like [www.burnworld.com](www.burnworld.com) and the like. There's another one called CD freaks or something. What I've determined from my basic reading re: media is that most blank optical media is made by a few companies. I try not to buy the super cheapie brands and resign myself to the reality of updating the file formats/file systems etc. every few years. 

My personal files used to fit on a 5.25 in disk, then a few 3.5's, then on a CD, etc. It's good to update your storage method as time goes on as well because it forces you to 'check in' on the status of your backup. Right now I have all my data on my laptop backed up to an external hard drive.

I'm learning how to use rsync to backup frequently used user files, and also wish to make a long term optical backup of my music and most importantly, my digital photos. 

I haven't much experience burning data to DVD in Linux yet but I'm seeking a software solution that will enable me to cram 10 or so DVD's full of photos and music in some readily accessible format.

I'm not very trusting when it comes to my data so I'd be much more likely to purchase an additional HD than backup files somewhere online. I guess the trick is to have lots of backups in lots of places. Enough rambling.
