### Post by afz12 on 2012-09-16
I think I agree with Galdov' line of thought. I doubt that Linux will ever have public mainstream appeal. Windows and Macintosh target the casual computer market and people in these segments typically use email, web browsing and play animation games. This is unlikely to change and I can't really see any Linux distribution competing in this  market sector.

Also, screen resolution problems are not the sole domain of Linux! I had to replace a XP OS on an older notebook and finding appropriate drivers was a mission. The worst driver search affected screen resolution with inadequate resolution. After tying several drivers that "looked suitable" one finally worked - but then only after I happened to install Google Chrome! Oddly, the screen crashed into ultra low resolution mode during the process then suddenly, for no reason, the high resolution option became available. 

People who are more technologically enabled may migrate towards Linux platforms and Ubuntu and Mint Cinnamon or Mint Mate are possibly the most accessible distro's. Fedora is a bit bloated with authorization rituals and I find this a bit disagreeable so I don't use it (except sometimes on Virtualbox). Other distributions such as debian are well used commercially, from what I understand. Debian/Linux is used in Linux boxes up and down various countries supplying Internet/network traffic. This professional/commercial application demands exceptional reliability and security - Windows is not well suited to say 6 sigma or better network outage prevention.

It could be argued that Linux virtues include free software - however not all of these offerings are usable. Many are poorly documented and some don't even work (eg sound card oscilloscopes) or require advanced telepathy on the part of the end user for set-up. This is understandable due to the enthusiast contributions made. In contrast, Windows and Macintosh offer slick products and some are free. Further, Linux and its distro's don't replicate all offerings available in mainstream OS'. For example Mathcad (NOT Matlab <-> Octave, in-part SciLab)  has no "clone version" in Ubuntu etc. Also I doubt that people who actually enjoy computer games would find much to their liking in LInux.

I summary, I don't really see the various OS' available as competitors. They tend to target different market sectors and for each sector they are well suited. Also virtualization software such as Virtualbox provides opportunity for people to step outside their technological comfort zones to some extent, if and when they choose to and trial out alternative OS'. Very few people however are this adventurous. Finally, the various OS' have seemed to merge into fairly similar UI's and share similar desktop items - e.g. icons. I don't remember seeing icons under DOS! I think it is best for multiple OS variants to remain as developers in each can domain learn a bit from each other, in addition to assisting a better understanding on differing consumer interests and needs.
