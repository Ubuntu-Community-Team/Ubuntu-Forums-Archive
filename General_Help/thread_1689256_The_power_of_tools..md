### Post by Stephann on 2011-02-17
In my humble opinion, the 'skill' of building computers is becoming increasingly less and less valuable.  In a decade or so, the idea of user replaceable parts will be as novel as rotary dial telephones.  It won't come in the way you think, though; it won't be computers getting smaller (though they are) but rather cell phones getting bigger.  Already, an iPhone is nearly twice as powerful and sophisticated as a the most expensive, modern computers that were on the market ten years ago.  This means that for the price of an 'average' low end computer ($50 used, with peripherals and monitor) underprivileged and under educated communities will be able to have hand held tablets with terabytes of storage, peer-to-peer wifi and bluetooth, on sleek full color screens.  Fully integrated systems may add to 'etrash' but that would only be because they've been well used.  Interchangable parts lead to users popping open the hood and potentially damaging their systems in a way that all-in-one units do not, with the added benefit of reductions in manufacturing costs across the board.

Just my two cents; good luck with your project.

### Post by Stephann on 2011-02-17
Hi Hal,

Honestly, I do understand where you're coming from.  I lived in Santiago, Chile for three years as an English teacher.  I arrived with just the sorts of components you're hoping to recycle (a PII 333hz processor and motherboard, 256 megs of RAM, and 3 gig hard drive with XP eating a third of that disk.)  What I'm suggesting is that you'll get a lot more bang for your buck if you focus on yesterday's laptops (Celeron 1.6 Ghz, 80 gig hard drives, etc.)  The amount of time it takes anyone with real skill to assemble and maintain functioning systems can be incredibly intensive; it can take two or three days for a technician to build one or two machines out of a pile of parts (speaking from experience; I have a small farm of old Mac G4s that I've managed to get Debian, Ubuntu, and Fedora running on.)  

The the other reason to look at laptops are the low power requirements.  In countries where there's no running water, and power comes from generators, the ability to run a machine that requires a mere 150 watts becomes much more preferable to the average machine that requires nearly 700 watts (monitor and computer together.)  You also have fewer parts to 'come loose' and a better mentality that "it works until it doesn't; that's when you replace it."

I'm not saying there's nothing of value in our 'old junk'; rather, the One Laptop Per Child program was brilliant, in saying that for $100, they can give a *new* laptop to a kid who needs it.  The machines you'd be looking at, by salvaging large quantities of used gear could end up being far more expensive, just in terms of time and effort to try and compile all of that hardware (which was a hard lesson I learned, when I volunteered to help a school I worked at make functioning systems out of a huge pile of parts, over the course of a few months.)

Best wishes to you in your endeavors.
