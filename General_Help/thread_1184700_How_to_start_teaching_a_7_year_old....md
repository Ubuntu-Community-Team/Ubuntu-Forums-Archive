### Post by ibuclaw on 2009-06-11
I'd do it in the exact same way as you'd teach an adult.

Take it in steps, and always build upon what you last taught him. Remember, children at that age are a sponge, they tend not to have time to question what you say, but use your words like gold and take them as the truth. So be careful just how you say your sentences when you teach him. ;)

You probably won't need to go too deep into the more advanced side of things, (servers, command-line tools and utilities, KVM, SSH and VNC), but by the time he is 12-13 if he is still with Linux, that is usually the best time to pick that up. I have worked with quite a few teenagers in the Ubuntu Beginners Team, they can sometimes be difficult and noisy, but they grow up very fast in a short space of time, but that still doesn't stop them asking for my help every other day. :D

Regards

### Post by cwwilson721 on 2010-12-16
My kids have their own computers, and are responsible for installing/maintaining whatever OS they choose on it. 

Oldest (16 now) started (7) on Slackware, and now uses Ubuntu 10.10. The girl uses Windows 7. They can both install/format/partition/ the OS and whatever apps they desire. If it crashes, so what? They 'own' the box, so they have to fix it.

They have learned more from doing than any computer class could have dreamed of teaching them. The boy has a high-school computer class that the teacher just lets him audit, because he already talked to him in previous years about what he does and how he does it.

Let them learn at their own pace, doing what they need to do. The worst that can happen is a bork of the entire OS.

It's only software
