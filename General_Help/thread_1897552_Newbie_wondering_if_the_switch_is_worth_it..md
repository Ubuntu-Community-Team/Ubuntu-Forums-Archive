### Post by snowpine on 2011-12-19
Welcome to the forums! Don't get frustrated... find a source of instructions/how-to's that you trust, then work through your questions/problems one-by-one.

Personally I find that wiki.ubuntu.com and help.ubuntu.com are two sources that I trust for advice (in addition to these forums). Have you read the "Nvidia" and "iPod" pages at help.ubuntu.com? 

You need to be specific, instead of "I can't do it, it doesn't work, I had issues," say something like "I am following the official guide at (link) and when I get to step 7 I get this specific error message: (error message)." You will get the help you need and you'll be a Linux expert before you know it! :)

To answer the question posed by your thread title, no, the switch is not worth it. Ubuntu is just another tool in your toolbox, next to Windows, Mac, other Linux distros, etc. It is "worth it" to learn how to use a hammer, but not "worth it" in my opinion to throw out your screwdriver and drill just because you bought a new hammer. :)

### Post by wbm on 2011-12-23
Here is my take on your question.
1. it is a bad time to jump into Linux now if you have an older machine. All the new distros require a lot of power to run Gnome 3 or Unity or the newer KDE.

2. If you have an older machine like your laptop, sticking with an older distro would be a good idea, but you should probably stick with a LTS version (Long Term Support). In your case that would be 10.4.
[Info here]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/LTS")
Older versions are missing out on all the new cool stuff though ;)

3. I would probably recommend you use Linux Mint instead of Ubuntu. Mint is based on Ubuntu, and many people call it Ubuntu done right. Have a look at Mint 10.4

4. you will find the following if you compare Windows to Linux.
Linux: safe surfing (almost no virus), can do all Office (Libre or Open Office), Image manipulation (Gimp) and image organization (DigiKam) tasks, is great for keeping track of your checkbook (GnuCash), and updates and software installs are easy and it doesn't seem to crash.
Windows or Mac: use for Audio creation, Video creation, and Gaming.

5. iPod, you may be able to shoehorn it to somewhat work with Linux, but better stick with iTunes on Windows. I do not like iTunes and have an Android phone. On Linux I use (currently) Clementine for my music, and my Android phone is just seen as a USB hardrive when plugged in. Very easy.

Just fyi, I have been a die hard Mac User for 15 years, switched at home to Linux 4 years ago and just started using Windows 7 for my Digital Audio and Video work 6 month ago. At work, I use all three for various things.

One thing I would like to point out that people misunderstand is this:

"Why doesn't Linux support ....?"

Well, it isn't exactly that way.

Microsoft doesn't support anything but Microsoft.  If you can plug in a gadget and have it plug and play it is because the OEM bent over backwards to make sure the hardware and its driver were compatible with Windows.  Many a first born child is sacrificed on the Altar of Redmond for the coveted "We have Microsoft's blessing" sticker on their packaging.  You can't blame the OEMs, really.  They know that 95% of the machines in the world run on Windows, so where are they going to put their effort?  They are making a wise business decision.  Microsoft does not write the drivers for XYZ Co's gadgets.  XYZ Co does.  If they are lucky, the driver gets put in Microsoft's driverbase and -- BINGO -- it's plug and play.

The reality is not that Linux does not support XYZ, but that XYZ does not have a driver (software version, library, etc) that works on Linux.  XYZ doesn't support Linux.

Your iTunes?  Sorry.  Proprietary and closed source. Do you think Apple gives a flying rip whether it works in a Linux system?  Snort!  No.  Instead, some nice guy or gal figures something out on his or her own time in the evening and on weekends so you can use your iPod and then gives it to you for free.  A lot of sweat on the part of someone who is probably getting paid nothing at all other than the satisfaction of helping the community out.  Remember to hit the paypal button and make a donation.

Just keep that in mind.  Frustrating, yes.  Linux' fault, not entirely.

I HATED it at first, I felt so lost, I hated the way everything was setup, the way it acted and responded didn't make sense to me but by the end of my degree I adapted and got more in tune with it. Ubuntu was even a worst reaction when I first tried it (was sometime around version 7), I didn't know how to find programs or install them, I was confused about the filing system, I was forced to use the terminal alot (back then I only ever used the command prompt in windows a handful of times so I was totally out of my realm). Over time I tried it several more times, each time I learned a little bit more and used it for a bit longer. Every OS has a certain behaviour, certain downfalls and certain benefits. When you're new to an OS all the problems stick out like dogs balls and all the benefits take a back seat (you only notice the bad, not the good). To a long time user, those problems are expected and instinctively fixed, you do the same thing without realizing it on windows. 

Give it a couple of months, if by then you feel it's getting in the way of doing what you want it to do then maybe it's not right for you and try something else or go back to windows.
