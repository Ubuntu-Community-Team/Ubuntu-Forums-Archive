### Post by apelete on 2006-11-06
According to me none of these players are eligible for an official Gnome's Music Database Player, execept Banshee perhaps.
Let me explain: it has something to do with HIG (Human Interface Guideline), which aims to define a way of designing GTK apps' interface for Gnome.

If you think about it, you'll see that Exaile, Listen (I didn't see Quod Libet's interface) and perhaps more of them are trying to copy Amarok in order to give something "as good" on Gnome.
They are failing IMO because they all have something reminiscent of Amarok in their interfaces, and I think it's because they're too much relying on Amarok's design and features.

Rhythmbox DOES lack some features, but it's very HIG compliant, and Banshee is very close (if not ahead) too. All the others seem to be too cluttered, or  "messy" for official Gnome's apps, AFAIK.
That's not the point with Amarok, because Amarok "is KDE", and KDE is all about "plenty of features/buttons/menus".
I used to be "in love" with Amarok (it totally "ROKS" !!), until I decided to go "all Gnome" (it was hard at the beginning to switch to Rhythmbox, I confess, but I get used to it and like it a lot).

All in all Banshee should be the very best choice to replace Rhythmbox, let's see what the Gnome Team will choose if they ever decide to switch...;)

Banshee - Don't like the playlist system at all.  Too much work required to make a playlist from scratch.

Quod Libet - Not bad, it just seemed Listen really took what was good from this and improved on other parts.

Exaile - too much like Amarok for me.  Again, browsing the collection isn't as intuitive as it should be.

Songbird - Has fresh ideas, but I again don't like its layout.  Anything concerned with being an iTunes killer doesn't interest me, because I don't like the layout of iTunes, either.

For the record, I don't think amaroK is too flashy, or bloated, like others regularly criticize it for.  I like all the eye candy and stuff, but the layout just isn't intuitive enough.  My ideal player would probably be amaroK, just laid out more like Listen.  Or, Listen with more eye candy :)

All the stability issues in Listen for me were based on the Wikipedia tab, and he fixed that for the 0.5 release by changing the engine from kHTML to Gecko.  Listen is now just as stable as any of the other ones, on my machine with my collection, most of which are .flac or .ogg - maybe it breaks more on mp3s which is why people have problems with it?

Its a shame Listen doesn't seem to have had much work done on it recently, though.  The svn was last updated two months ago.  I hope it doesn't die.

### Post by aysiu on 2006-11-28
I use Rhythmbox because it works for me.

I've tried a whole bunch of players, and even though some have fancy features, I always keep coming back to Rhythmbox. 

**AmaroK** has amazing features, but it always annoys me in some way. Back in Dapper, Breezy, and Hoary, it would just take too long to load playlists or switch songs. Once Edgy came around, it was a bit faster, but it wouldn't refresh my collection when I added songs to my music folder. I found that annoying. I've also never been able to get KDE to recognize my multimedia keys, and I much prefer to use the play/pause, next, previous, and volume keys built into my keyboard than define other keys for global shortcuts.

I don't know if I've tried Exaile (I may have briefly), but with all the hype about **Listen**, I just didn't see what was so special about it. What can Listen do that other players can't? It seemed about as feature-filled as Noatun or Muine to me...

Finally, **Banshee**. There were definitely things I liked about Banshee. For one thing, its DAAP plugin actually worked (I could see my wife's iTunes library, and she could see my Banshee library). It also had ID3 tag editing before Rhythmbox did. As with AmaroK, my abandonment of Banshee came down to simple functionality. My multimedia key support on Banshee was fickle. Sometimes the play/pause would just stop working in Banshee. Oddly enough, previous/next and volume keys would all work.

**Rhythmbox** turned me off when I first saw it in Hoary, but now that I'm in Edgy, it's really grown on me. It has smart playlists, easy rating of songs, play counts by reaching the *end* of a song (something I've grown accustomed to from my iTunes days), and it *always* works with my multimedia keys. Ultimately, I don't care that much about fetching lyrics or DAAP support. I just want to listen to my music and to be able to change songs easily and reliably.

Others have had experiences different from mine, of course, but I just thought I'd share why I stick with Rhythmbox.

### Post by AusIV4 on 2007-03-11
I use KDE, so Amarok is the obvious choice, but I've tried out a variety of other music players and I think even on Gnome, I'd still be using Amarok. It's the most complete, most stable music player I've ever used. The only thing I'd wish for is a fairly complete, DRM free music store, and that's not going to happen any time soon.

I use Amarok to manage my iPod, which it does quite well. I use a plugin called "weekalarm", which lets me set different wake times for different days of the week, which makes it very convenient for a college student who wakes up at four different times on a five day school week.

I also like the ability to switch playlists without having to stop the song. iTunes is my main basis for comparison, and it always irritated me that if I wanted to change from one playlist to another, I had to stop listening to my current track, even if the same track were on the other playlist. Amarok automatically continues on whatever playlist you have active in the playlist browser. It also lets you queue songs to be the next played, which I find very convenient. Some of the Gnome players may have these features as well, but they were definitely things I liked about Amarok.

Then there's the scoring system. Some people don't like it because it demotes a song if they skip it, but I've found that given a few months, it generates a fairly accurate representation of how well you like each of the songs in your library.
