### Post by aysiu on 2006-05-16
The same in terms of functionality--not the same in terms of defaults.

For example, Kubuntu does not come with *kpersonalizer*. It can be installed, but it's not included with a default Kubuntu installation. Kubuntu also has big mouseover bubbles (not just tooltips... *bubbles*) on the panel by default. You can turn this off, but most distributions do not have those bubbles on in the first place.

### Post by GeneralZod on 2006-05-16
Kubuntu has the buggiest KDE desktop I've seen so far, alas, so in many ways it's not *quite* like your typical "KDE linux" ;)

Regarding Linus's comments - this was more a rant against some of the GNOME developers' design choices, specifically the outright removal of useful options from the Print dialogue in the name of "not confusing users".  KDE and GNOME both have there benefits and any statement (even from the Great God Linus himself! ;)) that one is "way better than the other" should be taken with a pinch of salt.

GNOME is often a good choice for new users though as it is far less overwhelming than KDE and has very good defaults, whereas KDE has tons of customisability options scattered everywhere (which is great for "power users" like Linus) but rather bizarre and confusing defaults.  Neither can be said to be exclusively for "beginners", though - some will cut their teeth on Linux using KDE and move to GNOME once they're a seasoned veteran; others will do the reverse.  For the record, my 57-year-old mum who'd barely used a computer in her life uses KDE :)

### Post by Ensnared on 2006-05-16
[QUOTE=syxbit]btw. Ensnared, why did you switch ?[/QUOTE]
In short, Gnome got too much dumbed down and simplified for my taste.

To elaborate, I like to have all the options readily available without needing to edit the registry (or gconf, as it's called), and I don't believe that users get too confused if they have a lot of options to play around with - I know I don't. Quite the contrary, I believe more options is fun for new users to play around with and try out, and find out what they like best. To me, versatility and freedom of choice have been a big part of what Linux is about, and Gnome doesn't provide that anymore. But since I'm still dealing with Linux, I have the freedom to choose another GUI, so that's what I did ;)

I'm not saying Gnome is a bad GUI though - simple is better for those who prefer it that way. It's just not the right one for me anymore - I'd rather have a lot of options and not need them, than not have them when I need them.
