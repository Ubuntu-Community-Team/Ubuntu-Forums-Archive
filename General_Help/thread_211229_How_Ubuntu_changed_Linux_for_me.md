### Post by jackkerouac on 2006-07-08
I am a life-long Windows user.

I began using MS-DOS on a TRS-80 Model I with an attached tape drive. Depending on what software you were loading in to the machine, we had two volume settings that needed adjusting. High for the word processing program, low for Android Nim.

You couldn't do a lot with 4 KB of built in RAM and BASIC, but I tried. And as software and computers got better, I upgraded. First, it was MS-DOS 6.22, then Windows 3.1, Windows 3.11 for Workgroups, Windows 95, Windows 98, Windows 98SE, Windows ME (shudder) and finally Windows XP with its numerous and seeminly neverending patches and Service Patches.

Sure, during this time I had heard of Linux. Heck, I even tried it once. After loading a version of Red Hat, I immediately removed it, having no clue how do do anything in it and having zero desire to re-learn the basic functions of an OS.

After years of being semi-happy in Windows world and even making my living in it, I stumbled across the world of open source software. My first piece of open source software was FileZilla. Like everyone else my age, I was into the warez scene and could download pretty much any software I wanted, free of charge, if not free of guilt.

But FileZilla was different. It worked as good, if not better than commercial competitors. It was excellent and drove me to look for other open source software to replace my collection of perhaps not 100% legal Microsoft products.

OpenOffice came next, along with a variety of other programs. Life was good. I had an operating system I knew how to use and software that was just as good as the commercial stuff.

One thing lead to another and I decided I would finally take the plunge and try a Linux OS. I chose Ubuntu because it seemed the most popular and easiest to use.

This is my experience up until now.

**_Installation_**

My first attempt to install Ubuntu was a disaster. 

I decided to install it as a dual boot with my Windows XP, so I could still use my work programs. I have a 125 GB HDD and a 40 GB HDD and thought I would install Ubuntu on the 40 gigger. I downloaded the Desktop CD, burned it, and booted up.

Ubuntu loaded up fine, recognized every single one of my devices no problem and looked great. So far so good. I started the installation process and never looked back.

Perhaps I should have.

Ubuntu crashed five minutes into the install and after I re-booted my system, I realized my Master Boot Record has been erased, so my Windows XP partition was toast. After I screwed around with that for a while, I finally fixed the problem and tried again.

My second attempt to install Ubuntu went just fine. It installed in about 15 minutes and I was up and running quickly.

**_Initial Issues_**

There were some problems.

My ATI RADEON 9250 didn't have any drivers. I couldn't watch DVDs. My MP3 collection was useless.

What the Hell? I thought. What kind of operating system doesn't allow you to play MP3s?

A quick Google and I came across EasyUbuntu.

What the Hell? I thought. What the Hell is 'apt-get'?

My improptu tutorial on apt-get and the Terminal, a cut-and-paste and EasyUbuntu had me up and running with my ATI card, DVDs and MP3s.

Things were rolling.

I use Mozilla Firefox and Thunderbird for surfing and the web, so I decided to install Thunderbird. Using the Synaptic Package Manager was simple and in no time, I had Thunderbird up and running. Very quickly I was receiving my requisite 100 spam and porn messages per day.

I did have a couple of issues, however.

Part of my job is developing web pages and for that, I needed Internet Explorer. I learned about Wine, but installing IE through Wine seemed pretty daunting. The Ubuntu forums came to my rescue, as I learned about a script, the name of which escapes me, that installs IE versions 5.0, 5.5 and 6 into Ubuntu automatically.

Voila, I was Chauncy, as the kids say.

My second problem was two-fold. I could not access my Windows drive and I could not access my home network. My home network consists of three computers and two laptops, all running Windows XP Media Center Edition and all with wireless cards. A D-Link DI-524 wireless router connects the mess together, but that runs through a D-Link DVG-1120M VOIP router that controls my phone.

Sigh.

I checked the Ubuntu forums and some had a post telling someone else with a problem similar to mine to install Samba, do a bunch of other complicated stuff and everything would work. What could it hurt? I installed Samba through the Package Manager and was getting ready to do the other stuff when on a whim I decided to check my Windows drive.

It was there.

The network.

It was there too.

I now had full access to all my drives, computers, network, etc. with little or no effort.

Very cool.

**_Final Issues_**

So what is keeping me as a dual booter? Why don't I just dump Windows and get it over with?

A couple of reasons.

One, I use Macromedia's Dreamweaver 8.01 to design and implement websites. It just doesn't work in Ubuntu, no matter what I try.

Two, I connect to the office network using Cisco's VPN Client software. I have the Linux version, but for the life of me, I have no idea how to install it.

I installed QEMU and KQEMU (once again, using a script from someone smarter than I), and installed Windows XP Professional in an image. It works great and Dreamweaver runs from that, but it's not optimal.

Ubuntu is great, no question about it. It's easy to use, aparently easy to install (grr!) and comes with just enough software to be useful without being overwhelming. The community is unlike anything I have ever seen and help is extremely forthcoming. If I could get my work software working on it, I would switch over permanently (and Bluefish/Quanta Plus/Nvu just don't cut it).

Until then, I work in Windows, but I play in Ubuntu.
