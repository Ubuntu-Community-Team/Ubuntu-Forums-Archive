### Post by bumanie on 2010-06-03
Linux has a number of different desktop environments - kde, gnome, xfce, enlightenment, fluxbox and there are many more. Why??? because they have been developed over the years for various reasons - some are ease of use, some are for being light on resources at other times for a specific project. As it is open-source development, they are then all released for anyone to use - gives you choice and if you are prepared to experiment, you may find you prefer one desktop environment over another  - and there is nothing to stop you using the desktop environment you prefer.

### Post by Iowan on 2010-06-03
The different Ubuntu versions are (usually) because of the desktop environment. You can choose the one that serves your needs - standard, light, bling, etc... "best" depends on what you want/need. ### Post by daamaya1982 on 2010-06-03
If you're trying to learn Linux to have marketable job skills, I would tell you that you don't need either. You need to sit at a command line and learn from there, because in the real world you're most likely just going to SSH into servers and you're going to need to know command line, vi, and some scripting. Also, CentOS would probably be a better install, because honestly most major companies go with RHEL for their server environments.

If I do have to use a desktop environment, then I typically go with Fluxbox. Why? It's lightweight and I really don't need all the garbage that comes with Gnome or KDE. I have never installed either, and never plan on doing so. Everything I need can be done from CLI.

The problem with Linux as it is now is that so many people think they know how to use Linux, and even list it on their resume, simply because they run Ubuntu and they can navigate their way around Gnome. Really, most Linux users at this point (95%+) are just novices in comparison with the guys and girls who have been around since Slackware 4 and text based installs, and that is because of the windows-like desktop environments bundled with distros nowadays.
