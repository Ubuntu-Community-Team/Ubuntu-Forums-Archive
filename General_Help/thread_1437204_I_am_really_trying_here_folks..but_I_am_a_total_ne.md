### Post by arashiko28 on 2010-03-23
First, welcome aboard!
Second, don't just go copying and pasting stuff on your terminal, once you press enter, it's entirely your responsibility. Check the malicious commands post here > [http://ubuntuforums.org/announcement.php?f=326](http://ubuntuforums.org/announcement.php?f=326)

Everyone is willing to help, but there are few that aren't, always check the command someone gives to you, and ask if you don't know.

Now, I do a lot of video encoding and burning.

AcidRip is great ripper, also k9copy, dvd95 (9 to 5) and dvd::rip. For video encoding, avidemux, in case you need subtitles gaupol, subrip...
All of this have gtk interface, means no need to use command lines in terminal.

For installation, go to System> Administration > Synaptic Package Manager, on the search line, type the name of the package and there you go, yo can select all of what you want to install by right clicking and selecting mark for install, and once you are finished selecting packages, click on apply on the upper panel, once this is done, first it will all depend on your internet connection for download and a couple of minutes for installation.

Good luck!!

#1: Welcome to the wonderful world of Linux.  It can be both fun and frustrating at times.

#2: It *is* possible that you could use wine to run a Windows program for ripping, etc...however, I've yet to see an encryption that DvDRip couldn't handle.  I've used it myself for a number of years to rip my own personal collection and store it on a USB backup drive.

#3: Just because it's "free" doesn't mean it's "cheap."  I think we  might have more developers overall in Linux than Bill has employees at Microsoft.  Updates are done quite frequently although the main focus is on security first, usability second, minor bugs and annoyances third (if ever).

#4: The first responder actually nailed it on the head in my opinion:
```
sudo apt-get install ubuntu-restricted-extras libdvdcss2 libdvdread4 libdvdnav4 dvdrip
```
Pop that into your command line, give it your password (note the **sudo** at the beginning, that means "**do** this as a **s**uper **u**ser" aka ROOT) and *poof* you're good to go.

Open a terminal
Applications > Accessories > Terminal

sudo apt-get update
```
