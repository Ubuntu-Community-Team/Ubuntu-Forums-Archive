### Post by marshag63 on 2011-05-01
Dear New Linux User:

Most of us have experienced your frustration, funny thing is that now I get more frustrated with Windows now that I have converted to Linux at home (still use it at work).  

There are lots of ways to accomplish all your tasks and, yes, it will seem crazy different.

Most important is to update your system after install.  I recommend either a bootable USB flash drive with Ubuntu on it so that you can learn how to do things in linux.  

I suggest grabbing unetbootin for windows and downloading an ubuntu .iso file and making a bootable flash drive that always boots into "live cd" mode (means it will not save settings or programs) so you can lean without doing damage to your windows or ubuntu install.

Pick one suitable for your computer specs - is it older or newer?)

[http://unetbootin.sourceforge.net/](http://unetbootin.sourceforge.net/) - grab the windows version and make a bootable flash drive.  If you cannot boot from a flash drive, just keep booting the live CD and explore, play - lean.  When you have questions, come back and check out these forums for posts on your particular problem or start a new post, or even go to the IRC forum for ubuntu.

Once you are more comfortable, you can actually install.

Here's a little tip about finding USB drives, CD ROM drives, etc.: find the Disk Utility Program (Menu > Administration > Disk Utility).  It will show you the paths/device names (i.e. /dev/sda1) - no C:/ or D:/ drive in Linux.

File structure is different too, i.e.: ~/YOURNAME/ is where your documents, music, pictures, videos will be.  (The "~" is a substitute for /home/YOURNAME/ -- A "shortcut way of saying your "home" folder.

In windows, there are two major ways to get software apps - one is the distributions repository (which is setup in Synaptic [the program you use to install apps] and by adding the "ppa" - i.e., a third party "repository" (if the app is liked by many it gets added to the distros repository eventually).  (Here's a tip:  its easier to copy paste the commands into a "terminal" [dos like app] to add a ppa than anything else - they are usually listed for you to copy and paste.  Be sure to always run "sudo apt-get update" after you add a ppa.  Once you are more experienced, there are other ways. As a beginner, stick with Synaptic.

I would suggest you make a list of all the tasks you *must" be able to do and also note the tasks you *want* to be able to do.  

Take the *must* be able to do list and figure out what programs can handle that task (ex. file manager, text editor, calculator, web browser, email, picture viewer, video player).

Take it slow, then add the *want* things (ex. downloading youtube vids, recording desktop or whatever.

Hope this helps!  I've experienced your frustration.  It gets way easier.


MDG
