### Post by ezsit on 2009-10-17
> This kind of cráp should be kept out of education. The librarian probably agrees, but you can complain to whoever made the decision to choose DRM-encrusted material for an educational course.

Actually, as a librarian, I disagree. Education is a business, publishing is a business, writing is a business and a livelihood. Publishers and authors do not want their works stolen and copyright infringed, they need to make money to live and feed their families.

My library uses OverDrive.com as well to provide downloadable audiobooks and ebooks. The DRM protections allow publishers to license their materials, safe in the knowledge that most people will not be able to subvert copyright restrictions. Without DRM, publishers would not license the thousands of titles each year for users to download and enjoy. The system works fairly well.

The reason Linux is not supported is because Linux's market share is so low and support demands are quite high. I provide the support for users at my library for OverDrive downloadable materials. As a Linux user at home, I'd love to see Linux supported, but I fully appreciate why it is not.

> I told her Ubuntu Linux...and she said it should work and if I have problems, to call and someone'd walk me through it. >___>

As the support person for my library, I doubt this librarian knew what she was talking about. Linux is not supported. If she knew that installing Wine, to install OverDrive Media Console AND Windows Media Player would work, then she should have told you that upfront. I have never attempted Wine and do not run any Windows software under Linux. Maybe the librarian was talking about running Windows under a virtual machine and using Linux to run a virtual instance of Windows. This might certainly be the easiest way to accomplish your goal.

### Post by ezsit on 2009-10-17
Ah, where to begin?

> Historically none of these is a business. 

Ask Gutenberg if it was not a business when he invented the printing press. The business of printing goes back further, but I believe several hundred years will suffice for historical proof. Education, universities were charging fees and professors were making a living from education long before Gutenberg printed his first book. As for writing for a living, that goes back to Plato, at least.

> Digital Rights Management of library materials was not even feasible until the 21st century.

Who cares. Digital media was not available until the later half of the 1990s so why would anyone need DRM until then at the very least? Do you even realize the stupidity here. What is your point? DRM is not necessary until two conditions are already met: the availability of digital media (audiobooks, ebooks, evideos, and music) and the availability of the means to easily and cheaply copy and redistribute that media. These two conditions did not become generally available at consumer costs until the late 1990's. Any talk of DRM before that time is purely academic.

> I would never choose any restricted medium for any part of a course. I would also never choose to tie my students' study to a corporation's profits.

Good for you. What do you think books are? Books, for all intents and purposes, are the same as digital books or audiobooks. Each format carries copyright restrictions and each format was made available to you and your students by a corporation, thank you. So, to understand you, you do not require your students to read anything?  

> Teachers and students alike are quite able to respect every aspect of copyright without restrictive, commercial technologies hindering their access to study materials. 

So, every student purchases a legal copy of the material you require for class? The same is true of an audiobook. Libraries PAY for the rights to use audiobooks so our patrons do not have to pay themselves. Either way, it is the same damn thing.

> Publishers have licensed many millions of printed texts without DRM.

Yes, and those publishers have, in effect, given up on those titles. If a publisher releases a book title without DRM protections, there is nothing to prevent piracy. Publishers know this and are very aware of the consequences. Releasing a title without DRM protections is the publisher's way of being charitable. Be grateful some publishers do this.
