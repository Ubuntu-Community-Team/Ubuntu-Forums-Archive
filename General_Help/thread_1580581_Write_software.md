### Post by QIII on 2010-09-23
Not necessarily a formal class.  But a lot of sweat.  You don't just wake up one morning and say "I'm a software engineer!"  You don't walk into GM one day and say "I decided to be a mechanical engineer this morning.  When can I start?"

When I set out to create a new software application, there are hours, days or months of up-front prep before I even think about writing a line of code.  I both engineer (that's the design portion) and develop (that's the implementation portion).

I would say that I spend 90%+ of my time thinking and planning, working through use-case scenarios and consulting with clients.  Something less than 10% of my time is taken up actually developing.  Of that 10%, a substantial portion is taken up by planning implementation, setting up test methodologies, processes and test harnesses ...

### Post by QIII on 2010-09-23
There are really two components to this:  the language (which can be anything); and the more important skill of *problem solving*.

With regard to the language, you will need to have a solid understanding of the basics.  And I do mean SOLID.

But you will need to go beyond that.  Learn and relearn the basics until you have them cold.  Write small programs that use the basics.  Write them again using a slightly different metaphor.  

Then, get a slightly more advanced book or find a more advanced tutorial on the web.  Get that down cold.  Do some programming over and over.

Then an advanced book or tutorial.

You will know you have gotten somewhere when, in some language of your choice, you can sit down and solve some simple to moderate problems (after a bit of thought) almost by "muscle memory."  That's when you know the language is embedded in the deep-dark recesses of your brain and securely ensconced there.

(When you actually start dreaming in code, it's time to take the wife for a two week vacation to the Bahamas.)

Problem solving is a more difficult beast.  This one sometimes takes years.  For this, I would recommend some sort of study in Math or Natural Sciences.  Not necessarily classes, but something that will give you a grasp on how to think critically.  Another thing would be Logic.  Learn to think in terms of what is logically valid and what is logically fallacious.  Third (and people look at me funny when I say this) study some modern Philosophy.  Philosophy informs and enlightens every endeavor we humans undertake.

Finally, learn some history about great thinkers of Western Society from ancient Greece to the present.  (It wouldn't hurt to study Egyptian thought and Arab thought either.  Remember that the Arabs (and some Irish scribes) kept our Western culture alive while Europe was wallowing in the Dark Ages.)

Language is an expression of what you think.  The ability to think is what is important.  You need to be able to break down complex problems into discrete parts that are easily handled one at a time while still being contextually related to the larger problem.

As for books:  Again, there are sections dedicated to this in your Public Library and your local bookstore.  The library has the advantage of minimal cost and you can run through as many books as you want.

Edit:  Here are some Software Engineering titles from Powells.com.  You may be able find these locally.  Notice that some of them have titles that suggest Philosophy, great thinkers, Science...

[http://www.powells.com/s?kw=software+engineering](http://www.powells.com/s?kw=software+engineering)

> **pont.andersson said:**

Your last issue raises an issue that hasn't been mentioned: To get "further than the basics," as you put it, you'll have to start learning more about specific topics. The knowledge and skills that will help you write a word processor are very different from the knowledge and skills that will help you write a game, to pick just two categories. There are books on many different programming topics. Some of these are applicable to a wide array of programming topics, but others are very specific to particular types of applications.

Thus, you may want to think about what types of programs you want to write, and then study with that in mind. If necessary, ask for advice, but be sure to specify your goals in terms of what you want to write. The advice you've gotten so far is, I suspect, vague and unsatisfactory in part because your question is vague on this score.

One more point: You can dig into the source code of most Linux programs to learn how it's done. Try adding a feature, or just tweak something, even if it's not a useful change. That'll help you see how other programmers do things. Of course, sometimes the examples aren't good ones, but if you look at several programs that are of interest to you, I'm sure you'll learn a lot.
