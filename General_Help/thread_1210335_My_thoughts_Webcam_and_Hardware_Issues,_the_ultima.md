### Post by t0p on 2009-07-27
Yes, webcam support is poor in ubuntu.  And I often see users asking here for info on how to use their cam with their favourite IM program, so I know it is a sorely-needed function.

Thing is, posting about your problem here is not really the best way to have your words heard by the right person to fix it.  I'm not saying you shouldn't bother posting about it in these forums - this is the best place to get community discussions off the ground.  But you really need to look for the place to make *Feature Requests*.  I think there's a forum on this site that's dedicated to feature requests, but you'll have to check the forum list at the bottom of the page to be sure.

Another good place to go to get your voice heard by the right people is the app developer's site.  For instance, a google search for "pidgin development" will turn up such webpages as [developer.pidgin.im]("http://developer.pidgin.im/"), which has links to mailing lists and community pages.  And that's just one google result - there are [1,379,999 more]("http://www.google.co.uk/search?hl=en&q=pidgin+development&btnG=Google+Search&meta=&aq=o&oq=")!

In brief: this forum is not the wrong place to post your views on this - but nor is it the *only* place to post.  Let the devs know you want webcam support.  And tell everyone else to do the same.  When the devs know there's a huge demand for the feature, maybe they'll sort it out.  And maybe they won't... but you won't know til you've tried! :p

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2009-07-27
> **NonStop said:**
> I am posting it here because I have only been really playing around with Ubuntu, but I definitely believe the biggest issue with Linux OS's is the lack of webcam support (none for YIM, and only some programs support it for MSN).

That is a big issue. Not so much webcam support (there's a standard driver for webcams now called UVC, that will solve these problems once all new devices are UVC-compatible) but with video chat on ordinary IM networks.

> In addition, I think some of the hardware difficulties (for example some modem support requires quite a lot of work to get going)

Modem use is dying out - you might as well criticize Ubuntu for not shipping with an enabled "floppy" module. Try a wireless broadband modem some time, you'll be impressed :-)

> deters users, as well as some of the initial installation steps, for example the work that one has to do in order to install Ubuntu alongside Windows.

I'm not sure about that, really; you tell the installer to resize your existing partition to create free space. The installer does the rest. It's no different to installing dual-boot Windows.

> The lack of "Windows language", I think also scares some users when installing for the first time (would be cool to have Windows terms in brackets here and there).

Lack of Windows language is a bit of an acid test. If they can't get past their drives being called "64 GiB volume (/dev/sda)" rather than "C:\", I don't know how they will manage any operating system other than Windows. I can't really think of many examples where the terminology is different, and where it is, it's mostly because the concept doesn't exist on Windows (like repositories) or where the name itself is Windows-specific (like ".EXE files")

Remember, Unix was here before Windows. If any operating system should change the way it names things, it's Windows! :-)
