### Post by Mike_Walsh on 2014-09-13
Hallo, Sarah.

I'm not exactly an experienced 'user' (only been on Linux since May), but I think I can answer this one for you.

To take your points in order:-

When you 'suspend' your computer, what the system does is akin to a state of 'suspended animation' (hence the name); the computer will shut down ALL running processes, but maintain the information in RAM memory with just a trickle of power; 1-2 volts is all it takes. When you turn back on (which you can do by hitting ANY key on the keyboard), you're instantly back up-and-running, without having to go through boot-up again. It's like a 'snapshot' at that point in time. If you have 'lock screen on suspend/hibernate' set, just enter your password again.

As for your second point; If, like me, you're using a desktop set-up, the easiest way would just be to hit the power button on the monitor, and turn IT off; I know a few people who do this. If you're using a laptop, I'm not so certain how that would work; you might want to keep this thread open, and see if anybody a wee bit more experienced with laptop usage responds...

Hope that helps.
