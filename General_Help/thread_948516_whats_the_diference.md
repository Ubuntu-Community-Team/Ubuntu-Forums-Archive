### Post by perspectoff on 2008-10-22
My 2 cents:

Kubuntu (KDE desktop) is made to look and feel a little more like Micro$oft Windows and is a much easier transition for users accustomed to Windows.

The Ubuntu (Gnome) interface is more Apple Macintosh inspired.

You will hear that the two are interchangeable and both Gnome and KDE programs will run on your system, but this isn't exactly true. If you attempt to run KDE and Gnome desktops simultaneously, you will get a lot of conflicts. Having tried it a lot, I don't recommend it at all.

KDE (Kubuntu) is more customizable, and there have usually been more scientific and specialized programs available for it than in Gnome.

If you happen to like Gnome, stick with it. There is simplicity in the desktop, but it is rather difficult to customize.

Xubuntu is barely usable, and was created to be fast and slimmed down for low processing power/memory systems. But with 4 Gb USB sticks now for around $50, you don't need Xubuntu. Give it a miss.

Edubuntu has some customizations that supposedly appeal to grade-school kids -- some odd drawing programs and math programs that are pretty ridiculous. It is a dead distribution, and the few interesting programs can be installed individually in Kubuntu. Don't bother with this one.

Gobuntu is loaded with Google products. Essentially, your computer will belong to Google, which is like the Matrix. Don't do it. Remember, every Google program reports back to Google. Your privacy will be non-existent. (Resistance is futile?) 

Mythbuntu is an attempt at a Media Center version of Ubuntu (based on the MythTV PVR program), and is not terrible. But I don't like it, and it is still rudimentary to my taste. I think sticking with Kubuntu and then just adding the MythTV package is better than Mythbuntu.

I have had Debian, Ubuntu, Kubuntu, Xubuntu, MythTV, and LinuxMCE (which is based on Kubuntu) on a few computers using the Dapper, Edgy, Feisty, Gutsy, and Hardy versions (and the last 3 Debian versions) over the past 3 years.

I now only use two: Kubuntu Hardy and LinuxMCE (which is based on Kubuntu Feisty). 

The reason is that Kubuntu is much easier for Windows users (who are the majority of users transitioning to Linux). Hardy has been the first version that has been stable (for me) as far as networking, wireless, USB and Bluetooth, and printing.

LinuxMCE can't be duplicated (it is such a comprehensive and unique multimedia and home automation control system), but is for power users who like to fiddle.

So, for a new user, my vote is Kubuntu Hardy, without adding the Gnome desktop (which can cause conflicts with KDE).
