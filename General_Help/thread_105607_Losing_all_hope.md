### Post by arctic on 2005-12-18
Yes, comments:

First of all, many, many users went through that kind of "midlife crisis" you experience now. I needed some six months before I felt halfway comfy in Linux. So take your time, no one rushes you, no one will hate you for using Windows. The truth is that there are some apps that don't have equall powerful Linux alternatives. But other apps are better in Linux than in Windows, so it depends on which apps you need. The sad thing is: the less people stick to Linux, the less likely it will be that a company will release a Linux version of its apps. But the more people continue using Linux, the bigger the chance of a considerable market-share increase and thus more "respect" and apps for Linux (e.g. by Adobe).

I bet that you only scratched the surface of the software available in Linux. After all, there are more than 15.000 packages in the repositories. Browsing through them takes some time. Take your time. ;)

You consider yourself a power user. Many people who came from Windows felt that way. But I assure you that 90% of the self-proclaimed power-users or geeks coming from windows will NOT be power users when they get into Linux. It is a whole new world that needs to be explored. Everything is DIFFERENT. Now think of the time you needed to "master" Windows (in reality, no one can master Windows, not even Gates. :P) and compare it to the time you spent on Linux. Quite different, eh? If you've spent maybe five years in Linux and still say "It's unusable for me", then you have every right to say that. But not after three months. At least that's my point of view.

About your speed issues: A general question are your system specs. RAM? Processor?, RPM of hdd? You can get almost the same functionality you get in Gnome, using e.g. Fluxbox or FVWM2. But configuring it to do all those marvellous things you want will take some time and I guess you are not up to that task yet. But you should continue to learn by doing things, breaking things, starting all over. The best way to learn everything about a system is to break it, repair it and break it again until you are really unable to fix the damage done to the system. But few are willing to go on that long and hard journey.

So: take a deep breath, use whatever you feel is better suited for various tasks and continue to learn, learn, learn. :cool:

### Post by poptones on 2005-12-18
It's funny because Hollywood has been moving away from teh Mac for years now. if you go to a production house you'll see lots of machines running various versions of linux, several running osx and even many running windows.

I don't see anything unintuitive about gimp. I remember when I was learning to use photoshop what a pain in the butt it was; gimp is little different except it's similar enough you should be able to apply the basics from photoshop and not have to relearn everything from the ground up.

As a person who does graphic art, "plays" with music (I've built my own synth and I love music sequencer type programs as well as sound editing) and who has (although not recently) produced video (and not just three transition wedding video type stuff but mtv style music videos) I can assure you there is no lack of power or features here - you simply have to learn how to access them.

You don't "download" widgets to do ten thousand different things because it's already there. If there are tasks you do a lot then you make scripts of your own to make them easier and quicker - this is how you make a tool that fits your hand. 

Everything you can do in windows with Premeire, Photoshop, and Vegas Pro can be done in linux and can even be done *from the command line*. "Visual" video editors like Premeire are more rare in linux, but the funny thing is that tool really isn't suited to *sophisticated productions* anyway - it's far too limited to be of much use past the "wedding album" type stuff. Production houses use cut sheets and time code and production notes - all text based stuff - and then render edits and screen them and then edit and screen some more. This stuff you can do fantastically well in linux - the same tool many folks use just to watch videos (mplayer) is also an incredibly powerful general purpose video editing tool. It cannot do everything but if you combine its capabilities with imagemagick you can  script, frame by frame, virtually any effect. If you don't want to do it frame by frame then you use bash to build a library of effects the way you want them and "automate" the task.

The power is there, it's just not bottled and labeled and stuck on the shelf for you. It's admittedly a different mindset but it's very rewarding if you will stick it out. 

Linux is the stereotypical hot librarian: "she" is stand offish, rude at times, fairly plain looking and conservative in appearance. She appears cold and can be extremely hard to "get to know." But if you stick it out and learn to push the right buttons it's a ride like you've never experienced and it will completely spoil you, for anyone else, ever again.
