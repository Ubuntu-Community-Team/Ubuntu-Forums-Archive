### Post by flopwich on 2010-10-30
I was trying to figure out how to generate compressed files in zip format and searched on here.  The search produced a list of forum entries on the topic, but all of the instructions were on how to do it in terminal, how to download obscure programs and install them from terminal, then run them from terminal, with all these arcane sets of switches and parameters.  Eesh.

I'm curious why so little of the help offered on here provides instructions on how to do things in Gnome.  It comes with Ubuntu, after all.  In the case of zipping files, all you have to do is to go to the File Manager, find the file(s), select it or them, right-click on it or them, and select Compress and file type zip.  It's so simple.

After crawling through broken glass to get the job done, and normally screwing something up so it's not quite right once I get it going, I figure out how to do it through the GUI and find out it takes a fraction of the effort.

So, why aren't these forums more oriented toward the graphical interface?  Is it just geek pride?

### Post by peculiar penguin on 2010-10-30
Also consider that textual description of GUI interaction can be fairly ambiguous, especially with people who simply don't know the basic terminology (just what or where is "File Manager", for example? Not in any of my menus. Context menu, side pane, location bar, toolbar, properties dialog,...?). So you can either end up going into really detailed descriptions and type out a short novel, or end up having to post multiple screenshots or maybe even video tutorials. Having them open a terminal and enter (or maybe even just copy&paste) a command is simpler than that and gets the job done. Figuring out the supposedly easier GUI way is left as an exercise to the reader (and they'll even get help for that if they ask, it just doesn't seem to be the default action here) :P

### Post by snowpine on 2010-10-30
If I am using Kubuntu/Xubuntu/Lubuntu/etc, I have a different file manager and the GUI instructions might be different. If I speak a different language, the GUI option is not called "Compress" but something in my native tongue. If I am left-handed, then a right-click for you is a left-click for me. And so on; hopefully you see where I'm going with this.

Terminal commands are universal and unambiguous on any Linux system, and that is why we use them so much on this forum. If I were sitting at your desk looking over your shoulder, maybe I could give you GUI instructions, but since this is a text-based forum, it is less confusing for me to tell you "type these three words in the terminal."

Also remember these forums are a two-way street: if you feel you have a good GUI solution to a problem, you are welcome to share your solution with other users. :)

### Post by drs305 on 2010-10-30
Another reason is that the GUI interfaces are different. We deal with Gnome, KDE, xfce, etc and some of us don't know our way around the different interfaces. The terminal/command line is universal to all of them.

I think our goal should be to use GUI when appropriate. In some of my guides, especially in the Ubuntu community docs which allow graphics, I try to include GUI methods. 

But for the reasons mentioned - speed, accuracy, universality - the command line is often a better method of troubleshooting/problem solving.

Which leads us to the next topic - explaining what a command does when you are providing help.  But that's another (recurring) thread topic...
