### Post by Daremo_06 on 2009-04-16
So I have been on Ubuntu around a month now, maybe slightly longer and after playing around with the desktop configs, I had some more questions.

I have seen a great many themes with docks such as cairo-dock or awn, and I am running a dock now.  One thing I miss about having the standard application bar at the bottom though is when something changes in that application, it highlights or flashes.  Also, you can have multiple instances of applications open and chose which one you want to look at (ie multiple web pages opened at the same time).  The docks I have tried, while making starting commonly used items very easy, don't seem to inform you when say someone sends you an instant message on pidgin.  Is there something I am missing in the setup of these themes that gives you that functionality.  Or is there a tradeoff between the dock and the traditional bar?

Thanks!

### Post by SkyIsFalling on 2009-04-16
> **Daremo_06 said:**
> Also, you can have multiple instances of applications open and chose which one you want to look at (ie multiple web pages opened at the same time).

The Window List deskbar applet lets you group by application, I think this is the behaviour you're looking for?

If you browse multiple webpages using the tabs feature in Firefox, you won't need to use the deskbar for this :)

> **Daremo_06 said:**
> The docks I have tried, while making starting commonly used items very easy, don't seem to inform you when say someone sends you an instant message on pidgin.

<= Intrepid, notifications were a mix between dialogs, "flashing" windows and the notification area (near the clock). In Jaunty, there's been somewhat of a [push]("http://www.markshuttleworth.com/archives/265") to concentrate notifications in and around the notification area.

Pidgin has always allowed you get notified of new messages via the notification area though, see Preferences -> Interface.

> **Daremo_06 said:**
> Is there something I am missing in the setup of these themes that gives you that functionality.  Or is there a tradeoff between the dock and the traditional bar?

I think rather than a trade-off, this is simply a different design philosophy. OSX's dock and the Windows 7 taskbar blur the lines between launcher and window manager, GNOME (so far) does not. IMHO the former encourages sloppy window management behaviour by the user and is better avoided, though I'm sure many Mac users would disagree :)
