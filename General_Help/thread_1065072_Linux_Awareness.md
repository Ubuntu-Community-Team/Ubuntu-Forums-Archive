### Post by mb_webguy on 2009-02-09
In my experience, computer science clubs are great places to start looking for interest in Linux, as well as support for spreading awareness of Linux to the wider student population.  If you don't have a CS club at your school, you might consider starting one.  Student government associations often provide funds for student clubs and activities, which you could use to fund your Linux awareness program.

The official Ubuntu CDs available through ShipIt would certainly make Linux an attractive choice for a lot of people, since they look very professional.  However, while they aren't terribly expensive, anything is expensive if you don't have money to spare.  A cheaper solution (but possibly a less attractive one from the perspective of the people who receive them) is to buy a spindle of CD-Rs, which you can get for very reasonable prices on eBay, and burn a bunch of LiveCDs yourself.  If you can get sufficient funding for your efforts (such as through an SGA-sponsored CS club), then you could opt to purchase some official CDs.

You could also print out some pamphlets informing people of the advantages -- as well as the disadvantages -- of Linux, which you could hand out with the CDs.  The important thing here is to *be honest*.  Don't tell people that Linux is a free version of Windows.  Let them know up front that while some of the software they currently use on Windows is available for Linux, in general Linux uses different applications to do the same job -- and you might want to include a short list of Linux equivalents of Windows software.

When telling people about Linux, try to only discuss the issues that are meaningful to them.  Most Windows users don't have an opinion on open-source versus proprietary software, so the fact that Linux and much of the software for it is open-source is a distraction rather than a selling point.  The fact that software is free and easy to install by simply selecting it from a list, however, might arouse their interest.  Likewise, the ability to customize Linux and the desktop environment however you like isn't necessarily a selling point to everyone.  Some people want conformity when it comes to using their computers, and putting too much stress on Linux's flexibility might actually scare them off.

Another thing to think about is your terminology.  People who aren't aware of the Linux world don't know anything about "distributions", and tend to get information overload and lose interest if you try to explain it to them.  If you're going to use Ubuntu to introduce people to Linux, say "Ubuntu" rather than "Linux".  Once people are introduced to Ubuntu (or whatever distribution you use), they will start to grasp the concept of distributions, and that they're actually using the Linux operationg system.  (And please don't go into the Linux vs. GNU/Linux issue, since even long-time Linux users can't agree on that.)

Finally, the most important thing when getting people interested in Linux is your follow-through.  Don't just hand them a LiveCD and think you're done.  Be available to help them try and/or install Linux, or at the very least point them to other sources of support if you're not able to provide it yourself.  This is where a CS club can be extremely handy.  Such a club could provide online troubleshooting, tutoring, or any number of other forms of support for a Linux awareness program.
