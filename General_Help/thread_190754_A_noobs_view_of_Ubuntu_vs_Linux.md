Intuitive does not "totally" depend on your experience. Despite cultural differences, we are all wired in very similar ways, both physically and mentally. For example, those of us with two working eyes see a visual field that is wider horizontally, which is why monitors are generally wider horizontally than they are tall. A keyboard designed for five hands would not be intuitive, because most of us have two hands.

Each of us has a mammalian brain, which can store and retrieve certain kinds of information more easily than others.

### Post by exclipy on 2006-06-07
Someone I know, who started their first year of uni this year and had hardly used a computer before, was given a computer with Fedora Core 4 installed on it.  I was surprised to find that he was quite content with it, and didn't want to just pirate a copy of Windows so he could be more easily assisted.

After all, he could browse the web, type up his assignments and play his music CDs quite easily.  Actually, at first, he didn't want to type up his assignments with it because of incompatibility with MS Office, but when I showed him that OOo could save to MS formats, he was quite amazed.  He (and I) were also quite amazed that it found the network printer automatically and printed to it the first time he tried, though because the interfaces for printing in OOo and Firefox were inconsistent, he was a little confused when sometimes, it worked and sometimes, it didn't.

However, even in this day and age, you can't expect Linux users to never have to encounter the command line.  My friend wanted to connect to the DirectConnect network, a filesharing network used by almost everyone at uni.  He found instructions for setting up LinuxDC++ (which is currently only available through CVS) which basically said: "Type these commands in:"...  (you'll also find this pattern in almost all the Howtos on this forum) The commands basically checked out the latest CVS source, compiled it and installed it.  So where was he supposed to type this in?  How did he know that it worked, or how to fix any errors?  How was he supposed to know that he had to install cvs and build-essential?  He said to me, "See, I don't know how to do any of that stuff," and seemed to be a little more scared of computers than before. I replied, "Yeah, you don't really have to know this." but I don't know how true that statement is at the moment.

So in summary, yes, Linux is in some ways better than Windows for newbies, but it still needs to wean itself from the command line.  More applications and games available for Linux will also help, of course.

### Post by aysiu on 2006-06-07
[QUOTE=elamericano]Of course it does, multiple choice is inherently more intuitive than a blank command line. Of course, sometimes the interfaces you have to learn are the most powerful.

Or maybe it's just my mammalian brain. :lol:[/QUOTE] But intuitiveness is highly overrated. People do what they're used to, not necessarily what's intuitive.

If people are used to having a "show desktop" button on the bottom-middle of the screen, it wouldn't matter if it were "more intuitive" to have that button elsewhere--it would confuse the hell out of that user.

It may be more intuitive for me to point to my groin area and make gestures that indicate I need to urinate, but I'm used to speaking, so it's a lot easier for me to say "Excuse me. I have to go to the bathroom." A game of charades isn't my idea of fun when I have to pee.

Likewise, a wizard may be just fine for a task if you do that task only one or two times. If you have to that task every week or every day, it gets annoying... like a game of charades to tell someone you have to pee. For some tasks, it's easier to just issue a command, and it doesn't really matter if it's intuitive.

For example, not too long ago, if I wanted to fetch a file off the internet, I'd launch my browser, type the location in the location bar, and then wait for the download dialogue to pop up. Then I'd tell Firefox where to download the file. Once I learned the *wget* command, I could load up the terminal and then just type ```
wget http://www.nameofdomain.com/nameoffile
``` It may not be "intuitive," but it's a hell of a lot easier... and faster.
