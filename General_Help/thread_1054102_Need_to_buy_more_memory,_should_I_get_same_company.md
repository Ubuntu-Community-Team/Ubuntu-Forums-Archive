### Post by redilyn on 2009-02-02
Headroom just means how high you can push the front side bus before your ram can not handle the speed.

Okay, its been a long time since i've played with intel processors so i'm going to assume the front side bus is still quad pumped (fsb x4)

You have 800 ddr2 and your fsb is 200mhz. The only way to overclock an intel processor is to increase the fsb. Since you are already at the max rated speed of your ram you will not be able to go much higher past 200mhz before you start getting memory errors.

Now say you have the following:

You have 1066 and your fsb is 200mhz. You ram is capable of going as high as 266mhz (fuzzy math but you get the point) so you will be able to increase your fsb which will increase your processor speed much higher then you can with 800mhz ddr2.

How far can you push your proc past 200mhz fsb you ask? Well that depends on your motherboard, processor, power supply etc etc.

I hope this helps clear up some of the confusion.

### Post by redilyn on 2009-02-02
Yes, Apples to Apples 200mhz < 266mhz. The faster the fsb the more responsive the system is in general.

The thing is we are getting into something that you really need to do a lot of research on to make an informed decision.

For example, just because you buy ram capable of 266mhz fsb doesn't mean that you will be able to run 266mhz. I suspect you are running a 200mhz fsb right now and that is what your current ram is capable of. In order to increase the fsb you would have to overclock your processor at the same time.

Your processor speed is based on fsb X Processor multiplier (ie 200mhz fsb x 10 = 2GHZ processor speed).

So say you have a 2ghz processor and you want to try and push it to 2.2ghz. You would have to increase your fsb to 220mhz. Your current ram probably can't handle that speed but 1066mhz ram can.

Is this making any sense?

Oh, one other thing. The reason i strongly recommend the 1066 is that if you get a processor down the road that requires that speed you will have to toss the 800 ram and get 1066 anyways.

### Post by Gizenshya on 2009-02-02
there is a lot more to overclocking that applies but hasn't been mentioned.

what redilyn said about ram speed is true.. but only if your mobo doesn't have tha ability to 'unlock' the FSB and memory speeds.

to keep the numbers out of it..

say, you overclock your system 10%.  10% faster rpocessing, 10% faster RAM 10% faster FSB (north bridge).

and if everything can handle it, it will work fine.

now, lets assume your processor can overclock 50% (which yours might actually get close to).  RAM 20% and north bridge 25%.

if you try overclocking to 25%, it fails because RAM is the bottleneck.

Then (the vat majority of mobos that support overclocking support unlocking of RAM and FSB), if your mobo has an unlocking capability, depending on how its set up, you can set it as 667 mhz (if its originally 800mhz), then your RAM would be fine with that overclock, but any more and you would need to do something with the north bridge. (because 667*1.25= (about) 834 Mhz, which is less than a 20% overclock for your RAM).  then you could overclock quite a bit before your RAM would be your bottleneck.  Some mobos allow you to set RAM speed directly.

In my case, my northbridge is my bottleneck.  It supports up to 1600mhz, where my processor has a 1333 mhz FSB.  so thats about 20% increase.  the northbridge only goes up to about 1700 mhz max (overclocked), so I just keep it at 1600 mhz to be certainly stable.  and my RAM runs fine at 20% above 800mhz (960Mhz).  I've tested it at 1100 Mhz on another computer and it runs stable.

I highly doubt that there would be any noticeable increase in performance between 800mhz ram and 1066 (stock).  In games, where the GPU is not the bottleneck, there might be a slight increase, though it would probably only be noticeable in benchmarking tests.

If you do a lot of RAM-intensive things, like video-editing, there might also be a slight difference.

but thats only where the RAM is the bottleneck.  Which isn't often anyway.

Ohh, and I can't believe I forgot to mention this earlier... are you running 32-bit or 64-bit OS(s)?  any 32-bit OS can only handle/recognize ~3 GB of RAM.  So you'll have to limit the amount of RAM usage to under the limit.  64-bit OS's are good to go.

also.. any particular reason for those choices?  (ohh, and the wintech ram seems to be out of stock)  They don't seem to be very popular.

I've gotta say, though... you'd probably be much better off asking about this type thing in a forum specific to overclocking.  All the stuff that we're talking about happens before an OS boots up. the only thing that would affect your OS (ubuntu et al) would be whether the OS is 32-bit or 64-bit.  although... they'll likely try to pressure you into buying a whole bunch of junk you don't need lol
