### Post by mcduck on 2015-05-09
How simple / powerful tool you want? There are plenty of options, ranging from iMovie-style basic editors (try [OpenShot]("http://www.openshot.org/") or [Pitivi]("http://www.pitivi.org/") )all the way to professional stuff. (I use [Lightworks]("https://www.lwks.com/") for any bigger projects, and do my quick edit stuff, compositing and effects work in [Blender]("http://www.blender.org/"))

And of course simpler tools like [Avidemux]("http://fixounet.free.fr/avidemux/"), and command-line based stuff like ffmpeg/avconv can always be useful to have around, if not for actual editing but for encoding/decoding, very basic trimming  etc. tasks.

### Post by mattharris4 on 2015-05-11
If you are running Ubuntu or Edubuntu (and not Lubuntu, Xubuntu, Kubuntu, Kylin, etc.) the Ubuntu Software Center is installed on your computer by default.  A quick search with the term video editor comes up with a reasonably long list of options.  None have a price next to them so all are free to install (although if you like one and use it regularly it is recommended to make a $20 contribution to that program's project, it takes money to host the program, computers, keyboards, monitors, mice, printers, internet service, etc. to tweak and improve it and developers miscellaneous non computer related expenses in developing and improving it -- you should also make a similar contribution to Canonical if you can afford to for Ubuntu development expenses, I am actually due to make one myself).  I would take a look at the descriptions of those programs that catch your eye, ask about them by name here and after a couple of days for people to respond install whichever program(s) you want to try.

If you are running a Ubuntu-lite version like Lubuntu the OS will have its own software center.  I know in Lubuntu you can use its software center to install the full Ubuntu Software Center, I recommend doing so.  The vast majority of programs that run in Ubuntu will run in Lubuntu, Xubuntu, etc. if you have sufficient processor power and RAM to do so.  With Ubuntu or Edubuntu you should run at least 3GB of RAM, with Lubuntu, Xubuntu, etc. at least 1.5GB RAM to even attempt video editing.  

As for CPU power I think a Core 2 or better should work (no Celerons, Atoms, older Pentiums from Intel or E-1s from AMD), an i3 or better would be preferred (or the AMD equivalent).  Check the Passmark score of your CPU (Google is your friend here), if it scores higher than about 1250 it should work, of course 2000 plus would be better especially if you plan to edit videos professionally.

If you need a new computer for this you can buy an i3 laptop computer (Core 2's are no longer sold new and if you are going to buy new you may as well spend the extra $50 and get the i3) from Dell for less than $500 at regular price (earlier today -- 11 May 2015 -- they had a laptop with an i3 for $330 with a website discount -- search Google as I forgot which site had the link -- but they may have sold out of them by now anyway) although a desktop will work just as well.  Dell (and Lenovo) computers usually take a Ubuntu install quite easily but search for instructions on what to disable in the UEFI before attempting an install.  Avoid new HP computers like the plague, installing any Linux OS on them is more painful than having your teeth pulled without Novocain.  Of course a refurb with a Core 2 or better is OK as well, Tiger Direct and Newegg have a wide selection and the dreaded HP UEFI is not a consideration as most of them come with a BIOS and Win 7 instead of UEFI and Win 8.1.  A good refurb desktop is about $140-$220 plus shipping.  Check the Passmark score of whatever processor is in the computers you are considering (even for a Core 2, the ones I checked were OK but a early one without the needed computing power may slip through if you don't), 1250 is the bare minimum for resource-intensive tasks like surfing the web with 5-7 tabs open while playing music, audio or video editing, running a virtual-box, etc.
