### Post by SeanBlader on 2009-04-02
This is something that's been bugging me for a while with Gnome. Why is there a menu bar at the top of the screen for the OS and then there's a second menu bar for applications in their respective window?

Apple has one and it combines them in the top of the screen. Windows has one and it pretty much combines it in the application space. But Gnome has both, which seems like it's potentially confusing, and also uses up more screen real estate than is absolutely necessary. I'm not saying I'm confused by the interface, but it does bother me that when I have firefox open I have to dedicate 2 rows of space to menu bars rather than content area.

At one point I had my lower panel to autohide, and then to also only be as wide as the contents of it's application buttons, that would give more vertical space to the application, but then it made changing to different apps more effort. I'd hide the top panel, but it's used so generically as a launcher and monitoring panel that it would be hard to do without hiding something else that's likely necessary.

So what's up with Gnome's twin panel view? Anyone know the history of why it's like that?

### Post by CatKiller on 2009-04-02
All of the elements of either panel (menu, window list, notification area, clock, and so on) are simply panel applets. If you don't want both panels, pick whether you would like a top panel or a bottom panel and move all the applets to that panel. Then delete the panel that you don't want.

There are other alternatives to the panel as kanikil mentioned (avant-window-navigator is another popular choice), but it sounds like you would really just prefer to have one panel.

### Post by mb_webguy on 2009-04-02
At the left side of the program tray on the bottom panel, you'll notice there's a handle.  Right-click on this and uncheck "Lock To Panel" if necessary.  Then you can simply drag it to the top panel.  Or you could drag everything on the top panel to the bottom panel.

Gnome is *extremely* configurable.  I set up the desktop in the attached composited screenshot to be as Windows-like as I could within a couple of minutes.  To save space, it has a Windows-style menu (i.e. main menu) instead of the menu bar used by Ubuntu by default.  It also has a few launchers in the style of the Quick Launch toolbar.  Then comes the taskbar (i.e. window list), system tray (i.e. notification area), and clock.  It's not missing anything you'd find on the Windows taskbar, and the only addition is the workspace switcher applet.
