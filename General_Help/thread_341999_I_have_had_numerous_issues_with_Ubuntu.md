### Post by cmost on 2007-01-19
Though your complaints are a bit vague, it sounds as though you expect everything to "just work".  Since you mentioned streaming video specifically, let me reccommend that the first thing you should do after a clean installation is run Automatix2 (home page:  [http://www.getautomatix.com/)](http://www.getautomatix.com/)).  If you don't want to go that route, but still want everything setup pretty well after initial install, then consider Linux Mint 2.1 (a.k.a "Bea")  This is essentially Ubuntu Edgy except with a lot of proprietary video and audio codecs and plugins installed by default.  It also includes a nifty script, Envy, to help install your proprietary video card drivers (both nvidia and ATI in the latest version.)  You can download an ISO for Linux Mint here:  [http://lm.ltv2.nutime.de/)](http://lm.ltv2.nutime.de/)).  Finally, please don't give up on Ubuntu (and Linux) just yet.  You're experiencing some growing pains.  Once you get everything working you're going to love it - I promise!  You won't go back to Windows again.  Please report back and don't be afraid to ask for further assistance.  The forums are a vast and invaluable resource.  Take advantage of it.

### Post by Henry Rayker on 2007-01-19
> **presbp said:**
> One of the big points that made me want to try Ubuntu was the thing that everything just worked.. 

I'm not sure where you got that, but it probably wasn't here (or any other official source). The problems you've addressed have been dealt with to death on these forums. Games won't work due to the fact that they are made for Windows with DirectX...we don't have DirectX, so we suffer. Streaming different file types requires that you have that particular codec installed (just like in windows) and the reason they aren't installed by default is due to intellectual property issues.

You mentioned that you've been at this for a week and can't get your streaming camera and all sorts of whatnot up and running, but how long have you been using Windows? You have to give any new OS a "break-in time" and, the longer you've used the OS you expect to replace, the longer you'll have to spend with the replacement OS...I guess you could call it breaking bad habits, in this case.
