### Post by Herman on 2009-03-12
Ubuntu offers a choice of either stability or the latest and greatest, newest software.

Ubuntu has a six monthly release cycle and every fourth Ubuntu release is a 'LTS' (Long Term Support) release. The LTS releases are supported for three years, and they become very stable in a few weeks after their release date. Those are probably the best ones to go for if you need an operating system you can depend on and that every program will work in.
Hardy Heron 8.04 is the most recent LTS version of Ubuntu.

Intrepid Ibex features some great improvements over Hardy Heron, especially in the Xserver and boot loader, but I can't use it as my main operating system because one of my favorite programs, Kompozer, (for making web sites), crashes in Intrepid Ibex. UPDATE: Unless you use an alpha version of Kompozer 0.8, [How to use Kompozer 0.8 Alpha]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showpost.php?p=6930162&postcount=3").
If all your programs will work in Intrepid, then it's fine to use Intrepid.

Jaunty Jackalope will be better than Intrepid Ibex when it is released and has had at least two or three weeks to stabilize. 

Links: [Ubuntu 8.04 LTS]("http://www.ubuntu.com/news/ubuntu-8.04-lts-desktop") Hardy Heron & [Ubuntu 8.04.1 LTS]("http://blog.canonical.com/?p=15") Hardy Heron & [Ubuntu 8.10]("http://www.ubuntu.com/news/ubuntu-8.10-desktop") Intrepid Ibex.

For business, I'd stick with Hardy Heron if it can do all you need, but if I needed the more advanced Xserver for some reason, I'd advance to Intrepid. It's too soon for most people to try Jaunty Jackalope, but it has an even better Xserver. 
Sometimes you could be forced to go for the release with the best Xserver, if you happen to have a video card that earlier versions of Ubuntu don't function with very well. If part of your business is showing presentations on a projector or something like that maybe, the Xserver might be very important to you.

As for comparing the differences between Linux distros, You'll probably find that the programs and desktops are much the same.
Differences between distros are the way they install software, Ubuntu and Debian use apt-get, and there are other distros that use something called 'yum', or 'rpm', I'm not sure, but I suppose you get to like the package management system you get used to.
Then there is the Web Forums that support the distro, that's important. Most web forums are full of good, knowledgeable people who will try their best to help, but each web forums seems to have it's own prevailing 'personality' of it's own. You need to spend some time in each forum to see if you like it there or not. How long do you want to wait for an answer? When you do get an answer, do you expect a good quality answer or will any answer do? Do you like a detailed answer or do you prefer forums that keep it brief and to the point, (Maybe they'll just refer you to answer #8 in the FAQ or something like that, and not give you any personal attention at all). A little browsing through the web forum  of each distro you're considering might help you to make up your mind.

Regards, Herman :)
