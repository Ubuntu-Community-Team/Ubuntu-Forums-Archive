### Post by QIII on 2010-09-08
+1 for dual booting.

You need not go cold turkey.  Dual booting will give you a chance to poke around and get the feel of the thing while still leaving you the option to go back to the old Windows stand-by.

Many of us have gone this route and dual-booted (or multi-booted) for some amount of time before going Linux only.  Some stay with dual booting for any number of reasons -- a particularly necessary Windows app, for instance.  (Some, like myself, have used Linux exclusively for many years.  If I absolutely must use Windows -- say a client wants a Windows app -- I run Windows in a virtual machine.)

While dual booting, you will have the opportunity to install and try out the Linux alternatives for the things mentioned.  There are often many suitable Linux alternatives for any particular Windows app and you can choose among them for the one that fits your needs.

### Post by QIII on 2010-09-08
Three more things, as I have had a chance to think...

1.  Use the 64 bit version.  You will have access to all 4GB of your memory (less what is reserved by your video card, etc, if such is reserved).  You could use the 32 bit version with PAE enabled, but there is a performance penalty.   So, why do that?   You may hear all manner of FUD with regard to the 64 bit version.  It's all ancient history for the most part.  You will find a few 32 bit applications that won't work well, but much of that can be solved by installing the 32 bit libraries.  64 bit is pretty much the industry standard right now.  Your CPU supports it.  Are you old enough to remember those who held out with 16 bit when 32 bit became the norm?  (Or, if I may show my age, 8 bit when 16 bit became the norm...)

2.  Every time you boot Ubuntu (or any Linux distribution) repeat the following three times:  "Linux <> Windows".  

Sure, you have a mouse, a keyboard, a monitor.  Pretty things appear on the monitor.  You type with the keyboard and characters appear on the monitor.  You move the mouse and your cursor moves on the monitor.  You click stuff and things happen.  But if you expect Windows, you will be frustrated.  Your learning curve and sanity will be much improved if you divorce yourself from the expectation that Linux will be just a different flavor of Windows.  It's not.  Things are not where you have grown to expect them.  Tasks are often performed in a different manner than that to which you have become accustomed.  You might just get bold and want to use the command line interface (CLI) and live on the wild side.

3.  Will your experience be better?  We can't begin to tell you that.  You will have to make that decision after using Linux.  For some it turns out to be a distasteful experience.  Nothing wrong with that.  For many, many people it turns out to be a revelation, a release from the world of Redmond and a journey into a free and unconstrained land.

But take care.  You are going off the edge of the map.  Here there be monsters.  Ask us before you jump into the jaws of one!
