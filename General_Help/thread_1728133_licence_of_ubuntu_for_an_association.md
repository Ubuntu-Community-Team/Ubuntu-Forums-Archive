### Post by earthpigg on 2011-04-13
> **daxbau said:**
> thanks for your promt answers

am i allowed to charge some money for installing and helping to configure the system/mails etc?

yes - this activity is encouraged, and Canonical even has a certification scheme if you want to 'officially' be recognized as an ubuntu tech. the certification is certainly not required, but it may look nice on a resume if you want to do this for a living.

If you wanted to, you could even cell burned Ubuntu CD's. Check out e-bay and amazon.com, lots of folks do this.

pretty much the only limitation is that you cannot place additional limitations on your clients/customers that were not placed on you when you downloaded ubuntu. for example, you cannot ask your clients sign a contract agreeing not to make additional copies of ubuntu. if you sell an Ubuntu CD for $20, you may *not* prohibit someone from copying that *exact* CD and selling it for $10. doing so is the Ubuntu/Linux/[GPL]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gpl") version of software piracy.

Apple pirated open source software when they put [GNU Go]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gnu_Go") and [VLC]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VLC_media_player") in their app store, for example, because of exactly such a promise Apple requires of users - the app store terms of use require the user to promise not to make copies of software acquired from the app store. Because users must agree to those Apple terms of use prior to using the app store, putting certain open source software (specifically, GPL'd software) in the App Store is thus plain and simple Software Piracy. 

You must be willing to freely give to others what was freely given to you (freedoms, rights, and permissions), so to speak. If you (or Apple, or anyone) can't handle that, then you are prohibited from distributing the software at all. Not to be mean to you (or Apple, or anyone), but to prevent you (or Apple, or anyone) from being mean to others.

ethically, i would request that you be reasonable in your pricing and honest, unlike the person [described here]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1727844").

edit: so, as we can see, open source enthusiasts generally ***like*** copyright laws and ***dislike*** when people violate copyright. contrary to popular stereotype. :)
