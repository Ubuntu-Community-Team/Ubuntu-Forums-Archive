### Post by srs5694 on 2010-09-22
My understanding is that the LPIC exam, which is officially distribution-neutral, is the core of the UCP certification. [This LPI page](http://www.lpi.org/eng/certification/ubuntu_certified_professional) gives some details. The UCP certification, however, seems to require an additional exam (at a cost of another $155).

A book that covers the LPIC-1 material, such as the one you reference (which I wrote) will theoretically be sufficient to get you to pass the LPIC-1 portions of the UCP certification. I can't speak to other LPIC-1 books, but mine definitely does not explicitly cover the UCP-specific exam. (Actually, the last I heard, mine was the only LPIC-1 title that covers the latest version of the exam; but maybe there's something new I don't know about.) I don't know what's on the UPC-specific exam, so I don't know how much of that material overlaps what's on the other exams or might have been covered in my book just because I thought it was important or relevant. I recommend you research that issue and then either judge for yourself how well whatever book(s) you buy cover the exam objectives or find UCP-specific training materials.

Red Hat's RHCE certification is entirely separate from the LPIC certifications. There's bound to be a great deal of overlap in the material covered, but I've never taken the RHCE exam, so I can't comment on it. I'm not sure offhand what other distribution-specific certifications exist, if any.

### Post by srs5694 on 2010-09-23
You'll have to speak to a potential employer about what will "qualify" you to work for them.

That said, being vendor-neutral, the LPI certification is intended to provide evidence of competency in any mainstream Linux distribution, be it Ubuntu, Debian, Red Hat, SUSE, or others. Being vendor-neutral also means that there's no coverage of the things that make each distribution unique, such as the installation tools and distribution-specific GUI configuration tools. Red Hat is no different in this respect from any other distribution; the existence of the RHCE just means that Red Hat wants to provide their own certification.
