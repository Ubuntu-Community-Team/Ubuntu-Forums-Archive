Are you actually doing something that requires lots of raw CPU power? If yes, then overclocking is worth it. If not, then you won't benefit anything.

Overclocking a quad core (or any multicore CPU) is no different from overclocking a single core. The thing here is that modern computers already have far more computing power than what is required for normal computing tasks like watching movies, surfing the web, text editing etc..

But the days of overclocking are not near coming to end, as long as there are people who really use all the power their CPU's have, there will also be people who benefit from pushing their hardware's limits.

### Post by P4man on 2009-08-19
I wonder why you bought  a quad core to begin with ?
Anyway, overclocking today is no different than it was 10 years ago. You get a free (cpu) performance boost, at the expense of some risk when it comes to stability and data integrity and longlivety of your cpu. 

Overclocking a quad core or single core makes no real difference here. Its not because you have 4 cores that software makes use of it, or autmagically starts using extra cores when 1 core is bottlenecking you. Most apps are single threaded, meaning they use only 1 core no matter what. Some use 2 threads, precious few can use 4 or more. Having higher clockspeed will help every cpu bound app, while having 4 or 40 cores will do nothing for most apps, save some media encoding and 3D rendering stuff. Some games as well, though not many.

One thing has changed compared to 10 years ago though. Today even the cheapest cpu's and yesteryear's machines are more than fast enough for most tasks. I used to upgrade my computers about every year or so, and now I find myself with a 3 year old core2 duo, a 5 year old single core Athlon 64 and a 4? or so year old Pentium M based laptop. All these machines are plenty fast for my needs. Never before did I have such "outdated" hardware, and yet I feel no desire to upgrade to anything faster. Perhaps Ill even downgrade my laptop to  something smaller, lighter, with longer battery life.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2009-08-19
You'll probably burn a few watts more out of 90 to 120 watts of continuous power use with an overclock.  The CPU can take it.  It's the motherboard that will wearout faster.  The extra heat can warp the motherboard near the CPU causing issues down the road.

Unless you are working on a project with a deadline (say an animation movie), I would leave it at stock or perhaps +10% overclock.

### Post by SeanBlader on 2009-08-19
Faster is not always better. That's why netbooks have taken like 30% of the portable market, turns out portability and battery life are more important who would've thought. :rolleyes:

What you should do is watch your Processor speed with the "CPU Frequency Scaling Monitor" applet. If your CPU is always running at it's maximum speed then add the "System Monitor" applet next to it to see if you're actually running it at 100% usage. On my laptop, probably 90% of the processor time is idle while waiting for me to press the next character in an email message or a source file. On the other hand if I'm playing a game on my workstation at home, my processor is probably seeing higher usage, but a lot of that ferrying data to the video card. You might find through analysis that overclocking your processor is useless if several other things aren't keeping up. You could be bottlenecked by the memory bus, the cache, the hard drive, the video card, even audio causes problems sometimes to frame rates if the application hasn't loaded the sound you need off disk yet.

So play your game at normal CPU speed, and monitor the framerate in places where it seems to drop frames and get choppy, then overclock it and try the same thing. If the frame rate improves, then you know it was worth it.

Really if you're not doing audio, video, image processing, sharing processor cycles with Seti@Home, or playing games, then you are likely to not need overclocking.

Ooh, and if you are going to overclock, try out [this case]("http://www.extremetech.com/article2/0,2845,2332330,00.asp"), I noticed 20 degree temperature drops when I pulled my motherboard out of the CoolerMaster case. It looks industrial and weird, but it really has amazing thermal performance, and with my EVGA GTX280 board, it's really quiet. Previously I had an XFX 8800GT that was super noisy, but the GTX is really quiet in comparison, and it moves more pixels than god.
