If your getting XP, you won't be able to recognize over 3 gigs (technically 3.2) of ram, unless your using 64-bit Professional Edition, so no need to splurge on that. Its going to be hard to find any PC selling XP on it though, since Microsoft has officially ended selling XP for over a week now. The most important thing to look for if you are getting a retail pc is make sure you are getting a Discrete video card, not integrated, or if you absolutely can't find one in your price range, find one that has a PCI-Express x16 or PCI-Express X16 2.0 upgrade slot so you can buy your own and put it in there. I can't stress how important having your own graphics card is to having a good user experience, especially if she loves photos.

Also, AMD makes better CPUs for the lower/mid price range.

### Post by Xiamen on 2008-08-02
You'll have to get a 64-bit edition of vista in order to recognize over 3 gbs of ram. Now, I have built computers with both 32-bit and 64-bit Vista installed, the 64-bit for a gaming pc and 32-bit for my parents general use PC, and I would say that there are a lot more potential headaches using 64-bit, because many companies have been slow to include 64-bit drivers. Now since its been out for over a year, support is much better than before, but you still may run into hardware incompatibility issues, like finding a compatible wireless USB adapter (the issue I had). So, if this is for grandma, 32-bit may be a better choice, even though you won't get to have all the ram you want.

Another note on Ram, 5 gbs is a rather difficult setup for a pc because the way ram is designed. Ram is designed in doubles, so you have 1 gb, 2gb, 4gb, sticks. And in order for the Dual channel ram to be enabled, it has to be in a certain configuration when plugged into your motherboard, usually indicated by the color of the ram inserts. So lets say you have four slots. In order for dual channel to be enabled, you might need either 2 together in the first two slots and the other two empty, 2 together in the last two and the others empty, or all four filled. Now in order to get your dual channel setup for 5 gb, this means you need a 4 gb stick and a 1 gb stick, or 2 2gb sticks and 2 512 mb sticks in the motherboard. It may be difficult to find that kind of setup in retail PCs.

### Post by Xiamen on 2008-08-03
I'm looking through the circut city website, looking at your price range, and unfortunately most of the in store stuff doesn't look to promising. The "budget" gaming pc's, aka ones below 700, actually look pretty good, but you are going to basically be ordering online, which you said you didn't want to do. The problem with most of these retail pc's is the lack of graphics card. I know I sound like I'm beating a dead horse, but here's a situation I'll relay to you about Vista and retail pcs. 

 Around a year ago a friend of mine had a computer die on him in the middle of a project, so he walked into best buy and picked up the best looking gateway he could find for around 900 bucks. 3 gbs of ram, Intel 2.8 ghz core 2 duo, pretty solid seeming. Ever since then, he has had nothing but problems with vista. His biggest problem though, is that he feels its just way to slow. Games look like crap, websites load slow even on his fast network, and just using the desktop feels sluggish. Now around a month ago my parents were looking to finally get a new computer themselves and I built it for them. Now this computer cost much less, but I did build it myself. However, the specs seem lower. 2 gbs of ram, an AMD 4400 + dual core processor. Not bad but not fancy, and not as strong as my friends gateway. Yet my parents love vista. Its way faster than their old computer, and most importantly for this story, way faster than my friends gateway. They have had a great vista experience, and the reason is: I put around a 50 buck graphics card in their pc, whereas that 900 buck gateway had an integrated chip. Processors now are getting so good that most average use programs won't take advantage of all they can do, and ram is great for having a lot of aps open. But for anything visual, like photos, web pages, games, movies, that takes graphics, and a inexpensive slightly sub-gaming card from a year or so back makes a great addition to giving a good vista experience.

 Finding a pc retail with a graphics card may be tough, so what you may want to consider is ordering a made to order PC from the dell or hp website, and under upgrades put in a graphics card. I you could also buy a cheaper retail pc, say 599 instead of 699, making sure its upgradeable (many dell systems are), and then spend some extra cash on a low end graphics card. You may have a power supply issue, because most of the time retail pcs have very low powered power supply units. You probably won't have an issue with an under 100 graphics card retail, but I'm saying it could happen, and it seems like you want to do this as straightforward as possible.
