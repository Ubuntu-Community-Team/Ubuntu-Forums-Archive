I am Windwalker52 here in Ubuntu Forums and it is something totally different than any corporate infrastructure to which I have been in the past assimilated. There is a very good article available on demand about how the Ubuntu user is not the center of the universe and does not have a right to their money back if this does not work.
 About Windwalker52. I am an experienced Vietnam veteran and electronics technician with Navy training in hardware and sonar and I have an Associate's degree in Electronics from when transistors were considered a novelty and optional training but needed due to the number of transistor radios out there at the time.
 I started shortly after Vietnam with a Tandy TL/2 with a Radio Shack interface with lots of eye candy that I promptly crashed about a dozen times before I learned what not to do. I progressed into eventually XP from 3.1 after running a DOS based TriBBS BBS with a 3.1 sysop user's setup behind a D'bridge front door and then went under and into the Internet where I am now with a Ubuntu 10.04/XP dual boot. I do anything I can't rebuild easily on Ubuntu which leaves Hoyle's card games, Flight Simulator 10 Deluxe and a DVD viewer alongside Freelancer Discovery 4.85. That  is a MRPG that is relatively non-violent and is almost quasi-open source or at least out Microsoft's hands which has me encouraged about it's future and the future of the genre as a whole.
 Why I love Ubuntu? I think it is a relief to find something that actually works and treats me and expects me to be a competent human being with reasonable skills and education. In exchange I get 3,000 supported programs available on demand which run the gamut from radio communications training including Morse code to programming in C++.
 I have several questions and I probably will get flagged for this but quite briefly is there a single universal repository for all supported Ubuntu 10.04LTS programs?
 How do I get started with or without further college education in computer programming and what is the benefit?
 The third and final question is that I just signed up for the testing team for new releases and I plan to either build a separate partition or just a second operational machine which may come from existing parts and where is a good source for used computers that are reliable and cheap that cope well with 10.04? I looked at System76 and they are a bit too expensive at the moment.
 Write back either here or in the appropriate forum so I can hear other voices on these subjects. It really is a different mindset that requires a different mind to run Linux and Ubuntu is usually the first distribution that people including myself (except for Debian) (big mistake!) try out. Coming from XP can change you and if you don't change then you usually struggle with Linux. Talk to you soon.
