### Post by mandz on 2007-12-11
Hi all. 

I felt compelled to let you all know how damn impressed I am with how well Ubuntu is working on my aged, decrepit Dell D600. It used to run XP but I became slowly interested in linux and the concept of open source, and well now after a few months of learning and tinkering with Ubuntu (starting with Feisty) I'm amazed with what can be achieved with a combination of community graft and support, many hours of paid and unpaid help, and of course a bit of company backing too. 

I'd actually written off this laptop and was playing around with Ubuntu on my desktop. But installing Gusty on it, despite some issues with freezing with Firefox (which seems to have all but disappeared now, I'm not accomplished enough to put my finger on what could have fixed it but I haven't done anything to fix it myself...) its been pretty painless. Now on my 3 year old+ laptop with ancient 32mb Radeon 9000 graphics, I have a brand new, state-of-art OS with all the bells & whistles and functionality I could wish for. I mean, I have a 3D desktop (which beyond just being  something to impress non-linux users with is actually very useful), with effects which far surpass those on OSX or Vista, on a laptop that would have a heart attack as soon as the Vista CD was inserted! The cube effects and expo and all the rest of it work flawlessly. Not to mention the inclusion of a free and very capable office suit and all the packages that are included to make this an infinitely more productive OS than any other straight out of the box. And the ease of getting anything else you need as quickly and easily as you can say Synaptic. I really do feel like I've got a brand new laptop.

The main reason for this post (besides the fact that I've just finished my uni exams so I have a little too much time to spare) is to show my appreciation for  the effort of all those involved and to commend the push for affordable computing for the masses (and by that I include developing countries, such as the OLPCP). It sickens me to think of all the money people must have needlessly wasted upgrading hardware & software for vista, or buying a system with the insanely high spec it needs to run it. The capability that Ubuntu/linux seems to have squeezed out of my old laptop puts into stark focus just how poor Vista is. My Dad has bought 2 new laptops in the past few months for his business, both with Core Duo 2 processors running Vista with 1gb ram, which I assumed would fly (not knowing the beast that is Vista), but there was none of the freshly installed OS speed, just delays, endless loading and waiting. So sluggish. I no longer have to replace my laptop when software becomes defunct or outgrows the hardware, but morelike when I want to – when the hardware gives up, which is how it should be. 

Obviously there are many people who haven't been as fortunate in getting Gusty to work on their computers and there are things to iron out, but this is still a mightily impressive achievement. I also understand that such a community relies on the kind donation of time by many people. What is the best way to assist in the development of linux distros such as Ubuntu? I have no programming ability and not the greatest amount of spare time, but I don't want to just be a free-loader. That's not the idea and spirit of it all, its not just “free”. I'd like to hear how I can help, in whatever small way, and don't feel I can really criticise something for which I parted no money until I've put a little effort in myself.

For me, Ubuntu (as my only experience of linux) is freedom and empowerment. The refreshing feeling of not being constrained by what a big monopolistic monster wants or forces you to be, but what you want to be. Clearly I shouldn't get carried away, its only a computer after all, but its symbolic of what community projects in general can achieve, such as the Freecycle network. I know there are other agendas than simply “the greater good” for a lot of people involved (career development etc) but its heartening to know that human-like, community projects can survive and prosper.

Thanks Ubuntu and all those involved.
