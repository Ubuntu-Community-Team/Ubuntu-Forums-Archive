### Post by akashiraffee on 2010-02-16
You didn't say what kind of card it is.

If you do not have an accelerated 3D card with a GPU, I don't think this is worth pursuing.

Overclocking usually refers to the CPU, and it exploits the fact that low to mid priced processors are often identical to high end CPU's except for one thing: they tested lower at the factory.  That is to say, Intel will make a whole line of cores from what was actually manufactured as just one kind of thing.  I don't know the exact details, but the manufacturing process cannot precisely control the factors that determine the final frequency at which the processor runs.  They can only determine a range, and predict what percentage of the product will fall where within that range.

They test them at the end to determine their actual safe operating frequency and then catagorize them that way.  But the goal, obviously, is the top of the range.  So if you buy a 1.5Ghz version of a chip that has a 3.0Ghz chip, that chip was actually made to run at 3.0Ghz but deemed inferior in testing. That means you stand a good chance of being able to overclock it quite a bit.

On the other hand, overclocking the 3.0Ghz chip will be less rewarding.

### Post by OliW on 2010-02-16
Overclocking a laptop isn't wise.

It's usually possible but the cooling equipment inside your laptop (heatsinks and fans) was designed for normal use. Desktop cooling is altogether more flexible and upgradable - giving you a lot more room to manoeuvre.

Being new to overclocking is another reason not to overclock your laptop. Forgive me for saying so, but you'll likely have little idea of the physics involved to get a stable OC or the warning signs that your laptop is going to melt.
