### Post by lazyart on 2006-10-28
It's been almost three weeks now since I put in the live CD and decided to learn Linux.  I've read a lot, posted some questions, and have had fun answering questions... in fact that part is a bit of a game I play.  Search for unanswered posts and see if I can answer the question before someone else does. :)

Maybe it's because I set my expectations kind of low.  I know a lot of solutions are simply created by folks who got tired of waiting.  There may or may not be an app that does something that I was really used to in Windows, or a driver for a particular piece of hardware I might have.  But I installed Ubuntu on spare equipment so I can always fall back to get something done quick and dirty in Windows.

So, back to the title of this thread-- What to do when you start getting comfy with Ubuntu...

Just show up and view the new threads.  A few times a day if I possibly can.  I was amazed to find some things out--

My Brother 7820N multifunction printer works in Ubuntu.  And it does so faster than Windows.  Print and Scan, no problem.  Fax is an option but I haven't bothered with it.

My Dell Axim X30 PDA running WM2003SE can sync with Evolution, using Multisync and synCE plugin.  I duct-taped Thunderbird to use the calendar file in Evolution, and with ReminderFox I see my reminders there.  Not perfect, but good enough.  I can even browse and install software on to it!

Connect your phone and exchange data with BitPIM (ok, my Razr V3 isn't supported, but it was a good idea... can always bluetooth to/from PDA and then sync)

gdesklets-- similar to Yahoo! Widgets/Konfabulator
xzip -- play old school Infocom games in Linux

There's more.. I'm just amazed when I see new threads come up and I think "Wow, I can do this TOO!?!".

It's not everyone's solution... but things work pretty good for me. :)

### Post by Mike'sHardLinux on 2007-02-03
Hi Lazyart! ):P 

I am glad you like Ubuntu. The more people, the better! :grin: 

I  too have been comfy with Ubuntu for a while. I have used it as my main desktop for several months (at home AND at work!). I am still finding out new things - sometimes on my own, but more often in the forums.

My approach is similar to yours:

Have you found Linux replacements for ALL of your windows counterparts? If not, this is a place to keep looking. You won't always find an exact replacement. Often, you may discover a different way of doing something.  You may need to use 2 programs to do the job that 1 program did before. Or you may need a command line program. For example, I used to use HJSplit to join downloaded movies in windows. Now, I use avimerge (part of Transcode) on the commandline.

One of my interests is audio recording. :guitar:  I am still playing around with audio software in Linux. I haven't found the perfect Linux setup, yet. I still have things to learn about compiling the low-latency kernel and stuff like that. I haven't figured out how to consistently get a fully working setup.

Another area to learn is servers. In my opinion, Linux is the way to go for *almost* any server setup I would ever want to do. Our server at work is Linux powered. There's a lot to learn here: apache, mysqld, php, samba, vnc-server, openssh, etc....

If you are a developer or aspiring to be, Linux is awesome for developers. I have heard and read stories of developers switching from windows to Linux and say Linux really is a better (and often easier) OS in which  to develop.

I saved the fun stuff for last. GAMES!!! If you like games, it's fun to find out what's out there for us Linux gamers, and there really is a lot! You can get native Linux games. You can get some games to work in WINE. If you don't mind spending a bit of cash, you can get Cedega, and a few games work with Cedega. Also, there's the whole emulation scene. I have emulators for NES, SuperNintendo, Sega Genesis, Atari 2600, NeoGeo, Commodore64, Apple II, and old DOS games (and applications), all running in Linux. I am sure there are other machines that have Linux emulators, too.

Basically, try to figure out what you are missing, and go find out (or figure out) how to do it in Linux.

Is there anything you are missing? Or maybe something you want to do, but haven't yet?

I just had to add that I agree with AmandaKerik. Even when I am not using Linux to be productive :-k  , it's a blast just to tinker with stuff!
