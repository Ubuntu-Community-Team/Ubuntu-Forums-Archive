### Post by snowpine on 2010-11-15

Debian Stable uses older (2+ years) applications that have been thoroughly bug-tested and security-patched. It is very popular with server administrators who want a rock-solid OS.

### Post by VanillaMozilla on 2010-11-15
The "stable" vs. "unstable" terminology is not only amusing, but it's also a misnomer.  If you are using two-year-old software, that could mean that you are missing two years of bug fixes.  That's why people are so often advised to update to the latest version of something to fix problems.

Conversely, using an up-to-date version could mean that chimpanzees have vandalizing it, and it will take two years to fix the new problems.

Whichever you believe depends on your world view.  My own experience is that bug fixes really do improve programs and solve problems, and that Ubuntu is solid.  Remember, you can always get an old version of a program if you really want to, and with Ubuntu, you can also stick with the long-term-support versions.

It's also a [myth]("http://mdzlog.alcor.net/2009/03/08/ubuntu-is-based-on-debian-unstable/") that the Debian released version is "stable", while Ubuntu is "unstable".  They are *both* derived from Debian unstable, and both receive additional testing and work before release.  Which one has fewer errors, I have no idea, but such an conclusion should not be based on misuse of a word, or on a few anecdotes.
