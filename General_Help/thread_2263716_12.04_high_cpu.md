> **ovasilis74 said:**
> I haven't noticed anything about graphics driver and I'm not either concerned about Xorg.
The previous (rare, I had never seen **clamd** or **clamav** before in **top**) doesn't give the full image, as I can understand. Perhaps I say so bacause I don't understand what "us", "sy", "ni" etc means in **top**.

The third line of your top output indicates how the CPU time is being divided between the users (**us**) and the kernel (**sy**) by displaying the percentage of CPU time used for each.
The percentage of user jobs running at a lower priority (niceness - **ni**) is then listed. Idle mode (**id**) should be low if the load average is high, and vice versa. The percentage of jobs waiting (**wa**) for I/O is listed. Interrupts include the percentage of hardware (**hi**) vs. software interrupts (**si**). Steal time (**st**) is generally used with virtual machines, which has some of its idle CPU time taken for other uses.

From your screenshot it's visible that your load average is of 1.68. The load average determines how busy the system is. A load average of 1.00 per CPU indicates a fully subscribed, but not overloaded, system but when it goes above this value, it indicates that processes are competing for CPU time. When the load average is very high (like in your case) it generally means that the system is having a problem, such as a runaway process or a process in a non-responding state.

### Post by slickymaster on 2015-02-03
They can occur for numerous reasons.

Orphaned processes can occur when software applications are not exited properly, i.e. applications terminate abnormally or unexpectedly and as a result they clog up the process table making the system eventually unusable.
Runaway processes are especially problematic since they haven't exited abnormally, but they take up CPU cycles and other system resources (memory, disk space) which can result in sluggish performance of the computer.
