### Post by Alterax on 2009-05-08
Well, Wikipedia shows an apparent lack of knowledge on the theoretical abilities of woodchucks to chuck wood...aside from that, pretty much, yeah.

Unix was an operating system built in the 1960s.  It spread quite a bit, but each vendor had their own version of it.  There are still versions used, like Berkely Standard Distribution, Solaris, and AIX.  Mac OS X is even considered a Unix-based system.

Thing is, Unix got to be very expensive, and would only work on certain hardware.  Which hardware depended on the vendor.

Because of prohibitive costs and a desire for portability, Richard Stallman started the GNU project, with the aim of getting a working alternative that was similar to Unix.  They did develop the bulk of what was needed, but they were missing the kernel.  They are working on the HURD kernel, but still haven't managed a viable version of it.

Linus Torvalds, on the other hand came up with a kernel, with a slightly different design than Hurd.  He based it on Minix (a Unix knockoff), and originally developed it so that he could learn how a kernel works.  He wanted to call it "Freex", but people using it called it "Linux", which is a conjunction of "Linus's Minix" or "Linus's Unix."  Combining the GNU tools with the Linux kernel, we get the GNU/Linux operating system, which is usually abbreviated as Linux.  Because both GNU and the Linux kernel were based on the Unix design, they are called Unix-Like.  

Now we move on to Debian.  Debian is Unix-like, as it is a GNU/Linux operating system.  It is a completely community-sponsored distribution.  Novell is the company that backs Suse, Red Hat is the company that backs Red Hat.  There's no corporate sponsorship for Debian.

Since Debian is a nearly pure free operating system, it is used as the basis for other distributions.  They grab a snapshot of Debian's testing or unstable branch, and build their own O.S. from it.  So you get Debian-based operating systems, such as PuppyLinux, Knoppix, Ubuntu, and Linux Mint (which is based on Ubuntu).

I hope that answers your questions--and that I didn't butcher it too much!  There's a lot of history here, and it's very fascinating...I encourage you to look into it!
