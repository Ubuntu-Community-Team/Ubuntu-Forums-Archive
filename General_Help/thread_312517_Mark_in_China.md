### Post by FrankVdb on 2006-12-04
I'm having a lot of trouble lately with Ubuntu and Kubuntu and I've spent several miserable, totally non-productive hours on my laptop configuring rather simple stuff without success. One example: I added a panel with self-made icons to my first Ubuntu desktop on my laptop and the result was... a broken Gnome desktop. Logging in was no longer possible, each time the X server would crash and bring me back to the login screen, which I started to hate thoroughly. Reconfiguring didn't help.
Then I tried to go back to Windows 2000 but since I didn't know how to remove GRUB, I wasn't able to get the Windows 2000 installation booted. So I installed Kubuntu. Had to spend several hours once again to get the damned system configured. That's roughly the time I needed to get my wireless connection working through ndiswrapper. Although I had found a native driver for the wireless Intel card, I just couldn't get it to work. 
As to my self-made icons: I just don't dare to add them to my KDE desktop, afraid as I am to break my desktop.
Now I'm trying to add other languages to Kubuntu. I managed to make German and Chinese available to my Dutch default installation (once again thanks to this forum, I owe you dearly, guys), but KDE refuses to add Chinese when I click "Taal toevoegen" (Adding a language). It will take me another couple of hours just to get Skim working, just like it took me two hours to get Scim up and running on my first and fresh Dapper Gnome install. 

The other day I started to learn Scribus - a marvelous DTP application, BTW - but when I wanted to install the Polymer skin, it gave me a dependency error referring to Qt libraries lacking on my system. Since I couldn't find those libraries (not through Adept, neither when Googling), I dropped the installation. However, failed installs always leave behind files. No-one seems to know where all these applications leave their stuff behind. No-one seems to care either... 

I just don't understand. Why do things need to be so difficult? Although I'm new to Linux, I'm not scared to figure out stuff through the console or otherwise, but when I can't get things to work in spite of some rational troubleshooting, it just gets on my nerves.

Another example: I'm also having a lot of trouble with Wine. Most of my dictionaries simply don't work. I just don't get it: dictionaries are not exactly the most sophisticated Windows programmes around. They just look up entries in a database file. I'm an interpreter of English and German by training (my mother tongue is Dutch), so I just need that stuff for my work. However, Wine doesn't come with an interface that is worth the name. You're supposed to mess around yourself until you get it right. 

Is this what Linux is all about? Trial and error? Of course I realise Linux is not a company where everything and everyone is supposed to work together, but as long as configuring things through the graphical interface is not working, it's no use making graphical interfaces at all. Skim is not working out-of-the-box and nowhere it is explained what I'm supposed to do to get it working...

It just takes hours of tweaking and of trial and error to get things right. :( 

Now, I'm sure you are convinced by now that I'm some Windows arse (which, I admit, have been for the last 15 years) complaining about stuff not working out-of-the-box. However, I truly believe in open source and what it can do for the less well-off in this world, ranging from third-world countries to the little school at the corner of our street.

Now, today was pretty stressful and my laptop problems were not making me happy either. However, when surfing the net this evening I stumbled onto a blog about Mark Shuttleworth's visit to China. Have a look at [http://www.amyjiangsu.com](http://www.amyjiangsu.com)

It just made me put things into perspective. It made me realise that Ubuntu is still a little baby that needs love, nurture and understanding. It's about helping people, not about getting angry at little stuff that won't work. 

If I ever win the lottery, Canonical will get a nice share of what I don't really need. Until then... well... I'll keep working and hoping to get my laptop problems solved. :) 

Sorry for the rant guys, I just needed it.
