### Post by JohnnySage50307 on 2009-07-20
One thing I'd sugest is to get a copy of "Linux Pocket Guide" by Daniel J. Barrett, published by O'Reilly.  I've been working on Linux systems for about 5yrs now, and I keep this by me for whenever I can't remember anything.  It's set up to be a simple reference and is very concise.
 
With enough *tinkering*, anyone can figure out how to get what they need out of the graphical end of it, expecially most applications can simply be downloaded and installed via the Applications-->Add/Remove Applications menu item.  The real power of Linux lies in the ability of using the terminal/console.  The installment of individual programs (rather than packaged programs) usually requires you to build them, meaning for you to compile the sourcecode and install it in the proper directory.  While this may seem like a little more work compared to traditional Windows installs (let's be honest, it is), it's also a little more secure--by including the source code, anyone is capable of inspecting it and find potential flaws, therefore the best possible solution is easily achievable.  Lastly, being able to master the shell allows you total control over your system, so with enough patience and learning, you could tailor your system to work the way you feel it ought to.
