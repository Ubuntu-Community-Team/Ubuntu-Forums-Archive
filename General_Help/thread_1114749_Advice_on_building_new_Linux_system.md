Hard to really make any recommendations as you haven't really mentioned a budget or what it will probably be used for ... 

I think just about anything that has come out in the last couple years would probably fit what little vague description you're giving though, as far as performance goes.

32-bit vs 64-bit is *halfway* moot, as almost all desktop CPUs are 64-bit. Which version of Ubuntu you decide to install will depend mostly on whether you want to address > 3GB of memory, or if you decide that you want to do things like encoding/rendering/folding/etc. 

As far as ATI vs Nvidia goes for graphics, they both have their own sets of problems and benefits. Good luck with either choice on that count.

Most motherboards seem to do fine sound-wise ... try it first to see if it suffices first before thinking about shelling out more cash for a card. 

A lot of monitor's can be good. I've personally had good luck with Samsung, Acer, and Viewsonic LCDs.

With CPUs you get what you pay for mostly. More $ translates into more performance. Compatibility won't be a problem here either.

The hardest part to pick about the system though is probably the motherboard. If you're going to have problems with anything in your build, I'd think it would be the motherboard. Do some very careful homework on this part!!

I just built a system for a friend - it runs Vista and Ubuntu as dual-boot. Works really well.

CPU: Intel Core 2 Duo @ 2.8GHz
Motherboard: Asus ?  I'd have to check with my friend, I can't remember what motherboard it was; but it was definitely an Asus
RAM: 4 gigabytes of Kingston DDR2
Optical drive: Pioneer DVD+-RW DL on SATA2. Two of them.
Wireless: Netgear WL111 USB.
Hard Disk: WD 1000GB SATA2
Power supply: Antec 500w Earthwatts
Case: Thermaltake Xaser
Monitor: BenQ 2400HD 24 inch LCD
Keyboard and mouse: Wireless Logitech combo, not sure of the model number.
Graphics: Galaxy 9600GT with 512mb.
Speakers: Logitech 2.1 system with iPod dock.

Worked out-of-the-box with Ubuntu; graphics card was enabled with the Nvidia 180.xx driver. The whole rig cost $AUD1651 excluding the power supply which was one I already had sitting around. You could comfortably save some money by choosing a smaller screen, wired keyboard and mouse, half as much RAM (you probably don't need 4 gigs of RAM, he didn't), half as much HDD space (500 gigs is still heaps) or just one DVD burner.

I purposely chose components for him that satisfied his desires but also provided good value-for-money. The CPU is excellent value; the best performer before the price starts jumping up. The graphics card is also a great deal, and I got the RAM on special. Basically, if you spent more money, you'd get something that's a little better but a lot more expensive.

This is also a case of "build your own and save". JB Hifi advertised a similar machine from HP but with 1x optical drive, no wireless, 2 gigs of RAM, 500 gigs of HDD and a 22 inch monitor for $1993. I saved my friend a couple of hundred and got him something better!

6) Not sure about other brands but HP has very good Linux printer support, avoid Lexmark like a plague. There is an open printer database (google it) where you can find specifics about individual printers.

### Post by mb_webguy on 2009-04-03
[This]("http://www.tldp.org/HOWTO/Hardware-HOWTO/") is a fairly good guide to hardware that is specifically either supported or unsupported by Linux.  It's far from comprehensive, but it should put you on the right track.

As others have said, the motherboard is the most important consideration.  I'd definitely suggest a 64-bit architecture if you're going to be dealing with video, since transcoding will go a lot faster on a 64-bit system than a 32-bit system.  Pick the processor you want -- Intel and AMD are equally supported -- and then pick a motherboard with a socket to match.  Most recent motherboards should be supported.

If you're going to be playing around with MythTV and video conversion, you'll probably want a good video card and probably a video capture card.  I've had better experiences with nVidia than ATI video cards.  As for video capture cards, you should have a look at the [MythTV site]("http://www.mythtv.org/wiki/Video_capture_card") to see which compatible cards have the features you want.

For a printer, go with either Brother or HP.  Both are well-supported.

If you're going to have a wireless card, [this page]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_of_open_source_wireless_drivers") should help you choose one based on the chipset.

The rest of the general hardware doesn't matter much, as Linux has pretty good support for the basics -- hard drives, disc drives, keyboards, monitors, etc.  You'll have to do a bit more research when it comes to the more specific things like webcams.  A good rule of thumb is that if it says it will work with both Windows and Mac OS X, then it will work with Linux as well.

I'd start looking for a motherboard with the right specs and connections. For a HTPC a motherboard with better then average onboard graphics should do. Try finding one with DVI, HDMI and displayport connectors, so it's easy to connect to monitors and TVs. You can always get a graphics card later if you really need the performance.

One thing you didn't list was the power-supply. I suggest you pick one with an active-PFC (power factor correction), these are more efficient, especially at times when you're PC isn't consuming that many watts. Once you put your list of hardware together you can calculate the size of your power-supply with the [_eXtreme Power Supply Calculator_]("http://extreme.outervision.com/psucalculatorlite.jsp"). At [_80 PLUS_]("http://www.80plus.org/manu/psu/psu_join.aspx") you can find a good list with efficient power-supplies (these should all have active-PFCs)
