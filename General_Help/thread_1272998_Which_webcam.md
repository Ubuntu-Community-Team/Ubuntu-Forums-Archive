### Post by StuartN on 2009-10-11
> **3rdalbum said:**
> I'm not sure what is meant by "still image function". I have a UVC camera and I can click the "Take Photo" button in Cheese and it works.

A lot of software uses vgrabbj to capture still frames from the image stream, such as the animation package Stopmotion. At the moment it seems many of these packages will not function at all with a UVC camera, while the more configurable ones (like Stopmotion) require you to install your own frame serving function (Motion works well enough). This introduces a significant lag between the preview and the captured image, which can be annoying for some kinds of animation.

Still image capture is rated as a low priority in the UVC driver milestones.

### Post by StuartN on 2009-10-11
> **PsychedelicWonders said:**
> what do you guys mean UVC camera?

UVC is USB Video Camera, a new class-compliant standard that would work like class-compliant USB storage devices, or hmi devices like keyboards. If it says "UVC" on the box (which many new Logitech cameras do) then the standard UVC driver should support it.

### Post by StuartN on 2009-10-14
> **PsychedelicWonders said:**
> As of right now, my MAIN focus is it acting as a webcam, so I would really like the best quality I can since I'll be doing business via the webcam & VoIP.

Going back to your earlier post, I would suggest that the microphone and the sound settings are absolutely critical for online communication, much more than the image. Plug in a high-quality cardioid mike on a heavy stand. And good light is essential for good video.

As far as the various cameras go, there will not be much of a difference in quality within one brand once the video conferencing settings have been selected - if anything, the lower spec cameras might out-perform higher spec cameras because they are directly suited to video calls (so long as it is a decent brand). You could pay $30 or $120 on a webcam and see no difference in image quality within video-conferencing applications. I would personally prefer a well supported webcam for computer video and a separate camcorder for other recordings.

I suppose the Orbit is supposed to sit on a monitor or tower case, not on the desk looking up at you. I don't see any reference to a tripod thread, which the camcorder is bound to have. A tripod is nice for a steady shot and easy placement. None of the clippy cameras seem to have tripod thread either - I used gardening wire to bind my webcam to an L-bracket that I screwed to the wall (super-steady-shot, excellent time-lapse movies!).

If you can find a good excuse to videocall a lot of people, then ask them what camera, where it is, what microphone they use and what lights they have.
