do you have ANY standard camera lenses at all?  do any close friends or family members shoot either nikon or canon so that you could borrow their lenses if you get yourself a canon or nikon body?

my digital camera collection is at 1.  it's a sony dsc-r1, which is quite unique and sadly discontinued (since sony bought out minolta and is now doing full dSLR stuff).  look it up, though, and see what a GOOD camera should have - a separate flash shoe, a viewscreen that you can at the very least tilt, a large (full size or nearly full size) sensor.  of course, i still have three old-school film cameras, all nikons, but they haven't seen much use lately since the r1 is soooo nice.

for picture quality, sensor size is much more important than pixel count.  large sensor size means that each pixel is bigger, which means less noise and a cleaner signal.

look at the entry-level dSLR stuff from canon, nikon, olympus, and pentax.  try sony (ex-minolta) too.

lens quality is less important, but in a weird way: a small (crappy) sensor needs a lot less glass in the lens, which makes it easier to make a GOOD lens.  a large (good) sensor needs more glass in the lens, which makes a GOOD lens design more difficult.  kinda sucks how the sensor-lens combinations end up.

### Post by bluepowder7 on 2008-07-04
nikon and canon both make great consumer dSLR stuff, and their lenses are quite comparable. i suppose that canon has the edge when it comes to that silentwave thing (instant manual focus), whereas nikon wasn't quite as quick to come out with it.  then again, when it comes to bodies - how they feel, the controls, and the metering system - i've never had a situation that my nikon couldn't handle (this is back in the days of film).

go to a store and pick up the canon digital rebel, and then pick up the nikon d40 or whatever their entry level model is.  see which one feels better, has controls in spots you prefer, and gives you more sensible info in the viewfinder.  some people prefer heavy solid cameras, others prefer lightweight ones.  some prefer thick meaty grips for their big hands, others prefer slimmer less bulky ones.

you can probably get TONS of advice and pointers if you hit up some photography forums.  this is one i used to use and be on back when i was using film:

[http://www.photographyreview.com/](http://www.photographyreview.com/)

i haven't checked it out in a while, but it's probably a good place to start.

### Post by tashmooclam on 2008-07-08
Zooming in with the camera lens will produce more red-eye. The problem is the wide angle pics distort the face somewhat. If you get a newer digital camera, like the Casio Elixm, it has settings like "candlelight portrait" that don't use flash. Newer digital cameras are "faster"=higher ISO, so you can shoot in lower light with no flash going off. The camera you use helps more than the software. I have f-spot and it gives me fits.
