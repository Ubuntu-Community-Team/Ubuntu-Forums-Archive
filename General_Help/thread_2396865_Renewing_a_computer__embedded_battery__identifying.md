### Post by TheFu on 2018-07-22
That's a bunch of questions.  The answers are "it depends."  Some of the questions require a tinfoil hat. Be careful with those - they just keep the exiting electrical waves inside!  ;)

> **bjngchn said:**
> Which parts of a laptop are worth renewing or possible to renew?
New battery, maybe a new fan if the fan is noisy, new storage, new keyboard, if there are any issues shown.  Usually, these parts will cost as much as a new laptop.  Laptop parts are like car parts. They are worth much more separately than shipped in that nice bundle - a complete,working laptop.

> **bjngchn said:**
>   Can a naive person do it?  
It depends.

> **bjngchn said:**
>   I mean things like replacing video card, screen card with new ones.  And cleaning: Can we clean a laptop by oursevles if we are not experienced at all? 
Video cards in laptops are specific to that model. They are not considered "user replaceable".  If the laptop vendor produced different versions of the same laptop, just with different video adaptors AND cooling wasn't modified to deal with the different heat produced, then you might be able to swap in different video.  But with most intel-based laptops the last 5+yrs, those some with the iGPU integrated into the CPU, so there isn't any video adaptor to replace.

Inside laptops things are exactly positioned. Each is a little different.  Getting at different parts can be trivial or very difficult. It just depends on the laptop model.  I have a 15inch dell laptop. Replacing the keyboard on it is 5 minutes. Easy.  Then I have another laptop.  To replace the keyboard requires purchasing an "assembly" and removing everything from inside the case, including the motherboard.  The laptop was $199 new. The "assembly" is $90 and contains the keyboard, touchpad and the case you can see around the keyboard.

> **bjngchn said:**
>  Can such things be done by computer technicians who don't   work at a  (LaptopsBrandName) service without taking creating a  risk for the  laptop?
It depends. If you are extremely detailed, can follow specific orders, forwards  AND backwards, and keep track of each different screw and part removed in a highly organized way, then an average person with screwdriver and tiny prybar can do it.  If the wrong screw is lost or put back incorrectly, the laptop will never be the same.

Overheating is just a cleaning issue most of the time. Not hard to solve, unless the specific design for that laptop model has an overheating problem. That is more common than people know.

It is impossible for any electronic device to be 100% safe unless you build it from the basic components yourself. Nobody builds their own computers. Nobody.  If you need this sort of assurance, get your PC from the NSA.  Everyone else will have to accept the risks.  A battery isn't the worst part of your scenario.  Onboard NIC and onboard audio are scarier to me.

> **bjngchn said:**
>     Is a new installation of k/ubuntu uniquely   identifieable on a  network, like when doing updates, or visiting internet?

Yes. All computers have uniqueness which can be traced.  Advertising companies have worked hard to ensure they can take people everywhere.  As different sensors are uncovered and blocked, it seems new standards and capabilities are being exposed which aid in fingerprinting. The EFF has a page to help you estimate the uniqueness for your setup.  [https://panopticlick.eff.org/](https://panopticlick.eff.org/)

The best way to foil this is to use a standard, unmodified, read-only, setup when on the internet.  Something like the TAILS distribution would probably be best - and never use the same physical hardware for the different purposes.  People who download TAILS or TOR are tracked by the USgovt.  I expect other govts track that too.  I downloaded them at a public library, using their computers, didn't bring any computing device or phone with me that day.  Just an empty, throw-away, flash drive.  USB hardware has serial numbers which are logged whenever connected to anything capably of logging.

If you are in places where govts abuse their power  on massive scales, there isn't much you can do except use flash memory to transfer data with very strong encryption.  For example, in parts of China, only certain "approved" routers are allowed, which allows the govt to track all visited IP addresses regardless of the device.  TOR and VPNs are the only help, but I would expect the hardware to have been modified to provide access to local data.  If I was in mainland China, I'd expect any hardware purchased there to be modified before it left the factory for govt "monitoring."   Govts that censor are the ones I'm most worried about.  Govts that are well-know for spying are concerning too.
