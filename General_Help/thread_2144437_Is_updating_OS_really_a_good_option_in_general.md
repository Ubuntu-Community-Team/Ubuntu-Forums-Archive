### Post by grahammechanical on 2013-05-12
> [COLOR=#000000]Why do we have all these new systems coming up while we will use them for the same tasks?[/COLOR]

Answer = profit. The desire to get better performance from the OS drives people to buy the latest hardware. The desire to sell more software drives developers to push for innovation in the hardware. And then there is the desire of consumers for the latest fashions.

Ubuntu has had a six month release cycle since Ubuntu was first released. This was to drive development. It was in synchronisation with the development cycle of Gnome the default Ubuntu desktop environment. And we see the benefits of this today.

When people ask on this forum what Ubuntu should I install? Should it be 12.04 or 12.10, some of us say: Install 12.04 it is stable and has long term support. I agree with that advice even though I have installed every release of Ubuntu since 7.04 came out. I think it is now the official opinion. Mark Shuttleworth has said, that if people want stability they should install the Ubuntu LTS. If they want the bleeding edge of Ubuntu development, they should install the development branch. I see a question mark hanging over the future of Ubuntu interim releases (now called standard releases).

I am surprised at the number of people who upgrade and then do not like what they have got. I just cannot understand upgrading to the next release just because it has been released. Some people think that the latest must be the greatest. They even go to the extent of installing Alpha and Beta software. And then they wonder why something is breaking the OS. I do not see the point of doing that unless you want to experiment and report bugs.

Regards.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2013-05-12
OS upgrades are always a mix of adding new features and removing old features.

Look at all the attention that MS got by simply removing the Start button in Win8!  This has made Stardock, and others, LOTS of money by their producing third-party add-ons for Win8.

Ubuntu removed Unity2D and now, has removed Wubi.  So, Canonical takes things away, as well.

We've become conditioned to automatically presume that newer must be better -- but in reality, that is not always the case.

BEFORE you do any OS upgrade, you should first make an image backup of what you have now -- as returning to your current state can be difficult, if not impossible, should the upgrade turn out to be a disappointment (as in the case of many Win7-to-Win8 upgrades) or problematic.
