### Post by Ovist on 2010-08-28
It worries me that you wish to use the paste, but do not know where it goes....spells trouble.
 
Before the paste issue, I am of the opinion that you have other problems causing excessive processor workload thus creating too much heat, or the fan may be clean but may no longer spin at sufficient speed. I put odds on too much crap running on your pc. Also, the fan on a laptop should always run in my experience. 
 
Now if you must try the paste:
 
The paste helps to transfer heat between the processor and the metal surface on the UNDERSIDE of the fan assembly. IE it goes between the processor and the fan.
 
You do not want too much paste, and you do not want too little or you will screw up your laptop. You want just enough paste to thinly cover the part of the processor that contacts with the fan base. Too much and the paste squeezes out causing potential problems, too little and the heat transfer wont be good and you'll fry that processor.
 
If you forge ahead and take the fan off the processor you will most likely see some white paste that is dried on both your processor and the underside of the fan. This can normally be cleaned off with isopropal alcohol, and should be. Put the alcohol on a papertowel an swab off the old, sometimes it is stubborn, but they must be clean prior to the new paste being applied.
 
Apply the new paste evenly and thin over the top of the processor (should be a metal square). Place the fan assembly back over it and clip it back down.

### Post by Ginsu543 on 2010-08-28
This process gets even more complicated because you are trying to put thermal paste on a laptop processor. Not as much room to maneuver when you put the stuff on. If it's indeed a silver compound, you should read the documentation to see if it is electrically conductive. If it is, then you must be EXTRA careful not to get any of the stuff on anything else on your motherboard except on the heatspreader of your cpu. Having said this, you should be that careful anyways, but you can fry your motherboard by short-circuiting something by getting electrically conductive paste on it.

If you've been sufficiently scared, you can still proceed and be successful. I've hand-built every one of my computers and I've been putting on Arctic Silver thermal compound on my processors every time without a single problem. Ovist is right though, you don't want to put too much on. I usually start with a very small "droplet" and spread it over the cpu's heatspreader as evenly and as thinly as I can using a flat razor blade. You want good contact between the cpu heatspreader and the heatsink, so make sure to minimize anything else getting on there, like dust. Once the heatspreader is evenly coated, replace the heatsink/fan assembly back in place.
