### Post by TheFu on 2024-07-11
All laptops are limiting when compared to what a desktop can provide.
I have no idea what RTX means in this thread. Please clarify. I've only heard it related to specific GPU models. Don't know why that would matter compared to other models from the same vendor?

Intel or AMD are the only real choices for computers of any power for home users or students. They each have pros and cons, but usually those don't matter.  AMD GPUs are well supported by Linux. nVidia are not and there can be hassles.  

Intel iGPUs are almost always problem free, but usually with lower graphics performance. For non-gamers, Intel iGPUs are not an issue and probably much more power efficient, which can be important for a laptop, if you must have a laptop.

If you get a laptop, typically Dell or Lenovo have the best Linux support. Much of that is because they make so many that most of the chips used for peripherals will have Linux drivers.  There are exceptions, especially with really new or really cheap models.

For laptops, I've only bought Intel-based because, at the time, Intel was better for longer battery life.  I don't know if that is currently still true or not.
For desktops, the last Intel I purchased was very cheap in 2015. Since then, I've purchased 3 AMD Ryzen CPUs and have been very happy.  Desktops provide much more flexibility for peripherals and addons than a laptop will. This may be very important or not important at all.  I suppose if you live in a part of the world with lots of power issues, then a laptop would be a better choice.  I'm lucky. Power is very stable here, even so, I have multiple UPS devices to prevent power spikes or brown-outs from harming my computers.

Anyway, it might be best to ask this question in a Spanish language area or a Spanish language forum, so the some terms don't confuse native English speakers.

They've moved the different parts of the numbering around. Segment, year, Architecture, Differentiator, and power target - but these don't mean what you think they mean. ### Post by grahammechanical on 2024-07-11
Buy a laptop with a distribution of Linux pre-installed. This is what I did. The supplier includes all the drivers needed. Especially, the keyboard functions such as backlight. My laptop was made in Taiwan but the supplier was English. Several distributions of Linux were offered. I choose Ubuntu.

The motherboard has Software Guard Extensions (SGX) and Trusted Platform Module (TPM 2). My laptop has both SGX and TPM enabled and Ubuntu still works and I can install other versions of Ubuntu without any problems.

Desktop computers and laptop computers are designed to meet the standards that Microsoft requires to makes its operating system secure. I do not think that you will be able to buy a modern laptop without these security features.
