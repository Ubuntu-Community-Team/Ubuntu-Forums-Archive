### Post by cascade9 on 2010-07-12
IMO-
Make sure your power supply is actually putting out the right voltages- 12v, 5v 3.3v. A small amount of variance is allowable, but anything greater than +/- 5% (that is 12.6v/11.4v, 5.25v/4.75v and 3.465v/3.135v) is bad (I'd even reject 5% myself), and its much better if they voltages are within 1-3%. 

Dont let your HDD get really hot. Fan and/or heatsink cooling is never a bad idea. 

Vibration is VERY bad. If you live near a major road or highway, on top of a disco, etc, get some nice soft feet for your computer case or move.

### Post by isecore on 2010-07-12
Pretty much what cascade9 said. Proper cooling, a decent PSU, avoid vibrations. 

Oh, and make sure you don't spin disks down and then spin them up - that wears out the drives quite fast, which is ironic considering it's meant to save power. My experience with consumer-drives are that it's better to simply let them spin, the strain of powering back up again over and over wears out the motor.

### Post by plypencil on 2010-07-12
If you use a solid state drive the life would increase dramatically, however your question was how can you maximize your current hard drive life not get a new one.

1) Make sure it is not running too hot
Use a tool such as Speccy (Free), if your drive has a temperature sensor you can have a look at how its doing, also Speccy offers you advice on what temperature to keep it at. A good dusting of the hard drive every once in a while is good. Speccy will also give you S.M.A.R.T codes, which is basically the way the hard drive tells you there is a problem, or there will be soon.

2) Minimize vibrations
Make sure the hard drive does not have a jolt of more than 5G, manufacturers normally rate the drives to 10G but personal experience has taught me not to trust them. You can buy special caddy's for your drive which encapsulate the drive providing a cooling system and an anti vibration system.

3) Use Protection!
Im referring to your power supply. Use an extension lead which provides surge protection, make sure it has a warranty or an insurance on it as well. A high warranty = high reputability.

4) Fragmentation
When files are written do disk they are scattered all over the place. Cant be helped. However running a defragmenter (If you can find one for linux) will significantly increase the drive life because the internal needle will not be required to move as much over a long term period.

5) Children
If you ever remove the drive from your case and if you have children, don't let them get their hands on it. Its just a big NO. And don't leave it on the worktop while a member of the family is around with a pint in his hand, my hard drive did not fare well to a pint of Carlsberg.

SSD's introduce their own other unique problems to maximizing longevity. SSD's have a limited amount of write cycles, and a careless operating system will wear these out surprisingly fast. They will still eventually wear out no matter what you do, due to flash-memory having a finite amount of write-cycles.
