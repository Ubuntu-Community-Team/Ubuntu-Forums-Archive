### Post by Buce on 2009-07-15
The short version: Read the last paragraph.

The long version: A long time ago, a friend of mine installed Skype on his grandma's computer so that he could chat with people while he visited her. It's a Windows box, and he had Skype configured so that it automatically starts when the computer boots, but he thought he had set it so that it wouldn't save his password and automatically log him in. Turns out he didn't do that, and he was getting signed on every time his grandma used the computer, but so what? At worst, all that would happen is that someone would send him a message saying "hello" or something, his grandma wouldn't respond, and that would be that, right?

Wrong.

Turns out, Skype lets you log into the same account at two different computers simultaneously. Not only that, but if a conversation is being held via chat on one computer, at least part of that conversation also shows up on the other computer. I'm not sure about the details, but my friend got a call from his grandma about his messages showing up on her computer, and that was while we were having a private conversation about a very sensitive subject.

Needless to say, we are not happy. What kind of IM/VoIP service allows a person to log in twice without so much as a warning? IIRC, AIM will kick the person using the account off their service. MSN does the same. How did Skype miss the ball on this one? It seems like a basic security feature -- if someone is logged in twice to an account used for private communication over the internet, don't you think that person ought to be alerted to the fact that they may have done so inadvertently, just in case someone else is listening on the other end, unbeknownst to them? Or worse, got their account details and are posing as them in order to, say, spam their friends with advertisements? Dunno about you, but if I were the account holder, I'd like to know.

I mean, yeah, sure, I shouldn't trust any info transmitted over the 'net to be secure, but it's not like I was trading government secrets. I figured that at worst, my conversations were being stored on a server somewhere, not being broadcast (I'm not sure whether in part or in whole, and I don't care) to every other computer that happens to be logged in under that account at the time. And sure, the fact that another computer *was* logged in was due to user error, but it was an error of the kind that's preventable with good programming, that *is* prevented by other IM clients, and that any software developer worth his salt would include precautions for.

So. I'm looking for an alternative to Skype that works on Mac, Windows, and Linux. VoIP is a must, a text-based chat feature would be nice-but-not-necessary, and the more secure, the better. I'm presuming this means that it needs to be open source (at least in part), is well-developed, and has a big enough community behind it to ensure that the source code has been scrutinized to within an inch of the package maintainer's lives. But I'm not completely closed off to the proprietary world, and if a proprietary app has a reputation for security and standards that I can trust, I'll consider it. I know Gizmo is OSS, but only in part according to Wikipedia, and I don't know enough about VoIP to know which parts are important for security. Suggestions?

### Post by aaronchall on 2009-09-20
I know this is old, I hope there's no prohibition about responding to old posts, but I'd say your friend got what he deserved.  You don't just set up a program to start every time the computer comes up on someone else's machine.  You're lucky if they give you permission to let you leave it installed.  But to have it come on automatically?  That consumes resources and adds to the startup time.  Convenient for your friend when he decided to pay his grandmother a visit, but inconvenient to his grandmother.

I have a friend who's grandkids slow her computer to a crawl every time they come over, installing AIM, YIM, GIM, and then there's about 3 extra toolbars at the top of IE.  Of course she won't make them stop, or they won't love her anymore in her old age.  Ah, karmic retribution is a wonderful thing.
