### Post by nickleboyblue on 2010-12-11
Word Processor, Spreadsheet, and Drawing:

OpenOffice.org, the office suite that comes default in Ubuntu, does those and more.  It's the leading open-source word processing engine and office suite.

Photo Editing/viewing: if you want one program to do both, you might want to try installing Google's Picasa for Linux from here: [http://picasa.google.com/linux/](http://picasa.google.com/linux/)

...Picasa is easy to use and allows you to do simple photo editing and such without much difficulty.  You can also get Gimp from the default repositories.  It focuses more on technical users who already know quite a lot about digital photo manipulation, but you can learn how to use it by reading "Groking the Gimp" (Downloadable from the repositories) and from following online video tutorials.

Disc Writing/burning:  Don't even bother too much with looking for anything that doesn't come in Ubuntu by default for this one.  Brasero disc burner is so well integrated with the operating system be default that it seems like just another facet of Ubuntu.  Simply copy your files to the blank cd's folder, and burn away!

Dictionary:  Ubuntu comes with a built-in dictionary, which you can find in Applications->Accessories->Dictionary.  It requires an internet connection, and provides results from multiple sources.  Works quite well.

Video Editor:  There are several good video editors available in the repositories, including Pitivi Video Editor and Kino, both of which are worthy of some attention.  Just download and install them, then try and see which one you like to use the most.  You might find that each has their own strengths and are worth keeping around for the areas that one does well in that the other struggles in.

Games: See this link: [https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Games](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Games)
I will only suggest Battle for Wesnoth, Warzone 2100, Armagetron, and SuperTux, because honestly, the beauty of a game is in the eye of the beholder.  Just look in the repositories games section and you'll find a lot of good stuff.

Typing Tutor:  I haven't used one under linux.  just search "typing" in the Ubuntu software center and it will bring up about eight results from the repository database.  Try a few of them out and see what you get.

Educational:  Again, most everything you mentioned here can be easily found in the Ubuntu Software Center.  Just open it from the menu, and you'll see several icons that narrow your software search to genres like education, games, office, graphics, etc.  I personally like Stellarium and Celestia, as I have a healthy interest in astronomy.  See what you can find!  You'll be pleasantly surprised by how many useful and powerful programs are out there waiting to be discovered.

One more thing:  If you are planning on watching DVDs, take a good look at VLC.

NOTE:  The grand majority of what you asked for comes in Ubuntu by default.  It might take some time to learn how to use those tools, but they are almost invariably more powerful than their proprietary counterparts once you know what you're doing.
