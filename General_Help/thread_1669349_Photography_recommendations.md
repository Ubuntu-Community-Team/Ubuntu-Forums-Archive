### Post by Neobuntu on 2011-01-17
I've been fervently trying all things Linux, for photography. If you're heard of it, I've likely tried it.

All I can really do, is give you my opinion. Not that facts, are not involved. They are.

One thing though, we are all different. For simplicity, it really breaks down to lite photo management vs. people who do it all. Of course, many are in-between. Plus, changing; sometimes back and forth. 

This is why I've written about Raw developing. It is one example, of this. My main point about Raw vs. JPEG arguments, is there's really is no right or wrong there. You should probably do both, depending; with different people falling more toward one or the other. This depends on the camera, a lot, too. As I said, someone shooting mostly JPEG, may very well Change and shoot mostly Raw. All that said, open software development for both, MUST continue.

Now, I have also extensively used the latest Windows offerings. Call it my opinion, but Lightroom and Photoshop hold no great attraction, they just work slightly differently. Say, you used Lightroom, with all it's tiered (new every year) costs (legally) and catalog issues, and forcing you to upgrade; to obtain its (Camera) Raw module, for your new cameras. Well, you still need to shift over to Photoshop (better the Gimp), when and if you "outgrow" that, or need more advanced manipulation. I can tell you that Lightroom is slower, all things considered, too.

Then there's the idea of getting a super "sale", on mid-level Adobe products. I have some swampland to sell, you guys. "Oh! I got just what I needed, no more, and I even saved X amount, doing it." Duh. ..and I'll stop there. If you can't ask the question of, what if something free, is better or what if I need more later, then I'm sorry; I can't help you. 

You probably know, you have your organizers, and you have your "pixel manipulators". What's confusing is various levels of overlap, between managers and editors. On any system, you're still better keeping them separate, as two different programs, so as to maximize their given abilities, when you need them! A combo app, will always weight; more toward one or the other. What's better, than free options, for both of theses? Yet, don't be deceived. While cost is certainly a factor, leading to the best value, please understand; I evaluate fairly (with my opinion), as if they were all free.

All that to say; given the very, light user, having light photo needs; you may simply start with Picasa. For it's pros and con;, the automatic use of wine, embedded in the Linux Picasa version, just works. It's independent of any other WINE stuff. I can easily imagine people with modest photo needs; having the absolute best experience, with something like Linux Mint 10 and Picasa. The red-eye reduction (When it works; because some Red-eye is harder, and require advanced fixing) is a fine example. Easy sliders let you do a little bit, with ease, in Picasa. Not to mention, Picasa Web may allow a slightly bigger rendition, of your photo. (However, this is at the whim of Google.)

But that's not me! Next stop, among all these, many choices (a very good thing), is DigiKam. DigiKam beats the pants, off of Lightroom for total management; even as it's has a different look and not that same interface. Left to right, right to left, who cares? It's easy enough to get used to. Never let familiarity out-weight your choice. However, if you agree with my opinions you will be faster acclimated, to just where you best be learning.

Stop right there to say, I know what you're thinking; that there should be zero learning. I also believe in user friendly progress! I tend to agree, where possible; but lets be realistic. Powerful choices, might take you a few seconds to realize. That's just the way things are, and closed options DO NOT stop that process. In fact, it can actually be overall worse, with those closed programs. Thus, why I'm telling you, per a user "level".

DigiKam, FULLY covers anything, you'll desire, for organizing your photos and including a thumbnail viewer for the Raw files, on less main stream cameras. Lightroom included.

Then here, you get into the DigiKam editor. This might be your favorite editor; for mid level editing. However, what people miss, is you may be better served, just using Gimp for your editor; along with DigiKam's management parts. This would be, for a more advance user.

Advance users can even use the excellent file manager, and the Gimp. Not that they couldn't benefit from photo management.

Technical note: DigiKam, and it's KDE parts, works just fine, with the "Gnome" environment; MOST of the time. Occasionally, this needs a work-around; that it shouldn't (doesn't usually). With a new install of Linux Mint 10 and the 64-bit version, DigiKam is currently working fine. With Ubuntu 10.04 and 32 bit, I've only had the occasionally frustration with stopping an editor process. All in all, far less issues, than with OS-X, or Windows photo programs. I still recommend DigiKam as the best photo manager.

Note: If you're looking for a Gnome alternative, don't. F-stop (I mean F-Spot) stinks, and not just because it uses Mono. Shotwell is far better but it's no DigiKam. Shotwell has a better editor, in the gnome world, but remember; for down and dirty editing, advanced users will probably choose the Gimp, anyway. A light use editor, is kind of a sink hole, for your time; with less ability(and slower). You might as well pull-up a free YouTube video, of how to do it, with the Gimp. That's a far better use, of your time; IMHO. It's overall, less time. However, If that's not you, remember my Picasa recommendation.  

I use mainly Gimp (ufraw for Raw), for editing and yes, you have to load up all the freely available plug-ins and give them a whirl. The same people, who would complain about all the choices(see Picasa instead), inside the Gimp, would also be the first to complain if something wasn't there! What ya going to do?

ufraw is best for Raw; when you get used to it (DigiKam has Raw too, and so does Picasa, as automated)! You do not have to adjust everything, in ufraw. It does not have everything in it. It's not supposed to... I do not currently do NR, in ufraw, for example. Gimps "Wavelet Denoise" plug-in, is outstanding, instead. What you'll need to know, doing Raw, is how to adjust curves (instead of global contrast and brightness) in ufraw, to maximize your 16-bit levels and 16-bit white balance, where you have the automatic dropper, (for any Grey patch). Then, just saving it as a .PNG (your work file; that you may then run through any number of editors, to test.)

Note: You can just set your computer to open you Raw files by their extension, in Gimp, and then they will automatically go through ufraw (if installed) in one of two different ways. Auto develop (see the Gimp plug-in for ufraw), or go into ufraw, for it's finer 16-bit adjustments. You can do both; that is, set ufraw for a particular cameras character, and then also, do picture by picture group, batch functions. Were talking 16-bit Raw here, and DigiKam has extensive batch functions for this and other JPEG manipulations too. If you have any trouble, just load your Raw files into ufraw and save them as a PNG. Then load the .PNG, in Gimp.   

Use em', if ya need em'. 

Yes, I tried Lightzone, Bibble, Fotoxx and others. Meh.

Just remember, you're not going to compete in any photo enhancement challenge or pro job, with a light duty editor. I believe, we should make all theses software components as easy as possible (you should see some of the one click, photo enhancement choices, through the Gimp plugins), but you still have to pick up the proverbial brush. Do you know what I mean? It's artistic flexibility. At some point, you can't program art.

All this is not to say, that we can't employ some pros, from the closed-up software world. However, we're already past closed software pros, overall. You just have to know here to look. I sincerely hope I have been of some service. :)

..it's ALL, about the Light, my friends.
