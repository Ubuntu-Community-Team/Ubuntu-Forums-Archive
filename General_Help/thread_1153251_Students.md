### Post by Alterax on 2009-05-08
I recommend it...with one caveat, which really need not be:

Educators are not usually well-informed about the choices available to students.  I've gottes many syllabi from professors that made it quite clear that Microsoft Word was a requirement, or Excel.  I complained at first, to no avail--if I wanted to take the courses that I paid for out of my own pocket, I had to purchase Windows and Office.

Suffice it to say that I did not.  I just saved my OpenOffice documents in word/excel/powerpoint formats.  They weren't any the wiser.

Another issue that I have is where my niece was bawled out by a teacher for not having a Microsoft Operating system, that she couldn't do her schoolwork, et cetera ad nauseum.  I investigated, and it turns out that she tried to take her classwork to school on an EXT3-formatted drive.  I actually asked to see the teacher, plugged the card into my laptop, and converted it to VFAT.

I talked with the teacher for a bit--she expressed that my niece had to use a Microsoft operating system and Microsoft office.  I asked her if all students had computers (no--so evidently it wasn't a proper *requirement*).  I explained that a fresh install of Vista would cost $299.00, and Office would be an additional $299.00, and that I would be more than happy to install it if the school or the teacher were willing to pay for it. (again, no--and she had no idea it was so expensive.)

By time she and I had finished talking, she was eager to get a liveCD for students to copy as well as a CD of the Windows and Mac OS X ports of OpenOffice.

So to make a long story short, Ubuntu is just fine for students.  In some cases, it can be preferable to Windows.  The only resistance you're likely to get is from educators that don't realize the difference between a specific program and a file format.  And deep down most of these are quite reasonable people if you take the time to talk to them about it.
