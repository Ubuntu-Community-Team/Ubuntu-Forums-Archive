### Post by Icey-Net on 2010-03-29
Hello

And a big welcome, to me, for my very first post on these forums. :P

I'm a designer. I design... things... in Photoshop. I mostly do web designs, but recently, I've become more passionate about interface design. Usability improvement in interfaces and general, look and feel improvements.

So, they released Ubuntu 10.04 Beta 1 which threw fuel into the fire and today, I can present you with a fully featured Ubuntu Concept design. This took me about a week. Over 300 Photoshop layers, 50 megs of them layers, 30 hours (spread across a week) and an A4 with every millimeter packed with design documentation. I hope you enjoy it.

### Post by camporter1 on 2010-03-30
Very well thought-out, user-friendly design. I think there are a lot of elements from this concept that could really improve the effectiveness of Ubuntu, or any other flavor of Linux. Some thoughts:

The slab menu: I don't really mind this kind of menu, it's just that many implementations are clunky or slow. If it could perform as well as the default gnome menu we have now, I think more people would adopt it.

I think it might be handy if the menu were intellignet enough to know if something is already open, so that it isn't cluttering the menu, which is sort of opposite to the recently used applications at the top of your slab. It appears to use lots of real estate for other things.

Software Center is on your menu, but as an aside, I think it would be interesting if the menu were intelligently expanded to include anything in the repos, so a user wouldn't even have to consider necessarily installing something. Just an extra thought I had from seeing your menu...

I like the elements where devices are put organized together so a person can sanely take care of them, similarly to the Places menu we have now, but allowing for right click on those items to do the usual operations.

The 'Me Menu' (which I sort of prefer over the Social Networking name), is really well done. I think that the basis for a lot of the great ideas in your concept come from not limiting the panels to applets with just menus, but their own intelligent areas. Plain menus can only be hacked around so many times before they don't fulfill the purpose they are expected to do!

Status area: great consistency, keeping it simple and allowing the user to find the things they need quickly.

Tray menu: I would consider just merging it with the places menu and making it more consistent, possibly renaming that menu?

Nautilus: Not much to say, because not much changed, although conceptualizing all of the ideas no one has come up with (or has) for managing files is probably out of the scope of this.

Mouse: Great design too!

Overall, the design is probably more KDE than your average Gnome user would expect :)

Well done sir!

### Post by Icey-Net on 2010-03-30
People usually feel like that about things that change something that they're used to. It's the sort of thing that once you use it, you later end up wondering how you could ever live without it. That's what happened with W7's App launcher. For me, anyway... :P

That's how this "Main Menu" was designed. In fact, the whole concept was done not to change things just for the sake of change, but to reinvent everything, making it more usable and useful. I'm confident that, while yes, the "Main Menu" as it stands here is different and has more options, it's still *that* much better than a simple list of applications.

On a side note, I didn't quite declare the customizing options available for various different aspects. I intend to make the Main Menu itself fully customizable.

Also, I would say that there is a good chance that this will be released one day. When? I can't be sure. It's likely it will have minor modifications as well (design aspects, like the scrollbar and buttons). Obviously, main ideas will remain as is. :P

[*]The panel is already mostly implemented, see [Docky]("https://launchpad.net/docky") (panel mode). All you need is to make a skin.
[*]There are several implementations of main menu, the closest one to your mockup is gnome-globalmenu. However, if you choose Docky for panel, you'll have to code all menus from scratch.
[*]Docky menus are already half-transparent, transparency of other windows (e.g. tooltips) can be easily done by adding a rule to compiz. It also would be cool to make inactive windows be half-transparent, compiz has a nice plugin for that.
[*]Partial transparency of some GTK+ widgets is most likely possible in experimental GNOME 3 CSS-based GTK+ engine. Maybe it's even possible in the latest murrine engine, but I doubt that. You'll have to use CSS engine for some advanced features anyway.
[/LIST]

My advice is to post your mockup to [Ayatana]("https://launchpad.net/~ayatana") and [Ubuntu Art]("https://lists.ubuntu.com/mailman/listinfo/ubuntu-art") mailing lists. These are the best place to make the right people see your concept.

And I couldn't help but add some bits of my opinion. Your concept is a great job, but it has several cons, like everything else in this world. Placing the same apps on the panel and to the first row of main menu is irrational. Side-by-side categories and apps look pretty, but such lists tend to grow looooong and become unusable (that's one of the worst KDE diseases). On the other hand, it's better than GNOME-Shell menu. And I'd prefer to see a real tray, not a menu.
And those copyright symbols are nasty, but some subtle pattern on all backgrounds, just like those (C)s, would be great. It would be even better if it wouldn't move when the window is moved, but compiz plugin for that work pretty badly, and I don't know if it's possible with CSS or not.
And one more thing. A special Ubuntu system font is coming in 10.10. Your mockup will stop looking like windows 7 if you use that font. Unfortunately, it's not released yet.
