### Post by WorLord on 2010-03-04
With the exception of color scheme, Ubuntu has been steadily heading down Mac-lane for a long time: 
- top panel placement, layout, and function; 
- the move to monochrome status icons; 
- colored scroll bars; 
- and (now) round and traffic-light colored minimize/maximize/close buttons.  

All of this is, IMO, a good thing.  Mac hires a lot of HID people who actually do human-interface studies; in fact, I read a statistic somewhere that said that compared to Redmond, mac has 400% more HI designers.  It is hardly a surprise that the Gnome HID guidelines more and more frequently end up agreeing with a lot of the ideas established in the Mac HID guidelines - Mac's interface applies a surprising amount of good and useful ideas that more frequently than not resonate with the way people think computers should work.  OS X has its share of minor UI issues and mistakes, but for the most part, its interface is easy to understand, easy to use, and visually stunning.  And I, for one, see no reason to shy away from those concepts simply in the interest of being different.  

Purple lowlights, though... unique choice, and I have no doubt that it will be more welcome than brown (which, though fine with me, seems to be a problem for most people).  Whatever, though, **at least it isn't blue**, which is a very very good thing (blue is THE most bland and overused UI color scheme pretty much *ever*, and I can't help but to be sick of it and reject it outright whenever it is proposed, which is all the time).  

What I'm really interested in is this: Is there any way I can install and use this on Karmic?

### Post by angularsofty on 2010-03-05
> **alex.rayu said:**
> For all reasons, window border icons should be on the left top, like in Mac. Why? Because most are left-to-right, and so left top is where we are looking for functionality in the first place. That is more intuitive. It was not for any specific reason that Windows moved those to the right - only to differ.

No, I disagree with that interpretation of the left-to-right principle.

We begin to read at the left side, so that is where I first want to see the program icon & title--so I know immediately what the window is and what to do with it. As the eye is drawn to the right, we conclude at the close button. That seems natural to me--left to right, start to finish.

If as you suggested the left top is where we look for functionality, should vertical scrollbars also go on the left? Most right-handed people will instinctively retract their mouse pointer to the right side when they're looking at a window. Having the scrollbar on the right makes it readily available and means I don't have to get the pointer in the way of what I'm looking at. Likewise, I don't want to move my pointer all the way across a window to get to the min/max/close buttons.

It would be great if Ubuntu's UI design recognized that different people are different in this regard and could adapt to handedness with some simple, prominent Appearance settings. But the changes we're given in the new layout are just a mishmash forcing nearly every user, new or experienced, to readjust their ingrained behavior. That's not a welcoming approach when it comes to making converts from other OSes.

So I hope there's still time to get the design team to reconsider.

I agree.  Specific problems:

- I think it was a great idea to move away from "+" signs (representing expandable tree items) and towards triangles that rotate when that tree is expanded.  Further, the "+" signs in the new theme have a rounded-corner box drawn around them, and this causes weird transparency/graphical problems if the expander widget is on darker backgrounds.

- I'm OK with the window title text being on the left, but prefer it in the middle.

- I don't see the application icon anywhere; that was a handy visual cue to help me distinguish which window I am actually in if I can't be bothered to check the contents of the window.

These are some of the specific issues I have with the new themes; regardless, I like where its headed.

### Post by diazamet on 2010-03-24
> **alex.rayu said:**
> Ok. Check this out. Even if you are not a fan of Mac - there are few objective things to it.

1. Menu taken out. Why? Because you are accessing only one menu at a time. So why have a menu take space of every window on your screen?


There are several issues with the Mac menu:
[LIST=1]
[*]Many applications are often run maximised so the menu per application is not an issue.
[*]For applications that are not maximised, having the menu in the window makes more sense for an object-oriented paradigm.  You know the menu is for that particular application regardless of which window has focus.  
[*]If the window is not maximised, you working in a much smaller area of the screen but to use the Mac style menu you still have to move the mouse beyond the bounds of the window to the extremes of the display.  
[*]If you use multiple displays on the Mac (IIRC) the menu may not be on the same display as the window.
[*]If you have a single menu and prefer 'focus follow mouse', the window in focus may change before the mouse reaches the top of the display therefore changing the menu before you get to it.
[/LIST]
