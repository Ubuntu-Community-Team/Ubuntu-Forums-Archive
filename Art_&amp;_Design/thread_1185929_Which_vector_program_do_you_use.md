### Post by warreno on 2009-08-16
> **mdgrech said:**
> a proper artist?  what you think that you need to buy $dobe junk to be a proper artist?  What Da Vinnci didn't have illustrator so hes not an artist either I suppose?  Don't fall into Adobes Marketing trap of believing Adobe products will makes you a better artists, after all a good artist never blames his tools.

That is true up to a point, but only up to a point. While both Inkscape and the GIMP are nice packages for doing vector and bitmap work respectively, neither is quite the set nor suite of tools one expects from the "high end" dedicated apps.

As one example, the GIMP still can't natively handle CMYK colorspace. (There's a [plugin]("http://cue.yellowmagic.info/softwares/separate.html"), surprise, that might help.) CMYK is absolutely indispensable for DTP work. No amount of Leonardo talent will make up for the fact that an RGB image is in *the wrong color mode* for direct-to-plate prepress work.

Atop that, exporting to PDF is still less than ideally supported; color modes are wrong and the quality is sub-par. There seems to be little support for profiles. This matters to those of us who make our living doing it every day. I don't know what a *proper* artist is, but I do know that there are some things the Linux application suite can't do well yet.

Adobe's CS app set is, I think, very overpriced; but have you actually worked with any of it? There are some things you can do in Illustrator that you can't in any other vector engine, and there's a reason Photoshop is regarded as the industry standard for bitmap editing. Adobe's marketing efforts are not that reason.

### Post by alfu on 2009-09-21
Open Office Draw.  But it has some issues:

**Export to PDF:** All layers, printable or visible or not, are exported to PDF.  You have to select the items you want and then Export only the selection to keep guides or traceable elements out of the PDF document.

**Layers, assigning objects to:** Good support for adding layers, but moving between them and assigning objects to them is clumsy.  I would suggest a "Layer  ->" right-click pop-up menu item to easily assign a selected object to a layer.  Layers, as identified by tabs, are not associated with the "Bring to Front" or "Send to back" arrangements, which can be confusing -- this is more a philosophical choice than a bug, tho.

**Layers, changing properties of:** Right-clicking on a layer tab brings up a short menu consisting of a random subset of various items including Delete, Modify, Insert New, Cut, Copy or Rename.  Rename is redundant, since it is a field in the Modify dialog, giving you more than one way to do the same thing, in the Micro$oft tradition.  Cut and Delete seem redundant too.

**Layers, visibility of:** Right-clicking on a layer tab brings up a short menu consisting of various apparently random items including Delete, Modify, Insert New, Cut, Copy or Rename.  "Visible" and "Printable" should be included in this menu layer to save steps.

**Panning:** While Open Office Draw supports the system scroll wheel vertical panning and Shift-scroll wheel horizontal pan, there is no modifier key you can hold down to enable free mouse panning, so navigation is a clumsy.

**Shortcuts:** You cannot assign your own keyboard shortcuts to the program.  Sadly, any single unmodified keypress is ignored unless an object is selected, in which case text is inserted into it.

**Simplify Outline:** There is no algorythm for automatically removing extra unwanted points from a Curve or Contour.

**Trace Outline:**  While there is no Trace Outline function, it does allow you to Convert to Bitmap on a right-click pop-up menu.  Then you can Convert to Contour, after which you will probably have to remove many gratuitous points.  [COLOR="Red"]However, last time I tried to convert a contour to bitmap, the object simply disappeared.[/COLOR]

**Zoom to Selected Object: ** Here it is 2009 and so far no drawing program, Open Office included, seems to realize that when you zoom in, the selected object should be held in the center of the window.
