### Post by graigsmith on 2006-07-15
> Do you have any specifics as far as graphics cards, capture cards, that type of stuff that you've worked with and know that it works well in linux?

if you can find an ati 9200 you get to use open source drivers.  the drivers come built in with ubuntu.  And they work with 2d and 3d great, right out of the box.  no need to install ati's buggy drivers.  The benefit to having open source drivers is the stability, and plus never needing to install the driver makes it easy.

the only downside is that the 3d is a bit slow for games, but if your not much of a games person, an ati 9200 can be had pretty cheaply.  

then i would spend the rest of the money on getting ram and proccesor.  at least 1 gig.  and if you have any money left over, get a wacom tablet, it's very easy to set up in ubuntu. and being able to draw with a pen and pressure sensitivity is much more intuitive for drawing and photo editing.

### Post by HanZo on 2006-08-02
I have an epson perfection 2580 photo and it works ok in sane (although I have some problems here and there) if you have to buy a new scanner make sure you check a hardware comaptibility list. Wacom works good with me (have an intuos3). I'm using Photoshop under wine (crossover office pro) and must say, for some tasks it's faster than under windows! gimp is ok, but if you plan to do some professional work it often shows its limits. Scribus is a nice program for layouting, although don't expect too much of it.
And check out xara LX, it's a really good vector app... in fact I find it to be superior than illustrator!
If you want to edit some type, try fontforge... it's a great app, even though it looks like it's 20 years old.
One thing I'm not sure of is how to deal with colour calibration... some apps support ICC profiles (like scribus) but i don't know how to calibrate the whole sistem, like I'd do in osx. And another thing that may be an issue is font management, you'll have to copy fonts manually into your fonts folder for activation/deactivation... works ok once you get used to it... but it's far from being an optimal solution...
I you really want to work (and by work I mean to make a living on it) I'd suggest you try it a bit before you dive into somthing you'll later regret... not that I think linux is not good for doing graphics stuff... you just can't be as professional as you can with other operating systems.
