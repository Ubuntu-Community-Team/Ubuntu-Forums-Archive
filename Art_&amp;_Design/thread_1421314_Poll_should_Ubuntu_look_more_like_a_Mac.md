### Post by 2cute4u on 2010-05-17
People who say that ANY currently available theme is like a mac is focused on all the wrong aspects.  What makes the mac look so great are the subtleties.  Rather than trying to copy their color scheme and widget styles, and proprietary artwork; we should be trying to copy there sense of balance, proportion, alignment, lighting, shadow and contrast.  


Personally,  I think that gnome theme are inherently ugly.  Why? Because the toolkit was created by geeks, not designers. So at best creating elegant themes requires a lot of tweaking and work arounds. 

For example:

1) The ubiquitous "application font" is the number 1 problem.  There need to be a separate toolbar font, menu bar font, sidebar font, input box font. window font.  Fonts also need to have better kerning and leading. 

2) Gnome themes have way too much whitespace and are unbalanced.   If there is one thing that should be copied from the mac, it should be the metrics.  Apple designers have a very good sense of how many pixels of white space are needed, and how big the various interface elements should be. A theme with mac metrics, but entirely different colors and styles, is more mac like,  than a theme that copies apples colors and widgets, but has typical gnome metrics.

3) Another thing that Mac OSX does really well but Gnome does really poorly, is handle mouse-over and mouse-click animations.   When you press a button on a mac it looks like pressing a physical button and mousing over toolbar icons and highlighting toolbar button icons is confined to making the icon brighter and leaving the surrounding whitespace unhighlighted.  The menubar like, color highlight on Gnome toolbars is ugly and should be eliminated.

I like my Ubuntu (and the future releases) to start out clean, like your new room or your new desk or a blank paper; I like its design to be minimal at first because this will give user's the feeling of freedom and that feeling will be Ubuntu's ethos.

I like my machine to be as customizable as my real life desk where I can rearrange things or mess things up and then clean it up anytime I want and with the least effort, because in real life, you can do pretty much whatever you want with your room or your desk or with a piece of paper.

I want its default installation as clean as possible but with all the crayons and pencils, erasers and markers and the tools that I need to mess it up however I want. And that freedom will be Ubuntu's most distinctive aspect. It won't rely on the themes or the colors, or the docks and applets, but on the freedom it gives.

### Post by capraMambrica on 2010-06-12
The reason why OSX and mac apps look the way they do, is because Apple sat down and put together a rigid set of interface guidelines. Well, I know they did this for their iPhone apps, and I imagine the same is true for the rest of their apps. Tools like the Interface Builder in xCode, make it very easy to make a 'osx app' looking app.

This is often a frustrating way to work for a designer who whats to make something unique, but it's a blessing for someone who is just writing a tool that they want to share with others. And of course a blessing for apple, as they have done all the design work for you, your tools look polished on their system (just look at all the nasty Visual Studio tools on windows, compared to their OSX ports). Plus Apple have conditioned their users to expect certain things in an application. Look at the success of the iPhone. A user, new to the technology, takes only a few hours to get used to using one or two apps, and then every other app ever released is picked up in seconds. Applications I see on Android and other smart phones don't have this sort of co-ordination, and users find it difficult jump between apps.

So what I believe Ubuntu could benefit from, and may in fact have it and I'm just not aware, is a design spec (and possibly some tools) to help developers make apps that look more polished, and give users a more comfortable experience. 

But, that's a really hard thing to do in an open-source community. Apple can do it, they own the tools to make OSX software (xCode) and the distribution outlets (App Store). Do it our way, or starve. Here, that sort of attitude wont fly. Thank god. But Ubuntu apps, are also general Linux apps. So that comes into the equation as well. 

So a better question (IMO) is: "Should Ubuntu look more like a mac", but "How can Ubuntu give users a polished and consistent, yet highly customisable, interface".

There are a lot of leaves that should be taken from Apple books,  there are a lot to be replaced and added by the open source community.
