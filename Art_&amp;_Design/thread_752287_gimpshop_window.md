### Post by duckgoesoink on 2008-04-11
> **M00MPH said:**
> Does anybody know why gimpshop is opening in different windows instead of one?

That feature is only for Windows (not linux). Gimpshop for linux changes all the menus around to resemble photoshop ones, but doesn't include the deweirdifier (unfortunately).

You can change the GIMP (or Gimpshop) preferences to *kind of* keep the toolbox and docks inside an open image window -  go to Window Management in the preferences and change the Window Manager Hints to "Utility window".

### Post by duckgoesoink on 2008-04-12
> **hessiess said:**
> gimp isn't that difarant from phtoshop on mac. I don't see what the problem is. 

Mac doesn't use a task bar with a list of windows - it has the dock instead. The problem everyone complains of in Windows and Linux is that when you open GIMP there are three windows listed in the taskbar, and to minimise the program takes a few clicks (not just one). 

But changing the WIndow Manager Hints to "Utility window" means there is only one window listed in the taskbar (the image window), and the toolbox and docks stay inside the image window and kind of minimise with it if you have other windows open (not very well, but it's an improvement).

### Post by st0n3cutt3r on 2008-07-02
> **hessiess said:**
> gimp isn't that difarant from phtoshop on mac. I don't see what the problem is.

One EXTREMELY important difference between gimp and photoshop on mac is that in photoshop (any version) when you click on the tools or layers 'windows' your keyboard commands still go to the front image when you press them, so if you hit CTRL/CMD+A it will select the entire canvas no matter what in photoshop you clicked on last. In the gimp you have to click back and forth between the windows constantly if you don't know all of the keyboard commands (and can thereby get away without ever clicking away from your image window).

> **hessiess said:**
> whats the point in having all that wated gray spase when you could have something open in the bacground? prsonaly I dispise the way graphics programs work on windows.

The gray background for the window is to keep your eyes from adjusting to any non-neutral colors. It's very important to have a color/value neutral space while trying to do any color corrections/adjustments, because without it your are very likely to visually compensate for the background colors on your screen.

In archival/professional image editing, editors will frequently sit in a gray room, have a medium-gray desktop background (no picture; just "color"), use a gray color scheme on their computer, and have the lights in the room at about 'sunglasses dim' so that their eyes will not accidentally adjust to anything and affect their work.
