### Post by rmccutchan on 2010-09-08
Unfortunately, there is always a trade off.  With the smaller cameras that have small sensors, it is more difficult to get a good soft bokeh effect than the dslr's with bigger sensors because the depth of field is greater.  I had an Olympus E-20 as my first professional "dslr" with a maximum aperture of 2.8, and it had a small sensor.  You had to be really close to your subject to get the background to really go out of focus, and even then it never really quite got there.  I then moved to a Canon 10D and 20D, and that made a lot of difference, if I used a good quality prime lens, not a cheap zoom.  I bought a screw mount adapter for the Canons, and use Takumar lenses from the '50's and '60's (especially the 50mm 1.4), and  you just can't believe how soft the background is.

I am not trying to talk you into a dslr, and I understand about going with a smaller camera.   I wish my dslr's were a little smaller sometimes.  But you can pick up great deals on older dslr's on ebay.  I am just sharing my experience.  It may not be much help, but it is my 2 pennies.

Check out [http://www.dpreview.com/](http://www.dpreview.com/)  They have lots information on ANY digital camera, present, past, and future.  They give lots of specs (sensor size, shutter lag, picture samples, dimensions, etc.........)  Here is a sample page from their site.  The top half contains current cameras, and the bottom half has older cameras:  [http://www.dpreview.com/reviews/specs/Canon/](http://www.dpreview.com/reviews/specs/Canon/)

BTW-Sony has a new small camera with a bigger sensor that accepts their standard dslr lenses.  A small camera with a 50mm 1.4 Zeiss lens and APS sized sensor would be a fantastic rig for just about any occasion :-)

Also, don't let the mega pixel game fool you.  More is not always better.  The quality of the sensor is what is important.  More pixels sometimes translates into more noise, ESPECIALLY if the camera is a low priced camera.  I've had beautiful 16x20 inch portraits printed with the 5 mega pixel Olympus.

Also, there is a lot of software out there that can be used in Ubuntu that can work with your photographs.  Imageviewer will open any .jpeg or .tiff, and probably many other formats.

Sorry for the lengthy post.
