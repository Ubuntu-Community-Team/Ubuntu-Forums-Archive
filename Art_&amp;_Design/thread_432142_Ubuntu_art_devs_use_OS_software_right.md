[LIST=1]

### Post by affected on 2007-05-20
Jeez.

Yea, a skilled person can create great work with a stick and mud if need be, but how often do you see that actually happening? Whatever your idealized view on the matter, a highly skilled oil painter will prefer high-quality paints and brushes, and similarly professional artists in the digital field will use the best tools they can find, not any piece of crud they come across. A tool won't turn anyone into an artist, but an artist can use a GOOD tool to excellent effect. Name one truly outstanding digital painter who uses Gimp or any other OS 2d application. I have never heard of anyone, and if you think there isn't a reason for that, you're deluding yourself. People who work with time constraints use applications with an efficient workflow: Photoshop, Painter, and I know of one guy who uses Corel Photopaint. Painting is difficult enough as it is, one does not want to struggle with clunky tools in addition to the mental challenge the task one is working on presents. That's why, when weighing the option of taking the time to boot into Windows to use Photoshop vs. taking the time to struggle with the Gimp, I will choose booting for all but the most mundane of tasks. To be honest, I found even the quickpaint tool in Shake better for painting than the Gimp.
