### Post by esalnoj on 2009-08-11
GIMP (Gnu Image Manipulation Program) comes stock with ubuntu.

It works similar to photoshop, with layers and similar tools. There are tutorials for GIMP available.

You could also dual-boot with a windows distro if you are used to photoshop that much.

If you build an even more powerful machine, you could run windows in virtual machine for photoshop.

If you have a internet connection and cd burner, it wouldn't hurt to download an .iso of jaunty, burn the image to CD, then boot the CD to "try ubuntu before installing", and have a look at GIMP (applications->graphics->GIMP).

### Post by VeeDubb on 2009-08-26
One of the things that get's left out of a lot of discussions on this subject, is the difference between a ***photographer*** and a ***graphic designer who takes pictures***.

A photographer learns how to use their camera properly, and capture the right image the first time.  Raw processing software (like BibblePro or LightRoom) simply takes the place of what used to happen in a photo lab, plus a few extra things relating to digital.  Basic corrections are made to color and contrast, cropping, straightening, correction for lens distortion, and then sharpening, noise reduction and spot correction are carried out.  Also, digital filters may be used in place of filters that used to e more common with film cameras, since digital filters can completely replace all filters except for ND, polarizers, and a few specialty filters like IR filters.

None of those things require Photoshop ***or*** gimp.  In fact, none of those things are even any easier with photoshop or gimp, and most are more difficult, because while both programs 'can' do batch processing, neither one was specifically made for batch processing.

There are a couple of batch raw processing programs which are open source, but while they are developing quickly, none of them can come close to the functionality of lightroom or BibblePro.  It's already been pointed out in this thread that BibblePro comes in a native linux version, and the next release which is already out as a beta, is at least as good as, if not better than, adobe lightroom.

If a person spends any meaningful amount of time doctoring their photographs, they are not a photographer.  They are a graphic designer who takes pictures.  There's nothing wrong with that, but it's a different thing entirely.

### Post by rmccutchan on 2009-09-15
I have been a semi professional photographer, or weekend warrior, if you will, for about 10 years.  When I went digital, I used and Olympus E-20 and Photoshop 7 and shot in jpeg in the camera because there was no way to shoot raw efficiently with that camera.  It had a 4 shot buffer and 30 or 40 second write time for raw images.  And compact flash cards were VERY expensive at the time.  There was no way to do a wedding like that!

In my opinion, GIMP is everything PS7 was, or more.  I use the healing brush extensively for senior portraits, and most all of the tools are VERY similar to PS.   I also use layer masks and a couple of dodge and burn methods extensively for black and white fine art type photographs.

Fast forward to last month:  I had been using Lightroom/PSCS as my work flow, however I did not use Lightroom as a file management tool.  I grouped images into their respective catagories inside a folder on my desktop.  I just needed something to convert the raw Canon images efficiently.  I would pull up the various groups of photographs and make the major adjustments in lightroom (exposure, highlight recovery, color balance,....), and save them as 8 bit tiff images, and then do the finishing touches in PS (blemish removal, diffusion, crop, black and white, and various others), then save as tiff or jpeg depending on where the photograph was going to be used.  Usually at this point, jpeg was fine.  Many wedding professionals claim to use jpeg right from the camera.

Fast forward to today:  I now use Rawtherapee to do everything I did in light room, but convert the files to 8 bit PNG files instead of tiff, unless I don't need to do any touchups, then I save them as tiff or jpeg.  From there, I pull up the ones I need to touch up in GIMP and finish the process.

It may not be the best raw work flow for everybody, but I works for me.

One thing I have noticed is that when I save images as PNG and open them in GIMP, the files are huge and there seems to be a lot more latitude towards lightening and darkening, especially compared to jpeg.

As you can tell, I don't have a strict color management system.  The lab I use (Mpix) requires that the images be in sRGB.  I had them send me a calibration kit and I adjusted my monitor as close as I can  to the print they sent, and have had great results.

So far, I haven't missed a beat in the way I do my raw work flow.

Just thought this might help.

BTW - I am surprised that nobody has mentioned digiKam.  I tried it, and it seems to be along the same lines as Lightroom.  Also, you can edit images in 16 bit with Cinepaint and Krita, but I don't believe you can get Cinepaint for Ubuntu.
