### Post by VeeDubb on 2008-12-26
I own two Nikon digital SLR cameras.

An older Nikon D50, and a relatively new Nikon D80.  Over the last year I have been building a business (which will hopefully become my sole income over the next year or two) as a wedding portrait photographer.  You've gotten some super responses already, but I'll add a few more details.


1. There's no such thing as "mixed optical/digital".  All cameras are optical.  Period.  What you're talking about is mixed FILM/digital.

There is no quality loss using lenses which were originally designed for a film camera on a digital, and no loss of features provided that the lens is a recent enough model that it has the correct mount, electronics, and focusing mechanism.

However, lens technology has grown by leaps and bounds in recent decades, and some kit lenses today have higher quality optics than the pro lenses of 15 years ago.  So, a film lens is fine, but old lenses suck regardless of their intended use.  (There are exceptions of course, like the Nikkor 50mm f1.4 which has been around for ever and still takes beautiful photographs)

Also, unless

2. Before I say this one, let me admit that every camera I have ever owned has been a Nikon, and I am highly brand loyal.  That being said, I would advise AGAINST a Canon dSLR.  The reason for this, is that for years now, Canon has focused on increasing the resolution of their image sensors to the exclusion of all other factors that affect image quality.  

The fact of the matter is that pro quality 35mm film breaks grain at roughly the same degree of enlargement as a 6mp digital camera.  Having a 20MP dSLR is a joke, with no real world application.  I forces you to spend idiotic amounts of money on HUGE memory cards, and makes working with the images increasingly difficult.  Plus, you just end up down sizing them to print anything but a poster anyway.

For several years, Canon really took the whole pro-market.  But, in the last year or two, photographers have finally figured out that they neither want or need 20MP and larger sensors, and have started moving to Nikon for their superior optical quality and low light performace. (The latest line, including the D90, D300, D700 and D3 have better low light performance than any dSLR ever made before now)

3. Regardless of whether you get a canon or a Nikon, you probably will not want to re-use your old lenses anyway. Here's why:

The image sensor in a digital SLR (unless you buy a $2-4k studio model) is much smaller than a frame of 35mm film.  What this means, is that you will get a smaller field of view.  Nikon is the easiest to figure out, because it's a crop factor of 1.5 (I think canon is 1.6 or 1.7)

In terms of depth of field and all of the other things that are affected by focal length, a 50mm lens is a 50mm lens regardless of what format you're using it on.  However, in terms of a field of view, a 50mm lens on a Nikon dSLR, will look like it was taken with a 75mm lens.  So, your normal lens just became a portrait telephoto.

So, let's take a popular kit lens.  The 28-80 was a popular lens length for a walk-a-round lens on several film cameras.  It get's you from a nice wide angle, to a portrait telephoto.

But, on a dSLR, it would have the same field of view as if you were using a 42-120.  That's a pretty awkward focal length for a walk-a-round lens.  42 isn't really wide angle at all, and 120 is a nice improvement on the telephoto end, but not long enough to replace longer glass.

On a Nikon dSLR, you would need an 18-55 to get the same range as a 28-80 on 35mm film.  (my primary lens for wedding is a 17-50)


So, in short, buy a nikon, and buy new glass.

### Post by VeeDubb on 2008-12-30
While I may have "slightly" overstated the lack of use found in a 20MP+ camera, HDR has **nothing** to do with this conversation at all.

HDR means "*High Dynamic Range.*"  It refers to the processes of merging bracketed exposures into a single image with a dynamic range that is larger than would normally be possible.

(Dynamic range refers to range of exposure in the image.  i.e. it's impossible to take a picture from inside a darkened room, looking out a sunny window, and have both the room and window properly exposed. You could accomplish the image by taking two shots from the same perspective.  One that is exposed for the wall, and one that is exposed for the bright sunny scene outside, and merging the images together)

The resolution of your image is absolutely irrelevant to HDR.

There are only two reasons to have a super-hi-res camera. (not counting unusual, commercial and scientific applications)

1. You plan on making extremely large prints which will be viewed from a very close distance. (Many billboards are not high enough resolution to use as desktop wallpaper, but they look smooth and sharp because they are viewed at a distance)

2. You are a rotten photographer, and you need the extra resolution so you can stink at composition and fix it later.


And for the record, using a 20MP image to make "normal" sized prints can actually cause a lot of really ugly visual artifacts if it's not downsized carefully for printing.

### Post by kayosiii on 2009-01-28
Because all cameras (except pin hole camera's) have lenses... that is just the way they work. Every lense that is made is some sort of compromise between weight, price, quality and flexibility... The best quality lenses aren't the most flexible and are expensive to manufacture and usually quite bulky. The more zoom a lens has generally the more compromises have to be made on quality... 

When you buy a compact digital that compromise is chosen for you when you buy the camera... usually you get something that is quite flexible and of Adequate quality. Often the lens can be the determining factor of how much detail the camera can capture (moreso than the number of photosites on the sensor). Digital trickery can go so far but it can't make a photo that was taken with an ordinary lens look like it was taken with a fantastic lens.

With a digital SLR... you have the choice to choose between several compromises of quality, cost,  weight and flexibility... 
Most serious DSLR users end up spending a lot more money on lenses than they do the body. (and usually end up being tied to the same brand of camera for this reason)... I do the majority of my photography with a good Prime (non-zoom) lens and the difference in quality between that and a general purpose lense is very noticable.
