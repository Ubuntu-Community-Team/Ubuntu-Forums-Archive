### Post by em3raldxiii on 2006-08-13
A couple of points (my opinions mostly, but others would likely agree).
 
1.  Complementary colors.  You don't typically want to mix clashing colors (Green and Purple would be an example of badly clashing colors).  It is indeed possible to make them work, but you are far better off with complementation (Beige with burgundy highlights, or Blue with white highlights).
 
2.  Continuity of design.  You want your icons to have a certain collective family quality.  If some of your icons are bubbly, they should all have some kind of reference to bubbliness.  If you like high-tech icons, then you should go with all high-tech.  The icon that is different will stick out like a sore thumb.
 
3.  Contrast.  You want the right balance of contrast.  Excessively high contrast is harsh on the eyes and may make you irritated over time.  Excessively low contrast can make things hard to read simply because everything blurs together.  So instead of choosing super-dark blue with bright white text, you may want to mute them slightly, but avoid medium blue with medium blue text.
 
4.  Clear differentiation.  You want your fonts to be legible, but appeal to you.  Arial and Verdana are two interesting examples.  People typically like Arial for medium sized fonts, but for very small they prefer Verdana because it has some extra width to retain legibility.  Sharp clear lines are more aesthetically pleasing for the "active" portions of your computer (icons, text, windows, etc).  Your desktop image, however, is best suited to blending colors with very few drastic color changes.  Excessively "noisy" backgrounds can obscure destktop items.
 
5.  Familiarity.  People like icons from other OSs because they have a certain recognizability.  If the shapes and ideas are too abstract, the learning curve can be too sharp and can decrease productivity.  On the other hand, everyone likes the idea of using "unique" or "original" material on their computers, which is part of the draw of Linux I suspect.  Regardless, if your icons are fresh but immediately recognizable, you've got a potential winner.
 
Otherwise, colors for your computer can be closely tied to traditional color psychology.  Microsoft chose blue for its cool and soothing effects, particularly combined with smooth blending lines and crisp edges.  Ubuntu has chosen the beiges, red-browns, and taupes because they are warm, inviting and friendly.  They appeal to family-oriented people.  For more on colors, perhaps check out the Wikipedia.
 
And yes, I would be interested to read a draft of your little piece.  What format are you using?  Expository essay style, or free-writing perhaps?  just curious :D 
 
Keep up the good work!
