### Post by kuniyori on 2009-04-28
This post is a little long so please do not be put off by it!!!! Also please excuse any typos, I tried to keep them to a minimum.:lolflag:

Most of the people that submit design ideas to Ubuntu are not the average home user. (This applies to me also!! :D) We are use to (and sometimes demand) the quirks that come with using Linux. You have to actively find, install and customise the distro yourself. This is not what the average home user wants. They do not want to spend hours searching for codex's so they can play MP3s or want to learn how to use rsync to do backups. They want to push a button and *POOF* it does what they want. We can see by the way open source programs are developed that they start out as the itch of developer who needs the tool themselves. The final product fulfils their need and is not too complicated for them... This leads to a situation where the majority of these programs are not suitable for home use. This is the purpose of this discussion, to go through Ubuntu with a fine tooth comb, to debait the choice of programs bundled with the distribution, to focus on the look and feel of the distro and critique the end user experience.

There is also another major issue to be tackled that is the distro distribution model. There are different flavours of Ubuntu each one limited to that size of a single CD image. There are two problems with this model. The distro's are not split up upon functional lines but divided up according to the windows manager and desktop environment they use. The limiting of the size of the bistro to a single CD ISO also creates its own problems. With bandwidth increasing and no difference in the price of media size should not be as much of a priority and could serve as a major disadvantage in the future.

A single desktop ISO file is meant to serve both the home and workstation/business/corporate environment, is this the right way to go? I would argue that this is a foolish thing to do especially because of the limiting factor of the ISO size. There are numerous ways to improve the experience for both user markets. There are two options that could be implemented:
	[1] Divide the distros into different user groups.
		#This would allow for tighter integration of programs and for Live CDs that are replicas of the normal installer.
		#Must be used sparingly, you do not want to end up in a MS Windows situation.
	[2] Create a single DVD ISO with a new installer that allows for install classes smiler to the Red Hat installer.
		#Allows for the building of one distro that will do everything with as many different set-ups as you can ask for being catered for.
		#May not allow for as tight an integration between all the programs as a dedicated distro but it offers a more tailored approach than the current system of distribution.
Using a mixture of both techniques may be the way to go. For example: a home distribution that has the ability to cater for the photo enthusiast, or a gamer.

There is still a problem of what programs should go into a basic home OS.There are many programs in the distro that no home user would ever use or ever need to use. Home users are not Linux power users. They do not need to use remote desktops or even terminal services for the most part. They need things to just work out of the box. Good defaults are better than the ability to change everything you don't like (good defaults = less stuff you don't like).

The home market OS has certain pilers which have to be in place or it is useless. These are:
		#It should just work out of the box with good defaults. (It should be easy to use!! I should not need a degree to use it!!)
		#It should look good. (Shiny!!!)
		#It should be relatively stable and secure. (NO VIRUSES!!)
It also has certain application requirements:
		#It needs to be able to play/store users media.
		#It needs to be able to do basic editing of users media.
		#It needs to be able to make backup of users media.
		#It needs to be able to fully use the internet.

Here are some of my notes on applications to improve the user experience and quality of the distro.

Graphics Applications:
		#There are numerous graphics applications for Linux but most are very basic or very advanced editors (F-Spot Photo Manager, GIMP, Inkscape). They are good programs in their own right but are they right for the home market? The answer I have to come to is no. There are numerous reasons for this. Most of them being that they are not comprehensive enough, as in the case of F-Spot (no meta-data support and no RAW file support), or they are overly complex like GIMP and Inkscape. A model to look at for home use would be Adobe Lightroom and Apple Apature. They both contain an integrated work flow system, from initial import of photos in various formats (JPEG, RAW, etc.), to basic adjustment (meta-data, tags, filing, colour correction, cropping, conversion, printing, archiving, etc). They do all this in an easy to use, intuitive interface. The only problem with these packages for the home user is their price. They position themselves perfectly at both the professional (through decreasing time spent on important, repetitive tasks) and at the home market (through integrated work flow and ease of use). An all in one solution such as this would make any Linux distro an excellent choice for both the home and professional photography market. The best program that matches this discription at the moment is DigiKam. While it is a good system it has problems. It is designed to work with KDE not GNOME and its layout and GUI are not very polished.

	Sound and Video:
		#There needs to be support for iPod Touch and iPhone included without jailbreaking. It is one of the most popular media players and to not have it supported is a serious turn-off for home users who might be looking to switch or to an OEM looking to give a Linux offering aimed at the home market. There is also the major problem of a lack of installed/available codex's. No standard media support out of the box is just not acceptable in the home market. Media playback is one of the pilers of the home use market along with the internet. There also needs to be better support for ripping, burning and converting media. As it stands Linux falls short of the mark when compared to any of the commercial offerings (Windows/MacOSX). There is also a lack of an effective, viable video editor. Media is one area where Linux really is lagging behind due to its stance on DRM and copyright.

	System Tools:
		#With more and more of peoples lives being placed on computers (photos, video, music, documents, mail, accounts, etc.) there is an overwhelming need now more than ever for the home user to back up their systems and personal files. This has always been possible but was overly complicated or hindered by expensive media. Today with there being relatively cheap reusable media the hinderence seems to be the ease of use of backup software (or lack there of)! To not even have any installed as default, even a hard to use program. It is as important in the home environment as it is in the business market. This needs to be a high priority for the development team. TimeVault is a step in the right direction but needs to be more stable and have a more user-friendly layout. e.g. A basic settings and an advanced settings section.


*CORE PROGRAMS*
There are numerous programs that should be integrated and included by default to improve both looks and work flow. These are as follows:
		#Advant Windows Manager			(MacOSX like dockbar)			Easy access to commonly used programs; added benefit of eye candy
		#GNOME Do				(Quick launch and search all in one)	Great tool to launch and find any program or file, also has good default skins for eye candy
		#Compiz Fusion				(Advanced desktop effects)		EYE CANDY CENTRAL!!! Brings the power of OpenGL to any basic desktop for 3D effects and animation.
		#Screenlets				(Widget Manager)			Easy way to integrate programs into the desktop and to add extra functionality. Once again an opportunity for eye candy.

*CONCLUSION*
		#This should not be taken to be a set in stone blueprint, but is intended to get people to think about how the distro is developed.

While many people will not think that this is in the appropriate section I would argue otherwise. Every part of this is a design issue. I would really like to hear peoples comments on this as I am hoping to submit numerous design proposals to different sections of the ubuntu team.
