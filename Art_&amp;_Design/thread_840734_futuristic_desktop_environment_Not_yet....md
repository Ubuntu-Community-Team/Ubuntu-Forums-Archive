### Post by Tuxoid on 2008-06-26
Sorry, it would have been a lot easier to understand if I explained it.

This is a Usability project I am working on. It's called the Work or Recreational Basis Desktop (or WoRB for short). The name is meant not to sound exciting; rather practical, or "says what it does". Much of the work I am putting into the project as of now, is Usability research. I have been looking back as far as the Xerox Star, and Xerox's decisions on that technology. I am trying to take a discriminating look at every component used on desktops today. From the way programs are accessed, to task bars. I have taken this to the point of questioning the desktop metaphor itself. I am aware that Usability is only effective if others can use what is developed. This post is not intended to test such.


Okay, here it goes from top to bottom:

- In the top left is a welcome window that runs when the desktop. It is only meant to explain the desktop to the user. It will only run the first time the desktop is used.

- In the center of the screen, there is the main access method programs. This the circle in the middle; it is called the WoRB Portal. I have put it in the center of the screen to help draw-in the user. It is split up into four categories. Once a category is clicked, the three other options disappear, and the options in that category appear, as well as a back button. If the WoRB portal has been hidden behind windows, the Windows Key (or Super L) can be pressed to reveal, or the user can click on a button in the bottom right corner of their screen. Anyway, here is a description each category:

* In the top-left: Activities. This hold all general, everyday-usage applications. Word Processors, Media Players, Graphic Editing applications. The different choices between each between genres of applications (like word processors), are called methods. For WoRB, the terminology is meant to be more understandable for both experienced computer users, and people quite new to computers.

* In the bottom-left: Work and Data. This launches the file browser known as "Peeper". That's pretty much it.

* In the top-right: Experience Preferences. This holds much of the same type of stuff that Gnome would hold in System>Preferences.

* In the bottom-right: Management. This is for Administration. I didn't call this section Administration, because I believe that would have sounded intimidating.

- Near the WoRB Portal is a shiny red cursor.

- At the bottom of the screen is something called the "task spinner". This is the WoRB equivalent of a task bar. As more tasks are opened, the get categorized by purpose. Parent windows are also automatically grouped with their children (that's why you see a cerberus Wilber). In the art category, you can see, The Gimp (and its three window setup), Blender, and an extra image loaded in the Gimp (that's from left to right). Above the Art tasks, is the Internet tasks. There is a Web Browser loaded here.

The rest of the mock-up, including the text at the top, is the desktop background (which I will admit, could use some work).
