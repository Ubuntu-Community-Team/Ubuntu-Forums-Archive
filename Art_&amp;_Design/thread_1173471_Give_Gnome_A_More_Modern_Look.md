### Post by alex.rayu on 2009-06-02
Come on, don't twist my words out. I mentioned just the two things that I would like to see. 

Modern means, that there are things happening in the desktop world, certain design trends and technologies get employed that make desktop more aesthetic and intuitive. Of course, there have been and still are people, geeks, for whom a command line and Win 98 look is all they need "'cause it works". I am not talking about this attitude - this is the past of Linux, there has been a conscience leap away from that geeky red-eyed attitude, so whoever wants that, there's kernel, there's Gentoo and Cent OS, go, enjoy, compile.

But as for the "modern", there are a few trends, both in technology and aesthetics, that are there. And "modern" does not have to mean slow, of which the upcoming Win 7 is a proof, which has not yet been released, but has already won warmest comments, for it's great modern look, and it's wonderful performance. 

Aesthetics is important, and there have been numerous themes out there for Gnome, either imitating Windows, Mac, or proposing own vision of a modern design. But there still are fundamental problems with all these designs. Problems, or, limitations, which result from the underlying GTK toolkit. It is old, and does not have and RGBA, which results in a whole are design problems with applying translucencies, transparencies, etc. There is also a fundamental lack of in interface graphical acceleration. As someone has pointed, without compiz, form elements appear after the form, and disappear after the form is already gone (especially evident under certain configurations). So,there's a need to do what was done to QT - a great new look, great new API, great new abstraction and scalability. Without it, Gnome will die out, like the Enlightenment desktop did.

So, it *is* RGBA, that is a given, then, it is an OpenGL integration (there is no graphics card today that does not support that). Tha will allow for better animation and direct rendering, that will also be more performance-efficient. Take again, Apple's quartz. Back in 2001, on the old equipment, already ran it's interface animations smoothly and with great performance.

Then, there's integration. We have it now, and it's good in many areas in Gnome, except for that Evolution monstrosity and everything that has to deal with it. And there's more, of course, but without these it's really hard, or almost impossible, to compete.

With KDE 4 already there, Win 7 almost there, and Apple having been around for quite some time now, Gnome's future now is very unclear. The devs don't know what to do. There is a huge argument right now whether to redo the toolkit, or to just continue "dragging it on".

That's how I view this.
