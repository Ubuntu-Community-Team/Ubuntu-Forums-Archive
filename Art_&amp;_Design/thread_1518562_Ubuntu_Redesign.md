### Post by mbarvian on 2010-06-26
I just submitted the following message to Canonical. Hopefully the reply: 

Hi guys, 

First off, let me start by saying Ubuntu. I love that it's free and has 
great software and a great community, etc. 

That being said, I really think it's time for a major overhaul of the 
user interface. I realize the time it must have taken for 10.04, and I 
appreciate the extra polish in it (like the ubiquity slideshow). 
Moreover, I adore some of the improvements you guys yourselves came up 
with, like the new notification system, which is attractive and rivals 
Windows and OSX for functionality, in my opinion. However, I believe 
that for Ubuntu to be attractive to the average consumer, it must have 
looks that compete with Windows or Mac OSX (even if you are not trying 
to compete with these two directly for average consumers). I understand 
it's not all your fault: Linux itself is rather ugly, with its dull 
gradients and icons, and outdated interface. I tried doing some theming 
myself, and found that there are not many elements in GTK to control, 
and I felt very restricted in changing what I could. 

The wallpaper is also something I feel needs improving. Almost all 
Linux wallpapers I've seen look blurry and somewhat amateur. In my 
opinion, the best wallpaper in Ubuntu was the brown elephant texture. 
But this is just a minor issue, as wallpapers are easily changeable. On 
a side note, I'd recommend having nautilus-wallpaper pre-installed 
out-of-the-box, as dragging the wallpaper into the appearance window 
right now is somewhat less intuitive. 

Another thing I know you are concerned with is accessibility, and 
support for older hardware. While I whole-heartedly agree with your 
decision to keep older hardware in mind, I also believe that solid 
colors and gradients alone are not appealing. Maybe you could try 
something like Windows 7 Basic vs Aero? I know you already do something 
similar with Compiz, but I think a more noticeable change might be 
better. Again, I'm fairly sure that Metacity does not support 
transparency (I'm no expert by any means). 

Some examples of great Ubuntu designs (or mockups, rather) that I've 
found on the Internet are: 
[http://is.gd/d4rH6](http://is.gd/d4rH6) - a very attractive one, especially the window 
borders and panel colors 
[http://is.gd/d4rMA](http://is.gd/d4rMA) - another good one, simple yet elegant (even though I 
disagree with the author's decision to include a "Porn" folder in his 
mockup, and I hope you disregard that) 
[http://is.gd/d4rU8](http://is.gd/d4rU8) - an actual screenshot, not mockup (elegant, with 
nice icons) 

Also, even though I know you removed the feature of changing the login 
screen appearances in 9.10, this GDM theme is very, very pretty and 
functional: [http://is.gd/d4s1P](http://is.gd/d4s1P). 

One last thing is the color choice. I hope I don't offend anyone by 
stating that purple and orange are not very mainstream colors. A new, 
more consumer-friendly color scheme would be greatly appreciated, like 
neutral blues or even greens. It's up to you, obviously. 

The reason I am sending this to Ubuntu and not any other distribution is 
that I am under the impression that you have the most resources than any 
other distribution. While I don't know for sure, I judging solely by 
your innovations like the brilliant Ubuntu One service and the popular 
social network integration. The Ubuntu team never ceases to disappoint. 

Finally, I understand that a complete makeover would not be an easy 
task. However, I feel that, in order to excel with the operating 
system, it is necessary. Not only would it be more appealing for the 
average end-user, but it would also significantly differentiate your 
distribution from the other popular ones. 

I'm sorry if I come across sounding "pushy". Looking back at the 
distribution a year ago (the 9.04 release in particular) makes it easy 
to see just how far the project has progressed in such little time. 
This makes me confident that this somewhat debatable flaw with Ubuntu 
(and Linux in general) will eventually be solved. 

Again, I love Ubuntu. I'm using Evolution right now to type this 
e-mail. 

I greatly look forward to your response and opinions. 

Sincerely, 
Maxwell

### Post by masque7 on 2010-07-25
Take into consideration that the other operating systems you keep mentioning don't allow the user to easily change the appearance and themes without third-party applications. We have the freedom to do so, as GNU/Linux is renowned for its level of customisation.

At the end of the day it is about freedom of choice. There will always be difference of opinion about the default theme and that is something you have to accommodate for. Understand that including more by default will make the installation larger, and it needs to be ~700mb to fit onto a normal CD-R. A lot of this eye-candy also requires 2d and 3d acceleration. Practicality is the issue when it comes to GUI customisation. Do MacOS X and Windows 7 with all their flashyness consider the array of hardware that Ubuntu does? No. That is why their hardware requirements are greater.

Whilst shiny buttons and effects from Compiz and docks might please some people, the idea is that you have a fairly robust operating system ready to use without having all the bloat from Compiz, the GBs worth of themes, and extra resource requirements that drag you down. Artwork and theming sites like gnome-look exist for a reason, as do Compiz and docks and other effects. They don't make it a better operating system. It doesn't help you get your work done or run application any better. That is why Compiz is not fully enabled by default. If you want to paint your house certain colours, fair enough, but don't paint the whole neighbourhood.

We have a theme and desktop by default on Ubuntu. The whole idea of this is to keep it neutral. Not too bloated, yet not too minimal. Something in the middle of hot and cold colours; a wallpaper that isn't too dull, but not to flashy. Don't disable desktop effects entirely, but have some, yet don't affect the users on setups that can't handle it.

When you're providing any type of product or service for people to use, you have to consider your choices from a neutral, broad perspective. Whether it be the design of hospitals, offices, schools, or an operating system interface, a large number of people are going to be using it.
