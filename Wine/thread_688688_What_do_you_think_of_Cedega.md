### Post by dmn_clown on 2008-02-06
> **ondo311 said:**

Transgaming as a company tends to use highly misleading advertising[1] to get paying subscribers, their extremely understaffed company has for the past year or so been overwhelmed due to their cider contract with Electronic Arts to support games on Mac.  

Their marketing says that any and all advances made in cider are being added into the cedega source tree but realistically this will only help the people that have the games that use cider to run on Mac. 

The main reason people are willing to use cedega over wine is the copy protection issues, an argument that is quickly loosing steam as more versions of copy protection are being supported by wine.  

Wine also has better MSI support, so much better that transgaming has recently made the move to update their codebase against the current wine tree.  

Supposedly transgaming are contributing patches back to wine to further development, but it remains to be seen if their actions are as good as their word.  I only know of three patches contributed back.

In short:  Transgaming as a company is terrible, their support for the core linux base is atrocious as evidenced by the huge numbers of open support tickets and  I haven't seen any real reason to buy their product when winehq's product is free, supports more games, I have full access to the source code, and new bugs are quickly dealt with.

[1] After the release of TES:  Oblivion, it was found that the game would run in cedega using the Oldblivion patch, the initial press release made no mention of that requirement only that the game ran in Cedega 6.0 (their web page was subsequently edited to include this fact, but the game ran in wine without the oldblivion patch)

### Post by buntunub on 2008-02-08
There are some fundamental flaws with Wine. Biggest one is that its a Linux project that tries to get Windows based programs to run on Linux using a Windows compatibility layer! That in itself defies logic, but there are some apps it does do fairly well only because someone who wanted that Windows program to work on wine had some programming know-how and had the time and willpower to make it happen. Thats fundamental flaw number 2 right there. Someone has to put in the time and effort who actually knows wtf they are doing to make things work. Gamers are typically unlikely to be the ones to do this 1) because they want to spend their time playing, and not slaving at a terminal, and 2) most people who love and play Windows based games all the time are not likely to be very Linux savvy. This is where Cedega has some edge over wine, particularly in the case of EVE Online, where CCP pays them to maintain a Linux Client. Even in that case, the wine client is usually not very far behind, because of upstream contributions from TG or CCP. Then there is flaw #3, which is that the concept of wine is to create compatibility with an inferior Operating System. How can anything good come from that?
