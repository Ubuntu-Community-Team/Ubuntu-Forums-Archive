While I haven't bought one of the new Ubuntu Dells I _have_ talked to some of the people who work at canonical support.  They really encourage you to use community support before paying for their service.  They spent a lot of time building the community on here (the forums), on IRC, in the Loco groups (look for the one in your area to meet with other Ubuntu users) and many many other community avenues.  In fact, they would rather that the kid next door learn how to fix your computer, or that a series of businesses arises that provide Ubuntu support rather than them doing it.  Why, because Ubuntu grows faster that way.  They prefer their support be for BUSINESS USERS who can't have the kid next door help... who have a tech staff.

So my advice.  NO.  don't get the support.  You'll have it from the forums, from IRC, from google, and even from your neighbor.  :-)

### Post by kidcharles on 2007-06-12
I just got my Inspiron laptop yesterday.

My experience with the video was that the factory install did not have the proper resolution available (1440 x 900) as a choice under System -> Preferences -> Screen Resolution.  However, as soon as I installed nvidia-glx it was fine. The /etc/X11/xorg.conf file needed no tweaking.

I would say you shouldn't worry about problems, due to the fact that quite a few people are going to get these things, the forums will probably be a good up-to-date source of fixes should anything come up.  These forums are well known for their friendliness, speed, and quality and they are totally free. Also, since these machines are designed to be compatible, it is that less likely you will have difficulties.

I encourage you to give Ubuntu (or Linux in general) a shot if you haven't already, on a Dell or not. It's a mature operating system that can be a real joy to use.

### Post by CaptainTux on 2007-06-13
Hi Doobey,

I was the author of the [http://digg.com/linux_unix/The_saga_of_buying_an_Ubuntu_Dell_with_Complete_Care_tm](http://digg.com/linux_unix/The_saga_of_buying_an_Ubuntu_Dell_with_Complete_Care_tm) story.  I received my Dell today (with 3 years of warranty support and 3 years of Dell Complete Care).  I have had my Dell for about 8 hours and have had it on for about 3 hours.  

I think they did a fine job with this machine and the configuration is well done.  

I used to run a online computer store that sold pre installed Linspire Linux systems.  The only thing I did better was my computers came with a laptop bag (true it was a cheap laptop bag) and I included a copy of a book called Linspire 5-0, the no nonsense guide.  It was a short book with many screenshots that got people up and running in no time flat.  That said, the Ubuntu disk has a sticker on it that points people to go to the community support section of the Ubuntu website.

Now, the nvidia is not broken.  If you do get the nvidia graphics card, it is not armed, but when you boot the oc, you get a Restricted Drivers Manager window that allows you to arm the Nvidia drivere with one click.  

Some people here have suggested some great sites for you to see and the community here is bar none one of the best in Linux land.  I am one who likes books as well.  My geek library fills about 3 shelves.  Though it was written for version 6.06, I would suggest Moving to Ubuntu Linux by Marcel  Gagne'.  There are also some great books coming down the road written around Ubuntu 7.04.

[http://amazon.com/s/ref=sr_nr_i_0/002-6822241-5193603?ie=UTF8&rs=&keywords=ubuntu&rh=i%3Aaps%2Ck%3Aubuntu%2Ci%3Astripbooks](http://amazon.com/s/ref=sr_nr_i_0/002-6822241-5193603?ie=UTF8&rs=&keywords=ubuntu&rh=i%3Aaps%2Ck%3Aubuntu%2Ci%3Astripbooks)

This link will take you to the Amazon Page with Ubuntu books.  The Unleashed and Bible series are very exhaustive and at your level may contain more than you want to know and the small print and dry toast writing style may bore you.  I like em.  But I have compulsive reading issues.  The Official Ubuntu book is one I hear many good things about.  I also have loved reading many articles by Jono Bacon in Linux Format magazine and he is one of the authors of that one.  If he is part of the project, I have to assume it is good.  Introducing Ubuntu:: Desktop Linux by Brian Proffitt may be worth looking into.  He wrote "The Joy of Linux".  It is one of my favorite books that humorously explains navigating your way through the OSS culture.  

Now, as far as the Ubuntu laptop.  I am very happy with it so far and not only do I feel like I have purchased a good computer, but I have voted with my pocketbook that I want pre installed linux to a tier one manufacturer.
