Windows has the advantage of installed base.  Every hardware maker out there will develop a Windows driver.  Same with commercial software - most vendors will make a Windows version of their app because that's what most potential users run.  Mac's don't have nearly the installed base of Windows, but it's still significant enough to get decent support from most hardware and software makers.

Until recently, Linux on the desktop was pretty much non-existent from a market share standpoint.  Hardware/Software makers had no incentive to provide support for anything that wasn't server focused.  As Ubuntu and other distros have become more popular, that's beginning to change.  You see more and more support.  Some companies look and see a profitable market providing Linux support - they can battle it out against 30 other providers for market-share in Windows, or they can support Linux and have a playing field with less competition.  Assuming these companies see success, other competitors will essentially be forced to provide Linux support as well.

Having a company with the size and credibility of Dell pre-install Ubuntu is a -huge- step in getting Linux support up to parity with MS/Apple.  As Linux gets more and more support, it becomes a more and more viable alternative to Windows/OSX to the masses.
