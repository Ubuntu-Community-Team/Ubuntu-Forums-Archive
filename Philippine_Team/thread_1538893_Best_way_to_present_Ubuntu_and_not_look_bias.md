### Post by Samhain13 on 2010-07-26
"Presenting to your bosses", I think is the key here.

Are these bosses techies? If ever you find yourself comparing Windows to Ubuntu/Linux, will they be able to fully understand the comparisons (bias or no bias) you'll be making?

If these bosses are techies, then highlighting the virus/no virus, defragmentation/no defragmentation comparison might not be good talking points. Being techies, they'll know that no anti-virus nor disk utilities can replace proper system care; and they'll expect you to know that too.

I suggest you concentrate on talking about what concerns your work as a programmer: why it is important for you to work under a Linux-based system. I would imagine you talking about IDEs, compilers, testing environments, servers; and that these things, which are available in the repositories (of your preferred distribution), are available at no cost yet are quality-assured because the people who maintain them also use them in their professional, "mission critical" work.

Then you might want to cite some case studies done by the major Linux companies like Red Hat, Novell, Canonical, etc.

From there, it will be easier to segue to the subject of how using Linux will be advantageous to your company in terms of savings and such-- which your bosses will definitely understand (and won't hurt their feelings even after you've made them realise that they've been MS sheeple for a long time, LOL).

FUD is a two-edged sword, and it cuts deep no matter which edge hits you. Remember. :D

Ubuntu and Linux in general can stand on their own merits. I think, if you really personally believe that Linux is the right system for your job and it's right for your end-users, then discussing "the other OS's" flaws isn't really necessary. It can be tricky to answer because while the software itself is free, there might be concerns about personnel retraining and such; and converting old MS-format documents to the Open Standards that most free office software implement.
