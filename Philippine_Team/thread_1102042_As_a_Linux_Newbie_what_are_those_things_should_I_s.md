### Post by daxumaming on 2009-03-22
> **SHENGTON said:**
> Hope someone will reply my post because I want to learn everything about Linux.
This is nuts. Although not impossible, it's freakishly difficult *"to learn everything about Linux"*. Heck, even the great Linus Torvalds hasn't mastered Linux - and he's the kernel-godhead. You see, Linux is both an umbrella/generalized term and (oxymoronically - if there's such a word) a specific part of an operating system (the kernel). 

And now that's out of the way, let's talk about the specifics. What is it you really want to learn? 
1) Distro (Ubuntu)
2) Desktop Environment (GNOME,KDE, etc.)
3) System Administration (Networking, LTSP, Samba, LAMP, etc.)
4) Development stuff for your distro (Packaging, etc.)
5) Software Development (using Python, C, C++, etc.)
6) the Linux Kernel
7) a specific Software (Firefox, Amarok, etc.)
8) etc. etc. etc.(there's a lot, lot more....)

If you want to learn everything listed above, then figure out where to start first.

> **SHENGTON said:**
> 
1. As a Linux Newbie what are those things should I study and learn first?
My counter-question is... what do you do? and what are your interests? Start with what you know, and everything will come into place. 

I was a DJ when I started using Linux, obviously, the first app I touched is Audacity. I then started learning about my hardware and bought dozens of soundcards - that's when I started using CLI. The basics first, lshw, dmesg, etc.
Then I got employed as a Call Center Representative. I learned SugarCRM, Compiere, and LAMP. I also studied the dark arts of VoIP - Asterisk.. I struggled, yes, but I learned... Now, I'm a software developer for our electric cooperative. I learned Web-languages, PHP, SQL, and Java. I also got good at setting up Apache, MySQL, and Subversion servers and configuring/maintaining them over SSH.

So now my advise is, start with whatever skills or knowledge you have at your disposal, then improve it. Buy a book (or download the free eBooks on Linux/Ubuntu) and read. Take notes - write down anything useful you get off IRC, Forums, and the Web.

And most importantly, don't go dual-booting every now and then. Stick with the distro you have installed. Don't worry about your files, there will always be an open-source app capable of opening it - even those exotic formats.

### Post by creek23 on 2009-04-10
I know that this thread has been answered -- I just want to share my opinion.

Like Dax and everyone else said, just use it. There'll be time that you'll see problems (unable to play FLV, MP3, etc) then you'll know that you have to download some stuff... but where? So then you post to ask again, and people will be more happy to answer you.

This are the common problems you'll meet and probable solutions to that.
* Unable to run Windows game/application -- try use WINE
* Unable to hear sound -- try different sound card driver
* Flickering game -- try different graphics card driver
* Not able to do this-and-that in GIMP/OpenOffice like you do in Photoshop/MSOffice -- google and read tutorials
* etc etc

In the process, you'll surely learn a thing or two -- and will surely remember just how to fix stuff the next time you encounter it.
