### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-08-19
Scopes, native apps & web apps, what is the difference? It does not matter. If the user gets the user experience he/she is looking for then the user does not need to know if what they have just installed from the App store is a scope, native app or a web app.

I am not sure that scopes were touted as the solution to app fragmentation on today's smartphones. I did not know such a thing existed. I have heard about Android fragmentation as each OEM modifies Android to suit their own commercial aims. And then there is the issue of the user having to rely on the OEM to push out an upgrade to the OS. So, an Android phone can become out of date. And security flaws in the OS may not get fixed on certain devices unless the OEM pushes out a patched version of Android.

Read a real life example:

[http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/technology-33914826](http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/technology-33914826)

On the Ubuntu phone scopes are used to allow the OEM to brand the device and distinguish models from those of rivals without fragmenting the OS. Canonical takes responsibiliy for upgrading the OS and at present is sending out Over the Air (OTA) updates every 6 weeks. In this way the first BQ phones will have the same OS as the latest Meizu phones. And if the plan comes together exposed security flaws can be fixed and a patched OS pushed out as an OTA to every Ubuntu phone user. And not just to those users of a certain brand.

In that link the comment is made that only 2.6% of Android phones are running the latest version of the operating system. That will not happen with the Ubuntu phone.

It is said that scopes are easy to write. I have not tried to write a scope but if it is truely easy to write scopes then scopes are an entry point for non programmers to become part of the Ubuntu phone development community. And that experience might then lead on to writing native apps.
