### Post by QIII on 2015-12-22
As I said, it is more likely an OS failure than a hack.  People often jump to the conclusion that they have been hacked or have been infected with a virus when their device does not behave as expected.

Although Linux can be compromised by malware, the mechanism of  virus a la Windows is currently foreign to linux.  A rootkit is a possibility, but rootkits are often designed to persist through reinstallations.

Have you visited questionable web resources?  Was the phone behaving in any other obviously odd manner prior to this?  Had you connected to an unsecured wireless network?  Had you recently done any updates?  Had you recently installed any new software?  Had any unexpected system notifications appeared?

### Post by QIII on 2015-12-22
(We took a couple of posts out, if you were wondering.)

That's not a very good assumption, unfortunately.

What the installer would have done is nothing more than make sure there was connectivity.  They would have done nothing to ensure security.  Two quick examples of what the user needs to do would be setting up WPA-PSK2 with a strong passphrase and creating a wireless access list which limited access to only those devices you approve.

All that said, I still think your issue was software related, not a compromise.  Powering down and restarting would probably have sufficed.  Unless some pimply-faced youth in your neighborhood is making sophomoric attempts to cause mayhem, the most likely hacker would have had a profit motive.  In that case, they would certainly not have alerted you to a problem by messing with the operation of your phone.  More time without you noticing anything amiss is more time for them to move coins from your purse to theirs.  Finding that sort of attack would take careful and regular inspection of logs (often it's not what is there that gives it away, but what is not there!).
