### Post by houseworkshy on 2012-09-15
I think most people just change wallpaper.  So it is only a first impressions thing.  That said it could be used with newbies in mind.  Quite a lot of "help" wallpapers have been made, usually not exactly beautiful.
[http://i.imgur.com/1c9y0.png](http://i.imgur.com/1c9y0.png)
[http://www.ubuntugeek.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/line.jpg](http://www.ubuntugeek.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/line.jpg)
There are some things that a newcomer won't know.  
That the firewall on the distibution is not turned on by default springs to mind and, unless one *goes online with no firewall running* in order to get a ufw gui one needs the command line to sort it.  First timers just won't know that.  
It is also usuall for the first few minets of using a new gui to feel around rather than immediately open a guide, even guides placed on the desktop.  With gnome, KDE,  Xfce or LXDE anyone familiar with windows or mac will have a rough idea of how to get to most things, even though they don't know what half the things are yet,  in seconds.  This is not the case with Unity.   So a few tips, including how to change the wallpaper, would actually serve a function.
It's not fair or recomended practice but many people will decide if they like or don't like an operating system  in those first few moments.  I've read a lot of posts along the lines of "I hated Unity at first but persevered for a week or two ( or came back to it )  and now I really like it".   Most of these posts seem to be from people who already use and like Ubuntu enough to put in some effort to stay with what is already a favorite.  The first impression does matter.  Users used to other OS's will be less put off by an ugly wallpaper, because they'll be used to changing wallpapers,   than by not being able to find their way around easily and quickly.
For that reason it's probable that more 'just having a look at it' people would convert to being users of Ubuntu if there is a help wallpaper rather than an aesthetically pleasing one.
I suppose one try to do both, maybe "sudo ufw default deny" *could* be made into a great work of art,......perhaps done in oils with a knife, sort of urban command line art.  "Darling I simply  love it!  That "ufw enable" just blows me away every time, such colours, such vibrancy, such passion!"
