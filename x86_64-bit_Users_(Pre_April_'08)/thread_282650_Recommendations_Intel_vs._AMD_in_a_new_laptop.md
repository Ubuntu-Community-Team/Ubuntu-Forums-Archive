### Post by incubus on 2006-10-30
I've always been an AMD fan, but this time Intel got it right, in my opinion.  Especially good for laptops, the Core 2 processors are very energy efficient -- less heat, less fan use, less noise.  That's my priority for a mobile device, as I leave the hardcore computing for the desktop.

[I hope AMD strikes back soon, with a better product.  Till then, we consumers are the winners.]

I agree with anaconda and Pinoy: I would get an nVidia card rather than an ATI.  It's true that ATI support in Linux has got much better now and will hopefully get better with AMD's acquisition.  But traditionally nVidia has shown much more respect/support for Linux users.  That said, I have an ATI card on my laptop and it works -- just not as fully as an nVidia counterpart.

Remember that for laptops design is very important.  Read some reviews to see what people have experienced in terms of heat dissipation and other details with the two laptops you're considering.  In the long run, in my opinion, that's more important than computing power.

### Post by Fully216 on 2006-10-30
I am a big fan of AMD, but I think Intel might be the better buy here, from a performance stand point.  AMD dual core chips reportedly suffer because they are not able to independently ramp the speed of the cores up and down, being that the design was not properly modified from the single core design to a dual core conterpart.  Even the cache size is reduced, evidentally to keep cost of the chip down.  If core #1 is working at 100%, core #2 is running at 100%, even if it is crunching no information.  This kills all battery life gains, which is essential for a laptop.  In most cases, AMD is a good 6 month to a year ahead of Intel with regards to processing technology, although this looks like the chip feel behind in the design process.

### Post by John Jason Jordan on 2006-10-30
Others have recommended the Intel over the AMD because of heat and power consumption. I have only an AMD-64, so I can't compare. My Compaq R3240 is not dual core, and was designed long before the energy efficient CPUs came on the market. I got only a little under two hours out of the OEM battery. So what did I do? Instead of the 8-cell battery that came with the computer I bought a 12-cell, which gives me about 2.5 hours. That's still not a lot, compared to a lot of laptops, but it is enough for me. I'm a university student, and 2.5 hours will get me through a class. Plus, I bought a second power brick that I keep in my locker at school. If I'm going to be there studying for hours all day long, I grab the brick before going to the library, and select a place with a power outlet. So far, the short battery time has not been an issue for me. My point here is that you must start with what your own needs are. If you are a road warrior who needs the computer running without AC power for five or six hours while traveling, then you better get the most energy efficient computer you can find. If your needs can tolerate shorter battery time, then you can save a lot of money by getting a less power efficient CPU. And as for heat, my computer may use a lot of power, but it never gets hot. In fact, the internal fan rarely comes on.

As far as compatibility is concerned, there is no difference at all between AMD and Intel. Just put that thought out of your mind. It is not an issue.

As for ATI vs NVidia, there is no contest. Never buy a computer to put Linux on that has ATI graphics. ATI's support of Linux is pathetic. For me, it's NVidia or nuthin.' Repeat: You'll rue the day you tried to put Linux on a computer with ATI graphics.
