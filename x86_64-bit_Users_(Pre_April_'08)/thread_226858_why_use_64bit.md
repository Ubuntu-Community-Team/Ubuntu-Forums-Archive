> **xXx 0wn3d xXx said:**
> ...
Why choose 64 bit:[/B]

...unless you're doing audio/video endoding/decoding, where x86-64 is approximately 33% faster on average.  Or doing cryptography work, where some implementations (SSL, for example) are twice as fast.  Or doing 3d rendering/modelling... essentially if you're doing anything processor intensive, you want the 64bit version.  If you're just going to be browsing the web, doing email, office work, etc, it really doesn't matter how fast your processor is.  You could be using an old Pentium III, and still get about the same performance.

### Post by bbzbryce on 2006-08-02
One thing I'd like to mention although it isn't urgent at this current moment is the limitation of 32bit regarding time.
Unix timestamps are typically signed integers and in 32 bit means the most negative is 0x8000000 corresponding to 1901-12-13 12:45:52 and the most positive is 0x7FFFFFFF corresponding to 2038-01-18 19:14:07.

This basically means we have less than 32 years to switch to 64bit.  Of course we'll probably be way over 64bit by this time however why not start making this software better now?

Just for completeness the max int in signed 64bit is 0x7FFFFFFFFFFFFFFF which corresponds to a time greater than year 100 billion.

I don't think we'll be forced to upgrade from 64bit in our lifetime.

Overall I think the linux community should be more willing to use 64bit.  How can we convince people to use linux (alluding to the fact that more people use Windows) if linux users wont make an attempt to push or try 64bit under the reasoning that 32bit is more popular?
