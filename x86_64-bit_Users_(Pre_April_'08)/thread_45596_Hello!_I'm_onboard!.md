### Post by DancingSun on 2005-06-30
Well just got my first taste of Linux last night.  And it's in Ubuntu AMD64 flavor!

The install went much smoother than I thought it would be.  Actually almost flawless!  The only problem I had was when the setup needed to restart my computer and tried to unmount something (my guess is that's the CD-ROM drive).  It got stuck on 88%.  But I just press the power off button, and rebooted anyway.  The install resumed on reboot (extracting packages) and everything just worked from that point on.

I did around 2 weeks of research before taking the plunge, and I must say, from the amount of problems posted on the forums, I wasn't expecting my install to go so smoothly.  All of the dual boot install guides that I read stated to use QTParted or some 3rd party partitioner to resize and partition the HDD.  Apparently, with 5.04, the partitioner in the installer works just fine, and certainly simplify things a bit.

I must say that I am impressed with the software, but not with the documentation.  Documentation is certainly the weakest link for Ubuntu right now, especially for newcomers.

Anyway, I'm happy to be onboard, and I'm sure I'll have many more newbie questions to come.  \\:D/

### Post by DancingSun on 2005-07-01
[QUOTE=rachii]welcome and enjoy  (or continue to enjoy).  just curious, is there something about ubuntu that made you install it over others?[/QUOTE]

I have been following GNU/Linux development for about 4 - 5 years before I made my decision to install one.  In the beginning I was looking at what was then Mandrake.  I also was looking at Redhat, SUSE, then Gentoo and various source based distros.

While sourced based distros were definitely interesting, I don't think it was practical for my needs to compile from source that often.  Plus, I'm a C/C++ dummy and don't really like the languages much.  I wanted something that was simple to use, yet has a quality that Windows could not achieve (although I must say starting from Win2k MS did a very good job in at least stabilizing the OS).  Mandrake was really on the top of my list, but when I started looking at the way software are installed in Linux, it sort of deterred me away from Linux.  Windows has installers which makes installing/removing applications relatively painless, but Linux mostly have rpms and its variants, which pale in comparison to Window's Add/Remove software support.  Moreover, hardware support was shaky in the Linux world.  It's not the Linux community's fault as these should be provided by the hardware manufacturers,  but it's definitely something that kept me from taking the plunge earlier.

When I found out about Ubuntu early this year, the clean design immediately attracted my attention.  The inheritence of apt and Synaptic was a big motivator. I've read about apt and Synaptic, but they were for Debian, and Debian gave me a more old school command prompt impression.  I wasn't very impressed by it.  The marriage between a slick GUI interface and practical and easy to use software management tools was something that was very hard to resist.  The fact that the Ubuntu desktop looked LESS like Windows also felt attractive to me.  In fact it gave me a clean, refreshing Mac-like feel that made me felt nostalgic.  And of course, reading through the reviews made me felt that this free distro just might be the one to bring Linux to the masses.

However, what really made me decide on Ubuntu though, was it's philosophy, mission statement, and community.  Funny how it's the non-technical side of Ubuntu that convinced me to take the jump.  But I think it's important that the organization and community behind the distro make its users feel secure and that they are in good hands.  When I use commercial OSes (Windows) I get the (initially) secure feeling that this company dumped millions of dollars developing this OS, and all major software and hardare support this OS.  But Linux in general made me felt insecure.  These guys developing the OS didn't take my money, and they are not obligated at all to produce quality software for me.  There are many distros that disappear into the thin air over time.  Moreover, software and hardware support was spotty.  But Ubuntu felt different.  From its logo to the color scheme that was chosen, you can FEEL that this distro's developers took great care in making Ubuntu something that is targeted to the masses.  It has the professional quality of a commercial product, but is driven by a philosophy that will keep it free for as long as the community around it wants it to be.  What's more, the man behind all this is somebody that has the proven drive to get things done.

So there you have it, I took the plunge.


A little side blurb:

Now, realistically speaking, the philosophy and mission of Ubuntu doesn't allow it to have a tried and trued business model to survive financially.  It doesn't take a business degree to notice this, although I do have one in economics  :wink: .  So here I am, I hereby make a pledge to help Ubuntu in its efforts to fulfill its idealisms, whether by financial contributions or other means.  And I urge other people on this forum to do the same.  This world is getting more and more materialistic.  A gem like Ubuntu is truely rare, and reminds us that technology does not have to make us more inhumane.  Conversely, it has the power to bring us closer together.

### Post by DRF on 2005-07-01
IWelcome to Ubuntu. I know what you mean about the documentation, however it's not so much lacking as lacking a central point. I can often find information on what I need but I tend to have to search for information in this order:

* [www.ubuntuguide.org](www.ubuntuguide.org)
* [www.ubuntu.com](www.ubuntu.com) - Search button top right searchs the Wiki's and articles here (for me it never seems to act as I expect as it returns it's results but when you open a link  you get a 'No article found' and you have to open the link from the 'Related links' section on that page)
* [www.ubuntuforums.org](www.ubuntuforums.org) - A search here finds quite a lot. Also there are a few howto's that are setup here (rather than the wiki). If you've got a problem chances are your not the first (unless it really is something technical).
* The applications own website.
* [www.google.com](www.google.com)

On top of this there are the readme's and man pages. (look in /usr/doc or /usr/share/doc and there are lots of extra documents installed there when ever you install a program). With a prompt command type "man aCommand" (minus speach marks and replace aCommand with the command you want info on) to get a small manual page that covers the basic options etc of that command.

So if you follow that you should find most information it's just often quite technical and often varies a lot in writing styles and of course not centralised. (the builtin help icon on the top panel hasn't really been that helpful but maybe I'm just looking for the wrong things)

If none of that works of course ask in the forum and hope someone else here has the answer.

I can understand you picking Ubuntu. Most of the source based distributions looked to technical and debian is nice but a bit to complicated and paranoid for a desktop OS (hence my move). In debian Apt was my best friend :) now in Ubuntu I still have apt and now Synaptic :)
I've tried mandrake but didn't like it in the slightest, might be better now but my memories were of a slow OS which had a nice control panel but was not complete enough to replace the console completely. Seemed too much like it was trying to be like windows and ultra user friendly but at the same time losing sight of some of Linux's good points. (but as I said this was years back to probably been refined no end since then)
