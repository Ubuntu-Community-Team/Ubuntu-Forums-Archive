### Post by jdp on 2006-05-13
One problem with the whole idea that things not working is an issue that people seem to forget the whole "free as in beer" part of Linux.  We pay little or nothing to get Linux.  We can complain a tad that something doesn't work, but we can't have high expectations that little things like S-video out don't work when there are much more immediate things that need the work of the programmers.  In the free model, we get the fruits of the labor that people are willing to work on projects that they want/need.  One of the ways to get something done that **you** want/need that others might deem as not particularly important is to create a bounty and put some incentive money down so that some programmer might give your pet project a closer look.  There are many bounties, and few good programmers to take the time and research to get features working.

There is also the whole issue of even having documentation available to write support from.  There are many pieces of hardwrae that we would love support for, but the comapnies that make them don't want to share the details of how they work so that a programmer can easily build support.  Think of it like building a house blindfolded.  Sometimes you just have to hope and guess that things are going ok, until the house falls down in the middle of building it and you have to start over again, still blindfolded.

Writing code ain't easy.  Writing code with no documentation is a nightmare.

### Post by gregfulkerson on 2007-08-28
While I have had issues with some hardware in the past, I find generally that Linux automatically runs my hardware and this saves a great deal of time. Compare this to all of the CDs with commercial drivers you would need to install on a Windows system installation. True, XP/Vista are moving in the direction of automatically installing drivers, but Linux has had to do this for years, because commercial hardware makers have rarely written drivers for Linux.  They are starting to come around now thankfully, due at least in part to the large share of the server market that Linux enjoys (in comparison to the desktop market).  

It is remarkable how much hardware is supported when you consider that the makers of the hardware have played a very small role in this.  Most of the drivers have been written by the Linux community. 

To illustrate the power of Linux hardware support, take an example from my experience. I tried to reinstall Windows 98 on an older laptop once. Hardly anything worked out of the box. Then I installed a debian linux distro and--voila--the laptop worked and even detected my wireless card.  I can now take it to my favorite coffee shop and surf the web in a way that it never knew it could when it was made.  I never searched for a CD, contacted a hardware manufacturer, or visited driver download sites. It just worked! Now that is reason enough to use Linux. But there are MANY other good reasons.
