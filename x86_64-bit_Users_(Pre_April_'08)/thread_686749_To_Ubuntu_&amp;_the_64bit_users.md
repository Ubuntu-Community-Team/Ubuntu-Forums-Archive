### Post by craigsn on 2008-02-03
Being a computer user for many years, developer in the early days, and now just a person who needs their computer for work and fun. I have a gripe with 64bit systems. I do not have any statistics to back me up on this, but probably close to 90%, or more, of the computers sold in the past year and even more going forward will be 64bit systems.

This brings me to my gripe. Why doesn't Ubuntu, in this case, develop for the 64bit users. Sure there is the distro for 64bit, it works great and I use it, and have for the past 2 releases. BUT!, the drivers, the plugins, the other "stuff" that makes a computer just "work" are few and far between for us 64bit players. 

I would like to see the Ubuntu development slowed (or halted) on new development for 1 cycle (or maybe 2 if needed), and take the developers, parcel them out to the various relevant places and FIX that problem. That means, assign some developers to Flash (yes I know it i owned by someone else), to ATI, to google, to etc ......., and their specific job is to create the "stuff" for 64bit. 

The way it is now, it will be 2 or 3 more years before everyone catches up and moves to 64bit. Just crazy in my opinion. 

If Ubuntu wants to be the Linux for everyone, then take a leadership role in this area. As an example of what not to do, MSFT isn't doing it with Vista, they are doing the same thing Linux is doing. Waiting for the world to get to 64bit. Apple is better, and so is Sun. Maybe there are other interested Linux's waiting to do this to, so the job could be spread around some, share the work. 

Or maybe, it is happening, and I'm just not seeing it yet. Right now I use the work-arounds to get things to work, but I'm not happy with it. 

Thanks for your time.
Craig

### Post by Yellow Pasque on 2008-02-12
It would be nice if someone would coax Adobe & Sun (et al) into offering native 64-bit versions of their popular software. However, when the source isn't open, there's little we can do except work around (and we've done that amazingly well).

As far as Ubuntu itself not having 64-bit software or devloping explicitly for it, I disagree with that statement. Ubuntu, where possible, follows open-source design principles and makes its packages available for its amd64 distro and other alternate architectures. Remember, if the code is well-written and follows POSIX C standards, porting it is as easy as recompiling it.

In short, while I support your fervor to make 64-bit more prevalent, I think you're barking up the wrong proverbial tree.
