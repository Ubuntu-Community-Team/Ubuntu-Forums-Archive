### Post by detroit/zero on 2009-03-15
Something happened lately in the "Post your .conkyrc's" thread in the Cafe that makes me think to mention it here and point it out/ask for a solution/whatever. This has been happening for a while now, and lately it seems to be getting more and more "popular" among certain crowds, and I think it's cause for concern.

A new (or even more experienced) user will post a thread looking for help on a particular issue, whatever that issue may be.  Some replies will come in, and eventually, someone will post a response along the lines of, "Can you please post the output of XXXXXX?" And then (either in the same post or a follow-up post) they will add, "Check out the {tutorial/instructions/post} on my {website/blog/whatever}" - with a link to an external website. I doubt that people who do this are as much interested in helping their fellow Ubuntu users as they are in driving traffic to their blogs to make advertising dollars.

Now, I'm not going to name names, but there are a number of users who actively engage in this kind of behavior repeatedly. I personally have reported one especially egregious perpetrator at least half a dozen times since last summer when he targeted me in one of my threads in a manner similar to the one outlined above. A quick check of this users profile and his past posts will show a quickly recognizable pattern where almost all posts made fit the mold above.

I would think that this would be a concern for those in charge here for a number of reasons:

[LIST=1]
[*]The instructions posted at off-site blogs/websites and such by random users may or may not be correct, and may or may not be the best/safest/most reliable way to do something. Who are these people? What makes them an authority on whatever subject they're tutoring in? What are their credentials? This leads to the next;
[*]Oftentimes (we've all seen it happen), someone may reply to a thread with one particular solution to a problem. A more knowledgeable user might then come along and offer a different solution that's more efficient or easier to understand (i.e., five or six or seven commands condensed into one, or a little-known piece of software that will do in five minutes what takes an hour to accomplish in a terminal...) Unlike regular forum posts, these offsite blog posts are not reviewable, not correctable or editable, and may or may not be kept updated and current. This could pose a problem to many users, especially the n00bs.
[*]Without the structure, rules, and peer-review of the official forums, there is absolutely no accountability for people who decide to host these offsite tutorials. Obviously, this doesn't necessarily apply to users like Ubuntu-Geek and (forgive me for not knowing off the top of my head - Is it ayisu?) whomever puts together the Psychocats tutorials  - these people are established, trusted, and respected members of the community; they have everyones best interests at heart. These other people already have one ulterior motive - driving traffic to a blog to make money from advertising. What's to say they don't have other motives - like getting kicks out of making n00bs erase their harddisks or having people install malicious software.
[/LIST]
Obviously, each and every user is ultimately responsible for their own selves, and should take care when visiting websites, clicking links, reading/interpreting instructions, executing commands, etc.. But why is ubuntu forums not taking a less lenient approach to the kinds of things that get posted here and passed off as help?

At best, it's spam. It could end up being a lot worse if one of these blog superstars decides to insert some malicious code into a script or instruction set and target the Linux n00bs in the Absolute Beginner forum.
