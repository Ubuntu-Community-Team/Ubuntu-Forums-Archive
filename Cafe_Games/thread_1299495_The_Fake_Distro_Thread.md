**Crapix**

A project that started as a fork of a development fork that was abandoned by its only developer, Craig, **Crapix** aims to put you on the bleeding edge of the boundaries of stability and usability. ### Post by koenn on 2009-11-14
PUNX

An early fork of slackware, created by a small group of punks who found that the distributed, decentralized nature of open source development (mainly the linux kernel at that time) aligned very well with the do-it-yourself ethic and the anti-consumerism of the punk movement.

The OS somewhat reflects the anarchistic tendencies in the punk movement : the file system is loosely organized and doesn't follow standards at all, and programs are not expected to always abide by their config files. Critics say the system is just a bunch of binaries stuck together with safety pins.

End user programs' focus is on graphic design, such as the gimp graffiti extensions, and on file-sharing programs for exchanging music, (punk) poetry etc. The man pages include sections of"Recipes for Disaster: An Anarchist Cookbook". 

PUNX has a quite unique installer, in that it is capable of detecting any unused space on any disk or partition and install the system in it. This technique is called squatting. The downside is that other operating systems or other programs already installed in that partition, may at any time reclaim this space and remove the punx. 

There is no clear roadmap for further development, but this is intentional ("no future"). Interest in this distribution has declined somewhat, although you can still see it advertised occasionally, usually with its trademark slogan "PUNX's NOT DEAD".

### Post by koenn on 2009-11-14
Dummix  -- Linux For Dummies

Yet another attempt at a linux distro suitable for non-geeks.

Everything you need comes on one CD, providing a complete working environment. Additional software is available online.

The graphical installer enables you to get up and running quickly and easily. A standard installation should take less than 25 minutes.

Once installed your system is immediately ready-to-use. On the desktop you have a full set of productivity, internet, drawing and graphics applications, and games.

The system is designed to "just work", with a strong focus on desktop simplicity, a "1 task = 1 program" software selection,  keeping system administration out of the user's view (eg with automatic updates), and so on. It features "the very best localisation and accessibility infrastructure that the free software community has to offer", ...  

This distro was rather unsuccesful in terms of adoption, until it got corporate backing. As a result, the distro was renamed and rebranded, and quickly became one of the leading desktop linux systems.
