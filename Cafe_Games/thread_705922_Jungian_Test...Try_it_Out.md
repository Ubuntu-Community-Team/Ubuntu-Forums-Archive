### Post by Zeroangel on 2008-03-14
**rune0077** you are right on the idea of cold reading, the forer effect and such, and its good that you maintain a skeptical viewpoint. 

The only ultimate test of this would be to administer a blind test and give half of the participants incorrect profiles on purpose.

I have the belief that MBTI/Jung/Enneagram typing systems have validty:

- It's based off of the study of actual people and correlations on how preferences (thinking or feeling, intuition or sensation, etc) influence or relate to behaviors and personality traits. (to contrast this with, say, astrology which fits 'common' human traits into every description and relies on the users own biases to validate itself)

- Pick a random personality profile that is *NOT* you, and try to match your own traits with it. With all likelihood, if an overwhelming majority of traits match, then you have been fooled by the forer effect and typology has no validity.

- If you take a sorter and are shown a profile that does not match you, first all evaluate which of your traits were weakly scored (a close tie between P and J traits, could indicate for example that you were mistyped as an INTP when in fact you are an INTJ), and how iffy the results of that test are. Less decisive results are as I mentioned are a flaw in the particular test being administered (because they rely too much on subjective self-evaluations). Which is why typology tests like MBTI and Enneagram are most often recommended to be done so through professionals.

- The instances where the profiles are 'wrong' on descerning individuals has been such where the user was assigned an incorrect profile on whichever traits were 'iffy' (to put it scientifically). In other words an ISFP with 45/55 on T preference might say 'this is not me' when shown the ISTP profile, but will more or less likely 'ah-hah' as the ISFP profile. These cases as such are flaws in the testing being done, not the system itself.
