### Post by Rog-Mahal on 2007-12-06
I made the decision to single boot Ubuntu on my white Macbook. So far I am generally satisfied.

-Suspend and Resume worked out of the box.

-Wireless required me to compile and install the newest madwifi drivers, but after that it's worked without a hitch. (Most of  the time it even auto-reconnects out of suspend. 

-Graphics worked out of the box

-Sound worked out of the box, but there are occasional problems with a crackle coming from the left channel.

-Touchpad and keyboard required a little tweaking, but there are plenty of easy how-tos. 

-Battery life is respectable (3 hours seems to be the max. I custom compiled a tickless kernel that gives me 3.5 hours if I push it).

-Flash works, I'm running a 64-bit system, if you do that, make sure you install 32 bit compatibility libraries.

-DVD player/burning support works wonderfully.

So yeah, that's the general rundown. I kind of wish I would have kept a small OSX partition for firmware updates etc.

### Post by AnimalMachine on 2007-12-09
I guess I'll offer a different opinion. For me, running Ubuntu on my first gen macbook pro is a pain compared to os x. 

With os x, everything works without question. Wireless, graphics, touchpad, isight, iTunes and iPod nano ... none of these are a problem. The UI is easy to use and effective. I get gcc, a terminal, and a UNIX like environment to develop with. Macports makes bringing in a lot of open source software relatively painless (though there can be some version lag, just like with ubuntu).

In summary, with os x, Things Just Work ...

... but the os AND ESSENTIAL LIBRARIES are not open source. (so what if the kernel is open, but the things that really make mac os shine are not.) So that's my motivation to use linux and ubuntu in general: **freedom** [linux] and **ease of use** [ubuntu]. Being able to run off a completely encrypted partition with 256bit AES is nice too. Actually, there are many things which I love about linux and want to use, but I don't think I need to list them here, on a linux forum.

As a preliminary excuse, I'll say that I've been running linux off and on as a primary OS since `96. I'm not a newbie, but the cool factor of having to work to get my hardware supported and operational has worn off long ago. I'm aware what I'm about to complain about can be worked around with enough effort, but I don't care. I'm tired of putting so much effort into things like that ...

1) In order to get my native resolution of 1440x900, I had to install non-free drivers. I don't care so much about 3d or flashy effects, but I couldn't even get my correct resolution with the autodetected vesa driver.

2) Wireless and audio worked out-of-the-box on my first gen core duo. This will not be the case, I'm told, with recent revisions of the macbook pro ...

3) Trying to get my iPod nano 3g to sync was an exercise in torture, and I've completely given up on trying to get it to work in linux. I really don't like iTunes, but it at least gets the job done.

4) At launch of 7.10, I could not get suspend/hibernate working with restricted drivers and I've not gotten around to testing it yet with my fresh install as of this week. Also, I couldn't connect to Panera's open wireless with ubuntu, while os x had no problems connecting to it. At the time I was using my macbook at work on an ambulance, so this was a dealbreaker - i needed free wifi access and ability to close the lid on a moments notice and not come back to it 2 hours later only to find out it didn't suspend and it's now blistering hot. Right now, I'm still recompiling my kernel (which I shouldn't have to do for basic feature support, but oh well) so I'll test this later.

5) Latency. As shipped, X and gnome can appear to stall on me when my macbook is under load. The mouse stops moving, etc. In my kernel recompile, i've switched to low-latency to see if this helps things. Off hand I can't remember os x ever doing that to me (not talking about general application freezes ... just system load causing blocks of input) for my modest use (photography and programming).

6) Playing videos is a little rough. Installing mplayer and codecs gets the job done for the most part, but I find that VLC is generally better on os x (and certainly easier to get working with all the codecs). Flash support didn't work from apt-get for me, and had to install from tarball. Gnash didn't work well with youtube.

7) The default ubuntu gnome theme is ugly as hell. At least I can change it though, unlike os x.

So there's my list as it stands right now. So far, I've barely used my linux install because I've wasted so much time trying to get my iPod and free x driver working. On the other hand, I have a *mostly* free operating system meeting the majorities of my needs. If you feel adventurous, I will still gladly recommend giving ubuntu a shot. The only thing it will cost is your time, and the benefits may outweigh the negatives for you.

Apologies in advance for the rambling, unorganized nature of this ... I don't have much time to proof/polish it right now.

### Post by iThinkergoiMac on 2008-04-01
Late reply (talk about resurrecting dead threads) but I thought I'd add my 2 cents anyways.

I've been a long time Mac user. I've used systems 8.0-9.2.2 and 10.3-10.5. I skipped 10.0-10.2, but did get a chance to play around with them a bit. I've used a Mac as my main computer since 1998, when Apple came out with the 233MHz iMac. That is, until I came to my college and they issued me an IBM ThinkPad R50.

The R50 is technically superior to the iBook G4 we have at home in most respects, equal in all others, with the glaring exception of the video card (Radeon 7500 for R50 vs 9200 for iBook). But it's not so much better that it's an unfair contest. So I will attempt to outline my experiences.

I've run both 6.06 and 7.10 on the R50, and run Mac OS X 10.3, 10.4, and 10.5 on the iBook. I had to give the R50 back to the school when I left for a year, so I missed 7.04. Be that as it may, I think it's given me a decent background in both systems. Here's what I've found, in no particular order. These are my own subjective experiences, I have no benchmarks to back them up.

Speed - IBM wins. May be the dichotomy of the 1.5 GHz Centrino (Pentium M) vs the 1.07 GHz PPC, but I think it mostly has to do with the lighter system load of Ubuntu. Even the composite graphic effects in Compiz Fusion are at least as fast as they are under Leopard on the iBook, despite the inferior graphics card. (I should note that the iBook got a performance *boost* with Leopard). Still, the general system response tends to be a bit snappier under Linux. It's fairly close though... I don't feel that the system is sluggish on the iBook when using it after the R50.

User Experience - iBook wins. This is due to the incredible integration of Mac OS X. Especially under Leopard, it's just easier to use. The interface is more streamlined, things are set up properly by default, and everything just works. Ubuntu is nearing this level of integration, but it's not there yet. I also tended (the iBook is quite old now) to get better battery life with the iBook, but that's to be expected.

General Satisfaction - I have to say it's a tie. When doing "real work" such as Photoshop or other graphics work, there's no contest - I choose the iBook. For everyday life, I might just stick with Ubuntu. There's a certain satisfaction that comes with having configured the system myself to work just the way I want it to. There's certainly more configuration I can do with Linux than I could ever do with Mac OS X, but then again there are things I can do in OS X that don't happen in Linux. Especially QuickSilver.

I find that the experience difference between OS X and Ubuntu is less severe than Windows and Ubuntu. It's one of the main reasons I installed Ubuntu on my R50 (though I still need Windows for Adobe CS3). Certainly nothing in OS X can match the Synaptics package manager of Ubuntu; it's one of its greatest strengths.

So, for me as a graphic designer, I still give OS X the edge. But, were I to get another Mac, I would certainly install Ubuntu as well on it.

Specs for reference:

R50:
1.5 GHz Pentium M
768 MB RAM
40 GB HDD @ 5400 rpm
Radeon Mobility 7500

iBook G4:

I've been a *nix n00b since Mac OS X came out, but I've always been interested. Installed Enlightenment on my G4 tower back in 2000, just to see what it was like. I was running Ubuntu on an iBook G4 in 2005, and liked the possibilities, although lack of wireless made me eventually lose interest. I've just installed Gutsy on a MacBook C2D, and am really pleased with how it's improved. I'm way too embedded in the Mac OS mindset to "convert," but I'm definitely looking forward to learning what Ubuntu can do (that Mac OS X can and cannot do). I've found installation and setup to be a bit of a pain, but the support here has been great. I'm sure each distro release brings a new wave of n00bs that require handholding just as I do, and I'm really impressed in how the power-user community is so helpful without being condescending. I'm glad I've (re)joined this party.
