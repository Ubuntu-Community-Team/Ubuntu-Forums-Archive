### Post by keyboardashtray on 2007-10-23
> **arkara said:**

Yeah this is one thing I struggle with understanding as a newer user. From what I've gleaned, it seems like the reason they aren't fresher is that the overall opinion is, older programs are more stable. They are tried and true, and most updates are thought of as including new features, features that haven't stood the test of time and that might interfere with stability. Even if it has one or two bugfixes, if it implements a bunch of changes, they lean towards the older version.

The other reason, is that there are a limited number of people whipping up packages. So there might just not be someone on the job (although that wouldn't be the case with something like Firefox). And even when there is, the new package has to go through some red tape and pass the hurdles to be acceptable to put in the repository.

If there is a pretty big bug for a major program, like Firefox, they will usually rush through important bug fixes. And there is a new software source checkbox in Gutsy, under third party software, unsupported updates. I'm interested to see just how this will work, because I'll be happy if that includes for example, universe games, which always seem to be way behind. 

I'm not arguing, though - I'm with you, I like to have the new versions of stuff too. But the thing is, when most of the work is done by volunteers, I'm beginning to get why it seems like there is such a gap at times. When a big company for proprietary software has (virtually) unlimited resources at their disposal, and it's all for just their project, they can afford to send out monthly updates, etc. Under this system, they try to make things count to maximize efficiency. 

This can be good and bad. One of the benefits is not having dozens of upgrade nag screens. I sometimes have to stop and think about how annoying it was to have Itunes tell me it needed to upgrade (almost every month), then my antivirus, then this or that little program that I didn't even use anymore. And a lot of times, each running their own little update checker, taking resources and making me paranoid about whats running in the background.

One of the drawbacks, to me, is, just to give a niche example, online games. I just don't see how this model will ever work conveniently with online games where everyone needs to be on the same page with updates. Especially with online games from a proprietary source, where they are even more likely to be strict about what version is running. 

Anyhow, believe it or not Ubuntu from what I gather is pretty cutting edge. The whole balance of stability/up-to-date I guess is an ongoing battle, with every distribution having their own idea of where to draw that line. For example, Debian, which Ubuntu is based off of, is notoriously on the side of stability over cutting edge. For a laugh check out the[ uncyclopedia article ]("http://uncyclopedia.org/wiki/Debian")on them. Other distros, I think like Fedora, have the reputation of being the exact opposite, a "[testbed for bleeding edge software]("http://fedoraproject.org/wiki/FedoraMyths?highlight=%28myth%29#head-60f1f50c131102346be18e1ea2b97f39de37a096")". These are stereotypes but you get the picture.

### Post by linuxlizard on 2007-10-30
The way I see it is this:

How much difference is using the latest greatest firefox, gimp, open office going to make to me, really? Might have a feature or two that I've never used before and therefore probably can wait until the next ubuntu release for.

On the other hand, I've had updates on ubuntu (in the distant past) and other distros that have broken my system and made things partially or the entire system completely unusable. For a couple of years there I made it my policy to stop running updates after an install because I had this experience not once but a few times with a few different distros. You have to remember, it's not just the big apps that get constant updates, but those big apps depend on the latest updated dependencies- it's a chain reaction where things can go wrong. When they put together a new ubuntu release it's not simply like shopping in the supermarket buying a tossed salad from what's out there (let's see, I'll take a kernel, a sound system, oh I've got to have the latest firefox, glibc...) and then they just take it home and toss the salad. They are doing bug fixes, making sure all the latest apps and dependencies are playing nice together without crashing the system by the release date. Each of the thousands of libs and apps comes from all over the place - each independently developed while using each other's work, so things can and do go wrong.

From having lived through it- I'd much rather wait 6 months for the new gimp or firefox release than have it now and have the system break down, or have the gimp or firefox not work at all on my system because the developers didn't develop them on gutsy gibbon but used some other distro and there was an overlooked dependency on gutsy or call to something that had a wierd reaction in gutsy, or dependency that is latest and greatest that gutsy doesn't have working and the guys at firefox/and or ubuntu won't know it and fix it for a week or whatever, and meantime I'm just out of luck and don't have a working firefox until the fix.

I *don't* need the .08 the day it's released if the .06 is working fine. I *do* need a stable working computer.

[edit] One last thing- for games and some other apps you can get the latest versions of some and install them from here:
[http://www.getdeb.net/](http://www.getdeb.net/) click on the categories icon on the left side of the page.

These are independently produced for the latest versions of ubuntu. Keep checking back if you don't see what you want the first visit- the list of apps grows constantly and is short after a new release like gutsy but grows long by the time the next ubuntu release comes around.  It's a great site.
