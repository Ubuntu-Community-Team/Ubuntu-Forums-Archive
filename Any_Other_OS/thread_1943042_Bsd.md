### Post by anewguy on 2012-03-19
BSD used to be (I haven't followed that in years now) almost the defacto standard Unix distribution.

Linux is new from the ground up for the kernel.  But you will notice many, many similarities.  Linux is not Unix, but the majority of the work done on it has been by people intimate with Unix.  Many things look the same.  Source code may even compile and run between the 2.

So, there will be similarities.  There will also be differences.  Learn one enough to be comfortable, play with others.  Who knows, you may find another Linux distribution you like more, or even standard Unix when you can find it.  Many "derivitives" of Unix/Linux out there.


I'll add something here to my first exposure to Unix at work, years and years ago.  I was working in the mainframe techincal support area, and the vendor came out with a coprocessor to run Unix as another process within our OS.  This was mainly so we had easy access to the TCP/IP stack.  Well, along comes this new stuff - looks like a foreign language to me.  I run into a problem, call national tech support and my friend there says "you know why it's so cryptic?  Guys sittings around in shorts, cutoff t-shirts and sandles, a tomato in 1 hand, the other hand on the keyboard, and at 3 in the morning yelling 'WOW!  Look what I made it do'".  

How often new things feel that way, but are now second nature to us.
