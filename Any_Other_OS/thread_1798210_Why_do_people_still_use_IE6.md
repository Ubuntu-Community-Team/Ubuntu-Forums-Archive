### Post by An Sanct on 2011-07-06
Large corporations have hundreds and hundreds of computers and they *have* to be uniformed, so most of them (like banks, schools, colleges, libraries, ...) have set up rules of conduct, enforced with primitive administrator rules (you have to be admin to change the desktop wallpaper or screen display settings :)), to make it easier to maintain the ecosystem working.

This "ecosystem" is a conglomerate of various hardware. Different types of motherboard-processor-ram capacities etc and as such could run different OSes (I'm talking Microsoft OSes here), but it would be hard to maintain such a colorful palette of OSes, that might even "argue" with each other, so they set the minimum common denomination and that levels the OS version down to XP.

Upgrading such ecosystems would cost a bunch of bucks/euros, so they tend to avoid it as much as possible, also system admins at such companies are spoiled with an ultra high level of XP knowledge and nothing more - they refuse to move on.

I personally know that where I live, bank employees have "terminals" to work with. Simply said: every icon they move on the desktop and every modification they make is going to be just like they found it the previous morning after they restart the machine. Every computer runs a copy of one single snapshot ... dumb, but does the trick...

(I cannot describe the horror I can imagine if that snapshot would be virus infected or would have a backdoor (!))
