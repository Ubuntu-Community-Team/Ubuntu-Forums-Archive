Yeah, my guess is that you'll really enjoy xubuntu.  xfce 4.8 and 4.10 both provide an excellent traditional desktop environment that is highly customizable and more responsive than gnome3 DEs like cinnamon, gnome shell and unity.

Kubuntu is not my cup of tea but you may really like it.

Lubuntu is fairly sparse but the nice thing is that it comes with very little pre-installed and then you can install desktop environments over it.  For example, you can install lubuntu and then install and session into openbox, xfce, kde, unity, gnome-shell etc.  This is a very good way to test different desktop environments to see what you like.

Along those lines, you can also install Mate as your desktop environment of choice which is a fork of the old Gnome.

Don't be scared to play around!  Try some different desktop environments and see what you like, what works with your hardware, what best enables your workflow.  

For example I run gnome shell on my htpc, xfce on my netbooks and unity on my laptop because my hardware and workflow are different on each machine.

Linux is modular so there are lots of choices for the 'buntu user!

### Post by arkanabar on 2013-01-09
One of the more stripped down distros out there is [Madbox Linux]("http://madbox.tuxfamily.org/"), based on 12.04.  It uses an openbox environment largely cribbed from CrunchBang.  Another that's really stripped down for installed apps is [Bodhi]("http://bodhilinux.com"), also based on Precise Pangolin. Installation and management will be much more like what you're used to with Mint than Arch.

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2013-01-09
There's an Ubuntu minimal iso which loads only a core command-line system.  From there you can load up whatever desktop or software you want.

Debian, Slackware, and Arch take a similar approach.

Last time I used them, Fedora and Suse also let you select what software you wanted to install during the OS installation, and they were both friendlier than the others I mentioned.

### Post by tartalo on 2013-01-09
The problem of the bare-bones approach for a newcomer to Linux is that it requires a certain level of knowledge to make the right decisions in order to achieve an installation that suits your needs and not just something that barely works.

Take Arch for example, it has an impressive documentation Wiki, very well organized and very clear: [https://wiki.archlinux.org/](https://wiki.archlinux.org/)

And the community is very helpful, but this is what is considered a newbie question in Arch terms: [https://bbs.archlinux.org/viewtopic.php?id=112061](https://bbs.archlinux.org/viewtopic.php?id=112061)

If you can't understand, at least superficially, what is going on in that conversation I'd say it may be too early and would recommend choosing a distro that "just works" and trying to learn, for now, how and why it works, and how to fix what doesn't work.

So, if you already tried to fix Cinnamon and after googling for solutions you couldn't fix it, and already asked in the Mint forums and no one was able to help, then I'd follow montag dp's advise and move to the Ubuntu derivative that works best for you.
