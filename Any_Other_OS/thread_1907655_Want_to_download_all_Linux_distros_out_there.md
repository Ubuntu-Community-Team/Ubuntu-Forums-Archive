### Post by Double.J on 2012-01-13
> **tech291083 said:**

Off the top of my head, I'd probably start by getting the list put into Alphabetical order, as opposed to popularity or according to their "family tree" Then I'd personally begin with debian, CentOS (I'll explain), Open SUSE, Ubuntu, Gentoo and Arch. These give a good balance of the most popular and influential distros to act as a reference point. The reason I'd say use cent as an experience of Red Hat is that once you've got your blog up and running with tens of entries, it may be easier to ask Red Hat for a licence to test their release as a journalist - probably the best way to comply with the EULA?

I'd then most likely give freebsd a go before moving on down the list alphabetically - also if you're writing it up, it'll look less biased alphabetically!

In terms of what I look for when reading a review, it's
*details about the installation process - graphical/ text, advanced or noob friendly? Also different distro's have different default install set ups - Ubuntu will just / and swap, CentOS uses LVM, SUSE likes to use the BTRFS using subvolumes, instead of EXT, and gentoo  typically has a classic 8+ partition setup. Not all distros even use EXT4 like ubuntu - Ext3 is still common.

*What's the unique focus of the disro? in terms of Ubuntu Christian Edition this is fairly obvious, with some of the "easy to use" systems, in what way do they make it easier to move to linux?

*What's wrong with it? No distro is perfect! if there's just been a relase, I'd mention but be kind - bugs are normal with a nerw relase if it's been out for months, or is claiming to be stable and you find all sorts of glitches - comment on them.

With you're voluntary work with the children and the scale of the project, no one expects 2000 word entries going as in depth as where a particular bistro prefers to put it's binaries, just an overview to help people choose :)

Best of luck! :)
