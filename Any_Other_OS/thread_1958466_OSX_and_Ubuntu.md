### Post by trivialpackets on 2012-04-14
I was given a Macbook running snow leopard and I have to say it's definitely an interesting experience.  Hardware wise, I absolutely love typing on this thing.  It's really a great experience.  I can definitely see that Ubuntu has taken some of their ideas from this OS.  With that said though, I'm not 100% sold that I like this any more than my Ubuntu with Unity.  I am eagerly awaiting the release of 12.04 and have not upgraded yet. 

I like that I can play Netflix on this, and I like that I can set this up as a nice development environment for ruby on rails applications, rather than having a separate partition, or whatever to do the two things, but this really draws my attention to Unity.  I think they have come a very long ways very quickly, and I think they really may be on to something.  I wish I could get Netflix on Ubuntu, but until that's resolved, I don't know where I stand.  I'll be buying a new notebook within the next 12 months or so, and I was leaning towards going with a Mac.  I may still end up there, but I may also look towards something like a System 76 machine if they are of high quality.  

I love the hardware, not positive on the OS yet.  But I'm definitely giving it a fair shake.  And boy do I love the responsiveness of gestures on this thing.  It's taking some getting used to not having to go over to the scroll bar, but I'll get it.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2012-05-02
I've used both Macs and Linux for a long time. While it's no longer the case that Linux is light years behind OS X in terms of design, polish, and usability, OS X is still ahead.  That's due, I think, to Apple's control over both hardware and software.  It's a big advantage, one that I think neither Linux nor Microsoft can match.

My current MacBook is the best computer I've ever used, period. Great screen, great keyboard, and the amazing touchpad.  No one else in the industry comes close to that.  Every Mac I buy in the future will have a touchpad.

Using the terminal in OS X is the same as in Linux.  Both use Bash as the default shell and both come with the usual array of Unix software.  For example. I build and maintain a static blog on both machines, using the same Bash script to update the site, rsync files to the server and then to a backup site. I use the same script on both machines to do local backups. I do things with OS X I cannot do on Linux, or Windows.

One area where I do find Linux seriously inferior to the Mac is in application development.  The variety, originality, and quality of applications made available to the Mac community by independent developers is amazing. I use and depend on apps every day on OS X that are simply not available for Linux, or are available in seriously deficient form.  

Remember, if you have enough disk room, you can shrink the OS X partition on that laptop and add a Linux partition.

Also, you should upgrade Snow Leopard to Lion. For the security patches, if nothing else.
