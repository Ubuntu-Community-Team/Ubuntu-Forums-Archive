### Post by deconstrained on 2011-07-04
Plain old Debian is pretty good and, in my experience, is far lighter/faster than Ubuntu. The only annoying thing about it I've run across so far is that the GNOME power manager inhibitor applet doesn't work properly.

I used Arch Linux as a desktop for a few years and grew to hate it as a desktop because of how often appearance and GUI configurations got broken/glitchy. The distro has very paltry package building/submission quality control guidelines compared to Debian and Debian derivatives.

**Arch as a headless/server distro, on the other hand, is excellent.** Its user-centric configuration and administration philosophy makes it straightforward and uncomplicated to set up a 24/7 Linux box running every service you need, configured exactly how you want it. I have such a machine running Arch, and it serves as my backup server, music player, web development test server, VPN gateway, etc., and although it took lots of time for me to get all those things configured, it's been very rewarding, and I hardly ever have to touch it now that it does everything I need.

### Post by 1clue on 2011-07-04
FWIW I really don't like a few things about Debian distributions.  Mostly I don't like the Debian decision matrix about what packages are suitable for inclusion in the distro.

On the other hand, Ubuntu is easily the most polished, least troublesome distro I have ever used and they still have a good non-business feel while maintaining good cross-platform and enterprise compatibility at the same time, no fuss.

My other favorite distro is Gentoo.  I love it.  I thought I knew something about Linux after using it as my primary machine for over a decade, then I installed Gentoo and got to chapter 2.

I bounce back and forth between Gentoo and Ubuntu.  Gentoo lets you configure absolutely everything in a rational way, and if you're a control freak I think there's not much better out there, in any distro or any other operating system either.

The bad thing about Gentoo is the amount of time you have to put into it.  Initial configuration is the huge hit, so if you get sick of it before you finish installation then stop and install something else.  The regular maintenance is probably an hour or two a week.

The part that kills me is that once in awhile something breaks, and it can be a total PITA to fix it, and you just can't put it off.  If you can afford to stop everything and mess with a problem for a few hours before getting on with your life, (and if you're like me in that you love a challenge) then this isn't going to be a problem.  I can't do that anymore.  I need to make a living with my systems, and that few hours of unscheduled "adventure" really ruins me.

If I were independently wealthy and only spent a small part of my life earning money, I would have one kick-*** Ubuntu system and one kick-*** Gentoo system.  And a Mac.  Sorry I like 'em.  Gentoo is far and away my very favorite megalomaniacal diabolical distro.  Since I can't afford that, I use Ubuntu.

### Post by 1clue on 2011-07-05
One thing that hasn't really been mentioned yet is that source-based distributions like Gentoo really suck up a lot of CPU when you're getting updates.

If you don't have a quad core or better I would probably leave Gentoo alone.  Every program you put on it is compiled on your system, and that really sucks down some CPU.
