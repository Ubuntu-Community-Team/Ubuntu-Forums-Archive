### Post by handy on 2011-06-19
So it would seem that the OP's question has become one of what did you expect out of Linux when coming from Windows.

I'd been fixing other people's computer problems for over 10 years, professionally. I was well & truly over the way MS did things. Though, so much of my work was due to the way that they did/do things. A double edged sword really.

I could afford to retire early, so I closed my business & did just that. I had looked at various early versions of a variety of different distros through the 1990s & early 2000s, but they really weren't good enough for me to take on & be able to do what I needed to do at the time.

I could see the progressive improvements of Linux through that time, & new that it was only a matter of time before it would be improved enough to suit me at the desktop.

When I retired the first thing I did was install Debian, I had a few problems & was searching them out on the web. In doing so I kept coming across this funny unknown word Ubuntu. Curiosity got the better of me & I searched it out which didn't take me too many jumps to eventually land at the Ubuntu forums.

I got the help I needed for my Debian install from mips. I didn't let on at the time that I was using Debian.

Anyway, after having a little experience with the Ubuntu forum, I installed the fairly fresh 5.10 version - Breezy Badger. It worked well enough for me to stick with.

Dapper was a great release that served me well. Then Edgy came out & it caused me no end of trouble. At this point I was Linux familiar enough to start distro & BSD hopping, I used PC-BSD for a while, it was a terrific implementation of KDE (which I generally don't like - too much like Windows for me) & it had a nice little community. I ended up landing on Sabayon, which handled my difficult for Ubuntu hardware beautifully. So I stayed with it until they had an upgrade that no longer handled my hardware & caused me to look elsewhere.

I hopped some more & found Arch, where I have lived happily on Box.1. for over 3 years. I still hopped around a bit on Box.2. I liked Mint XFCE, & used it on Box.2 for a while, but ended up sticking Arch back on it too.

These days I rarely look at another distro. Though I had a look at Fuduntu not long ago, primarily due to fewt's enthusiasm, intelligence & humour. I recommend it to new users these days along with Mint.

I also keep tabs on Haiku, as I like its speed, & the file system is absolutely brilliant.

So to answer the question of what did I expect: I was escaping from far too much exposure to the MS way, which led me into the unexpected Linux community way, which I obviously enjoy a great deal. It somehow shares a similar quality to that of the Amiga community that I experienced through the 2nd half of the 1980s & the first half of the 1990s, though the Amiga community had to thrive in such a different way, as it was during a time before the internet became common. 

These days, I carry no negative emotions re. MS. We haven't had Windows in the house for over 5.5 years. Under those circumstances I've just forgotten about all that stuff that I had to deal with that I ended up really not liking. Which is nice. :)

### Post by BrokenKingpin on 2011-06-21
I think the majority expect Linux to allow them to do what they did on Windows. This means use the same hardware and same applications (or a decent alternatives).

A lot of the time when someone goes back to Linux it is because they have a piece of hardware that will not play nice under Linux (printer, webcam, wireless, etc.) so they get upset and go back to something they know. Or they will expect that they can still use a piece of software that does not have Linux support (Photoshop, etc.), and they are not satisfied with the alternatives. In these cases going back may be the correct choice, and other times they may have given up too early or were not willing to accept any change.

I think in a lot of situations new users to Linux are just not willing to learn a new platform. It is not that Linux is hard, it is just not what they are familiar with as they have been using something completely different for the last two decades.
