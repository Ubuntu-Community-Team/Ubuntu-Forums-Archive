### Post by VanillaMozilla on 2012-05-23
1.
I think the main reason for Mint is a little purism over non-free (as in liberty) codecs.  They come installed in Mint, but you have to install them in Ubuntu.  Not a big deal to me.  Mint also comes with a different mixture of software, which may or may not appeal to you.

2.
But there's another rub, and this applies to both.  Some codecs that are essential for everyday multimedia use come with a warning that they may be illegal [in the U.S. and elsewhere], in most cases because they are patent-encumbered.  This is true for *both* Mint and Ubuntu.  *So someone could come after you.*

If you use one of those other operating systems (you know, the usual ones you pay a lot for), you've already paid for the non-free (as in beer) stuff and no one can ever bother you.  Well fine, if you're really getting something that's patent-worthy and makes multimedia use possible, I'm happy to pay for the license, but just how the *[expletive deleted]* do I do that?  Anyone know?

3.
Some people are going to Mint because it still(?) has Gnome 2, while most others have already moved to the dreaded (by some) Gnome 3 or Gnome 3 plus Unity.  This has been a big boost to Mint.  *However,* this is only a temporary solution, as Mint can't keep Gnome 2 forever.  So, they are reimplementing something that's based (I think) on Gnome 3 but looks and acts just like Gnome 2.  Any way you slice it, folks, in the long run you're either in for major whiplash or a frozen retro system.  Read it and cry in your free beer, I guess.
