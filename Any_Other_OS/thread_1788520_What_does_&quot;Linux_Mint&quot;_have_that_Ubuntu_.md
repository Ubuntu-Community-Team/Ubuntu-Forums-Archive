### Post by Dangertux on 2011-06-22
I use both Mint and Ubuntu as well as a few other distributions on a regular basis.

You will find that Mint and Ubuntu are extremely similar in MOST ways. 

The most notable difference between Mint 11 and Ubuntu 11.04 is the fact that Ubuntu 11.04 ships with the Unity desktop manager by default, as opposed to Gnome (which is still present). Mint forgoes Unity for Gnome, personally I greatly prefer Gnome to Unity however that is entirely a matter of preference. 

Overall, I would not say that one is "better" than another they are really just different, and in the case of Mint they really aren't all that different.

Mint experiences slightly and I mean very slightly faster boot times than Ubuntu, not enough to make it important in my book. Also Mint tends to have a little bit better support for proprietary drivers out of the box. Although I have never had issues with Ubuntu having any drivers, this is just something people commonly reference between the two.

Some users are reporting slightly better printing support (out of box) in Mint then under Ubuntu. Again, this is not something I have particularly noticed in either case. 

Outside of minor configuration details and certain tools which may or may not be included in each distritbution the major features between the two are very similar if not the same. When talking about major features I'm referring to things like package management (ie: they both use apt as opposed to say yum)

In my opinion, and I know there are those who would argue this to the bitter end, both are distros designed to be easy to use for beginner users while still providing a fully functional Linux distro. Either one are great choices. 

I would say try both and see which you like best, in the world of Linux while you CAN make any distro similar to another I find it much more enjoyable to learn what each distro has to offer.

### Post by BrokenKingpin on 2011-06-24
As stated above, the the default DE is Gnome2, not Unity. On top of that they add a lot of their own utilities:
- MintMenu --> A really good applications menu for the panel (has a really nice filtering option

- MintUpdate --> A system update utility that ranks the severity of the updates

- MintBackup --> Simple to use backup utility

There are a few more things as well, but nothing too major that makes it all that different from Ubuntu.
