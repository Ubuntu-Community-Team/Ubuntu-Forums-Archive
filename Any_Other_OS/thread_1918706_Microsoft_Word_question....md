### Post by underquark on 2012-02-01
Always supply it in a particular format if asked to do so. Failing that, or for blind e-mailing, I'd save it in RTF format. Most word processor applications can read it and most companies will open such files without worrying that it might contain a virus. If RTF doesn't support a particular formatting that you feel is essential to your cv then I suggest that you simplify the cv.

Some companies don't use software that will allow them to read DOCX files and won't - for security or other reasons - upgrade their users' machines to do so. Some companies perceive all DOC files as posing a security risk as they could contain malicious code. A file with an RTF extension isn't actually completely immune to this as you could rename a DOC file (complete with malicious-code macro) to have an RTF extension and MS Word might still open it but then most companies don't know this so they will allow opening of files with an RTF extension but might block DOC or DOCX.

### Post by Double.J on 2012-02-01
As others have said, .doc is best - if possible do it from MS Office if you have it - every now and then libre/open don't quite save it as word does, and since it's a CV, best to make sure it's going to come out exactly how you want it.

Never send a document as docX - you have no idea what software they are using, it's more than likely by now they can read docx, but why risk it? MS Office is the business standard, so as I say if you have it use it :)

Bare in mind when sending CV's to companies to never send a generic CV - you should tailor each CV you send to the company you send it to, and it should have a covering letter laying out what you can do for them. In general a cover letter should be no more than 500 words, and a CV no more than 2 pages - remember that 90% of the time in medium to large companies CV's are forwarded to HR and NOT the people who will actually be hiring you - HR then match what's on your CV to what's on the job description's for a partitcular department - they don't know the job in question, never had to do it; it's just a list of required duties and what they are looking for - you have to match this to get forwarded on to someone who matters usually ;)

Also bare in mind that very large companies with very high application rates such as banks, Gov departments, etc. scan incomming applications with software, rejecting all that doesn't hit the right keywords - so be sure to put the right sort of buzz words for your choosen profession in both the covering letter and CV ;)

Hope it helps!

### Post by TheFu on 2012-02-02
How you choose to provide your CV says something about you.
If you really need the job and the headhunter/company is working with you, then provide the format that looks like what they want.  I'll try to summarize other posters' ideas and merge in mine:
* there is no need for you to use MS-office. Use LibreOffice instead.
* provide PDF by default. If they don't like it, they will let you know.
* if they demand something compatible with MS-Word, send RTF and tell them it will work, just double click the file.  It amazes me how many end-users don't know about RTF.
* if your resume can't be displayed properly in RTF (huh?), then send a very old version of .DOC.
* if you want to make a F/LOSS statement, send only the .ODT format from LibreOffice and let them figure out how to be compatible with open standards. This may really **** off the other person.
* if they want something in a really open format, send them TXT or plan, simple HTML.

Lots of options for you.

Most HR and headhunters will have tools to copy all the text from your provided PDF, RTF, HTML, TXT, or DOC file. They will shove your info into their placement DB and will wrap the CV in their company format before shipping it to the decision makers / clients. Mainly this will have their company header and footer and may remove all your contact information besides your name.  Seeing my resume completely reworked at a job interview is really maddening, but that's how this stuff has worked the last 15 yrs.

If you are looking for a job at a mostly F/LOSS using company, LibreOffice will be appreciated, however, most companies have never heard or it and will only use MS-Office internally., especially the non-technical parts.  The IT side should know what LibreOffice is, especially if they use Linux.
