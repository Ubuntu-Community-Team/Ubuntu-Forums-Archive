### Post by therockindonkey on 2009-04-01
I've tried different Linux distributions over the past few years with varying levels of success.  However, recently I decided to take an old windows machine I purchased in 2002 and install Ubuntu on it as the only OS.

I was having trouble getting the partitioner to work in the first place on the PC (I suspect a hardware issue), but I didn't much care as I have another windows PC now and this was really just my backup.

Well, after spending about a day or so configuring, I have Ubuntu running the majority of the apps I use daily.  There are a few gotchas along the way (I may need to research some video codecs in the future -- the only one recommended for a video I was trying to watch on the Apple website was not free and I don't want to pay for software if I don't have to -- I'll research it when I have the time).  I was also unable to locate a driver for my scanner, but, as I said, I have a windows machine I can use for that.

So, for the first time I find myself in a situation where I can use Ubuntu for the majority of what I need to do and will only need to use Windows in rare instances.  That's right.  Now the windows machine is the backup.

I'm currently studying for the SCJP exam, and need my windows laptop for the study disk, but will probably give Ubuntu a whirl on that when I'm done.

So to all of the members of the open source community that have made Ubuntu what it is today: My hat's off to you.  Good work!

From what I've read, the hitch seems to be with hardware manufacturers releasing the specifications of their hardware to allow the open source community to use them to write the drivers.

My Canon scanner, for example, is a case in point.  Canon has never released the specs, therefore nobody can create a proper driver for the unit without reverse-engineering it.  It's an older scanner and my guess is that the ship has sailed as far as it goes.

As for the video issue, I will eventually look into it.  I just don't really have the time to right now.  It's not something I need to be able to do right away anyway so I'll just keep plugging along.

In the future, however, I will take care when purchasing hardware to ensure that I can get a suitable linux driver before the purchase.  It's not that I have a real problem with Windows.  I'm just ready for something different.

I don't want to be nit-picking, but saying that HP always had excellent Linux support is a bit of an extreme statement imho.

If you look here [http://hardware4linux.info/search/](http://hardware4linux.info/search/) you'll see that HP is doing well for the printers that *are* mentioned there, but at my work for example there's are two different HP scanners which are *not* supported by Linux :(

Also, your statement makes it sounds like Linux owes HP something, that might be true partially, although I suspect it is more like that Linux developers made the drivers work, with or without information from HP.
I remember reading some years ago that Bruce Perens started to work for HP, and even then I noticed that HP continued producing devices which were "designed" for that other OS from Redmond,WA,USA, and I think earlier on HP was one of companies which produced ... win-printers. 
See also here : [http://perens.com/about/bio/](http://perens.com/about/bio/)

I'm only writing this down to prevent people thinking that anything that HP makes will be supported by Linux.

Look at Asus with their EEE, it all looked great concerning Linux support, till Asus came with their EEE top, see a review here :
[http://www.phoronix.com/scan.php?page=article&item=asus_eee_top&num=1](http://www.phoronix.com/scan.php?page=article&item=asus_eee_top&num=1)
