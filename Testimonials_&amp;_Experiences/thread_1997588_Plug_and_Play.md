### Post by VietCanada on 2012-06-05
I was never really sure what this term (plug and play) meant when I was a Windows user. Even a USB didn't work that way. Having been an Amiga user I was astounded to discover that I couldn't use the physical button to turn off my PC without problems when I turned it on again. But then ME was my first MS OS. I was equally astounded the market would pick that over anything. Including punch cards.

As a Linux user, Ubuntu 10.10, Edubuntu and Mint Mate I have found that this phrase does indeed have it's literal meaning. 

Here are some examples over the past three years of my Linux OS experience.

My school decided to use wireless and put PC's in every room. I had installed Edubuntu as a dual boot instead of XP. We were given a USB thing to plug in to access the wireless. With Linux I just plugged it in and had the internet immediately. The XP users had to use a disc and configure some software that only the tech understood.

Visiting a school I wanted to connect my laptop with Edubuntu to their 42" flatscreen TV. I just plugged in the HDMI cord and had twin screen. The XP girl had to configure software which she had had to figure out at some time.

Today I bought an HP printer. The store had a policy of setting it up with one of their PCs  (XP again) to make sure it worked before I took physical possession. after about 30-40 minutes they had determined that the supplied ink cartridge was faulty and gave me a new one. At home I just plugged it in selected printer from the gnome 2 drop down and had a test page printed in 5 minutes. That includes unpacking and plugging everything in.

Oh yeah. When I plug in my USB it just opens. I can just pull it out when I want to as well. Imagine that! I can also use the physical button to turn off my PC.

Windows 7? When I turn on my Linux machine it just boots and runs. When I turned on my MS7 I usually had to wait for 5 to 10 minutes while it configured updates before I got to the part where I wait 5 minutes more for everything to start up. Then my antivirus hijacked the machine to check for updates.

Gnome 3 has problems with seperate x-screens that sent me to Mate. But isn't Mint just Ubuntu 12 with gnome 2? 

When you buy any OS you have to check to be sure your hardware will work with it but in Linux when it works it truly is plug and play. My camera, hand phone, Nvidia card. When it doesn't it's usually the short sightedness of the manufacturer. Lexmark comes to mind. Perhaps ATI as well.

Great job guys.  ):P

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2012-06-06
I remember when Windows 95 came out and supposedly had "plug 'n' play".

As a Mac user, plug 'n' play meant "Plug in the device, start your Mac and it works".

I'm not sure what Microsoft's definition was, but it seemed to be "Put the CD into your computer, install the driver, shut down, plug in the device and then start the computer again.".

Basically, everything you had to do before on DOS except assign an IRQ number.

Microsoft still doesn't get it right. I had a similar experience to you with an HP printer. Windows detected the wireless printer and offered to get a driver for it. Ten minutes later, it had finally finished looking for a driver, and discovered that it didn't have a driver available. So I had to insert the CD and take another ten minutes to install hundreds of megabytes of HP bloatware.

The Ubuntu machine took about five clicks in the space of thirty seconds to set up the printer. The whole process was so simple, it reminded me of my old Macs back in the early days.

I'd like to find out this, though: In older versions of Ubuntu, it would literally set up a printer for you. You'd plug in the printer and a notification would come up saying "Your printer has been set up successfully". It doesn't do that anymore. Why not?
