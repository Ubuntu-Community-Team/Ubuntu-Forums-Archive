### Post by POW R TOC H on 2008-03-21
**Phase I : The first encounters**
Using linux was a bit hard at first, and I tried it out of pure curiosity. Things were rather confusing, the filesystem especially. 
I disliked KDE because of few things that bothered me, but that's probably just me being a prejudice a-hole, so I looked for a Debian based distro with GNOME. I came across Ubuntu. At first, the name sounded awkward, and I disliked it, again because I'm a prejudice a-hole, but I decided to give it a try, anyway. I was amazed by how fast and easy the installation was (that was Ubuntu 7.4,the fawn thingy), and how clean and pretty it looked.
The terminal drew my attention pretty fast, and I started learning about bash...
**Phase II : The Event Horizon**
Soon enough, I encountered the Event Horizon of Operating Systems : The point of no return. I began to understand why linux is so fast, flexible and powerful, and on top of all - fun! I remember that few years back I looked down upon linux users, and couldn't understand what the big deal about it was, but then I began to realise. The only boring thing I had to cope with was "I told you so"... Well, OK the first encounter with linux can't really compare with the pleasure of your first sexual experience, but it's surely the next best thing. Except maybe Nescafé... 
**Phase III : Messing with space and time**
As I was already into programming, learning C in linux proved to be much more productive and efficient than in Win. A couple of books and articles later, I ended up writing shell scripts for all sorts of things, attempting to write device drivers in C, customizing my system and messing with kernel itself. I screwed things up quite a lot, had to reinstall the whole system a few times, but all in all- it was great fun. I created my own little repository of programs, and shell scripts for settings I changed, so If I screw up again (which I will), getting my OS back will just be a matter of minutes, with no permanent data loss (system has it's own partition, of course). 
**Final thoughts, right before breakfast...**
So, to finalize my thoughts (and stop boring you), I would recommend Ubuntu to anyone. The fact that it's beginner friendly doesn't mean you can't be a pro and still use it. Many things can be done clicky-way, but when they can't (or you just like getting dirty and all) you can always fire up the terminal.

PS : Sorry if there are any spelling or semantic errors, English is not my native language.
