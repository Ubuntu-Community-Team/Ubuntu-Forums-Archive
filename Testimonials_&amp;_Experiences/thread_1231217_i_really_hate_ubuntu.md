### Post by junglejuice on 2009-08-04
i've been using ubuntu for almost two years now. not as a duel boot or on a secondary machine but as my only os on my only computer and i've come to the conclusion that ubuntu is far from being the user friendly linux solution that it's developers portray it as. superficially it is quite pretty looking and definitely runs faster than windows ever did on my setup (when i used to have it installed).
however when it comes to installations and upgrades ubuntu is a friggin' nightmare! forget about running ubuntu if you plan to install software that does not come distributed with the os, and you don't have a high speed internet connection with a high cap. you'll end up pulling your hair out over dependencies and dependencies of dependencies etc etc... and although binaries are becoming more popular for ubuntu there are other problems associated with using such media, that contradicts some open source philosophies governing ubuntu. the kernel developers need to decide whether they want an os that is user friendly or one that is pure open source, cause right now the halfway solution they are offering is (in my humble opinion) just not working.
if you think installing new software can be a mission wait till you have to upgrade for some reason or the other. i've tried to upgrade ubuntu four times and each time has been a dismal failure, and i really don't think it has anything to do with a lack of competence on my part. my first attempt at an upgrade from 7.04 to 7.10 failed and i had to do a clean installation of 7.10 to get my computer up and running again. fair enough it was my first attempt at upgrading my system, so i took the blame and thought it was me that had a lack of understanding of upgrading protocol on ubuntu. on my second attempt, months later, to upgrade from 7.10 to 8.04 yet another failed upgrade and so once again i had to do another clean install this time luckily i'd backed up most of my important work. are you starting to see a pattern developing here? and i'm not refering to the consistency of the user (ie me) doing the installing. moving on to attempt number three (8.04 to 8.10) and, several months later, number four (8.10 to 9.04) yep that's right you guessed it both upgrades failed, this time due to the update manager crashing during the "upgrade". and you can forget about diagnostic tools unless you are comfortable using terminal commands (very user-unfriendly).
although ubuntu works, i wouldn't recommend it on a production computer. after all what production computer uses only the tools that come with the os? no serious one's that i've encountered.
my suggestion to you if you are considering using ubuntu as a substitute for your current os is ...don't. rather just stick with what you are currently using and try to improve on that. 
finally so much for the free software philosophy. now i have an understanding of the concept of freedom, freedom of speech, expression etc. but ubuntu also claims that it is a free operating system in terms of monetary value. and in the strictest terms of this definition it is, but there is more to it than that. if you want to keep your system current and up to date and it is more than likely in your interest to do so (particularly in the fields of graphics, music, animation, design, programming to name but a few that i am familiar with). you will have to endure the upgrade process, which may or may not work and it's definitely not a given that upgrades happen as easily as most linux enthusiasts will have you believe they do. you will also need a fast internet connection, and alot of bandwidth, and if you don't live in america, the uk or south korea this can be quite costly. i spend several hundreds of my money on a monthly basis to keep my 'free' os up to date. perhaps if the developers considered having fewer official releases and focused more on quality, then one could obtain a single cd or dvd (downloadable) once a year that would make the update process a little simpler. i also understand that many people can't wait that long, then how about having minor releases that don't effect the entire os in the interim? this is were cononical needs to determine who their interest group is, because this 'take on the entire world' approach they have adopted is just not working!

### Post by dreamingdarkness on 2009-09-19
Not the right place to vent, but let's make a note of something:
You're not required to upgrade every time a new release comes out.
This is why they differentiate LTS releases.
You can set Ubuntu up not to prompt for updates, just like people do in Windows, or Mac.
And you can pay the same penalty, loss of security and no new features, but once you run stable you should stay stable.

Ubuntu isn't perfect, nor is Linux as a whole. Nor is anything else in the world. 
There are distro's out there which are focused on a slow release cycle. Their releases are on ultra-stable kernels. They don't fold anything into their distro until it works on everything they expect it to. But you don't get all the latest candy either. So, it's up to you - find something you enjoy.
