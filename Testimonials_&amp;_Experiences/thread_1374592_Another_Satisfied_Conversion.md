### Post by 1roxtar on 2010-01-07
I posted this thread last weekend...

[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1371118](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1371118)

My brother, who is a new Ubuntu user, told his brother-in-law about what I did for his laptop.  His Bro-in-law's laptop (Acer 4720Z) was riddled with hundreds of viruses and wasn't even bootable.  Last night, my brother asked if I could install Ubuntu on his computer too.  Not really knowing his bro-in-law too well, I told him that I would try to clean his laptop first and if it wouldn't work, I would go the Ubuntu way.

Deep down inside, I didn't want it to "fix", but I gave it a good effort.  Even in Safe Mode, the virus crap kept popping up and freezing the desktop.  I tried the repair option and then tried a clean install, but kept getting errors during the install.  With a giddy grin, I pulled out my Ubuntu-installed USB flash drive.  I booted to my flash drive and made sure that everything would work first.  The Acer 4720Z has 2gigs of memory and an Intel dual-core processor, so the installed took an amazing FIVE MINUTES to install and reboot (wired connection).  The updates took a whole TEN minutes.  The next 20 minutes I spent just customizing it to look really pretty. 

Today, after work, I went to drop off the laptop.  I had the bro-in-law boot up his new Ubuntu laptop and as I was giving him a tutorial, he let me know that he already gotten familiar with Ubuntu, using my brother's own laptop.  I had him navigate through it (I turned on all the effects and he got a huge kick seeing his boxes wobble around) and walked him through setting up a Sprint 3G broadband card.  Everything was blazing fast and he kept going "Whoah!...WHOah!!...WHOAHHH!!!" over and over again.  I was grinning ear to ear.

I know....a lot of you might start saying, "Just wait till something goes wrong, but I get those calls all the time with Windows now.  I'm confident about Linux and more specifically UBUNTU that I'm going install it on as many computers as I can.

### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-01-07
As long as you take peoples' needs into account when introducing them to Linux, and guide them along a little bit, they'll end up liking it quite a lot.

P.S. You don't MAKE your friends use Ubuntu. What I've done with my friends who still use Windows is just tell them, "sorry, I haven't used Windows for such a long time, I wouldn't even know how to fix that problem." That's actually almost true, to be honest.

The reason I help people learn Linux at first is so they can help themselves and be free with Ubuntu. Windows binds them to more 'geeky' people like me so they have to constantly ask for help.

Ironic really, that people think you have to be a geek to use Linux, when you actually have to be a hardcore geek with too much time on your hands to run Windows without breaking it.

### Post by 1roxtar on 2010-01-07
My success with installing Ubuntu on these two laptops can be attributed to SHOWING people Ubuntu rather than just talking about it.  I let my brother try Ubuntu with my netbook and his brother-in-law got familiar with Ubuntu with HIS laptop.   It's pretty much created a chain effect....as an above poster described, it's almost "viral".  

When normal people get to see what this unfamiliar thing is, they realize that it isn't that scary and really easy to use.  I enjoy them shaking their heads and asking....."and it's all FREE???"  As another poster shared, we can refuse to install pirated OSes and software on our friends computers, charge them for fixing anything Windows and/or offer Free and Open-Source software, like Linux/Ubuntu.

As my life moves forward, I don't even want to use a Windows OS anymore.  If I can't have my laptop with me, I carry Ubuntu on my flash drive everywhere I go.  This also allows me to to demonstrate Ubuntu to newcomers.  As a local tech, I'm preparing to hold small demonstrations of Ubuntu and FOSS in my small town.  Along with that, I am fixing some older computers that I am installing Ubuntu/Xubuntu and planning to donate to several home school families.

Instead of preaching Linux and Ubuntu, I'm learning to create awareness through demonstration.
