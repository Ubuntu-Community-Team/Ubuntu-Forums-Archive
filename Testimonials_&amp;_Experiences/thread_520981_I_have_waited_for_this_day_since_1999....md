### Post by Crick on 2007-08-13
I have waited for this day approximately two years longer than you. My first linux install was in 1997.

Every year or two since then I would try another distro and give up in frustration. Whether it was redhat, debian, mepis, pclinuxos, centos, DSL, puppy, mandrake, I've pretty much tried them all, even OpenBSD. Everything seemed to take so long to configure in linux. And often, something would just be not implemented.

I'm blown away that I can do everything I used to do, but in Ubuntu and not XP. My laptop is just humming away. I can touch the CPU heat sink with a finger and hold it there for as long as I want. It's using between 4 and 7% CPU, and no doubt stepping the speed down. In XP, it would be too hot to touch for more than a second.

It is responsive - more responsive than the XP experience ever was. There is no spyware clogging my system, and no sign of any yet. The lack of a root account by default has made me a less lazy administrator - it's far easier to just type "sudo whatever" than to figure out how to configure a root account to just su to. So I don't anticipate any spyware either.

It's not lacking for applications either. Everything from web, email, ssh, keepass, skype, movies, mp3s, ripping, PPTP (windows) VPN, Remote Desktop, document reading of all types, it is handling with ease. And then there is the printer support and the plug and play mounting of external USB enclosure drives. And installing programs! A breeze, just open up synaptic, click reload and then search. Read descriptions, google if I feel I need to, then install. Easy!

Oh, it has taken work, but not an inordinate amount of work. Maybe a week worth of hardcore work on it. And I have given back everything I struggled with in the form of guides to help beat a path for whoever migrates next.

One of the things I'm most surprised with is that the game support is actually pretty darn good under WINE. So much so that I will be burning install CDs for my friends, and guiding them through an install. Who knows, they might switch too.

All in all, I think that Ubuntu might really be the tipping point for mainstream adoption. I've just got a strong feeling about it.  If I've got this great experience, and I'm not a unix sysadmin, then all it will take is for other people to try it. It's a bit like being one of the first people to play Doom. At that point, I just KNEW it was going to be a huge hit. There's something similar going on here.

Certainly, Ubuntu has the potential to be the OS that all the smart nerdy kids will run at home. I'm probably more than a power user, but most of the install stuff I did was firmly in power user class. e.g. minor edits of text files, apt-get etc. If it's easy enough for me, it should be easy enough for them. And it's getting easier all the time. With every new user Ubuntu gets, there will be a chance that he will write a howto or even hack something up, increasing functionality.

All it takes for mass adoption is that for every person installing Ubuntu, that person causes on average more than one more person to adopt it, and stay using it. As the userbase increases, network effects increase the rate - more people hacking, writing guides, providing free support, handing out CDs, writing new software, porting software, etc.

And then the genie is out of the bottle. Not a good time to be holding MS stock, I think. I wouldn't short them, but at the same time I wouldn't be holding them either.

Thanks to Mark Shuttleworth, thanks to Canonical, thanks to Linus, thanks to RMS, and thanks to Bill Joy (creator of the excellent vi editor), thanks to Bram as well.
