### Post by mikemcfarlane on 2007-05-31
Hi

I have messed around with various Linux distros over the last few years, starting with something like Red Hat 4? in the mid 1990's and various other downloads and magazine CD covers over the last few years and whilst I kind of enjoyed the experimentation in the early years when I was 'into' computers, there was no way I could devote the time and energy to getting a machine set up for serious business use. Never mind the time, the applications available just weren't there.

Recently my parents laptop went down and having lost the original Win 2000 CDs and not wanting to install a hookey copy, I wondered about Linux again. I had heard good things about the Ubuntu, so tried it along with Knoppix and Vector. I couldn't get the latter two to install properly and well the GUI is horrible in Knoppix, sorry. I've worked as a landscape photographer for the last few years ([www.mikemcfarlane.co.uk]("http://www.mikemcfarlane.co.uk")) and whilst the business side of me needs a stable and easy OS, the artist in me wants not only a good looking GUI, but also one that works the way I do. I like Win XP, it is a great business product, stable and easy to maintain, but all the usual issues bother me and it looks horrible (Vista is a nightmare to my eyes!) Yes, I know I should have bought a mac, but Photoshop licenses do not come cheap and it was only later that I learned about cross-licensing.

Anyway, I installed Ubuntu onto the laptop with a bit of messing around only to find that getting the winmodem running was troublesome, so I have ditched that plan, but Ubuntu is now running on my main computer, a Dell Precision 470 with dual Xeon processors and 2GB RAM. Wow. It took a bit of messing around especially for the Wacom mouse, but it took me 1 day from scratch, XP takes me half a day working from notes. Next time, setting up the machine should be a few hours.

Fast as in blistering, stable, easy to install extra software, all the business and DTP software I need, secure, quick to setup, open source, no EULAs and beautiful. OK, I still need XP for Photoshop, accurate color management and my Epson 4800 printer, but for the rest of the office work, Ubuntu rocks. The Gnome interface is just lovely to work in.

Nice one Ubuntu - keep it going.

Mike

### Post by mikemcfarlane on 2007-06-01
Hi

To answer Perimere, I've not had a chance to fully compare GIMP to PS yet, although with a bit more time it will happen. My main concerns apart from adjustment layers (don't think GIMP has), color management (I need to investigate Argyll etc), Epson printer drivers (??) and quality RAW conversion (??) is how the time (and heaps of money) I have spent fitting PS into a professional workflow along with other tools can be efficiently transferred to Linux.

Current workflow while not complex is:
- Import RAW files from camera (easy either OS).
- Rough edit of photographs in Adobe Bridge - review photographs, delete duffs, add copyright info, add keywords, rate photographs. (I don't know what I can do this in Linux. I fiddled with digiKam, but need more time.)
- Sometimes optical correction with DxO Optics Pro. (I can do this with Panotools, which suits me because I do a lot of panoramas anyway.)
- Processing in PS. Heavy use of adjustment layers and PS actions. (I need to get my head round GIMP and rewrite actions.)
- Print out from a colour managed set up to an Epson 4800 printer that has ink/paper specific printer profiles. This means I have to calibrate my monitor with a Spyder, so I need Win anyway, and have the control of the print output also.
- Ingest all resulting files into DAM software for indexing and searching.

It just seems to big a step at the moment and reading around the net I think this is still the general consensus. Sure if you are happy to spend the time then you can get somewhere, but my living (and my happiness) is dependent on my photography, so I'm just not sure.

If anyone can point me to any sites, like the link kindly given above, or give me advice on the transition I would be grateful.

Mike
