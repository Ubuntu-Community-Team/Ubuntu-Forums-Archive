### Post by sicofante on 2006-10-18
> **bdb said:**
> Picking your brain here.... Why does it matter to you (or anyone else) if others use Ubuntu or linux in general?
It's explained before in this very thread, but I'll do it again:

I am a consultant. People asks me what to use. No matter how good my reputation, it will always be limited. So when recommending any software, one of the good things I can say about it is that other people have trusted it before succesfully. The more people have trusted it successfully, the better reputation the software gets in the eyes of my customers. In other words: a bigger market share is a guarantee that many different people are using the software with no or little complaints. The corporate market will NEVER move to an OS unless it's proven to be used by a lot of users.

On the other hand, I also install software for family and friends. They want to share both files and ordinary experiences with their own families and friends. If everyone in their social environment uses Windows, it's hard for them to share their experiences and learn from others. They feel isolated and they don't like that. The home market will have a hard time adopting an OS with little market share for reasons like that.

Little market share also means that hardware manufacturers won't have good reasons to care for Linux. The lack of drivers is probably issue number one from a technical point of view and is a direct consequence of little market share.

But on top of these issues, which are important, the quality of the software will never break the barrier imposed by a "made by developers for developers" that Linux suffers so badly until its market share shows that regular users far outgrow advanced users, geeks and developers themselves. This is probably the most serious problem Linux faces today and it might as well never get out of it. Most people here won't even know what I'm talking about because almost everyone approaching these forums is either a geek, an advanced user or simply has fun with computers.

By the way, 10% market share would be enough to solve any of these issues succesfully. Current <1% is not. (These figures belong to the desktop market. Take a look at the server market and you'll see that none of this issues affects it. Why? Because Linux has around a 30% of the market there.)
