### Post by llcooljsl on 2008-04-20
I felt reasonably confident this time around.  It has been about 6 months since I last tried linux, Ubuntu to be exact.  I have been trying it in various forms for the last 5 years and every time I am left feeling disappointed.
I work in IT, predominantly windows computing.  I basically do web design, and computer repairs etc.  So I am not an expert but equally I am not a newbie.
I am having to type this from vista (because my Ubuntu has frozen twice in 20 minutes of running it), an operating system I hate and have stripped down as much as I can to remove the bloat and eye candy which really offers very little.

The only reason I moved from xp pro was that I got a new laptop from an insurance claim and xp was not an option with the laptop I was offered.  

I am writing this simply because I have once again tried linux, full of enthusiasm.  I love the principle behind open source software. I love the security that comes with linux. I love the idea of the reliability of linux, although in reality it is far from reliable in my experience on home computers.  Probably due to driver issues rather than the operating system itself.

I am not a microsoft hater, nor a linux lover.  I simply want an operating system which works, and as this is the slogan of Ubuntu I was hoping to install it, and it just work.
This slogan could not be less warranted than with Ubuntu. If microsoft released ubuntu as its next operating system it would be crucified.  Sure it is secure and more resilient against spyware and viruses but I have not once been able to install linux and get everything to work on my system.  I either have no sound, poor graphics, no wireless support or random crashes and freezes.  
Granted these are all likely to be driver issues and with windows I would need to run the cd for drivers and no operating system can be expected to include all drivers from install.  

My main concern is that fundamentals should be there from the start, and by this I mean wired and wireless networking support should be fundamental, I don't care as much if sound is, or office is, or mp3 software is included from the start.  If I cannot connect to the internet from the off then how can I get updates, or drivers.  
I had to find an rj45 cable as it has been ages since I connected directly to my router for me to be able to get any internet from linux.
I think vista took everything that was bad and unnecessary with windows and made it worse, how many of us spent 20 minutes saying wow the aero glass looks nice before turning it off in favour of a faster system, after I had disabled all visual effects and unnecessary services I ended up with something almost identical in appearance to xp, yet its less reliable, less compatible and much slower than xp.  
Xp pro looks dull but it had become pretty stable, pretty quick and compatible with almost all windows programs and devices.  Xp pro's main problem is its security and vulnerability to viruses and spyware etc which to me leads me quite logically onto linux. 
Instead of making 1000 distros all going in slightly different directions and supporting niche markets or the needs of the minority, I long for a linux distro which has the security, speed, reliability and universal device support that would make even hardy microsoft supporters sit up and listen. Something which I can't see happening until one linux distro wins domination or all linux fans make the choice to put their efforts into one distro.  Choice is great but too much choice can be a limiting thing.
It has been five years since I first tried linux.  I have tried suse, mandriva, red hat, ubuntu and linuxxp and not once have I felt the system was ready.
If im not spending hours finding wireless drivers, or graphics drivers which fit my type of linux then I am spending 40 minutes finding a video program which will play a dvd or music player which will play mp3s.
Once I am on the internet in XP, I go to any manufacturers website, click on my graphics card, or printer or whatever and I get the drivers. It self extracts and installs without any command line or terminal use.  
To me the terminal should be an optional function for advanced users who prefer to use it.  Home computing took off thanks partly to GUI's and I see no place for command lines or terminals in home computing.  
Linux is still years from being adopted mainstream in my eyes and wont be until it adopts the following:
  [LIST]
[*]Remove any need for command line/terminals
[*]combine red hat and debian based linux into one unified effort
[*]Increase number of supported devices and quality of drivers
[*]Have a genuine 'it just works' experience  with inbuilt wireless and wired networking support from the start, MP3 support and DVD support from the start.
[/LIST]
I am sure as people read this they will be picking holes in everything I say, and I concede all of the issues I mentioned probably do have solutions or workarounds but my point is not that I hate linux or the idea of it, just that if a windows geek like me cannot get it working or is struggling then what chance has my dad or nan, or my children? 
I have not posted specific faults or issues because that is not the point of this post, its more an overall impression that I am trying to convey.
I will check back in another 6 months and see if things have changed but I wont be holding my breath.  
So many people are desperate to use anything but windows, but with linux not ready, and mac os x only legally available with apple hardware I am stuck with windows again.
Any way I welcome any useful comments or discussion.
Thanks

Command line/terminal requirements have been dramatically decreased, but i **prefer** it over GUI, since it runs faster, and gives you more output.  However, when i first started, it took me a while to get used to it (and a bit of frying of my system), but once you get used to it, it's really quite simple.

Combining Red Hat and Debian is completely counter-productive, as red hat based distros run on rpm builds, and debian runs off deb builds, and in turn the package managers handle things differently, and basically if you merged the two, you'd come up with something many times worse than DOS (in my opinion).  Also, Linux isn't linux for me, Red Hat and Debian are pretty much different base systems, it's like trying to add x+y to get 2x, it just doesn't work.  These days if someone says "yeah, i use linux too" i usually ask "what system exactly?".  Also, i got a question the other day of "where can i download Linux?" and i spent the better part of 2 hours explaining it's not one linux to download, but a wide range of options, and helped him decide which would be best for his laptop.

Linux is steadily increasing support for hardware, but every day new hardware comes on the market, if they don't supply drivers for their own hardware, no one can ever expect devs who reverse-engineer the drivers we use to ever keep up.  Got a problem with lacking support?  Talk to the manufacturers.

The "It just works" experience is there, but due to the LiveCDs existing and the small size of the CD ISOs, they can't possibly put drivers on there for all hardware, and think of it: Ubuntu comes with a lot more pre-installed than XP.  Lastly, about the codecs: Ubuntu can't supply them pre-installed because of size-issues, and there are some inconsistencies with laws about supplying them pre-installed.  However, it's not hard to install them...

If you come to Linux expecting Windows, you're going to be disappointed and end up posting threads about how much it sucks.  Why? because you've come to expect something that isn't possible without the kind of market share or money that Microsoft wields (not very well i might add) when producing their OS.  Come to Linux expecting something new, and being willing to learn, then you will come to realize the beauty of this OS, and the customizability.  If you're not ready to do that, then don't try to install Linux on your own, order a pre-installed notebook/Desktop system from Dell, because they work "out of the box" and will give you a more windows-like experience.  But that, in my opinion, defeats the purpose.

I have somewhat mixed feelings about Ubuntu trying to support every piece of hardware on the market. There really aren't many things left that *require* the command line when you're using Ubuntu. Typically, people who are writing tutorials or offering support use command line instructions because it's a lot easier to say:

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-04-21
I realise that you're not just trashing Linux to be a troll.

Drivers are a hairy problem, because every time a new wireless card or chipset is released, it communicates with the comuter in a different manner, so a new driver must be written. Until mainstream manufacturers write Linux drivers for their hardware, we're always going to be a little bit behind. Or, alternatively, hardware manufacturers should get together and work out universal standards for communicating between operating system and hardware, so a single driver could work with all devices of a particular class of hardware. It's been done for USB Mass Storage devices, and it needs to be done for wireless, printers and scanners.

Even *sound cards* require different drivers. It's ridiculous, but that's how the manufacturers are playing it. They won't even release specifications, most of the time. Specifications would help Linux developers to implement support quickly.

Having said that, many wireless cards work "out-of-the-box". The last two computers I built with wireless worked out-of-the-box. Wireless networking support is there in Linux, it just needs more drivers (or a standardised driver supported by the manufacturers).

DVD playback out-of-the-box is not achieved even on Windows. The reason it's not done on Ubuntu is because of a law in America called the DMCA. DVD playback involves decryption, and the companies that make the legal decryption software charge a per-head fee for it.

I suspect that some of your trouble is due to you not realising that Linux is fundamentally different to Windows. We do things very differently in this world. Not necessarily more difficult (in fact, I personally think it's easier). We find that people with a lot of Windows knowledge struggle because they have to unlearn things that have become second-nature. Don't worry, we all switched from a different operating system, so we're proof that we can adapt to Linux's differences :-)
