### Post by unoodles on 2008-08-11
Most people who are new to linux install a linux distribution and say "Yes! I have a completely free operating system!" what they don't know is that this is not exactly the case.
As most people know, there are restricted or non-free packages that can be installed on a linux distribution. Most people install these because they are necessary to get wireless, or compiz, or even flash player.
I will not stand for this. I have completely removed all restricted and non-free packages from my laptop. Inluding linux-restricted-module and flashplayer-nonfree. Fortunately, I DON'T NEED THEM!! I have an Intel 3945ABG Pro, which runs on completely free drivers. My video card is an intel 965GM x3100. Desktop effects? YES! WITH free drivers.
Flashplayer? No. I prefer swfdec.
msttcorefonts? who needs them anyway?

It felt great removing all these non-free packages. But it was not enough...

I installed the tool vrms. It showed two packages as being non-free. human-icon-theme and tangerine-icon-theme. So, I removed them. This also uninstalled ubuntu-desktop and ubuntu-artwork. Too bad. I will not let *that* restrict my freedom.

But... Still not enough.

Firefox is not completely free. It is restricted by trademarks. So, purged. Galeon works just fine.

Think that was enough? Not in a lifetime.

There was still non-free data on my hard drive. And it had to be removed. I had Music, Movies, and other misc. data that was not free. So, purged it all. THATS RIGHT! over 20GB GONE!

Enough? never.

non-free data still gets onto my hard drive. Through the Galeon cache. So a simple python script, to monitor the Galeon cache and delete files that come in.

Ok. Now I've deleted enough. :)

### Post by Keyper7 on 2008-08-12
I don't know if this whole thread is just a parody or not, but if you're really taking this freedom thingie seriously, then I must say you are *very* far from complete and total freedom yet. :)

The iwl3945 *driver* is free, but your Intel 3945ABG Pro wireless won't work without the *microcode*, which consists in non-modifiable proprietary binary blobs. The [iwlwifi license FAQ](http://intellinuxwireless.org/?n=FAQ&s=license) says very clearly that

> The wireless drivers themselves are licensed under the GPL version 2. Each of the wireless adapters requires a corresponding microcode or firmware image to be loaded in order to function. The microcode for the ipw3945 driver (ieee80211 based) also requires the use of a user space regulatory daemon. **All of those binaries are distributed as binaries under a proprietary, free to redistribute (but not modify), license.**

You can see in [here](http://packages.ubuntu.com/hardy/i386/linux-ubuntu-modules-2.6.24-16-386/filelist) that Ubuntu ships those blobs by default in linux-ubuntu-modules package. This is a very old exception in the free-by-default Ubuntu policy. [Mark Shuttleworth himself](http://www.markshuttleworth.com/archives/84) has no problem in admitting that

> Ubuntu has included firmware, and used proprietary drivers since its inception. That’s always been a slightly uncomfortable proposition, (...) but it’s been true since the Warty Warthog.

And as for mp3's and DVD's, you do realize that open-source and free are different things, right? There are patents involved in mp3 encoding and css encryption that do not allow gstreamer-ugly and libdvdcss to be free. That's precisely why they are in the multiverse repository, which, according to the [the Ubuntu site](http://www.ubuntu.com/community/ubuntustory/components),

> contains software that is "not free", which means the licensing requirements of this software do not meet the Ubuntu "main" Component Licence Policy. The onus is on you to verify your rights to use this software and comply with the licensing terms of the copyright holder. 

So you either must stop listening to mp3, watching DVDs and using wireless or simply face that you don't have a completely free system yet. If you want one, why don't avoid the hassle of finding and deleting things and simply install [Gobuntu](http://www.ubuntu.com/products/whatisubuntu/gobuntu)?

Meanwhile, I'm very happy with proprietary codecs and drivers, thank you very much. ;)
