### Post by 3rdalbum on 2009-04-27
> **Saabtite said:**

Funny thing you should say that. A friend of mine has a computer running Vista, and his monitor stopped accepting digital input from the DVI port. It was reporting No Signal. The system would work fine on Safe Mode as it sends analogue video across the DVI, and it would work on normal mode until the graphics card driver loaded. When the graphics card driver loaded, the output would switch to digital and the screen would go blank.

After swapping out the graphics card and cable, checking Vista system logs (nothing important logged), going to an earlier restore point and reinstalling Vista, we finally have the idea to take the monitor to my place and try it out on Ubuntu.

There couldn't be more difference. When the graphics card driver loaded and Ubuntu tried sending digital video, it realised that the monitor wasn't picking up the signal, switched back to analogue mode and reported "No screens found", and offered to run in safe graphics mode. This was MUCH more helpful than Vista, which didn't even realise that the digital signal wasn't reaching its destination.

The point is, if anything goes wrong in Windows, you're completely up the creek. Ubuntu could at least recognize a problem and work around it.

### Post by Yeti can't ski on 2009-04-28
Saabtite, 

Sorry that you got some “not nice” replies. I apologize on behalf of all. I  understand your frustration, but - believe me, I have been there - it is more of a temporary cultural shock . 

As discussed above, the feeling that M$ Windows "just works" is the result of the role reserved for users in the universe of proprietary software. Users are supposed to be passive and dumb "read-only" parts of the process.

With Ubuntu (and Gn-Linux in general) you have much more freedom, power and possibilities, but that means accepting more responsibility (as an active “read-write” user) and having to learn. 

That said, I do agree that some aspects of Ubuntu could be more polished (sound configuration, for instance, is still too messy). However, it is constantly getting better and better. Remember also that devs not only have to address GNU/Linux's own problems but have also to deal with uncooperative hardware producers that are captives of M$ and won't release firmware code. 

If you want peace of mind, my advice is to avoid Beta versions and recent upgrades. Use only LTS versions and wait a few week before upgrading when most priority bugs are addressed. 

Sorry for the long post. 

Good luck!
