### Post by Pausanias on 2010-02-11
I've been using Ubuntu as my main OS since 2005 (Hoary). I love so much about it---the community, the "just works" aspects of it, even the color theme. I'm writing this from Ubuntu 9.10 running on a Mac Pro. I consider myself fairly experienced, able to compile the kernel on my own and so on.

But I'm getting worn down by the regression cycle. It goes like this: Ubuntu works mostly well, but something essential is flaky. I upgrade, because the new version fixes this problem. However, the upgrade causes something else essential to become flaky. This is the "regression cycle." It leaves me with a choice of either staying with a current problem or taking on a future one.

Often a simple trial of the new version is not enough to discover the regression. I'll give you a specific example. I was affected by the Jaunty freeze bug---machine would hang inexplicably, usually in the middle of some CPU intensive activity. Upgrading to Karmic has fixed this bug. However, it caused iwlagn to no longer support wireless-n connections (it always drops down to wireless-g, see [https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/linux/+bug/437990](https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/linux/+bug/437990)). I didn't discover this until a month into the Karmic upgrade, when I actually needed n speeds.

I know that with enough work, any single issue can be mitigated, and that it will be resolved in the next release. But the uncertain and messy stated of the binary blobs means that I now have great fear in any upgrade. There is always chance that the new NVIDIA driver, the new wifi driver, and the new kernel won't get along on my particular setup. More importantly, there is a chance that whatever regression is there won't rear its head until some months in the future. And, with my free time becoming increasingly scarce, I no longer have the luxury of patient digging through these forums and the launchpad bug reports to fix the problem.

Sticking to LTS releases should help with this problem. From now on, I will avoid non-LTS releases like the plague, as they have caused me to lose much valuable time searching for solutions. I should have stuck with Hardy, but was lured by the likes of usb-creator and non-crashy compiz. Never again.

I don't mean to be ungrateful---I greatly enjoy the OS and the community, and recognize the value and the constraints of having a volunteer-driven organization. However, I no longer have the same enthusiasm for the OS that I used to. I am less likely to recommend it to non-experts, and I greatly regret the loss of time caused my decision to try non-LTS releases. 

Thanks for listening to my rant.

### Post by GodLikeCreature on 2010-02-12
> **jdrodrig said:**
> Thanks for the response.

I would say, definitely YES.

The moment we start forcing people to do stuff, even if we think is for their own good, we become "Microsoft". 

For me, Linux is about choice; and that choice includes my personal taste for how deep I want to go in terms of computer knowledge and how much I just want a point-and-click experience.

I couldn't disagree more.

Whenever you have a problem with a Microsoft machine, there is nothing you can learn, for all choices were made for you.  Meaningless error messages pointing to hex memory addresses are not really useful.  Ultimately, when a device is not properly working, you can either reboot and hope that fixes it, or try to uninstall/reinstall.  If it does not work by then, you just move on.  Linux is different, settings make sense, and if you want to fix something that didn't work, you will most likely find that you can dig deep enough to find the solution.  If you don't want to, just move on like Windows users have for ages.  Linux is awesome, but not perfect.

On the other hand, what you say is all very nice, but just utopia.  It is impossible to provide an interface that is all simple, yet as powerful and flexible as Linux is.  You can only make it so simple without starting to remove functionality.  

A good example is the security hole GNOME and KDE both have due to .desktop shortcuts.  If you heard about it, both desktop environments sport a feature via which a .desktop shortcut can elevate privileges, thus resulting in a potential security flaw which makes Linux virus vulnerable.  (you can find more about it from this link: [http://www.geekzone.co.nz/foobar/6229](http://www.geekzone.co.nz/foobar/6229)).  Are you going to tell me that is a decision you made?  Obviously not, but it makes things easier on the final user.  Would you decide otherwise and enforce the user to validate his credentials every time they double click on a shortcut?  Hmm...  No easy win.

You can make it flexible and powerful, but that implies lots of options available, and the need for understanding.  On the other hand, you can make it simple and easy, but that will either lead you to HUGE security problems (Windows) or just dumbed down functionality (say Google Chrome OS).  

Let's face it, there is an element of user responsibility that should be implicit.  It is implicit for Mac users, and I don't see any of them complaining.  I know quite a few people who have recently moved from Windows to Mac, and instead of complaining about the differences or its limitations, they can't stop highlighting its strengths:

- No word about the lack of good games to play with
- No word about the limited compatible hardware available 
- No word about the obvious look & feel and functionality changes
- And last but not least, no word about paying twice as much for a computer/OS combination which does NOT perform better than a well setup Linux box does.

The flexibility in Linux does not come solely from a simple interface that works under each and every possible scenario.  This flexibility comes from the fact that you can go ahead and customize (choice, as you said it) it to the maximum.  You can change the code from any application or system feature to suit your needs, but you obviously need to know how to do it.  That knowledge would be worthless under any propietary OS, but in Linux it does make a difference.  

Therefore, choice is there for you, be sure about it.  If you happen to decide to use Gnome, or KDE, or XFCE, or Enlightenment (ah, choice again), then you obviously decide to use a piece of software designed by someone else who already decided certain things for you.  You could obviously decide (as some people I know do) to not use a desktop environment, or decide to create your own.  Please, don't say there is limited choice in Linux or that we are becoming MS.  We are light years apart from MS or Apple, fortunately.

If anything, I believe the Linux community should close an important information gap, so that new users fully understand what they are about to get into.  I am convinced most of the frustration that comes from Linux shortcomings is closely related to not having the right expectations.  

Why is there an informal understanding that you can install Linux in any machine and it should work?  That is ********, and the whole Linux community should ensure we make it clear that is not the case.  

Linux works on a wider range of hardware than any other OS out there AFAIK (try Windows on a Mac, or both Mac and Windows on a random PC), but it does not support EVERYTHING, and that should be made very clear.

Moreover, I think we should make an effort to explain the difference between an OEM installation and a standard installation.  People are so used to getting their new PCs with a custom image of Windows preinstalled, that they just assume that is how Windows works in general, when we all know that is not the case.

I simply think we should be realistic, be the first to admit Linux limitations, and be open and clear about them.  Once we have clearly communicated what a new user is up to, we should stop being so self critical and embrace the endless list of awesome features available in our favorite OS.  Don't get me wrong, I like critical thinking, and I think it is the main reason why Linux has achieved so much, but sometimes I am tired to see so much negativity about something so incredibly good.

Ultimately, we are all responsible for the future of Linux, and if we take an honest, open and enthusiastic approach, I think we can continue to improve its great potential!

I don't know about the whole discussion going on in here. 

As a comment to it, all choices have tradeoffs. No choice is perfectly considerate of all options. Some are OPTIMAL given a wide range of criteria, but this can quickly become a matter of user opinion or conditioning. 

In my opinion, because I am always somebody willing to do more to get something working **exactly the way I want it too**, an operating system should be flexible and highly extensible. Being thus, it should not be insecure; it should be highly robust and stable. 

Windows operating systems generally don't fit **any** of the above criteria in the short term or long term. Mac OSX is halfways, but the closed architecture damns it in my eyes. I like using them, but the experience is just like using any given Linux + Compiz. Only difference: plethora of options and applications that Mac OS X cannot compete with. 

In that case, Linux is a very nice operating system AS IT IS. Understand that it is like that and that it shouldn't change for no good reason. I think the UI can be streamlined, but let the fundamental OS stay as extensible, modular, flexible, robust, and stable as it is. 

Personally, I for one am for as much backwards compatibility as possible, but sometimes that costs time that just isn't there. Arbitrarily, however, backwards compatibility should not be discarded if it can be helped. I.e., "we don't support computers more than x years old." Arbitrary doesn't computer in the universe. Everything in the universe is with respect to one or more other variables. Nothing exists for no reason. Ergo, nothing in our universe should be for no reason. 


**As for the original poster: **Your post is a bit vague. I can't exactly condone things that aren't well planned and researched. If you have the LTS installed, and you are satisfied, that should stay the same until such time when the next LTS has been released. Once said  release, spend a little time researching how well this will work for you. Don't just automatically upgrade. In fact, I bet within a week, all the **worst, most horrible, atrocious, most egregious** bugs will be reported. And within a given amount of time... fixed. So, let's say worst case scenario you end up waiting a month or two before upgrading to the new LTS, you still have almost 2 years of stability and reliability. You also don't have to worry about pulling your hair out wondering what happened. 

There are two ways I install Linux operating systems: robust and testing. I installed half a dozen Linux flavors on my laptop testing how they used my hardware. I was patient throughout this process knowing I was just "fooling around". I KNOW HOWEVER: I have the the Dell Ubuntu 9.04 recovery cd that supports ALL MY HARDWARE. This is my blanket... [metaphor] To you, your "blanket" might be the last LTS. 

So, plan well: backup your system before testing. If things work great now, that's your primary blanket... A blank reinstall of that older version is your backup blanket. If things work great with the new install.. Congratz!! But don't just assume it will go seamlessly. 

Hope that helps. I post this because I hear lots of people "frustrated with Linux installation". Well, first off, Linux installations are typically painless. It's the poor planning that goes into them [users fault 9/10] that's painful. Sometimes, if its hardware, just googling for a few minutes to search for that hardware relating to that Linux distro/version is **all** it takes. Sometimes just commonsense can stave off frustration: **if it isn't broken, don't fix it!!! **
