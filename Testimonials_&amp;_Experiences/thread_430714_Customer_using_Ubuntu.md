### Post by SonicSteve on 2007-05-02
Good morning, (depending on where you are)
Last week I had a customer request a computer without windows. Before you get too excited it was because she thought that she had her own windows XP CD. So I configured up the computer and tested it. Before I gave to her I wiped the Hard Drive. I gave her all the CD's and documentation and I also gave her an Ubuntu 6.06 CD. I wrote a little note on it explaining what it was, and I also showed it to her before she left. 

So...she got her computer home and her** daughter** put in her windows CD and it didn't boot up properly. It wasn't my fault I set the boot order to be CD then Hdd. When her daughter called me about it I asked her to put the windows CD in the drive, It was then that I realized that the CD wasn't windows but MS office!! I then told her she had two options, 1. I would sell her a discounted OEM version of windows. or.. 2. Give Ubuntu a shot. 

Time will tell but so to my knowledge she is still using Ubuntu. I expected a call once setup had completed but it  connected to her internet connection, and installed perfectly, and to this point no call. edit; I know this because I called her the next day to see how things were.

In case you're wondering why I didn't send her home with Edgy or Feisty it's this. I didn't have any pretty Ship it CD's for those versions and the graphics card can't support Desktop Effects. So I weighed the pros and cons and figured a nice pretty ShipIt version of  Ubuntu would look better for a new user.

### Post by aysiu on 2007-05-09
Wikipedia and Open Source operate a bit differently as far as I can tell (someone please correct me if I'm wrong about this).

My perception is that Wikipedia allows just about anyone to edit or write entries (regardless of intention, bias, academic background) and relies on the community to "correct" any factual errors that may appear.

Open Source, on the other hand, allows anyone to submit patches to the original developer(s), who can then accept or reject those patches; or allows anyone to fork an existing project and modify it and release it as her own.

It's fine distinction, but they're not quite the same thing.
