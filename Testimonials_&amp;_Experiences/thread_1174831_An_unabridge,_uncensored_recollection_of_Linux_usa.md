### Post by Digital Hick on 2009-05-31
One day I walked into a discount store and found a book about Linux.  I did not know the first thing about Linux except that it was an operating system like Windows.  I considered myself a geek more or less, so I decided it was time to find out what this Linux was like.  I had never installed an operating system from scratch that I could remember.  So I spent the seven dollars or so and bought this thing.  I got home and started reading through this considerable tome.  It talked about partitioning and formatting hard drives, accidentally erasing your data, and file format incompatibilities.  All the stuff you want to tell someone new to your software to scare them off.  

    So with no idea of what I was doing I partitioned may hard drive, and installed Fedora Core 3.  I later found out that Linux was supposed to be hard to install.  It worked on the first try, and FC3 was running within 24 hours.  So much for that myth.  I would like to say that everything was peaches and cream from there on out.  But then I found out about the REAL problem with Linux.  Configuring the OS to do something.  FC3 could not talk to my modem, printer, Windows XP, or anything else for that matter.  So, while the installation went fine, it was just a novelty after that.  Oh, look, another operating system, and it can't do a thing! Yipee!  Unlike most people who would be trying to get it off their system at that point I decided to let it in place.  Who knows, maybe I'll figure something out.

    Some time went by, I read more of the book, looked things up in the Internet using Windows XP since FC3 could not connect and started learning new things.  To my pleasant surprise the terminal/console in Linux is essentially like Unix.  I had a former job where all my work was done from the command line on a VMS/VAX.  Mind you, I did not program or anything, I just ran stuff other people made, but still this was good.  I had someplace to start from to understand what was going on.  I soon found out that Linux was basically Unix's (grand)child and that they had a lot in common.  The FC3 interface, which was less than impressive, suddenly took on a different sheen.  Unix with a GUI!  OMG!  What a great idea.  I never had a GUI on the VAX, not unless you count ASCII art.  Now I can do both, CLI and GUI, whatever works.  Unfortunately I also found out that Linux does not like winmodems and that little work was being done to fix the problem.  So FC3 was basically setting there, going nowhere, fast.

    I liked the license agreement on Fedora, it was like a breath of fresh air.  The legalese seemed less dense and there was not this constant droning of NO-DON'T-NO-CAN'T that I had gotten from everything else up to that point in time.  It was not long after this that I started to grasp open source software being more than just Firefox which I had been using religiously for a year once I found out how bad Internet Explorer was.  I was also finding out that I did not know nearly as much as I thought I did about computers.  The years at community college got me introduced to a lot of things, but not nearly enough.

    Around this time I was trying to find a job.  I was called in for an interview and was asked point blank if I knew anything about Linux.  How about that, this penguin is coming out of the woodwork all of the sudden.  I told them I had starting working with it, yet I had not set up any servers.  I did not get the job.  Another job interview happened, the penguin came up again.  I didn't get that one either.  But that left me with the distinct thought, Linux is in the server room and the classroom. I went home and started picking with Linux some more.

    I went back to the discount store and found other Linux books hidden in corners and cracks.  Soon I was working with Knoppix, Fedora Core 4, Suse 9, and other stuff.  I was beginning to understand distinctions between the different Linux distributions and between Linux and Windows.  But Suse's licencse read like Redmond's, and I did not care for that.  Yes, Suse has mp3 support, but there is more to life than mp3 support.  Unfortunately, right about when things start looking up with Linux, something would go wrong.  I wrecked the Fedora Core installation.  Fedora Core 5 would not put up a GUI.  I already knew X was flaky.  But this time I really did it.  I could not get anything except a GRUB error.  Now I was mad, all my data, still in XP was gone.  What now?  I remembered Knoppix ran off the optical drive!  I hauled out Knoppix, put it in the DVD drive, booted up, mounted the NTFS partition, and burned a CD of all my personal stuff on the XP partition.  Linux gives and Linux takes away.  Linux was junk as far as I was concerned.  At least Knoppix has a real practical use to it, it could be a rescue disk.  Next, I got the pleasure of finding out that GRUB is not a sweet heart once it is broken.  Eventually I got XP reinstalled, freed myself of this Linux mess, and swore I would never install Linux again.

    A funny thing happened on the way back to the Borg Collective though.  I had the original version of Windows XP, not service pack 2, not even service pack 1.  That meant downloading 4 years worth of updates on a dial up connection.  Oh, the joy!  I finally broke down and got cable broadband.  There was no way I was going to torture myself at 2.5 kb/sec for the next month waiting for everything to update.  And besides that when I connected to the Internet, I could tell something was trying to hack my machine because I was getting pop-ups on a freshly installed XP telling me to go to thus-and-thus site to scan for problems.  That was unnerving.  How can I know my machine was not already compromised before it was even updated?  I couldn't, that's how!   I went ahead and updated XP as far as I could and reinstalled all my programs.  I noticed something delightful in the process.  BROADBAND IS STINKING FAST!  I could do stuff I could never have done on dial-up.  

    As I moved around the Internet at warp 5 now instead of impulse speed I noticed that Linux could be downloaded and burned onto a CD.  Hmmm.  That is interesting.  As I wandered around I found out that there are more Linux distributions than Carter had liver pills. I kept hearing about Ubuntu.  Ubuntu this, Ubuntu that, Ubuntu on waffles, Ubuntu at the mall, Ubuntu on everything.  So what, lets try Ubuntu, it can't be worse than Fedora can it?  So I tried to download the ISO file and found out that a resumable downloader would be good about now, and XP did not come with one.  So I found and open source one.  Once I got it downloaded I tried to burn it to a CD, and XP could not do that.  So I had to find an open source thing that could do that.  Once I got it burned I installed it.  That was easy.  Six clicks and boom, a new operating system.  Orange, really, that is the best they could do for a color scheme?  I guess blue is taken.  So I called my ISP and asked, &#8220;Can I use Linux on the network?&#8221;  I did not want to fall afoul of them less than a month after getting on line.  Who knows, maybe they do not want Linux wandering around on their network?  The girl who answered the phone said, &#8220;As long as you have Windows XP or a Mac you will be fine.  You can run anything on them you want.&#8221;  I told her that Linux was another operating system, it was a replacement for Windows or a Mac.  She said, &#8220;Let me talk to the tech.&#8221;  God, I just love script-jockeys at call centers.  (I used to be one, so I know what it is like.)  At least she spoke clear English.  The tech, to his/her credit, had enough sense/compassion to say 'yes'.  Stand back, I thought!  I got out there and updated Ubuntu pronto.  The update sizes are too large in my opinion, and they still are, but it was seamless at least.

    So now what, start trying out hardware.  Well the mouse, keyboard, and monitor works.  The printer works.  It can read and write floppies and CDs.  But then I ran head long the next biggest problem after configuration.  Multimedia.  Ah, nothing runs on Ubuntu.  No movies, no flash, almost nothing.  Then I found out about the patents, copyrights, and what not that keeps Ubuntu, and Linux in general, from shipping this stuff.  Yipee, from one broken system to another.  I can either live in an insecure cake-and-ice-cream  concentration camp built on a flood plain of Windows XP or a beans-beans-and-Oh-here-are-some-more-beans fortress of Linux.  I eventually started to find out that things are not as bad as they seemed.  Open Office came with Ubuntu, and its MS Office support was improving, Java was available, Adobe had a Linux version of Flash, and there was Nvidia drivers to smooth out the graphics.  Real Player can do mp3, its own formats, and some of Windows, and a couple of others.  There is also the open source formats for multimedia, OGG, and even people who use them, gasp.  So there was a way to plug most of the holes.  But DVDs and Quicktime were still out.

    So time went one, 2007 came and went, and so did the Ubuntu versions.  Each taking a crack at replacing XP, but each falling just a little short.   I was going to give up on getting off of XP and live with the insecurity until I noticed that Windows Genuine Disadvantage had forced itself into my computer despite me turning down having it installed.  Then I saw something checking if I was running Vista every time I started the computer.  I did not like this, not one bit.  I found out that Redmond, as near as I could tell, could turn off your computer remotely if they thought your OS is invalid.  Or if the software malfunctioned and did it anyway.  Which did happen to some people with the WGA server.  That was the last straw.  I had to get off Windows as much as possible.  I am not going to have my digital property(papers, pictures, etc) held hostage by an OS that was both insecure and could be remotely turned off by someone or by accident.  It was Linux or bust now.

    I eventually found a replacement for Windows XP.  It came with my new desktop.  It was called Vista.  (Insert scream of horror here.)  You have to be kidding me, this thing uses 900 MB of RAM idling!  WHAT ON EARTH IS IT DOING!  They moved everything around in the interface.  It takes up like 20 GB on the hard drive.  You still need a virus scanner.  It comes with a program that in the process of looking for malware may damage programs and void your license to use them.  It still needs activated, validated, pasteurized, and can be remotely turned off.  It does not come with a decent photo editor, office suite, web browser (okay, IE 7 is an improvement, but not enough), or the ability to move it to another machine if this one fries on me.  One top of that was the reports of all kinds of compatibility problems Vista was having.  I mean Vista could not see my scanner but Ubuntu could.  XP had not been able to read floppies it had written, but Kubuntu could read them.  If I were a rich man I would buy a Mac, perhaps, but would that solve all the problems?  A Macboy off in the distance shouts, &#8220;YES!&#8221;, but have my doubts.

    So Ubuntu 8.04 comes out.  I download it, install it, configure it, and I am happy.  The sun is shining, the birds are singing, and it is a brand new day.  I only have to boot Vista once in a great while.  Blah, blah, blah, you know what is coming.  Someone complains that they can not figure out how to use the computer because of the weird screen (read GRUB boot loader) and other things.  Sigh.  I try to explain, I realize there is no point, I uninstall Ubuntu and completely reload the computer with Veesta Inquisition.  (Right about now XP is looking good by comparison.)  It's a wonderful life, shoot me now.  At least Vista ran faster after reloading it.

    In the meantime I start taking classes again at a local college.  Computer science major, what am I thinking?  It did not work the first time!  Not a speck of Linux in sight at first.  Then I find one guy running Kubuntu, next semester another running Ubuntu, and a hard-core Linux lover running Gentoo.  I don't feel like compiling my own stuff, not yet at least.  Some Macs setting in the lab too.  I try using a Mac for the first time in 17 years I think.  Not bad.  Way to expensive for the hardware you get.  But a nice, clean, secure OS.  To bad Apple does not have the good sense to sell OS X off the shelf for any PC.  But then again, if they did it probably would not work as well.  There would be Mac bugs and driver issues then.

    I eventually got a laptop.  It came with 64-bit Vista.  It only takes 1.2 GB of RAM to idle.  (COUGH)  Plus it has all the other 'benefits' of any other Windows OS.  In fairness 64-bit Vista does run better than 32-bit I think, but YMMV.  I try Ubuntu 8.10 on the lap top.  No go, won't boot.  I wait, try Fedora 11 preview.  First try of Fedora in two-and-a-half-years.  Not bad, graphics is smoother with nouveau driver than Ubuntu 9.04 when running off the CD.  But Fedora is Fedora, wifi is too flaky, and I need that.  Fedora does take firewalling seriously, but why am I saddled with Abiword!  So I install Ubuntu 9.04 64-bit edition on the laptop.  Not bad, but to many things are to hard to get running.  Fall back to 32-bit.  That is when the trouble starts.

    It ran fine on the CD. But once it was installed and configured it started crashing right and left.  What is going on?  Linux was never this unstable.  Even my worst Fedora Core experiences the kernel rarely choked, it was just the X server that would roll over and die.  Real Player crashing, Evolution crashing, printer configuration crashing, theme installation CRASHING!  My God!  It looks like I am going to be trapped in the Redmond Collective forever!  When I say crash, I mean kernel panic, total meltdown crash.  Hold down the power button for 2 seconds crash.  It was ugly.  Then I figured out what was wrong.  The Nvidia driver, the 180 driver is junk.  I realized this after running the Live CD, and seeing all the stuff that was crashing running, slowly, but running.  I uninstall the 180, install the 173 and things stop crashing.  But there is still graphics corruption on two dialog boxes.  Resizing the windows makes that go away.  Hmmmm.  Well, this is close.

    So here it is, Ubuntu is 98% working.  There is still bugs, but I am able to get my work done without any more hassle than in Windows.  (Mind you Word had the good sense one day not to save any changes I made to a document despite hitting save every two minutes, and what am I paying for?)  I wish I could say it was just wonderful, but the truth is that Ubuntu still has some things to work out.  But it is working well enough to get my work done, and that is what counts.  A fairly satisfied customer.  My attitude towards operating systems is that I want to get my work done in as free an environment as possible.  Thankfully I am there.  Have a great day!
