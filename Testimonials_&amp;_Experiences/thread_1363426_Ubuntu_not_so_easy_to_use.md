### Post by bumanie on 2009-12-24
I suggest your problem is that you have just moved from windows to linux. Linux/ubuntu is not necessarily harder than windows, but one has to 
be prepared to learn a new of doing things. The reason for needing to do things as super user, needing permissions etc. is part of the security that *nix systems have built in to them from the ground up - that is one of the reasons why there is not much, if any, problems with viruses in linux. Things are quite simple once you do some reading and learn how to do things, but it takes time to learn. If you think back, you would have needed to learn how to do things in windows too, but once learned, you know how to do them and it becomes second nature - the same with linux/ubuntu, once you learn how to do things, it becomes easier and you will know what to do to complete a particular task - but you need to take time to learn.
Most drivers are included in the kernel, did you wonder why you did not have to use a 'driver disc' to get the hardware and motherboard synchronized? For most 'proprietary' drivers, one does not need to go and download anything, they are usually found under System --> Administration --> Hardware Drivers ; Click on the drivers that are not activated and they will automatically download from the ubuntu repositories. Believe me, once you learn how the OS operates, many things are easier than windows, but there is one caveat, you have to be prepared to learn and read and try things.

@tamccullough I think you are confusing two things, ease of use != familiarity

You are familiar with Windows and you know how to accomplish your tasks. As you gain experience with Ubuntu you will find the process is the same on both os, if you do not know how to do something you need to read up on it or ask for help.

Either way, you need to learn a new application, either gparted or a windows application, take you pick.

More modern operating systems (be they from MS, Apple or one of the linux vendors) are more restrictive specifically to harden the OS against external threats.

It is this kind of logic that leads to you needing to authenticate things and change permissions and so on....By default the OS assumes things are untrustworthy and dangerous...it can be annoying, but it is much safer.

(N.B. for the pedants out there...yes I know unix has had this kind of permissions system from the year dot, and I know that any NT kernel based MS software also has the capability...I'm just saying that now its pretty common behaviour across all platforms)

> **tamccullough said:**

There's an assumption in your question that Windows is the default, and that Linux is designed as an alternative to it. That's not the case. Linux has been around since 1991 (Windows 3.0 era), and is based on a Unix tradition that goes back much further, to the 1970's. Windows was never an influence on its design in the early days, although I think it's increasingly seeped in over the years. So "Why change that?" is the wrong question -- we can't "change" things from a way that, in Linux, they never were. We could only change things *to* the way they work in Windows. Which, as a long-time Linux user, I have to say I find unappealing.

### Post by Methuselah on 2009-12-25
Welcome to the linux community tamccullough!

You'll find many things different but in time you'll become accustomed.
I used the terminal first in windows but whenever I use windows now I almost always type ls, or cp instead of the required dir or copy.
