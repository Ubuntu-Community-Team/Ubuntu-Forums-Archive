### Post by gemma.laming on 2010-07-01
I want to remind everyone that in some African languages, “Ubuntu” means community; coming from South Africa, this meant a lot to me, and it was one of the reasons I chose the operating system - it has a nice inclusive name.  It was also relatively easy to install, unlike many versions of Linux which require a technical knowledge well beyond my abilities.


There are some outstanding features of the system, namely:

 

 Plusses:  
 

 Stable
 

 Multiple desktops (papers)
 

 Compact on the hard drive, implying good design.
 

 For laptops the side panel of the mousepad being the equivalent of the Microsoft mouse wheel is a solution as practical as it is elegant.   
 



But for me the downsides were just too much to deal with: 

 

 Minusses:  
 

 Profoudly difficult to use with external machinery: an example is of my printers (a perennial problem for me, and perhaps the major reason for abandoning Linux).  The printer would appear as though it was correctly installed but would only give out error messages of varying kinds.  Sadly my technical knowledge - and my determination to learn the finer technical details of the system - was lacking.  
 

 9.10 did not work for me, and no help was available from the forum.  Whether 9.10 is better than 9.04 is something I will never know!  
 

 Lack of support: Not being able to post queries on this site in “ordinary language” posed a big problem for me.  Even trying to understand the manner in which a query should be posted, was as big a challenge as sorting out the problem itself.  
 

 Does not work with some website software (this was a big minus)   No doubt some tweaks in some well-hidden registry would solve this problem quickly - but going into the code that runs an OS is well beyond my capabilities, and certainly beyond my ability to trust myself not to make one spelling mistake.   
 

 In short, Ubuntu posed problems for me that were insurmountable.  Please remember that “Ubuntu” in translation means “only for techies”.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2010-07-01
Never ceases to amaze me when someone gets fed up with the problems in Ubuntu, posts an "I'm out here ..." thread, and then all kinds of folks jump in with offers to help.

This whole exercise presumes the OP is hanging around jut to read the comments ... which, if they really meant they were leaving, are not.

Personally, having been around Linux distros for several years know, I readily sympathize with the unwillingness of folks to become "overnight techies", having to invest hours of their times, just to get something simple working like printing.

MS Windows is much-maligned in these forums, sometimes for good reasons, sometimes not ... but the simple common fact is that, more often than not, it "just works".  You can blame the equipment providers all you want for NOT providing working Linux drivers, but most of the folks using that equipment don't care WHY it doesn't work; instead, they just want an OS in which it DOES work.

It only takes a couple of frustrations like this one to turn them away ... and in some cases, for good.

It also doesn't help the situation when each major release seems to break stuff that has been working for some time.  Look at all the threads now that start out with "I just did an upgrade and my machine won't boot anymore ..." and similar complaints.

These are folks whom, if their cars start making "funny noises", they don't put on mechanics overalls and pull out a set of tools; instead, they take the car in to their dealer (or favorite mechanic) and have THEM fix it.  They don't know how it works; they don't want to know.

Remember, they invested hundreds of dollars (or their equivalent) in MS Windows products; they got ours for free.  So, they probably don't feel a sense of having to hang around to "get their money's worth", as they would in MS Windows.

### Post by 2edgesword on 2010-07-02
I think Mark make some excellent points.
 
Out of frustration with Windows and at the recommendation of a friend I installed Ubuntu on a laptop at home. The benefits were an amazing increase in speed especially with respect to boot-up and browsing. The downside was no sound.
 
I'm no geek by any means but having been around computers for 30 years (since the early days of the Atari 800XL) I've done a little in the way of DOS commands and Basic programming so following the instructions for suggested fixes for the sound issue wasn't a major hurtle for me, BUT for the vast majority of PC users even that little bit of challenge to change configuration files and swamping drivers would be enough to turn them off.
 
Like a bitpull I grab onto a problem and stick with it to find a fix. My wife will comment "you're still trying to ______ (fill in the blank)" and after a week I have "beautiful" sound (nothing makes you appreciate it more then working on finding a fix for over a week :) ).
 
It seems most of the frustration people using Windows have has nothing to do with issues like no sound, problems installing a printer, scanner, wi-fi, etc. but rather long boot times, sluggish performance and frequent system crashes. For the most part dealing with those issues just requires learning patients and how to reboot Windows (power off, power on).
 
On the other hand fixing a driver or other hardware installation issues in Ubuntu in many cases requires reading, carefully following instructions and sometimes trial and error until the fix is found. Many of today's PC users just aren't up to the task. If it isn't a simple matter of "plug and play" to add a new device, or at a maximum putting a disc in the CD drive to load in new software, they won't mess with it and will quickly get frustrated only to say "Goodbye Ubuntu".
 
For many, computers are viewed as appliances, like a television set or microwave oven. Plug it in, turn it on, add the peripherals and go. I'm sure that's what the Ubuntu developers are striving for but making that happen 100% of the time, given all of the various possible options with respect to hardware combinations and software to drive them, make that task incredible difficult.
