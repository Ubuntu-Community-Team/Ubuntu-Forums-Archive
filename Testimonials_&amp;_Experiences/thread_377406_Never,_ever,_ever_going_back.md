### Post by kielcary on 2007-03-06
Read the whole post so you can understand what it's about :)

I had a horrible week with Linux. 

 I bought a new Alienware about 4 months ago.  I owned AW back when they were the top-notch small-guy, and man have things changed over the past 7 years since I first bought one.

Now, they suck.  I have some great overpriced hardware.

AMD 5000+
2 GB Ram
8800 GTS

Ran Windows well enough.  To make a long story short, I lost a HD on another machine, reinstalled, went through an absolute horror with MS trying to get it reactivated, and was basically told to screw off.  I decided to finally make the transition to Linux.  I got some recommendations, looked at several distros, and selected Ubuntu.

Downloaded basic Ubunto i386.  Took me 20 minutes to figure out how to add "noapic" (remember I"'m a total newb here) Couldn't get to the graphical installer.  Tried the x64 installer.  Same thing.  That was a waste of a lot of time.

Got Fedora Core 6 to install.  Got an xserver error.  Spent a day trying to fix that.

Went back to Ubuntu.  Got the alt x64 ISO downloaded.  Hurray - it installed.  Rebooted.  No xserver.  Turns out I can't load it without the new Nvidia drivers.  At this point I'd spent 15 hours minimum troubleshooting this.

This was the first time I seriously considered giving up.

Next day I went back searching the forums.  Tried everything I could.  Finally took out the 8800 and plugged in a 7900...walah!  Hurray!  But how to get the 8800 working?

Tried lots of things.  Manual driver install worked, but after I updated everything it suddenly didn't work.  I spent two days, finally got it all worked out.  You have no idea the elation I felt when i got Ubuntu running on my 8800.

Then I went on to try to install Beryl.  This caused more problems.  I nearly gave up again... Got Beryl working with the 7900, but not he 8800.  Finally, after a couple of more nights hard at work (the second night being tonight), I got Ubunu, Beryl, and my 8800 working together.  6 days for a total newb to get it to work.  Almost gave up several times.  

Here is my conclusion.

I've browsed the web.  I've downloaded.  I've installed.  I've learned.  I've smiled.  I've wept.  I got things working.  I've witnessed the wonders of Beryl on an Nvida 8800.  I've installed Cedega and played games.  I've worked with Open Office.  I've messed with web development tools.  I will tell you this:

I will never, ever, ever go back to Microsoft. I'll keep a partition so that I can run something if I can't find any other way.  However, I think that with Linux I can do it all.  I had to push through so much crap, but I am SO VERY glad I did!

To all of you new users - don't give up!  Keep trying!  Keep LEARNING!  I feel so happy right now I could probably jump around!

Many of you helped me and you don't even know it.  Thanks to everyone who posts recommendation, tips, and scripts!

### Post by ANTDx1 on 2007-03-07
This reminds me of my switch to Ubuntu, which took me 6 months before I had everything working.  I ended up breaking everything and starting over.  It took only two days after the clean install...very good learning experience.  The learning curve can be daunting if the GUIs don't work exactly right for your system, but it's manageable, as I have experienced.  For those who are curious, my hardware is an HP dv6000 with an AMD 64 Athlon 3200+, an ati x200m graphics card, and a Broadcom 4318 wireless card.  Just to give an idea of what all this hardware is like, the 64-bit version of Ubuntu is lacking in many drivers and browser plugins.  A few programs were installed in a 32-bit environment.  The Broadcom 4318 is famous for not working with the normal Broadcom drivers, as a forum search on that card will show.  The x200m is also famous for not working with the open-source drivers, forcing me to use fglrx for Beryl (note: once you have the wobby windows, you can't go back).  I'll probably have to fix everything for the upgrade to 7.04, but oh well.  Ubuntu will remain my primary OS, because it's more fun to use, and way quicker and easier on the hardware (most of the time) than Windows.
