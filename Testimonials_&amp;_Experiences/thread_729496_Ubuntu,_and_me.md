### Post by WFGeppetto on 2008-03-19
I created one post about my Ubuntu experience, and it was a bit negative.  I created another thread in Absolute Beginner's Talk, and it got moved to this forum as well.  It was also negative, and mostly about Envy.

I am now running my 5th installation of Ubuntu, and I have learned quite a bit.  I feel that my other two threads were a bit overly opinionated, and unfairly negative, so I would like to re-illustrate my experience.  I understand much more now.

Here is what I've learned:

If you don't do it right the first time, it's probably going to suck to fix it.  

If you do it right the first time, it stays right, unless you screw with it (if it ain't broke...)

No matter how much you want to believe that something is wrong with the software, if it isn't working, either you screwed up, or you are using the wrong software for the application/distro/hardware etc.

Yes, one missed letter, much less line of code, or sentence of explanation can make **all** the difference in a proper function.

I was once of the mind that Open Source just isn't ready for the general population.  Of course, that cannot be true of all apps, as some are the best there are, including proprietary software.  Ubuntu isn't as user friendly as some would like you to think, but if you stick with it, and become familiar, it's one of the best OSs I know of.  I am incredibly more learned in computer processes by learning how to deal with this system, and I am astoundingly more effective, and efficient in windows because of it.

I think of Open Source more as a philosophy, than a kind of software now.  People here (99% of the time) try to help, and want to teach.  I think that it is more about learning, than making a program usable for anyone.  If more people learned how to utilize all the aspects of Open Source software, not only would the software improve at a greater rate, but proprietary companies would sooner be forced to lower their exorbitant prices.  I'm not trying to give programmers the cold shoulder, just their bosses.

This is a fantastic operating system, and I think one of the main problem is that most people can't leave the original installation alone.  Ubuntu (especially the 32bit Gutsy) will do anything, and everything that the more popular OSs do, and better, without as much fuss.  Unfortunately, most of us want the cool bells and whistles, as well.  That is where most of the problems arise, I think.

In anycase, I thought it necessary to make this post, and amend my older opinions.  Ubuntu is great, and if you think it's too easy to mess up, then stick with it, and not only will you get the OS that you  want, but you will also be more effective in others.

### Post by DMK62 on 2008-03-19
Congratulations on sticking it out during the frustrating moments. Even very experienced Linux users have those moments ( some are more vocal than others about it ). I know all too well the urge to hard boot the computer out the window.

Although it can be frustrating when things break or do not work it's an excellent time to learn why it happened. I keep notes on fixes in a text file and have hard copies of those.

As you learn more about Ubuntu / Linux you will start to see that there are many ways to accomplish a task. Some methods are definitely easier than others but with time and effort more things will become second nature.

With other os's such as Windows you are only given access to a small portion of the os ( well unless you love spending endless hours hacking your registry ). Linux offers you access to basically the entire system and for new users that can be dangerous but Ubuntu does protect you a bit by the use of sudo and not being logged in as root.

Compiz and other effects have been shown on youtube and I think that has attracted a lot of new users. They want the effects to work right away and end up being surprised when they encounter problems. I wouldn't say it's the fault of the user as they are more than likely not familiar with the fundamentals of linux including X and editing xorg.conf. Your knowledge of hardware will definitely increase with Linux. 

I hope that you will continue to learn and enjoy Linux.

### Post by freebeer on 2008-03-20
Nice post.  Like anything else, there are "tricks of the trade" and once you get a few under your belt (and learn where to find the other tricks ;)) things become so much smoother.

A simple trick: when installing Ubuntu, create a separate /home partition.  If you ever need to re-install or upgrade the system, you can do so without fear of borking your data.  (But you should still make backups)

Knowing this, then you're free to tinker all you want.  A re-install is just 30 minutes away (not including updates).

I had to help someone install a VPN client on a Windows machine today.  It easily took longer than 30 minutes to complete.  Why?  Well, the VPN client wanted .Net 1.1.  So we had to download and install .Net 1.1.  reboot.  download & Install .Net 1.1 SP1.  reboot.  Head over to Windows Update and apply the hotfix.  Reboot.  (Note that I've done this .Net install many a time and was intimately familiar with exactly what had to be done and knew where to go to get these files.)  It still took longer than a re-install of Ubuntu would.
