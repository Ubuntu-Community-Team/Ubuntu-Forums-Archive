### Post by zheepeez on 2007-06-13
sub-titled: A List Of Annoying Things About Ubuntu Which, If Fixed, Would Lead The Author Of This Post To Love It Like His Own Child.

A long title.

I like Ubuntu. Hell, I love Ubuntu. It's fast, which is important on a 1.2GHz laptop, reliable, which is important for a kid who likes to take things apart, and it's safe, which is important for someone who spends 12 to 14 hours a day on the Internet. It's closer to perfect than XP (never used Vista), but, of course, not yet. So here's my version of a post called 5 Reasons To Stick With Windows, re-sub-titled 5 Things To Raise Your Blood Pressure In Ubuntu:

**Internet** can still be a pain. I'd be interested to see a list of the market share of different wireless cards showing how many of the world's computers could immediately work on Linux. My bet is it's not big. I have the misfortune to have a Broadcom driver, which caused me headaches (it actually stopped my first attempt at Ubuntu, back with Dapper Drake, which I gave up on) but was eventually fixed, and has not broken since.

**GUI**'s are still ugly. Just look at Leopard, revealed a couple of days ago. Compare that to the default Gnome screen (I've never used KDE, but from what I see it's not much better). There's a solution to this, too. I saw a post in a blog about why Mac releases don't include popular software like Quicksilver. The answer, of course, is that they're closed source and proprietary. Ubuntu doesn't have this problem. Why aren't some of the great graphical tools out there pre-loaded into Ubuntu? Why do newbie users like myself have to hunt them out seperately? And by the way, folks, Compiz just doesn't float my boat. I've heard people go on and on and on and on about the wobbly windows - it's just not that spectacular. IMHO, of course. I've used Enlightenment 17, which was promising in terms of usability but didn't play nice with my GTK programs (I'm planning on having another shot at it when it moves out of beta).

**Media** isn't that flash. Most computer users use their computers for their media, but I've not found anything to rival most Windows media centers. VLC is good for playing single files, as is XMMS and MPLayer, but the jukeboxes and browsers - AmaroK, Banshee, Quod Libet, etc etc, just don't cut it. My biggest gripe with these is the old customisability problem. It seems that as programs get more popular (and thus, stable) they become less customisable. Call that Ross' Law :D. My ideal of a media player is foobar2000 or J River Media Center - customisable to the point of insanity, fleshed out with extra codecs and add-ons. In Linux I've given up and am looking at buying a completely separate computer to use along with Elisa Media Center over a shared hard drive. There's just nothing in terms of a small program that does the job.

That being said, I'm very needy in terms of media - I've got 45 GB of music, all in .flac format, and all organised, tagged, and album arted. I guess most mainstream users need less in terms of organisation.

**Themes** are good but, IMHO, could be improved. I spent about an hour last night following a guide on the Net about how to customise Ubuntu to looks like Mac OS X, but it seems to me that themes could include things like a replacement for the top panel background and applications like CairoDock in and of themselves rather than relying on long and intricate guides (though, again, infinitely better than Windows). Not to mention more application themes.

**Office Programs** suddenly aren't important, having graduated high school, but I have to say that despite the amount of raves it receives, OpenOffice is nowhere near a substitute for Office. Actually, I've always been a bit confused about OpenOffice. As far as I can see, its goal is to be as perfect as possible a clone of Office. Why? What if Ubuntu was just a clone of Windows? I would love to see OpenOffice implementing completely new features that set it apart from Office, rather than just playing catch up. I'm sure the OSS community can think up loads of innovations in terms of office productivity to use in the OpenOffice suite, although I must admit I haven't used it much.

Small aside: To all those who say "the GIMP just isn't as good as Photoshop", I say it is. I've been using Photoshop for about 5 years, and I made the jump to Linux just before designing my school's entire school yearbook, and I can safely say the GIMP made it easier than Photoshop ever could have. Even if I were on Windows, I'd prefer the GIMP.

I guess that's all. Quite a rant, I think - one more perspective on the few flaws of what is one great operating system

### Post by MattDTownsend on 2007-06-13
Some good points, here.  I left Windows so long ago that I couldn't list one reason to keep Windows, but some of these are very valid criticisms.

Here are a few thoughts.

Re: Wireless support, it's been my experience that wireless technology, still fairly young, doesn't work all that well in Windows, either.  I've had throbbing headaches trying to help others get wireless networking set up in Windows, even if the hardware is working properly.  And it isn't always.  So, I would say that if your hardware works, you're much better off in linux.  Network-manager and wifi-radar are far better than native Windows wireless tools (that I've seen), and the network-admin approach to wireless is rock solid.  That is, when I enter the proper information into network-admin for a dedicated, home wireless network, it never stops working for some inexplicable reason.  I don't want to say this of M$.  Yeah, hardware support is crap, but  ndiswrapper has helped Linux a lot in hardware.  However, it, too, is spotty.

Re: GUIs, to each his own.  I think OSX is hideous.  I can appreciate the implication that Gnome and KDE are too simplistic.  However, I rather like that Gnome runs fine in this laptop I'm on, which is four years old.  The system might as well be new -- it runs better than XP did when I first purchased it.  I agree that compiz and beryl and unimpressive.  I miss the days of Enlightenment, and look forward to getting a stable DR17 in a two-for-one pack with Duke Nukem Forever in the year 2085.

Re: Music, I have far fewer files, but I just setup mpd with Sonata last night and I'm impressed.  Very impressed.  Sonata isn't what you want, but perhaps there is an mpd frontend that would suit you.  I like the idea using an intelligent daemon to dish out music. . .the XMMS model has gotten really old.

FInally, Re: Office, OpenOffice has its problems.  It also doesn't cost US$500, or whatever the hell Redmond is currently charging.  I'm willing to forgive a lot because of this.  I think it's so much like MSOffice because they've been working very hard just to get it production-ready, and to make it easy for people wanting to switch.  I think OO and StarOffice forked at Star5, at a time when StarOffice and OO were both junk.  OO2 shows more maturation, and it has been around for less than two years.  

Are you aware that the OO team is implementing an extension system, like in Firefox?  I think once OO is more modular, you'll be able to make it stand on its head.  Firefox isn't terribly different from IE until you fire up those extensions.  Well, except that it, you know, works properly.
