### Post by Atro on 2008-11-18
Hello everyone
I wanted to give my view about ubuntu 8.10 which I tried lately.
I installed it on 3 compuers. Mine, my dad and a test install on one of the companies I work for part time. They asked me to find a cheaper alternative for their day to day used software, because of their limited funding ( a charity human rights organization)

I first tried it on my computer.The installation process was straight forward. I was a little bit confused about the partitioning but managed to do it right. Everything was smooth and fast. I knew what to expect from it and it did a better job than what I was expecting . I am still amazed how many programs are available for download and how people managed to build a fully functioning operating system and then offer it for free. 
I am still bothered that I can not use my favorite Adobe programs on linux.I know about wine, but because of my job I have to have a reliable system, and a Mac is doing the job for me currently. That is not Ubuntu's fault, Adobe have to be  making some of their programs available on linux. and if it wasnt for Adobe only, I could easily switch to linux permanently.

Then I installed it on my Dad's computer. A genuine computer newbie. He only knows how to browse internet and take care of his ebay business. He liked it very much in the begining, he was shocked that he didn't have to pay for openOffice. and he found it much easier to work than Microsoft Office. He has the same firefox browser as he had on windows.
I checked back on him a week later and found out that he was a bit frustrated with ubuntu. His reasons mainly were:

-I can not install MSN and Yahoo messenger. I dont like this Bird. I want to have the same messenger than I had in windows
-I tried to change the look of the system. Went to this "Art Manager" thingy. I chose my theme, I pressed intall and nothing happened.
-My Nokia CD doesnt work on this
-it asks for password eveytime I want to do something with system, I put it once, why should I repeat it everytime
-The printer wont print sometimes

I know most of the things he mentioned can be fixed by a linux Guru, but for him, it was just to much to get used to the new environment and find a solution for this small problems. Small things make the difference, He asked me to buy a XP home edition a week later.

In the company tho, everything was going smooth. They were a bit confused about usual functions in linux but after a short training they became comfortable with it. Now they have 5 computers running Ubuntu and openOffice saving them a lot of money.

I am very optimistic about the future. I hope software companies start to make the swtich too. We are waiting for them and they are waiting for us. I dont know where this loop will be broken.

Special thanks again to everyone involved in Ubuntu and open source. You are all doing a great job

### Post by fluxlizard on 2008-11-18
My folks are in their mid-70s, never used computers before their mid-60s and have been using ubuntu for a few years now. They use yahoo mail, open office, printing photos from their digital camera, and internet radio. The only problem they've had is the occasional printer overload because my mother used to not be able to resist hitting the print key over and over if the printing wasn't instant and she was impatient. LOL

Its been a very nice experience for me because I don't get constant phone calls for help- can't say the same for the years they used windows xp.

It sounds like your father's frustration is that it isn't windows (as in he can't use his nokia cd or his windows messengers). As a mac user you should understand because mac isn't windows either.

The concept of user versus administrator seems to be a little bleary and how it protects the system as well(the passwords).

A few suggestions that might help make things more comfortable-

Try a variety of messengers and see if you can find one he likes. There are a few popular ones on linux that can all handle his msn and yahoo accounts.

Set his user account to autologin when you first turn the system on. That will skip that first password that he has to mess with every time he turns the system on. Then he will only need to password when installing new software.

Explaining how the passwords protect his system from viruses and keep it running smoothly and quickly because it doesn't require macafee or norton or whatever running in the background all the time may also help him see it in a new light. 

One other suggestion- search the forums here and the net for answers to any problems you don't know the solution for, and don't hesitate to ask for help. I'm not a linux guru either- but I get on just fine by searching for the info I need.
