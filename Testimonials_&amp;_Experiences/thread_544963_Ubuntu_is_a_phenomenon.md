### Post by modyl on 2007-09-07
I am not an experienced computer technician of any sort. I was introduced to Linux and then Ubuntu by trying to rehab a used PC I'd been given. It was running Windows ME and had no installation/backup disks, etc. so I knew it would be just a matter of time before I needed to install a new OS. I had started to wipe the hard drive and installed an old distribution of SuSe that I got in a magazine several years ago and had never used. I later installed a version of Red Hat, thanks to two humongous Linux books with CDR disks at my public library. Then I found Ubuntu. 

I installed Edgy Eft and was amazed that I was able to get online and begin to update this old PC which had a passable CPU and a fair-sized hard drive for being 8 or 9 years old. I quickly found that the ethernet link to my high speed modem was very dependable and later I networked a router to the other computers in the house. I'd never done anything like that before. One running XP, one a Mac and this Ubuntu Linux PC box. With this PC none of the interfaces with a new mouse, modem, router or different digital cameras have required anything beyond plugging in and clicking a mouse here and there. I have, of course upgraded to Feisty, have installed Ubuntu Studio, then gone back to Feisty 7.04 and reinstalled the entire system again without some of the audio apps after deciding not to dual boot, and to straighten out headaches I have caused myself. The only limitation I can see with Ubuntu to some degree would be in the midi and music software realm, though some of the audio, synth and sequencing apps available with Ubuntu work very well. I haven't tried to expect Ubuntu to be Windows and the biggest limitations I find are those of my box's upgradeable hardware. I certainly haven't learned to start writing scripts or drivers yet, but have become relaxed with editing some scripts and using the terminal, checking updates and installations with simple commands. (I practically never used DOS commands on a Windows machine). Synaptic Package Manager seems to perform better every time I use it. I have come to a new understanding of the entire software/hardware universe thanks to reading and listening to  the observations of Richard Stallman as well as several others. I would like to thank Mr. Shuttleworth and the entire Ubuntu team for their efforts at 'democratizing' the grassroots pathways to the 21st century.
Thanks!
