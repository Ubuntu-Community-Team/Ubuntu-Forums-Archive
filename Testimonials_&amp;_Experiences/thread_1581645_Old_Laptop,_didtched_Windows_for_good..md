### Post by Super Nade on 2010-09-25
Folks,

I'm running 10.04 on my old Inspiron 600m. I've ditched Windows for good. I had a go with a few other distros before settling on Ubuntu. Why did I choose Ubuntu?

1. Painless installation. All hardware recognized.
2. Insanely fast bootup and shutdown! Wow, just wow! I am already impressed!
3. Very good for Linux noobs who just want things to work. It is stable, no random crashes etc.
4. Tons of software available. My main use is for typing and a bit of PCB design. Kile and EagleCAD are available from the repositories.

Some suggested improvements:
1. Is it possible to make Adobe-Flashplayer better? It really really sucks. Crashes, CPU overheating...garbage.
2. Give those who graduate from newbie school a bit more freedom to customize things from source. Documentation in this area is a bit sparse.

Overall, I'm not switching to anything else in the foreseeable future. Great job Ubuntu Team!

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2010-09-27
> Super Nade
2. Give those who graduate from newbie school a bit more freedom to  customize things from source. Documentation in this area is a bit  sparse.

First, I'd like to thank you for sharing your feedback/experiences.

I completely agree with the quote above. I think it might help if there  was more emphasis placed on the "system/help & support". Much of the  customization is identified in the section under "Customizing Your  Computer". One thought may be a specific link in the main menu to this  section of the help & support menu.

Even that has some limitations. Certain changes must be done in the  gnome config editor or elsewhere. For example, To "completely" turn off  one's laptop touchpad in 10.04. Many customizations consist of multiple  tasks and often detailed explaination of these tasks is lacking.

However, when put in perspective - customization is a personal choice  and thus it's almost impossible to document customization without  writing a book. With each and every new release, configuration of the  editor (including apps) and other customization activities would require  dissemination. Not likely with a new OS version coming out every six  months.

Granted, certain changes in the config editor and customization could be  better documented. But overall the task of keeping pace with new  releases/updates would be one of an almost impossible mission. Just  about the time one version was completed and all the documentation  errors were eliminated, a new version would be released.

It's been my experience, that extended customization requires some  knowledge of the OS and rightfully so. Imagine a new user attempting  extended customization without any fundamental knowledge of the OS. It  could quickly progress into a nightmare. Especially, if the new user  didn't document the changes they made and relied solely on their memory.  They may even receive assistance in this forums help sections  (consisting of a copy/paste command) and have no idea what they did.

I agree that documentation is a critical part of Ubuntu. But, we must  also remember that users vary in experience and for many, customizing  their system may simple mean changing background or panel color. Not all  users are interested in advanced customization. Providing detailed  descriptions of customization might actually be detrimental, rather that  an improvement. A wise man once told me to be careful what I ask for -  as I might get it. :smile:

If customization documentation is to advanced, some users may find it to  complex and complain. While other users might find the same  documentation lacking. Perhaps, the solution may lie in user education  and the learning curve. Like any OS, the more one understands the OS the  better they become at customizing it.

A mentoring program might be a  viable option. It's been my experience that the more I invest in helping  new users (that have asked for my assistance in installing Ubuntu), the  more likely they are to be comfortable making changes to the OS.  Knowing, assistance is only a phone call or e-mail away. This may even  apply to the forums. A forums mentoring program might truly entice new  users. By providing them with an opportunity to accelerated the learning  curve and advance their knowledge of the OS.

Just my $0.02
