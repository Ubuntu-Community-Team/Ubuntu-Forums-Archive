### Post by NurdCore on 2011-03-06
p { margin-bottom: 0.08in; }  I have always used a Microsoft OS since Windows 95. I just never really gave a Linux OS a shot until around the year 2000 when a buddy of mine showed me an early version of RedHat Linux. I was intrigued by the OS, but it was way to much of a learning curve at the time for me to really mess with it. This was back when the hardest thing to configure was Internet because of those darn Win Modems and I did not want to hunt down hard to find and expensive hardware for an OS I just plan to play with. I was one of those computer users that thought “I would love this OS, if they made it as easy to use as Windows.”  
 

 The years passed as I began to further my IT career focusing on Microsoft by obtaining my MSCE certification and the Linux OS further became a distant memory for me. I really don't have nothing against Windows (except for ME and Vista), and regular daily administration was pretty pain free. Then one thing made all that change, Viruses. Until the makers of Anti-Virus software caught up to speed, Windows viruses were the biggest pain for a Network Administrator. Even when the viruses were being stopped by the software, the cost of the software itself really increased the cost of operation for the company I worked for. I have been dealing with this headaches for the past 10 years.
 

 Now it's 2011 and I'm helping startup a family business and I was put in charge of IT and finances. I decided to take a look at Linux again because one thing I did remember about Linux was that it was a free OS, stable, and have few viruses targeted toward it. I decided to try RedHat again and was disappointed to find out they now charge an expensive yearly subscription for it. So, I looked around some more.
 

 I bought a new laptop last month from Dell (Vostro v130). The laptop came with Ubuntu 10.04 LTS. Right off the bat, the cost of operation is already cheaper because this laptop was 250 bucks cheaper then the Windows 7 version. I didn't have to worry about buying an Office Suite because of Open Office, I didn't have to pay for anti-virus because of clamav, and no need to buy photoshop because of gimp. Now I know that you can get most of the free software for windows also, but Ubuntu still wins because I was able to get it running comfortably on some pretty outdated laptops for Windows requirements. Thanks to this, I do not have to buy a bunch of new machines bringing down startup cost for the business. The OS installs virtually on anything and it just works with good plug and play support now and it is easy to use without much need for the command line like 10 years ago.
 

 I still use Windows, for Gaming only. But when it's time to get down to business, I pull out my Ubuntu Box.

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-03-09
> **arunb said:**
> please explain what you mean by this ??

Linux AV software look for Windows viruses and Linux is not affected by Windows viruses anyway. If you download a file with a windows virus, then that virus will not affect your Linux system. However, if you then send this file to someone using windows, they can get infected. The purpose of Linux AV is to have it protect windows systems that you may interact with. The Linux machine itself is just as secure with AV as it is without one.
