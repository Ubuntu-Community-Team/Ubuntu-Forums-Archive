### Post by SZF2001 on 2007-01-30
Alright, so it's been two years since I've started using Ubuntu. Here comes the story!

My school is dirt poor (old town I used to live in since then, btw), and the CIA donates these old computers (even though we had plenty for some reason). I ask the tech guy if I can buy one - so I grab one for $20, and the journey had begun!

I'm still using the same computer. It's an old Compaq Deskpro - 128 MB Ram, Pentium II processor, ya, not the best computer. But hell, I might even end up taking it to college. It's a working box.

Anyway, the same day I buy it, the tech guy tells me, "One thing - your going to have to install your own Operating System on it. I can't throw out any Windows freebies here, your on your own."

Operating System? What was this guy talking about? I had no idea, but hey, it's $20 for a computer, who cares?

With my box sitting in my room, I go out to the garage and find an old monitor that we didn't use. I told my friend about getting a new computer, so he let me have this old wireless mouse and keyboard combo - now the keyboard is broke, but the mouse is alright, so I'm still using the mouse. It's kinda neat to have this little mouse in front of me, no wires, I can move the pad wherever my hand wants to lay on the desktop, etc.

I turn on the computer - and it tells me it couldn't find any OS or boot file or anything. I really had no idea what was going on, but I was determined - damnit, I was going to make the best $20 I could out of this!

What prompted me to buy a computer in the first place was that I bought this LaCie DVD burner, with the option of USB or FireWire. I was using a Windows 98 computer in my basement that my dad had brought home because no one at the school was using it, so for a while that machine was my primary machine. Except the player didn't work - the way I saw this whole thing was that if I had a computer for the DVD player in my room, along with being able to write documents for school and stuff, then I'd be set.

One of my teachers was willing to let me use his Windows XP OS discs to install on the computer if I let him borrow the DVD burner - I did, we exchanged, he got what he needed done, but I couldn't - I tried using the registration key for the OS, and MS contacted his house, letting him know his copy of Windows was cracked, or something. He didn't get to mad at me - in fact, he somewhat saw it coming and warned me. But I had no idea. I got my burner back and now didn't know what to do.

What does someone do when they are in a rut? What do you do when you have questions that no one around you seems to have the answers for?

Google, of course!

So I'm searching around Google, and - well, I'll spare all the little details. Eventually I found the Ubuntu website. I was facinated - I could have a computer that wasn't Microsoft? The thought of a Mac didn't even enter my mind. That's how computer-sheltered some people are, sadly, and I used to be one of them.

I download the Breezy .iso, burn it, and take it to my computer in my room. I had no idea what I was doing, so thank God the setup basically held my hand in the whole process.

When everything was done, there it was - the Breezy desktop! At first I was confused - why are there two bars instead of one, like in Windows? Why aren't there icons, like in Windows? I just download the programs...? That's ILLEGAL!

But I quickly found answers to my questions in the forums - found out about the repositories, the command line (if you look at some of my first topics I'm a complete retard), all the fun little tidbits.

Like I said before, this computer isn't exactly the best - it came with USB 1 instead of 2, so my DVD burner wasn't exactly performing like it should. But I was content with the idea that I could at least copy in CDs and keep my own music library in this computer! (Only about a month ago I found out you could buy a FireWire card, how silly of me - now I use my computer for watching new DVDs, it's just more convienent)

After a while, I got wireless figured out, and Dapper came out. Which messed up my wireless configuration, which frusterated the crap out of me. At that point, I decided to try SuSE and other Linux distro's - and through it all, I've always found my way back to Ubuntu.

Now I've had Xubuntu up for about two months now - I've switched back from Dapper to Breezy to Dapper, just to try out which I prefer (Dapper won) - and now, I can say that I am a Linux convert - better yet, a Ubuntu convert.

I was, and still am, average Joe - I'm just one of the few average Joe's that found out and figured out Ubuntu. All it takes it a little patience and time - just like with every OS, like I did back in 97 figuring out my dad's brand spankin' new Windows 95 computer, and I was only a little guy.

Ubuntu does exactly what I need it to do - store .mp3's from my CDs through goobox, keep an updated music library and a player with Rhythmbox, be able to transfer music to my Sansa player, watch the occasional movie via DVD player, write documents for school (and hopefully be able to print when I find a Linux working printer), surf the web... Yup.

A few days ago, my brother needed help on our other computer - it's Windows, so I thought I could handle it no sweat... Well, apparently not, really. I had almost forgotten where a majority of stuff was, and soon asked myself "Why doesn't it just **work**?" I'm not sure if that kind of mentality is good when you get used to Linux, but maybe it's the right step...

Thanks for everything, Ubuntu. You've made this average Joe happy.
