### Post by snakeman21 on 2009-10-07
I just wanted to share how Ubuntu has amazed me yet again.  I used to have an Acer Aspire One netbook.  It was a good little machine, but I longed for a full-sized notebook.  So I wasn't *too* disappointed when my cat knocked it over and broke it.  I had an excuse to buy a new lappy.  So I found an ad on Craigslist from someone who was selling an HP dv6000 Entertainment PC.  He was selling it for only $200 USD.  Too good to be true?  I called him up and he told me that he had a newer computer, and he was selling the HP because Vista crashed and he didn't bother to make recovery disks when he bought it.  So we arranged to meet the next day.   Honestly, I was expecting the computer to be at least a little bit beat-up, but it looked brand new!  There was a slight crack in the plastic "slats" that make up the vent for the fan, but that was minor.  He told me he was surprised that someone wanted to buy it knowing that Vista didn't work, so I told him that I was just going to put Ubuntu on it anyways, so that made no difference to me.  He said "Okay" and showed me what the computer came with:  A charger, two remotes, two batteries, and a really nice laptop case.  He turned on the computer to show me that is worked, and then turned it off and put it in the bag.  I gave him the money, we shook hands, and went our separate ways.  

Anyways, I'm getting WAY off-track.

The HP dv6000 has "touch buttons" above the keyboard for playing media.  They are supposed to be tied in with Vista to control the playback of music and videos.  I assumed that I would have to do some hacking to make them work, if they would work at all.  But I was wrong.  I installed 64-bit Ubuntu via a USB drive and rebooted.  It was two days before I even thought about playing with the media buttons.  Lo and behold, the QuickPlay button automatically opens Rhythmbox!  The "Play/Pause", "Previous", "Next", and "Stop" buttons all do their respective functions.  

So then I had an idea.  I set up Rhythmbox to run at startup in the system tray.  Since Rhythmbox remember all of your settings, I put it on "shuffle" and rebooted to test my theory.  Success!  Now, every time I start my computer, Rhythmbox quietly starts in the system tray.  At any time, if I decide I want to hear music, all I have to do is touch Play, and it happily plays a song at random.  I control it all from the touch buttons, and never have to even see the Rhythmbox interface.  

Ubuntu is f@#king great!
