### Post by Master Shake on 2005-12-18
My first computerwas a Coleco ADAM.  I ran the CP/M OS on that.

'Nuff said.

My next computerwas an Atari 520STfm.  Used that for the better part of 12 years.  Served me well, including my first taste of online, VIA Delphi, GEnie, and Compu$erve

THen I entered the world of the PC.  Firstwas a Packard Beelzebub, second one was home built.  ON this home built PC, I had win 98, and I decided to try Linux out to see what the fuss was.  I installed a version of Mandrake (Don'tremember which).  It wasn't too bad, but I could see no reason to keep it, as it did not detect my USB DSL modem, and thee wasn't much interesting about it. I tried with the next version of Mandrake, with the same results.

My next (and current) comp was (is) a Dell Optiplex with Windon'ts XP  It's not too bad.  I haven't really experienced too many problems, but I started tiring of being tied to MS all the time, as my main job (Tech Support) requires me to fix MS problems day in and day out.  Bleh.

THen a friend at another forum suggest trying Ubuntu.  I ordered the CD, and promptly forgot I ordered it.  Two months later, the CDs (got the ten pack) arrived in the mail.  I tried the live CD.  Not too bad.  I then tried installing the 'install' CD.  Well, the partitioner baffled me, and I ended up wiping out my XP install by mistake.  I finished installing ubuntu 5.04.   First thing I noticed was that it automatically detected my network card, and I was instantly able to get on the web (I'm currently on a cable modem).  I finished setting up the MP3 and DVD codecs, and finished tweaking my system.  Total time installing, three hours.  Includes the tweaking.

NOw I had to reinstall XP, ax the wife loves XP.  I broke out my restore CD's.  The install went fine, but I noticed XP did not detect

-- Network card
-- onboard INtel video
-- sound card
-- 56 K modem

I had to go online for these drivers, as they were not on any CD I had.  BUt since it didn't detect my NIC or modem, how could I do that?  I had to run across town, and use a friends dial up connectyion and burn the 12 MB of drivers to a CD.  Total time on this step alone:  90 minutes  I got ack home, and it took me 30 minutes to install these.  Then I noticed I couldn't play CD's.  Couldn't figure why.  I went on line to find the solution.  The driverswere on the power DVD CD.  I had thought that was just a viewing program.  I had no idea that the codecs were on there too.  Total time on this step: 1 hour.  I THEN was finally able to run XP.

It took me 3 hour total to get Ubuntu running.  Three and a half to get XP up and running to spec.

Ayway, I then decided to explore the repositories.  I've found way cool software such as

-- Inkscape (I'm creating a comic strip using this)
-- AbiWord (I actually like this better than Open Office's word processor)
-- Blender (I actually have used this in Win, but the Linux version seems lots better)
-- Gaim (I like the interface better than Trillian
-- Galleon (I think this is a decent web browser.)


Games?  I own Neverwinter NIghts (Don'treally like the game, but it was cheap), and I decided to try installing it.  It actually went pretty well, and ran decently on my machine.

As far as other games go...

-- Planet Penguin Racer
-- Super Tux (Waiting for them to finish this awesome game!)
-- Abuse (This is one addicting platformer)
-- Trackballs (Clever Marble Madness clone)
-- Frozen Bubble (Need I say more?)

And it's not a game, but the kids LOVE Tux Paint!  (Limited, but I kinda dig it myself. :) )

Right now, I'm experimenting with different desktops.  I like Gnome, but I'm experimenting with Kubuntu and Xubuntu.  I like the variety of desktops that opn source OS linux alllows.  NIce to not be tied down.

I have to admit that currently, I can't go totally Ubuntu.  For one thing, I'm playing Half-Life 2 on XP (Even though my computer has that horrid sound stuttering problem that Valve refuses to fix), and I find MAME On XP to be easier to use (although I haven't really explored it on Ubuntu drastically yet)  plus, there's the Wife factor.

But the things that strike me about Ubuntu...

I was able to bring up a login screen which is similar to XP Home's, thus making it easier for my kids (age 6, 4, and 3) to og in.

My kids know how to load (and play) Planet Penguin Racer and Tux Paint all by themselves without any parental help.

I'm totally satisfied with Ubuntu, and I will keep it around.  I cannot say the same for XP, however.  Its days are numbered.
