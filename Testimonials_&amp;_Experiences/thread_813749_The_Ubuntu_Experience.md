### Post by wateenellende on 2008-05-31
It was 1997 when I switched, ditching Windows95 for Slackware. Not much after, I switched to Debian - version 1.2 or so. Ever since, I've stuck to software supplied in .deb format.

Debian has been fairly nice, and attempts to install RedHat, Suse or FreeBSD were, in comparison, just painful experiences. But Debian had a downside: you could always choose between the 3-year old "stable", or the more recent "unstable" branch - guess why they call that unstable. It worked, usually, but the look and feel were definitely sloppy.

Then, the gods sent us Ubuntu, which came to me in the form of Dapper Drake. And I loved it. All the good of Debian (apt-get!), but recent but stable software, and nicely polished. In short, it solved every issue I ever had with Debian. Next came Edgy: Brilliant! Feisty: Fantastic! Life was good.

And then, "Gutsy" happened. Having updated through at least 5 Debian version and 3 Ubuntu versions without a single issue, I was unprepared for what was about to happen.
Of 5 systems I maintain, 4 were left unable to boot after the upgrade. One didn't recover at all, but "machine not bootable" was considered "not a serious regression" by Ubuntu's kernel team. Thanks guys. Really. WHAT THE HELL WERE YOU THINKING ?!?!?

Now, we have Hardy, a so-called LTS (read: very stable. really.) release. The unbootable machine boots again, and I only had to wait half a year for it! In turn, one other machine has started locking up see: ([http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=768200](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=768200)).
Worse: the number of updates I install every day rivals that of Debian unstable!

Why is having many updates worse that random freezes ? Let us  do some math. Once something is unstable, people report bugs, and things get fixed. At first, many issue are found, then errors happen fewer and fewer. The software converges towards stability. AFAIK the curve is exponentially distributed, but please don't pin me down on the details. Point is: bugs will not suddenly stop appearing. It's a process of decay, like radio-activity. So, such a large rate of updates spell one thing, and that is "U N S T A B L E". Hardy is not ready. Having random freezes is only one problem, here we have mathematic founded reason to believe there are many more. There always are, of course, but what I mean is: many more than there should be in a stable release.

Now, this 6-month release cycle is a good thing, so that's not the problem, although a delay of a month would have been wise.
What worries me is that the recent releases are simply not ready, i.e. Ubuntu is evolving faster than it can. Management of software development is the art of retaining control of an evolutionary process (Beekhof's Law). If the process is spinning out of control, as we see here, either increase your productivity or slow down the evolution, i.e. have less changes from release to release.

Please Ubuntu, make me believe again.
