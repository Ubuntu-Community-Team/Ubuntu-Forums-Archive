### Post by t0p on 2010-12-01
For over a year now I've been running Eeebuntu 3 (based on 9.04) on my 701, and the experience has been very trying at times.  But Eeebuntu was customized especially to run with the EeePC, it came with a custom  kernel so everything allegedly worked "out the box" (despite the annoying hissing that came from the speakers...) - so I figured what the heck?  Probably the least bad solution and all that, y'know?

Well, actually: no.  A few months ago I downloaded the .iso for Lubuntu 10.04; various things stopped me from trying it out until today; and I gotta say, I wish I'd tried it earlier!

I'm running it from a live usb, and still the speed and responsiveness leave Eeebuntu gasping in its wake.  All the hardware seems to be running fine (apart from shooting video with the webcam - but I've never managed to shoot decent video with the cam anyway).  I'm writing this from Lubuntu right now, and I'm also downloading the .iso for 10.10 which I intend to actually install on my lil netbook.

One problem I haven't figured out a fix for yet: when I boot into Lubuntu, the wireless card is disabled by default.  I have to hit F2 or something when the EeePC splash screen appears and manually enable the wifi.  Anyone know how I can fix it so wireless is enabled on boot?  I know it's not a drastic problem, but it is nevertheless a hassle that I could do without.

Something else that I find interesting.  On my desktop machine (running Ubuntu 10.04) I swear by Firefox.  There are so many add-ons I use, and I love the interface.  Same when running Eeebuntu on the EeePC.  But I'm really getting into Chromium on Lubuntu - I dunno why, maybe it's just because I love trying out all the "new" stuff that Lubuntu has thrown my way.  But this raises a couple of questions: what is the state of the add-on scene for Chromium?  And will I be able to import into Chromium all the bookmarks that I've got on Firefox (they're saved as html I believe).

Anyway, I strongly recommend that anyone with old or puny hardware give Lubuntu a whirl.  It comes as a live .iso, so what have you got to lose?  I really hope that the Ubuntu/Canonical folks decide to "adopt" Lubuntu as an official Ubuntu variant.  It's a sweet, sweet lil thing.
