### Post by AICollector on 2009-05-28
I had grown up using MS-DOS sitting on my Mother's lap whilst she showed me the commands, we then upgraded to Windows 95, then 98, then ME, and finally, XP as they become available.  Part of Generation Y,  computers played a huge role in my upbringing. I didn't have an imaginary friend as a kid, I had a computer who had the magical ability to talk to me and guide me. (No, I never did blame it for anything, other then my boredom when it broke down). Silly, I know, but I was a kid and the borders between the real world and my imagination were very, VERY thin.

I became net-aware, aware that there were some programs that said they could do exactly what I wanted, but they damaged the computer. Aware that there were others who may seek to take advantage of a small kid on the net. I never did join any of the greifers then, even though I was probably a prime candidate. I knew my computer would get slower and slower every time it was used...and I knew each time I looked for a program to fix it, I would have to sift through about 200 useless/damaging programs. OpenOffice? Yep! Pidgin? Yep! Free software, sometimes not Open Source, was proving to be better then propriety counterparts.

In the summer of 2006, I encountered my first piece of FOSS software; Firefox. I liked it a lot, once I got used to tabbed browsing and addons. Sometime later, I found VLC Media Player, which allowed me to actually use most of my media without having five different media players fight over my media files.

Then I really got into Artificial Intelligence.

AI is an odd field of interest, my greatest regret is that most of the interesting projects were in Linux (I was still running with XP at the time). I toyed with the idea of switching to Linux, after making a few hesitant forum posts, I wondered if what people were telling me was true; Linux was easier then Windows. I didn't need to keep shelling out for software to protect my computer.

Now, lets move forward to 2008; I was worried about XP. When XP was 4 months old, I decided to try running with Windows 95 (Couldnt afford it, then) It took all of five minutes for my computer to start spewing ads on the desktop. I was concerned that XP would meet the same fate; Microsoft would move onto Vista and stop applying crucial updates to XP, thus meaning XP was about as safe as a grass hut in a tornado. That may have been the main reason I am where I am now, perhaps a far more truthful reason was that I had seen Compiz Fusion in action on YouTube.

So, I attempted to switch to Linux.

Big bloody mistake.

My desktop computer isnt exactly a buy-off-the-shelf deal, it was built out of spares, many parts taken from the first computer I had used and others thrown away for the scrapheap. (I'm disabled, I'm not exactly rolling in money here) Ubuntu just wouldn't run. Nor would Mephis, or Fedora, nor Debian...Mandriva One, however, did. Rather thankful that it did, I proceeded to try and connect to my wireless network.

Oh dear.

Ndiswrapper, anyone? For some, it works wonders, but here? Zilch.

So, I left Mandriva sitting side by side with XP, somewhat annoyed that the apparently fabulous world of Linux was just not something I'd be getting into.

Then a major disaster occurred; My copy of XP was indeed legal. But for some odd, odd reason, the system reactivated it's authorization protocol, telling me I had 10 days left which to activate the product. Belatedly, I spent 5 hours going through my stuff until I came up with the XP disk with the code on the side of the case. I then went to the activation wizard; “This copy of Windows is already registered”. Great, and it was still asking for activation. Quickly, I tried to find every online dropsite I could, I dropped my articles into Google Docs, spent 6 hours or so downloading the rest to the Google Mail servers, and shoved my 4.5 gigabyte music collection onto my memory stick. I then waited for the timer to run out, hoping the program would just ask me to activate the product, and not acutally lock it down.

Lock it down it did. It kept giving me the same 'error' message each time; “This copy of Windows is already activated”.

I screamed, I threatened to dissemble my computer. It took it like a man; just blinked it's lights at me.

So, XP was down. I put in the Mandriva CD and made a full install; I'd be damned if I didnt have a machine I could at least write on.

Then, during Christmas, my parents bought me a wonderful little thing, a laptop.

I hesitated as I looked at my vast array of CD's storing the dozen distros or so I had burnt. I reached out and took my Ubuntu 8.10 CD, carefully wiped the dust off it, and inserted it into the disctray.

This was an Asus X50GL laptop (Pentium duel-core), designed for multimedia, three times as powerful as my desktop, (which was a great big hulking machine I had nicknamed 'Beast' which contained an earlier single core Pentium) with intergreted DVD/CD burner...I hadn't even SEEN a DVD Burner until then (I'm a technologist at heart, but very rarely do I get anything younger then 6 years old) and never before have I witnessed such awesome power thanks to Moore's Law. It had a graphics card that could handle even my most advanced programs with ease. (SecondLife, I'm looking at you!)

Only one problem; it ran Vista, I could hear the fans attempting to rip themselves to pieces, the thing was so damn hot. Even idle, the CPU's were clocked at ¾ of usage.

This wouldn't do at all.

So, I restarted the machine, and installed Ubuntu. When the installation was finished and the machine rebooted, I could of sworn something had gone wrong, it was more quiet then my own breathing. Somewhat happy the damn thing wasn't falling over, I looked at the idle CPU usage. 


13%.

Christ almighty on a pogo stick! Normally, CPU's for me stayed idle at about 30%.


Ubuntu has been a dream, yes, I've tried out other distros, and either they weren't prepared for my laptop, nor was the laptop prepared for them. I've looked at KDE and whilst I found it rather beautiful, it requires far more work to it, for me to use it right now would require editing X.org, I task I do not think it is worth  the effort.


I am not a programmer, but I know enough to realize just how much work was put into Ubuntu and the Linux applications. I am now enjoying a trouble free system that satisfies every need (though I can think of a few that could be improved)

So, I'd like to thank you, all of you. The developers of Ubuntu, the developers of all the brilliant apps out there and the rest of you for supporting myself and others who may not be as technical as Linux's history would think we'd be.

I offer my services to the FOSS community, should you ever need someone fluent with AI, that is (and looking at GNOME 3.0, I suspect that may be soon) or a good brainstormer.

Thank you.

Welcome to the great Ubuntu community.  It is wonderful, isn't it, when something as good as Ubuntu just works on a machine with practically no messing around with drivers, applications, etc etc, and leaves you with a complete operating system with no restrictions on what, why, when or where you do anything you wish.  You can play around with it and even break it if you are not careful, and all you have to do is start again, no need to ask anyone for permission or agree to any silly licenses which will still not let you own the OS.

The few times I use windows nowadays just fills me with dread, and I get none of the comfortable feel I have using Ubuntu.  I have even persuaded my wife away from windows on her old laptop, and having just bought a new one with Vista (UUURGH!!!) I have put 9.04 on it and everything works, wireless, webcam, graphics with compiz, and this on a fairly bottom of the range Compaq CQ70-211EM.

Keep an eye on the forums and you will probably soon find yourself answering other's questions as I did.  It is the one thing I can do to pay back in some small way for the great work done for me in making Ubuntu what it is.
