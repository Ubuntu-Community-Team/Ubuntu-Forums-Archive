### Post by ledzepjes on 2012-04-15
I just spent a good amount of energy getting my systems upgraded from 10.10
all the way to 11.10 recently, and my experience with the new UI doesn't seem to have been justified.

I will say that Unity is for a lack of a better word, very unifying, which was the goal obviously, it's very polished and it does make things pleasing to look at, but as I explain, it makes things unpleasing from a usability standpoint, things are either omitted, missing, hidden or just take one to many clicks to get the same job done.

Now my rant on the new User Interface:

I wish I had kept 10.10, the effort required to upgrade did not outweigh the usability issues I'm having with Unity, I much preferred gnome 2 with all my Compiz effects and menu's and things all working just the way I liked it, Dash and Unity are not as useful for a desktop user like myself. I have since gotten Compiz effects working again, but why they are completely shutoff just by upgrading the operating system is annoying. I also don't like that I can't add items to the top menu bar like System(to show the cpu usage history) or Giplet(showing my current IP) or Parcellite(allowing easy access to the copy-paste functionality-which is why I installed it in the first place-how do I use Parcellite now?-am I expected to move my mouse left then hover over dash and left click then wait then type parcellite then selecting it then waiting for it to launch an interface because that doesn't work- where as before I could rt click the icon on the top menu bar and left click an item in the copy/paste history list which was functional and quick) Unity is not unifying my experience or making it easier to accomplish things with my desktop workflow, it's inhibited it and made it harder, more clicks, and more things hidden.

They've just dumbed everything down and made it harder to do regular tasks that were quite easy before, like selecting open windows from the task tray - there is no task tray to select from - the option to restart has been omitted in the shutdown menu and is only visible after clicking on shutdown then you get a dialog box and inside that window you can select restart. This had me scratching my head how to shutdown since I had previously disabled the annoying shutdown dialog boxes so that I could quickly shutdown, not be bombarded by endless pop-up dialoags asking me "Do you want to shutdown?", "Do you really want to shutdown?", "ok, do you want to actually really really logoff now?, or are you just pressing buttons?", by this time I have sternly replied to my computer "YES SHUTDOWN OR LOGOFF, DO THE ACTION I JUST CLICKED ON, WHICH IS WHY I CLICKED IT!". All those dialoag boxes confirming something I just clicked on can be very annoying sometimes, but I also see why some people like them to safe guard against shutting down when they don't want to or to prevent shutting down or logging off when the clicked by accident. I think third option besides having and disabling the dialog boxes, would be a graceful shutdown showing the time in a countdown box, defaulting to X number of seconds, let's say 5 seconds, with the option to stop the action (shutdown, logoff, etc...). And while I'm on the shutdown menu subject, something got borked in the upgrade process or Ubuntu 11.10 doesn't support suspend or hibernate, to which my remedy would be to replace those 2 menu items with "Restart" since it's currently missing from the menu and it actually works. When I click on suspend, it appears to be saving my open programs and current session information, and hibernate appears to save my session data as well. But after pressing the power button it appears to resume from suspend and/or hibernate, but when you log in, non of the open programs or windows are open. And when resuming from hibernation in particular, it lists that it's restoring the hibernation image data, but loads as if it was a fresh startup. 

It still shows verbosely the Linux startup process up to a point, around step 3, then it shows the Ubuntu loading symbol, this behavior is they way I selected it using startup manager to show the text process verbosely during startup - it's neat watching the process go from 0-1-2-3-10-11... and you can watch all the mouse and hard drives initializing - it's one more bit of information I like to see and not be hidden from me so I can actually tell whether or not Ubuntu is actually loading or stuck on a startup process and I can tell right away what process it's stuck on if it does. Showing information and giving me the options not available in Windows has been the differentiating hallmark that I've come to expect from the Ubuntu and the Linux community as a whole, not this current push to dumb things down and make things hidden or not available at all. It makes me feel like I'm just renting the operating system, which is how I feel running Windows, which is why I made the step to switch from Windows to Linux based operating systems in the first place. It's continuing to show less information the more I upgrade my Ubuntu versions. I like the option to see what's going on. So I dislike the current startup graphic, it doesn't provide any useful information or status on the progress of the bootup process like the previous versions of Ubuntu first had when Canonical first added the startup graphic. So since it doesn't even have a progress bar I say just have a black screen, if it shows movement that means the computer is on, but no progress bar movement then what's the point? I know that the computer is on because the noise it makes. so the only logical reason is that it makes it look pretty. Or, do what Microsoft usually never does, give the consumer a choice out of the box, give me an option to show verbosely the bootup process (either in the background through transparency or full screen with no graphic covering it up or in a side window or half the screen) making guys like me feel warm and fuzzy that the computer is actually progressing from step to step through the bootup process. (on a side note - an interactive bootup where I could hit pause/break key and temporarily pause the bootup process would be an added bonus - for learning purposes or to write something down, lets say it initializes a certain hard drive before another and you expected different, or for what ever reason wanted to see the boot order of certain hardware like a webcam or a video card if you have more than one). All that being said, the reasoning behind removing the verbose bootup process was supposed to be that it was faster, to which I don't care about a half of a half of 0.0001 seconds versus the tradeoff of not knowing whether the computer is progressing to bootup and what process it's on.

I also don't get why in startup manager, it only lists items that are added to the start-up process through the user istalling 3rd party applications, it should have shown both. Put the user added items at the top, then a seperator, and the default system startup items listed below that unhighlighted and with a label of "System Startup Items" and show an unlock button which after clicking shows the system startup items as being editable. For me, that would have been better, since I don't have bluetooth devices on my desktop and would like to remove bluetooth from starting up and running. I like a clean system and having the graphical choice makes it easier for me to optimize my system to have one less unnecessary thing.

Also, global menu and the push to make it more mac-like is not my preference, as others have pointed out, global menu doesn't work well on a LCD screen that desktop users have. It's bringing elements that are useful for small hand held screens but not useful for desktops.

I also find that Unity is slower in responsiveness and seems sluggish on my older and newer computers, the snappiness of Gnome 2 just isn't there. It's like using an e-reader to turn a page while reading a book, you have to click next ... aaaaand wait. When I click Dash, wait for it, ...then it opens. Same with Home folder, click on it, and wait a couple milliseconds. It was a few milliseconds quicker with Gnome 2.

Also, the option to rt click folders and files as admin within nautilus is gone.

I also would like it more similar to Windows 7 in the address bar of nautilus. I'd like too have the full address in the address bar shown when I click the mouse into it, and change back to the location entry when I click off, or for those that like the full address by default and the path bar when you click it with the mouse. Maybe Ubuntu Tweak will have this option in the future, currently it's either one or the other. It needs to be an option added to the preferences of nautilus in my opinion.

I'd like to see a default button besides all options, any changes made should have a default position, whether changing the desktop wallpaper to default mouse cursor that ships with Ubuntu to a default menu item being hidden or not hidden. Ubuntu Tweak does a lot of this. I'd like to see gconf and dconf also carry through with this idea.

My mind is confused as to why they've omitted the Applications - Places - System menu's, it was very intuitive and organized way of using menu's, and was easy for me to show other Linux/Ubuntu novices how to use the computer, the UI of having a Graphical Menu (humans do things graphically - the graphic center of our brain is after all the largest part) just made sense in Gnome 2, searching for things in Dash is just plain dumb, for me and really dumb for new users.

If Canonical wanted that, just include Gnome-Do by default, and have a little intro beginners video explaining how to use it by pressing window-key(super-key) plus space-bar and off you are searching for files and applications through a quicker interface than Dash.

I must say, that it's obvious that a lot of effort has gone into implementing Unity, and for what they've focused on it's good, but it's not the interface I was hoping to have. For me personally it's a step back, and the same distance backward has been achieved with Gnome 3, (no shutdown in the shutdown menu, come on people-homey don't play that)

I like that the current version of unity has the left bar hide during usage of full screen windows, but this feature is rumored to be turned off in 12.04, hope it stays or has the option of being turned on or off.

I also like the feature of the past couple releases of Ubuntu to include the option to turn on two-finger scrolling for mouse pads, which know I can't live without. However, that brings up another point about resizing windows, it is very difficult to resize windows in Ubuntu, it's very tedious because the "grab-area" is so tiny, it's as if it's a pixel wide and if you don't have the mouse EXACTLY on the edge or in the corner it won't work and I sometimes end up selecting and moving an entire group of folders that are in a window directly behind the edge of the one I'm trying to resize, shaking my head in frustration. If this had happened to you, you know how frustrating this can be. I would like to suggest making a triangular shaped grab-area, either on full time or hidden by default-you'd probably make it the later) the size or more than 20 pixels or an area that is customizable in size. This has got to be an issue with touch screens and don't foresee resizing windows being very doable on a touchscreen, of any size. This should be addressed, and of all my ranting, this should be something trivial that maybe even I could help address, I'd like to contribute to make this happen, but how do I do that? is the first step filing a launchpad bug, and waiting for someone who knows how to edit the source code change it, I'd rather make this my first attempt at helping make Unity better, how do I personally contribute to editing the source code?

I do not intend to be personal or disrespectful with my rant (as a lot of love and hard work was obviously put into Unity and Gnome 3 and I don't wish to stifle experimentation or future developement), or have it taken personally. This was merely constructive criticism meant to bolster the linux community and help Unity, Canonical, and Ubuntu. For me it's just not "there" yet.

I will be awaiting Mate when it comes, and keep a hopeful eye on Unity and Gnome 3 in the future.
