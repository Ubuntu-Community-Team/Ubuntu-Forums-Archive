### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-07-10
Today my daughter brought her laptop to me asking if I could look at it. She said her children had used it yesterday. 

She started it up and entered her password. When it should have displayed the Windows 7 desktop, it only displayed a fake anti-virus program. I couldn't get to the desktop to do anything with the computer. I also could not use Ctrl-Alt-Del to get to the task manager to kill the rogue process. I had to hold the power button down to shut the computer off. 

On my computer, I went to the Internet and searched for this so-called anti-virus program and found where it stored its files. It was a new Trojan discovered 2 days ago. I booted from the live USB, navigated to where the files are stored, and deleted them. Hopefully, the anti-virus vendor she uses will come out with an update that fixes anything I may have missed.

After shutting down the computer, I asked my daughter to reboot and log in. All she could say ways "thank you dad, I would have had to reinstall Windows". I told her that "not everyone has a USB thumb drive laying around with an entire operating system on it".

My son is 34 years old. He uses Ubuntu. Microsoft did something that got him angry with them so he asked me how to install Linux. 

He knew I used Linux distros; because, it has been installed on the family's shared computer in the family room for about two decades. The family room computer was used for browsing and email before there was Netflix. Now that the Family wants Netflix on a big screen TV attached to the family room computer, the family room computer runs Windows 7.

I gave him a Live USB of Ubuntu to try. He tried it and installed it on his HDD himself. I was surprised that he didn't need any help getting it set up. He eventually installed a LTS version when it was available and stays with the LTS versions. 

When I started with Ubuntu, I didn't know about LTS releases so I guess he heard that nugget of information from me. If you've used other distros, then you know about rolling releases and other systems. When I started using Ubuntu, I had no concept of a LTS release. 

He is in the medical profession; therefore, he just wants to use his computer and doesn't experiment with Linux distros nor do very much in the way of modifications. He installs some applications he wants, changes the wallpaper, installs multimedia support, and that is about it. 

If it weren't for the Live USB, I don't know if he would be using a Linux distro. He didn't have to learn about downloading the iso, checking the MD5SUM, burning at the slowest speed the CD drive offers, and so fourth. He can do all those things now; however, he didn't have to do it when he first tried it.
