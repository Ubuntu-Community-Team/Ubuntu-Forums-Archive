### Post by the8thstar on 2009-10-05
I have used Ubuntu on several computers for the last two and a half years. I still use Windows but this is getting scarcer as time goes by.

I am concerned that several users quit using Ubuntu because of their frustration(s), often backed by a long explanation of hardware incompatibilities and the lack of quality in the software they used. It is not my place to tell developers what to do or advocate to retain the departing users - I don't have authority to do that.

I think a lot of new users don't understand how Linux works. They just see the fact that they didn't have to pay for it. They expect their system to work (and who could blame them).

In the end departing users often blame Linux and the way it works as a cause to theirs problems. This is giving Ubuntu a very bad wrap... and they don't care what they say because they are leaving Ubuntu anyway.

Personally I'm still taken aback by the many graphical glitches between the BIOS boot screen, the usplash, the GDM and the final desktop. I understand that it just has to be that way, but is it good enough? AWN loads with big blotchy gray squares (with both intel and nVidia chips mind you). Ugly.

I got used to the command line, but I'm glad there are GUI tools to do some of the same things (BleachBit and Ubuntu Tweak to name just these two).

I have to say that switching from XP to Ubuntu was a difficult experience when it comes to supporting the hardware. To this day I cannot make my printer work (Canon Pixma ip1500) or my webcam either (Microsoft VX-6000). I also have a Belkin USB wireless device... useless in Ubuntu. Blame it on the manufacturers of these devices for not releasing a Linux driver if you want, but this is a *real* problem for me. Picture this: I have to switch back to Windows if I want to print anything. Pathetic. 

I am glad Wine exists, for it allows me to play *some* of my Windows games and most important I can run Office 2003 (fully updated to SP3) and Office 2007 (the SP don't work though). OpenOffice has some page formatting issues which I can't live with.

On the Windows side now, I won't upgrade Vista to Windows 7. The benefit is slim and the price is high (130 euros). Plus my hardware would not give me any productivity boost with it.

So I am going to stick with Ubuntu. On a side note though, when I look at the time I spent on trying to figure things out with Ubuntu and the money I would have spent for a Mac, I really wonder if the latter wouldn't have been a better solution for me.

All of these things perplex me. Feel free to share your feelings.

### Post by Methuselah on 2009-10-06
Are some people labouring under misconceptions or does my sarcasm meter need upgrading?

Programs designed for Windows ****DO NOT WORK**** on Linux.
Do not let anyone sell you Wine as a routine solution for working around this law of nature.

Wine is a bonus and should not be relied upon for mission critical activities.
Do not expect it to work flawlessly or you will be bitterly disappointed.
I only use Wine for things I could happily do without or which happen to work satisfactorily.

Don't get me wrong, Wine is an extremely ambitious and surprising piece of Open Source Software.
However, it's important to keep expectations realistic.
It will not turn your Linux box into a free Windows. 

As for hardware incompatibilities, there is only one solution: buy compatible hardware.
If you have a Mac you can't buy any old PC peripheral, plug it in, and expect it to work.
In fact, if you buy a Mac you will be investing in a hardware platform that will limit some of your choices anyway.

I do sympathize with the difficulty in knowing what is compatible.
There are very few 'Linux compatible' stickers so you have to do independent research or find out what has worked for others.
Alternatively, one could buy Linux pre-installed much as one would buy a Mac or a PC.
When you look at things from that perspective you'll realize you're doing something very different when you install Linux yourself.

As for aggressive complainer, I've started ignoring their threads.
It was not a conscious decision but arose out of a desire not to be bothered with people who make one self-important, inflammatory proclamation and disappear.

I installed 3-4 Ubuntu versions before using one as my main system.
Not once did I post bashing it when I determined that it did not yet suit my needs.
I appreciated all the effort being put into it and the improvements that were being made.

Most importantly, I was informed about what it was and how it was different from what I'd been using.
However, in an age where information is more accessible than ever before many people seem to prefer being uninformed. 
That is why so many lies can be told and perpetrators can get away with it because few people know the facts and even fewer care to know.
But that's another matter...

EDIT: Note that this is not directed solely at the OP.
I had been reading through the thread and picked up various points I wanted to mention.
