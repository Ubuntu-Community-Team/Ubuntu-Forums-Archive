### Post by Indras on 2006-08-28
I remember once reading on these forums that giving up Windows is kind of like kicking the habit of smoking.  You'll try it a few times, each time with limited success, and then, even after you've finally "quit" you can relapse back again at the drop of a hat.

It was sorta like that for me.  I had to use Linux (Redhat 6.4) for a few of my college classes and really loved the idea of using free software.  Ever since then I've always kept a partition open on my system for some version of Linux to play around with.  I usually never got much past installing the OS.  I tried lots of versions of redhat, fedora core 1 through 3, and a couple copies of mandrake.  There was always something that put me off immediately and made me switch back to Windows again.

They basically fell into these categories:
1. Hardware - especially this 17" LCD monitor of mine.  A couple versions of Red Hat autodetected horzontal and vertical refresh rates that were *negative* numbers.  Naturally, all I got was a big empty black screen whenever the graphical installer tried to run.  Every other version simply detected a max resolution of 800x600 or some other nonsense.
2. Multimedia - I was never able to get a distro to play both MP3 files and DVDs.  I can't tell you how many hours of my life I wasted on this.
3. Games - I've always been a gamer.  I love RPG's especially, and graphics aren't important.  Castle of the Winds is just as fun as Diablo, really!  But, wine has always been very poor at running windows games.
4. Dependency Hell - This was the real kicker.  This one issue actually caused me to break a keyboard.  And I'm not a violent person.  I was working on a redhat box that couldn't detect my network card, so I'd have reboot into windows, download the rpm that contained my network driver, then reboot to redhat and try to install it.  Only to find that it had 17 unmet dependencies.  So, you switch back to windows and track down all 17 of those files and reboot again.  When you try to install all 18 rpm files at once, it throws an error saying you have 10 new unmet dependencies, and upgrading 4 of these packages will break a major component of your system... it went back and forth like that for over twelve hours straight before I broke my keyboard out of frustration and went to bed.

Three months ago, I was reading an article on slashdot about all the crap that Windows Genuine Advantage does without your permission... like executing code that it just downloaded from Microsoft's website and phoning home with hardware specs and stuff.  I've always been frustrated with Windows, but I stick with it because of the four items above.

However, as I read some user comments regarding linux (and not having to worry about WGA), I suddenly realized that MP3's and games weren't that important to me anymore.  My wife has a perfectly good Windows XP box for that stuff, there's no reason why I can't run Linux on my machine full time.

I did some research.  I posted in some message boards about what I was looking for.  Many laughed at my dependency frustrations, saying that yum, apt, and portage had made that situation obsolete.  Just pick a distro and run with it.  I've always heard good things about Debian, and read that Ubuntu was a 'friendlier' version of it... so I had to try.  I downloaded and burned the iso and restarted my computer.  Installing linux doesn't scare me... I do it all the time.

But I was not prepared to start up into a usable linux desktop.  It really caught me by surprise... I guess I didn't read the website carefully to see that it was a live CD.  I was blown away.  I fell in love with Gnome Partition Editor.  I cruised around my hard drive with nautilus to see what would open and what wouldn't.  Naturally, multimedia didn't work... but I was used to that from other distros.

I installed in record time with no complaints.  I registered for these forums here and began looking at how to get DVD's to play, when I ran into automatix.  Twenty minutes later my system could play every audio and video file I could find.  Realmedia, quicktime, xvid, mp3... I thought I had died and gone to heaven.

I played with Synaptic and thought I was going to cry with joy when it automatically resolved dependencies for me and kept me from breaking my machine.

Oh, and for the first time ever, my monitor actually worked right off the bat, at 1024x768 (I had to manually edit my xorg.conf to get it up to 1280x1024, but I'm not complaining!)

Oh, and all my favorite RPGs work wonderfully in VMWare (Fallout 2 and Arcanum, for instance), and I can actually play SimCity 4 flawlessly with Cedega.

This weekend, I decided that maybe I'd outgrown Ubuntu and it was time to try something more powerful.  I immediately jumped to the hardest one of all, linux from scratch.  Yes, I got it working, but due to inadequate instructions or my own incompetence, neither my sound card nor my network card would work.  It was a good learning experience, but not something I could devote my time to in order to keep it maintained.

So I downloaded the minimal install CD for Gentoo, and followed the instructions to the letter.  After issuing the 'emerge -rsync' command to update the portage database (kind of like apt-get update in ubuntu/debian) I waited for a half an hour for it to finish, and it never did.  While I was waiting I read through the rest of the documentation and found that the average time to emerge the kde desktop from the basic install was 1170 minutes on a 1.6Ghz machine, which is close to my specs.  That's over 19 hours, folks.  I'm sorry, but I have a hard enough time waiting the two hours for windows xp to install and update, I'm certainly not going to wait 19 hours for a linux install!

So, I thought to myself: What the heck am I doing?  Ubuntu works and has everything I want, why not leave it at that?

So I'm back, and here to stay.  It was a fun and educational weekend.  I thought I was going to be writing a post on these boards saying goodbye, but it didn't quite work out that way.  Now I know a heck of a lot more about linux, and I can definitely give an informed opinion when I say Ubuntu is the best operating system I've ever used.
