### Post by pcjunkie on 2008-04-25
As an office SOHO administrator I feel the need to push a need for what I know is missing in Hardy.

The office consists of 7 Computers, All windows (Except mine).

What Ubuntu needs to focus on if wants to replace XP/ Vista or at least compete with them is small stuff but obvious stuff in my mind. 

1) 

The first one is fixing SATA boot sequence and loader mapping. Its horrible! I can honestly tell you 100% of Computers with 3 SATA drives will screw up 100% of the time and you will not be able to boot. Its a shambles! And its stopping people using Ubuntu simply because they cant get grub to list the drives in the correct order. They will not bother to fix it rather, they will panic and get XP back and forget it ever happened.

2) Native soft raid support for firmware makers. 

This should run exactly like it does in windows. No screwing around with complex setups. It should see the drives the way the BIOS sees the drives and use it "as is". This will keep the masses returning because I know of many cases myself where people have corrupted entire drives because they expected Ubuntu to work the same way XP does when it comes to soft RAID. It doesn't and any inadvertent writing to a RAID 0 disk will corrupt the entire drive/s ruining potentially an entire (XP) setup. 

3) 

Networking is good but far to complex for a small SOHO setup where the user "has no idea what they are doing" It should seek the computers and then automate the samba setup to the point where its as simple as yes, no, share this but not that. No logins unless requested. Currently its still asking for logins on folders that are totally open. There should also be an obvious way to map a network drive and have Ubuntu default or load that drive every time it starts. Currently its a convoluted and drawn out process. It must be easier.   

4)
Printing is still a pain in the rear with particular need to get Multi Function work stations printing properly. I have a Konica 7022 and its difficult somtimes to get it working in Windows over IP. Getting it to work in Ubuntu is harder again then when you do, it prints test pages in constant loops and nothing else. There should be some way of simply getting the Post script driver from the windows driver file and simply dropping in to a folder and pointing the system to the IP. (Sounds like magic)

if Ubuntu can do that and do it better than XP then you have your target market. I am not saying its hard or easy to do, that's not up to me but its what Ubuntu needs to be if wants the SOHO market. At the moment its fluff and tedious file manipulation to get something working but never properly.

What the SOHO expects is to install, set up the network share, set up the printer, and go strait back to work and in that order exactly.

At the moment its a good day or 3 on forum reading up on incomplete and sometimes incorrect methods of setting this up. The Wiki is helping but not completely and the complexity of the setup to the non programer /administrator also means the WIKI cannot possibly contain the information needed. When you can then it should be simple.

In an ideal world, any release deemed stable should; not LTS releases in special. However, in this non-ideal world of ours, some major bugs **that affect certain combinations of hardware and software**, thus have an impact on a certain subset of users **only**, can slip by, because of any combination of the following:

[list][*]The bug may not be observed on time for fixing in the final release
[*]The bug may be affecting an obscure combination of software and/or hardware
[*]Even if the bug is observed and apparently reproducible, it may not be triaged on time
[*] The bug may be in the upstream code and it may not be possible for upstream to provide a fix within the Ubuntu release cycle
[*]There may not be enough resources for fixing the bug, even though it's triaged and reproducible[/list]

### Post by Maupertus on 2008-06-01
> **Thirtysixway said:**
> Well everything can't please everyone I guess.  I like Hardy, except I can't fix my login screen resolution :p

A little bit of topic, but I hope it can resolve somebody's problem.

Personaly I have helped myself and a lot of other people with this thread: [http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=804175&highlight=login+window+resolution](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=804175&highlight=login+window+resolution)

You can edit the xorg.conf.file by using
```
sudo gedit /etc/X11/xorg.conf
```

Please remember to make a backup of it before you go and edit away. 

[QUOTE=Summary]Change the Screen section to something like this.
```
Section "Screen"
	Identifier "Default Screen"
	Monitor "Configured Monitor"
	Device "Configured Video Device"
	DefaultDepth 24
	SubSection "Display"
		Depth 24
		**Modes	"1280x1024" "1024x768" "800x600" "640x480"**
	EndSubSection
EndSection
```
Pay special attention to the Modes line. The *first* listed resolution there will be the default, and as such, what's displayed at the login screen. The other resolutions will be available to users upon having logged in.

Add and remove resolutions there as you please.[/QUOTE]
