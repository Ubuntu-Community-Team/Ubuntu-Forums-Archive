### Post by cloyd on 2010-01-31
A little while back, my wife and I both decided we wanted netbooks.  My wife always seems to argue for getting a higher end product, so rather than walking out of Best Buy with one of the little Asus 1 Gig machines running XP, we both walked out with Gateways 2 Gig machines running . . . Vista.  The purpose for buying them was they were light, and would be lighter to carry to the office than our full fledged laptops, and would access the internet, do word processing and spreadsheets. Of the two of us, I insisted that I must be able to print at the office. Also, I thought I couldn't live without Winamp.  When I installed the printer driver, Vista slowed to a very slow crawl.  Walk away from my desk for 15 minutes, and it simply locked up. It booted s--l--o--w --l--y. I really thought I'd have been better off with one of the cheaper machines with XP.

My step-son is an engineer, and he recommended Linux. So I started looking. I took my time. The first I tried was a little thing called Moolux, and it wouldn't recognize my wireless. Then I tried Ubuntu NBR (both on live flash drives) and everything worked out of the box.  Ended up installing a dual boot system. In fact, with Ubuntu NBR it works better, faster and is more stable than the 4 Gig 64bit laptop. 

Yes, there are things I still can't do in Ubuntu that I could do in windows. But I learn more every day. There are a few problems I can't solve, but they are getting fewer.

The netbook is on its way to being windows free (haven't made the jump yet . . . one problem with flash drives I must solve yet).  

Now, my wife used to be a system engineer on Unix systems.  I thought she might really go for this Linux system, yet, she says, "I'll switch to Ubuntu when you get all the bugs worked out."  In reality, I think she fears not being compatible with associates who send us highly formatted documents done in Microsoft Office. Open Office doesn't always display the formatting the same. She has tried Open Office, but she doesn't seem overly fond of it.  

Ubuntu does all either one of us really wants to do. Neither one of us are gamers. Yet Ubuntu is not Windows, and (thank God) never will be. Open Office is not Microsoft Office, but for the price, it works for me, and would work for her _if_ she learned more about how Open Office handled formatting (especially bullets and tables).  

She watches my machine boot and shut down and says "Wow!" but doesn't seem the least bit interested in trying it herself. She knows Unix; I'd think she'd take to the command line. I guess it really doesn't matter, but its like I've found something great and want to share it with the world.  I don't bug her about it, but  .   .    . have any of you been able to convince anyone reluctant to change to do so? If so, how?

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2010-01-31
IMHO, the best solution is actually quite simple. PATIENCE...

It's been my experience that all one needs do is wait for the inevitable opportunity. Then sensibly point out the obvious. ;)

The key is to not be annoying or apply any pressure. After pointing out the obviously, walk away and wait for them to come to you. I always act as if I'm totally disinterested.

### Post by Irihapeti on 2010-01-31
> **Tamlynmac said:**
> ...After pointing out the obviously, walk away and wait for them to come to you. I always act as if I'm totally disinterested.

Very good point. I've been told that people rarely change their minds when someone is actually talking to them about the topic. It usually happens when they are thinking about it on their own, and if you were respectful with them, that will also be an important factor. When I think about it, I realise that this is the way I do it.

Quite often - maybe even most of the time - the reason given isn't the real reason. The real reason may be something like fear of change, fear of being seen as a weirdo or some other negative thing, fear of being seen as too easily pushed around, not wanting to learn something new because there's too much change going on in other parts of life - and lots of other things. Of course, the person may not be very keen to own up to their real reason because they're worried about being criticised/laughed at.

To really get anywhere, it's important to find out (respectfully) what the real reason is and do something to address that ... if that's possible.

But my question would be, first and foremost, why is it so important? Can we not just let people do their thing?

Mind you, if they are expecting you to fix their computer for nothing, then that might be a good reason in itself. That's the kind of thing that would get on my nerves very quickly. I'd probably just tell them that I'm not fixing their computer if they're running Windows. Nicely, of course. :)

### Post by Juan Largo on 2010-02-01
@cloyd

I'm sure your Unix-savvy wife knows that all computers running Windows are vulnerable to viruses, spyware and other forms of malware, and that computers running Linux aren't.  Make ***that*** your main selling point.

In my case, convincing my wife to switch was easy.  We share a desktop computer and we both got fed up with getting infected with malware (despite taking every precaution imaginable).  So one day I just installed Linux on it.  There wasn't any discussion about it -- I just did it.  

After I got everything configured, I gave my wife a 10-minute tour, showing her how to log in, which icons to click to get to her email, launch a web browser, to get to her files, and how to shut down.  If she can't figure out how to do a particular task on her own, she asks me and I explain it to her.  It really wasn't any harder for her to learn how to use Linux than it was for her to learn how to use Windows.

In our family, I do all the system upgrades, back ups, configuration changes and tweaking -- the same as before when we used Windows.  It's just that now everything is easier.  My wife simply uses the computer for ordinary tasks, and everything just works for her.  She definitely notices the fact that the computer always runs fast and never slows down because of "Windows rot."  The main thing is that both of us know that we don't have to worry about viruses, spyware, etc. and that our system is secure.
