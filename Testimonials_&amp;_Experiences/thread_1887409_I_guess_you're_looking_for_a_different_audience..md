### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-11-27
Ubuntu is a distribution that has always changed very quickly. There's nothing in Ubuntu's past that has screamed "Software will not change".

Ubuntu is a fast-paced distribution and this will not change. If you don't like the pace, you could try sticking with their LTS releases, or going back to Red Hat.

However, you can't stay with Gnome 2 forever. It's been abandoned by its own developers. You can't ship abandonware as the cornerstone of a Linux distribution; that's madness. You can't blame Ubuntu for not using Gnome 2 or for "not allowing customization" as this is the decision of Gnome's developers.

A few seconds of Google would reveal that you can customize the Gnome 3 Fallback Session panel merely by holding Alt while right-clicking. It's actually the Gnome 2 panel, slightly reworked, so it supports the old panel applets. However, this probably won't last forever either; it's old buggy software.

If you'd like to go back to a different distribution that still ships old, unmaintained software, then that's your choice and I'll support your right to choose. But Ubuntu has never been a distribution for people who like everything to stay the same. The entire computer industry is not for people who want everything to stay the same, to be honest.

But the truth is, desktops, notebooks, are gradually going away, and operating systems have to anticipate the needs of mobile computing devices in order to survive in the market. We resistors are, believe it or not, in the minuscule minority. Take it from me, I work in a campus bookstore environment. Even their textbooks are gradually migrating to reading devices. They submit their writing assignments electronically. Those of us who still prefer the desktop paradigm have to accept that momentous changes are taking place, globally. 

I don't know what the new paradigm is called, but it's going to accommodate smaller mobile devices, and become more and more ubiquitous. By that I mean it will be present in your car, your phone, your credit card, TV, probably even your refrigerator, eventually even in eyeglasses and clothes. OK, ok, the TV and refrigerator aren't mobile. But I mean they'll one day communicate with each other and all work together. We have no idea what the future operating system will be like. Probably no one knows. But it's going to be wildly beyond anything we can imagine now. 

I'm betting that anyone who doesn't accept change is like Xerox back in the 80s rejecting development of the personal computer as not having a future. (ref. [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerox_Alto](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerox_Alto)) We live in exciting and terrifying times.

But here's some good news. Take a look at Classic-Menu-Indicator for Unity. It allows me to keep some of my old working habits in Ubuntu 11.10. It think it's a great add-in, and you may like it too.

[http://www.florian-diesch.de/software/classicmenu-indicator/](http://www.florian-diesch.de/software/classicmenu-indicator/)

### Post by scratman on 2011-11-27
I agree that with the demise of Gnome 2 there had to be a decision over which DE to use.  I don't have a problem with Canonical deciding to create their own DE.  However, as has been pointed out in the past, a DE that is perfectly suited to a touchscreen phone/tablet is not the best option for non-touchscreen hardware.  The only piece of hardware I have that is touchscreen is my HTC Desire Z, and frankly, I wouldn't want to use Unity on that.  Nor would I wish to use the Android interface on my PC.  They are simply not the best option for that scenario.  I'd argue that they're not even necessarily the least bad option.

My issue is not change, but that the change is not intuitive.  Anybody that picks up an Android phone can work out exactly how to use it within an hour, the hardest part for most people is remembering all the usernames and passwords to set up their social networking accounts.  With Unity, I have seen and discussed with scores of people simply flounder.  It is too different to our preconceptions based on past experience, it is in many aspects simply counter-intuitive, but the worst part, in my considered opinion, is that it is difficult for the average "point and click" user to change things so that they "just work" for them.  Please don't come out and shout that "It's really easy to change things, all you have to do is....."  That's not the point, the point is that people have to ask at all, it's just not simple enough to configure the last 2 Ubuntu releases for the average user.

Personally, I prefer the Minimise/Maximise/close buttons to be on the top right of my windows, I prefer drop-down menus listing the software that I have, and a specific piece of software what I can open as a portal to software that is available for install.  I know the names of the software I run, so I don't need the OS to tell me which software I have that will open things for me.  I like a row of boxes at the bottom of my screen that tells me what I have open currently.

On the other hand, I appreciate that this does not cover every single user that may ever wish to use the operating system.  What annoys me to is that the operating system is essentially geared towards people that do want function buttons to be in unexpected places, and the operating system to take inspired guesses of what software you want to use, installed or not, and believes that people want to use a system with menus that take up ~2500 pixels per menu item (which I confess is great when I try to use my desktop from 10 feet away, which did happen once.....)

Fundamentally, though, I do not understand why a working method that is counter-intuitive to a large number of people was the option selected.  It is not beyond the wit of Canonical to develop a DE that is similar enough to Gnome 2.x to be immediately usable, with the option to select more bizarre and counter-productive amendments to suit the more eccentric working patterns.

I do not like Unity, it is not in my opinion fit for purpose, and as a result I am still using Ubuntu 10.10 because it is as close as Ubuntu has ever got to it's mission goals:-  It "just works", and it is "Linux for Human Beings"

I wonder whether Ubuntu will start branching out into new areas of eccentricity??  Maybe a real life car controlled by a Playstation controller, with a secret custom key map?  Boot to terminal and leave you to it?  Default to go into screensaver/power saving standby mode after 3 seconds?  Would any of there surprise me.........

### Post by viperdvman on 2011-11-27
If anything, Tablets will end up replacing netbooks and smaller laptops/notebooks... especially if other tablet makers start to go the route that ASUS has with the Eee Pad Transformer and include a keyboard dock.

I highly doubt the Desktop PC is going anywhere... and the larger desktop replacement Laptops. People do use the full-sized computers for productivity and heavy-duty gaming, while Tablets are mostly used for web, e-mail, IM, social networking, e-book reading, minor to moderate gaming, and other similar stuff. In addition, Tablets, like Smartphones, are build upon numerous variations of the ARM architecture, a number of them incompatible with each other. Sure, almost all of them can run Android, but there are some apps made specifically for OMAP-based tablets/phones, ones made for Snapdragon-based tablets/phones, ones made for Tegra-based tablets/phones, etc. Desktops/Laptops/Netbooks, however, are all built on a very universal x86/x86-64 architecture. Apps that work on a decent Intel will work on a decent AMD will work on a decent VIA. Why do you think Apple ditched PowerPC in favor of Intel (well, one of the reasons)? 

The other plus note with desktops over laptops/netbooks and tablets/smartphones... you have the ability to build your own to whatever specs you want **and** to upgrade it as well, and not go with what a name brand builds for you. So for PC builders like myself, desktops are still the way to go. 


I do agree that tablets are skyrocketing in the market and are very much here to stay. And Microsoft, GNOME, and Ubuntu are all making strides toward unifying Desktop/Laptop and Tablet interfaces with their OS's/DE's. But desktops/laptops will always have a place and are here to stay.
