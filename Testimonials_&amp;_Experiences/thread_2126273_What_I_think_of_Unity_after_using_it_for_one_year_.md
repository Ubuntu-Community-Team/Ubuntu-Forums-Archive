### Post by freshw on 2013-03-16
Please note that I am not a native speaker.

I've been working with Unity for some time now. Not sure how long exactly, but at least since 12.04. (I remember that it didnt work so well for me in 11.10.) And I like it a lot. So much in fact that I wouldn't go back to the classical Gnome2 style desktops out there. First of all it looks and feels great. I've been on the Gnu/Linux desktop for more than fifteen years now, so I am used to graphical environments being reliable but ugly as hell. People always used to stare at my screen with a disgusted look on their face, like &#8222;Why is he using this outdated piece-of-**** software?&#8220;. With Unity that's different. Unity is attractive with its fancy window shadows and transitions and what not. In the old days of not-so-nice GUIs I wouldn't have traded my valuable Gnu/Linux desktop for something else just for the looks, but now that I can have it both I want both. I'm staring at this screen for several hours a day and it's way better to stare at something nice-looking. It's obvious though, that the visual design of Unity is heavily &#8222;inspired&#8220; by OSX. It's not just the Globalmenu, even those top panel icons look boldly similar. But I don't care about that. Design is mostly about stealing ideas and concepts anyway, very much like art.

 Globalmenu is great. At first I was a bit sceptic about it because I was used to the window focus following the mouse without me having to click on things first (which doesnt work well with Globalmenu). But on the other hand I love minimalistic (i.e. space-efficient) interfaces. I got accustomed to the click-focus behaviour quickly and now having the menu in the top panel is a must-have, I don't want to have it at the top of every single window anymore. Then there's this ctrl-w thingy that shows all open windows. Love that. Although in some cases it can be hard to see the right window to pick. So there's still need for some alternative, but still, it's the nicest way of switching windows now.
 
 And of course there's the Unity Dash, which is great to. I am a fast typer and I always prefer using the keyboard over mouse/ touchpad/ trackpoint, so hitting <super> and typing in what I want is the way to go. Sometimes though, while surfing the web or scrolling through some other text I have my fingers on the cursor-device anyway, so I intuitively prefer to visit the launcher and click on an icon to open an application. The launcher is hidden by default on my desktop because otherwise it would distract me. That's very important! It's to visually intrusive with it's shiny icons in many colors, always offering diversion while I am trying to concentrate on a specific single task.
 
 Than there's this nice feature of dragging a window against a screen edge to maximize it or to place it on one half of the screen. I use that heavily now. It became a natural impulse quickly and I miss it every time I use an older desktop. Probably I should mention the HUD as well, but I don't use it at all. I tried to use it, but it never shows the commands I want. I guess I just don't get it. Don't care. Don't need it.
 
 Now, after about one year of using Unity almost every day, there's just one thing I miss. It's the traditional taskbar. Several days ago I installed xfce4-panel which is the only panel I found, that integrates well with Unity. (Gnome-panel interferes with Globalmenu somehow. Other panels don't hide windows from other virtual desktops.) I placed just a taskbar on it and started using it again like I did in the past. Instantly I rediscovered how efficient it is, to navigate the virtual desktop using an old-school taskbar.
 
 I'll try to explain why.
 
 When I am working on a task that involves switching between different windows a lot, I put those windows on one virtual desktop and keep other windows on the remaining virtual desktops. This way I can have the windows of different tasks organized on one virtual desktop per task. At any time I can re-decide what I think of as one task and re-organize my open windows accordingly. Ctrl+s is great for this since I can move single windows arround inside that view. (Don't now what to call it.)
 
 But when it comes to managing open windows on one virtual desktop logically or practically belonging to one task, nothing beats the traditional taskbar: It helps me to keep focused on what I am actually doing right now by showing me all the open windows that my current task consists of and nothing else. All I have to do is lower my eyes and look at the taskbar. I cannot stress enough how important that is. It keeps the current task strapped together inside my head somehow. And switching between windows is always just one click.
 
 The launcher doesn't give me that same experience. It's not supposed to I guess. It doesn't show me just the windows I need to focus on right now. In fact it doesn't show windows at all, but applications instead. It shows all the applications with open windows on any virtual desktop (and maybe some closed applications) and highlights the important ones for my current task with a little filled triangle. Focusing on these triangles always kicks me out of my working flow. Taskbar entries are so much better. They are exclusive to my current task and they combine an icon with some title text. I instantly see the entry I want, click on it, and there's my desired window.
 
 Ctrl+w isn't so helpful either. I like it a lot, as previously mentioned, but it's not there all the time like a taskbar. To let it show me all the open windows on the active virtual desktop I have to interrupt my working flow inside the current window visually and with a gesture, instead of just looking quickly at the panel and back again. This way it doesn't have this nice effect of keeping my current task as a whole strapped together inside my head.
 
 It's also slower at switching windows. It takes an initial gesture (e.g. ctrl+w), a quick visual search for the right window and a final click on that window. Of course I could just click on a launcher icon. But like I said, there's to much noise there to find what I want as quick as I would with a taskbar. Besides, if I select an application from the launcher that has several open windows, it might bring up the wrong window. Than I have to click on the icon again to show all windows of that application, find the right one and click on it to select it. I could instead right-click on the icon and select the window, but still, the taskbar is much faster.
 
 In conclusion, doing serious stuff (involving multiple windows) in Unity and without a taskbar is much more difficult. It's harder to keep focused on a task since the flow of it constantly gets throttled down or even disrupted. So, I am going to keep my newly re-discovered taskbar at the bottom of my screen. Nevertheless I'll stick to Unity on my Ubuntu/Linux desktop, at least as long as I can find a taskbar that works with it.
 
 Sorry for my bad English.
