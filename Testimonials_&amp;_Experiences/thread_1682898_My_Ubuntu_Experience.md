### Post by Sohrab Kakakhel on 2011-02-06
My journey to ubuntu has been a strange one, but I'm sure many many users stumbled upon it in exactly the same way I did. It started out when installed grease monkey on my mozzila firefox in an attempt to personalize my internet experience. I loved how I could just download scripts and use them to do a tonne of stuff. After that I started reading up on mozzila and the open source philosophy. During that time I remembered how my brother showed me ubuntu many years ago. I remember him being excited showing me how the repository had tonnes of applications one could download. The thing I most distinctly remember was how he loved the sticky notes which he could paste on the desktop. Anyways, I downloaded ubuntu in sheer randomness and installed it side by side with windows. At first I didnt understand alot. I being a hardcore windows user since I started with computers tried finding programs to install from their respective websites and ending up downloading tar.gz and tar.bz2 files. I thought i'd just click them and theyd get installed haha but slowly after searching online I got a hang of the software center, and synaptic. After a week I shifted completely to ubuntu. It was a big leap for me because I enjoy playing guitar and recording music, so I left behind all the software I was used to like adobe audition and superior drummer. 

All I can say is I love ubuntu, its a treat breaking free of the shackles of years and years of monotony with windows. Its so much fun waking up in the morning and booting up ubuntu. It loads in 20 seconds, shuts down in 4. Everything works perfectly. I spend hours sometimes browsing the software center finding things that might be useful to me. I love playing around with compiz and emerald themes and have made my desktop look exactly the way I want. Apps like rhythmbox, pidgin, deluge and VLC are just a damn treat to use. I love laptop-mode-tools. I can personalize my harddisk spin ups, battery life. Pretty much everything. And I learnt all this stuff in just a month. The learning curve is steep and hard in the start but it takes a day or two to orient yourself. Any problems along the way and you know deep down its just a google away. 

I want to thank all the people who made this possible. I know none of them might read this, but know there are people out there who appretiate the work you do. Sometimes, I come across an app, or a plugin and it feels like it was made just for me. Haha, but in reality theres some guy out there who had the same needs as me, and decided to write a small program to do the job. That ends up in the repository and I can just click and use it. Thats the philosophy of open source, and what ubuntu signifies. If I can feel that with just one months worth of use then whatever you're doing....you're doing it right.

I am very interested in supporting the ubuntu community. I'm good with computers but have no formal computer education. I would love for someone to tell me where I can learn more about ubuntu/linux specifically learning code, the terminal, and all the technical stuff and how I can input every step of the way.

### Post by MZ250Supa5 on 2011-02-07
I too have found Ubuntu a revelation.  I first started trying it out about three years ago, as I was beginning to become very fed up with the lack of security and bloat in Windows,as well as an increasing feeling Open Source was the more ethical way to go.  After a struggle finding my way around it, getting my various bit of hardware to work correctly I came to value Ubuntu, and for the past two years I have used it as my main OS.  However, the proposed change to using the Unity desktop will quite possibly set me on the road to searching for another Linux distro, as such a desktop belongs on a mobile phone, or possibly a netbook enviroment, but not a desktop computer.  

Unity, as does the forthcoming Gnome3 represents change for the sake of change.  Gnome 2.3 may be old, but it works. Gnome desktop will be an option in 11.04, but for how long will this remain the case? I wish I could say that I was looking forward to 11.04, but the truth is, I am dreading it.  There is nothing broken with the current implementation of Ubuntu, so why is all this effort being put into fixing it?  Maybe 'shiny' desktops look good, but I am personally more attracted by functionality, which Unity certainly isn't as I tried it in 10.10... okay, so it will change, get better, but I shan't be using an interface that belongs on a mobile phone on my desktop.
