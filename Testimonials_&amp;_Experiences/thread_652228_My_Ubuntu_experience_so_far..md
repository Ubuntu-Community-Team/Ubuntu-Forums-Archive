### Post by rafarquhar on 2007-12-28
Where to start?  I'm pretty new to Ubuntu, I started off with Fiesty and I recently upgraded to Gusty, though I haven't noticed all too much of a difference.  Currently, I have Ubuntu set up on a meager 10 gigabyte partiton (with a 2 gb swap partition) with Windows eating up a massive 140 or so gigabytes, making my HDD nearly full.  Since I have about 3 gb of free space, my Ubuntu experience is somewhat impaired, though I hope to be making a more permanent switch soon when I buy a second new HDD, to be partitioned exclusively for Ubuntu.

I have throughly enjoyed Ubuntu so far.  It's very aesthetically pleasing, and it's nice and fast.  I haven't had much experience with gaming so far, but that's only because I don't have enough room on my Ubuntu partition.  From starting with Ubuntu, everything worked exactly how it should.  My only vice is dual-monitor support.  It's relatively annoying to get working, and once it's working, it messes things up (such as playing games like Frets on Fire full screen).  I have disabled the second monitor in Gutsy, but it's still a vice of mine.  I also have a few nit-picky things that I didn't like about Pidgin, but I learned to live with them.  Over my course of using Ubuntu, I have began to boot into windows less and less.

My biggest problem?  MP3 player support.  I have Zen Vision:M and Gnomad2 doesn't work all to well for me, as it's buggy and prone to crashing.  I'm also picky and I prefer to have all my album art and such.  I'm sure I could learn to adapt though, once I have a bigger Ubuntu setup running.  Overall, my experience is great!

I should also mention that I have an old IBM T21 I found (128 mb ram, 800 mhz or so processor) that I set Xubuntu up on, and it works great!  I love it, and I don't even have a copy of Windows.

However, two of my friends have had me install Ubuntu on their machines after seeing mine.  One of my friends machines had a problem with drivers for his USB wireless adapter, and the other can't seem to connect to the internet for some strange DNS issues.  I'll probably just downgrade him to Fiesty though, that might work.

Conclusion?
Ubuntu is a great OS with a couple of bugs and bumps in the road that prevent it from being more or less perfect.  My experience is great so far, and I plan on using Ubuntu more in the future.
