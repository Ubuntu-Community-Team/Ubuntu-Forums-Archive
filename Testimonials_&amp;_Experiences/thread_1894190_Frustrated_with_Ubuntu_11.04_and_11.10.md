### Post by Doughy on 2011-12-12
Dear fellow Ubuntu users,

I'm frustrated with the latest two version of Ubuntu. It's especially disappointing to me to have so many frustrations with the latest versions, as I am typically a huge fan and evangelist for Ubuntu. Here are my complaints.

1) Unity/Dash/whatever is so slow. The whole interface is based around searching for what you are trying to do. Instead of clicking on an application, you are supposed to start typing what you want, and it should come up. Only problem is it takes like 5 seconds for your options to appear. For example, if I'm trying to open my "photos" directory, I type "photos" wait for like 5 seconds, then finally I see something to select. Gnome-Do is so much faster, as things appear almost as fast as you can type. It baffles me that Ubuntu has cooked this right into the operating system, and yet it's slower than a third-party application.

2) Ubuntu 11.04/11.10 is very unstable. My sound crashes a ton when I'm using Unity. The only solution I've been able to find is either rebooting or running a bunch of restart scripts from the terminal. Graphics are even worse. The default 3D version of Unity doesn't even work on my computer, but previous versions of Ubuntu I could run any 3D effect on my desktop and it worked perfectly.

3) The idea of searching for what you want to do is great... if you know what you are looking for. What if you just want to browse through and see your installed applications? Unity is terrible when it comes to browsing. Just to see what games are installed for my son, I have to click the menu, then click the games, then click show more. It's way worse than the traditional nested menu.

If Ubuntu could fix these problems I'd return to my normal Ubuntu evangelism. I just can't recommend it in it's current state.

### Post by spielball on 2011-12-15
I got used to Unity somehow now. But I still miss a lot of things. I love the fact that there is the side panel to launch applications. As most of modern laptops have widescreen, this is nice for those who use a laptop. It saves just so much space on the bottom.

But I can imagine it's not so nice for desktop PC users. There I understand very well that many prefer a top and bottom panel as in Ubuntu 10. Anyway, I hope that Unity will improve. Because some things just annoy me. For instance, the behavior of the global menu. I'd like to "point and click", not "hover, point and click". Who at Canonical had the idea to let the menu items disappear and only have them appear when you hover over them? Doesn't make any sense, and reverts the idea of Fitt's Law into a nuisance.

Also, I really miss more support for "mouse clickers", like I am. I don't want to have to put my hands on the keyboard all the time for the most basic tasks. So why not use the space on the upper desktop panel and put icons there that represent the most used basic things, such as "Show Desktop" (CTRL + Shift + D) or "Zoom Out to See All Windows" (Super + W)? By the way, Super + W isn't a good choice as the default shortcut for zooming out. It happens way too often that users accidentally hold the CTRL key instead of the "Super" key (Windows key), thus ending up closing their current browser window.

Small details like this are what frustrates users. And I hope that Canonical will start to listen to their users and also pick up ideas from the two major operating systems on the market. Honestly, Windows 7 for example, is actually well thought through in many aspects of its user interface. For example, the ability to simply pin and unpin often used folders to the Explorer in the task bar with one single click -- this is actually great, because it gives very fast access and makes the workflow easy. But instead, Unity so far seems to focus too much on fancy effects than on actual usability. And so it's no wonder that it still has so many bugs.

I'd really like to make the complete switch from Windows 7 to Ubuntu. But in its current state, Ubuntu isn't ready yet. However, it's still my top choice among the available distros. Because I tried many  within the last two weeks. Gnome 3 isn't an alternative either, because it's a huge mess on so many levels.  And KDE still sticks to a GUI principle that reminds me too much of Windows 95 times.

Even if Unity hasn't matured yet, I think it brings at least some fresh air into the realm of OS shells, raising a new discussion about how an OS interface should be or not be. I think Canonical made a smart decision when they decided to follow the path of Unity, without getting intimidated by the mass of users who don't want to try some new concept. Nevertheless, they have to be careful not to mess it up. After all, users need usability much more than shiny effects, even though it's also important to have an appealing look. It's like food. There's more appetite when it's served on a nice plate, but when the food itself is rotten, not even a golden plate can compensate this.
