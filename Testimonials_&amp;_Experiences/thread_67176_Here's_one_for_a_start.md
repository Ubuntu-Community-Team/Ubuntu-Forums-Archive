### Post by canadianwriterman on 2005-09-19
So, I see that no one has tred on the virgin territory called the Ubuntu Testimonial Archive. So, let me be the first, if I may.

**Background:**

In many ways, I'm a typical computer user: I've used my PC at home and at work for about 15 years and those PCs have all been loaded with Windows. I'm a professional writer by trade, so I'm basically an "artist-type." However, I'm fascinated by technology and have a burning desire to "tinker," not so much with hardware as with software. I've become increasingly dissatisfied with Windows for a variety of reasons and, when a friend suggested I look at Linux, I jumped at the idea.

**My Linux journey:**

The first Linux distro I tried was Xandros. Xandros is a great distribution. It installed easily and recognized and configured all my hardware: an important thing for a Windows user and someone not yet comfortable with command-lines. Xandros has some drawbacks for me, though. Like many Linux distros, there is limited availability of software through the Xandros repositories and installing software manually is a huge challange for me. Another big issue is the rendering of fonts on the screen. Everything has different sizes and quality of fonts and I went bugeyed tried to tweak and adjust the fonts all over the place. Still, a great distro. A key was that "it felt comfortable" to me. I used the Open Circulation version, which is free and the programs that are on the repository are mostly free. If you want all the bells and whistles, however, you must buy a commercial version.

Next, it installed Linspire. Awed by slick marketing, an easy install which recognized and configured all my hardware, and a pretty GUI, I liked it immediately. It only comes in a commercial version that you need to buy. Linspire has two major drawbacks: most of what's in the Linspire repository, CNR, is only available by purchasing a membership; and, to make it easier for Windows users, it cannot be easily tweaked. In other words, it was too limited for the tinkerer in me.

Next, I installed SUSE. The beta version was freely available to download... on five disks! SUSE was obviously very sophisticated and pre-loaded with a ton of the lastest applications. The trouble was that it was designed for people with a fair level of Linux technical expertise. The install did not recognize everything and I was forced to try to configure even my network connection. I never did get SUSE to connect to the Internet, even after trying everything in the user forums.

Finally, I installed Ubuntu's Breezy Badger preview release. First of all, I was inspired by the whole concept and philosophy of Ubuntu (I check out the Web sites all all my distros before downloading). The install went very smoothly, even though I found the un-graphical install a little daunting. Ubuntu recognized and configured all my hardware. When Ubuntu opened up, I immediately loved the look and feel. The fonts are clean and crisp. With a little exploring of the menus, I was able to do most of the tweaks I wanted. I'm now going to spent the time until the final release in October using Ubuntu to its fullest.

There is something else that is unique to Ubuntu that is critically important for a Linux newbee like me. The community is wonderful, especially the user forums. Most of the forums for other distros have some pretty impatient and unfriendly participants. Yes, many of them are knowledgeable, but they certainly do not share the basic Ubuntu philosophy. I'm also struck by the number of people on the Ubuntu forums. Xandros had very few participants and questions would often go unanswered. I don't know how Ubuntu managed to attract such a large following, but it really makes a difference when you need help, support and answers.

This is the first distro that "feels like home" and that makes me want to participate. I'd love to lend my literary expertise to help Ubuntu prepare the most effective and understandable documentation in the world.

That's my testimonial. I hope it's what you expected when you set up this forum. Now, I invite others to share their experiences, with Linux and with Ubuntu. Take care, everyone.

Stephen in Canada
