Dell made a nifty ultralight desktop a few years back out of their ubiquitous Optiplex GX150. It's a socket 370 machine (up to 1.4Ghz, if you're willing to spend $60 for that chip), has only one connection for a hard drive and uses a laptop CD and floppy to fit it all into a case the size of a dictionary.

This one is a rehabbed machine, recovered from my local recycling center at the ridiculous price of $5. It was beat to hell, but not heavily damaged. (The Ubuntu sticker covers an unfortunate deep gouge that couldn't be erased.)

A 1Ghz processor was swiped from another Optiplex, a 6GB 5400rpm hard drive was scavenged from an old P2 machine and I got seven sticks of 64Mb PC100 for $10 at a charity sale, with two going into this box.

Two cans of Krylon Fusion (red pepper and gloss black) were $9, and a couple of hours on my day off. The results are ... priceless.

### Post by K.Mandla on 2006-08-26
> **bodycoach2 said:**
> Did you do any prep to paint the machine?
Nope, just scrubbed it with dish soap and water. I use a normal scrub brush like you might find at Target or any department store.

The omnipresent black (midnight grey, actually) Dell machine is made of a plastic that has been dyed, not painted overtop. So you can directly spray the casing, once it has been cleaned.

Don't sand it; the texture of the case will be marred. At the same time, don't overpaint it or the excess paint will pool and make a shiny spot. If you get it right you should still be able to feel the texture of the case, and not look glossy.

If you can get the Krylon Fusion, I heartily recommend it. It bonds straight to the plastic and doesn't need a primer. It also dries very, very fast, and I haven't had any problems with chipping or flaking.

Don't bother with a clear-coat; Fusion will dry shiny enough ... as you can see in the pictures. ;)

The most difficult part of this one was getting the plastic casing off the aluminum frame. I actually had to take a rubber mallet to it to free it from the clips -- and use it again to get it back on. It made me sick with worry that I would snap the plastic or break the mounting tabs.

It's not hard to do, really. It takes some time, but it's worth it. And it's tough not to reassemble it right after it's painted. But you must have patience. ... :)

### Post by K.Mandla on 2006-08-26
> **asplode said:**
> Where is this recycling center you speak of, and where can I find one nearest me?
Check your local phone book. Most larger towns have a junkyard or a recycling center that will take old computers. 

In some places the state government is actually required to recycle old computer parts (in other words, take them to a recycling center instead of just tossing them in a landfill). If you can find out where your city-county-state government dumps its computers, you might be able to get some old ones. 

Remember, most government offices are on scheduled budgets and get new computers every few years. So they'll likely toss out perfectly good ones for no reason other than ... they've got new ones.

If you want old machines and are willing to pay a small amount for them, you might try [PublicSurplus.com]("http://www.publicsurplus.com"). A lot of state and county governments got it in their heads to auction their junk off -- and that's a good thing. The downside is most of those places want you to pick them up. And we're talking about PALLETS of old 450Mhz laptops, or PALLETS of beige PowerPC machines. It would require a dump truck to carry some of that stuff home.

Of course, some places might not let you wander around the lots (safety hazards and all that). That's something you'll have to manage on your own.

If you don't have recycling yard or you can't get inside a junkyard to tear apart their old computers, try putting a classified ad in the newspaper. People come out of the woodwork with "broken" computers they don't want any more. 

I have two or three people at work trying to foist P-3 or even P-4 machines on me that have been in the garage for a year, because they wouldn't start or show errors on boot. There's nothing wrong with them a new power supply or a reinstalled OS wouldn't fix. :)
