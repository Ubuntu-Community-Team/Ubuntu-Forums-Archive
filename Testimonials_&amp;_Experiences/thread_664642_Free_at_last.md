### Post by Leetbumble on 2008-01-11
So... I've been a linux fan for a while but had never been able to move fully over to an OS that would meet all my needs.

I built a new computer recently, 

Core 2 duo 2.4
2Gb of ram
8800 GTS 640 MB Nvidia (BEAST)
and a segate 320 GB HDD
all enclosed in a Thermaltake Armor case.

For good 5 months I was forced to have a dual boot with linux and Vista. The system ran fine but to do any gaming, WoW. I had to go to the dark side and use vista. 5 days ago Vista crapped out yet again and I said no more. I logged on to linux and spent a few hours looking at my system and realized I wouldnt be trashing my Ubuntu install just to get windows working again. Spent a few hours on the forums looking at this and that. got the new version of some apps (Wine, VMware) and went to work. 

The easy of use, the security, the power to do whatever I want because the OS is not being gimped by a big brother system, all this and I still get to play some video games. Yes World of Warcraft is up and im looking to get other games going as well. But after the past 5 days I've realized something.

Freedom is not free. To escape the clutches of evil sometimes you have to pull out some of your hair, sometimes you have to "RTFM" and most importantly you have to celebrate when it all comes together.

Linux for life here on out. Thanks Ubuntu team!!!

-Bumble

### Post by mivo on 2008-01-11
Games were the major aspect that kept me chained to the Windows platform for a long time, as well. That, and convenience. When my interest in WoW waned (or rather, when I fully realized how much like chores it had become), and my gaming moved a little to board games and consoles, I dropped Windows. Also, my employer's application was tweaked to run with Wine (I nudged the programmers often enough, I guess!), so nothing held me back.

While the philosophical "freedom" aspect is important to me (the "gratis" aspect not so much, since Windows usually comes with new computers, so for practical purposes it is gratis), what really made the difference to me is the flexibility and quality of Linux in general. I'm more productive (and more happily so) with Linux, and that is why I haven't had Windows installed on any of my machines for quite some time now.

Yes, it comes at a price. It requires effort, learning new concepts, the deternination to find solutions. and the will to earn the freedom and the flexibility. Windows isn't a bad OS. and it is probably a good choice for a great many people. But I think it is limiting in crucial ways and new versions more and more run the user instead of being run by the user. It is also Microsoft's "visions" that I disagree with (DRM, Trusted Computing, etc.). The only way to "protest" is to remove myself from their audience.
