### Post by Docaltmed on 2010-02-25
Yesterday was a wonderful day.

It marked, after slightly more than a year of transitioning, the day that my company began to run on an all-Ubuntu IT infrastructure.

Yesterday, we conquered the last remaining nut -- the one that sticks in the craw of every small business trying to achieve the cost benefits of Ubuntu-based computing. 

We finally found/installed/implemented a small business accounting system. And I can't tell you how pleased I am to get away from Intuit. Nobody is really talking much yet, but it is pretty clear that QuickBooks 2010 is a train wreck. It takes so much computing power, is so bloated, is so slow, and doesn't really do anything more than previous versions of QB! Worse than bloatware, it is hogware. On my relatively recent AMD hardware with 3 GB of memory and processor galore, the thing routinely took 6+ minutes to boot. And that's after I turned over the computer to the tender mercies of Intuit support for -- get this -- 5 1/2 hours! Then I spent another 2 hours on the phone with them trying to make it work. Even after all of that, the slightest transaction takes 30 seconds to complete. It was awful.

I only really kept with QuickBooks for two reasons: 1. I couldn't find a decent replacement, and 2. My accountant had a hissy fit on me when she found out I was thinking about ditching it.

I was desperate. My $200 QB upgrade had blossomed into a $3,000 barely-useable clustermess that my bookkeeper could barely use. Over the course of 36 hours, I searched, read reviews and tested a slew of linux accounting software packages.

Yesterday morning at 6 a.m., I installed Appgen MyBooks Pro. By noon, the company's chart of accounts was created, and all of the necessary reports were printed out of QuickCrap. At 2 p.m., I made a backup for the accountant and shut down QuickJunk for the last time. At 5 p.m., my office manager smiled for the first time in two weeks, nodded her head and shut down her computer.

Today we go live with Appgen. Yeah, I know it's got some shortcomings, but it will be head and shoulders better than having my financial data, the core of my business, owned and trapped by an outside agency who couldn't care less whether my company lives or dies. Yeah, I know it's not FOSS, but lets' face it -- I'm in business and I understand the value of things. I am willing to pay the comparative miniscule amount that this software cost me in return for a sky-high ROI. 

So today I walk into an office that is FREE from an operating system that had me on a never-ending hardware chase just to keep the monitor lit. It is FREE from having to live by corporate policies that were not decided by me, the owner, but were enforced upon me by an alien IT infrastructure. 

I want to give one big huge THANKS to the Ubuntu community. Frankly, if I've got a problem, I'd rather spend a little time poking around here and solving it myself than turning my cursor over to somebody's tech support for a half-day. You guys are a lot more fun.

I'm a small-but-growing outpatient alternative medicine facility. Everything is Ubuntu, either 9.04 or 9.10. Now that I got rid of QB, almost everybody is equipped with netbooks or "nettops" because that's really all the computing power we need. Ubuntu and FOSS are so light that only two computers in the building are *not* Atom processors, and those computers aren't exactly high-end, either.

For a small, under-10-employee business, this setup works wonderfully. For years I struggled with XP and its networking issues -- now printer and file sharing is seamless. For another year, I struggled with Vista problems, and that's what sent me over the edge into Ubuntu.

I even have FOSS that allows us to view and store radiology images. I'm using a great electronic medical records system. Everything is now humming just like it's supposed to, and I can quit spending 10 hours out of my week pretending that I know computer stuff.
