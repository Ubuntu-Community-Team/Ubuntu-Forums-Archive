### Post by MattDTownsend on 2006-08-10
Hey hey, kids,

I've been a Linux user for around eight years and a serious (uni-boot) user for four years.  And I've been around the block.  I remember and miss Enlightenment (The Hand of God is why my then juvenile mind was drawn to Linux).  I always have at least one xterm open, and I do file management by console.  In other words, I am a serious dork.

I went from Windows 98 to Redhat 5 to Slackware to Redhat 6 to Slackware to Windows 2000 to  FreeBSD to BeOS to SuSE to Mandrake to Fedora to SuSE to Mandriva to SuSE to Ubuntu.  Along the way, I also tried Debian (and it has always flunked on install because of my hardware), Gentoo, Conectiva, and a few others.  I swore off Windows long ago – when I bought a laptop three years ago, XP was on it for 30 seconds (longest I ever owned it) – and never looked back.  The end of dual-booting was some time around the first SuSE.

Linux was on my desktop when “Linux on the Desktop” belonged in the realm of chimeras and minotaurs.

Having spent years as a mythical, hypothetical, non-existent user, I must confess that my switch to Ubuntu does not represent an earth-shattering conversion.  I have not recently discovered that I need not fight viruses and spyware, and nor have I found that stealing software is now deprecated – and what a wonderful realization to have!  I found those out years ago, when The Gimp was still crappy, KDE wasn't bloated and XMMS looked like Winamp (and Winamp looked like Winamp).  If I was contented then – when I used FreeBSD as a desktop – you can imagine the sheer ecstasy I feel now.  I always learn quickly when I'm desperate, and I've always been desperate to ditch Redmond.

I've no doubt in my mind that Linux is where it's at, and I have benefited greatly from it – as all of you newcomers will.  You will never give more to Linux than it will give to you.  I went through my entire undergraduate experience without losing a single paper due to computer failure.  I never had my OS mysteriously croak on me in an exam week, and although Linux and X11 weren't always free of problems, there was always some recourse, some alternative.  In Linux, Murphy's Law is suspended.  And that's pretty damned cool.

* * *

Ubuntu is the best distribution I have used to date.  Like Debian, it has the excellent dpkg system.  And unlike Debian, it installs on my hardware without panicking.  I am happy to use an OS where real package management exists, and now, I can see no looking back from Ubuntu.  Additionally, it never hurts to be greeted in your new OS installation by Nelson Mandela.

The ease involved with maintaining and expanding Ubuntu means that I don't have to compile programs and the kernel from source anymore.  But this is almost trivial, as Ubuntu's elegant simplicity gives me the confidence to do something I never have before – to spread the Good News of Linux to people who could not run other distributions.  I am actively trying to convert several friends from Windows to Ubuntu and am gaining ground – I should install Ubuntu on my best friend's systems this week.  See, I used to think it irresponsible to install Linux onto someone's system if they don't, can't comprehend.  How will they get software if they can't walk into K-Mart to buy it?  How will they manage upgrades?  What happens if they encounter an error?

Ubuntu answers most of my questions.  The improvement in open source software answers many others – and yes, many open applications are better than their pricey competitors.  Remaining concerns are addressed by a simple truth: the proliferation of malware (because of Redmond's irresponsible lack of outward security) and the full-tilt adoption of paranoia-ware (because of Redmond's unconscionable obsession with inward security) means administering a windows system has become unbearably difficult.  To be honest, I had been out of the Redmond world for so long that I had no idea how bad it had gotten.  And I thought Windows 98 was a steaming pile!

Okay, yeah, Windows 98 was a steaming pile.

Anyway, I can now sing the praises of Ubuntu on-high, and I will do whatever I can to indoctrinate others.  When a windows user comes crying to me about their inoperable system, I won't turn them towards re-installations or pirated,  six-year-old operating systems.  Today, there is a new option.

I've always known that Linux will take over the desktop.  With Ubuntu, I see a distribution that will not only play a part in that process.  I suspect it will be solely responsible for it.

MT
