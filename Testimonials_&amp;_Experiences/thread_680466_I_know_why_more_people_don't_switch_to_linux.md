### Post by aysiu on 2008-01-28
No, you're absolutely right about taking time to get to know a new OS.

However, there's also the additional time it takes to *unlearn* the old OS.

Someone who knows nothing about computers would find it far more intuitive to go to Applications > Add/Remove to add and remove programs than to go out and do an internet search for a program, find the download page, download the .exe file, double-click it all the way through some "wizard," and then reboot. ### Post by ibuclaw on 2008-01-29
This is probably one post too late...

But we all do feel for you!
Personally I learned GNU Linux from the Debian Terminal up... and a good solid 3 Months of it.
I have recently just bought a new motherboard, removing my WinFast N15235 with a slightly more superior (okay, it only has an included PCI-Express port) ECS GS7610 mATX (bundled with an Athon64 3000 for £39.99 from amazon, but this isn't an advertisment...)

Since, I have noticed an immediate change. For starters, I have installed Ubuntu on my machine and there is nothing wrong with it and/or nothing that will make me switch back to the *hint* *hint* 'very conservatively' stable Debian/Etch.

I have taken the liberty to look up the card for you.
And here is a (hopefully) user friendly tick-sheet for you to do:

 1) Always check for compatibilty and drivers. This can be found easily just by googling 'linux wireless support'. But for a more direct answer, I recommend [starting here.]("http://linux-wless.passys.nl/") This site has an extensive compatibility guide (in three easy to read colours....)

Now I know your card is a Linksys  WMP54G, on the [site]("http://linux-wless.passys.nl/query_part.php?brandname=Linksys") it says there are two different chip models that the card could be, now this is for you to figure out using a little hardware knowledge (neither Windows or Linux specific...)
Now I have a Netgear WPN311 Version 1.2
I gathered this evidence by reading the back of the Wireless Adapter (the underside) and in clear writing the words "WPN311 V1H2" were inscribed on it.
Now it may be slightly different for Linksys cards, but the same principles apply when looking for it.
Look for a "WMP54G" signature on it. There are about 4 different variant models.
1) WMP54G (no version number or V1)
2) WMP54G V3
3) WMP54G V4
4) WMP54G V4.1


 2) Now acquiring the drivers is needed, this for the moment requires a hardwired Ethernet connection.

  a) The Versions 1 and 3 have a Broadcom chip in with a BCM43xx driver, so for the driver, presuming you still have Ubuntu Gutsy, look up this very handy [Ubuntu WifiDoc.]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/Driver/bcm43xx/Gutsy") It's basically a step by step tutorial.
