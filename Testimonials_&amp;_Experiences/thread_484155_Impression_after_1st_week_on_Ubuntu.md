### Post by nunnst on 2007-06-25
I've been running Ubuntu's distro of GNU/Linux for a full week and have many thoughts about it, most not original, I'm sure.  Bottom line - Thumbs Up!

First a little background.  I'm not a programmer nor a geek.  I spent 20 years in the technology industry mostly with NCR Corp. in sales/customer relationship roles and, as such, have a pretty good understanding of micro computers.  However, I'm not really a power-user, perhaps a notch or two below that level.  

Why did I decide to try GNU/Linux? Well, to keep a long story long, it all started when my old Compaq laptop began to literally fall apart.  The LCD was non-functional and had been out for some time.  The battery had long been defunct and the final straw came when the CD drive died. So computer shopping I went.   At that time Apple had just released the Macs with intel chips and OS X.  So, I checked them out, and loved them.   Not only is the visual design of the hardware beautiful, but, OS X was just wonderful.

I almost bought the Mac mini (not sure it was called that then), but I prefer laptops and just couldn't afford a MacBook.  So I bought an HP Dv8210 laptop - reasonable price, nice screen, and easy to move my old MS software.  But damn, I really wanted OS X.  Oh well, that would just have to wait.  I already had an Windows XP license on my old machine, but live in a small town and couldn't find a hardware platform that didn't come with XP pre-installed.  So I bit the bullet and bought the HP w/ XP pre-installed.

A year passes and I still want OS X and I'm still poor.  So I decide if I can't have the real thing, I'll try and simulate it.   I spend several days making my XP look and feel like OS X - all the while trying not to spend my meager funds for it.  I ended up with a GUI that looked quite a lot like a MAC, but was still XP underneath.  Frankly, I wasn't satisfied.  In hind sight, I wasted a ton of time, but I would have wasted it any way watching TV, so no big loss.  I learned a good deal of new things about operating systems and GUI's in my misguided effort to simulate OS X.   I could have gotten a much better simulation of OS X's look and feel by buying WindowBlinds or Style XP software, but I would have still have XP so why bother. 

In my view, Microsoft built a an OS that had a GUI that was flexible, but they didn't really include the tools to take advantage of it.  This led to more thoughts about Microsoft and how they have become to the desktop world what IBM was (and still is, in many ways) to the data center (mainframe/mini-computer) world.   The bottom line, Microsoft has gotten too big for its own britches. To the desktop world they are the big-bully and I don't much like bullies.

I decided that I wasn't going to play with the bully any more (particularly after seeing the prices and bloated requirements of Vista).  There had to be an alternative to Microsoft on the Intel platform.  If Apple could use a Unix base on Intel architecture with a GUI like OS X then I was sure some non-US company had a _*[COLOR="Red"]reasonably[/COLOR]*_ priced Unix-like OS for the Intel platform with a decent GUI that would give me some, if not all, that OS X offered.  Time to do a little research!

Having been out of the systems sales side of the industry since 1996, I wasn't really aware of GNU/Linux or BSD.  I was **[COLOR="Blue"]shocked[/COLOR]** to find [COLOR="Red"]**[COLOR="SeaGreen"]FREE[/COLOR]**[/COLOR] Operating Systems that not only supported the Intel CPU and most of the other system hardware components as well.  A bit more research, and I decide on GNU/Linux over BSD and specifically pick Ubuntu.  

Well, I love it.  Easy to install, bunches of free software, multiple GUI options, stable, and affordable.  What more could you ask for?  

[LIST]
[*]How about totally free?
[*]How about robust?
[*]How about an involved community that freely supports the distribution?
[*]How about no blue screen of death?
[*]How about not being nickeled and dime'd to death?
[/LIST]

Did I mention it being free?  I still don't fully understand the business model of free open source software.  If a whole bunch of really smart people want to collaberate to develop an excellent software product and then give it away, who am I to argue with them?  Perhaps, the capitalistic culture of the US is too engrained in my mind to comprehend the free part of open source .   If  anyone has read this far without being bored to tears and cares to help me understand the business model of free software, then post a response! 

Anyway, I'm pleased with the ditribution to put it mildly.  Sure I had a few bumps in the road and still do, but the same holds true for my experiences with Microsoft products.  

I have read a few complaints about having to use the Command Line Interface (CLI).  I know I have used the CLI in Windows from time to time and fail to understand why Linux is any different than Windows in that regard.  Is it because the available commands in Linux are so much more robust that those in Windows? 

Some people complain about the free applications not being as powerful as their commercial counterparts in Windows. Most comments I've seen like that are referring to The Gimp.  Not being a user of that type of application I can't comment specifically, other than to say not every piece of software meets everyone's needs.  That is _not_ the OS's fault.  Linux certainly has the ability to support just about any application running on Windows.  

If you must have some specific features not available in Linux applications then stay with Windows, but, don't blame Linux because the owner of the software has not ported to Linux.  With open source application software it is naturally going to be more general in nature (for several reason beyond the scope of the post) until such time as it gains enough of a strong hold for a commercial developer to spend the money to port it.  And Linux is rapidly approaching that level based on what I've read.

The same holds true for the hardware side. Certain hardware will not be supported by Linux until the manufacturers (or someone else) writes the drivers for the Linux environment.  Vista does not support every single peripheral currently running on XP/98/95 and the same applies to Apple's OS's,   My point is, an OS is what it is, nothing more and nothing less.  If it matches your requirements, then use it.  If it doesn't, then don't use it.  But for goodness sake, don't blame the OS for issues beyond it's control.

Have I mentioned yet that I didn't have to pay even a penny for this OS and application software?

OK, time to get off my soapbox.  Ubuntu's GNU/Linux has exceeded my expectations by far and I would like to thank all of the people that have worked so hard to make it what is today and for then giving it away for free.  Now, if I could just find a free dual-core system with a couple of free 21" displays.

### Post by rillip on 2007-06-25
Well, Ubuntu doesn't really have a business model, as such.  It was started by a really, really, really rich guy and is still in the gaining momentum phase. It did just partner a deal with Dell to have Ubuntu preinstalled on it's computers, but I'm not aware of any monetary gain in this.  There might be, I just don't know what it is.

But as for open-source in general, most of it is, as you say, really  smart people just donating their time because this is something they like doing.

Not all of open source is free, however.  For example, Red Hat really pioneered a space for Linux as a comerical product by essentially offering their version for free, but making you pay for services.  Not just their support services, but for example, you have to pay to download their user manual. I believe you have to pay and signup to use their repositories as well.

Other programs, like Cedega, have a monthly fee for connection to the service, for updates and such.  This solves the hassle of trying to do a difficult reconfiguration of a program after a kernel upgrade or something of the like.

Glad you're liking Ubuntu, welcome to the community!
