### Post by Sorivenul on 2008-10-23
I joined the Ubuntu Forums one year ago.

Previously I had had experience with Slackware and Debian, but after reading an article about Ubuntu, I decided to try it.  

My first install was Kubuntu 7.04, which was honestly a nightmare, especially for being part of a distribution designed to be easy to use.  I quickly switched to the GNOME release of Ubuntu within a week, and was pleasantly rewarded.  After Debian, Ubuntu was familiar territory, and I had very few issues I couldn't resolve myself, and anything I had a problem with was easily resolved with a search here.  Today I use a custom Ubuntu install, amongst other systems.

Historically, I haven't frequented many forums, and for some time Ubuntu was no exception.  However, after another year of further exploration, I've firmly decided that no matter which distribution I'm booting, Ubuntu's forums are the place for me.  I find the people here to be both the most helpful, and easiest to help, of any community online.  The numbers of familiar "faces" I see when browsing or posting in various subforums is a tribute to the devotion of many members.

I've had a few friends show an interest in Ubuntu, and they always ask, "So, what all do I need to know to get started?"  I always tell them, "Just try the LiveCD, call me if you have problems installing, and the forums are your friend."  A few have chosen to switch, and I applaud them.  As to those who haven't, I always like to know why not, not so I can pressure them, but for ideas about what could make the transition easier.  The conversation usually ends with me saying, "Did you use the forums?"  And the answer being overwhelmingly, "No."  While that's each person's own choice, I think it is important to fully explore any software used, especially support options. 

Even beyond support, I would say that the word done by the Ubuntu Forums community is as important as the work done by the developers behind this great project.  Indeed, this community is proof that Ubuntu is "Linux for Human Beings".

Peace.
