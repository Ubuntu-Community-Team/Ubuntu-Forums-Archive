### Post by hiway on 2007-07-16
I had wanted to set up a system for my daughter who is a higher functioning special needs adult with an easy to use bombproof wireless laptop to surf around the house on... well, we all know "bombproof" is an arbitrary term.

I have experience in the IT field. I am a hardware technician by trade. I knew absolutely nothing about Linux. I had an old Dell Latitude C610 pentium 3 with 512k of ram and a brand new 80 gig hard drive and I was considering buying a version of xp home to use as an OS. After an evening of damage control and thinking about money lost on a system that was sure to get destroyed on a regular basis by the absolute sheer curiosity of my daughter and her willingness to explore a computer in ways most people wouldn't care to ( she found cmos on one system and locked me out because of forgetting the password- had to pop the battery and start over!)- I decided to investigate alternative OS options. After much research and weeks of discussion with a whole host of geeks from the far corners of geekdom... I chose UBUNTU. Hect, it was free, and it would only cost me some time to install it and try...

I spent the better part of 3 to 4 days attempting to install the UBUNTU OS on that laptop. I was frantic, angry, disheveled, bug-eyed, confused, and most assuredly out of my element; but in the end, and with the wonderful help of this community, as well as every single other "penquin-head" I asked, I was able to load it. The thing is, there are a thousand references to the "alternative OS" in all I read when making the CD's, and in the end I was trying to load the wrong version repeatedly... in other words, the reason I spent 4 days was due to user error! My fault! MEA CULPA!

The laptop is configured with the latest version (Fiesty Fawn) and I bought a Netgearwireless pcmia card that works perfectly "out of the box". The desktop is smooth, the graphics are awesome, the use and ease of administration allows me to control my daughter's access to sensitive areas of the laptop... it is an admin's dream!

I write this testimonial because I have turned over the system to her, and I have not had to do a single fix, correction, or any administrative control for almost a month now. I am so impressed, so astounded, so unbelievably grateful... I actually have tears in my eyes. This is the best gift she has ever received (It was an 18th birthday gift) and because of the hard work of the UBUNTU community, and Linux knowledgable people out there, and especially the generosity that is so prevelant, I want to thank all of you for giving me the tools to provide her with that gift.

You all ROCK! ... And Penquins RULE!:guitar:

### Post by JSpaceCadet on 2007-08-03
Hi there hiway!
My son also has aspergers/high functioning autism.  He is 12 and will be entering the 6th grade next week.

First, I'll just say that I am disabled and on some meds that make me a bit slower that I was a few years ago.  I am at home and trying to do things I enjoy - that's important for improvement.  Nuff said.

At the end of the last semester, I learned about Edubuntu - the educational distribution of Ubuntu.  I played with it for a few days and thought it would be nice to offer some computers to the other autistic kids in his class.  I have 21 computers to work with.  I set aside 3 for my kids (that were previously running Fedora Cord) and 1 for me to use as a reference PC.  That left 17 "Computers for Kids" (that's what I've called the project).
I've been technical all of my life - a computer tech for 4 years and a software engineer for more than 12.  I know my way around computers.  I've been playing with UNIX and Linux for years.  I've coordinated and participated in small to medium size projects.  In other words I'm no dummy.  I thought ;-)
I broke my cardinal rule from the beginning.  I didn't come up with a reasonable plan, estimate time for bugs and problems, documentation, etc.  I thought the project would be a breeze.
I let the teachers at 4 schools know what I was doing so they could get word out to the parents.  In the district I'm in and some of the others, most of the parents aren't well-off.  The kids don't have computers.  But I'm sure you know, these kids love computers and just thrive on learning to use them.
I started the project just at the end of May.  I started to get word on who would want the computers shortly after.  Oh, I also let the local Autism Foundation and one of the special-needs organizations know.
The computers are all Compaq DeskPro's of various models.  All are P-3's.  Most are 600MHz, but I also got some up to 933MHz.

I started by working with the PC I picked for myself.
I had to go through a huge troubleshooting effort to find, in the end that Edubuntu wouldn't install on most of the systems because the hard drives weren't configured for Cable Select.  How bizarre.  Then systems with 2 CD-ROMs were a problem.
My pick of PCs turned out to be a bad choice because the system didn't recognize the network card.  I got out my box of cards and found one that worked.  I had problems with the display as well.  Eventually I began working with one of the other PCs.  It's worth mentioning that the PC I picked initially was a different model than the rest of the 11 PCs I have on the rack now.  The thing that worries me is that I have a closet full of PCs just like the first and I'm worried I won't be able to get them to work.
Anyway, for the last few weeks, I've been working on building a configuration script that will set the boxes up the way I need them set up.  Almost all of that time has been put into trying to find a way to build up the multimedia layer so that it works.  The kids need to be able to surf their favorite web sites and have the myriad types of videos and graphics actually work.  That has proven to be a very, very difficult task.  I have it close, but it's still not there.  I say I've worked on the script for weeks, but I've also worked on a lot of notes - some of which have been documented in the web pages I list below.

I worked in the Interactive Television world for the better part of 10 years.  I know what it means to have to install a box (cable box, PC, whatever) and it just work - period - and not break.  In this situation, most of the parents are not literate enough an not willing to learn to troubleshoot problems.  So if the computers don't work, I'm worried that they will get shut off and the kids will once again go without.

Last weekend I decided to bite the bullet and build up the computer for my son - the one with autism.  I also know that  a good way to work out the bugs is to let someone find them.  Besides, he had been bugging me for weeks to give him back his computer ;-).  I got it up with not too much trouble.  My install script worked pretty well, but the multimedia still isn't there.  A lot of the RealVideo feeds don't work - and they're all over the 'Net.  A big problem is that NASA uses RealVideo a lot on their multimedia page - and those don't work.  Most of the autistic kids I know are space fanatics and spend a fair amount of time at nasa.com to watch the movies.  They get to do that at school.  Well, if those don't work at home you can imagine the frustration of the kids and the parents and worst of all the meltdowns.  Maybe those would end, but I would rather then not even begin.

I'm using Procom Alba (I think it's Procom Latte or something like that now) for web parental control.  It's simple and rule-based.  I have it set up where he can go only to sites that I have put on the "white" list.  That means if he wants to go to a new site, like bioniclestory.com, he has to ask and I have to whitelist it if I want to allow it.  As an aside, there are a ton of lego videos on YouTube, but they will not respond to my requests to come up with a solution whereby kids will have a separate section so you can whitelist it.  If I give him access to YouTube now, he gets Lego's and porn - not acceptable.

Well, this ended up a lot longer than I intended.  I was inspired by your post and applaud you for putting it out there.  As for me, I'm just hoping that I can get this figured out soon as I have grown tired and frustrated and am getting discouraged.  My idea to help special-needs kids is a great one I think, but the effort was beyond what I could have imagined.

That said, I am ever-grateful to all on the Ubuntu forum and other sites as well for sharing so much information.  The xUbuntu products are going to thrive in the world I think - and already are in Europe where there are large scale deployments.  Reading about that is what inspired my project.
I am also grateful for everyone working on the xUbuntu projects and products.  That goes for the Free and Open Source folks too of course.  Without them, these wonderful tools wouldn't be available.

I hope someone else will read our posts and come up with and implement a plan, maybe like mine, definitely like yours so that we can get computers into the hands of these brilliant people who are so often not considered for such tools.

Once again, I applaud you.
