### Post by DMK62 on 2008-04-29
Hi

I have been reading the majority of the posts in the last week in regards to Hardy Heron and I have seen complaint after complaint about the new release.

I have been with Ubuntu since Breezy and I am not surprised that there are problems with Heron. Breezy worked ok with a few freezes here and there, Dapper was rock solid after the first couple months, Edgy absolutely refused to work, Feisty Fawn was the most stable of them all, and Gutsy was stable but I had problems with it. Not every release is going to play nice with your hardware / config. Wireless has and will continue to be a problem as long as the hardware vendors are not open to developing with linux in mind. The same can be said ( and has been said thousands of times on this forum ) for most categories of hardware. My best advice is before upgrading or doing a clean install is to do some searching on the forum for your hardware and see how many problems are being encountered by other users. If there are workarounds or fixes for your hardware then make notes and it will save you a lot of grief when you upgrade.

Having seen the same types of posts and complaints about every Ubuntu release I think there is a heightened level of expectation for each new release. Users are expecting / demanding that absolutely everything works right away and unfortuneatly that is not the reality of linux or any other operating system. If your current install is working well and is still supported then I would stay with that version unless you really need the latest and greatest ( often hard not to be tempted by it ).

I did try the beta's of Ubuntu and Kubuntu 8.04 and had mixed results. The Ubuntu install went without any errors until I attempted to log in and was promptly informed that I had no Home partition and my Mozilla folder had permissions set only to root. With Kubuntu I had my Home partition but the new jockey-kde refused to set my video drivers to enabled status. I knew both of these versions were beta's so I was not surprised when I encountered these problems. Even though Heron is in final release I don't expect there not to be problems at first. When you use something that is close to the edge you have to expect to get bit now and then.

Also there seems to be the impression that LTS means one and only one thing, stability. That stability takes time to mature over its 3 yr life cycle. Dapper took a couple months before it was stable. What I think is a much more important part of a LTS release is that the software / apps are not updated that often and there is a reduced chance of breakage. If it works don't try and fix it with something new. It just takes a while for some of the apps etc to get to that stable level and then only minor revisions along the way.

Check back here in 6 months and there will be similiar posts regarding Intrepid Ibex. Hopefully there will be a minimum of trolling, flaming, and personal attacks.

Dale

### Post by DMK62 on 2008-04-30
Hi

I do agree with your point on each release improving on the previous release. Most users that have used Ubuntu for a couple years or more will know about the advancements that have been made to Ubuntu with each release. 

What I was attempting to get at was many users were expecting something that Hardy Heron or any other operating system could not deliver. Maybe some people get caught up in the hype and excitement around a release and are disappointed when it does not deliver 100 %. 

Some major and minor bugs are still carried over to Heron from previous versions. I am sure the devs would love to see those bugs gone for good. Fixes are not always easy to implement as fixing one issue can cascade into breakage in other parts of the system.

The alpha and beta releases do go under a lot of testing by the devs and are assisted by users that submit bug reports. Something that I did think about which might actually help is a group of users with a wide variety of hardware and software needs and levels of experience be placed into a special testing group. It might require more work on the part of the devs but they could have that group submit bugs, error reports, feedback etc into a separate database that could amongst other things be used to isolate trends in issues encountered by that group.
