### Post by wizarddrummer on 2010-08-26
Recently I put up a thread cautioning people about ext4 because I had some bad experiences with it.
   
  Well, it turns out ext4 is more robust than I gave it credit for.

However, that being said, ext4 does misbehave a little more than ext3 and NTFS when you do not have great hardware.
   
  One day, after shutting down the machine, I could not boot up again.
   
  Every indication; every cryptic message on that machine that came up in the terminal window, during the fsck operation, was saying to me that there was corruption in the file system and the corruption was so bad that it could not be fixed.
   
  Then the flood of messages came from the thread. I like the one about the fact that Google uses it and the other one where this poster, that had ZERO empathy for me and  my lost data, ragged on me that I should not have put so much of my past experiences in the post and that it should be something easy to fix.
   
  I wish I could have teleported him here. After the fraking fsck process gagged and puked all over itself, I wanted to say to him "Hey brainiac, what's your plan now? C'mon, you said it was EASY! Wassamatter? Fresh out of ideas?"
   
  Now, before I continue, I will say this: IF you can afford decent hardware and a UPS then ext4 is probably the FANTASTIC file system that's just right for you.
   
  However, if you have a pile of crap machine, with no money to replace it; a machine that has a bad battery, some small BIOS problems (not finding hard drives at POST sometimes) and no UPS then &#8230; ext4 is probably NOT the best option you could choose. I had another hard drive with XP installed (on the crappy machine) I've had to do a few chkdsk's on it and it has never cratered like the ext4 system did on that machine.
   
Cheers!

I was able to save some data. When I took the hard drive out of the crap machine and put the drive INTO a different machine that I am borrowing to see if I could try and fix it, I was able to complete the fsck operation. I did lose almost 500 files, but the important stuff (the databases / other source files) were still intact.
   
  So there you have it.
   
  Considering ext4 on the next install or upgrading to it?
   
  I say GO FOR IT!
