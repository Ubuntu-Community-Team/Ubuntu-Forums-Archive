### Post by Sophont on 2009-05-01
Self-selecting polls are utterly worthless.
People with no problems are less likely to see the poll to begin with (because they read the forum less) and those with problems are more motivated to vote.

Like with any release of any OS there are some people with big problems and many with small ones.
A linux distribution is an insanely complicated beast even without the wide variety of hardware combinations to consider.

Of course - more Q&A would have reduced the number and severity of problems. OTOH what about those who had problems with the prior release and are waiting eagerly for the fixes that the new version brings?
At some point a cut has to be made - and some will have new issues - while many others get problems resolved.

Plus - the longer Q&A takes the more out-of-date the software base gets.
Ubuntu has a fixed 6 month release cycle. That has some advantages and disadvantages - like any other release method.

If the new version breaks something - stay with the old one until the issues get fixed.

The option to run a live system from CD helps a lot or checking before an upgrade wether there are some fundamental hardware problems.

At the end of the day the frustration of those that have serious problems is understandable even though there is no silver bullet to fix this. Accusing devs does not really help. Nor does telling frustrated users to write a better OS. Not very constructive.

MS spent billions of $ and half a decade on Vista - and even with an army of the best programmers wads of money can buy they couldn't deliver an OS that works for everybody with all hardware. Meanwhile another army of underpaid enthusiasts produced a competitive os that provides (IMHO) more shine, security and power (unless you want to play off-the-shelf games on release day) for a much lower price.

Those that have problems with Jaunty have my sympathies. I had to skip Intrepid because of regressions in PPTP VPN support (meahwhile Hardy kept working just fine). Just think of 9.10 as 6 months extra Q&A for jaunty. ;-)

Like clockwork we'll see another version in 6 months that support even more hardware, fixes many old issues and brings new features.

Stay with Hardy or Intrepid (whatever worked best so far), check Jaunty with live CD and then upgrade or wait for the Karmik Koala.

Important: In future research (I mean a few minutes of googling) all hardware before you buy it. If you play games - prefer NVIdia over ATI. Don't buy Lexmark printers until they provide decent Linux support. Etc... Not only will that ensure a low problem rate - but it will also penalize manufacturers that ignore Linux and reward those that support it. Money speaks very lowdly.

### Post by Sophont on 2009-05-05
> **mesmith said:**
> A once a year rock solid release would still be better than every other game in town and put the debugging emphasis back on apps and prductivity.

Not really.

For every release Ubuntu starts with a fresh base of Debian testing packages. Ubuntu patches get re-applied and the testing QA cycle begins. Particular packages get sometimes updated during the testing phase and particular patches will be backported from new up-stream versions or direct bug fixes.
But given that even the version in Debian testing could be months behind the upstream project you might get a 3-9 months old version of a particular application.

If the release cycle were a year then many packages might be 1.5 years behind the originating project.

Besides cool new features you might miss a lot of performance improvements or whole achitectural redesigns that perhaps resolve a whole class of issues.

6 months is a good compromise between decent QA cycles and package age.
If you want a more stable version either use the latest LTS version - or switch to a more conservative distro like Red Hat/CentOS (not Fedora - that's bleeding edge) or go one level up the stream and use Debian Stable.

I want decently up-to-date software and the improvements that brings.
I check for severe regressions before upgrading with a VBox VM setup and/or a live CD session.

Remember that you don't have to upgrade. If on average you get a release with regressions every second release and you avoid them by testing before upgrade with a CD live session then you effectively have your personal 1 year development cycle. But the rest of us still get the 6-month improvemmnts on the many systems where the regression does not appear.
