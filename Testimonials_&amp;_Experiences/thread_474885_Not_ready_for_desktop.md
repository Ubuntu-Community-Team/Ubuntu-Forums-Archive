### Post by lingnoi on 2007-06-15
The problem with operating systems is that they are very personalized. Whats good for one person isn't good for another.

For the majority of the population they web surf, email (normally web based), msn, use an office product and listen to music.

The problem is that in the eyes of the more vocal Windows Power user that spreads his thoughts across the web GNU/Linux will never be ready for desktop use.

Why? Name anything. It could be a specific game. A scanner, the color of the logo, whatever.

What the windows power user is really saying is that GNU/Linux isn't ready for **them**. These people are a lost cause as for them Linux will never be ready even if their specific problems were solved. There will always be something wrong with it. Something less superior.

If you're reading this then you probably know that Ubuntu is "ready for Desktop", you may have known for years now.

Building brands is important and thats why people choose Microsoft it is a more popular brand with better advertising. Thats why for some people "Ubuntu isn't ready for desktop". Its just not popular enough brand as Microsoft.

Even non-power users feel this way. Here is a conversation I had with my girlfriend.

Me, "[jokingly] When are you going to let me install Ubuntu on your laptop?"
Her, "Its too complicated."
Me, "Nonsense you have borrowed and used my laptop thousands of times without my help."
Her, "If it breaks I won't be able to fix it."
Me, "But I fix your laptop when it breaks so whats the difference?"
Her, "..."

There was a negative feeling there which just wouldn't go away. People have lived all their lives on windows and fear anything new.

### Post by MattDTownsend on 2007-06-16
There's no such thing as a Windows "Power User," since the term implies the user has any power whatsoever.  Vista has taken care of that.

vexorian:  I, too, miss the days of Windows 3.11.  I also thought WinNT 4 was heaven.  2000 was usable.  XP was garbage.  And Vista isn't worth addressing, along with any 9X/ME.

It's been my experience that those who think that Linux is ready for the desktop are those who understand computing well enough to install and use it, and those who understand so little of computers that it's installed for them.  I forced my mother to switch to Linux several years ago when she got her first "personal system" -- an ancient inherited piece from a friend.  I put icewm on it and the basics.  Now she's on a newer laptop -- inherited from me -- that's four years old but runs Gnome fantastically.  She does her e-mailing, word processing and bookkeeping on the machine with no difficulties.  She recently asked me why more people don't use Linux because, "Isn't it the same, just, you've got your titlebar with your buttons, you've got Firefox, but free and no viruses."  In four years of her using Linux, she has never once asked me to format/reinstall her system, clean it of anything, stop popups, remove spyware, or resolve the myriad complaints you get from your average Windows user.

Windows has gotten so bad now that, in my experience, the only people who can fix it are serious Linux users.  "Windows Power User," my ***.  Only recently have I started refusing to repair windows installations when they're seriously compromised -- Linux or bust.  So, sometimes the best way to show people Linux is ready for the desktop is to force their hand. :-)
