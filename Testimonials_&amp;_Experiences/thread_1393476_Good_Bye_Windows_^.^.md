### Post by mortalapeman on 2010-01-29
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]I'm not really sure how I made it this long without knowing that Linux was open source, or even that open source existed. I think back to only a few months ago when I first put ubuntu on my laptop that had been running vista with only 1 GB of RAM.... [/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]It literally took me 5 minutes to shutdown that computer and almost as long just to boot it up! I tried everything I knew how to do to speed up the system with only marginal success. As an engineering student, I am all about efficiency. Naturally I was really annoyed with Microsoft for even marketing such a piece of crap as Vista.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]Then one day I just asked myself, because I knew of OpenOffice, "I wonder if there is a free operating system..."[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]Well after doing a Google search, I got results back telling me use Linux free! I had always thought Linux was another OS you had to pay for. I had never even considered Linux something to use on a desktop computer.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]At first I was overwhelmed with choices, Fedora, Mint, Centos, DSL, OpenSUSE, and many others. I wanted an OS that was pleasing to the eye and beginner friendly, and from their descriptions, Ubuntu seemed to be the best candidate.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]Ok let me tell you just how much better Ubuntu was for me than Vista:[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]1. The boot up time was amazing, even faster than my older XP system which was exciting.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]2. Virtual desktops were something I had always wanted and now I couldn't live without.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]3. No more spyware, adware, viruses....yay.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]4. It came installed with almost every bit of software I'd ever need, and no bloatware to annoy me. That, somehow, just seems like the way an OS should come.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]5. The packaging and updating system, I'd never thought of it before, and it just astounded me.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]6. The wireless actually worked with my university internet! On Vista, for some reason, it just stopped working. I could ping sites, but I couldn't get a webpage up on the browser. The IT guys were baffled and said I'd have to leave my comp with them for a week. I said forget that. Ubuntu worked flawlessly "out of the box" and get this; it even fixed my Vista wireless somehow. Later when I logged back in to Vista, I could see webpages again! That&#8217;s what really sold me on Linux.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]After that I was determined to learn everything I needed to know to use Linux. Using the command line was new, but only a minor setback. Google provided me with all the code I'd need just to get started, and most of it came from this forum. This forum has been amazing, the support and answer of questions here is so much better at fixing problems with ubuntu than a Google search on how to fix my windows OS ever was for me.[/FONT][/COLOR]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]My wife calls me a nerd, my friends call me a nerd, but when their windows system craps out they come to me to fix it now. Live CDs are awesome, as I&#8217;m not exactly proficient with the command line yet, and the GUI really makes things more [/FONT][/COLOR]
[FONT=Verdana][COLOR=black][COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]intuitive[/FONT][/COLOR] to work with.[/COLOR][/FONT]
 
[COLOR=black][FONT=Verdana]I've been happily running Ubuntu for several months now and I don't plan to ever use windows again, if I can help it. Thank you to everyone who believes in open source, as soon as I teach myself to program, I plan to promote and support open source as best I can.[/FONT][/COLOR]

### Post by Whistling Nixie on 2010-01-29
And to think that just weeks ago, I thought "live CD" meant a re-issue of *Hot August Night*! Here goes... I noticed my laptop, with its pre-installed Windows Vista, kept freezing up more and more often (and that wasn't the fan: it was turning). And one day, when I booted up, my computer couldn't access the hard drive! Thankfully one of my housemates was willing and able to spend hours on end trying to fix whatever problem it had, and managed to revive the machine.

Having had that scare, as well as dissatisfation with Vista's performance at the best of times, I decided to give Linux a go. Ubuntu was something I'd heard about, although until recently I was unaware that it was an operating system - let alone a worthy replacement for Windows. Eventually I stumbled across some instructions I could understand, in order to download an ISO of Ubuntu, then burn it to a CD. (Fancy that, an entire OS, with office suite thrown in, fitting on a CD!) Installing it was the easiest thing in the world, and it turned out to be, well, nearly as easy to figure out as Vista had been. Initially I had to hunt around the screen to find how to power down, and having the three menus on the top left took a little getting used to. But it's familiar enough now to do the basic things, which is what I'd been doing anyway. Other than an inability to import music from a 5th-generation iPod, this is definitely a step upwards!

### Post by Endomancer on 2010-01-30
When I had the last reformat and reinstall of xp done the guy partitioned my hard drive 10GB in C drive for windows and other programs and 70GB in D drive for files. Then I got an external drive, so I transferred all my files in to that and decided to move all non windows programs into D drive, and due to my ignorance I ended up with a copy of each program in both C and D drives

Needless to say I ended up with a huge mess.

A mess which after two years ran extremely slow, refused to fully de fragment (even after running defrag 3 or 4 times in succession) and required several disc cleans each day using both disc cleaner and C cleaner just to keep going. Basically the hard drive needed reformatting, the partition removed and windows reinstalled.

I had known the existence of Linux and had been curious about it for a few years, but had always thought "what if I didn't like it?". Well at this point I was thinking "it needs reformatting anyway so why not try it and see, if I don't like it I take it to a shop and get windows put back on, nothing to loose"

So with that I did a little bit of research and found that what I thought was one operating system was in fact many variations with which to me seemed to be built around two possible desktop environments, Gnome and KDE (I now know there are more) and Ubuntu seemed to be the most popular distribution that dealt in both desktops so I decided to download both, Ubuntu and Kubuntu and check them out.

After trying both on live cd for a few days I noticed I felt very comfortable with the Gnome desktop and tried installing Ubuntu next to windows as a dual boot, but was unable to due to lack of free space on the hard drive. So I had to take the option of using the whole drive for Ubuntu and hope it would remove the partition in doing so (which it did) and was surprisingly easy to install in this way.

I have now been using Ubuntu for almost 2 months and I am very impressed with it. I am also impressed by the community  of Ubuntu users and how eager they are to help with any problems along the way, of which Iv had a few but nothing that's going to have me running back to windows any time soon.
