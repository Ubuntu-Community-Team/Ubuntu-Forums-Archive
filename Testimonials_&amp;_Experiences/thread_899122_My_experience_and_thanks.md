### Post by moyam01 on 2008-08-24
Ive been using Ubuntu since the first week of Hardy's release, and I have to say that the OS is something that people should seriously begin to look at this as an alternative to windows. (They already have, but I think that when people see that it is not like windows, they begin to run)

I used win all my life (starting with 95). Had a previous experience with Linux (red Hat), and it was horrible. Thinking back on it now, I have to say that I didn't give the OS a chance, and drew my conclusions too early on it (I was using a real old computer for it's time, and I just expected it to work out of the box, because I jumped into it because windows was giving me problems).

This time it was about that time where I had to reformat my pc and re-install windows xp as it was giving me problems again. I am very proficient in windows, but a combination of things like curiosity and rounding my self out as a computer guy (if I was going to take the time to learn a CLI it might as well be powerful), and the no virus thing and not having to re-install the os just took it over the top. This time however, I did a bunch of research to make the transition as easy as I possible, which did help, but not as much as I thought it would. Everything worked out of the box. I had a little issues with the nvidia drivers (it made my video card fan whine severely, but as soon as I switched to the closed source drivers it was fine). Installed office 07 with wine but found it really unnecessary, as open office did the job just fine. Transmission torrent client sucked, so I had wine with utorrent for a while, but then I found Deluge, and it is awesome. Now my desktop and laptop both run ubuntu, My only mistake is that I did not install 64bit, but I think that it will just be too much of a hassle to change. 

A word to new users: Google is your friend. I did have my share of problems to get things to work on my desktop and laptop, but everything is so well covered that I didn't have to post at all.

Right now I'm just learning the CLI as much as I can (tutorials all over the place), and learning to program bash, then I plan to move on to python, then c++ then c, then assembler. I want to be able to contribute to the project as much as I can, because I honestly feel that I owe you guys something.

Last thing, I want to thank you guys. I honestly don't know how you do this, but to make a product that is in my opinion better than a company that charges for it is beyond me. Keep doing what you are doing.

### Post by BlueSkyNIS on 2008-08-24
Enjoy your staying here. :)

With 64bit in addition to increasing the size of the general-purpose registers, the number of named general-purpose registers is increased from 8 to 16. It is therefore possible to keep more local variables in registers rather than on the stack. Similarly, the number of 128-bit XMM registers (used for SSE) is also increased from 8 to 16.
