### Post by Backharlow on 2007-07-01
I have been using Ubuntu since early Dapper, and I can say with complete confidence that I would not still be here were it not for the banter and how to's in these forums. Where I do feel a bit alienated however, is due to this preoccupation with contrasting Ubuntu with Windows. Frankly, I don't care how they compare, but I suspect that from a design standpoint it is dangerously limiting if your only real base for comparison is the very thing you're trying to avoid. I'll hold back on the Freudian quotes here because it isn't constructive, but I will throw this out... If MS is not the father of Linux, why does Linux act like such an angry teen?
I abandoned MS back in the Windows 2000 era, and moved to Mac OS8 and 9, because I needed Quicktime and a system that wouldn't die every six months.
So, migrating gradually to Ubuntu as of late, I have been able to move all of my publishing and communications to Linux, relieving the overhead and general clutter on my Macs. I've also enjoyed configuring and installing software on Ubuntu and really haven't had any major issues to complain about.
What I still dislike is this general assumption that all newcomers to Ubuntu are starving refugees from the Windows camp, who really just want a hot meal without having to pay for it, and have just heard about this thing called Linux.
Personally, I've known about Linux for years. I chose not to use it until now. The main reason I chose to is that it runs on common hardware, and is extremely customizable, which are just the two things lacking in Apple systems, even after you've spent a few hundred dollars on utilities.
What is still holding me back conceptually with this transition though, are the MS Windows-centric approaches to the desktop. I see two areas here...
"Window based," versus "application based" window management, and hotkeys.
Each window is treated as distinct, or discrete or whatever they call it, so that navigation is spammed by ungrouped masses of windows, which is what forces you to use several "workspaces" to contain all this stuff. For example, open six Nautilus windows, and this is no different than opening six different programs. If this were application based, like on Mac, you would have six instances of Nautilus as one easy to hold onto window entry, and I find this to be a much saner approach. I've tried beryl, compiz, metacity.
Hotkeys... 
keyboard shortcuts by default are windows hotkeys. Why in the world would I want to hit <alt>F4 to close a window. The human hand was not engineered to withstand an alt F4, maneuver, even under the harshest of conditions.
My criticism here is not to say that the bulk of new Ubuntu users are not or should not be migrating in from Windows, but that it should not be assumed that everyone is, and the default Ubuntu should not be specially tailored to one particular class of "Ubuntu as a second language 101". Rather it should be more like two or three or four classes, and I think a good starting point would be keyboard shortcut profiles, pre-configured for foreign layouts, like windows, mac, etc, so that if people come in with a committed to memory shortcut set, at least it is a conscious choice for all.

### Post by Backharlow on 2007-07-05
Thanks Ultra Magnus for the contrast. Agreed, web related statistics can be abstract and it is difficult to gauge who is using what from that. 
The Google trend results, while showing high interest in Ubuntu, may be the result of Ubuntu users querying Google for information about Ubuntu much more than Mac users Google about Mac.
Example; if we're scaling this as 1 hit = 1 person, then.. 1 Ubuntu user who executes 30 queries to Google using the word "ubuntu", will create twice as much Ubuntu interest as 15 Mac users who only make 1 query each to Google using the word "mac". So in that example, there is twice as much interest in Ubuntu even though there are 15 times more Mac users- they may just happen to be using Apple.com instead of Google to get information about their operating system.
At least the data used by the Digg statistics shown earlier was taken from the "user agent" code that records what OS is being used by the visitors. Again, the high number of Mac users, could simply mean that Mac users are more interested in Digg than Linux users are, not that there are actually more of them in the wild.
I think that we we need to look to some kind of independent study to get a census with an accurate cross-section.
