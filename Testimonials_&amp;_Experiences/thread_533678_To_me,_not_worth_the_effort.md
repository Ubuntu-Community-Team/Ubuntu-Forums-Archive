### Post by getmeoutofhere on 2007-08-24
Well, I tried Ubuntu on my Inspiron 6400, and all it did was make me appreciate Windows XP Pro.  And I've now scrubbed Ubuntu from my computer.  I cannot see what the point of it is.  It seems to be incredibly difficult to find drivers for Linux, and despite loading Wine I couldn't get Windows programs to work satisfactorily.  Also, only some USB applications worked on my computer - for example I couldn't get a USB microphone to function.

Linux OSs might be fun for people with loads of time on their hands, but for ordinary people, who don't work in the computer field, who nonetheless rely on their computer, Linux seems to me to offer little.  I know, there are lots of open source programs available, but how many of them do you actually need?  

However in a sense I think it's a shame.  Clearly Ubuntu has made a special effort to be user-friendly, but there still seems to be a gap, with particularly emphasis on the hardware support.  

Also, the learning curve strikes me as being too steep.  I had no difficulty move from Dos to Windows - use of Windows was intuitive, and I didn't have to search the Net for explanations of the simplest of things.  But maybe there are some good books on Ubuntu, though I never had to buy books on Windows.

I suppose if I had loads of time on my hands I could find a way of working with Ubuntu, but time has a cost, a cost which I'm not prepared to pay.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2007-08-24
Actually, when you moved from MS-DOS to Windows, it was a very gradual step. Windows 95 still required quite a bit of MS-DOS command-line hacking. Windows 98 required less. Windows XP requires less still. It's a gradual step-by-step that you were taking from MS-DOS to Windows.

Whereas you've thrown yourself into a new operating system overnight. You need time to adjust. This is not Windows. I switched from the Mac to Linux (a bigger step, as the Mac didn't have a command-line) and was alright in a short amount of time because I recognised that Linux wasn't like the Mac.

I think you've missed the point of Linux - the point is not to run Windows programs, it's to run Linux ones. Wine is a last-ditch resort, not a first line of compatibility. If you're disappointed with Wine's performance and compatibility, maybe you should try reverse-engineering Windows for a day.

But I'll admit, you were a bit unlucky. Looks like your hardware isn't well supported out-of-the-box yet. Most people have a good experience, where their hardware works fine without them even having to do anything. On the computer I'm currently using, I just installed Ubuntu and everything worked - the drivers are built-in. 

Maybe you should try the next version of Ubuntu when it comes out, as it will have better hardware support. Maybe somewhere along the way you'll actually realise what Linux is about.

By the way, I hope you don't try to update that computer to Windows Vista - you'll find that fewer of your peripherals are supported.

### Post by DaveAK on 2007-08-27
I've been in a similar situation over the years trying to get into Linux, but never getting very far, mostly through lack of effort.  I've had several dual boot setups, but would always boot into Windows for my established stuff, so Linux never got the time it needed.  I finally got to the point a few months ago where I decided to go all or nothing into Linux.  I learned one important fact, **DON'T** go dual boot. Dual boot was a hassle - boot up into one OS, decide you want to to try something in the other, shut down, reboot, etc.  Now I boot up into Ubuntu, I use OpenOffice and other applications that I'm still learning, but I've forced myself to give them the time by making Ubuntu my first, (and only), system.  If I need one of those Windows programs, I simply fire up the VirtualBox and there they are, while still allowing me to use Ubuntu apps.

It may not work for everyone, but it's certainly what helped me make the switch.
