### Post by grief -l on 2009-12-12
Thank you Ms_Angel_D for your topic above. I'm sorry it's locked but seems that's the way they do things here. I have felt the urge on occasion to make my frustrations known but, until I saw your thread, I thought "what's the use? Who cares?" So, since you obviously care you've given me reason.
 
 
I want to start off by saying that I came to ubuntu because I already have a long and active history in FOSS - Blender, the Gimp and OpenOffice. I'm a writer and illustrator. I already have an OS that works, but the FOSS philosophy is an empty skull unless one commits to it.


First, I downloaded the .iso, did the checksum thing and the CD didn't work. Tried at least a dozen CD's before finding out CD-RW's don't work, you need a CD-R. Nowhere was that mentioned - use a cheap CD - yes, but a cheap CD isn't a CD-R! Anyhow I now had my CD and I was looking at ubuntu! I was using Linux! How cool is that?!!!
I was excited, even though I had no Network. I tried this and I tried that and I typed out my password over and over again, but I couldn't get onto the net. Maybe if I install Wubi? Still no Network but hey - maybe just one more place needed my password and I'd be accepted into the wonderful world of Linux? Frantically I uninstalled Wubi and came back to this forum all in the same bated breath. I was so captivated I installed my wonderful Karmic Koala right alongside my trusty steed XP.
 
Alas, still no network. The dongle's light didn't even come on. So I rebooted into XP and found an active thread about WUSB54GC v3 and there it was! The solution to my problem - the Console! And some kind folks who seem to know more than the angles above had printed out commands that civilised folk are totally unaware of - and in a neatly packaged format that could just be copied and pasted into the dark, black depths of that magical console! What magic!
 
At one point in the copied procedure (which I could steal right out of the bowels of XP's C drive amazingly enough) it said "Reboot!" Well I'd done that more than a few times so I could do it while feeding the cat already. Ye Gads!!!! Suddenly my 1024 X 768 was gone and I had been banished to the realm of 800 X 600 with no way to get back! Even the panel to choose a resolution didn't fit on the screen and I couldn't see the "Apply" button. It disappeared off the bottom of the screen and must have been somewhere in the virtual space between the desk and the cat. 
 
Anyhow, I had three more commands yet to feed the console (and three more passwords) and hopefully I'd be able to get onto the ubuntu forums using ubuntu. How cool would that be?
 
No way! So, back to XP where I could actually use the damn computer to do the things I bought it for - research. I had to post my lsusb -C network, my ifconfig and my iwconfig and some other vital statistics that, if said out loud sound positively obscene, but no one could tell me what "Error E" meant. My enthusiasm had turned to scepticism and I finally decided that 50 bucks for another dongle or a wireless card was a better investment than playing Packman with the code and risking something worse than having to deal with an 800 X 600 blob of orange used space.
 
To get back the code I'd either lost or confused I reinstalled. Well, not really. See, when you get to the manual install option (as opposed to the wonderful automatic dual boot one I went with the first time), it seems the interface was written by Nostradamus himself. I've been in the military and have filled out tax returns all my adult life and never yet have I seen anything as cryptic as that interface. "Log in automatically" - research tells me "if you click this you will automatically be logged into the ubuntu desktop." Does that mean no more passwords? Ya well, no fine! I can dream, can't I?
 
I live 34 miles from the nearest town. I don't work in an office. I don't work for the government. I have no sensitive information on my computer. The only one who uses this computer - EVER - is me. The only one who can do any damage to the software and files is... ME! It doesn't matter whether I have typed out my $*&#! password a thousand times, if I am forced (and advised) to use the console it's ME that's going to cock it up. How many fricking times must I type the same fricking password that I just typed ten fricking minutes ago, again? Is there any way to shut this password thing offfff? Yes, just delete everything that says "Keyring" from that fancy gizmo that needs a network connection to be worth anything at all. AAGHHH! Now I can't do anything! I can't even log out! That was when I installed 8.10, but alas, the password palooka is even more anal there, if you can believe that. I'm lucky keyboards are relatively cheap and that I had two others lying around.
 
Okay, so, general consensus is the resolution has something to do with drivers. I suspect not. Back to XP to download Puppy and Freespire. Guess what? Yes, 1024 X 768, both of them. Just like XP! Drivers schmivers. It's that damn Packman! Now not even the live CD will go back to 1024 X 768 and nor will a fresh install of ubuntu 8.10. I used GPartED to wipe the partition before installing 8.10 but I still have that 800 X 600 blob of orange fungus on the right hand side of my drive. Sucks! All the enthusiasm I had; its all been sucked out out of me by now.
 
Now I know none of this is any fault of ubuntu, its developers or its userbase. It's all my fault. I should have spent a year or two more lurking on the forum and reading the user guides. But I'm not a geek, I'm a writer. I need to get online to communicate with editors, agents, do research, write, do illustrations and check copyrights. Even if I did that all in XP, ubuntu is still useless unless it can connect to the net since it can't get to the repositories or download updates without it. One would think that driver support would be one of the core priorities of the developers, but it seems they're too busy with Keyring to give the WUSB54GC v3 bug more than "moderate" priority. 
 
It seems as though the desktop is developed and packaged with the same paranoia about security as the server, and that the default level of security in the desktop package is the kind of security that only banks and the military would reasonably need as default. There must be very few other places where the nature of the work environment has people leaving a computer running and where the passing (and potentially malicious) traffic is so heavy that a password every fifteen minutes is necessary.
 
I still have the CD's and when I'm looking for trouble I'll probably give ubuntu another go-round. I know I made a big deal about passwords and connectivity, and I could probably solve both if I just settle down, but my biggest gripe, now that the enthusiasm's gone and I've had some time to think, is the cryptic nature of the interface. Almost every application in ubuntu has buttons with labels that one has to do homework on before they're useful. The FOSS community (and I have experience of this in the Blender, and Gimp forums) takes great pride in their ability to get under the hood and tweak things; to spend time (an inordinate amount of time in some cases) working things out. This is commendable, but it's ineffective if everyone has to repeat their steps and it all remains a console crusade.
 
That cryptic camouflage extends beyond the software itself. I still haven't found anywhere to download patched versions contributed by the community; still can't find where in the release notes it says whether a particular bug has been fixed or patch has been commited. I'm honestly not even interested in finding the answer any more. I've seen the ubuntu community, and linux users in general, referred to as elitist, but I don't agree. It's just that they know their way around (the software and the support forums). I've seen it in other forums where new users don't even know what question to ask, because they don't know the existence, name or function of the tool that they're hoping to find an answer about, and that's what's happened here. 
 
The software is not designed for someone who just wants to get on with his or her daily job. It demands too much (I feel) unnecessary team commitment. I don't want to join the anti-windows team, I don't want to join the Console Crew, and if I have to type another password, be made to feel like I'm breaking in, or going through airport security or being profiled one more time I'm going to get back the 7 Gigs I lost to aspiration. The cat has lived here for eight years now and in all that time has never managed to make me feel as guilty as ubuntu has in the two months it's been living at my knee.
 
I was born and raised in Africa (which may or may not be the problem here) and I know what uBuntu means, and I can assure you that this is not it! Neither is it Karmic! I don't want help, support, advice, valium or whatever else you think I might need; I'm writing this to the original poster. Your post is the first reasoned and sincere post I've seen since I've been here and I hope you find the same in my response. I wish I had the time to dig deeper and maybe add to the three things I've mentioned but when I type 'grief -l' in the console that's all I get. 


Sala kahle,


Gabe.

Unfortunately you seem to be suffering from a few misconceptions. Passwords are not there to protect the system from the user, or vice versa. The passwords are there to protect your system from those with malicious intent. A default Ubuntu installation is more secure than a Windows installations without having to add anti-malware programs and a firewall. It is not susceptible to drive-by downloads of malware, all windows viruses have no affect on the way the system runs.

As for the / thing, it works the same way as \ in Windows, in DOS the / was used the same as in Linux, for some reason when Win 95 was released the / became a \. 

I think what you are getting at is the difference between # and $. THe $ indicates that you are running as a normal user while the # indicates that you are running as root. Another thing I found that confuses new users is ~/ which is a shortcut for /home/<user>.

The biggest problem for people coming from windows is that they have a mindset that computers should work a certain way. With a Linux based system, you the user are responsible for the way the system operates, you can customize it to your hearts content, with windows, there really isn't much you can to do  change the way things work.

I'm sorry that you had such a hard time, but Ubuntu is getting better all the time, and I would suggest you give it a try again at a later date, using the latest version, and as always use the Live CD to make sure everything works before committing to an installation.
