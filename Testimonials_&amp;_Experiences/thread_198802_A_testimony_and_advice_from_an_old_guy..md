### Post by irv on 2006-06-17
After reading many of the wonderful testimonies out here I thought I would just add mine.
I am not new to Linux to say the least. Let me give you just a little history.
Hardware and OS's. (I built all my PC except my Laptop, it is a no-name case the kind Dell puts their name on).
I got started in computers in the late seventies early eighties. Some of my early computers were a Tandy with 4K RAM, a Atari XL800 16K RAM with tape drive and B/W TV. I did upgrade to a Hard Drive and more RAM as time went on. I then move to a Atari ST and then a TT computers. ST=16/32bit and TT=32/32bit. The ST had 2MB RAM and the TT had 4MB RAM. I still have the TT in storage if anyone is interested. (email me if you are). I then made the move to the IBM world and got a Intel 386 processor. Then move to a 486, and then the Pentium class PC. I now have 4 Computers: My main desktop which I am using now is a 2.8Gig Intel With all kinds of Hard drives (internal and external). 1 Gig of RAM 21” Monitor. My test desktop is a 800Mhz Celron with 256MB RAM 40Gig Hard Drive and a 19” LCD flat panel monitor. My server is a 900Mhz Intel with 512MB RAM 80Gig Hard Drive and a old Gateway 17” Monitor. And last my Laptop is a 2.8Gig Intel Mobile Processor with 1Gig of Ram and a 60Gig Hard Drive. I am running Ubuntu on my main desktop, my server and I use the live CD on my laptop. I duel boot my main desktop to Ubuntu 6.06 and XP. I use Ubuntu 99.9% of the time. My server is 100% Ubuntu 6.06 and my Laptop is a 50/50 thing. My test desktop is running Fedora as I write, but I am going back to Ubuntu because it runs much faster seeing it is a 800Mhz Celron machine. 
 As far as my OS's go, I started with DOS, then TOS (Atari's DOS), then Digital Research's OS for the Atari (Just like MAC's OS). Then Win3.0, 3.1, 95, 98, ME, 2000, XP. As far as Linux goes: I have tried Red Hat, Mandrake (Mandriva), Suse, Fedora, Damn Small Linux, Debian, FreeBSD, Xandros, Linspire, and a few others I might have missed. 
I guess I am settling in on Ubuntu because as I use it I find it fits like a glove. I find everything at my finger-tips. The more I use it the more I like it. I found this forum to be such a great help and it got me over many hurdles that I have struggled with with other distors. I found it easy to setup my server and I love the Synaptic Package manager, (it is much faster and easier to use then yam in Fedora core 5).
I consider myself a season user of PC's and Networks and my background was in IT or what is now called IS (Information Technology) now (Information Systems). I retired in January this year (going on six months and loving it). My hobby has been computers for many years now, and using Ubuntu Linux is like coming back to being in control of my PC world.
See TUX Magazine Issue #14, Letters. I responded to an article about getting control back.
see [http://www.tuxmagazine.com/]("http://www.tuxmagazine.com/").
In closing let me say that I believe Ubuntu is going to change the PC world when it comes to OS'. I already see big corporation coming over to the Linux world and Ubuntu is doing everything positive to help this happen. 
If there is anyone who is reading this and is trying to make up their mind whether or not to install Ubuntu Linux, my advice is try it you'll like it and it will bring joy back to your computing.
Irv
From Wabasha MN Home of the Grumpy Old Men (the Movie).

### Post by jonrkc on 2006-07-08
As another older user (66 years and counting), I agree that using Linux feels like getting control back--even with all the difficulties a new user may experience.  I switched totally to Linux in January 2003 when my patience with that other operating system said to be on 95% of the world's personal computers (how sad!) came to a boiling end.  I try to persuade all my friends to go either to Linux or Mac.  

I still have a lot of troubles with Linux because of my inquiring mind which moves me to experiment and break things on a daily basis.  But I'm not about to abandon Linux, or Ubuntu, which is by far the friendliest of the distros I've tried.  If I ever did go to another distro, it would have to be another Debian-affiliated one, solely because of the packaging system, which is incredibly wonderful after struggling with the rpm system for a couple of years.  When there are dependency problems now, they are almost always solved automatically for me.  I could shed tears of joy over that.

And I agree that the Ubuntu forums are very helpful.  

I used to really enjoy my computers when DOS reigned.  MS Windows(r) came along and diminished my pleasure a LOT.  Now it feels more like the best of two worlds--as fancy a GUI as I want, or a bare command line.  I love it.
