### Post by skippy81 on 2006-05-10
Well I've been using Ubunto pretty solidly for a couple of weeks now.  At some points its been a slightly rough ride, but all in all I think I choose the best distro availiable today and the ubuntu community are amongst the most helpful people on internet let alone on a linux distro :p 

Heres my story....  If anyone is affected by any of the issues I was feel free to PM me for a walkthrough :P

I decided to intall linux on my main PC mainly out of boredom/dissatisfaction with Windows XP.  Most of my favourite programs Windows programs were either open source or availiable on linux anyway so I felt I had nothing to lose. I have used linux a few times before, a home webserver running slack 10.0 and used to have SUSE on my laptop.  When I inserted my slack 10 disc and got a kernel panic I figured it was time for a new distro :p I choose ubuntu mainly because of its superb ranking on distrowatch.org and also based on the fact that it looked like a distro attempting to take linux forwards :D 

Alas my Breezy install didn't go too smoothly.  sis190 lan card not supported, neither was my southbridge sis965 (preventing me from running in DMA mode on hard disk).  I managed to install the lan driver fine, upgraded the kernel to get the DMA working.  I also managed to stop my printer churning out junk by disabling ECP in the bios.  Rather amusingly I used the PC with DMA disabled for about a day before I started to get suspicious about the boot time :)  After DMA my system really flew - faster than a 'worn in' XP installation by far.  

Once I was up and running with DMA enabled I found Breezy to be my favourite disto yet.  The choice of defaut packages was flawless, as was the begginer's documentation (starting guide).  The balance between shell and gui use is also spot on - a recent trend with linux distos seems to be to emulate Windows and deny the very existance of the shell.   Ubuntu appears to have chosen to innovate and concentrate on gearing linux towards what it does best - power and flexability.   To my initial suprise I found myself quickly migrating my essay writing and multimedia use to Breezy.   

In fact since installing Ubuntu linux I have only used windows once (and that was to download my lan driver).  I think whether a user can actually be productive in linux is the real 'acid test' of a strong distro and one which Breezy passed with flying colours.  It makes a far better net surfing platform than Windows also - after browsing the repositories I have a far more effective and fun internet suite than I ever had on MS.  

Yesterday I decided to upgrade to Dapper - I found my motherboard DMA to be supported, but unfortunately not the lan. Nevermind, I was prepared and had downloaded the kernel headers in advance :)  I ran into an installation snag with wvdial hanging the install, but recovered with a kill command on the wvdial proces.

Dapper is fantastic, a lot quicker and more responsive than Breezy (and Breezy was hardly a drag:) )  For the first time of my life I feel I have truly migrated to linux, despite my earlier forrays with Slack and Suse.  Thanks to all the members of the ubuntu community for making it possible.  The community is this distro's greatest asset IMO - no amount of money or know-how can replace helpful supportive people.

### Post by Personatech on 2006-05-10
As another newbie I'd like to second Skippy's sentiments.  For me, the test of a useful distro is how much of a pain is it to get my WiFi up-and-running.  Breezy could handle my old Orinoco card (I'm running Ubuntu on a Dell Latitude C610) but not my newer 802.11g.  With Dapper, the 802.11g card was recognized immediately!  Add a sophisticated (but not overbearing) suite of apps, a handy Add/Remove utility to add a few non-standard apps, and gDesklets to provide some eye-candy and all is good!  Best of all is the Ubuntu community - I really think the Uphilosophy attracts good people and might even possibly repel the typical flamethrowers sometimes found in distro communities.
