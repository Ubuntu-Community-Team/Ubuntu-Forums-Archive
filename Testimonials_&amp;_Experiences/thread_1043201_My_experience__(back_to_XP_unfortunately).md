### Post by tanis143 on 2009-01-18
First off, I will say I liked Ubuntu very much. I had a few reasons I went back, mostly due to hardware issues. First I'll post the good parts. Actually, I'll tell why I wanted to make the switch:

Recently I found myself not gaming anymore and doing mostly web stuff and the occasional irc chatting. So, I figured now would be a good time to make the complete switch. My hard drive is setup with two partitions, a 40 gig c and a 80 D. I took windows out and used the 40 gigs for linux leaving the 80 partition ntfs for all my backups just in case (stupid mistake that haunted me later). Installation was a breeze. The only hard part was getting my usual resolution to work (1280x1024). Figured that out and all was good. I loved the visual effects that 8.10 offered though I wish there was a better gui for tweeking it. Plus, running in 64 bit I noticed a increase in performance. I was able to get my email transferred over and found a good substitute for MS Money in Gnucash. Life was good for about a week. Then I started finding out things I wanted to do and couldn't. 

First was my home printer. We have a lexmark x4530 that is shared off my wife's puter for the house to use. Well, linux saw the printer, but I found out there are no drivers anywhere for this printer. Well crap, there goes my ability to print. Not that I did much printing, but now I had no choice. Well, this wasn't enough to cause me to switch back, but it was a minus point. My biggest problem came next. I have a V-Stream Xpert 2000 PCI video capture card that I was going to start using, transferring our old vhs to dvd. Linux saw the card and added drivers for it, so I thought I was good with it. Nope. I tried several programs and could always get video, but audio would not work. I spent an entire week looking things up on the net, in here, asking my linux friends in IRC to no avail. This is what caused me to switch back. XP needed the drivers installed, but after that even the basic XP home movie maker was able to capture vid and audio and record it. 

As soon as I can get a bigger hard drive and a new vid card for another computer I have sitting here, I will be making me a linux box. I liked linux, I liked how flexible it was, but I just couldn't lose one of the major things I have my home computer for. And I didn't want to dual boot, I've only got a 120 gig drive and was going to need most of the D partition for my vhs transfers. 

Overall, my experience was good. I learned a lot more about linux than I previously knew. If I could have fixed my sound issue with video capture I would have stayed.
