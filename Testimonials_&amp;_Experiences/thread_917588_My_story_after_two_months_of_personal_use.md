### Post by shatteredmindofbob on 2008-09-12
I've been reading this forum since I installed Ubuntu so I figure it's time I share my own story. 

My experience with Ubuntu actually begins about a year and a half ago when I burned a live CD out of curiosity. Booting it up a laptop I just bought, I was impressed with how fast it was along with the fact that for some reason, online video streamed much quicker. 

See, at the time I had a pretty crappy Internet connection. In order to watch a YouTube video, I'd have to pause it right away, then go turn on the coffee maker, have a smoke, pour coffee, wait for it to cool and drink it then come back and half the video might be buffered. 

Ubuntu, running off the live CD, there was no waiting at all. I had tried to install it as a dual boot but couldn't figure out GParted at the time...granted it was a half-hearted attempt since I only had an 80GB hard drive as well. 

After that, I mostly ignored Linux for a bit since I didn't have too many problems with Windows (XP, which I still think it a fine operating system...beats Vista, anyway.) Now my roommate on the other hand...somehow his desktop opened up a direct network connection to Hell and well...lets just say, we were measuring the number of viruses he was getting PER MINUTE. I tried in vein to clean it but failed. He finally got fed up and asked me to install that Linux CD he'd heard me ramble about a few months earlier. 

I'm probably more tech-savvy than the average person but I'm far from any kind of expert, but I do like a good project. Installation was smooth, only problem was his wireless adapter worked for five minutes and then couldn't connect anymore. 

Rather irritating since the box it came in had the picture of the penguin next to the Windows logo implying that it SHOULD work. Finally gave up and just ran an Ethernet cable (that said, the adapter didn't very well under Windows, either.) 

No real problems after that, I put in the restricted drivers and the NVidia drivers (they downloaded automatically when I tried to turn on Desktop Effects...this was Feisty Fawn which didn't have them enabled by default.)

As time went on, I started to get jealous every time I saw his computer (I'm insanely paranoid about malware, particularly keyloggers, so I'd always use his computer for online banking.) The GNOME environment seemed much more productive - you can cram a surprising amount of info into those task bars. 

Next thing I know, I'm trying to make Windows look like GNOME. However, nothing would get rid of my biggest annoyance: After booting up, the hard drive sits there's spinning for about five minutes before I can use anything. And that was after I stripped out most of my start-up applications except for essentials (can't live without Launchy!) 

No such problems with Ubuntu as far as I could tell and decided to research it only to learn that the biggest problem was the registry. 

Somewhere in all this, I bought a bigger hard drive and set it up to dual-boot. And wow...it'd a loong time I'd done a clean Windows install...it had to be one of the most frustrating things I've ever been through. It took...I don't remember how long to get it to even recognize the Ethernet port so I go online to find wireless drivers. Took about an hour to get that running, once I did, I started updating to SP3 which took all night. Literally. I fell asleep on the floor at some point and woke up beside my laptop in the morning and it was STILL updating. 

Went to install Live Messenger and for some reason kept getting errors. After about an hour of Googling for the answer I found it. I had to go to the DOS prompt and manually re-register a bunch of DLL files. 

After this, I decided it was time to do the Ubuntu install. It was...painless. Everything, including my wireless card, worked right away. It even automatically detected that it was a laptop and adjusted power settings accordingly (the clean install of Windows had no idea what I was using...but after my experience, I can say Dell doesn't pay the people who do the initial config enough...the kickback they get from installing crap like Google Desktop should go directly to them!) 

Then a funny thing happened. I stopped booting into Windows. Maybe once a week to play some game that didn't work under Wine or try some new app (Digsby is a pretty neat IM client, though word has it a Linux port is in the works.)

And now, two months later, Windows is gone. I'm one of those people who uses maybe 10 per cent MS-Office's functionality, thus OpenOffice is more than fine and as far as games go, well, I did blow $400 on a PS3 so I figure I should spent some quality time with it. 

Why I didn't do this sooner, I don't know. I didn't miss much, since I was already using Firefox and OpenOffice under Windows. And I certainly don't miss waiting five minutes after boot-up for the hard drive to stop spinning or the paranoia about malware even though my browsing habits are relatively benign. 

I've also noticed something interesting when it comes to open source operating systems: If a feature is missing, wait. 

When I first looked into switching to Linux, I checked to see if my Blackberry would work with it. I found Barry which at the time only provided command line access to the file system. 

Well, after making the switch, I went back to grab Barry since I was bored and figured with command line access, I could at least pull off some scripts to try syncing. Checking the site a few months later, it turns out it's already improved by leaps and bounds. They apparently have it working with OpenSync so it'll sync to Evolution. Woot! 

All this said, Ubuntu is still far from perfect. When something does go wrong, it's just plain weird. After my dual boot install, ALSA randomly stopped working and I had to switch to Pulseaudio which I found doesn't work that well with Wine. After being unable to fix it, I figured I'd just delete to drivers and reinstall them. Went in Synaptic, looked for anything ALSA-related to remove it. After I deleted the first item I found, it suddenly started working again. Flashback to troubleshooting Windows 3.1. 

There are also a few apps I do miss. I really wish there was a blog editor like Windows Live Writer and I wish Evernote 3 would work properly in Wine. It kind of does, but to my most used feature is hitting a hotkey to pull up a new note and that's gone. I've switched to Tomboy but sadly, it doesn't sync to a website I can access from my Blackberry. Maybe Conduit (AMAZING program!) will fix this at some point. 

Far as trouble-shooting goes, I still spent less time on it than I do defragging the harddrive, cleaning the registry and defragging the registry, moving most used programs to the outer edges of the hard drive in an attempt to keep Windows speedy.  

And that is my long-winded tale of the Ubuntu switch so far.

### Post by jaqie on 2008-09-13
I've never seen XP pro cost $300... where and when did you see that price!?

You can also buy computer cases on ebay with the xp license still on them, and use the license for your own PC... for almost nothing.  That sticker (certificate of authenticity) is what makes it legal for you to own and run that operating system.

Yes, for most of the advanced security measures XP PRO is needed, but home is still quite secureable.
[http://www.google.com/search?q=secure+xp](http://www.google.com/search?q=secure+xp)
Almost every single guide I have ever seen, and there are innumerable ones out there, need no pay-for addons to use. Some suggest you use a trialware program to tweak the settings (since it's easier then editing the registry yourself) but you don't have to, as all settings are basically easily changeable with a bit of registry editing. Its just basically a lot like one huge XML file (though it's not in xml format) with settings for everything.
Yes, using a third party firewall in XP is a very big mistake, IMO.  I experimented with several of them and found them all to be utter trash - there is zero need for them if you are behind a good NAT router (something that runs DD-WRT, which is a linux router firmware, that is the place where linux shines, in servers) then you don't even need a firewall in windows even without a lot of the security modifications hinted at earlier.

With all that said, I am very happy to have switched my server over to ubuntu, and my main system is back to dual-booting again.  I liken xp to an ill-fitting but comfortable shirt, it feels comfortable but if you wear it too long it starts chafing and annoying you.
