I have been using Ubuntu for about a month and have been learning the whole time. That really is sound advice.  When I started with Linux, I was well on my way to being a pro with the Windows XP/2003 stack.  I could do a lot more with the OS than most users could, could work directly with the registry, register and unregister DLLs, the whole nine yards.

Going to Linux meant that I had to leave the role of "uber-user" behind.  I was back to being a noob, the low man on the proverbial totem pole.  Things didn't work like Windows, and that irked me to no end.

My advice is to work with it.  Even if you decide to continue working with Windows, it'll do you good.  For me, I really needed the humiliation of being the "noob" again.  I could understand what it was like for a new user that wasn't quite sure what he or she could do, let alone not knowing how to do it.  It got me off of my high horse and reminded me that how I felt with Linux is how the beginning user felt.  I became a better support person.

Of course, there's more to it than just that.  You loved working with the computer when you first discovered it.  Everything was shiny and mysterious and fun.  Granted, now you have an idea of what the computer should do, an idea of "it works like this and only like this, and that's the end of it."  Bo-ring.  I had that.  Once I switched to Linux and let my ego get out of the way, I learned to tinker with it, play around with it, customize it.  It actually got to be fun again.

The advantage here is that since the whole O.S. is open, there's no limit to the fun.  You can try different desktops, different programs for anything you might want to do, just to see what you'd like.  I wanted to learn programming--Linux has the tools, several development environments, tutorials, full documentation and source code, all for the taking.  Gratis.  

When I was in the Windows camp, my training consisted of Office, Active Directory, and Exchange.  But since I switched to Linux, in roughly the same amount of time I've learned system management, programming in five different languages, software compilation, web application development, database administration and design, mail server deployment, Voice Over IP deployment, TCP/IP routing, embedded systems design, LiveCD development, and systems automation.  

Whenever I get good at something and get bored, there's always something else I can learn to do, and it won't cost me an arm and a leg to try it out; the only thing it costs is for me to take the initiative to learn it--and there's no requirement to learn any of these things.

If you're just looking for a Windows replacement, try ReactOS.  It's not a bad approximation.  But it'd be a shame if you didn't at least give it a try.
