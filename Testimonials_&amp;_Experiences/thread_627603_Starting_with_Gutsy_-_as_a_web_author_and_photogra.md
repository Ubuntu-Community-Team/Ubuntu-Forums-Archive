### Post by TimGS on 2007-11-30
I'm still with Ubuntu, but have Windows XP as a dual boot system. The idea of regularly going back to my slow Windows system (I have a hyperthreading Pentium with 2GB RAM - hardly low spec) fills me with dread. I say that first as below I've noted the problems I did have / do have with Ubuntu which could sound overly negative! 

In chronological order, I'll start with installing...

I would prefer to have been able to run something direct from Windows without having to burn a CD (minor pain as I only have a DVD writer and Roxio didn't like burning a CD image to DVD - however Infra Recorder had no such qualms and it worked).

The screen display was initially broken (I have an ATI Radeon X300 with a CTX 1451 crt) but on the second attempt for some reason it was OK. Much later, installing Google Earth resulted in the need to install ATI's 'restricted' driver as the results without were slowly comical.

Assigning partitions (for dual boot) should have been easier but for some reason i had a bit of faff working out what was what - when I do likewise on my Mum's machine I'll try and remember the precise issue.

Problems with my modem were solved by Steve Harper's (Thanks again Steve) USB ADSL package. Problems with playing DVDs were solved by looking at the forums and following various different sets of instructions until finding one that worked! VLC delivered the goods. Implications for Ubuntu's useability and readiness for the big wide world I believe are best discussed elsewhere in the forums, but these two problems were really the big black marks for Ubuntu.

Installing most programs is a breeze - just point and click. However every time someone says "type in sudo...." then somewhere a potential user probably goes back to the products of Mr. Gates. 

Using Ubuntu for web authoring is fine, as you'd expect. I use Bluefish - and apart from not liking the light grey that the comments go (against a white background) I'm happy - perhaps because its similar to Crimson Editor on Windows. IEs4Linux solves testing problems and although this package doesn't fully support IE7 yet, VirtualBox OSE running XP with IE7 solved that issue.

Photography is not so good, although I suspect the average Joe Public would be quite happy with what's on offer - as long as he can get a driver for his photo printer. For serious amateurs and upwards its not so easy.

I'm not hugely fussed (compared to some) about colour management (most of my pics for sale are B&W anyway) but I would like something like Adobe Gamma - Monica comes close but I have to re-do the settings each time due to a bug and it can't change the colour temperature of your display. Having said that, when I make a colour print I have no idea if A. Customer is going to put it in a dark north facing room under a flourescent tube or at the other side of the house just in a line with a patch of bright sunlight, which does make the idea of getting overly obsessed with colour rather silly.

I have used Adobe Lightroom, Photoshop (overated and overkill IMHO) and GIMP on windows. However GIMP being only 8 bit (and for B&W that isn't 3x8 bit - it is purely 8 bit!) results in many situations where it isn't enough. To be fair, 80-90% of the time it'll be fine, but I'm after 100%. As a result I'm investigating CinePaint and Krita - unfortunately a brief scan of the forums shows that many Linux newbies are directed to GIMP and only GIMP - which will result in many running back to Windows. Please remember that the requirements of Joe Photographer are not the requirements of Joe Public who will look at most of his/her pics on a monitor at not-many-dpi, rarely if ever print above 6" x 4" and probably never do any photo manipulation (I'm thinking of tone corrections and the like - not photo-fakery).

For RAW conversion, UFraw and Raw Therapee seem good, although a lack of batch conversion could be a problem for some. You can repeat the settings from photo to photo but the type of photographer who takes 20 variants of the same thing will probably soon tire of this. Sadly Lightzone from Lightcrafts no longer exists in Linux and Bibble don't support my camera.

Printing is a minor pain although the developers of HPLIP seem keen to sort out problems. I have however been venturing back to Windows for photo printing and scanning. This isn't pleasant but does at least remind me that despite the faults I don't risk falling asleep while Ubuntu boots up and generally programs in Linux don't have arguments with each other and crash the system (one and only one exception - on the first load, Google Earth in Ubuntu froze everything).
