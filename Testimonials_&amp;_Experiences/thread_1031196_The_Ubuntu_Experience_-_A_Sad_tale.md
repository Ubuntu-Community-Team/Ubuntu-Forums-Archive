### Post by bryanp on 2009-01-05
Hello to all Ubuntu folk out there.
I decided to do a grand experiment with Linux in 2008.
This topic may generate some debate, and if it does, and achieves a more coherant and solid Linux community, then I'll be happy. If it simply generates the usual rants, then it'll have been a waste of effort.

My background starts with PC's running DOS 3.1 onwards (circa 1986) so I am not unaware of command line etc. I have done some reconfiguration on PC's and rebuilds of both hardware and software. I regarded myself as a reasonable user. 

I was tired of the constant updating with M$ and the various whispers that M$ spy's on the PC via various backdoors and so on and so forth. Time to leave the M$ fold. I had an older PC which I wanted to take charge of properly, but retained a M$ laptop. (Which as subsequent events demosnstrated, proved to be a very wise decision).   I did my research and settled on Ubuntu, 7.10.  Set up the PC great and was starting to settle it down. 

There were a lot more updates that I had bargained for, but hey, that was part of the learning. The various forums are very good, but trying to get straight answers was oftentimes very frustrating; it seemed that everyone had a very good reason why X Y and Z was the best solution and try these settings&#8230; and Oh sorry if that doesn't work, but try these. 

For a newbie, it was very difficult, but I persevered. Many people suggested simply putting the question on the forum. I tried that a couple of times, but in all honesty, the tone of some of the comments from more experienced users to various newbies was and is quite intimidating, so I steered clear as much as possible. A couple of times I shared experiences or asked for help, but it was slow in coming and sometimes, not helpful. Rightly or wrongly, (and I'm sure this will spark some debate) I decide that helping a newbie isn't interesting enough to generate interest amongst the 'real' user community. 

Then I tried to update to 8.01.

What a disaster. Ended up reinstalling 7.10, which took several hard drive reformats. Then installed from a CD image. Looked OK, but certain things, which had worked well before, no longer worked now. The HAL bug was one such. There were other quirks, which made the whole experience difficult.

I reinstalled 7.10 and set everything up again. I added things and tried to find my way around the various forums, which again was intensely frustrating due to the sheer weight of numbers suggesting different things. For someone with little experience of the Linux experience it was very hard. The skill set that apparently was needed to really use the PC properly was growing and the ability to track down info taking up more and more time. And everyone has a different opinion!!!

I admire the dedication of the various people who contribute to the whole Linux community; I simply don't have that kind of time to spend. 
Just as one example, I was looking for a scan to PDF application, and there was one that appeared to very successful. This intrepid person, according to the forums I read (and yes, I read them all) had been on this project for 2 ½ years. I just stand in awe of such devotion.  But almost everything I wanted to do was taking more time to research and install, then tweak, then search out forums and get the right command lines etc. Again, the WINE project is fantastic and I have used it successfully (eventually!) but it took a while to get my application working under WINE, and it just never looked the same as under XP. 

Then I realised that my printer was using a lot more ink than it had previously; the ink was bleeding on the same paper that worked OK with the laptop.  The monitor that I was using, admittedly not the best, was slightly fuzzy and the fonts in OO.o didn't display well. I wasn't sure if it was something I hadn't configured properly, a function of Ubuntu, or some unknown gremlin, which was a system 'feature'.

What I really wanted was a computer that I could switch on and work with. Ubuntu should have done that, but it just didn't.

So very reluctantly and with a great deal of soul-searching, I've pulled the plug, bitten the bullet and gone back to M$ XP. I plugged it all in and the same monitor looks brighter and crisper. The printer inks no longer bleed. M$ office lookes better and the fonts display better. 

Oh yes, I'm back to the same problems of constant updates, Virus checkers and spyware monitors. I'm back to the paranoia about what the PC is telling M$ about my system. BUT
At a rudimentary level, I can understand what is going on. I simply switch on and use. Oh, and so can my wife, who was completely fazed by the whole Ubuntu experience.

So what's the difference?

I think that the whole M$ corporate thing is wrong. Completely wrong. I'll never believe that one company should hold all the power. But that's what a commercially based society gives us. We want a company to be successful and make profit; that's what a Market based economy strives for. Then we complain when we get it. 
Whatever we think of the ethics that Mr Gates and his management team employed to achieve what the have achieved, we cannot deny that they simply applied the whole market economy thing well. 

Equally, they realised that what the vast majority of people want, is a single platform that enables them to press a few buttons, without too much thought, and things happen. And people are willing to pay for that. So take away the efficiency, security and ability arguments of the M$ systems (and they are many, lengthy and mostly true); they deliver what the majority want &#8211; albeit at a price, both in terms of money and in terms of personal security.

Linux is however, (I believe) for purists. Its greatest strength is also its greatest weakness. It does what it promises and does it with an economy and security of programming that M$ can only dream of. It has an army of dedicated people who just want to make my experience better. The strength of its individuality is its weakness. It's just too individual.

And every flavour is different. And once on that flavour, switching to another is not easy. In this respect, its not a whole lot different from M$.

And here's the big downfall. As one of the mindless masses, I don't want to have to think too much. I want a platform that does what is says it does. (And yes I know all the arguments about M$ BSoD etc). Linux of whatever flavour will always be technically better than M$, but as a dumb user, I want to use. 

I read in various forums that this flavour is better than that one and who in their right mind would use the one over there; I read some quite vitriolic attacks on different camps; I read of Mr Torvald Linus attacking Gnome and favouring KDE. I see fragmentation at every level AND IT DOES THE CAUSE NO GOOD AT ALL!

 All it does is convince low level users, like myself, that despite all its drawbacks, M$ is one platform, internationally recognised and the chances are that whatever PC I log onto, it'll look like the one I've got. 

And that's quite simply why Unix based systems will never, despite the obvious advantages of them, ever achieve the dominance of M$. They are just too unique skill set intensive and too fragmented. The majority of Earth's inhabitants haven't got the time, skills or indeed the desire to learn what is a very steep learning curve.

So it's with a very sad farewell that I leave the Linux and especially the Ubuntu community. I have enjoyed my brief time with you and can really see the benefits of running this as an OS rather than M$ systems. But I just don't have the time or energy to give this project the attention it demands. Sorry!
