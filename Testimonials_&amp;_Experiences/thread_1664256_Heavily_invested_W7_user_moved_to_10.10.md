### Post by momotomo on 2011-01-10
Thought it would be appropriate to provide a testimonial for a first post. Up until a few weeks ago I was a heavily invested windows user &#8211; my bread and butter was in Windows 7, Photoshop and Illustrator. I&#8217;d had this setup (or variations on it) for about 10 years now. I&#8217;ll leave you to do the math on the cost of the hops from NT 4.0 -> 7, Photoshop 5 -> CS4.

[The least to say, it wasn&#8217;t cheap keeping up. I&#8217;d looked at linux a few times but generally assumed that the free packages were never going to manage the same level of function as commercial packs. In the meantime, most times where I needed a new function on my machine I was reaching for my wallet (and still getting some fairly sub-par software in terms of features and stability)

]I recently built up a Dell M101Z laptop, discovering in the process my external DVD drive had died. So I grabbed 10.10 with the intent that I could at least browse the web until I put W7 on. Long story short, after poking around a bit I decided to keep it on there. A few weeks later I&#8217;ve transitioned all of my machines to Ubuntu.

]I&#8217;m genuinely in awe of how capable ubuntu / the open source packages are. I loaded on gimp, inkscape, blender and some desktop publishing applications / misc utilities and it&#8217;s a genuine case that I can do pretty much everything I did previously. Most of the time the apps are stable, and much simpler (in terms of UI and workflow), additionally everything seems less aggressive with respect to system resources.

The experience isn&#8217;t perfect &#8211; I still need to disable APIC support on anything with a dell logo to stop it destroying the machine everytime it tires to sleep, and have had a few other teething issues with hardware support, but the experience is good enough that I won&#8217;t be switching back to windows. Ubuntu / Canonical have made a distribution that is coherent, easy to use, rational with regards to &#8220;open&#8221; (making a concession and allowing things like flash and MP3 playback out of the box is trivial, but so critical to some users adopting the platform), and just works as expected.

Especially the software centre &#8211; I&#8217;ve sold a handful of people on switching just on this feature. It literally allows you to go &#8220;I need feature x&#8221;, then a quick search later, you have feature x. It&#8217;s a brilliant testament to the power of open source communities. Ubuntu presents a version of linux that is solid and polished enough to allow me to confidently utilise it in a commercial / production scope. 

Thank you for this platform, and am looking forward to contributing back where I can.
