### Post by Irihapeti on 2010-05-04
Like one or two others, I didn't think I'd get to the point of posting this kind of message. But I'm feeling deeply tired and unhappy with my experience.

The trouble with Ubuntu is that it comes at a cost, and it's getting too much for me.

There's a cost in time: time spent Googling, setting things up, figuring out workarounds. So does Windows, OK. I accept that.

However, if I don't want to spend that time in Windows, I can find a PC shop or a mobile tech to do that for me. With Ubuntu, I don't have that choice.

Ubuntu means dealing with the hardware minefield. There's no definitive up-to-date hardware list to which one can refer. Nor can there be, when compatibilities change from release to release. I can have a 100% compatible machine with one release which won't work with the next one.

Many people are dealing with what they have, anyway. They didn't know when they bought that machine that they were "supposed" to be making certain decisions. Now they are told that they should go pay money so that they can run free software. Doesn't figure to me.

And I get really, really fed up with "it's not Ubuntu's fault" messages. Someone with a problem doesn't care whose fault it is. We want a solution. Think what it's like when you take something back to the shop and they say "It's nothing to do with us, it's the factory." It's not usually very helpful.

The cost that really bugs me is this. I know lots of people who run Windows machines. To them, their PC is just a tool to do a job. If they make one choice or another, it's no big deal, as long as it works for them.

But the Linux user almost has to sign up to an ideology, and be constantly wary that their choices and opinions may be harshly judged. (Just look in Recurring Discussions.) What in the everyday (Windows) world may be just complained about as a nuisance becomes laden with political implication. Same thing happened re the telephone companies in the BBS days. Some people might like this air of subversiveness, but a lot would prefer to avoid it and just get on with their work.

And what's all this about "evil"? I grit my teeth when I hear that expression used so lightly. Our lives are far too sheltered, for the most part, to understand. Talk to a refugee from one of the world's trouble spots who's seen their family or friends killed in front of them, or a survivor from a concentration camp, and get acquainted with what evil really is.

I don't know what I'm going to do next. Only one of my three computers would handle Windows 7. I've thought about another distro, but I'd be carrying the stigma of "Ubuntu user", and we all know where that can get you. Plus, the kernel is still the same, and that's where a number of the bugs are.

Sorry, it's rather long.
Irihapeti

### Post by Irihapeti on 2010-05-04
Thanks for your replies.

I know about not upgrading too early and not expecting instant stability. I've been using Ubuntu for close to three years, and seen several upgrade cycles come and go. Maybe in part I was lucky with the hardware that I had.

Recently, I got into bug testing, which I've been doing more of over the last six months. I've encountered some bugs that date back to Karmic and haven't been fixed; one has, for me anyway, got worse. (If anyone really wants the details, I can provide them.)

Hardy has some bugs that haven't been fixed even after two years, notably Bluetooth for my phone, which is why I might wish to upgrade sooner rather than later.


Of course, the real issue isn't just a few bugs. That is to be expected as part of any new software release. It goes further than that.

You don't mind fixing things when the underlying structure and environment and so on are good. Recent events, and in particular comments from long-term and experienced people such as aysiu and KiwiNZ and others, have caused me to question how sound the underpinnings really are.
