### Post by harrisupstate on 2007-11-23
Hi all,

I'm not a huge poster here, but I've happily relied on this forum for several tips over the past year, and it's been great.  So please bear with me when I say that I think what the original poster said had at least a kernel of truth to it (no pun intended!).

I happen to enjoy the process of tweaking the operating system -- I had been a professional programmer for years, and spent four years doing a lot of scripting in Unix/Solaris.  So I am a techie, and I enjoy doing command-line stuff, but it really is not something the casual user wants to do, or should have to do.

See, I think an operating system should be like paint on the walls -- you know it's there, but you shouldn't have to think about it.  I think another analogy is that of ice skating to a hockey player -- when you watch them play (and especially if you've ever ice skated yourself) it's absolutely amazing how excellent and fluid their skating skills are.  Now if you think about it, it *has* to be that way -- the game itself is so challenging and demanding, they *can't* be thinking about the skating...  it has to come naturally, and without conscious thought.  And I believe that's how lots of "regular" people view an operating system (if they think about it at all) - they just want it to be there, and to work, but not to make itself known.

With all of that in mind, to me, Ubuntu 7.10 is still not there.  One small example is when I started learning to play piano recently, and some online tutorials had wav files designed to show you how a song was supposed to sound.  Well, of course, Ubuntu didn't play wav files by default, and I started doing that Alsa install stuff, but then I happened to notice that my wife's Windows computer just played them effortlessly.  That's the kind of thing I mean -- the operating system is just completely invisible in a situation like that.

I happen to love Ubuntu, and I adore my System76 laptop (and that company's support) but I still feel this way about Linux's readiness for the casual user.

Harris
