### Post by ChrisG_UK on 2011-05-04
Xubuntu 11.04 is working really, really well on my old 3GHz Dell P4 with 512Mb. I leave this machine for guests to use in my holiday home. All they really need is Firefox and Skype, but I give them a music player and a few games and they love it. A Windows box simply wouldn't survive in this environment - it would be a dangerous mess after the first week. Xubuntu just works.

In order to work for these non-technical users, I spend a lot of time setting it up so that people can find things easily. I remove techy stuff like Terminal and make it kind of like Windows, which I know everyone says is wrong, but it has been a great success for the last two years.

I think I understand a bit about the fear that "ordinary" people have of using an unfamiliar O/S. Part of it is visual, but part of it is terminology, and I think that for Ubuntu to move into the mainstream (if that is a goal?) it needs to move on from the "secret language" it uses and use more generic terminology. I mean, how are people suppose to know that to search for something they need to use a thing called Catfish? Or to play music they must use Amarok (sorry, even I can't remember the name of the latest music player, and I only used it yesterday). The programs change from release to release and make no sense at all to anyone outside the Linux world. It almost seems like a deliberate attempt to alienate ordinary users. Contrast this with Windows, where the media player has always been "media player" - despite being a totally different animal from when it started.

Ubuntu and my personal favourite, Xubuntu, are a fantastic achievement. In terms of code, they are 99.99% of the way to being very serious competition for Windows for a huge proportion of users. The last mile is just to clean up the unfriendly terminology and add some flashy artwork. Why not?

And while I'm on a roll here, I have to mention Gimp. I've been doing graphics editing since 1989 and I'm quite good at it. But Gimp has me totally baffled - I have no idea where to start. Is it really the right choice for people who want to do simple things with their digital photos?

I see your point coming from Windows, but Linux is different. In order for any Linux distribution to be better, it first of all has to be different. Here are thoughts on your points:

- Terminal is way too important of an application to be removed. I am surprised you haven't had to use it yet.
- About Windows looks, there is an interesting fact. You can play around with themes and such and get Linux to look pretty close to Windows. But you cannot make windows look like Linux. Play around with menus, panels, applets and themes, you can easily go to a point where you can fool people.
- I can use GIMP. I use a graphical editor once a year, so I have one advantage that you don't: I have no preconceptions. I takes me only a few minutes to figure out what I need (and it is very basic stuff anyway). I have heard a lot of complaint about "high-end" graphical editors being scares on Linux, but I wouldn't know myself.
- There is no centralized authority deciding which app should be called what. There are dozens of media players out there and there is no way to tell some people: you get to call your project Media Player and you don't. (Gnome has a "Media Player" in the menu, although the true name of it is Totem)
- If you right-click on a file, it will give you a list of all the applications that are associated with this file type and you can select which one you want to open it. I never open Parole from the menu, I just click on a file.
- Catfish ... you have to google Linux/Ubuntu and what you need to do and Catfish will pop up. You can also search for generic "Media Player" or "Encryption" in the Software Center.
- A common criticism about Ubuntu is that it is not aimed at experienced Linux users at all ... it is interesting to see another point of view.

There are many discussions on what is keeping Ubuntu from advancing. I think the biggest argument is not to be made about working out of the box settings. In fact, windows hardly ever works out of the box. You have to install it, then add drivers, then add antivirus and then you have a bad browser and a media player (no office, no one-click repository, nothing of this kind comes standard). I think the biggest argument is to be made for Windows just coming pre-installed and people just using whatever comes on their machines. Companies that sell Linux pre-installed are either small or they don't think things through (like Dell selling barely compatible hardware hidden in the depths of their web-page or Asus that tried to push some crappy distribution).

I don't remove Terminal from the system completely, just from the launcher panel. No, I haven't done a scientific study, nor do I have any evidence, but I have been in the IT industry for 35 years and worked with thousands of users in dozens of countries and I have a pretty good feel for the things people use computers for. I don't have any numbers, but the vast majority are using them for some real-world activity other than IT. They couldn't care less what is going on behind the screen and wouldn't have the vaguest clue how to do any kind of configuration work.
