### Post by gamecraziness on 2010-12-24
I love Ubuntu; it is now my main operating system. However, my experience with running it on a laptop has been far from perfect.

First of all, the suspend function doesn't work, which is very frustrating. It just turns off the computer. This has forced me to change the lid close action to blank screen, which uses much more power.

Secondly, I am getting very poor battery life compared to Windows 7, which I found surprising. Perhaps this is because of Windows 7's power profiles and battery-saving features. Compared to that, Ubuntu has rudimentary power controls.

While this may not be as annoying as the previous two, I cannot change the brightness effectively. The Brightness Applet disappears when I try to click it, and the only way to change it is to scroll in the applet, which slows down the computer for some reason. The Fn keys don't work. I have also had this problem on a second laptop.

In short, I think Ubuntu should focus more on the laptop experience, since it represents an ever-increasing part of the computer market share. The fact that these basic functions do not work is very frustrating, and I could see it turning off newcomers to Ubuntu. I realize not all users have these problems, but I do see them come up often, and they are rarely resolved.

### Post by lancest on 2010-12-24
Notebooks are very proprietary pieces of hardware. (Cramming everything into a small form factor means designs must vary). Of course Windows notebooks are designed around Windows and for Windows.

Hardware designs and chips vary, even among similar models among various companies. 

There are hardware hacks/chip choices are specifically for Windows not Linux

To expect Linux to work well on every Windows designed notebook is unrealistic.
If you didn't buy your notebook for Linux use- then it's your fault.

Nothing wrong with hoping for better though.

Seek some help in future, there will likely be solutions to your problems.
