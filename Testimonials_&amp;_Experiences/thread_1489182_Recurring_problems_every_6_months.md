### Post by BoneKracker on 2010-05-23
Most linux distributions follow the same sort of release strategy, involving periodic releases that compress a number of major changes into a single event.  The rationale for this is that there is economy of scale in consolidating release management activities across a number of configuration management items (packages), for example, coordinated efforts at regression testing and remediation, as opposed to dissipated efforts at package- or subsystem-level.

The benefits are well-documented, and most complex systems have historically adhered to some form of periodic release management.

That said, the trouble with this you already feel the effects of: too many moving parts make regression testing against the real future-state system impossible and make fault isolation difficult; and when releases are infrequent, some items will be rushed to "get them into" this release and may not be feature-complete or adequately tested.  Synchronizing project-level development to external release schedules introduces an artificial schedule driver, as opposed to working at an optimal pace and releasing an item "when its ready".

There are, however, some linux distributions that adhere to a "rolling release" strategy.  Under a rolling release strategy, packages or subsystems are released as they are ready, and there aren't periodic versioned releases of the entire distribution (except for installation and configuration management purposes).  Basically, you just keep running your system for years, updating it as you go.

In reality, the number of changes and problems are the same (or greater), but they are spread out over time and isolated (so it's easier to tell exactly what you broke).

Some such distributions are Gentoo, Arch, Foresight, Sidux, and PCLinuxOS, all of which are good distros.  Since Sidux is Debian-based like Ubuntu, that might be a logical choice for you if you want to minimize what you need to re-learn (although Sidux can be a bit too bleeding edge for some people).
