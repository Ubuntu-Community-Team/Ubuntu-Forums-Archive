### Post by gerowen on 2011-05-08
So I tried to remain objective, I upgraded to 11.04 and tried to make it work.  I even tried using the classic Gnome desktop and unity-2d, and 11.04 is just plain bad.  On the same hardware as before 11.04 just feels unacceptably slow, even with the proprietary video drivers installed.  Unity is a dumbed down, un-customizable Gnome shell that prevents me from viewing all of the information I usually have plugged in as Gnome panel applets.  I couldn't even drag Totem around while a video was playing without the whole desktop dragging horribly, unless I was using Unity 2d then the video would flicker due to it not using proprietary driver acceleration.  Even the menu was bad.  The icons for each application took up a huge amount of space making it hard to see all of the applications in a particular menu category without more mouse movements and clicking around.

In light of what has happened I've come to realize that 11.04 was rushed.  Canonical's illogical need to release an entirely new version of Ubuntu every 6 months, instead of waiting until everything is done and releasing a quality product, is getting on my nerves.  I have since migrated both of my machines to Debian 6.0.1a and am quite happy with the move.

Ubuntu now looks like it might be a very good Linux OS for tablets, and perhaps general users wanting a Linux OS without the overhead of configuring everything, but for a technical user wanting to run Linux on their desktop/laptop, Ubuntu is no longer a viable option.  It was nice while it lasted guys, wish you all the best!

**Hardware**
Toshiba Satellite L355D-S7829
AMD Turion X2 64 Processor @ 2.1 Ghz
4 GB RAM
256 MB ATI Radeon HD 3100 Graphics

Hi, Gerowen.  Most of your message appears to suggest that you have used Ubuntu for a while without problems and only dislike the stability or your system's compatibility of the latest release, I don't understand that as a reason to leave.

I also don't understand your problem with the 6 month cycle, since it isn't forced on any one.  I think it's great to use such a systematic span to have lots of people's input on the development of the cycles.  You still have the wisdom of the developer's LTS cycle do keep something that have been very proven to your environment and more well tested.

Why didn't you just stick with the 10.04 LTS and leave the masses who like to venture our and participate in the growing and fast cycle of the computer hardware and software to catch up and keep up.

The team released the Long Term Supported (LTS) 10.04 in April 2010 with support for that version lasting until 2013.  That's a good 3 year cycle.  Or you could go with the 5 year cycle of the Server edition.

Personally, I'm sure, no matter how many years the developers worked on a version, when it's released they are going to be greeted with the type of problems that you're describing with 11.04 with some people.  It'll always happen when a product reaches the masses.

I believe it would have been best for you to have tested the upgrade on a Virtual Box or some other environment before moving your main system to the new environment and staying with the LTS for a longer period of time while the community dealt with the cutting edge development.

I'm glad the technicians are very much at the helm and work so hard to have the transition and upgrade as smooth as possible.

I could see a problem if each release costs a bunch of money and support was dropped from the formal release.  But I don't see a problem with the masses being able to participate in the development by the developers scheduled cycle to share with the masses what they have arrived at, and allowing those who want to wait longer for a fuller test of the environment before upgrading.

I hope you found what you like.  Hopefully a lot of the Ubuntu fans will see this explanation as a reason to celebrate the Ubuntu nix flavor, which as far as I can see, works very well (the 6 month release cycle).

Again, to me it would appear that the 6 month cycle would provide less of a need to rush.  If something isn't ready for this cycle, the team can table it for the next since it's only 6 months away.

Kudo's to the Ubuntu team! ...and than you for your expression.  You might consider that, since components of the environment that the people might not like, might be revisited and reconsidered based on the masses, we'd only have to wait 6 months before it might be reconsidered.
