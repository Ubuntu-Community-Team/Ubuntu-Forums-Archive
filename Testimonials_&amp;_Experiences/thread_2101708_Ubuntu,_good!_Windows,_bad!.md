### Post by ScottDeagan on 2013-01-05
I think my last post here was a "cya later Ubuntu, it's been fun!". I abandoned Ubuntu for several reasons, one of the main reasons was I couldn't get dual monitors working without one of them exhibiting tearing.

Some months on, I'm back! And here's why:

My wife was kind enough to buy be a band spankin' new laptop for Christmas (the Samsung Chronos series 7 - a.k.a NP700Z7C-S02UK). It came pre-loaded with Windows 8. When I booted into Windows 8, I just couldn't believe what I was seeing. It was ugly, attempted to join two different paradigms (touch and traditional mouse/keyboard desktop), was disorienting and unintuitive. While many people say "internally it's faster", all I saw was jitter when dragging windows and scrolling within applications (i.e. wasn't smooth and pixel perfect). The user experience was simply awful, and I thought Windows 8 was some kind of parody unleashed by the good folks at Onion News.

So, I takes the plunge, whack a freshly burnt 12.10 CD into the drive, and install Ubuntu. The first WOW slapped me in the face like a wet fish when the Intel HD 4000 graphics worked out of the box. No need to install proprietary drivers, hardware acceleration working so wobbly windows is smooth! It was truly amazing, but it didn't end there...

Something that I was never able to solve for years when using Linux was dual monitor support. While I could always get two monitors "working", I was NEVER able to get them working perfectly (i.e. without tearing on one of them). I experienced this problem with both nVidia and AMD/ATI graphics. I have 2560 x 1440 monitors, one of the reasons why I chose the NP700Z7C-S02UK  is because it has a mini DisplayPort able to drive 2560 x 1440 monitors, unlike HDMI. When I plugged the laptop in to the monitor's DisplayPort, I thought to myself "no chance". I was stunned rigid when it "just worked", flawlessly, perfectly, zero tearing when dragging windows around!!! My head dropped into my cusped hands as a cried tears of joy.

I have finally found hardware that works with Linux and ticks all checkboxes for me. Dual monitor support is very important to me, and with this new Intel HD 4000 on-chip GPU, everything finally works. I'm so happy with the Intel HD 4000 that I haven't even bothered installing Bumblebee (3.0) to get the nVidia 650m working.

Compared to Windows, the hardware accelerated compositing engine it fast and responsive. Everything feels crisp and smooth. I'm now in the process of installing my dev tools. I'm a Qt mobile developer, so am also looking forward to Ubuntu mobile. Very interesting times we live in, the rise of C/C++ and Qt is coming!!!!!!!

MICROSOFT CAN'T MAKE ME LIVE IN RAINBOW LAND!

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2013-01-06
I've known a number of Windows users that have never had a virus, worm  or horse. They have a good firewall and practice safe computing/internet  habits. Linux is not "immune" either. Not to mention the  learning curve and associated anxiety with trying something new. It's  been my experience that by taking a little time to help those who are  interested, it improves the odds they will not only try Linux - but  make a successful transition should they wish to do so.

Thank you for sharing your feedback and Good Luck.

Just my $0.02

While I see a couple of folks here jumping to the defense of Windows and  then using Windows 7 as an example of how Windows is good, my original  post was not about Windows 7 - it was about the preloaded monstrosity  that is Windows 8. I've seen many positive reviews/comments from early  adopters, and good for them! I'm happy it's working out for them!! I've  also noticed that the overall impression from UX/usability experts is  not a positive one. After trying to use it on a freshly purchased  laptop, I would agree with the UX/usability folks.

Anyway, such a  discussion belongs elsewhere, and only time will tell if Windows 8 is  accepted by the masses (time, and Microsoft's share prices). For me, the  good news is I wrote to Samsung and asked them for a refund for Windows  8 as it's a product I have no intention of using and a product I do not  want to be forced to purchase. Samsung was kind enough to offer me a  refund (it wasn't much, but during these turbulent economic times....).

I  should mentioned that I tried to install Elementary OS Luna (beta 1)  before I installed Ubuntu. I like the Gala window manager, and the  Pantheon terminal and file browser. eOS Luna is beautiful, fast and  snappy. I had it installed on my previous laptop.

Last night I  came across my first annoying problem with my new laptop running Ubuntu  12.10 - there's a bug with the graphics driver when using the SNA  acceleration method :(. The bug manifests in the form of titlebars not  being rendered correctly for windows that lose focus. This is not a show  stopper for my particular use case (mostly using compilers/dev work) as  the problem is fixed by using UXA acceleration instead. However, UXA is  significantly slower than SNA in for most things and negatively impacts  battery life. Phoronix comparison can be found here for anyone  interested:  [http://www.phoronix.com/scan.php?page=article&item=intel_sna_july2012&num=1](http://www.phoronix.com/scan.php?page=article&item=intel_sna_july2012&num=1)

I'm still happy having moved back to Ubuntu, just hope the SNA acceleration problem is fixed soon.

### Post by ScottDeagan on 2013-01-07
> **Tamlynmac said:**
> Opinions aside, why would we feel the need to bash another OS?

If you are referring to my posts, my apologies, I didn't mean to "bash" another OS per se, I was  simply calling it as I see it (while the experience was still in progress and fresh). It was intended to be a testimonial of my  experience (from the point of purchasing a new laptop, and why I  returned to Ubuntu).

> **Tamlynmac said:**
> Perhaps, we should simply point out that it is a choice and allow the  user to decide for themselves which choice best meets their needs...

I'd fully agree with you if we were living in a free and fair market. I find your use of the word "*choice*" in this context misleading. Let me try to explain the pain I had to go through so I could choose...

I  paid £1,100 for a laptop, and I certainly wasn't given a "choice" of  operating systems. I wasn't even given the "choice" to buy the unit OS  free. It came preloaded with windows 8.

When I first contacted  Samsung support, I was told that the laptop and the OS were a single  product, and that it was not possible to provide me with a refund for  the Windows 8 license (I knew I would never use Windows 8 after spending  a couple of hours with it).

It was only after I fired off an  email to Samsung's CEO that someone more senior contacted me about my  request for a Windows 8 license refund. Initially, I was told I would  have to travel a long distance with the laptop to a Samsung support  centre where a technician would examine the laptop to make sure it  wasn't running Windows 8, and that I would most likely have to leave the  unit there for a few days, perhaps a week. Quite a deterrent, but for  me, it was a matter of principle (even though the refund was only for 35  euros, I wouldn't spend 35 euros on some superfluous product).

After  an amicable agreement was reached whereby I would send photos as proof  that no other OS was installed, I then had to run through the actual  installation. After the first failure, I had to Google around to figure  out how to turn off the UEFI SecureBoot - something I'm sure most  average users would shy away from.

After installing Ubuntu 12.10  and applying all updates and using it for a few hours, I noticed that  the fan was running at full speed, and the unit was too hot to touch.  After looking at my system monitor and seeing one of the cores sitting  constantly on 80% and performing a "top" to see kworker hogging the CPU,  I again turned to Google and after some time found that it was  something to do with ACPI. The fix (or workaround?) was to turn off ACPI  IRQs by adding something in /etc/default/grub (and then running sudo  update-grub2). Again, I doubt many average users would persevere to get  something like this fixed.

How about buying a laptop with Ubuntu  (or any Linux distro for that matter) pre-loaded? Not many manufacturers  will offer such an option, especially here in the UK. The 2 or 3 that  did had a very limited range (and I had a very specific need - mini  DisplayPort to drive 2560 x 1440 monitors).

The entire process of  getting Linux working on my laptop invoked the rage within, and I do  apologize if I vented here (and bashed another OS). I have taken your  point on-board. However, I strongly disagree that average users are  given some magical "freedom of choice", and this is based on what I went  through to get my laptop working optimally (pretty darn close to  "perfectly") with Linux.

For me, the pain and suffering was definitely worth the end result.
