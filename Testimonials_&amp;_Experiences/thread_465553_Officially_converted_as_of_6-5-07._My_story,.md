### Post by demonic_fury on 2007-06-05
Ive always been pretty big into Windows (2000 and XP mostly) because Ive always loved playing all the big-name games, being able to install every little piece of software - no matter how obscure - without any problems, for the multimedia stuff, the hardware support, etc, etc. I never thought I would be able to like an operating system other than Windows. And until today I was right. But now, I stand (or sit on my **** in a computer chair) corrected.

Ubuntu 7.04 x86 Desktop, codename: Fiesty. 

Amazing.

I had been feeling a need for something new lately, so I said to myself 'Hell, Im going to try out Linux again'. I tried, and tried, and tried, and then tried again. I went through probably 8 different distros before installing the Windows Vista evaluation copy and researching and downloading more to play with. I was told to check out Ubuntu by one of my old gaming friends. So I checked it out. 'Alright, it cant hurt to try' I was thinking, as I pressed the 'Save to: Desktop' button. Went and ate, came back, burned the ISO, rebooted, popped in the disk, and waited. 

I took a drink of water, looked back at the screen, and there was a magical Ubuntu splash screen already up. Already this disto's LiveCD had gone further on my (pretty recent) hardware than most I had previously tried. But it kept going. All the way to the LiveCD desktop. 

After looking at some of the stuff that you could access, the GNOME environment, the various quirks and interests that I could find on the LiveCD, as well as the remarkable support it was already offering me (Fully detected my sound card, video card, dual core processor, network connections, hell even my bluetooth phone through my Windows network, all still on LiveCD, awesome), I decided to give it a whirl. I clicked on the nice little 'Install Ubuntu' shortcut they had conveniently placed on the desktop, configured the disk partitions, clicked 'Continue' and said bye bye to all my old Windows junk.

Rebooted. Booted up to desktop (through the bios and device checking phase and all) in under 25 seconds. Get XP to do that, and Ill give you my first born child. Not bad for a first impression. My first worry was game support. So I got on Firefox (preinstalled by the way. Finally no more Internet Explorer BS) and looked up WINE. Found the proper downloads and let them save and extract. After extraction, I go to the containing folder looking for 'install.exe', and was sorely surprised to find absolutely nothing. 'Thats fine, I guess Ill just drop these subfolders into the main directory and it should be fine'. So I opened File Browser up to the Computer section, accessed my hard drive...and almost pissed myself. Where were all the nice 'Program Files' and 'My Documents' and 'Windows' folders? Whats all this '/usr' and '/home' and '/tmp' junk? I jumped back on Firefox to figure out what the meaning of this blasphemy was, to learn that Linux doesnt use anything close to the same folder tree setup Windows does. Imagine that.

So I started searching around for info on how to set up WINE and a couple of my Windows games, and immediately came across dozens and dozens of forums, web pages, IRC chat rooms, etc with all the information I could ask for, from real, living, breathing humans, on almost any available computer subject I could hope to have a query on. Hows that for Customer Service, Microsoft?

After setting up WINE and Oblivion and messing around with settings, I finally got bored with that, as it wasnt cooperating very well, and I may or may not have technological ADD..Hey look a butterfly!.. Sorry. But yeah, it wasnt working out too well. 'Thats ok, Ill just mess around with this for the rest of the day, then throw Windows back on so I can play my games' I was thinking about that time. Well, as I started messing with other stuff, I remembered that Unreal Tournament 2004 played natively under Linux. So I unearthed my install disc and loaded it up. Then found the proper patches and got the patches working (again, with the great help of the Ubuntu/Linux community), and played UT for a while. After I got done shredding Skaarj bodies with Flak Cannons, I went back to messing around, and I found 'Add/Remove Programs'. Thinking I could see what I might be able to entertain myself with, I opened it, set the Program filter to 'All Open Source applications' and hit enter. About this time was when my jaw dropped. Hundreds, probably thousands, of things streamed down the open window, each one with an average user rating beside it, and detailed information waiting for you to highlight the program so it could tell you ALL about what the application was, what it did, etc.. Gaming being my primary concern at the time, I decided to narrow it to that field first. I looked through and found at least 30 games that caught my interest, and let them all download. Then I looked for music and video programs. Found a few more to check out. What about internet programs? Yep, plenty more enticed me to click the little check box before punching 'Apply' 

After a very speedy reboot (yeah, old Windows habits.. Sigh), I opened up a few of these new programs, some of which sucked, to be honest, but most of them were so good that they more than made up for the bad ones. The games didnt have the graphical prowess than other games I was used to had, like Oblivion, or Counterstrike: Source, but since when is that what makes a game? The gameplay and the random innovations I came across in a couple of them were amazing, once you figured out the controls at least. The multimedia programs were pretty cool, they all did what they were supposed to, and without any problems that I noticed. The network applications? Pretty nice. 

But you know what was even better than me liking a lot of these programs? The price. Everything was completely free. Even the operating system (which works, in my opinion, much better than an operating system selling for well over 100 bones) was free. Cant beat that deal with a ten foot long tree branch.

So far, Ubuntu has completely astounded me at how simple it is to work with. Smooth setup, smooth operation, smooth appearance, smooth everything. Looks like the open source community just scored another kill on Microsoft, because Im definitely going to be using Ubuntu from now on.


P.S. - Sorry for this amazingly long, and probably over-detailed post, but I thought I would share how much this has changed my opinion on my computing environment.
