### Post by Brunellus on 2006-08-29
pedantic note from an ex-schoolboy Latinist:  the plural of "virus" is NOT "virii." 

"virus" is a third-declension, neuter noun.  So, theoretically, the plural would be "virora,"  but we have no recorded use of this form.  The general consensus seems to be that "virus" (meaning "scum," "filth," or "poison") *had* no plural forms.  

For our purposes, "viruses" is entirely correct, since the English understanding of virus requires a plural.

I've read all the discussions.

Just for the record, since I *hate* bad Latin, here's how you form Latin plurals for common Latin nouns.

Easiest of all are fifth declension nouns like "species"--the plural is the same as the singular.

The next easiest ones, believe it or not, are those ending in -is or -ix.  Their nominative and accusative plurals end in -es.  (for words ending in -ix, the x changes to c, to give -ces).  Thus, crisIS becomes criSES;  matrIX becomes matriCES; unix becomes unices and so forth.

-us nouns are almost as straightforward:  the plural becomes -i in almost all cases.  where they end in -ius, double the i, so -ii:  focus to foci, radius to radii.  

(The complication comes from masculine nouns of the fourth declension, which end in -us, like "status."  Their plurals also end in -us.  I've never seen this in English, though.)

all nouns ending in -um pluralize to -a.  Thus datum to data (note that 'data' are always plural!)
