### Post by rgrashel on 2009-11-24
First, I want to send overall congratulations to the Ubuntu team, from the package maintainers, to the coders, to the testers, to the writers.  Overall, a good release.

On the plus side:

1) Intel graphics -- major improvement.
2) Wireless device detection -- major improvement
3) VPN client access -- works great.
4) New backgrounds are fantastic.
5) Not a lot of brand new changes.  A personal thank you.  I think a lot of Ubuntu users just want stability with minor improvements release over release.

On the negative side:

For Ubuntu maintainers -- if you strive to make minor improvements (release over release), I think this is fantastic.  However, if you are going to make alterations to the things/features that people use OFTEN -- every single day -- you have got to do a better job.  

The first big issue is with Empathy as the chosen IM client.  This is a travesty.  Empathy simply is not ready for primetime regarding basic capabilities.  Things like conversation usability.  New conversation notification.  There are IM clients that have been out for years... whose usability has been tested and improved over time.  Why not just take their lead?

The second major issue is with notification bubbles.  Another travesty.  First, they do not appear in direct connection with the notification area.  They are now lower -- disconnected.  This is not an improvement in usability.  Also, there is no way to dismiss the notification dialog.  Yet worse, if you hover over the notification dialog, it blurs to the point of illegibility.  How about just highlighting or emphasising it?

Something I read on another thread bears repeating.  Ubuntu needs a serious usability designer.  Not a graphics person.  But a real usability person.  Software developers are just not good usability designers.  Software developers tend to think that functionality == usability.  Unfortunate, but true.

Overall, I am still pleased with Ubuntu, but after seeing the aforementioned issues (as well as a couple of other things), it is clear that there is nobody with a real education around usability who is making decisions regarding interface changes.  Hopefully this can change in future.
