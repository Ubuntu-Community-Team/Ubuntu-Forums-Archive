### Post by jtauke on 2008-03-24
Hey everyone.  As I sit here bored at work I'll tell you my little linux story.

I first experience with linux wasn't that good by anyones standard.  I had dled a copy of fedora core 6, with the intent of installing it on an old  hdd I had installed on our family computer.  I went through the installation and thought everything was great. The OS booted fine after installation, but when I restarted the computer to change it back to windows xp.....I got no bootloader menu.  To be honest that was when I got a little scared (my dad's not to accepting of the idea that his os could be gone) After some yelling I told him that I'd fix it. Which I managed to do later that night. But needless to say, my ambition for linux was gone for the time being.

After that I resticted myself to live cd's testing kubuntu and such.  Then I stumbled across xandros (reviews said it was easy to install) so I went for it.  Installed great, the only downside being that it was only a 30 day trial.  I used it for 10 days, then lost intrest again.

Finally, a couple day's ago I was checking out the ubuntu website,and I decided to go for it again.  The os installed fine on the seperate drive. Then I went for the test, I restarted the computer and found a functioning bootloader menu. Victory at last!  

Ubuntu is one of the easiest and fun to use os's I've ever used, and I look forword to learning more about linux.

### Post by xjgnsdof on 2008-03-25
I first used Fedora Core 6 because I was bored one summer and thought "why not?" It was buggy and not particularly easy to use, so I ditched it and turned to Ubuntu after hearing about it from an acquaintance (while drinking, of all things) and looked at Beryl videos on YouTube. Now, I have Xubuntu on my two laptops and Ubuntu on my gaming machine (stats listed in my sig).

My advice to you is to keep looking for new ideas. You'll get bored with an OS if you never try to improve it. Also, make sure that you keep records on how you did things to your machine(s) or fixed problems plaguing it; one day, you'll come across someone on these forums in need of help with something you figured out. Finally, tell other people about Ubuntu. The more people you "convert," the better Ubuntu will eventually become because developers will have more people to please.
