### Post by rmccutchan on 2009-11-26
...is using Ubuntu.  She was having a blast playing with tuxpaint and watching videos on youtube!

Seriously....if she can use Ubuntu, it can't be that difficult to learn.   I showed her how to change the brushes and colors and how to use the erase tool in tuxpaint, and she took off and ran with it.  Granted, tuxpaint isn't a complicated program to use, but she was doing it all by herself.  It was so much fun to watch her get excited about all the bright colors and funny sounds in tuxpaint!

Also, I formatted an external usb hard drive today with Ubuntu and it was a really simple process that Ubuntu walked me through.  Now I have 80gb's of backup storage for important files in case something happens to my main hard drive.

I recently upgraded to 9.10 and it was a very simple and painless process.

I am taking some classes online, and the exams are timed exams.  I never hesitate to start the exams while using Ubuntu because I know I won't have any problems finishing (crashes, hang-ups,  etc...).

Many thanks go out to the creators of Ubuntu and the helpful people on this forum for giving us less computer literate people a choice.  Ubuntu has really opened up a whole new world for me.

### Post by rmccutchan on 2009-12-15
Well, I was in the other room one day, and I came back to the office, and she was playing extremetuxracer without anyone helping her.  She was even choosing different scenes for each race.  It was great:  Her feet are about 2 feet from reaching the ground and her had doesn't fit around the mouse, and she was grinning from ear to ear and having a blast!

A side note...I am a part time photographer and I am working up some fine art black and white photographs using Linux exclusively.  My two apps are Rawtherapee and Gimp.  With those two programs, I am able to do everything I need to do, and they do a fantastic job.  The more I use Gimp, the more I am impressed.  It truly is a top notch program, and it seems to offer a much wider latitude to operate in than proprietary programs.

Imagine:  Ubuntu is easy enough for a 3 year old to operate, yet powerful and secure enough to be a server for a corporation, and versatile enough to be a work station.

Is Ubuntu ready for mainstream?  I think so.
