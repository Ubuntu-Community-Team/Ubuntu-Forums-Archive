### Post by ugm6hr on 2008-05-20
I've been using Ubuntu on my personal laptop for over a year now, but haven't posted my story yet.

I don't ever mention my choice of OS to anyone who I don't already know that might be interested.  It's not about embarrassment; I just don't like coming across as a zealot.

This situation has changed over the past couple of months.

I installed Xubuntu 7.10 on a discarded work computer.  So well, in fact, that a few of my colleagues choose to browse on it in preference to the Windows machines which require a slow login process to the main network.  Interesting that Linux seems to be able to access the work network (with correct proxy settings and a password), while Windows machines can't unless set up by the IT department to do so.

Anyway, getting back to my story...  Yesterday I was asked by a colleague why I use Linux.  "What benefits does it have?"

I haven't ever been asked that directly before.  It does seem like an obvious question from someone who doesn't know much about it.

I offered some explanation about Windows progressively slowing with time and as applications are installed, and the absence of viruses and spyware.  But then I gave the question some genuine thought.  And I don't think that's it.

I started with Linux by just messing around with the Xubuntu 6.06 LiveCD.  Never installed it though.  I was just intrigued about this non-Windows OS that was making waves.

About 18 months ago, my XP installation started to slow, and I figured it was time to re-install it (as I had been doing every 12 months or so).  Then it just failed to boot one day.  Bloody Windows I thought, albeit wrongly.

As an emergency solution, I used the LiveCD again, which seemed to not only work just fine, but also access my HD files.  So I moved on to using PuppyLinux from a LiveCD for a couple of weeks until I got a new HD (i.e. it was a hardware failure, not a Windows issue).  Necessity; nothing to do with viruses, open source ideals, or any of the other commonly used reasons.

Since then, I migrated back to Ubuntu at Feisty's release, and have stayed with it since.

Why didn't I go back to Windows?  My friends asked me that at the time.  I think having been forced to use Linux, I preferred it.  Same reason I pick a certain pair of shoes.  I like it.

Having done a bit more reading, I now know about a variety of benefits associated with open source software and Linux.  But why do I stay?  Because I like it better; I am more productive.

Well.... All except for my addiction to this forum!

### Post by BigSilly on 2008-05-21
Great story ugm6hr. It's funny how many times I try to have lofty principles about it why I moved to Linux, when the simple truth is, like you, I just like it. 

I didn't at first though. Got a PC about 3 or 4 years ago from a local dealer that came with XP pre-installed. It had this for about 18 months, when it started getting BSoD a lot, and hanging on shutdown and little things like that. Did a couple of clean installs during that time hoping to fix it, and then one day bam! - WGA pops up and says I have an illegal copy. It then limited my use of the PC, and displayed constant nagging messages, urging me to buy a legitimate copy.

It was true to some degree, though it was unwitting. The PC had XP already on as I say, but I had no disc and no ID Key sticker either. A mate of mine gave me a tool for finding out what my key was, and an XP disc so I could perform re-installs when I wanted to. This had been fine for a bit but I guess eventually MS found that the key I had was illegal. I had no idea this was the case, as it was a reputable company I bought the PC from. 

Managed to remove the WGA, but the damage was done I guess. I didn't like how MS had the ability to lock me out of my own PC in this way. They might have their business reasons, but really it galled me that they were capable of this. People kept telling me to phone up MS and explain it, but I didn't much fancy begging for their services when I considered I'd done nothing wrong. It set the questions raging about alternatives, and then my mate popped up with Mandriva 2006 Discovery.

I didn't much like it at first. I couldn't get it to do anything, but I was intrigued enough to stick with it, and afterwards went to Freespire which seemed to have a good plan, and was very easy to use. I wasn't quite ready to move over to Linux completely until I discovered Ubuntu 7.04. It just did everything you could want, and even the PC seemed to respond well to it by being noticeably quicker. All my stuff just worked. I was getting more and more confident in the ways of Linux to be able to remove the XP altogether. I haven't looked back at all, and now it just fits. Really settled with it. The whole family like it - the missus has even just bought an Ubuntu Dell laptop.

I kinda chuckle to myself now when I hear of the hoops people are prepared to jump through in order to use Vista. And I'm only glad I wasn't.
