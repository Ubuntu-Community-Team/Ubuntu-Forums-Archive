While I usually run KDE 4 these days, I go through phases of using minimal wm's and small or CLI apps.  The speed difference is quite significant, even on very new hardware.  Try it yourself: install one of these wm's, log out, change your session, and log back in.  Unless your hardware is off the charts awesome, you will notice a difference.

My brain just functions better that way: I think "firefox", and I type "firefox". Now, add to that the fact that the person in that article works primarily with plain text.  Put yourself in the shoes of someone who is a developer, dba, or sysadmin: 
 - You work with systems in the console
 - You type text into a text editor
 - You read and write (text) emails

Why would you want to go through launching all the gizmos and whatsits that constitute GNOME just to do those tasks?  

Then of course, there's the ergonomic aspects of switching between the keyboard and the mouse.  I much prefer environments where I can stick to one or the other, and since entering text with a mouse is not terribly efficient....

Anyway, that's my take on it.  I'm still a sucker for compositing effects, though...

### Post by cardinals_fan on 2009-01-16
> **jrock612 said:**
> I'm not knocking on any of this stuff...but honestly I don't understand why anybody would want something so diminutive.  I mean, modern hardware can run Gnome easily, even slow modern hardware.

Is this just a preference thing?  Can the speed difference (if any) really be so great as to justify such sacrifices?

Enlighten me?
I hope the OP doesn't mind this thread yawing off on a crazy tangent...

1. There is a speed difference.  Is it significant?  That depends on your hardware and your expectations.  I will say this: with the exception of Firefox (text browsers just don't cut it for me), every single one of my apps loads faster than my mind can process.  I don't believe that I should ever have to wait for the computer.  It should be with me at all times, not lagging behind.  Except on very old machines, this has little to do with the window manager.  My software would probably load instantly on GNOME too.  However, I think that my selection of software goes hand in hand with my choice of window manager.  I believe in using the amount of resources and time needed to complete a task, no more.

2. Speed is not just an issue of resource consumption.  I don't *just* choose software based on minimalism.  I use Firefox because it offers the excellent Vimperator extension.  Vimperator has made me faster and more productive, whether at work or relaxation.  Which leads us to...

3. I choose my apps largely based on keyboard control.  I have a few reasons for this:

a. I have to use the keyboard.  Typing by clicking a virtual keyboard is painstaking and SLOW.  However, switching between the keyboard and mouse is a major pain.  Obviously, there is time wasted in the act of moving my hands back and forth.  However, this time is negligible and has few legitimate costs.  The real problem is what it does to my concentration.  Say I'm typing an essay in a word processor and I want to make a word bold.  I have to highlight the word and then click a button.  Even if I've set up the keybindings so I don't have to click the button, I still need to select the word.  That interrupts me and derails my train of thought.  However, when I type my essays in HTML with vim, I just add tags without thinking about moving to the mouse.

b. Typing is more varied than using the mouse.  I'm only 15, and I was already feeling aches in my index finger after a few hours of clicking.  The keyboard isn't perfect, but it isn't quite as hard on my fingers either.

4. Ratpoison and screen make sense to me.  I don't just avoid things like the spinning cubes and expose type effects because they take up some of my RAM.  I just don't find them intuitive.  That word is overused in computing, because it has different meanings to different people.  To me, intuitive software mirrors the way my brain works.  I think of things logically, in rigid order with clarity and simplicity.  I organize my life based on numbering things.  So, I like to use a window manager where my windows are numbered and I switch between them by entering an escape key (I chose Home) and then the number of the window.  I like it when everything has a proper place.  If I were using GNOME, I couldn't do this.  I suppose I could keep every workspace clean for one given app, but that isn't as elegant (to me).

5. My window manager doesn't just avoid wasting resources.  It also doesn't waste screen real estate.  What exactly is the practical use of a window border?  I would only use one when I wanted to close a window.  The rest of the time, it sat there and wasted my space.  Ratpoison keeps every window fullscreen (though I can tile them when I need to see both).  I have no panels clogging up my screen.  Since most of my active apps involve writing, browsing, or viewing, screen real estate is very valuable to me.

6. Ratpoison keeps my life free from unwanted distractions.  I multitask a lot.  At any given time when you see me around the forum, I'm probably also chatting with friends, listening to music, playing a game of chess, and doing my calculus homework.  However, the term multitask can be misleading.  I'm not really doing all that at once.  I focus on one thing at a time, with the others churning in the back of my mind.  For example, when I'm concentrating on a forum post, my subconscious is processing my chess game and that tricky calc problem.  With that many things going on, I don't need to be distracted by my window manager.  It is a tool, nothing more.  Shiny effects and bling just interrupt my thought processes.  There's another pro to minimal apps in multitasking.  I can run a file manager, web browser, editor, music player, shell, image viewer, IRC app, and instant messaging client with little impact on my performance.

7. I enjoy the configuration and control of my apps.  This isn't solely the domain of minimal software, but most of my favorites can be completely and totally configured to match my needs and desires.  My screen config (recently stolen from a kind soul in the screenshots thread) is totally customized to fit **me**.

8. Simplicity = stability.  My apps are simple in more than looks.  I carefully choose programs with simple design and execution.  My system doesn't have any strange daemons doing things I'm not really aware of.  This simple software often has fewer bugs and is easier to fix if things go wrong.  I like to *understand* my system.  You could argue that a slide rule is simpler than a computer, to which I would respond that a slide rule plays a very poor game of chess.  I can do everything with my setup that I could with heavier apps, but in a simpler and more rudimentary way.

9. CLI apps are scriptable.  I can create scripts to automatically run many of my apps.  This is very convenient and useful to me.

10. I like the look of the command line and other minimal software.  To me, nothing is more visually appealing than clean, simple lines and a clear font.  The stark contrast is interesting without being distracting and attractive without being overpowering.  I enjoy the aesthetics of productivity and simplicity.

Written in vim.
