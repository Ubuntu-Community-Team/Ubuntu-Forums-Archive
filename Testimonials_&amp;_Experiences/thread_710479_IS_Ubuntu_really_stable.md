Karlo,

I don't see any of the slow downs that you are describing. I run updates in the background while surfing the web and even downloading other stuff on my Ubuntu machine.

As far as Ubuntu asking you to reboot after an update, I only see that when I have done a kernel update. And even then I don't have to reboot. I can leave it that way until I feel like rebooting to take advantage of the new kernel.

### Post by NiceGuy on 2008-02-28
Hi karlo,

For most people the word stable does not mean multi-tasking, it means... well stable :-P In computer terms most, I think, would sum it up by 'it doesn't crash'. Now in most cases a 'crash' (as previously mentioned) won't be the fault of the operating system but more commonly a specific application causing the system to lock-up etc. Having said that ubuntu, as a whole ('non-default' software from the official repositories included) tends to be more stable, which is to be expected really as all the software has been tested and tailored to work with it and the graphical element of the OS is not tied in with the rest of it in the same way it is with windows.
 
However your complaints seem to be largely about the update manager and that it running being quite an intensive task for your pc. Well unfortunately this isn't really ubuntu's fault as such. It's just that doing all those updates is inherently quite a resource intensive operation and you'll probably find the same on any os you try on your machine. As previously mentioned the fact your getting so many updates (which is why you would notice it more compared to other os's) is a good thing really. But if it REALLY bothers you then I'd suggest that you make the update notifier check at a less frequent interval or even turn off the Automatic updates and check for updates manually at a time when you are not using the PC.

> How can you say Ubuntu runs better on old hardware, if many are making up Linux distros for older hardware like Puppy Linux? and Xubuntu?

I think he was saying it works better as in 'better than some other OS's I could mention'. 

For older hardware the gnome desktop environment can be a little heavy so maybe switching to a different DE or even a different distro which is designed to work on older hardware might be a good option. As Xubuntu is a fork of ubuntu, but tailored to XFCE users and XFCE is considerably 'lighter' than gnome this might be a good option for you, but I wouldn't like to say if it will improve your systems performance when you are doing updates or not... it might, but I dunno :-P

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-02-28
Laptops are very slow compared to desktops; due to heat concerns their motherboards can't shuttle data around very quickly. They also have slower RAM and much slower hard drives.

From your description, the computer only slows down when it's reading and writing a lot to the hard disk. When installing packages, dpkg does read and write the hard disk a lot. This is probably causing other data to get pushed onto the system bus later, hence your computer slows down.

I always have very good multitasking performance from Ubuntu.
