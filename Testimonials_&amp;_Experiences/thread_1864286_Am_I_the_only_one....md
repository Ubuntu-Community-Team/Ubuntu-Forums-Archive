### Post by WorLord on 2011-10-18
Am I the only one who thinks Unity (in 11.10) is **just fantastic**?  

Seriously.  No, seriously.  Stop laughing.  I love it, but am almost ashamed to say so, given all the negative press it seems to be getting from everywhere.  

I've been using Linux since '95, Ubuntu since Warty (4.10), and Gnome since gnome-panel was A) all of GNOME and B) at release 0.3... and think this is one of the best things to happen not just to Ubuntu, but to linux in general in at least a decade.  

I like it so much, that I'm just baffled at the negative opinions.  I do not understand the complaints about its usability, or supposed lack thereof.  It does everything my older Ubuntu did just fine, and often in a less confusing way.  I enjoy having *one* place for launchers and *one* place for indicators.  I enjoy that the menu system has changed, but is still there.  I enjoy that the Dash doesn't take up the whole screen if your screen is utterly huge.  I enjoy the messaging indicator, and think the "Power Cog" menu is just right.  

Now that I think about it, Unity strikes me as a series of design fixes for Gnome-Shell - which is right on the money, if you know the history of the two desktop environments.  

I actually do not understand the complaints about the inability to customize, especially since those complaints seem to be coming from power users and above - people who can easily learn how to do what they want in Unity.  I admit, the customization strategy has changed from "click an option in a box" to "install/remove software," but I kind of like that, because it removes software bloat.   Outside of having the panel and dash positions be set in stone - which IS a pretty big lock-down, I admit - I'm pretty sure I've customized everything about it.  Don't like global menu?  Neither did I, that's why I got rid of it (I have since re-enabled it and am enjoying the extra screen real-estate now).  Don't like the new scrollbars?  Remove 'em.  Missing a monitor?  Install the indicator.  Don't like an indicator?  Remove it.  Launcher too big?  Can't stand intellihide?  That's all *in there* and changeable.  

Why Gnome-Tweak-Tool, for themes and fonts and such?  Ask the Gnome team; they wrote it, and they provide it, and they decided that baseline Gnome shouldn't come packaged with it.  But you know, even though its weird, its *there*, and it works.  I argue it should be installed by default in Ubuntu, but that's kind of a minor defect if one at all.  

It is a *new* shell trying to do things a *new* way; I don't understand the complaints about it not doing things the old way, when there are entire environments (and 'buntu variants!) showcasing actively developed shells dedicated to doing things the old way.  I think "use one of those" is not only a *great* answer, but the only valid answer there is when faced with people who absolutely must have the older WIMP models.  

Are there things I don't like about Unity?  Sure.  I've never met the desktop environment that was 100% what I want; there is ALWAYS a feature or a tweak I wish for.  I don't like that Global Menus hide until mouseover, for example; and the scrollbars aren't *really* useful if you don't subscribe to Andrea's PPA (showcasing changes going into Precise).  I don't think one *should* be able to move the Panel or Launcher, because any other screen edge for either of them causes more issues than otherwise... but then again, I think the code necessary to make those mobile is so small that I can't believe it hasn't been hacked in there already.  I don't like that clicking on a Launcher tile doesn't minimize all the open windows for that application.  But these are minor nitpicks, hardly worthy of the vehement backlash I've seen.

I really don't like what Gnome 3 has become, and I really REALLY don't like how much they removed from the platform (screensavers, appearance control panel applet, etc. etc.).  I applaud Unity for putting that BACK, in fact.  But those are G3 complaints, not Unity complaints, so that's a different story.  

My only real problem with Unity was how unstable it was.  There were about 6 showstopping Compiz crashes, and I was really worried about those, but after 3 releases of Compiz/Unity at right about a week before release, I haven't had any stability issues with it at all.  Even that HUGE memory leak (in Unity 11.04) has gone away. 

I guess what I'm trying to say is: I think what's going on here is pretty awesome, and hope that Canonical keeps it up.  Even if I AM apparently the only fan.

Rock.  :guitar:

### Post by WorLord on 2011-10-22
> **leandromartinez98 said:**
> How come is Unity better for that than a panel with the button for opening a new instance (which you add to the panel for commonly used apps) and another button for focusing on the current one?

The idea behind it is to get rid of the unnecessary division between "application launching" and "application window focus/restoration".  That is the whole problem with the top/bottom panel metaphor, separating Application from Application Window is confusing and unnecessary.  

I wish you could have seen me trying to teach my brother how to use older Gnome2 in its default configuration.  To launch Firefox, he clicked a button on the top panel.  The problem is, in his mind, that was now the "Firefox button".  In his mind, that meant that whenever he wanted Firefox, that's what he clicked on.  Every time he wanted to go to his minimized firefox, or even when he wanted to give his open firefox focus, he would click that button again and open a NEW firefox window.  

He complained about the machine running slow, and Firefox never remembering where he was after he minimized it.  After trying to educate him about the bottom panel being his open Firefox windows, and the top panel only being useful for Firefox if he didn't have Firefox open already, he asked me if there was a way to change that, because it made no sense to have launching and focussing/restoring be on two completely separate sides of the screen.  He did not like the idea that he would first have to look at the bottom to see if he already had any Firefoxes open, and then go up to the top if he didn't... and I can't blame him for that.

This is important to my story: my brother *understood* what I was telling him after I explained it; he just didn't like it.

In response to this, I removed the bottom panel, installed Docky (which I placed on the left), removed the Firefox button from the top panel, and pinned FF to the Dock - exactly the layout Unity has right now, complete with middle-clicking to open a new window and left-clicking focussing/un-maximizing the app.  Not only did he like that much better (because he didn't end the session with like 20 FF windows), but he even used middle-click periodically when he *knew* he wanted a separate FF window instead of a new tab.  Now the metaphor matched his rather limited understanding of how a computer should work: there was a **single** thing on the screen that meant "Firefox," and clicking it gets you Firefox (whether or not it launches it or restores it).  

> **leandromartinez98 said:**
