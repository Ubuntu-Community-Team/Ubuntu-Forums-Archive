2) It took me another several days to have my widescreen LCD display at it's native resolution (1680x1050). The workaround was a simple dpkg reconfigure xorg. That took me another few days to find.

4) I don't have a laptop but one of my friends have and:

6) OpenOffice sucks. And I'm not saying it sucks at opening / saving Word files. I'm saying it has big usability problems. In that tone, Office 2003 sucks less and Office 2007 sucks more.

Note that I personally dislike Microsoft and I use alternatives (to Windows, Outlook, IE etc.) whenever I get a chance. I never buy hardware from Microsoft. And I HATE Vista (which is far worse that XP).

2) Do you think another distribution would be more suited to me?
I'm looking for a distribution that has rock solid stability and exceptional hardware support.

I'm very curious to know what do you think about this.

### Post by scorp123 on 2007-10-27
> **astrotzky said:**
>  1) Why should I stick with Linux? Well ... you don't have to, you know? Nobody is really forcing you. And if you really really want to have an OS that "just works" with superb hardware: Consider buying a Mac. Apple machines are simply wonderful and so is their OS; 

But if you really want to stick with Linux ... well. I use Linux since 1996 and trust me, it used to be *way harder* back then. What I learned over the years is that knowing how to operate a search engine can make a difference between finding a solution in just a couple of seconds or not finding anything even after hours of Googling. There are many good examples on how to get Google to show the results you're really interested in, in case you ever have a Linux problem. I suggest you make yourself familiar with all those Google options. Trust me: "Google is your friend" is not just an empty phrase, it's pure truth. It's a sometimes complicated "friend" though, and asking the "right questions" can make a big difference between finding the one and only real solution to a problem or not finding anything useful at all.

Ultimately you never ever have to learn any obscure Linux shell command or 'workaround' if you don't want to ... all you really need to know is how to correctly ask Google about this stuff. That's the whole secret. The rest will come alone by itself.

The second thing I'd recommend you: Keep your browser bookmarks organised! And make backups of them! Consider using web sites such as **delicio.us** and other similar ones so you can store your bookmarks on the web and use them remotely from other computers.

 Whenever I find a very useful page I bookmark it. And I have been doing this since 1996. Of course ... some pages die, they go away, they get closed, or they move to new domains. So there's some 'housekeeping' you need to do here and there. But nontheless I have an excellent collection of bookmarks that allows me to quickly solve any problem I encounter -- instead of Googling all over again, I just go quickly through my bookmarks ... click click ... and voila I have the solution on my display. Just make sure you organise your bookmarks in a useful way and all the solutions to your problems are just a few mouse-clicks away.

Once you start doing things this way you will soon find that you no longer need hours to solve any problems you encounter ... just seconds. And then you will also feel far more comfortable with Linux (no matter which distro) as OS. :)

But take your time ... :)
