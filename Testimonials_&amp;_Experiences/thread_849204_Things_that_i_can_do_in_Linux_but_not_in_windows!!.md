### Post by nickdbliss on 2008-07-04
I was going through these forums and found people telling, why they hate linux and what they can do in windows but not in linux. With my experience in linux and windows, i thought to list down what i can do in linux but not in windows. (Some text is taken from Dan Martin Blog. Thanks Dan for inspiration.you are cool man [http://dmartin.org](http://dmartin.org))

Ok so here we go:

[LIST=1]
[*]**Update every single piece of software on my system with a single click.** In windows, i get updates for my core system and at most office. But what about all the softwares i have installed. No problem with linux. I feel more safe n secure in linux as all bugs n loopholes are fixed in updates. Just a matter of click. In windows you have to update all your softwares one by one.lol
[*]**Update nearly everything on my computer without a reboot.**
I just love this feature. Even when the kernel is updated, your system ll continue to run smoothly over previous kernel until u plan to restart. But in windows even those non-critical updates require you to restart your pc. Ah!
[*]**Keep my system secure without software that consumes my system resources, requires my time, and frequently bugs me.** Examples are Antivirus softwares, Spywares, Firewalls like zonealarm.Never trusting software.  You have to go through life assuming every bit of software and every website on the Internet is going to screw you over.  What a sorry state of affairs that is. All of this requires your attention, slows your computer, and ruins the open experience of the Internet.  None of this is necessary in Linux.  You get your software through your distribution.  As long as you can trust your distribution, you can trust the software available.  Having a firewall is a good thing even in Linux, but most of us have a firewall built into our Cable and DSL modems, or our wi-fi router.  A software firewall in windows is as much used to keep malware from calling out as it is to keep outside intrusions from coming in, and you don't have the same concerns in Linux (since, as I said, you can trust your software).
[*]**Run an entire operating system for free without pirating software, and without breaking the law.** Whether you accept it or ignore it, but the fact remains that you are breaking the law if you are using pirated softwares and if caught, it ll cost you all your earned money till date. On the other hand, linux is completely free and comes with free cool softwares with the back of thousands of developers. And every night you can go to sleep without worrying about being caught.
[*]**Take my settings with me where ever I go.** 
This is a good one. Since all your settings are saved in your home folder, just copy the folder and paste on other system via USB PenDrive etc. You will still have all your settings. So much flexibility. On Windows, some settings are under your home folder and some are in the registry.  So your settings are not portable.
[*]**Understand everything that is going on in my computer.**
To be honest, this was the main reason why i switched to linux. In windows, which is just like a black box, you never know what is happening in the background and not much scope of learning. But in linux, i am the master of my system. I can see, modify and delete whatever i want. In windows,If you hit bugs, your only option is to hope Microsoft fixes it.Or, perhaps you can submit a bug report to Microsoft, spending your time improving software that  a company makes billions from.  Under Linux, you can look at the system logs, where you can see most issues.  You can search for the log messages on Google, and can usually track the cause and often find a fix.  If not, I can even go look at the source code to find the offending problem.  Granted, most people aren't capable or don't have the time to look at the source code.  But the fact that tens-of-thousands of geeks do is often very, very helpful.  And if you do spend the time filling out a bug report, you are helping other people just like yourself, not contributing your time to a rich software company.
[*]**Customize every aspect of my desktop.**
In Windows, you are more or less stuck with what you are given.  Sure, you can install buggy skinning engines, or you can pay Microsoft extra for the ability to put skins on your desktop.  But even these aren't very adaptive.  It's just a new coat of paint on the same desktop.  Under Linux, I can choose the window manager, the desktop environment, the theme, the GTK engine, the icon theme, the special effects (see Beryl or Compiz), the filesystem browser, and so on.  Nearly every aspect of the system has competitive options.  If you look around the internet at screenshots of various Linux desktops, you rarely see two that look the same. 
[*]**Benefit from competition between projects for each system on my computer.**
As I mention earlier, there are options for every aspect of the Linux desktop.  Not only is it fun to try the various options, but it leads to better software as multiple projects compete against each other to be the best.  Can you imagine competing printing backends, competing desktop environments, or competing USB mounting systems on Windows? 
[*]**Run thousands of great pieces of software that only run on Linux.**
Just like Windows, Linux has software that doesn't run on Windows.  Great pieces of software like Amarok, Bluefish, Neverball, Gnumeric, K3B, Beryl, gdesklets, and MythTV.  I know this is a chicken-and-egg point, where Windows has the exact same situation. Too often I hear "I can't switch to Linux because it doesn't run [insert Windows software]".  My reason for pointing it out is just to make it clear that this is a two-way street.
**[*]Learn about, support, and appreciate the value of free software.**
I believe free software is important to us all.  Even if you use non-free software, the free software movement ensures checks and balances on non-free software by offering an alternative.  By running a free operating system and becoming involved in the community, I've contributed to free software, even if only in a small way. As said by dan and now by me.
[*]**I can install only the parts of OS that i want.** (Thanks atomkarina for the reminder)
With linux, i can install only the parts of the OS that i want, which is very good if you have an old low memory system like i do. Mine old pc has the configuration which goes like this: intel P3 processor, 128 ram and 20 gb hard disk. It was quite obvious that hardy wont work like a charm. So i started with intalling an ubuntu command line system first. Then after adding repositories to the source list, i updated and upgraded my system. It was time for GUI,so i installed xorg. I had a GUI but without window manager. I had various options to start with and installed openbox (among xfce,Icewm,Fluxbox,FVWM-Crystal etc.) Then there were login manager, browsers,file managers and package manager. End result i got a sweet smooth working OS. So the bottom line is that linux has so many options for you ,which you dont get much with windows.
[*]**With linux, you get the privilege to be a part of huge linux community like ubuntuforums.**(Thanks to lisati)
If you get linux then unlike windows, you get a chance to be a part of the linux community. You get information and advice about your hardwares and OS from people who dont have any selfish motives. They never gonna ask you for your money. You can even end up having some friends.
[*]**My system is very fast with linux than windows.**(Ah how did i forgot a major point like this one. shadowman90 and cptasker gave the hint.)
My personal experience shows that when i was using windows xp , instead of showing a good speed (with the hardware), it was degrading day by day. It used to get so frustating at times that i had to format and do a fresh install always. I should mention here that i used to have dual OS but then i left windows for good. 
But why Linux runs faster than windows? Answer lies in the fact that linux is very smart and efficient at handling the memory usage than windows. 
[/list]
I just love the number of options, flexibility, learning experience and support that you get with linux. The only thing that windows is used in my house now, is for gaming. But since gaming companies are now realizing the power and growth of linux among users, they have started to work to develop games for linux as well. Though quite less in number, but days are not far when the scenario will change.

For the list of 42 Best Commercial and free Linux Games (Runs without any need of wine) which includes games like Quake4,Quake3,Doom3 etc. check these links out. It will give you the picture that wind has started to blow in other direction.And i am loving it on my face.lol [http://www.linuxlinks.com/article/20080530054213402/CommercialGames.html](http://www.linuxlinks.com/article/20080530054213402/CommercialGames.html)
[http://www.linuxlinks.com/article/20080510052539217/Games.html](http://www.linuxlinks.com/article/20080510052539217/Games.html)


Cheers for Linux. Love it.

Nick
