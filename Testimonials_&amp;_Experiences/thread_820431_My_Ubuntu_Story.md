### Post by Arthur Archnix on 2008-06-06
I started using Linux about four years ago. All I knew was that there was this thing called Linux and all the not-cool kids were doing it. So I downloaded slack, printed the slackbook, went home and sat down to install it. About a day later I installed Windows XP and said there was no way Linux would every be ready for the average Joe to use.

Maybe a few months later Microsoft did something that upset me. I can't remember what it was. Maybe it was some new DRM thing, or some thing I couldn't customize  because  Bill decided the colour purple  wasn't allowed, or maybe my dog died. I don't recall, but whatever it was I decided to try Linux again, and by this time I'd learned that there were these things called "distros", some of which were almost, but not quite, as popular as discos were in 2004. 

So I downloaded and installed Mandriva; Got a desktop. Then I downloaded and installed SUSE; Got a desktop and music. By now it's 2005 or so and I'm dual-booting XP because I still need wireless. 

Then I met Ubuntu and everything changed. Everything just worked. It was incredible. Sure, I still needed to tweak things, but with this forum community and the IRC channels it wasn't just easy, it was fun. I've been using Ubuntu full-time since Dapper (I flirted with Breezy, but we didn't hit it off and Dapper was out by the time I got stuff working). I've been completely Windows free for the past two years. Over that time, the progress that Ubuntu has made pushing Linux into the mainstream is amazing.

Ubuntu has been like Tiger Woods, someone so good they actually force everyone around them to get much better just to compete. Everyone starts reaching new levels, because there's this one monster on the playing field showing every else what's possible. 

Ubuntu has brought me to the point where I can use Linux for everything I need to do. It has taught me how to fix stuff that breaks. Customize things the way I want it. And just generally, do what I want with my OS.

And this is where our paths diverge. Because with Gutsy a little bit, and Hardy a whole lot, Ubuntu is now breaking Ubuntu more than I am. I can tolerate my own failures because... well, I've got to live with me all the time, so we/I/me have to get along. So if I apt-get remove an essential component of my system, well, I forgive myself and fix it. But when Ubuntu restarts on me for no reason, or goes to sleep for no reason... well, there are some things even The Dude will not abide.

This itch of dissatisfaction has been growing over the past year. I've tried Arch twice. Fedora 8, then 9. OpenSuse 10. And every time I crawled back to my Ubuntu womb, thankful that my old friend was still there, singing soothing songs in my ear like... like a sultry Amy Winehouse number from 2005. But with Hardy, I didn't crawl back into the arms of an old friend. I crawled back into the arms of Amy Winehouse circa 2008; you know what I mean: you know the talent is still there, buried deep down. But god, what a wreck.

And so I finally jumped. Into Debian. And it's like meeting someone who reminds you of your old best friend. There's a lot that's the same. Same old apt. All the major pieces are in the same place. Sure, this new friend is more complicated. You can't reboot after an install and start surfing wireless like your old friend. But this friend never lets you down. Once you get your wireless up. It's always up. If you don't tell it to standby. It doesn't. 

Anyway, man, this was a long goodbye. But I've never said thanks, or goodbye before. And really, how incredible does an OS and community have to be that you feel like saying goodbye to it? In my defense, I'm not usually so overly verbose. My goodbye to windows was much more succinct and in fact involved precisely nine less digits than it took to type this. 

I owe Ubuntu a great big thank you. So thanks to all the developers, the community, Mark, and everyone else who makes this thing happen. I really do appreciate everything you've done. Thanks very much. I wouldn't recommend you change a single thing. As far as I'm concerned it just works, and it just works great.

This is probably going to be misinterpreted as an extremely long winded experience and testimonial. But in fact the message is this: 

So long. And thanks for all the fish.
