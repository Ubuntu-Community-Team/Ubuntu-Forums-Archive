### Post by Weyland on 2007-01-05
Life with Kubuntu or Why I can't live without Windows XP...

Apologies to all those expecting yet another rant about Ubuntu. You are about to be disappointed.

First a little background is in order. I wouldn't describe myself as a geek at all. My first PC was purchased in 1997 and came with a whopping 32MB of RAM and Windows 95. I can well remember how strange everything appeared to be. C and D Drives were a total mystery to me. Nevertheless, by reading the manual and with a lot of help from Leo LaPorte's "Call for Help" and "Screensavers" series' on the then ZD TV channel, I started to figure things out. (Nice one Leo!) Soon emails were mailed, letters written and printed and internets were surfed. The frequent system lockups, crashes and "blue screens of death" were just accepted as a normal part of computing life.

I had heard mention of Linux on the "Screensavers" program of course, but hey, Windows was complicated enough, Linux belonged to a whole different universe. So I proceeded along the Windows path. In the fullness of time, 95 made way for 98, then got itself upgraded to 98SE. Win ME was a particularly frustrating era in my computing odyssey. By this time my knowledge had improved enough to build my own machine. During this period my curiosity led me to attempt the installation of Red Hat Linux, version 6, I think. I found the Gnome desktop to be very strange and the applications to be very spartan, even primitive. Needless to say, I returned to Windows and obtained the new XP version.

Now here was a breath of fresh air. Much more capable and reliable than earlier versions, I took to XP like a penguin to water. A clean install of XP, together with the associated drivers, delivers a good usable system. For a safer computing environment a wise operator also installs the XP2 upgrade, perhaps the DotNET framework and of course the obligatory Firewall, Anti-Virus and Anti-Malware programs too. Lets not forget the third party drivers required for the printer and scanner, all of which come with "helpers" who run quietly in the taskbar and let you know frequently what a good job they are doing. With all this running in the background that clean XP install takes a major performance hit. Is it any wonder that the system slows to a crawl and even locks up when you attempt to run too many applications at once?

Another delight is the monthly fight with Mr Genuine Disadvantage when security upgrade time rolls around. Windows tends to get somewhat flaky after a prolonged period of hard usage and experience has taught me to reinstall annually. Looking to stay within the fold, I downloaded a beta version of Vista to try out. What a disappointment. I have an Athlon XP2500 1.8 GHz processor with 512MB of RAM and Vista will barely run! Time to build a new computer and take a fresh look at Linux.

I had been using a Knoppix live CD for awhile, just for rescuing data from broken Windows systems you understand, so I was used to the KDE desktop. Not unnaturally, I tried installing Knoppix to my second Hard Disk. All went well, but alas, the sound system was not recognised or configured, In addition my USB ADSL modem posed problems too. The support for Knoppix amongst its users could be a tad better. Anyways, I decided to look further afield for a more "user friendly" distro.

I came across Ubuntu on "distrowatch" but initially rejected it because of the default Gnome desktop. It was quite a while before I realised one could have different desktops on the same  Linux installation. Even longer before I found out Gnome apps run uder KDE and vice versa. Well I finally realised a KDE version of Ubuntu was available on CD and decided to give it a whirl. The presence of an enthusiastic and above all friendly user base willing to post detailed answers to beginners questions was THE major factor in my choosing to fly Ubuntu.

Now a note to any potential converts from Windows reading this. Its important to approach installing and learning to use a new operating system in the right frame of mind. Know that you will experience problems and will need to learn a new way of doing things. Be assured, you are unlikely to be the first with any problems encountered. A quick search through the Ubuntu forums or even Google will throw up several useful pointers. Give Ubuntu a fair trial, it deserves that at least.

I decided to go with a dual boot situation. To make things simpler, Linux was to be installed on its own hard disk. Installation of Kubuntu was a breeze. In approximately twenty minutes I had a good bootable, useable system. Compare this with an install time of about ninety minutes (including the XP2 update) for Windows! 

Initial impressions were quite favourable. The screen resolution and refresh rate left something to be desired. I have a generic Nvidia graphics card and Kubuntu does not ship with the non-free drivers so I wasn't too surprised. This issue might at first appear a bit confusing to new users but I do understand and respect the reasons for not shipping the Ubuntu distributions with the non-free drivers. As per my previous experience with Knoppix, the USB ADSL modem was not detected. There is however an excellent walk-through article in the Ubuntu Community Documentation concerning the installation of this particular item. After following the instructions carefully and a reboot I found myself online.

Using the terminal was a very strange experience at first. Things proceeded apace when I discovered one could cut and paste instructions too. Very handy. Using the command line does tend to grow on one after awhile and after a month or so of regular use, I feel quite at home with it.

The "cut and paste" step by step command line instructions available in the online Ubuntu Documentation are particularly helpful to a new user.

Okay, now to fix my videocard drivers. I attempted to install and configure the Nvidia drivers manually. After a couple of hours without success, I managed to break my system! This was down to my ignorance and overzealous use of the sudo command. Rather than attempt to back track and repair my damage, I decided a reinstall would be a better way to go.

Well, an hour or so later and I found myself back online. This time, I did some more research and found reference to the "Automatix2" installer script. Now some folks seem to have trouble with this little gem but I can heartily recommend it. Using Automatix2 I managed to install the required Nvidia drivers, video codecs and mp3 capability without problem. Its a great tool to keep around on your system.

A reboot presented me with the Nvidia splash screen but alas, no improvement in the resolution or refresh rate. Having gained a certain familiarity with the xorg.conf file previously, I loaded said file up in the text editor to check the configuration. After looking through the file, it appeared that the Kubuntu installer script had not recognised my monitor. Why should it? I'm still using the same one that came with '95. Rather sensibly, the installer had played safe and installed a "Generic Monitor" with a reduced set of specifications. After a little research on the web, I edited the Horizontal and Vertical Refresh ranges with the correct settings for my monitor and saved the file. After a reboot, I found I could enjoy the resolution and refresh ranges to which I have become accustomed.

I have been using Firefox and Thunderbird for quite awhile now so once my data had been swapped over I began to feel at home right away. The file system is pretty confusing at first, then again, I remember my confusion back in the Windows 95 days too.

Kubuntu has been working away on my machine for a good six weeks now. It gets used everyday, so I can say I have given it a fair test. The verdict is... I like it!

Kubuntu is clean, polished and without all the excess security stuff, it's fast. I can do almost all the stuff I need to, in Kubuntu. The multiple virtual desktop spaces are a real neat feature. One strange thing, and this kind of creeps up on a new user, after using Kubuntu for a while, I began to notice that I was becoming more productive. Less time is spent fighting Windows, more time is spent actually doing WORK! Also, in the time I have been using Kubuntu, I have yet to crash the system. (Apart from the initial installation hiccup). Most importantly, computing has become fun again.

So to the Ubuntu development team and Mr Shuttleworth Sir, I salute you! Ubuntu is a fine distribution and has certainly won me over.

And why you ask can I not live without Windows?

Well, I can't live without eBay's Turbolister, Quickbooks and my favourite Auction Manager, all of which require Windows to run. Some people have managed to get Turbolister to run in wine, sadly I have not yet managed to get it to run with complete success. My Auction Manager application requires Internet Explorer to run and even then doesn't play fair in wine. Earlier versions of Quickbooks do run in wine but alas, my version is not backwards compatible. I find myself having fallen into the proprietory file format trap. Doomed forever to the relentless upgrade treadmill or to start afresh with a more Open application.

An unsupported UMAX scanner is another reason too. The next scanner I buy will be from a vendor who fully supports Linux.

For the foreseeable future, I'll be happy to dual boot but there may well be a market for a Quickbooks alternative that runs natively under Linux. How about some applications that work with the eBay API too.

Software and Hardware Vendors take note!
