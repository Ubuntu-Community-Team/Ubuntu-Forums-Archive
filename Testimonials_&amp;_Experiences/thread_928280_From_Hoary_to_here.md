### Post by Steve1961 on 2008-09-23
I've been using Ubuntu since Warty so in some ways I feel like an 'old hand', but in many ways I'm still very much a newb.  Anyway thought I'd just give a brief synopsis of my experiences here.

When I first tried Linux it was a case of necessity.  I wasn't working in IT at the time but I'd become increasingly interested in computers and had decided to build my own system.  Problem was, I couldn't afford a copy of XP.  So, back in 2004 I think, I tried installing Suse 9 (cant remember the exact version) and then Mandrake 10.  On the whole things worked, but usb sticks didn't automount, wireless networking was still very much a 'dark art' in Linux, and the learning curve was very very steep.  Anyway, when Warty was released I remember being really impressed that I could plug in a usb stick and it would automount without me having to manually mount the thing.  That really convinced me to stick with Linux, and apart from a frenetic period of distro hopping (2005 - 2006) I always kept a version of Ubuntu on a partition of my hard drive.

I learnt so much during this period that I decided that I wanted a career in IT rather rather than working in my previous job (university lecturer teaching and researching sociology and social policy).  However, financially this wasn't really viable, so I stuck with what I knew.  I just continued to learn from these forums and lots of reading.  By this time myself and my family (wife and three teenage boys) had four desktops and four laptops (I know I know, but I love buying new gadgets), all of which ran Ubuntu.  My boys are gamers and I'd managed to get World of Warcraft running in Wine, I had file sharing sorted out through Samba, all PCs shared a single networked printer, and once I'd learned how to ssh into each of the machines, administering the network was child's play.

Nevertheless, until 18 months ago Linux was still very much a hobby - until I was suddenly made redundant in March 2007.  As traumatic as this was it gave me the chance to start something new.  I had great plans about starting a mobile PC maintenance and repair service specialising in Linux installation, but after getting started reality hit home.  Lots of yellow pages, local newspaper and leaflet drops produced next to no business.  So in desperation I moved to the 'dark side' and took the Comptia A+ qualification to become a Windows IT technician.  This worked, and for the next 12 months I earned a reasonable living removing viruses and spyware, cleaning registries, re-installing windows, and setting up wireless networks for domestic customers.  Needless to say one of my main tools during this period was the Ubuntu live cd, which allowed me to rescue data from dead windows systems prior to a reinstall.

I can't say that I exactly enjoyed this period of my life, but it did give me the confidence to apply for other IT jobs.  I also continued to learn about Ubuntu/Linux whilst I was earning my living fixing broken windows boxes.  And finally, just three months ago, I secured a job as a support engineer with a company that manufactures DNS and web caching server appliances that run Linux. Better still, all the desktops at work run Ubuntu, and so what I've learned as a hobby I can now use to make a living.  Interestingly, they had experienced recruitment problems because most computer science graduates, apparently, had demonstrated an appalling lack of knowledge about Linux, and so they had started to advertise positions on the local LUG mailing list instead (which is where I heard about the job).

Anyway, long story short, what started off as a hobby has now turned into a career.  Working with Linux 'experts' all day long has made me realise just how much I still have to learn, but I'm looking forward to the challenge, and really enjoying the learning experience.  So thank you Ubuntu, and these forums, for playing a part in giving me the knowledge and experience to change careers.  It just goes to show that you never know what the future holds!!  From sociologist to Linux support engineer.  Go figure!!
