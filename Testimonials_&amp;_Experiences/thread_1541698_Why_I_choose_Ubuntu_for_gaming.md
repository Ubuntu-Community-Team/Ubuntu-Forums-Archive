### Post by 2Karl on 2010-07-29
This was originally posted as a reply to a thread in Gaming and Leisure, but it was suggested I repost here as a full testimonial.

Why do I stick with Ubuntu for gaming?

I gradually grew more and more fed up with Windows and Microsoft's continual "mandatory upgrade" drive. A little background:

I worked in the computer games industry for a long time, on titles such as Dungeons and Dragons Online and Lord of the Rings Online, and during that time we had a fair number of people enquire about Linux versions of the games. The results were always the same - redeveloping for Linux would be too costly. But the reason for this was that all of our games were developed on Direct X as opposed to easily portable systems such as SDL, OpenGL etc. The reason for the Direct X blanket was that Microsoft tended to thrust DX on the managers and even the programmers (while they were at college) until it became the "industry standard". Anything that was NOT Direct X was deemed to be sub-par.

I would argue the case for OpenGL, but I was usually laughed out of the office with such ridiculous phrases as "games have moved on from Pong" and other nonsense. When I pointed out that Doom 3, Quake 4 and others were developed using OpenGL my words fell on deaf ears. To my managers, OpenGL equated to Open Source which equated to loss of revenue. They just didn't get it.

And so I made the switch to Linux around the time that Windows Vista was released. It was as much to prove that it was a viable option as much as anything else. I think that was back in the Dapper Drake days... my memory's hazy. Anyway, I ditched windows and soldiered on with Linux. At first, my plan was to show that Linux could handle the best that windows could do and then some.

Over time, Ubuntu gradually improved (although the sound system still leaves a lot to be desired - I'm still using ALSA until that annoying PulseAudio latency issue has been dealt with) and more and more games were getting native versions. Neverwinter Nights, Unreal Tournament, Doom 3 all ran at least as well as they had done on Windows, and a variety of other titles ran admirably though Wine, but something was missing. The joy seemed to have been sapped out of them. I gradually realised that I had little or no desire to play these mainstream games at all. I then turned to the "underground" if you will - those small developers who were developing SPECIFICALLY for linux. The games they produced weren't always graphically breathtaking, but it didn't matter - the quality of gameplay was superior to the mindless run-and-gun or button-click-combat of the mainstream. Games like Eschalon and Penumbra blew me away with their storyline and atmosphere.

As well as these commercial games, I also had a lot of fun playing around with source-ports of old titles. Things like Doomsday, eDuke32 and Dark Places. To me these were games in themselves - making sure you had all the right components to build the source, tweaking your system to make sure the game was running in the best way it could... I think I probably spent more time building and configuring these than I did playing them, but I don't mind, because it was all highly enjoyable and it all taught me a lot about how my system works. As many of the games I play are 32-bit and I run a 64-bit operating system, there are often times when I need to fiddle around with libraries in order to get games to run, but again, it's all fun, it's all educational, and it makes me feel like I am in control, NOT the system.

So although I started out to prove that Linux was just as good as Windows, I now continue to game on Linux as it offers the same thing the OS does as a whole - something different. This is not just Windows for free, nor do I want it to be. Would I like to see more linux game development? Of course. Would I like to see the masses hopping across to Linux and bringing mainstream games with them? Probably not.
