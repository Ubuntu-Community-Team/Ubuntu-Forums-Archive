### Post by Golden Warrior on 2006-04-22
I've been using Linux for about 3-4 years now.  My first distro was Red Hat 9.  It worked fine except that I had a heck of a time getting X to run.  Once I got X to run it worked fine for a while, but I found it to be pretty limited (it was an old distro after all).

After that I decided to try Fedora Core and that seemed to work better except that I had all sorts of strange errors come up everytime I tried to update anything and naturally it was royal pain to go through that mess and sort things out.  It just seemed so ungodly bloated at the time.

After that I went to Debian 3 woody for something more robust so I can really buckle down learn more of the ins and outs of how Linux systems work.  I discovered that I really liked the apt-get tool and .deb packaging system, but I was really disapointed in the lack of support for anything really current.

Next I picked up Slackware 10.  Slackware was just the greatest thing to me at the time.  Not only did it work well, it was setup such that it it was easier for me to go in and install only what I wanted and it while it didn't have the greatest hardware support, it's still setup such that it's not as painfull to download and compile drivers.

The biggest problem I had is the packaging system had absolutely no way of resolving dependencies automatically.  Getting used to that was tough to say the least but for what it's worth, you do end up with a higher working knowledge of your software (not to mention really missing tools like apt-get).

While on Slackware I also tried some other distros like Suse and some odd ones like Puppy Linux, but was left very unimpressed.  Some of them were more interesting than others (ex Damn Small Linux requiring so little space) but I just couldn't find one to really settle on.

I found Knoppix to be usefull to keep around for repairing broken distros and problems with Windows, but it quickly looses it's appeal if you go and install it to your hard drive (basically becomes just like Debian and after a while becomes a real chore to keep up to date).

Then I hit Gentoo.  Gentoo was really impressive.  It had good documentation, the portage system worked extrememely well, and after compiling my whole system from scratch I had never seen such efficiency and performance out of my hardware.  I would have stayed with it, but it's such a chore to compile everything that you spend more time doing that than using your system (kind of defeats the purpose of having a system IMO).

Then I decided to try the other Unix-like OSes.  I gave FreeBSD a whirl and found it to be a really effecient and fast system.  I still keep it around, but just for fun really, not something I want to make my primary OS though.  It's a great OS in it's own right, but like Gentoo and Debian some things were just too much of a chore to set up.

The other Unix-like OS I tried was Solaris 10.  Now that Sun was giving it out for free I figured it couldn't hurt.  Solaris was OK, but it has a serious problem with hardware support.  Yeah, it's gotten better now that it's open source, but it trails behind FreeBSD and Linux.

I ended up going back to good old Slackware after that.  All this time I had heard about Ubuntu and Kubuntu, but at first I didn't think too much of it.  I thought it was just going to be another Fedora or Suse like distro with a bunch of stuff installed and running that would more than likely get in my way than anything else.

Then one day someone on another forum I go to brought it up and was praising it.  He was a fellow Slackware user and knows Linux extremely well and we was going on and on about the 3D desktop switching he had running.

About a month later I finally decided to give Ubuntu a try.  Everything seemed to go well, hardware support was good, I didn't have jump through hoops to get X running, etc.  What really got me was when I went to install the ATi drivers for 3D accelerated graphics and the driver for my wireless card.  I had never, ever had an easier time installing those.  That and how Unbuntu manages installed software.  I've never seen something that works so well.  I've manage to break the system in Fedora, Suse, Debian, and Gentoo, but Ubuntu is the first to stand up to me and that's definately a first.

So congrats to all the developers here.  Unbuntu is the first distro I've really truely feel is just right so thanks guys! :mrgreen:

I've been telling my friends who want to try Linux to try Ubuntu.  If anything they can get the live DVD and see how well it would seem to work before they install it.
