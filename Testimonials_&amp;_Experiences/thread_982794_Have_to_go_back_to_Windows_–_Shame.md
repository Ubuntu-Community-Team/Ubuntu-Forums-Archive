### Post by Quarkrad on 2008-11-15
I was hoping to ditch winxp and roll over to Linux/Ubuntu but it looks like this is not possible – I think Ubuntu is great and will maintain a partition to see how it develops but I get the impression it is not quite there yet.  There are three key areas that have forced the move back:

Local Backup
ATI video driver
iTunes

Backup.  In windows I have a small app (there are many for windows) that launches every time the pc is shut down. It sync's user folders on hd0 and hd1 so I always have a backup.  How simple is that?  No user intervention.   I have struggled for weeks with rsync and Unison but as far as I can get is manually forcing transfer between folders.   Doing it automatically via Cron just does not work (for me).  Also there seems no way of running the transfer at Shutdown – it has to be done at specific times or manually.  With all respect to other members of my family they do not want to mess around manually doing 'housekeeping'.  Their expectation is that the 'PC' does it for them.

ATI.  I have to run Ubuntu in low graphics mode because I have found it impossible to install an ATI graphics driver.  I was hoping that Ubuntu would support the two biggest players in the market, nvidia and ATI.   There are volumes of instructions re ATI installation but there are all set in an ideal world, when you get a terminal instruction that does not follow the 'instruction' you are down a hole.
I have had some great help via foums trying to get the latest ATI drivers installed but the latest brick wall, which many people appear to have it is:

iTunes   Banshee looks good but I'm I really going to re-format my ipod and then hope it works after – I don't think so. Gtkpod looked like the trick but how do you select all the playlists in one go – it appears to only let you select them one at a time.  Not very useful when you have hundreds!  I cannot find a forum site or 'anywhere' to ask questions or get help.


I think Ubuntu is great and am fighting the rest of the family to adopt – trouble is I'm loosing.  I have a bit of a  'mecano'  mentality so I don't mind – the problem is they jut something that works.  I get impression that if I wanted a 100 desktops and laptop PC all over the world inter working and sync'd it could be done with Linux (although it would be impossible for someone like me to set up) but for a simple home PC using internet, work processing with simple backup thrown in there are a few brick walls to be knocked down. I do not like win but installing apps/drivers is, for 99% of the time, 'kids play.

### Post by carusoswi on 2008-11-15
Looks to me as though you are giving up on Ubuntu over just a few issues that with some work you should be able to overcome.  I don't mean that critically, because like you and many here, I have had my moments with Ubuntu, also.  I don't know what ATI card you are using (if something in your post gave it away, I wasn't watching that closely), but, of all my Ubuntu issues, 8.10 seems to accommodate my 9600 better than any previous version.

I hope other posters don't jump you for your post - some here get quite defensive as though only geeks who contribute as developers or beta testers have any right to criticize Ubuntu.  To them, I would point out that, like most products, unless it is developed only for the sake of the development exercise, Ubuntu has little value until users with no OS interest other than using it start doing just that in big numbers.  I don't know the numbers and make no representation as to whether they are already big or not.  Just pointing out that criticism from users is also valid.

You do, of course, realize that when you point out that Ubuntu does not yet support this or that, generally the reason lies not so much with shortcomings in Ubuntu's development as it does in the reluctance of vendors to develop Linux compatible drivers or release code so that others can develop them.

Only time and more demand created by more Linux users will likely counter that reluctance.

My guess is that, if you are keeping your Ubuntu partition just to stay in touch, then you have the right blend of curiosity and open-mindedness that will keep you a-pecking at your Ubuntu problems until one day you overcome them.

I switched the Mrs.' computer to Ubuntu a couple years ago.  Her computer use is almost totally restricted to email, browsing, shopping, ordering concert tickets, etc.  Gmail was acting up on the XP side for her.  She could not send mail.  For some reason, it worked in Ubuntu, and she has been happily (if ignorantly) going about her business for several years now - still on 7.04 I believe.

I am certain someone will ponder why you "pollute" the board by making such a post, but I understand that you did it because you can, because venting frustration often has a clarifying effect.

Good luck.  I think you'll be back.

Caruso

### Post by cosborn72 on 2008-11-15
Sorry you've had so much trouble.  Linux does take some adjustment at first, but it is definately worth the effort.

Have you tried installing and using KDE?  The packages should be available in Synapic. Your family might find that a little more comfortable, and Amarok is a great improvement over iTunes.

As far as the backup issue goes, I don't have an answer for you, but there is probably an easy way to do it.

I'm not sure why your video card is having trouble.  It has been mentioned on the forums several times, but just so you understand- The reason windows works with most hardware is because the hardware manufacturers design their products for windows.  With linux, most of the hardward drivers have had to be reverse-engineered by linux software developers.  So it's not the linux community's failure that some hardware doesn't work, it is the hardware manufacturer's failure to support linux.  That being said, more and more hardware manufacturers are developing linux drivers.

Go luck back with Windows.  It is a solid operating system.  Not nearly as good as linux, but acceptable.  Keep fiddling with Linux.  Maybe someday you will switch back.
