### Post by ChrisRL on 2009-03-09
When I broke my hard drive that was running vista (dropped the lappy :oops: ) I thought it might be time to give Linux a try. I picked the latest Ubuntu version as it seemed the closest thing to an art student's Linux. After a few months, I think I'm going back to xp. I going to try to summarize, without this being just another whine-fest, my experience with Ubuntu Intrepid. 

First, here's what what I like:

**1. Its pretty.**
I mean REALLY pretty. First thing I noticed: not really a fan of the default look. But after a bit of downloading and playing with settings, I ended up with a beautiful and user friendly desktop. So far, so good. Sure its not the most important thing, but like I said, this an art nerd's perspective and looks count. Plus, it recognized my nutty screen resolution of 1680 x 1050 immediately. That was really a pleasant surprise.

**2. Workspaces(!!!)**
This is, without a doubt, my favorite thing about this OS. I really don't know how I lived without multiple workspaces. Why the hell isn't this a standard on all operating systems by now?

**3. The Community**
I honestly believe that with enough time and effort, I could get everything I could ever want, running on Ubuntu. A big part of what makes that possible is a really dedicated community, and I really appreciate that.

HOWEVER!

The Bad:

**1. Needing the Community**
As great as it is, I wish the OS was intuitive enough that I could fix a lot of the problems I come across without referring to forums and bug reports. I know, I know. Ubuntu is still in development and not everything is guaranteed to work immediately. Fully aware of that. And its not even that I'm afraid to get my hands dirty in the Terminal. I loved the feeling of accomplishment when I fixed my first major problem in the terminal. After several more times, though, the novelty wears off. Yea, I'm impatient. I'm sure the experience of using Linux for years pays off in the usability department, but I just don't have time to develop that experience. I'm in school and I have classes that demand hours of time that I don't want Ubuntu to suck up.

**2. Photoshop**
I'm sure Gimp is great. But Photoshop feels as natural to me as my own hand. Also, it's industry standard. And yea, that IS important if I intend to go into a art's related career. I need Photoshop (not to mention several other Adobe products.) Yes, I know that there are ways to run it through Ubuntu, but it seems that the most usable methods are through dual booting, requiring an installation of Windows anyway. I wanted to leave Windows for Ubuntu, and now I need to reinstall it to make Ubuntu run the programs I need? Why not simply run it in Windows, where my Wacom will be recognized immediately? (I've been running into problems getting the pressure working on my tablet. Given enough time and help I know it could be fixed, but again, in Windows it just works.)

**3. Games** I'm really not that big a gamer. The only console I own is a SNES. I don't own a nice rig that can run the latest PC games, but I do love indie games, which are notoriously hard to get versions for Mac and Linux. I have two can't-live-without addictions: Spelunky, which has no Linux version, and Dwarf Fortress, which slows to an unbearable crawl in WINE. Plus, I just miss quickly sampling the latest indie experiments that pop up in forums. In Windows, I never had to worry that something might not run, because nearly all of the one-man dev teams are making games on Windows machines. So while this is a relatively small issue, its important to me.

So thats about it. There are a few other minor things that bug me, but they're usually evened out by the Ubuntu's stability and flat-out charm. It just seems I'm not ready to completely give up the universality of Windows yet. I'm probably going to keep a small partition dedicated for Ubuntu use, because I DO like it, and I do want to see it continue to improve. It just won't be my go-to OS at the moment. Here's to Ubuntu's future.

### Post by issih on 2009-03-11
Glad you gave it a try and enjoyed some of the experience. In the end though none of the problems you found can be easily addressed by ubuntu.

Learning how to do things in any new OS takes a long time, it is a learning curve, you just got over the biggest of the humps with windows whilst you grew up. You only know how to do things on windows because you've done it before. I contend that windows is not magically more intuitive than ubuntu, or easier to fix, merely that people buy preconfigured computers, and have used windows for several years. Oh and bear in mind that often there is a gui way to solve problems, but that the advice from the forums will give you the terminal solution, as it is quicker and easier to explain how to do that via text.

As for third party software and hardware support, until the manufacturers put in some effort to support linux based OS's there will always be a lag in getting things working smoothly, as someone has to hack together a driver or an emulator, without necessarily having access to the specs of the originals.

Anyway, glad you enjoyed the bits of Ubuntu that you did, but if you need certain software or hardware, especially in a professional capacity, then fair enough, you have to use what works.

I hope you'll pop back and see where linux goes from time to time
