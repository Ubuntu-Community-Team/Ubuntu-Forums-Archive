### Post by sirdilznik on 2007-11-24
I've been a Linux user for nearly 6 years now.  I was always the technically savvy one in my family and amongst my friends and thus I basically served as tech support for everyone's Windows machines (and DOS back in the day).  It wasn't long until I became extremely tired of and frustrated with Windows controlling every aspect of my computing experience.  I was pretty poor at the time so switching to an expensive OS like Macintosh was out of the question, the price for Linux (free) however was just right.  I nuked my system several times during the initial learning process, but it was all well worth it and quite exciting because I was learning again and control was being put back in my hands (for once it was ME that was nuking my OS, not the OS itself ).  I dual-booted for a while to make the transition easier then I finally deleted Windows completely nearly 3 years ago and haven't looked back since.

The first Linux distro I stuck with was Slackware.  Package management was nearly non-existent (or I didn't know about it) at the time on Slack so I had to go through dependency hell to get things working.  It was a tedious process, but it taught me a lot.  I will always have a soft spot in my heart for Slack.  I stayed with SuSe for a while and liked it, then I first heard about Ubuntu (around the time it first came out).  At first I was apprehensive about trying it since it is Debian based and I never had much luck with Debian in the past, but I gave it a try and was quite impressed.  I wound up settling in with Gentoo Linux since at the time it had the best 64-bit support and I just got a shiny new 64-bit CPU.  I have been a Gentoo user ever since (until now) and throughly love the distro.  

Recently I cleared out an entire hard drive and decided to try out some things just for the heck of it.  It was intended as a way to try things out and just to see what's out there.  I did not intend to switch.  Among the OSs I tried was FreeBSD.  I must say I was thoroughly impressed.  It was rock solid and blazing fast.  Lack of 64-bit Nvidia drivers and native flash support (FreeBSD can use flash through Linux emulation, but it's far from perfect) drove me away, however if I set up a server in the future, it will no doubt be running FreeBSD.  Next I decided to try ubuntu again.  I dislike Gnome (hate with a passion would be a better description), like KDE, and really like Xfce so I went with Xubuntu, but all that really doesn't matter that much since I installed and use Fvwm anyway :p

Boy has Ubuntu improved since I first tried it years ago.  The installation couldn't have been any easier and it was very quick too.  The package management system in Ubuntu is also top notch.  I'm not going to say it's better than portage (Gentoo's package system), since that is one of Gentoo's strong points, but as far as binary package management systems go, Ubuntu's is as good as I've seen.  Ubuntu had also succeeded in making sudo very seamless and the root user less "scary" for noobs while still retaining the control an experienced user like myself craves.  I had always just used su in the past, but Ubuntu has really turned me onto sudo.  One of the biggest surprises was the performance.  Ubuntu just zips right along.  Honestly the performance is as good or better than any Gentoo install I have ever done regardless of what optimizations I did or didn't use, and all this without having to wait for hours for stuff to compile.  The only snag I have hit along the way was with the binary Nvidia drivers and that is a conflict between the drivers and framebuffer splash in the 2.6.22 kernel.  No problem, I initially disabled splash to get my graphics up and running, then compiled a custom kernel (not 2.6.22), which I was going to do anyway, to get my splash back.  Other than that everything has worked right out of the box, even things I have struggled with on other distros.  It's been smooth sailing and a pleasure ever since install.

Too bad Ubuntu wasn't around when I was making the switch initially.  it would have made my life a lot easier.  I had to take a much harder path.  I just want to thank all those involved for finally making a distro easy and "not scary" enough for even the most clueless n00b, while retaining all the power and control that experienced users will want.  I had intended to check out Ubuntu just to try it out, but honestly now I have every intention of staying (though I'll probably keep my Gentoo install around too at least for a while...) because the experience has been so positive.  Keep up the good work everyone and thank you!
