### Post by bill516 on 2009-07-29
I have written this post in order to help persons interested in trying Ubuntu.  My impressions might  also have some value for members of the Ubuntu team.  I have been using Ubuntu since the 7.04 release and I presently run 8.10, which is running too well to disturb.  I dual-boot with sidux 2009.2.  sidux is for fun.  Ubuntu is for getting things done.

About Me:

I am a retired academic who spent 38 years as a full-time faculty member and academic administrator at independent colleges.  My academic discipline was Political Science with a particular focus on international politics and American foreign policy.  I have no background in computer science or IT work at all, though I did once take very nearly the entire sequence of Novell 4.11 courses.

Why Linux

Windows XP was running well and still runs well on my wife's machine.  In addition I maintain it on another machine exclusively for flight simulation.  Watching several people struggle with Vista convinced me that Vista was not in my future for general computing work.  In fact I was increasingly bothered by the amount of  patching, upgrading, malware protection, etc, Windows XP required.  Leaving Windows after many years (My first Windows system was 3.11) was a hard step, but one I decided was necessary.  XP can stay where it is doing what it does.  There will not be a Windows version thereafter.

So I began to investigate Linux by reading a lot on various web-sites and forums.  It was not long before I found Ubuntu, which struck me as a variation of Debian that would be more approachable for a new user such as myself.  After several months of snooping 7.04 went on my Windows XP laptop (an Asus Z96J)in dual-boot mode.  (I knew nothing about dual-booting and fooling with the MBR on my hard-drive made me very nervous indeed.  Good news for others; I have had no problems with it whatsoever.)

Into Linux

What has happened over two years is that in terms of computing time I have moved from 90% Windows, 10% Linux, to 5% Windows, 70% Ubuntu and 25% sidux.  When I had to renew my laptop recently I did it with a System 76 Pangolin running 8.10.  That machine now dual-boots with sidux and it does not run Windows at all.  Windows is something I do only for fight-simulation, image scanning and technical support for members of the family.

Notice that Apple Macintosh is not part of this story, though I do have a number of Mac enthusiasts in the family.  Why not?  Let's just say "too much money, too closed" and leave it at that.  "My Computer" was about to become "MY computer!"  Leaving one closed system for another was not in the scheme.

Advice to Curious Readers

If you have found this thread you presumably are serious about trying Ubuntu.  Jump in!  Ubuntu runs quite well on a wide range of hardware and these forums are a great source of help and support.  In fact if you spend any time on the Ubuntu forums you will learn that there are at least four reasons why Ubuntu might serve you well.  They are:

aysiu
bodi.zhasen
bapoumba
LaRoza

Over two years time I have learned a great deal from these people (and others!) on these forums and so will you.  They and others devote an enormous amount of time to managing the forums and answering a wide range of questions.  I can only say "thank you and well done!"

If you have never downloaded and burned an iso file (as in, what's an "iso file"?) you are in luck.  Ubuntu has two documents over in the Community Documents section of the website that are worth your time.  [MD5Sum]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/HowToMD5SUM") explains how to check the integrity of a downloaded iso file.  [IsoHowTo]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/BurningIsoHowto") explains how to burn that iso file in order to create a Live CD.  Just follow along.

If you do dual-boot you will encounter GRUB, which works very well.  There is a first-rate tutorial on the finer points of Grubness at [dedoimedo.com]("http://www.dedoimedo.com/computers/grub.html").  Dedoimedo is worth your time.

If you need a "getting started" coach -- and you well might -- get thee to aysiu's [Psychocats]("http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/") web-site. It is a great help for people coming to Ubuntu from Windows.

And do make certain you look at the "stickies" on the "Absolute Beginner Talk" section of the forum.  I spend a fair amount of time looking at Linux forums.  I do not know one working any harder to get beginners off to a good start.

One last thought and we'll move on.  I read posts from very frustrated people saying "Good-By to Linux!" One frequent problem seems to be that Linux does not do something -- or several somethings -- the way Windows does them.  So please read "[Linux is Not Windows]("http://linux.oneandoneis2.org/LNW.htm")", which makes an obvious but very necessary point.   If you can treat learning as fun you are about to have a good time and meet a lot of nice people in the process.

What I Like About Linux/Ubuntu

Linux is a marvelous essay on collaborative creativity.  I value that highly and I think you should as well.  If nothing else the Linux story ought to teach us that we do have a capacity to work together and solve our shared problems.  If Linux did nothing else it would be valuable for that reason alone.

At the heart of Ubuntu is a finely crafted operating system that does what an operating system should do: It stays out of your way, manages your applications and it can be reliable enough to be almost boring.  Good!  When you have work to get done this is just what you want!  (And that is why I have sidux for fun.  I think maybe antiX will come next.)

The use of Passwords and the distinction between "root" and other users is absolutely essential in a world full of digital predators.  I would not want to see that approach compromised.  I do not like distributions that make it easy to bypass passwords and I think the Puppy Linux "run as root user" system is just wrong-headed.  I agree completely with the forum's policy on this question as discussed by aysiu in the "Security" section of the forum.

Upgrade Manager (as I have it in 8.10) does its task very nicely and I hope Ubuntu will stop fooling with it.  It works.  Leave it alone.

Network Manager as of 0.7.0 also works very well.  I rely heavily on wireless so I appreciate that.

The fact that Ubuntu is derived from Debian is no small virtue!  Over two years I have become convinced that the apt/synaptic package management system is quite simply the best I have seen.  For that reason alone I prefer Debian-based systems.  I would not like to see Ubuntu fork away so far that it lost its Debian foundations.

My desktop preferences are Gnome and Xfce.  I have used KDE and I can get along with it, but it is not my first choice.

Preferred Applications?

We all have our own hit-list because this depends so much on what we do with our machines, but here is mine.

If Abiword, Gnumeric, Tomboy and Force-Kill are not where I can get to them readily, I will add them.  GIMP is too heavy for my uses, but a combination of Gthumb, F-Spot and Picasa works well.  Nautilus or Brasero are used to burn CDs or DVDs.  I actually prefer k3b but I do not like downloading all the paraphernalia that comes along if I install it.  Xsane seems to be the only choice for scanning, so conversation stops there.  (Yes, it works.)  If there is an alternative I have not seen it.

Open Office is more than I need, as is Evolution.  (If I were still working this would be a different matter!)  I use Gmail so I have no pressing need for a mail client.  If I did Thunderbird would be the tool of choice, as Firefox is the browser of choice.  Chrome? Not terribly exciting, frankly.  I am not sold on wandering in the clouds.  (Anybody around here seen a file? .......)

I need Skype because nobody I know uses Ekiga, which I might otherwise prefer.

Many thanks for vim and vimtutor!  Vim is worth learning because it seems to be installed on almost everything in the Linux/Unix world.

Ubuntu comes loaded with applications and the choices are good.  Someone deserves a "well done!"

You are still thinking about Linux?  These are quality tools.  You might want to add up the dollar costs for equivalent Windows applications.

Things I do not like about Ubuntu

There are things I question, but nothing that I ardently dislike.  If there were I already would have moved to one of the other fine distributions available.  I do burn an iso for each major distribution and take a look, because I am curious.  I can move any time.  But I do not because Ubuntu works as well as it does.  But still there are a few questions.

Ummm, can we do something about brown, brown, brown and ..... brown?

Why package a fine C compiler without build-essentials?

I do not do much at all with compiz-fusion but many people do.  Why not load the settings manager by default?

Would we make a worth-while but easy improvement to system security if we retained sudo but required it to utilize a distinctive password from any being used to log on to the system?  At the moment if somebody gets my user password they have the keys to my kingdom.  Not good.

Rather than do a new release every six months should we move to an annual cycle?  As the system matures the rate of innovation should slow if only because it takes more time to integrate changes smoothly.  I have to think moving to a longer release cycle would take considerable pressure off the development teams.

In fact, would a rolling release system be preferable?  People new to Ubuntu could always download and install the current annually-new CD.  Then apt-get updates ought to catch them up to the current state of things.  Personally I do not like reinstalling my system every six months.  And I no longer do.  (But Debian is absolutely somnolent.  What we need here is a viable compromise.)

Ubuntu has a superb collection of well-done documentation but it impresses me as badly scattered.  Could we pull it together better in some sort of a User's Manual format?  I think people have difficulty finding what they need and that is too bad, because it almost always is very well done.

I leave the closed graphics drivers, whether from Nvidia or ATI, for last because their problems cannot be put off on Ubuntu or any other Linux distribution.  The problem is the frenetically competitive proprietary secrecy in that industry.  As such it is the very opposite of the open, cooperative creativity that drives Linux.  As a consequence the graphics industry's closed drivers are a blankety-blank nuisance but the available open-source drivers do not fully compensate.  As I type I have unacceptable scrolling issues that go back, I suspect, to the Nvidia driver I am using.  And the ATI driver I used on my last machine was little better.

I understand that Mark Shuttleworth banged his conference table and demanded faster boot times for Ubuntu.  Nice, but not the biggest problem on my list.  Now, may he bang the entire Linux table and insist that all of Linux-land get this video driver situation in better order?

In Conclusion

If you are still reading along you are a hardy soul, or perhaps one in need of other amusements!   But I thank you all the same and I hope something said here has been useful.

Now install Ubuntu and off you go!!  You are going to wonder why you did not do it sooner.

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2009-08-02
Just pointing out the obvious - "Again". The six month rolling release is not a mandatory upgrade. Simply because Canonical chooses a six month release, doesn't necessitate that one must participate.

From a stability standpoint, logic suggests investigating upgrades prior to performing an upgrade and remaining in an LTS environment. For those individuals that require stability and wish to minimize issues, upgrading IMHO should only be considered after every effort has been made to assure continuous system functionality. I'm bewildered by those that continuously upgrade to the newest release and then complain when encountering issues. The fixation to upgrade, should be tempered with the fundamental knowledge that one will inevitably encounter issues and should be prepared to negotiate with said issues.

Individuals like HappFeet, that get involved in beta releases have my appreciation and respect. For without their feedback, all releases could prove potential nightmares. =D>
