### Post by WhiteHorse on 2008-04-13
After reading lots of ./ posts and Ubuntu forums, I've decided to add my two cents to the community:

First of all, I'd like to declare victory, on behalf of the open source community, over Micro$oft et al. I believe it's pretty much done and over with; we won! I suggest we just quietly move on to bigger and better things. Here are my suggestions for what I'd like to see in the future for linux and Ubuntu:

1. **User-specific distros:** Ubuntu is great and meets the needs of most users, but there are huge groups of people who could benefit by having a choice of a more pre-loaded system. A good example is Edubuntu, tailored towards schools. An easy way to do this would be to make different suites that users can install, rather than wading through synaptic and trying to discern which app(out of 10 different apps) is best for a specific purpose. Here are some suites I would like to see, just a one-click install:
***Office Suite:*** This is pretty much part of the base Ubuntu but could include desktop publishing(Scribus), Dia diagram editor, Wine, PDF Editor, Java, and maybe the community can add some others. ODBC?
***Programmers Suite:*** Eclipse with WDT etc, Amaya or Nvu, a hex editor, possibly a LAMPP server, CVS, SVN, and other stuff I haven't even touched, maybe some dev libraries and kernel sources.
***Multimedia/Gamers Suite:*** Automatix pretty much has done this but I would include Azureus, Gimp+GAP, Google Earth, some cool games that actually work(yes I know I'm dreaming), all the codecs, cd burner/ripper, beryl?, and possibly all the audio/video software.
***Scientific Suite:*** TexMacs+Maxima+GNUPlot totally. This was a pain to discover and get working but is a dream come true for any scientist. Jabref, again a pain to discover. Stellarium, I'm still discovering apps but it would be nice if there was just a suite that did all the best things needed for science.
***Hacker's Suite:*** You know...

Perhaps this could just be a post-install window that appears to the root user when they first login. People who know what they want can skip it but new users or special groups can easily just pick and choose the top apps to add and don't have to surmount the enormous learning curve of which app is for office and which is for science. Like what the heck is Eclipse or Scribus? Oh, I see it's a programming suite or desktop publishing app...Just one more thing to make Linux for humans.

2. **Universal Packaging:** I know this is in the works but I do get tired of having a buggy Ubuntu repository version for 6 months while there exists a shiny new fixed version, if only I had rpm or could compile from source, etc. I mean, can't we just make one system for developers to distribute their software and then any linux user can run it without editing make files? I understand there is a testing life-cycle for each distro but come on... Do we really need to maintain every software package for every distro or can't the developers just release a universal package that runs on all distros?

3. **Guest Account:** I know this is a huge security flaw but I have installed Ubuntu in schools where random people come up and want to use the computer. Why not just make a one-click guest account for them so they can surf the web and get their hotmail? Currently, people are greeted by a usernam/password prompt and require a sysadmin to use the computer. Maybe just a "I don't have a username" button?

4. **Microsoft Hell Option:** This is not a joke! I have lived this scenario: You come on to a new job and everybody has been brainwashed to spout microsoft dribble-drabble and won't let you get a word in edge-wise. Furthermore, their hacked router redirects your traffic and drops packets to satisfy it's bot-net needs. When you try to flash the router and install a firewall, you are told you must wait until the next budget or whatever. Meanwhile the T1 is practically unusable and your machine is bombarded by SQL injection attacks all day. Maybe a pop-up that attempts to silence the hackers would be in order... Or perhaps a Solaris-type network take-over option which would allow one to black-hole these back-door systems? Can you say ARP MitM?

5. **Marketing and Support Groups:** I think people who are new to Linux get anti-linux propaganda thrown at them by marketers and it's important to counter this. Usually they are IE users searching through MSN and being redirected. Further, we should make available volunteer support like AA to help schools and companies convert and escape product lock-in.
