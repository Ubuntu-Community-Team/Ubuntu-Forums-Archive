### Post by zugu on 2008-12-28
If the PC came with an OEM Windows, the drivers should have been on that Windows CD or on a separate disc. When you buy an OEM version of Windows, you do not receive technical support from Microsoft, but from the OEM that sold you the license (and it's crappy, let me tell you). Lack of Microsoft support and being able to install on one machine only and accepting the crap that the OEM manufacturer bundles on your Windows disc is all part of the "OEM experience", it's what makes OEM licenses cost less than half of the price of a retail version.

The retail version of Windows does not include drivers for exotic hardware the OEM builder decided to include in the machine. I build my rigs from scratch and it's my responsibility to find drivers for the hardware and the hardware manufacturer to provide them. So it's not Microsoft's job to provide the drivers, and that's nice, because I know what manufacturers to avoid/buy from. I also get my Windows in its retail form - this spares me the trouble of "[decrapifying]("http://www.pcdecrapifier.com/")" it and allows me to legally use it on future upgraded or new machines.

In other words, you get what you pay for. Don't have the money for a retail version? Don't have the knowledge, time or interest to build your box from scratch? Then I guess you have no right to complain about the OEM version.

Microsoft being evil and monopolistic has nothing to do with the fact that the OP had no idea of how Windows licensing works.

I'm not harsh, it's the truth. People use OEM licenses because:

- it's cheaper
- the OS is preinstalled on the new PC, so this is a plus if you're lazy and/or don't know how to install
