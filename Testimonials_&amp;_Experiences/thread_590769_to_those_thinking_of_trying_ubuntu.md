### Post by aysiu on 2007-10-25
I've spent over twenty years using Microsoft operating systems. I've spent the last three years troubleshooting co-workers' Windows problems in person and the last two and a half years helping Ubuntu Forums users with their Ubuntu problems online. Based on my experiences, I voted *No, they are both different but neither is easier to use.*

There is a hefty chunk of Ubuntu migrants or potential migrants from Windows who migrate because they're sick of Windows problems--not because of the ideals of Free software or the GPL or GNU. That very fact alone means Windows isn't perfect. And the fact that so many potentially new Ubuntu users get fed up with trying to make Ubuntu work for them and then decide to post rants here also speaks to Ubuntu not being perfect.

Both OSes have their problems. 

Windows has the great advantage of having billions of dollars of Microsoft resources for development and advertising; of being deeply entrenched in corporations, schools, homes, and governments; and of coming primarily from a desktop (not server) background. It means prebuilt computers almost always have Windows preinstalled on them and computer peripherals almost always include Windows drivers to make up for the OS's deficiencies.

Ubuntu has the advantage of grassroots buzz, Mark Shuttleworth's personality and financial resources, and a Debian and Linux community base of volunteers. It has an uphill battle to fight, though, as even System76 and Dell Ubuntu computers have had rocky bumps. We still live in a Windows world, and Ubuntu does not get a lot of support from outside vendors yet (think Lexmark, Adobe, ATI, AutoCAD, iTunes).

I think software installation is a great example of how either could be considered "easier" depending on what your needs are. For anything that's in the repositories, software installation is much easier in Ubuntu. You go to Applications > Add/Remove and check the boxes you want for the software you want to install. You can search by categories and browse descriptions. For anything that's not in the repositories, it's kind of hit or miss, and you could end up in an ugly situation with a tarball no one knows how to successfully compile. So for Ubuntu, I'd give software installation a 10 for ease of use for repositories software and anyone between 1 and 5 for ease of use for non-repositories software. For Windows, having to go to a store to buy a CD or search online for software and then click through the setup.exe installer and possibly reboot, I'd give it a 7 for ease of use, but that's for any software. So which do you like better? 10 most of the time with a  possible 1-5 or 7 all of the time?

"Ease of use" greatly depends on your user needs *and* what you're used to using.
