### Post by MadSavage on 2008-01-11
I haven't been using Linux long (less than two years.) But my favorite distro. is Ubuntu, hands down. I've only had two problems with Ubuntu. (Yes; that's it: two.) beyond, of course, my own lack of knowledge, which has had me asking numerous questions of numerous individuals. Those two problems, in fact, are very minor, but between them, they were enough to push a few buttons. 
  
   1.) I have an ATI Xpress 200 integrated chip, which, of course, requires me to use either a) a fairly horrid driver (in my estimation.) that does not allow 3D Rendering, or OpenGL. Don't get me wrong, I AM impressed that someone had built the basic driver for use, or b) Actually tell the computer I want the restricted drivers installed. (My preference, of course, is to have 3D Rendering capabilities, so I really don't have a choice, here...)

   2.) I have a Belkin 54g Wireless USB Stick. This has been my biggest problem. In Feisty, I could not connect at all. It recognized the device, it tried to connect, and even successfully prompted me for the HEX Key to my router, but it did not connect.

   This second problem had caused me to do something I absolutely hate doing. Using a program in Linux to adapt a Windows file-type. ndiswrapper is by far the WORST headache I've had since I started toying with Ubuntu. 

   Now, I've only recently received my Gutsy disk in the mail, and I, just for the hell of it, tried to connect to my router using the Belkin 54g during the live CD tonight. I was stunned when I saw two beautiful green lights quickly replaced by a signal bar.

   The first thing I did upon seeing that happen was open a terminal, and type "rm /media/hdb1/Linux\ Files\ I\ Need/ndis*.deb"

   Yeah, it was a good feeling. So, kudos to Ubuntu. You've truly made my evening!

   Now, if I could only find a way to use visual effects without sucking the life out of my cpu.

   By the way, this little rant is my "Wow, Ubuntu did something I thought it would never do, so I'm going to completely remove Windows." statement. So this is a fairly new post from a fairly new convert. I am now completely Open Source...well, except for my video driver, but I can't get along without it.

### Post by theharshone on 2008-01-11
i dont really like the network manager for ubuntu so i chose to go command line :).
but first, does ubuntu detect your card?
```

sudo ifconfig <name of your card> up (ie mine is rausb0)
sudo iwconfig <name of your card> essid <your essid> key <your key>
sudo dhclient <name of your card>

```

It should work then

### Post by MadSavage on 2008-01-11
> **bloor75 said:**
> I don't think "it just works" is an acurate depiction of the life of an ubuntu user

   But, for the most part, it is. And it's slowly evolving, until it gets to the point where it--just--works. literally. Of course, with each new piece of hardware, there needs to be another driver to work on, or another compatibility issue to fix, but nowhere near as bad as Windows.
   Don't get me wrong, I'm not one of those people who hate windows. It does have advantages, just like Mac. But Linux (or, at least, Ubuntu/Debian Linux) is by far easier to deal with then any "new" version of Windows.
   The first time I used a True Windows environment (not Win 3.11, but Windows 95.) I received a "Developers Upgrade" in the mail; it was a disk that allowed you to change Win3.11 into Win95. Now, of course, Win95 is nothing more than flashy Win3.11. They both (as did Win98, I believe) use a DOS OS Base. So let's say that changing Win3.11 to Win95 is as dramatic as, I don't know, going from Breezy to Gutsy--or maybe just going from one Debian-based distro to another. (Let's use Debian to Ubuntu, here.) 
   If you switched from Debian to Ubuntu, what kind of Hardware/Software problems do you really think you could have?
  Now, switching from Win3.11 to Win95, I lost the use of my Modem, my video card, my sound card, and wasn't able open about 40 of my old DOS games. 
  This same issue occurred (of course, they were now "Win95" games) when I switched to win98, and again to XP. The only difference is XP was pretty decent at discovering proper drivers for my hardware, as long as I could connect to the internet (which I couldn't without the drivers.) But Microsoft didn't seem to mind this little issue...
  Finally, I tried switching to Vista, thinking it would be a nice change, after all, the developers claimed it would.
  Now, imagine, this whole time, sitting in a room with an Apple user, switching from From A Macintosh with OS 8, to OS 9, then getting his iMac with OS X. I felt both stupid, and extremely cheated by Microsoft. Sure, Mac has their stuff together, and they (had) IBM or whoever, build their CPU for their OS, instead of Microsoft doing it the other way. But I wanted that kind of functionality.
   And the closest I can get, with the Hardware I want--is Ubuntu.

  So--who needs x86Mac to make (at least most things) work the way they need, when between Canonical and free developers, we can get anything done?
