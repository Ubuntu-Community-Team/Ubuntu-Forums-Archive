### Post by Dragonbite on 2010-02-11
I'm a confirmed distro-hollic, or distro-hopper.  I have managed to narrow myself down to the "big 3" or so (Ubuntu, Fedora and openSUSE).

[SIZE="5"]In the Beginning ...[/SIZE]
When I started my Linux journey I had the Windows 98 CD and figured "if all else fails, I can re-install Windows 98 and be done with it."  That was around 2002.

Originally I started with Linux because I could not afford a Mac (and all the replacement programs to match what I already had), and a friend was suggesting it to me.  After downloading and reading this huge installation manual for Red Hat (8.0) I threw caution in the wind and installed it.

The problem was that I was dual-booting on  the one and only comptuer we (my wife and I) had.  I know it was not easy on her when I would leave for work saying "I did something to the computer, but I'll fix it tonight."

At this time, I was seeing posts all over the place by annoying fanboys about this distro called Ubuntu.  It was perfect! It worked out the box! It is the future!  They were so obnoxious, I had no intention of trying this thing (just to spite them, I know).

[SIZE="5"]When the trouble began ...[/SIZE]
So when I got a second computer (sans hard drive) it was not hard to guess my actions.  It was a toss-away that was 2x as powerful as my current (at the time) computer and with the new hard drive it was going to be OS-less!

My goal was to make it 100% legal (no pirated software) and since I didn't have the money to spend on Windows, Office or anything else that left the choices to Linux (twist my arm ;) ).

The difficult decision was "which distro", which was made harder (or was it easier) that I did not have access to broadband anymore at that time so I was limited by what I had downloaded through the years.

[SIZE="5"]Drew, meet Ubuntu ...[/SIZE]
Then curiosity tempted me, and I looked up Ubuntu (once I found the proper spelling and website ;)).  It offered free CDs?  What's the catch? I looked and looked for a catch and didn't find any, so I ordered one for myself.  I expected it to never come, and maybe for my information to be used by telemarketers and jumk mail issuers.

I actually got it in about 4 weeks time which was the first surprise.  The second surprise was that it worked, and worked pretty well though not perfect.

Unfortunately it became apparent that my wife is a very visual person (being an artist, should not be a surprised) and is not very computer-savvy or interested.

She was nearly in tears once because she coudln't find her file and nothing "looked like she expected".  We couldn't find it in the shared location, we couldn't find it by opening OpenOffice and using the "Open" command. We booted into Windows and she still couldn't find it under "My Documents".  Finally we found it, what she was looking for; In MS Word she clicked "Open File" and lo'and behold, that was what she was "looking" for.  She could find it so long as it looked the way she remembered.

So dual-boot with Windows was necessary, for her.

To help ease her into it, I started having her using Firefox as the default browser, and Thunderbird as her email client.  OpenOffice and Gimp were available, but she defaulted to MS Office and Photoshop we had.

[SIZE="5"]Fast forward a bit ...[/SIZE]
Since early last year I have managed to get my hands on some more powerful systems; Pentium 4 desktops and Pentium M laptops.

Also, the kids were getting more and more into using the computer, and Linux mostly, but I still needed Windows around to placate all of the family members.

[SIZE="5"]The turning point ...[/SIZE]
The beginning of the turning point was a combination of factors.  One of which was that our Windows computer crashed and was giving us a blue screen of death on startup.  While reading it, you can hear the hard drive spinning down.

Around that time we were also re-doing our wood floors which means removing everything from the living room and dining room, including the desktop computer.

When I finally brought the computers back into the living area, I had modified them a bit.  The desktop ran Ubuntu Linux and ONLY Ubuntu Linux.  The kids didn't mind, as the computer I brought up was a "new" one and more powerful than the previous one.

In the meantime I brought my laptop and placed it on the kitchen counter for my wife to use as well.  The ability for it to bring a comptuer into my wife's "control center" (next to the phones and her calendar in the kitchen) made for such conveneince that I got to use my laptop less and less...

Unfortunately the laptop ran into a little accident while on vacation. Rug + water + son = disaster!  While the laptop was out of commission, my wife had to get used to using the Linux desktop comptuer which was running Ubuntu.

It is only by luck that I was able to get another laptop exactly like the previous one, from the same source.  This time I've kept the system to myself and, having 3 hard drives and cases for them so I can slide them in and out like a game cartridge, I've been able to try out distributions a lot easier.

[SIZE="5"]That was then, this is now ...[/SIZE]
In spite of having fooled around with Windows XP on the main computer, or even the addition of Windows 7, I don't find a rush for my wife to use Windows, in fact she is kinda annoyed having ot use it because she has learned Linux, and how to use Gnome.

In fact, the biggest user of Windows 7 is my son, who says he does because it's "new".

Meanwhile, I have been fooling around with different distributions, with my 3 hard drives currently running Ubuntu 9.10, openSUSE 11.2, Fedora 12 and Windows XP.

I've enjoyed fooling around with Fedora and openSUSE but I keep finding myself relaxing with Ubuntu.  I don't know if it is the familiarity or the "tweaks" that make things easier.

In the back of my mind, when it is time for me to "do something" (other than fooling around with the OS/desktop) I find myself defaulting to Ubuntu.

Now, whenever I try something on a computer, I keep in the back of my head "if all else fails, I can install Ubuntu and be done with it.".

If you are only trying new releases of Ubuntu, then how do you know it is the best? ;) That's in part why I keep hopping from distro to distro...

I like Fedora's FOSS passion and it does make sense that if we don't push for free and open source philosophy then Linux just becomes another operating system, one that may technically be open but relies on closed sources which effectively shackles it like Microsoft or Apple's OSs.

I like openSUSE's Mono connection (I use .NET at work) plus "acceptance" of Microsoft interaction whether desktop or server, which both are pretty good. I also like openSUSE's "small town" feel where suggestions or work done feel like they would be more noticed than the more riotous Ubuntu community.

Alas, Fedora is alright until you try and make things "just work" or want a stable system to install and run with for a long time.  It may have the short release cycle like Ubuntu, but they are only supported for 1 year and 1 day (+/-) while Ubuntu has LTS releases available and an easy update process.

OpenSUSE looks like a nice desktop, but things are so changed in Gnome that things easily done in the other distros is .. confusing in openSUSE.  For example, to increase or decrease the number of desktops you right-click on the desktop switcher applet and chnage the number of rows/columns.  I spent a few mintues in openSUSE trying to find out how to do that and never found out myself.

I've been enjoying Ubuntu because of it's "balance" between ease of use and flexiblility.  It provides open source options, but makes it easy to choose proprietary or open source. Installing Flash is a great example; you are given 3 choices and you pick one the first time you go to a web page with Flash on it.

But I would not appreciate this if I haven't tried different distros, and operating systems.  Linux is lightyears ahead of installing Windows XP and Windows 7 is just getitng closer to the Linux experience but I wonder what else is going on underneath (like it auto-registered, I never had to enter in the key).
