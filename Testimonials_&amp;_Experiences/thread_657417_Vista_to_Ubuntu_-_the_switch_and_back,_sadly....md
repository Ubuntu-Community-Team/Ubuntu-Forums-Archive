### Post by inditech on 2008-01-03
Hi there,

I have just finished my trial of switching from Vista to Ubuntu, and I am sorry to say that I am moving back to the dreaded Microsoft...  PC specs at bottom of post BTW.

Now many people would probably navigate away from this post after just reading that!  Please don't, I am not a biased person and I just wanted to give a real world experience as to why I ended up 'retreating'.

Firstly a little about myself.  I have been around in the IT industry since 1983 building my ZX80 at the tender age of 11!  Upgrading this beast of a machine to a ZX81 a little later (when the prices came down!) complete with RAM-pack wobble and 1K chess (and yes – it could beat me, I'm rubbish!).  I learnt assembler on a Mattel Aquarius of all things and got into the Amiga scene shortly after.  Good grounding on the Amiga CLI as it was Unix based, before moving onto my first 286 about 2 years later.  I then taught myself 80286 assembler along with the 2GLs around at the time of Pascal, C and FORTRAN.  Smattered with COBOL a bit at college before taking the 3GL plunge with VB and  C++.  I got my first job working with Prime mainframes (anyone remember the old reel to reel tape path – sadly I do!), before a brief stint in contracting in VB and VBA for Y2K stuff and ending up as 3rd line support and DBA/Project Consultant – will not bore you with any more details!  I say this not as a boast or a ego boost, just simply to defer any flame posts due to lack of computing knowledge.  I would also like to add that my day to day job also include the administration of several Unix/Linux servers and SUN solaris boxes, so I am by no means a Microsoft aficionado!

I would like to start this (ok I already have!) by saying that I think Ubuntu is an astonishing OS.  The claims to work out of the box are largely unfounded, and the performance on a modern PC is superior to Windows in many ways.  So why the switch back?

I started by backing up important data as any conscientious person would do, including emails in the way I have always done – simply dragging and dropping these to various folders.

Gutsy Live CD inserted and booted, double-click and install and a little over 25 mins later I was up and running.  Very impressed with the ease of install.

My first problems came with my built-in Atheros wifi card.  My Unix knowledge is sketchy, but with the help of a mate I had a USB wifi adaptor up and running in no time and had 'net connectivity.  Onto the forums and google...

Another plus point – The people that inhabit the Ubuntu forums and the like are friendly without exception and very helpful.  It soon however became apparent that many people had had problems with my particular wifi card, so I resigned myself with the fact that I had to stick to the USB one – no big deal.  Shame, but not enough to tear me away!  I was fascinated by the fact that this was essentially a 'free' os, and wanted to give this a fair trial.  GPL noted btw...

First real problem I encountered was with the email – Evolution.  No matter what I tried, I could import my .EML files by any means.  But as I could read the EML files in a text editor, this again was no big deal.  I had not saved anything that was unreadable in this manner.

Next up was iTunes.  I had no problem in installing software to read my iPod and retrieve data, and indeed copy files each way, but (and I appreciate this is a major failing by Apple, not Ubuntu), there didn't seem to be an iTunes alternative that I could use to sync my iPod and access my iTunes account.  I did manage to get the old iTunes running under WINE (badly!) but I was not happy with this alternative.  I could not get Crossover to install satisfactorily (performance was terrible) and ended up installing VMWare and plopping on Win2000 to that.  No joy, could not get my iPod recognised under that either.  :(

Next was the graphics.  My card was supported by downloading Linux drivers and ran Compiz Fusion without a hitch.  However, general GFX performance was pretty bad.  Web browsing was a bit jerky (again liveable but not ideal), inline video ran ok (but not great) and games were a total no no.  Installed Tuxracer as a test, and it was horrendously flickery!  Black screen about every 1/2 second.  But again, and to make this fair, I am not a great games player so this didn't unduly bother me.  I would also like to point out at this stage about the very techy install of the drivers.  Again not a fault of Ubuntu (shame on you Intel), but still not very friendly!

But these were really my only issues.

1.Wifi – no biggy to me as USB one worked fine.
2.Email – Bit of a pain, but liveable for me.
3.iTunes – Big issue as I like my music!
4.GFX – Poor implementation of drivers for my card.

I think what swung it for me in the end, is that it just not 'feel' right.  The OS is unquestionably stable with far less crashes than under windows.  I do however get problems with firefox stalling on me occasionally and forcing me to quit, but I like the fact that it gives you the option to return to the previous session on restart – IE take note!

I don't consider that my laptop is hugely powerful, but at the same time it is of a more than adequate specification.  Indeed day to day running of the OS, as previously noted, was perfectly functional.  Its just that I seemed to spend my time between my normal computing activities (simply surfing, checking email, using online shopping sites, writing documents etc.) and feverishly searching google and online forums trying to get silly browser plugins and software issues resolved.  I feel that this is one area that is badly lacking.  The average user is not going to want to spend time trying to get something simple to work, they just want it to work.

If I had one big issue with Linux it is the over complicated method of installing software.  Yes I have read countless accounts of, “Its easy, simply open a terminal as ROOT and run apt-get install etc etc” but that is not simple.  It is way way beyond the casual PC user, and whilst I managed to sort out 99% or my problems with online help, the ordinary user would simply walk away.  Double clicking SETUP.EXE – next, next, next is easy.  Diving into a shell, unTaring files etc is not.

Pros and cons then, biased toward a non-techy persons perspective.

Pros.

Its Free
It mainly works out of the box
The online community is fantastic
It looks nicer (IMHO!) than windows with Compiz running
It impresses people, including nerdy friends at work!
It comes with everything you need to perform day to day work

Cons.

Far far too difficult and time consuming to resolve the simplest of problems
Companies need to add more Linux support for their apps (come on!)
It needs a more professional feel rather than seeming like a hobbyist OS
More GUI control of basic functions
A registry like database of configuration rather than text based file configuration forcing you to resort to the shell (this may already exist and if so, forgive my ignorance)

Throughout all of this I tried to remain impartial.  I have no particular love of a given OS as long as it meets my needs, and Ubuntu went a long way to impressing me, it just wasn't enough.  I am writing this post using Ubuntu just before my format back to M$ which is a shame.

I await Heron with anticipation, and I am willing to try that out in a fair and just manner.  Some of the new features look very good!

Please do not take offence at this post, although I am sure some of the forum members (who again I salute) will.  I have tried to give a real user experience of the journey from Vista to Ubuntu, and I am sad to leave and retreat to calmer shores.

The very best of luck to all the developers of Ubuntu and competing distros, but at this time I am afraid I have to remain loyal to the Bill Gates camp.

I sit back now with sadness and await the flames to this...

Pete

PC Specification
Fujitsu Laptop – Amilo li1718, Intel 200M GFX, 160GB HD, 2GB RAM, Atheros WIFI etc etc – google for more if interested!

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-01-22
Inditech, you have been using Ubuntu for less than a month, and some of the things you say demonstrate that you don't understand certain things about Linux.

1st: Text-based configuration versus a "registry". Windows uses a registry, and people keep having to "clean" it from time to time. The Windows registry stores only basic "Name, Value" pairs. Text files, on the other hand, can convery more complex information - take a look at /etc/sudoers, for instance. Text files are also very easy to "comment" - giving guidance to people who want to change settings as to what settings to change and what everything does.

Most users won't have to edit a configuration file by hand anyway. The most common "by-hand" file to edit is /etc/X11/xorg.conf - if all hardware detection has gone okay, there's no need to touch it. Besides, there's now a GUI for it.

2nd: Installing software. You may or may not be aware that Linux runs on lots of different computing architectures - everything from standard x86 processors to PowerPC to ARM to SPARC to everything else under the sun. Windows runs on precisely one architecture: x86.

Programs compiled to machine language on x86 cannot be run on PowerPC, ARM, SPARC, Itanium etc, and the reverse is true. Therefore, program developers on Linux distribute the source code of the program so it can be compiled on any platform needed. Windows only targets x86, so program developers on Windows can distribute a binary.

However, there is a growing number of developers who are distributing .deb files, which are precompiled binaries for x86. These are much easier to install than the WIndows "setup.exe".

Compiling from source also gives the user the ability to compile-in new cutting-edge features that might not be completely stable. Or they can compile-out features that they don't need. When you are asked to do a ./configure, first do a ./configure --help to see all the options you have.

Having said that, I do think compiling should be made easier and friendlier in general. It's not hard once you know how to do it, and it's not something that ordinary users should be doing anyway, but there's scope for improvement.

Asking users to use the command-line isn't "unacceptable" either. When you think about it, people were happy to use it in the days of DOS rather than pay a little extra money for a Macintosh (which didn't have a command-line). Microsoft expects you to use the MS-DOS prompt if you want to batch-encode WMAs; my very little Windows experience has taught me that too.

This isn't a flame, but it really does take more than 2 weeks to learn a different operating system. There are very good reasons why things are done differently on Linux. Unfortunately most people are virtually taught that Windows is "the way computers work", so anything that is different is feared as "unintuitive" or "a bad way to do things". It's not. Most of the time it makes more sense than what happens on Windows.
