### Post by larjan on 2011-08-23
I have used ubuntu since 8.04. Am now running 11.04 and have run all in between. I came from windows as most probably have. Here is what I have learned. You have to accept that anything new to you has a learning curve. linux will accomplish anything windows will ( and many things it won't), but it works differently. I have had issues with ubuntu but have had little trouble finding solutions. You can configure almost anything in it with a small amount of effort. It is not perfect, but evolves in a way that most issues do get fixed or changed. You have so many choices that almost anyone should be able to find a flavor they like (or make it into what you like). I have a windows machine ( windows 7 ) which is my wife's and ubuntu which is mine. I have not had a system crash or a lockup once with ubuntu. I cannot say the same for the other machine. So if your new to linux, give yourself some time to learn your way around it and get used to it. I have, and will never go back.

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2011-08-29
> Pirate Zoro
Take Unity for example, people would give it *maybe* an hour, then when it doesn't work right away they give up and say it's crap.

Perhaps, one might consider an alternative concept. Give up before you  get used to it. If a situation exists where one finds themselves dissatisfied with an tool. The question might be  one of - the more they use it, the more frustrated they become.

If one permits their frustration to overcome their inquisitiveness,  might that lead to failure? In other words might they decide  that any similar tool isn't worth their effort? I would prefer to  suggest that if one finds they can't cope with the tool, that it's  suggested they try a different tool.

This concept that users are resistant to change or haven't invested  enough time in the process, is IMHO condescending (at best). The idea  that one individual may find a specific tool useful, doesn't necessarily  equate to the concept that all will or should. Implying that they  haven't spent enough time with said tool may actually be offensive. It's  been my experience that questioning another's abilities or skill sets  without knowledge of said competencies, can often lead to embarrassment.

I believe that each user should decide which tool best fits their needs.  I've been Ubuntu "only" since 7.04 and my assessment of Unity is based  solely on my own perception. I don't require others to define my opinion  or the time it takes to develop it. Nor, do I wish to follow others  like a lost lamb. Choice IMHO is one of the true benefits of Linux.

Should one find their not satisfied with 11.04 or any other OS, we  should endeavor to understand and assist them in finding an alternative.  With multiple flavors of Ubuntu (X&Kubuntu for example) logic might  suggest we advise dissatisfied users to try other options, instead of  criticizing their actions. Which I believe is significantly more  beneficial than failure. How we react to frustrated users, may determine  they're view of the community and what they share with others.

Just my $0.02 and I will not debate it in this section of the forums, as per sectional guidelines.

### Post by 1roxtar on 2011-08-31
When I first tried Unity (Ubuntu 10.10), I just couldn't get myself to like it.  The more I tried it, it didn't "feel" right.  It was slooow and very unresponsive.  Fast forward to 11.04, I gave it another chance and I really came to like the improvements over the original incarnation.  Now after the last weeks massive updates to the 11.10 Alpha 3, I've come to really love and enjoy Ubuntu's default desktop. Even Unity 2D is blazing on my older machines.  Everything is quick and responsive.  

At first, I kept going down the launcher to click on the Applications Lens, which has now since moved into the dash.  THAT took a little getting used to, but even that is becoming second nature to me now.  All in all, like the original poster stated that we need to give it an honest chance before we discard it for another DE or distro. Does that mean that you will like it after a good effort?  Not always, but at least you tried and chose something else more to your liking afterwards.   Bottom line, use what works for you but don't throw the rest under the bus, because someone else may very well like that certain DE or distro.  We are all in the Linux family nevertheless.
