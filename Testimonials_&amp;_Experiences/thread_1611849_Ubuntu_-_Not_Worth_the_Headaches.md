### Post by Rellik Faros on 2010-11-02
Hi all.  I'm a life-long Windows user.  Mind you, not because I think it's a good OS or anything.  Far from it infact.  I think there are plenty of problems with not only the OS, but with the company that distributes it.  No, I'm a Windows user for the simple fact that it's what's been available to me.  Apple computers are simply too expensive, and up until recently, I had always thought that Linux was kind of like a super-OS that could only really be used by the especially computer savy.  Windows computers are affordable and relatively simple to use.  
 
I'm currently going to a technical school to study IT.  Admittedly, the school I'm going to is pretty crappy, but I *have *picked up a few things.  I'm currently taking an Intro to UNIX/LINUX class, and it's showed me that Linux isn't nearly as intimidating as I thought.  Most of what we do is Terminal operations on a virtual machine using Ubuntu 10.04.  The more I learned about the OS, the more I was impressed.  It was a free, simple to use OS that was relatively immune to viruses and malware compared to Windows, and came with many useful applications and features, none of it "Light" or "Trial" versions.  
 
Eventually, I decided that I'd like to start using Ubuntu for my own personal use, so I went to Ubuntu's website and downloaded the latest version, 10.10.  (I want everyone to take note of that.  I got my copy from the official website, not from some friend or some random third-party website.  Mine is straight from the source.)  Originally, I had planned to completely replace Windows on my laptop with Ubuntu, and simply use Wine and and Windows virtual machine to run my necessary Windows-only software, like MS Office 2007.  Some especially tech-savy friends and my Linux professor eventual suggested that I go with a multi-boot instead, since then I'd still be able to work off of Windows in case I had any problems with Ubuntu.  
 
I'm glad they did, because Ubuntu has given me nothing but problems.  I'll spare you the details, but so far, it's installed GRUB in the wrong place, I've had constant issues with the GUI, and for whatever reason, whenever I'm on the internet, regardless of what browser I'm using (I've tried Fire Fox, Chromium and Google Chrome), after about 20 minutes of surfing, I get nothing but Cannot Find Page or Could Not Connect to Server errors, even though there doesn't seem to be any apparent connection problems or issues with my router.  
 
This OS has just left a bad taste in my mouth.  Even Vista, which I used for all of 2009, didn't give me nearly as many problems as Ubuntu 10.10 has given me in the last two weeks alone.  I switched over to Linux, and Ubuntu in general, because it was free and had so many seemingly useful applications right out of the box.  I know that Ubuntu and it's derivitives are some of the most popular Linux distros, but I'm honestly starting to wonder if that really means anything at all.  Yes, having all these useful, free programs is nice, but if the OS that they come on is so horribly unstable, what's the point?

### Post by TBABill on 2010-11-02
You installed the very latest version of Ubuntu, which many have reported having problems with various hardware. There are hardware requirements for Ubuntu so it is important to run the system from the LiveCD and verify what works and what does not. That's not always the perfect answer, especially if an update can bork the system, but it's a good base to know if your hardware is 100% compatible. 
 
In particular, wireless issues are plaguing 10.10 for several brands right now. If you want a stable system to begin your learning curve upon, try 10.04 LTS since it has 3 full years of support (2 1/2 remaining). Versions between LTS versions only get 18 months of support and that's the norm for how Ubuntu has planned its distro. 
 
If you need help with issues like wireless, sound, etc., a great place to start is the forum. If you are willing to read more thoroughly, the Ubuntu help pages are great. They are at [http://help.ubuntu.com](http://help.ubuntu.com) and cover about every topic you could need to find.
 
Don't give up yet. It's just different and you need the patience to overcome the learning curve before really basing a qualified judgement upon the OS. It is easy once you spend a little time reading and learning. Just keep in mind how hard Windows would be to install and get running optimally if you had never used it before....that puts it into better perspective.
 
10.04 is pretty solid for most users and you may not face some of the challenges currently in 10.10, which will give you a better overall experience if your hardware plays nice with it.

Normally I don't reply to these but since you're planning to go into tech, I'll offer some advice.

Years ago when XP came out, we had a tech who installed a cracked copy onto his desktop to check compatibility with our software.  One of our main productivity applications crashed on his system, so he told the purchasing folks that people using that application had to stick to windows 2000.

After a year or so (and paying extra to get Windows 2000 for many machines), when keeping windows 2000 wasn't feasible any more, we ran into a situation where this application had to be installed on a Windows XP machine.  Lo and behold, it worked just fine.  Whoops.

Ask yourself, would Dell sell systems with Ubuntu, or would the French police have migrated to Ubuntu, or would there even there be forums brimming with Ubuntu users if your experience were the norm?

### Post by snowpine on 2010-11-02
Since you are going into the IT field, here are a few words of advice: just think of Linux as another tool in your toolbox. Just as a screwdriver cannot replace a hammer, so too Linux does not replace Windows. 

I would suggest (if you are sincere about learning Linux for your future career) that you continue to use Windows as your everyday operating system, and experiment with Linux in a Virtual Machine or on a spare computer lying around the house. Perhaps you can find one or two specific tasks that you can migrate to Linux (torrent slave, firewall, home media server, etc.) while you continue to use Windows for your email, IM, homework, games, and so forth.

You might also consider trying other Linux "distros" in particular "enterprise" distros such as Red Hat, CentOS (a no-cost alternative to Red Hat), SUSE, Debian, or Ubuntu's Long Term Support releases (8.04 or 10.04). I say this with no disrespect meant to Ubuntu, however the regular releases (like 10.10) are primarily targeted at casual users and are often regarded as buggy/unstable by IT professionals. Ubuntu 10.10 is less than a month old and still has some issue to be worked out from what I hear (haven't had a chance to try it yet myself).

Anyhow, good luck and thank you for sharing your testimonial! :)
