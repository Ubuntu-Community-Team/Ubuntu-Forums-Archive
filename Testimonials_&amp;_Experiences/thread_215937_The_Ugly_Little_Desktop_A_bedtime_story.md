### Post by K.Mandla on 2006-07-14
[CENTER][SIZE="2"][B]The Ugly Little Desktop,

*or* The Little Desktop that Could[/B][/SIZE][/CENTER]

**Once upon a time**, there was an [HP Pavilion XT856]("http://h10025.www1.hp.com/ewfrf/wc/genericDocument?lc=en&cc=us&docname=bph06288"). It was born on Christmas Eve in 2000, on the first Christmas Eve of the new millennium. Even at birth, it wasn't the fastest computer on the block (a [1Ghz Coppermine]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentium_iii#Pentium_III_cores") was available in May 2000, and a 1.13Ghz came out soon after, but if you remember, it was recalled), but it was more than enough to keep its owner happy. 

[CENTER][IMG]http://img211.imageshack.us/img211/7206/xt856thenwo1.gif[/IMG][/CENTER]

It had plenty of memory, a spacious hard drive and even a network jack -- in the days when it was rather extravagant for a machine aimed at the home market to have a networking jack.

It had a DVD-ROM drive and a CDRW which meant the owner could copy discs on the fly, or watch a movie while ripping audio CDs. A 40Gb hard drive could hold hundreds of albums, pictures and games, and the sound quality was wonderful. Life was good for the little XT856. 

But this is a bedtime story, and like all bedtime stories (and [classical Greek drama]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_drama")), the proponent must fall on hard times.

The XT856 shipped with [Microsoft Windows ME]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_me"), and as everyone knows, that was about as stable as a bowl of Jell-O. ME was confused and sort of short-circuited, so all that speed and power were wasted continually rebooting in hopes of reaching a stable environment. Life for the little XT856 was rather like a strobe light, shutting off and turning on, shutting off and turning on, with little flickers of the world beyond appearing and disappearing. Kind of like Plato's [allegory of the cave]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato%27s_cave").

**The years went by. **Things started to seem slower. Memory kept getting cramped, and there wasn't anything to do but store all those other thoughts -- the less important thoughts -- on the hard drive, in a page file. The file seemed to get bigger and bigger all the time. And every time Windows ME suffered a nervous breakdown, there were all kinds of scary, random thoughts etched in that space. They didn't make sense.

The owner heard from a local computer whiz that more memory would help. The owner added a 256Mb stick of PC100, and things seemed better. But the virus scanners and the spyware detectors needed as much space as they could get, and nothing could keep them happy.

The owner found a PCI video card, and added that in hopes of making things go faster. But it was an [S3 Trio64]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S3_Trio"), and while it was fast and powerful, it wasn't much of an improvement over the onboard graphics.

The little XT856 tried and tried to keep up. It wanted very much to keep the owner happy. But the new graphics card wasn't enough to play the games the owner wanted to play. The owner was tired of Duke Nukem 3D. The owner wanted to play F.E.A.R.

**And ME was still a problem.** More memory only meant Windows ME crashed faster. It was easier to have a nervous breakdown with plenty of memory on hand. It sped up the process of scratching all those random notes in the page file before retreating to a blue screen of death.

The local computer whiz blamed it on Windows ME. Everyone knows ME was incomplete when it shipped, the whiz said. It's faulty software, the whiz said. XP is coming, the whiz said. Try that.

The owner threw out Windows ME and backtracked to a copy of Windows 98SE -- one that was lying around from another computer. The owner didn't know that was illegal, or care really. It seemed to work better, although it was antithetical to move backwards in distributions. And Win98SE had that awful turquoise background, and it came with a suppressed guilty feeling, since it was against the law to be there.

Still, the owner said, it was better to just suffer with an earlier version of Windows than to buy the next.

The transplant worked for a little while, but by then, the scavengers had arrived.

**The first loss was described as an "upgrade."** That lovely CDRW and its sister drive were removed, and replaced with a drive that could do the job of both. DVD-ROM/CDRWs were faster and took up less space, the owner said. It was an improvement, the owner said.

Then the hard drive went away. There was a new computer in the house, and the owner wanted that one as a secondary drive. Instead, the little XT856 got an older one, a 10Gb 5400rpm drive. It wasn't nearly as big as the old drive. And it was noisier. And it smelled of cheap perfume.

The memory stayed, which was good. But by now, there weren't many chores left to the little XT856, except checking e-mail and surfing eBay. That's my Internet machine, the owner said. That's what I use to surf the Net. 

**But the years had passed** and the little XT856 had passed its prime. The flashes of life became fewer and farther between. It was starting to show its age. The little XT856 wasn't very pretty any more. Dust had built up inside the case, and the stickers on the front that screamed "128Mb of memory!" were peeling at the corners. There was cat hair in the power supply.

After a while, the owner unplugged it. It sat in the closet, in the dark, and thought about the good old days. Playing a downloaded DivX of "[The Matrix]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_matrix")." Ripping VBR mp3's of "[Under the Table and Dreaming]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Under_the_table_and_dreaming")."

One day the little XT856 was dragged out of the closet. How exciting! it thought. Are we going to play [Tribes]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribes")? Shall we watch "[Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crouching_tiger_hidden_dragon")?"

But it wasn't to be. The owner threw the little XT856 into the back of the car -- a different car than when I arrived, the little XT856 thought -- and drove away.

**Things became bad after that. **The little XT856 became the plaything of an awful spoiled child who typed mean things on Internet forums and laughed when people responded. The awful spoiled child kicked the XT856 when it couldn't run World of Warcraft. The awful spoiled child spilled Mountain Dew on the keyboard and ripped the cover off the mouse.

Children are like that when they are given things they don't work for.

Before long the little XT856 was put back in a closet. This time it was happy with that. The silver case that was once so beautiful was dented and scratched. The monitor was gone. The mouse was nearly destroyed. There was dust in the processor fan, and it made things very hot.

But the quiet was good. It was dark and cool, and that made it easier to forget.

Then one day the mother of the awful spoiled child emptied the closet. Where is the rest of this computer? she said. The awful spoiled child was too busy posting mean things on [Cute Overload.com]("http://www.cuteoverload.com/") to reply.

The mother put the little XT856 in the family car -- a 1982 Chevy Impala station wagon ... without the wood panel sides -- and drove it to the recycling center. There it disappeared into a giant industrial-grade cardboard box. It was trapped under two 333Mhz Acer machines that had worked in an elementary school and could only speak at the pace of a second-grader. This is the end, the little XT856 thought. And it was.

**But no, it wasn't the end! **Because this is a bedtime story, and like all bedtime stories (and no classical Greek drama), this one has to have a happy ending.

Last week K.Mandla went to the recycling center. He climbed over boxes of 17" monitors and Lucent desk phones discarded by the state. He clambered over the remains of a 1976 Dodge truck to where the computers were buried, and started digging.

Why? Nobody knows. K.Mandla is kind of weird that way (but he's the storyteller, so you'll just have to take it at his word).

Then he moved to where the little XT856 was buried. He moved the two 333Mhz Acers off the little XT856. He reached in and lifted it out of the box.

It's dirty, he thought. And the case is smashed.

But it's got memory in it, he thought. And a hard drive. And the processor scavengers haven't disemboweled it by prying off the heatsink with their long-handle screwdrivers.

This has potential, he thought. He put it off to the side.

He looked at the Acers, but they were too slow. Too many years with second-graders. He looked at an Apple IIGS Woz edition, but it was late, and a giant scoop was moving mounds of steel shards toward the computer piles.

This one will do, he thought.

He carried it into the office, and the man behind the counter traded him 12 pounds (about 5kg) of aluminum cans for it. That's about $3 at the going rate.

He took it home and tore off all the casing. He removed the hard drive and all the cords. He pulled out the DVD-ROM/CDRW, which was hanging by the audio cable now. He used an entire can of compressed air to blow dust out of the corners. He threw out the S3 video card and the smashed casing. He pulled the memory out and wiped off the connectors. 

He disassembled the entire little XT856, and when the case was bare, he hosed it down in the front yard. The retired car mechanic next door thought he was nuts.

And when it was dry, he reassembled the entire XT856. Then he plugged it in.

**And the little XT856 came back to life.**

A nifty program called [Killdisk]("http://www.killdisk.com") removed any trace of the owner, or the awful spoiled child, and all the things they downloaded or looked at or wrote.

And then, things got even better.

K.Mandla installed Ubuntu linux. Suddenly the hard drive was speedy -- far speedier than it had been before. He plugged the network jack into a [WRT54G]("http://www.linksys.com/servlet/Satellite?c=L_Product_C2&childpagename=US%2FLayout&cid=1149562300349&packedargs=site%3DUS&pagename=Linksys%2FCommon%2FVisitorWrapper") router and a cable modem, and the Internet appeared in blinding flashes of light! The little XT856 was moving at a speed it had never seen before! Downloading at 900kbps and faster! 

And no virus scanners! And no spyware scanners! And it never crashes!

That's pretty good, K.Mandla thought. Now let's see if we can bump it up a notch.

Killdisk came back, and this time a server install of Ubuntu appeared, on top of ext3 partitions arranged for faster access.

Then very few packages. A low-weight XFCE installation was built. No desktop manager -- K.Mandla was logging in at the startup prompt. 

It was amazing! Everything was even faster! K.Mandla watched "[O Brother, Where Art Thou]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O_brother_where_art_thou")" in mplayer to test the DVD-ROM. He burned copies of the [Suse Linux Enterprise Desktop 10]("http://www.novell.com/linux/preview.html"), to test the CDRW functions. Everything performed like new! The little XT856 was working again, and better than ever!

**Now everyone is happy. **The little XT856 has a home, he's faster and stabler than ever, and while he's very ugly now that he's lost that casing, it doesn't matter to the people who know him.

To them, he's just The Ugly Little Desktop. 

[CENTER][[IMG]http://img234.imageshack.us/img234/4145/xt856nowwj4.th.jpg[/IMG]](http://img234.imageshack.us/my.php?image=xt856nowwj4.jpg)[/CENTER]

**[SIZE="2"][CENTER]THE END[/CENTER][/SIZE]**

The moral of the story: Recycle your cans. And watch your classical Greek drama.

**[SIZE="2"][CENTER]CODA[/CENTER][/SIZE]** 

K.Mandla added a DVD-RW and a second hard drive. Now K.Mandla is thinking about a PCI video card -- maybe an [Nvidia GeForce4 MX 440]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geforce4#GeForce4_MX"), to match his laptop -- and maybe even dual 7200rpm hard drives, which aren't hard to come by. And maybe a 1.4Ghz Celeron, which he thinks was the fastest socket 370, although they seem scarce. It's still early, and there's no rush. :mrgreen:

**[SIZE="2"][CENTER]EPILOGUE[/CENTER][/SIZE]** 

There's one more thing to tell. K.Mandla has a friend whose children needed a desktop computer. His friend isn't rich, but works hard and has a good family. They have one computer -- a Mac -- but it would be nice to have another for the kids.

So K.Mandla, who wasn't having much luck getting a strong 3D card installed in the little XT856 anyway (BIOS conflict ... bad news), gave it to his friend for his children to use. Now they play RuneQuest and Snowfight on their own machine, and K.Mandla's friend can work on the Mac. The children don't care if it's not pretty, and K.Mandla's friend is happy not to have to buy a new computer.

So The Ugly Little Desktop has a good home, and will probably keep playing and having fun for many years to come. 

And now _this_ is *_really_* 

**[SIZE="2"][CENTER]THE END[/CENTER][/SIZE]**
