### Post by tlhIngan on 2013-01-04
I remember my first experience with Linux...
It was Slackware 96...
I still remember having to compile my own kernel like it was a normal thing to do, and you had to include all the packages that contained all the drivers specific to your hardware...
Then you had to get X Windows working...
And then...
There just weren't any useful apps back then and the internet was still clumsy (and slow)...

The reason I had tried Linux back then was that I was used to a prompt-based DOS and didn't like Windows95 doing things of it's own volition. But there wasn't much one could do with Linux back then, so I found a version of Windows that didn't have as much a mind of it's own: Windows NT 4. As my hardware upgraded, I moved up the chain to Windows Server 2000, and after that fell off the support wagon, I moved to Windows 2003.

But I still remember Linux...
I liked the philosophy behind setting up your own system, customizing it for your own needs and likes...

About 3 years ago, I bumped into a group of people of mutual interest, and it turns out the leader of our group is a big fan of Ubuntu. After reading into it, I found out it's the "hip" version of Linux these days. One day, I went to his house because there was a file he needed to show me on his computer, and there it was, as big as life, a slick and useful GUI that didn't at all look like the clunky X Windows from 96...

Fast forward to a month ago, a friend of mine bought a new computer for his Mom and my services were needed to move pictures and emails and stuff over to the new machine and setup a few things. I asked what would happen to the old PC, and they looked at me kinda weird... They said "Garbage?" I asked if I could have it, and I did.

Let's just say that a 7-year-old install on WinXP is bound to bog down any system. After a fresh reformat and reinstall, this thing was quite zippy. So then I thought, why not? I poked around and found that the current versions of Ubuntu were most likely too heavy for that computer to handle, and the older versions simply couldn't get onto the internet anymore. I poked around and found I could run the current version of Lubuntu, Ubuntu packaged with a lighter GUI. So I went for that, and I've been pleasantly impressed. So much so, I even wiped the Windows Server 2003 off my main PC and that's running Lubuntu as well.

I'm learning stuff by fixing stuff. The locked out root account knocked me for a bit of a loop, but I'm sudoing like there's nothing to it now.
:D

One thing that needs mentioning, is that under Linux, "Cancel" buttons actually cancel actions.
Under Windows, "Cancel" means "Yes, I'll finish what you asked me to do, then I'll cancel"

One note about root, you can still login as root from a terminal emulator using sudo su, or i've heard sudo -i works too.  Furthermore you can still be able to login as root if you use passwd as root, and change the password to something you know. I do it alot when i have forgotten the password for one of my accounts instead of sudo i use su -c
