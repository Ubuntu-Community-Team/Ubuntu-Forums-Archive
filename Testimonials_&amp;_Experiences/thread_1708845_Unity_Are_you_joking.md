### Post by jhargis1012 on 2011-03-17
Please bear with me here, my friends. It's a bit of a rant.

I have used and loved Ubuntu for about six months now. It is seriously great, and it has been my vehicle into the Linux world. I recently bought a netbook, and figured I'd give Netbook Edition a try. It would be a great oppurtunity to give the new Unity interface a test run, since it will be standard issue soon.

After some dabbling, I came to a resounding conclusion: Unity is, in my opinion, horrible. It is clunky, it is far from intuitive... I mean, why should I have to dig and dig through nonsense in the GUI just to reach the administrative tools? Why is it such a ridiculously clunky endeavor to customize the bar on the left side of the screen?

Unity is everything I would NOT want in a desktop interface, and I'm sort of shocked at how little I hear of others hating it. The problem becomes so much more ugly when you consider the fact that Canonical is going with this in-house project over those developed by the greater open-source community (GNOME, KDE, etc..), and the in-house project is awful! Seriously, what do you guys think of this? I don't mean to come off as a complainer with a chip on my shoulder, but it really feels like Ubuntu is headed in a bad, bad direction with Unity and I'd like to see it stay on its traditional course of excellence.

Thanks for hearing me out.

### Post by tlcstat on 2011-03-17
Greetings,
As for Unity in 10.10, this is in a state of almost daily updates. I am running Firefox4 (code name Minefield) and even that one app is in development so that it is function limited. If you want to use a Beta software or operating system or desktop you have to accept the limitations of its stage of development. With 10.10 you can switch the desktop from Unity to Gnome in the System Control panel under "Login". Even in Gnome you will see the daily Unity updates depending on the software you are using. 
Regarding 11.04, I keep my iso updated on a rewritable DVD via the zsysn system, but running the "try" from the DVD won't give you a fully implemented experience. I suggest that you do an install on a swappable hard drive so that you can get the full install speed effect of the system and desktop. Even then it will be in a limited state.
But saying that you don't like Unity or 11.04 is kind of like saying that you don't like Ocelot that is rolling out in October. 
Lastly, I couldn't stand Linux at all even though I had never tried it, until I tried it. Humans don't like change, partly because of fear, partly because of pride, but mostly because of laziness.
