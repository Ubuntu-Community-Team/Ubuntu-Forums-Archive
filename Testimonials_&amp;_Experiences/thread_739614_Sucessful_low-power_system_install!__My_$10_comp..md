### Post by gpilkay on 2008-03-29
I figured I'd post this in case it'll help someone who's looking at using Ubuntu on a low-powered, older machine.  There's a lot of odd rumors floating around that it won't work on less the 512.  I can tell you that's not true.

This experiment occurred to me when I saw an old HP Windows ME machine with a crashed hard drive at a yard sale for ten bucks.  As I have a friend with almost the same old machine I figured parts along would be worth it, so I grabbed it.  I found that aside from the hard drive the rest looked great inside.  Popping out the hard drive and replacing it with a spare old 60 gig one took ten minutes.  As it only has 375 MB of memory, a very odd number, I tried Xubuntu first as it's supposed to run with few resources. Oddly, while it ran ok, it could not play audio CD's or the like and would spontaneously reboot. So I switched to the plain old Ubuntu that I use for my other two computers and virtual machine.

WORKS GREAT!  Normal Ubuntu 7.10 runs without a hitch on a 375 MB Celeron 'coppermine' machine that a Windows XP CD refused to load on it at all...go figure.  Just tonight I was using this old clunker to surf the net, scan for viruses (with clamtk) listen to music (with VLC), edit some powerpoints, and most importantly, give Grandma her own sudoku machine.

The only problem I have left, aside from the non-issue of not being able to get Windows on it, is that the CD-RW drive, SEPERATE from the CD-R it also has, does not seem to work properly. It registers but can't load. It may just be a bad drive, but I have another in the junk bin I can pull. USB works fine.  Anyone know why the CD-RW would show up but not work?

While a lot bulkier, I think I just out-cheaped the EEE PC by several hundred dollars, and it only took a little more work with a screwdriver.  This is also my first system that has NO Windows programs...actually, the only software on this thing came from the CD and the repositories. 

My Grandma has a fear of 'breaking' the computer, I told her the entire software setup was free and could be replaced easy.  This may help her get over the weird phobia (as she always wants me to check her e-mail).  I'll use the sudoku to get her used to the keyboard and mouse, then I'll take her on-line.

I love open-source...

### Post by tgalati4 on 2008-04-01
A tip for old memory slots:  Blow out the dust, clean the SIMMS with an eraser, be sure to ground yourself, and use a hot glue gun to glue them in place.

As far as CD-RW's:  the laser diodes tend to burn out.  You can try a q-tip to gently clean the optics, but usually the write diode craps out.  The drive shows up because the controller works, but it can't read anything.
