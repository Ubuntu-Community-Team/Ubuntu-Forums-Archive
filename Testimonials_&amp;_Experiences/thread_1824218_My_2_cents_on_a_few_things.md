### Post by TheNixObserver on 2011-08-13
Hello,

I am new to Linux, but not a regular user.  I have dabbled with a few Linux distros including Red Hat, Sabayon, Ubuntu, Mint and Fedora as also Knoppix (the last three as Live CDs).  I have been reading the threads in the testimonials sub-forum and I wish to share my experience.

I am primarily a Windows user (I hope it is not a sacrilege to say so on this forum :)) and I am constantly watchful about viruses and malware and have third party software installed on my system.  Even then, I am yet to muster enough confidence to switch to Linux and so all my experiences with Linux are either as dual boot or as live CDs.  I would love to permanently make the switch, but a few things hold me back.  I am wary of the learning curve for which I do not have enough time or patience.  The not so pleasant experiences I had with permanent instal of Linux had also put me off.  I had this with Sabayon when I couldn't get the dual boot option plus I couldn't hear a peep from my sound card and after a few days, I ditched it.  I had no dual boot problems in Ubuntu 11.04, but I had nothing but a pinkish purple screen and nothing else.  Whenever I moved the mouse, the icons would appear and then disappear (I am not asking for support, so please bear with me).  I couldn't do anything with it and I found that this is the famous or infamous (depending on how to view it) "Unity".  Probably because I use a very old PC and the integrated graphics card was not good enough.  So I downloaded Fedora 15 Live CD.  IT was a bit slow, but faster than Knoppix which too I had used. I had to manually download and instal the codecs for mp3 and video.  The video quality was terrible.  Next I have downloaded Mint 11 which is much cleaner and faster.  I haven't used it much, so I can't say anything on it. This in a nutshell sums up my Linux experience.  

I did some reading on the Net and on this forums and I find that there are certain issues:

Some Linux distros do not come with accompanying codecs and it has to be downloaded separately (I don't know about Ubuntu since I never got around to using it).  There are many free software around like VLC Media Player.  Can't they be made part of the download with the necessary credits for the developers?   This of course is a purely business decision, but I was just wondering.  

Given a choice, I am sure that many Windows users (including myself) would like to switch at least for the sake of security.  But the biggest hurdle seems to be the "Geek" tag still attached to Linux, though it has come a far way from the days of command line working.  Lack of drivers and support from manufacturers of hardware is another reason.  After all, you cannot expect me to buy only Linux compatible hardware just because my existing hardware is not compatible.  What then, is the use of a "free" software, if I have to spend again on hardware?  Using Linux also has a longer learning curve than Windows and from what I read on this forum, updates can mess up the system.  This hardly happens in Windows (at least it hasn't happened to me).  Some of you may not agree, but everyone likes to work *with* his PC and not *on* his PC.  Reason may be why people still prefer Gates' product in spite of the constant virus threats.

And lastly, I appreciate the Linux developers for all their hard work.  I realize that Linux is not Windows, but for the time being I will continue to stick to Live CDs whenever I feel like using Linux.
