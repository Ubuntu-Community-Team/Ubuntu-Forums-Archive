### Post by Welly Wu on 2012-12-26
I've been using Ubuntu since version 9.04 64 bit back in April 2009 before I joined Ubuntu Forums on an ancient Toshiba netbook. I switched to using Ubuntu 11.10 64 bit full-time on January 4th, 2012 on an old ASUS N61JV-X2 notebook PC before the internal fan died and it over heated to the point where I could no longer use it. Then, I bought a new System76 Lemur Ultra Thin (lemu4) which I am using right now and it came with Ubuntu 12.04 64 bit Long Term Service.

The interim releases are really quite shoddy in terms of quality. There are so many bugs and the performance is usually dog slow. The biggest problem is stability because there isn't any for interim releases especially once I start customizing it to meet my needs and preferences. I've run into numerous random problems with interim releases like low resolution screen, lightdm failure, gdm failure, login failure, failure to upgrade to newer releases, and many other problems. The other problems that I noticed are that interim releases don't support nearly as many third-party PPAs and software packages so I would wind up with broken dependencies for outdated software.

The latest Ubuntu 64 bit LTS releases are usually quite solid. I can go about my day and use them with few problems and all of the problems that do pop up are easily resolved with a few Google searches. I have plenty of software packages that I can choose to add and install and they don't cause major problems.

Canonical made a bold decision with Ubuntu 12.04 64 bit LTS to support it for up to 5 years. They are also monetizing their Ubuntu brand to make it a more commercially appealing platform for developers to compete with Microsoft Corporation and Apple, Inc. I balked at these new initiatives, but I resigned myself to what it is and I accept them. One thing that I don't accept are the interim releases for my personal and daily usage. They just lack the stability and robustness for my needs.

I think that I have a pretty clear picture of what Ubuntu 13.04 and 13.10 may look like and I am somewhat lukewarm about welcoming them onto my System76 PC until the next LTS release is shipped. Some of the things that I hope to see come to reality are official support from Nvidia Corporation for Optimus technology and I am also looking forward to a more robust selection of premium content in the Ubuntu Software Center. I think that the Ubuntu Unity Dash is developing quite nicely with the ability to search, download, install, preview, and execute along with remove software applications or apps plus the ability to make instant purchases for premium apps or content. I agree that those new features are well worth adding in 2013. Another feature that I am hoping for is better virtualization support especially for VM Ware products that will allow me to integrate and manage guest virtual machines. This feature is found in Microsoft Windows 8 Pro 64 bit with their client Hyper-V technology. I find that the cloud integration especially with Ubuntu One to be quite good, but what I would like to see is the ability to upload and run guest virtual machines within the Ubuntu One from any Ubuntu powered PC. I would also like the ability to have more features rich and tighter integration of cloud apps within Ubuntu desktop versions so that I can synchronize and execute software apps in the cloud. Finally, I want to see a bigger selection of e-books, magazines, pictures, videos, music, and apps available to purchase for Ubuntu users and I want that premium content to be available on Ubuntu One so that I can synchronize it to other PCs regardless of the operating system.

We shall see if I get any of my items on my wish list fulfilled, but I have my own doubts. It's going to be quite expensive to implement and it's going to require huge data storage and bandwidth to manage across multiple users in different countries and locations worldwide.

The latest Ubuntu 64 bit LTS releases are the only versions that I will consider to download, install, and upgrade to in the long term future. I'm not a beta tester. I don't want to try out new features until they are reasonably well tested across multiple PC hardware configurations and the bugs have been quashed. LTS is a special release that deserves praise. I know that it takes a monumental amount of work to ship a LTS release and I appreciate the effort that Canonical and their partners pour into it every two years.

One thing that I will do is to use VM Ware Workstation 9.x 64 bit to try out Ubuntu 64 bit interim releases in a guest virtual machine so that I can safely test the new features and examine the performance without installing it bare metal on my System76 Lemur Ultra Thin (lemu4) notebook PC. This seems to be the most sensible thing for me to consider. That way, the next Ubuntu 64 bit LTS release won't be a complete shock for me every time I decide to upgrade every two years.

LTS is the only way to go for me. It's the only way that I can stay somewhat sane and not lose my mind trying to solve bugs and re-installing Ubuntu from scratch bare metal every two weeks when something goes wrong with an interim release version. I've seen the headaches that interim releases produce and I'm tired of pulling out my hair and teeth over impossible problems.

### Post by Welly Wu on 2012-12-28
I tried OpenSuSE 12.2 and Tumbleweed 64 bit and I tried Red Hat Fedora 17 64 bit. I noticed that these are based off of the RPM system and they were a lot harder to learn how to use than Ubuntu.

Ubuntu 12.04.1 64 bit LTS is really the best GNU/Linux distribution for my needs. Everything is super stable and reliable and everything works right out of the box. I don't have any problems right now with Ubuntu.

Other GNU/Linux distributions make too many compromises that sabotage usability. Red Hat Fedora was a pain in the *** because it uses 100 percent free and open source software packages. I have a couple of specialized PC hardware devices and I could not get them to work with Fedora. OpenSuSE was a little bit better because I could choose to download firmware and device drivers, but it was still a pain in the *** because I had to go get them manually since they were restricted.

Ubuntu does not have these sorts of problems. The System76 device driver makes it easy to restore my system back to the factory settings and I can get on with my life.

I'm looking for a stable and easy to use GNU/Linux distribution that won't make my life miserable. Ubuntu seems to be the perfect choice to meet those needs.

Interim releases are just garbage in my opinion. They are too buggy and unreliable to use on a daily basis and there's too few new innovative features to justify the price of upgrading every 6 months. I'd rather wait 2.5 years for the next LTS release before upgrading so that those new features get more time and work done to fix bugs.

The good thing about LTS releases is the long term support. I don't have to worry about making major changes or upgrades for a long time. I don't have to worry about re-installing my operating system from scratch repeatedly. Everything just works right out of the box and it is well supported.

New features are nice to have, but it's just not worth sacrificing stability for them. Let others take the leap of faith and beta test those new features on their PCs.

Hopefully, I will have another good two more years before I have to upgrade to Ubuntu 14.04.1 64 bit LTS. Hopefully, I won't need to re-install my operating system from scratch again. That would be good news to me if that happens. We shall see.
