### Post by shreepads on 2009-07-01
**2007**

Our old home computer (yes we just have one at home!) was doing poorly with Win 2K due to virus problems caused just by clicking the wrong links. We did a complete refresh of Win 2K and added a AV program. Of course now it ran like a dog. In addition I had to continuously source and apply various patches, which was no fun at all.

So I got my dad's permission to go Xubuntu promising better performance. Partitioned and setup Xubuntu 7.10 (I think!) and it still ran like a dog. If anything it was slower than Win 2K. Further it just refused to connect to the internet. 

Eventually I figured out that the network card (yes those ones on PCI) manufactured by Intex was not supported and needed some serious work to get it working. To save everyone a lot of hassles I just swapped it out with a DLink card. I then had to do a fair bit of config to get the old scanner working, my first brush with the dreaded Hardware Compatibility List, lsusb, dmesg etc.

As you can imagine, this was not getting us anywhere. So I put some new apps notably Firefox (which was quite light at that time!), VLC etc onto Win 2K because I knew these are available on Ubuntu and bided my time. VLC in particular was good because it could play DVDs that Win Media Player just died trying to play.

 
**2008**

Frustration with the old PC was growing, it was just too damn slow. Well it had every reason to be, given it was state-of-the-art some 8 years back...

I waited for the LTS 8.04 and once it was avaialable I proposed an upgrade to a spanking new PC. Specs as follows: Intel C2D E7200 on Intel DG33FB motherboard, 2 GB RAM, 3 Gbps SATA drive.

Got Win XP SP3 and Ubuntu Hardy on partitions and copied all the data over. Also got AV for Win XP in the mix. And created separate admin and user logins with a complex admin password that was not shared for both XP and Ubuntu...

Now started the dual use life for the poor PC which alternately booted XP and Ubuntu depending on whether it was me or my dad using it.

Over the course of this I did a few things to move to a complete switch
- Installed on XP and asked my dad to try out all apps I knew were available on Ubuntu, Firefox, VLC, OpenOffice etc
- One by one got all the existing devices, scanners, cameras etc working with Ubuntu. This was the most tiresome thing I had to do on Ubuntu
- Got Pidgin, Skype setup in Ubuntu so that my dad's other main use, chatting, was solved
- On occasion asked dad to use Ubuntu because I was already logged in, rather than restarting and because he mainly wanted to browse/ chat...
- Occasionally told them about the serious flaws discovered on XP and got/ applied patches with a lot of complaining..

Then one day the XP Anti Virus license ran out... And I said, why pay and renew this when we have Ubuntu??

I told 'em to use XP only when they are not online and not connect any external hard drives and didn't install any more XP patches.

This soon had everyone booting by default into Ubuntu. The main thing they missed was Win Solitaire so got Wine and setup a desktop shortcut to that.


**2009**

All is working fine, except for my dad's occasional impulse to install/ download something one of his friend's has sent which he can't do easily in Ubuntu...

Apart from this, he's a regular user of Firefox, Pidgin, Skype, OpenOffice, VLC, F-Spot Photo Manager, X-Sane, Tux Paint and Win Solitaire on Wine... Apart from these I mainly use Dosbox (for games), OpenArena, Comix, GIMP, Transmission and Brasero and very rarely Kino...

I've just installed a webcam for Skype and that is working fine as well...

The only reasons to boot Win XP now (offline, no hard drives connected) are
- Me to play UT!
- Dad to download pix from my son's mini Disney camera

<Rant>
Hardware compatibility is my biggest sore point. I'm quite happy to download modules and mess around with config files, but I draw the line at downloading code and makes.

Why can't we have some kind of reference platforms for each Ubuntu LTS release and certify a bunch of devices against it in a planned manner...

I realise that the manufacturer's have no incentive to do this given the installed base for Linux/ Ubuntu so we should do this ourselves right?

And making existing devices work seems to be one of the main things I can think of to increase the adoption of Ubuntu..

The existing Wikis that track Hardware Compatibility are nice (and yes I have added stuff there) but they are hardly comprehensive.

I'm suggesting comprehensive lists only for LTS versions because of the effort involved and my somewhat naive belief that it it works on a certain LTS release, it will be possible to run it in the few suceeding releases with some tweaks.

Of course this assumes hardware drivers are not kicked out, with the intent of either fitting it all on one CD or due to the proprietary nature of the drivers.

</Rant>

Lastly a big THANK YOU for all the hard work put in by the peoeple who build, maintain and support Ubuntu!
