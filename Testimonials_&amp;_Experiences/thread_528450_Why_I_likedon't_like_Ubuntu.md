> 
1. Can't play most games

This is true for pretty much all linux distros and not specific to Ubuntu.  Game developers are really only developing for a market where people are paying and has the majority (i.e. windows).  Cedega allows you to play pretty much most games and certainly some of the most popular titles are supported. From some counts it runs them even faster than in Windows.  Wine is free and can play most games as well.

One of the reasons why I support the likes of ID software and their pro-linux attitude by releasing linux clients for their games. Epic games release linux installers natively with their packaged games (i.e. Unreal Tournament 2004), and will continue to do so.

> 
2. You have to find the right plugins to play music, movies, etc

This is somewhat true for Windows as well, sure mp3 is supported out of the box, but everyone does a windows update anyway....  You need to download ffdshow etc. to play things like divx/xvid content.  Ever tried playing h264/aac content in Windows? What about MOV's and MP4's from Apple's quicktime site?  You end up downloading some application that installs the necessary codecs for you.  For linux just apt-get mplayer, xine or totem-xine and you should be covered.  For those that setup home theatre-pc's (yes the windows one), the most flexible to use to enable playback of most content are the codec packs that include the ffdshow filters and they're open source.

> 
3. Some hardware and most software doesnt support linux.

This is true.
However it's getting a lot better.  Driver support for some hardware are either implemented in the kernel or as loadable modules (tv cards etc.).  Sound and Graphics etc. are pretty covered.  things like web-cams, printers and scanners are getting better.  Most of the big vendors are seeing the popularity in linux and are starting to make drivers.  My recommendation is to just get linux supported hardware - it may not be the latest and greatest, but it'll be the most stable :)

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2007-08-22
> **kh1116 said:**
> medibuntu is cool, but i dont really understand, does it just add to the list of programs you can download?

Exactly. If you go into Synaptic, click the "Origin" button and then click on the two Medibuntu entries at the bottom of the list, you can see all the packages that Medibuntu offers.

Actually, Medibuntu also offers modified (and newer) versions of certain programs. For instance, K3B in Medibuntu is built with the ability to rip DVDs, whereas the version in Ubuntu isn't due to legal reasons.
