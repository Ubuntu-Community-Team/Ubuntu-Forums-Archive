### Post by KOld Iron on 2007-11-02
Hi all,

after reading a bit here in the forum, it appears to me that I have missed a bit by not using Feisty. Fact is, I finally made the switch from Windows XP to Linux, but I had waited for Gutsy to come out, and that's what I am running on now.

I have made several tries over the last years with Linux and even though I work in the IT (Windows environment only), I never had the feeling that it was worth the effort to switch. However, when I was involved with the evaluation of Vista at work (for the last two years or so), I had the feeling this is it. This is finally the Windows version I do NOT want to upgrade anymore. In our company it is considered to be a version that provides no real advantages over XP, and at least today, offers only the usual hassles of a new Windows version.

So what could I do? Well, step one was of course to look for software in the Windows world, which is Open Source and also is available for Linux. Using those with my XP would ease the switch, since I would not have to go through the learning curves of Linux AND those of the applications. So I started to switch from IE to Firefox, from Outlook Express to Thunderbird, VLC instead of Mediaplayer, OpenOffice Writer and Calc instead of Micrososft's Word and Excel and so on. And it wasn't so hard really, since I was doing it piece by piece. Step two was then to look around for a Linux distro, which was "easy to use". Years ago my first try was SuSE (6.x or so) with KDE. It was okay, but not really convincing for me at the time, also because of the applications (no Firefox yet, OpenOffice 1.x, which was terrible, etc.). But KDE was a step in the right direction. The Linux community finally acknowledged that a graphical desktop was needed for broader acceptance. Over the years I have tried Red Hat (now Fedora), Mandrake (now Mandriva), SuSE again (by now OpenSuse), but none really got me going. By now I only had laptops at home and WLAN, and that combination was a challenge for Linux for quite some time.

Anyways, to make a long story a bit shorter, about half a year ago, I heard about Ubuntu (can't remember how it came about) and I looked into it, Kubuntu to be specific, since KDE still appealed to me. I got a Live-CD (Feisty) and started in on my notebook to have a look at it. WLAN?, no problem. Printer connected to an old no-name printserver box?, no problem (no connect with Vista!). My NVidia graphics?, one click on "Restricted Drivers" and it worked. Wow! I checked all the items, that were important for me and it all checked. Okay, there is some software that is not running on Linux (like my chess program from Chessbase), but hey, there is Virtualbox, another Open Source software, where I can have XP running for those rare and special cases.

So I waited for Gutsy to come out and made the switch. For explanation: My notebook has two hard drives and XP runs on one and Ubuntu on the other one, so currently I have a dual-boot configuration. Advantage is also, that I have access to all my data, because I can access data on NTFS drives also (another check mark). All in all the switch went without a hitch :-) Of course, there are some minor issues (some of them even annoying like the non-working microphone), but so far I did not feel the necessity to go back to XP. For any real problem so far I have found answers in the forum without a even asking questions, a quick search usually has provided plenty of information on possible solutions. It is a great community I must say. I wish I would have had that in the beginnings of Windows ;-)

Therefore I can say, that so far I am quite happy with Gutsy, even though it appears the Feisty may have even be the better choice. But I am using the opportunity to learn (having another old notebook now as a test machine, where I can try out things without worrying) and most of the time it's being pure fun. So I don't think I go back to XP and I certainly will not go on to Vista. And by the way, I am using GNOME now instead of KDE. After all I found KDE a bit confusing and when I started to look at GNOME I found it easier to manage. Naturally that does not mean KDE is worse than GNOME, it's just that GNOME works out better for me.

So there you go, that's my testimonial on a successful switch to Ubuntu. And over time I even may become one of the guys answering questions instead of raising them here in the forum. I sure hope so.

Heinrich
