### Post by ireneshusband on 2009-04-28
I gave up on Windows a long time back after it committed suicide on me three times in two weeks. I used Gentoo for a while, but then switched to Ubuntu because it was easier to manage. It was great up to Hardy. I used Ubuntu variants on fast PCs, slow PCs and a clapped out beige Powerbook.

And then with Intrepid I found that I could no longer use GoogleEarth and some other applications because of a breakage in the graphics driver. I could manage without those (although I wasn't happy about it), but what was worse was that the realtime kernel was broken. I worked round this by rolling my own kernel, and that was OK.

Naturally I was very keen to upgrade to the next release because I wanted my problems fixed. So I did, and now I am regretting it. The Intel graphics drivers are even more broken, even with the fix you can find in the wiki involving downgrading xorg. glxgears renders at half the speed it used to. And compositing no longer works, meaning that I cannot use the exposé clone feature that I absolutely depend on to cope with a low-resolution laptop screen. Not only that, the realtime kernel causes lockups and is unusable. And the home-rolled realtime kernel that had been working OK with Intrepid, suddenly stopped working at all OK, requiring me to keep recompiling it to resolve problems such as frequency scaling not being available. In the process the display in the home-rolled kernel has become unusable and I can't work out why. And in addition to this I find that Amarok no longer connects to PulseAudio because of something to do with Phonon, but I don't know how to fix this, so I'm having to make do with another player. Meanwhile all the playlists and album covers I have saved are no longer accessible. I am going to have to start again.

If the current version of X was broken, then it should not have been included in a release. It should have been frozen at the one that worked in Hardy. And if the current version of the realtime kernel didn't work, a package should have been made available in the recent Ubuntu releases of an older version that did. Unfortunately I think that the six-month release cycle has become an end in itself, and new releases are put out there just for the sake of it,ready or not.

Today was the day when, having wasted an entire afternoon and most of an evening just trying to get my computer to a modestly acceptable level of functionality, I thought to myself "I am going to get a Mac." That won't happen next week. But the remaining months I spend with Ubuntu are going to be like serving out the end of a prison sentence.

I am really sorry that it has come to this. I have tried to contribute to the project as best I can, albeit modestly, by filing bug reports, some of which have resulted in fixes, and I will probably continue to do so, but I will be very glad (but sad) when I see the back of it.

As far as upgrading goes, it's been my experience that one's experience with a new version is proportionate to the newness of their hardware. If new users are to tune their expectations to reasonable levels considering the absense of hardware manufacturer support, they can make good decisions about what hardware to buy and what to expect if they get the radest, badest junk to hit stores.
