### Post by N00b-un-2 on 2008-09-30
once, I was a satisfied Microsoft customer.  I used to believe that Windows was the best operating system around, but system crashes, viruses, and the need to reformat my computer after catastrophic system failures (usually brought on by said virae and the impending repeated system crashes/rebots).  Couple that with the fact that I hate Vista and Microsoft's impending support termination of XP next year (hell, you may as well just consider XP 'obsolete', gone the way of the dinosaur), and you have a disenchanted former customer.

And then I discovered Linux.  I've been aware of it for years, but I've never really taken any interest in it until recently.  I've been running Ubuntu 8.04 pretty much as my sole operating system for a couple of months, and aside from some of the quirks of trying to get things like wifi and Nvidia graphics to work, it's been pleasant.  No system crashes, no viruses, no bloated operating system that takes forever to do anything and doesn't even allow you to do what you want it to do. Generally because there is profit to be made in forcing a user to upgrade or buy new software to allow you do anything useful. Linux is not like that, simply because it was designed by the people who use it, for the people who use it.
Linux is a lot like legos--if you want to buy a toy car you go to walmart and buy a hotwheels.  But if you want to *make* your own car, you buy a box of legos and make your own design.  That is the beauty of Linux, it's flexibility.  But along with that comes inherent instability when you try something different or out of the ordinary.

I've been using Hardy on both of my computers and on my desktop, I've had a virtually trouble free experience.  On my laptop though, I'm on my third reformatting because of doing something that I don't know how to undo or can't be done.  The first time, I accidentally installed a very early beta version of 8.10... Needless to say, it was less than polished.  The second time I had to reformat because I finally did away with my Windows partition and upon resizing my ubuntu partition I corrupted my hard drive.  The third time involved an unsolvable graphics setting problem with xorg.conf.  I have a Toshiba satellite with the gl960 chipset, which is known to have problems for some users.  my first two installs went off without a hitch in terms of graphics.  In fact, I'd say that my onboard Intel graphics card exhibited performance that rivaled my overclocked nVidia card on my desktop.  I won't look back on it as a negative experience though.  I've learned from my experiences and it's made me more knowledgeable for it.  Basically, I just tried to push the envelope, and ended up pushing it too far and broke my system.
