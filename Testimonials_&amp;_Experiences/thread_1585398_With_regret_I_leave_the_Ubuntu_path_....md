### Post by jstalnak on 2010-09-30
Dear Canonical,

I have been a (k)ubuntu user since v6, so I've been walking the Ubuntu path for a while (and this after using both RedHat (pre Fedora) and Suse (pre OpenSuse)). I've used five different computers in that span of time (two personal and three work). Ubuntu has, for the most part, been excellent (and for my work Dell Mini it is IMO *far* superior to the Win7 that came with it). But the last laptop I purchased for my personal use, an HP dv4 1222nr Pavilion, has been an absolute and continuing disaster for a year and a half. I spend more time fixing it than using it for useful work. 

I'm an IT guy (a sysadmin for a large public university) so I'm no shrinking violet when it comes to getting my hands dirty (I installed my first Linux distro, Slackware v3 I think, nearly 15 years ago), but when I rebooted this laptop after the latest rounds of updates a week ago and the boot failed (wouldn't even display the grub menu) I had had enough.  When, after yet more hours diagnosing and fixing and reboots, I finally had a working desktop and the bloody network-manager panel display was no longer visible (it, of course, HAD been visible before that last reboot).  All I could think was "WHAT THE ****ING HELL?" I could not even get on the internet with my Sprint Broadband USB card to attempt to search the forums, let alone fix it. 

I have had enough.  I've had to do 'clean' installs of every Ubuntu release since 8.04. Every one of them. Dunno about you, but that is simply not a good track record.  The preparation for an upgrade, every six months, then the restore of a working desktop afterwards, takes DAYS. I'm no longer willing to do that.

I understand the nature of computing and the tremendous variability Canonical must deal with, but I simply am no longer willing to spend literally HOURS after every update fixing something that worked before the update. I need my laptop to be a tool for useful work, not an on-going experiment in the latest features or design implementation of the boot process (usplash or plymouth or ... or ...), etc. that may break without warning every week or so.

I know *why* Canonical devs are making the decisions they make, and in many ways I agree with them, but I, personally, need far more stability than I have with this HP laptop.  There was a time in my life when I relished the challenge of trouble-shooting problems -- it was a detective story I was in the middle of -- but no longer.

I'm hoping to find a more stable, and reliable, Linux desktop that works with this Pavilion.

So, I thank you for a good ride and I wish Canonical/Ubuntu continued health.

Regards,

Guy Stalnaker

### Post by SeijiSensei on 2010-09-30
If you value stability over bleeding-edge features, I'd try installing CentOS 5.5.  You can have a KDE desktop, though it won't be the most flashy version possible.  You can have all the same applications as well.  There's always Debian, too, of course.  It's the underpinning of Ubuntu but is much more stable with many fewer releases.

I generally agree with your assessment that Ubuntu is more unstable than it should be.  I have a USB wireless adapter with the hoary Ralink rt2500 chip, one of the most common wireless chipsets in the world.  Ubuntu's support for this device was once excellent but has become abysmal in recent kernel updates to 10.04.  (I started running Maverick because it had kernel versions that incorporated all the driver patches when Lucid did not.)  I don't blame Ubuntu developers entirely for this because the kernel keeps shifting upstream.  Nevertheless the profusion of requests on this forum for help with balky wireless networking is a testament to a design decision in favor of adding new features rather than maintaining what's already there.

My best Ubuntu experiences have been with 8.04 and 9.10.  They both worked pretty much out-of-the-box with a variety of hardware I own.

The Xubuntu developers decided to include buggy PulseAudio in Lucid by default as well, even though none of the applications it ships with depend on PulseAudio. It's as awesome as Xubuntu Jaunty was on my hardware. 

But I have this sinking feeling about the future of Ubuntu/Mint on my machine once Karmic/Mint 8 reach the end of their support life. If there was more focus on fixing bugs than on adding new features, it might be worth sticking around after next April, but I fear I'll have to switch distros then if the Plymouth and PulseAudio bugs remain by then as they do at present. Perhaps CentOS or good ol' Debian.
