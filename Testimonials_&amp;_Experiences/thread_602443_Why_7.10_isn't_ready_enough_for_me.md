### Post by steveneddy on 2007-11-04
When I bought my laptop, I bought from a vendor that installed Linux on the laptop and I knew that the hardware worked well with Linux.

Many times one has a hardware setup that doesn't work well with Linux. Most of us have come to accept this fact and run hardware that already works well with Linux.

Intel wireless works very good, nVidia does a great job and asus and intel motherboards that don't have onboard wireless seem to work better than others.

[You may try some different hardware]("http://www.linux.org/hardware/") and you will probably have a different experience.

Sorry you had problems, but actually Ubuntu is a great OS and is even better if you don't have hardware issues.

Take a look at [System76]("http://www.system76.com/"). Great hardware and you are supporting someone in the Linux community.

Never give up, never surrender.

### Post by Shay Stephens on 2007-11-04
I had some difficulty a couple of years ago.  But that was because my hardware was so windows dependent.  Your best bet is to stop buying that kind of hardware.  Instead buy hardware that is more robust and standard to more than just windows.  Buy hardware that can run in linux.  You can do this now if you like and the frustrations will melt away.  But trying to work miracles on sub par hardware (from a linux perspective) is like bashing your head against a wall.

If you can't replace the hardware now, then do it when it fails.  Eventually you will be able to run linux without the frustrations you are facing now.

### Post by mivo on 2007-11-04
> **luisromangz said:**
> I would say, "why my hardware isn't ready enough for my Ubuntu".

:)  Well, realistically, software needs to adjust to hardware, not the hardware to the software. Most people are not in a financial situation where they can just get a new computer that may be more compatible. The issue is that some hardware manufacturers only consider Windows and nothing else, but if you bought your computer before you were thinking about getting Linux later on, you just didn't think about this aspect.

And sometimes there are issues even with hardware that "should" be supported. When I installed Gutsy on a brand-new desktop, there were problems with both the Jnicron controller of the motherboard and the Nvidia 8800GTS video card. I could not really figure out how to work around the controller issue before I tried to install a different distro that provided a meaningful error message that pointed me in the right direction. Equipped with this knowledge and reading quite a few threads about the Nvidia troubles, I was able to get Gutsy running on this new box.

If this had been my first experience with Ubuntu, I would not have gotten "into" it. I also speculate that a more casual user may have given up sooner. I really wanted to get Gutsy to work because I greatly enjoy the distro, can identify with its philosophy, and appreciate the rich and incredible community.  But still, in terms of hardware support and stability I think that Gutsy had some weaknesses when it was released. (Although it supports some hardware that was not supported previously, so for quite a few people it is a really good version.)

But anyway, it was a larger upgrade with new technologies, so it was more bleeding edge than some of the previous releases. Hardy/8.04 is going to be a LTS version, so I believe stability and solid hardware support will be paramount aspects of importance. I'm upgrading my old desktop to the alpha version of Hardy right now so I can "do my bit".

Gutsy runs beautifully on my new desktop, by the way -- just getting it on there was not a cakewalk. :) But the effort paid off!
