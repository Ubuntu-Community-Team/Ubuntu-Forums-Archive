### Post by JWCoffell on 2012-08-05
Before I get started, I thought I should let any readers know that I am a total noob with Linux. This is my first experience with this operating system, so I apologize in advance for my general ignorance of this software. For the record I don&#8217;t plan on being ignorant for long!
 
I am a Windows 7 user. I am a video editor by trade, and use the Adobe CS6 Master Suite. For years I&#8217;ve heard about Linux, but never really tried it until a few days ago. The install process was easy. I ran into no issues at all.
 
After booting up Ubuntu for the first time; I was so pleased to see that despite the fact that I had not installed any drivers yet, everything works! This OS is fast! It barely scrapes the surface of my system resources. To put it in an analogy; if Windows was a Honda, then Ubuntu is a Porsche! (Yea, for me it&#8217;s that fast!)
 
The UI isn&#8217;t all that hard to grasp. In fact when Windows 8 launches, I don&#8217;t plan on buying it because from what I gather, it&#8217;s completely different and I need something that feels like home.
 
Now I see that there is a wealth of software for this OS. However the method of installing some of the 3rd party software is a bit foreign to me. For instance: I use Teamspeak 3 a lot. Thankfully there is a version of it that works in Ubuntu, but after downloading it, I open the file and to my surprise there is no executable file! Instead this little window pops up that contains source code. There is a loading screen, after reaching 100% I get an error message. I know I&#8217;m doing something wrong however the instructions I&#8217;ve read so far is a little beyond me. 
 
The other issue I&#8217;m currently dealing with is I currently have two monitors connected to my system. I used the driver utility that came with Ubuntu and it did find a driver for my card. But the kicker is that after installing the driver I can&#8217;t use both monitors. In the Display Settings I can tell that the OS only sees one monitor, and in the upper right hand corner of the picture of the monitor it reads: &#8220;Laptop&#8221;. I have a PC, not a laptop. The &#8220;Detect Displays&#8221; button does nothing at all.
 
Finally, as stated earlier I am a video editor. I realize that I&#8217;m not the only person saying this, but we (I say we because I am NOT uninstalling Ubuntu, I am here for the long haul!) need an ally like Adobe to support this wonderful OS. In reality being that it&#8217;s open source, I believe that the Adobe Suite would work better in this OS then any other! Adobe could customize Ubuntu, so that programs like After Effects could run in an environment that is designed just for that purpose!
 
If you made this far, thank you so much for your time! To be forth coming, I will always use Windows 7, but I want to use Ubuntu as my primary OS, and leave Windows 7 for gaming, and leisurely computing. Then only thing stopping me from doing this is the fact that Adobe doesn&#8217;t support Ubuntu, we need to change that! 

You made me a believer in Linux, and I&#8217;m one tuff guy to convince about anything. So if anyone from Adobe reads this; I would happily dish out another $4000 (even though I already own it for Windows) to have it in Ubuntu. 
 
Keep up the good work! 
Joseph William Coffell, II

### Post by JC Cheloven on 2012-08-05
Hi JWCoffell, and wellcome.

Blender and [Cinelerra]("http://cinelerra.org/") are two of the most advanced programs for video editing available in the free software realm. The first one is in the repositories (the usual place from where we install programs: simply open the "software center" launcher). The second is not, but you can find out in the link.

OpenShot is a nice simpler editor, also in the repos. Kino is perfect to take out the stuff from a digital camcorder. There are some other specialiced editors, as AviDemux... all in the repos.

But don't expect a drop-in replacement for Adobe's products. You would be probably disappointed. 

It is very unlikely that the 2nd-champion of proprietary software (=adobe) will ever release its products for gnu-linux. I'm affraid they see the free software world more as a danger for their business than an opportunity. You know, we're people that trust in a collaborative way of building software among other "strange" ideas ;)

Enjoy your Ubuntu journey, and come back to ask if needed.

Best
JC
