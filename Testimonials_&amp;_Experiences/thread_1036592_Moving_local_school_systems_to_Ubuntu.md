### Post by joshuablount on 2009-01-11
As a tax paying citizen, I've been thinking about coming up with a case for the local school system to move from a Windows centric viewpoint, over to more free and open alternatives. 

Some of the areas that I can see changing include the following: 

Microsoft Exchange Server -> Google Apps (For serving shared calendars, email, etc for the teachers as well as more public things such as school events)

Microsoft Office -> Open Office

Microsoft Windows -> Ubuntu (or Edubuntu)

Obviously this would be a huge undertaking, but the local school system (Lake County, Florida) is publicly asking for suggestions for budget adjustments. 

I'm pretty sure they could make the changes incrementally, rather than moving everything over at the same time. One of the largest problems I can see facing is the training (and re-training) of the IT staff and faculty of the local schools on the new software. 

Does anyone have any experience presenting a change like this to their local school board? Alternatively has anyone seen or read any documentation of someone else making a similar change? I'd love to have a bit of research in previous situations to help my case, but I'm unsure where to turn (other than a bit of googling :)

Thanks in advance!

### Post by Riffer on 2009-01-14
I think people over estimate the amount of money that can be saved by going to FOSS, for the following reasons.

1:  The vast majority of money goes to hardware upgrades and IT staff, probably 90%.

2:  What software that is bought is pretty well windows centric, such as a grading program, an Admin program for keeping track of who's enrolled their grades etc, course specific software like AutoCad and such.

3:  Educational pricing of software.  At present in my district you can get a copy of MS Office 2007 for under $20.  I can get AutoCad Lite for free and StudioMX as well.

4:  Cost of training.  With any move to new software let alone a different OS, people have to be trained and that cost money.

I am a user and believer of FOSS and firmly believe that Linux/Ubuntu should be at least part of any schools setting.  There are some things that Linux can do better then either Windows or Mac.  Some of the areas I'm focusing on in my school are, resurrecting older machines that are still capable of being good basic machines for web browsing and word processing.  Also the use of thin clients in which savings can be made in the cost of hardware upgrades and again older machines can be kept in service longer.

### Post by oldsoundguy on 2009-01-14
What has not been mentioned here is the cost savings for school districts on computers .. the need to replace hardware every time MS scratches it's whatever and changes something major in THEIR kernel.

MS does NOT write those driver changes.  They sell their code to the manufacturers in the guise of a "license" and the manufacturer then has to decide IF they even want to support that older hardware, or if they are going to drop the support and come out with some new hardware to make money .. both for profit and to cover the costs of writing those drivers for their newer gear and brand new gear.
Drivers that they submit back to MS for testing and insertion into the MS build.  That is why there are driver updates usually the day after a major MS change because something went south.
Good example was Creative and the 5.1 Live Drive card/setup.  When XP SP2 came out, they decided that for fun and profit, they would not support the upgrading of the card! ( a very expensive card when they came out!)

Not saying that Linux developers are not guilty of the same thing .. the total neglect of the older NVida video cards in the change between 8.04 and 8.10 is a perfect example.  And nobody has picked up the ball to run with it and fix the issue(s) completely .. a new driver is fine IF it works (which it does NOT on some cards) and the rotate function seems to be something they do NOT want to be bothered with so they abandoned it.)

But Linux does it less often .. and seeing as the developers write the drivers along WITH the code, much less chance for orphaning perfectly good and working hardware and that has instant obsolescence inherent with anything MicroSoft.

### Post by Thirtysixway on 2009-01-14
I do see why schools should move to Ubuntu, but I think switching from XP to Ubuntu too quickly will confuse people.  Most people want to login, type a paper, print, logout.  While Ubuntu makes this free and ms does not, it's easier for people who are familiar with XP.

At my high school, the tech department tends to setup the computers in a way that's easy to manage and not easy for the user.  At one point, we had to login to a thin client.  This had internet access but nothing else (not even word). Then remote desktop to a windows 2003 server, login again, and it had office 2007 (but no internet access)
While this setup was nice and easy for tech people (and financial people paying for licenses of office 2007) it was very messy for normal users.

You can't just think about how it will cost less or be easier to manage.  You have to also think about how the end-user will be able to use the system.  Ubuntu, as of right now, would be a huge jump forward for linux but possibly a huge jump backwards for confused students.
