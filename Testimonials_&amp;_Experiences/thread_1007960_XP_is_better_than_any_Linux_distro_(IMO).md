### Post by ynneb on 2008-12-10
XP is better than any Linux distro (IMO)
I'm sure some may take this as a flame, while others will take it as an insult, but I wish to explain why I think this.

Ubuntu has been my first Linux expereince. Ubuntu has made Linux very usable for new users, and has led the way for many to easily cross over. I love the way it installs,either as live or through a GUI interface, similar to Windows. This in itself gets 10 out of 10.
Since using Ubuntu, it has awakened me to Linux. I have installed heaps of other distributions to see what they were like as well. Some installed easily while others lost me with choices I knew nothing about. Ubuntu made the choices minimal and easy to understand. I used Ubuntu for 2 months to see if I could break my Windows addiction. There wasnt much I couldnt do, but in the end I find myself typing to you from a XP environment.

WHY?

Drivers are my main concern. The idea that one size fits all for drivers does not work for me. I get far better graphics performance on the same computer running windows. While it may be hailed as good that Lunux does not require drivers, I would much prefere to install drivers that are written specifically for my hardware than one size fits all, drivers as with Linux.

I have also found that there is loads of free software that works just as well in XP as in Linux. Open office works in XP, Paint.net is free and even is better than GIMP in terms of all the plugns you can get for it. Firefox works as well in XP as with Linux, and you can use your scroll mouse in it with XP, plus it has some small but annoying bugs in Linux.

Yes Linux is free,but I dont mind paying for something that you know has most of the issues worked out. And you can get the same free software to run in it. 

The navigation and files structures are different enough to be a pain to learn for a new user.

I have some software that Linux just doesnt have an equivelent to. I cant run My tv capture card in Linux. 

Etc Etc.

I truelly appreciate EVERY thing that the Linux team have contributed, but there are still too many missing links for me to stay in the Linux domain.

Great effort guys for an unpaid team of volunteers. I will check Back in a years time to see you the cross over has advanced any further.

Regards Benny

### Post by beniwtv on 2008-12-11
> **ynneb said:**

While it may be hailed as good that Lunux does not require drivers, I would much prefere to install drivers that are written specifically for my hardware than one size fits all, drivers as with Linux.

The above statements of yours are  common misconceptions about Linux. 

Drivers in Linux are not "one size fits all", and nor are they intended to. There are thousands of drivers that Linux carries, each one for particular devices. It may occur that different brands of devices from different manufacturers do have the same chip inside, so all of them work with the same driver, which has support for them. This is no different than in the Windows world, and just to put an example, look at graphic card drivers. The NVIDIA driver will be the same across all manufacturers. Same with ATI. Same with many other devices based on the same chip.

So, as you can see, Linux does need drivers, and many of them. The difference between Windows and Linux only is that Linux has them shipped by default, Windows not.

The reason for your poor graphic card performance is most likely because you own a ATI or NVIDIA card, but you haven't installed the latest (proprietary, though) 3D drivers. So, Ubuntu is using a generic driver for it, just as Windows does if you haven't yet installed the drivers after a fresh install. 

Note: This post of mine is not intended to keep you using Linux, but I just felt I needed to say this :lolflag:

### Post by jbysmith on 2008-12-11
Exactly, the best OS is the one that works best for you; what everybody else says is irrelevant.  

For me, I grew up with Microsoft since way back in the early DOS 2.10 days. It was a pretty rough switch to Linux.  Not better or worse, just different ways of doing things.  

I do miss some of XP's simplicities.  Fire and forget it installs, a more consistent GUI, I actually miss XP's start menu; tried a few different menu apps in both Gnome and KDE, and nothing is really close.  And I have to agree with the OP; Paint.NET is absolutely fantastic, minus the whole .NET dependency. 

On the other hand, I definitely don't miss the whole "security" thing with XP; spyware, terrible permission system, having to load up a bunch of security apps just to cover a poorly designed OS, etc etc.  I don't miss the monolithic design, and having to reboot on the slightest upgrade of a system level program.  I don't miss having to crack the bloody operating system just to change how it looks; that's just inane.  Slower performance across the board.  

The kicker for me though is that XP is on it's way into retirement quickly as far as MS is concerned.  I absolutely *hate* Vista.  It's not so much the performance, as that has improved somewhat, even though its still a majorly bloated pig of an OS.  It's just some really stupid design choices.  Not going to list them; Googling "Vista sucks" will say everything.  And Windows 7, granted still in early alpha/beta, is looking to be more of the same with a few more addons, and I would bet real money it'll be loaded up with even more anti-piracy measures, DRM, "may I please use my system?" checks, and other things that'll make you scream.  Pass.

But that's just me.  I'm no fanatical Linux zealot.  I still have XP installed on a smaller partition for the odd game once in a while; I'm not embarrassed to say it.  Rarely boot into it but meh it's there if I need it.  I do work in a virtual machine from time to time for some Winders development environments; just cant kick the Delphi 2009 habit.  I do have Lazarus/FPC but it just can't touch 2009's IDE, not even close.

But 99% of the time I'm in a Linux environment of some sort now, I just can't do without it anymore.  Absolutely love it, even with its little quirks here and there.  Usually in Ubuntu, but flirting with the latest KDE4.2 beta.. still a lot of rough edges but shaping up to be amazing.  

And I hear you about the directory structure heh.. intimidating at first, but once you see how it works it makes a *lot* of sense.  A lot more organized than just dropping every file under the sun in a "System32" folder.  That's something Microsoft could get a clue with; that and lose the damn registry; if you think that editing a configuration file is complicated you have no business messing around in that insane mess either.

I had a lot of problems with my nVidia card at first, but nabbing the 180.11 beta's cured what ailed me; working smashingly well now. Can't blame  the Ubuntu or Gnome devs for nVidia's mistakes.  Winders had a few turkey drivers too in recent history.

Short version, kudos for at least trying it and seeing its strengths.  Maybe a bit down the road it might be more your speed.
