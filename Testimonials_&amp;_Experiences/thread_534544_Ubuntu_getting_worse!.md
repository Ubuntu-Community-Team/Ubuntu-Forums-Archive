### Post by Wolfie Lee on 2007-08-26
Dude, don't give up yet!! While I'm not sure about your ati 3D problem, Automatix2 (download here - [http://www.getautomatix.com/wiki/index.php?title=Installation#Ubuntu_7.04_.28Feisty_i386.29](http://www.getautomatix.com/wiki/index.php?title=Installation#Ubuntu_7.04_.28Feisty_i386.29))          
Will fix ALL the other problems you mentioned. Be sure to follow the instructions to install manually in console (EASY! don't worry -  just copy and paste...) for whichever version you are running. It is a powerful tool that picks up where symantic leaves off, By automatically downloading, installing, and configureing some of the more tricky stuff for you. Even a great P2P client (Frostwire - ubuntu version of limewire). I can recommend using Gxine (install in symantic) for all video stuff (yes EVEN DVD's, I never once got a dvd to even PLAY on a windows rig, and now I am RIPPING and TRANSCODEING them with DVDRIP). I am not sure about the wireless problem, either, because I had a similar problem with my usb 802.11g card, but have it under control, now. With that, you want to go into manual configure, wireless connectionn properties, leave ALL boxes BLANK, and selecting enable roam, then choose wired network properties, and select automatic configuration (I used DHCP), and selecting that. Don't change any of the variable settings in either properties dialogs, UBUNTU is great at autodetecting those kinds of things, EVERY time you fire up your rig. inshort, the wired connection box has a check mark, and the wireless has a dash, and shows roaming enabled...GOOD LUCK, and DON"T give up.
P.S. I am running 7.04 in an old DELL, too (OptiPlex GX400)!

### Post by iceportal on 2007-08-26
Interesting. Having been with Ubuntu since before 6.06, I've seen things get easier and easier for new users. I've seen better hardware support, better applications, better community involvement... I have yet to see anything get worse.

Maybe I'm not looking hard enough.

Or maybe you've just been that one, unlucky user, who just happens to get screwed over because the hardware they chose is completely borked in Linux (which is EXTREMELY rare these days).

But, most likely, I'd figure this is a case of PEBKAC. Human error, as it were. Either you didn't get a good burn of the ISO, or you didn't download it from the official server, or you just plain don't know what you're doing. Or maybe the opposite is true. Maybe you're an old-hat Linux user, and you compile everything from source and do everything yourself instead of using the tools provided to you by the Ubuntu system. Either way... I'm willing to bet the problem is you.

Now, yes, that sounds harsh. And yes, I am expecting an op somewhere to tell me to be nice. But I'm being blunt and honest.

Here are the three options you have:

1.) Ask for help and follow directions.
2.) Become actively involved in fixing the issues you have.
3.) Throw up your hands and go buy that overpriced Mac you were talking about.

Options 1 and 2 don't have to be independent... If you really want to you can learn how to fix your issues by asking for help, and if that doesn't work you can submit bug reports and help the coders get things right, so that other people don't have to suffer through the problems you had. Or you can do both... Get help and give help. It's definitely worth the effort to try to fix the issues you've had.

But option 3 is simply selfish. Yes, it may just be easier to go back to Windows or OS X, but in the end you've just denied the Ubuntu/Debian community the opportunity to enhance their system to work for people like you. Instead of filing bug reports which could lead to better hardware support for other users, you've just given up and fled back to your proprietary origins.

Yes, it may be the path of least resistance. And no, Linux isn't for everyone. Heck, I don't blame you for having difficulty. But ranting on the Ubuntu Forums site then ditching us cold-shoulder is counterproductive.
