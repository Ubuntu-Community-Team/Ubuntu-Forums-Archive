### Post by kenuf on 2010-01-16
I've been reading a lot of posts saying that Karmic (9.10) has lots of problems and people going back to 9.04 or 8.10.

And while there is truth to that, I don't want someone reading the forum prior to install and deciding to not even try a Karmic Live CD.

This is the first version that I truly felt was ready for my wife to use without much assistance.

There are a lot of BIG improvements from the last version I tried, 8.04.

This is the first distro that supported my wireless keyboard without any real tweaking on my part, that included unrestricted drivers for my video card (Thank-you ATI?), that allowed me to add my networked HP Color LaserJet 2600N with less work than adding it in Windows.

All of the problems I've had, were handled with a little patience and the wonderful resource of these forums.  I even have to confess that the most of the problems I've had were a result of me assuming that it was going to be more difficult than it really was.

I've worked in helpdesks for years and have scoffed when people talked about this flavor or that flavor of Linux being a real 'Competitor' for Windows, but in Karmic I see the seads of a true Windows replacement.

I know that many of the posts about problems are well founded.

But if you are a user who is new to Ubuntu..  I encourage you to at least give Karmic (9.1) a shot with dual boot or a live CD.

Hopefully, those reading this section of the forum use their own judgment. It's been my experience - reading this section of the forum could easily result in some not even trying Ubuntu. ;)

I generally suggest new users start with the latest LTS release and become comfortable with the new environment. Then upgrade to newer releases. This has proven to be an effective procedure, assuming their system is compatible with the LTS. I've (personally) found the LTS releases to be extremely stable and they provide a new user with an opportunity to gain confidence prior to installing a new release that may require some knowledge of the OS to implement.

I also suggest to "existing users" that they maintain a spare HDD for which to test new releases, prior to commitment. It tends to eliminate all the frustrations experienced by dedicating ones system - prior to assuring functionality. An inexpensive tool that confirms not only compatibility but if said release actually improves one's computing experience. If not, then all one need do is continue using the version that is installed on their primary HDD. The spare HDD does not need to be new or large. Mine is a 40 gig used drive that I've had for few years and can be used on all four of our systems to test new releases.

I'm not debating your assessment, only offering another option.
