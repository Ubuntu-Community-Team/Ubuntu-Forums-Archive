### Post by $am on 2010-03-22
I originally posted this in the accessibility board, but I thought it was more appropriate here. Since writing the below statements I have found out that disabling pulseaudio helped some of the issues with orca lagging, but much of the problems remain. 

There is little in the way of documentation on how to use orca with ubuntu, keeping in mind that a blind person (especially a totally blind person) is totally at the mercy of the screen reader for information about the desktop and other parts of the computer. Just as I experienced when moving from jaws/windows to voiceover/mac, there needs to be well-described tutorials on how to use orca, and ubuntu, since people new to orca will probably be new to ubuntu. 

Also, as i mention below, considering both ubuntu and gnome tout their supposed superior accessibility, I am unimpressed (although the compiz magnifier is pretty good). Orca, again is too temperamental to be reliable, I reiterate, dealing with a temperamental screen reader is like a sighted person trying to fix an OS in which the screen keeps flickering on and off, or becomes to dark, or doesn't turn on at all. It's just not acceptable. 

here's my original post
===

I'm blind, and my friend is always going on about how great open source is, so I've tried Ubuntu a number of times. From what I know, Ubuntu, and also GNOME, push themselves as having accessibility in mind. However, I've tried Ubuntu (and Kubuntu) and haven't been impressed with their accessibility features. In spite of reviews claiming that Orca would soon surpass JAWS in terms of overall quality, my experience has been just the opposite. Orca seems buggy, and the magnifier is slow, not to mention the occasional missing portions of the screen when in full screen mode. 

As for the screen reader itself, it handles webpages very poorly. Rather than reading webpages according to the DOM, it seems to read the text straight through, without regard to tables or other formatting. Another issue is eSpeak. Every single voice sounds like it has a British accent, even the american english voice. Also, the 'flat review' thing, whatever that is, which seems to be taking a cue from the voice-over cursor in OSX, is something I just can't get the hang of. 

Lest you think I simply came on here to bash Ubuntu or open source. I want to know, is all of this just a result of my inexperience? After all, I got used to voice over after using JAWS, and now I like voice over better. I really really want to get into open source software, but the lack of stable, reliable, robust accessibility compared to retail software is a serious barrier. Putting aside Orca, Ubuntu and it's derivatives are obviously quality software. However, me dealing with orca is like a sighted person dealing with a monitor that simply goes dark, or flickers, or is missing 3/4 of the screen. It just won't work. If you can't see it, you can't fix it.

So help me out, is Ubuntu right for me? I really want it to be.

### Post by $am on 2010-03-24
Another thing that bugs me is that nobody's ever thought to make an accessible bios. what if I need to repair my hard disc, or dual boot, or whatever. People who are totally blind just can't do anything with the bios. 

To the best of my knowledge, the bios is part of the motherboard, so why don't MB manufacturers think about making an audible bios menu? Since it's not a gui, it would take next to no time to program.

I wouldn't be so mad if canonical and GNOME didn't tout their supposed superior accessibility on their websites. Mainstream developers I think are scared to mention whether or not they're accessible for fear of alienating the normal end user who doesn't need any ease of access features. 

I think having the screen reader integrated into the os like with voiceover and os x is the best way to go. That way, if nothing else, you can be sure that you can at least access the basic features of the os. 

I've also tried vinux as a live cd, but there's just as much problems there as well.

Just some random thoughts I guess.
