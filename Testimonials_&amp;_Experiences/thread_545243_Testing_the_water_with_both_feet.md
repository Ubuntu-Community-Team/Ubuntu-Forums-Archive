### Post by trim17 on 2007-09-07
Hello all,

I'd like to entertain you all with a story of my own experience with Ubuntu. It's long and still in progress, but I'm sure you'll all enjoy it. 

[warning: long post is looooooooong]

I guess you could call me a computer geek, but I am no means an all-knowing expert on all things technical. My first experience with Linux, believe it or not, actually came from the terminal. At a summer program called the [Pennsylvania Governor's School for Information, Society, and Technology]("http://pgsist.org") (quite a mouthful, I know), we were tossed right into the thick of things: we SSH'd into an account and learned some MySQL. Easy start, eh? Anyways, after seeing a lot of the terminal but not really understanding it, I was intrigued. Since I have dial-up access at home, I didn't want to download an entire Ubuntu .iso file to burn to CD, so I settled for the less demanding (but still depressing long) download of Damn Small Linux. I popped that distro into an old (400Mhz, 128Mb RAM) laptop and messed around, but without any meaningful internet connection, it kind of sucked. I think Ubuntu needs to consider some of the limitations of not having broadband internet, but more on that later. Neverless, I read a lot about Linux in general online, and also bash and the power of the terminal, so I tested out all kinds of examples, demos, and commands on my ancient old lappy. The best part was that sleep actually worked out of the box, and by shutting off the extremely loud hard drive and fan, working entirely out of RAM made the system completely silent. That was a sweet benefit, and I began to see how Linux could really be awesome. 
After a few weeks of generally just messing around with Linux, but primarily sticking with Windows on my "real" box, I found out about Cygwin, which I loved. Again, with dial-up, it took forever to update and get anything, but I started using the terminal as part of my Windows experience. The sysadmin at the high school I went to saw me one day installing Cygwin on a school computer and asked what I was doing. After I explained to him what it was, he actually went to his office and gave me five install CDs for openSUSE 10.0! He was pretty awesome. I installed it and dual-booted XP and Linux, but I never did get dial-up working under SUSE, so that was the kicker. I eventually stopped using it all together and went XP the entire time. I always had it in the back of my mind to try Ubuntu, though, especially after seeing videos of Compiz and Beryl in action.

Well, I graduated from high school, made some money over the summer, and decided to build a computer for college, something some of my friends (and my older brother) had done, but I personally hadn't. One of the things that saved me some cash was Newegg. The other was not getting a copy of Windows. I was hesitant about some things, my father was even more so, probably in his unfamiliarity with Linux in general. I knew there were replacements for Microsoft Office, iTunes, and others, but I needed to know I could rely on them to be not buggy. My hardware was also an issue: would Linux recognize and support my (fairly) new hardware out of the box? I tried putting Fedora 7, but it wouldn't even boot. SLAX actually got me to a login screen, but to do so, I had to disable hotplug(and consequently my USB keyboard, and so I couldn't login :(). I finally grabbed a copy of Ubuntu 7.04, deciding also to try the 64-bit version, and popped it in the CD tray.

Nervously holding my breath--it was the first day of school already and I was very frustrated and at my wit's end--I waited as the Ubuntu LiveCD did its magic. After triumphantly giving me the (bland) desktop, I hesitantly moved my mouse around to confirm that, yes, it did indeed work, and I began checking to see if all my hardware worked. It did indeed, and when plugged into my university's pipeline, I got all the bandwidth I needed. So I decided to go ahead and install. 

A quick word about the people still in the "Ubuntu is too hard for the average user to install camp." I installed a beta of Vista at the Governor's School program, and it took nearly three hours of that damned music. It's not a cakewalk. It also is very attention-consuming (at least it was in beta). 
Ubuntu, on the other hand, asked me a few questions (name? location? hard drive to use? you sure about this?), and it was off to the races. I literally left for class and came back to an installed OS. That was sweet. The first boot off the hard drive went well. Only problem was the ugly resolution my monitor was at. A quick google search told me to enable my restricted driver. Although somethings took me awhile to find, mainly because I was unfamiliar, I quickly learned where many apps are. The first few times with Ubuntu, you have to reboot your computer a few times, but after that, it's rock steady, unlike the Windows XP model of "update several times a week, restart after each one, restart after you restarted because all it did was say 'You have successfully installed ____, you must restart your computer--again.'" So that was nice. Similarly, the amazing power of Synaptic/apt-get makes installing new apps trivially easy in Ubuntu. It seems so long and drawn out having to get installers in Windows now: with Ubuntu, if I want to get python, I just go to synaptic, look for Python, install the relevant packages, and then I have python and can use it immediately. No waiting for an arbitrary blue bar to tell me, "Give me a minute, I'm almost done downloading, then you can go and wait for another blue bar to install, clicking through several screens and Microsoft 'please don't hurt your computer, are you sure about this?' dialog boxes." Apt-get is a definite plus for Linux. 

The installation of Compiz-Fusion was also very gratifying. I wasn't sure that it would even run on my computer, since I was using integrated graphics and not a discreet graphics card, but I can attest that it runs fine on an ATi 690G chipset. It took me awhile to iron out all of the kinks--I went the XGL route--but once I followed Vorian's guide, the process was simple and straightforward. You could probably install many things knowing nothing other than copying and pasting into a terminal, but it's reading and finding the guides that helps. Compiz now runs smoothly and it's sweet showing off the effects, like the utterly pointless fire paint and the more productive window switcher and cube. Windows-using observers love the cube, they're stunned by it. 

I found OpenOffice more than adequate for my schoolwork needs, although saving to the professor-required .doc format sometimes loses some of my formatting. I still don't know how to install new fonts, though I'm sure a quick search on the forums here should clear that up. Pidgin is a great replacement for AIM, Firefox works great, Flash works (except sometimes it randomly doesn't after awhile, I think after my computer goes to sleep), my music plays (except for two or three Real Media files), DVDs play, my iPod is recognized, my USB drives work, and I love having an awesome terminal (especially tilda). It's been a good leap into Ubuntu, and I'm not even missing Windows. Everything I need for college works, despite the anxiety of my dad, who emails almost daily asking "Are you sure that you have everything you need? Do you need a copy of Vista?" I am thinking of putting XP on a virtual machine, to use some Windows software, but I am largely satisfied with Linux, and happy to use it full-time now. 

While there's always room for improvement, I'm happy with the strides Linux has made in competing for the desktop. I haven't had any major problems, and although I am "in the know" as far as tech is concerned, nothing I did couldn't be accomplished with some patience, searching, and following directions. It's the patience part people usually don't have. On dial-up, that was made infinitely worse, since downloading 200mb of updates on the first boot isn't an option. But with a fast internet connection, Ubuntu is awesome. Jumping in with both feet into Linux made me more familiar and knowledgeable than all the reading I did. I'm especially grateful for the ginormous Ubuntu community, the years of archived problems and their solutions, and the guides written by senior members for Linux newbs. It is very nice to be to Google "x doesn't work in Ubuntu" and find a howto written somewhere. 

Things I like about Ubuntu:
1. Plenty of support via online forums and tutorials.
2. Good hardware recognition (but I don't really have exotic hardware).
3. Viable alternatives to Windows software.
4. Plenty of software choices.
5. Synaptic rocks.
6. Compiz-Fusion is awesome to show off and seem much cooler than you may really be.
7. Infinitely customizable.
8. Fast boot and shutdown.
9. The terminal is amazingly powerful.
10. Free as in beer, free as free speech.
11. Wait a few weeks and someone will probably fix/stabilize/improve anything.
12. Copy and paste with middle click.
13. Complete lock-up/kernel panic is rare, and you can kill stalled or frozen processes much better than in XP or Vista.

Things I don't like about Ubuntu:
1. Needs work to look and run well. It's not very impressive to see a huge, ugly, aliased brown desktop running at 1024x768 on a 20" display the first time you boot up. After a few days of tweaking (probably 3 hours total work), you have a desktop that smokes XP's and handily beats Vista and OS X.
2. Fonts are different. It's a small, aesthetic factor, and after a bit you get used to it, but OS X's beautiful fonts and rendering do make me long for better fonts.
3. Ugly radio buttons in Firefox with GNOME. Again, aesthetic, but still is one of those "fine touches".
4. Sometimes things just stop working or work wrong. Maybe I'm missing something here, but Flash stops working sometimes in Firefox, and Compiz occasionally draws the menus, pulldown selectors, and tooltips behind the main window, making them unusable until I restart X.
5. Random X restarts. They've happened three times. Luckily, Firefox saves sessions, but I wouldn't want these to happen in the middle of a big post, for example. They seem to have gone away after updating compiz.

After jumping into the water, my Linux conversion is complete. I'm sticking with Ubuntu. :)
