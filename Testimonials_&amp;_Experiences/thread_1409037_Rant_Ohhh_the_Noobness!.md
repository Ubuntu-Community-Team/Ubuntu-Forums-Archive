### Post by LFMZZ on 2010-02-17
It's been a long process. My first computer was a TI-99. Then my dad bought an Apple II, I was an Apple user until my Performa 6360 back in the '90s. Then for the sake of being cheap I changed to PCs with Windows, probably around 1996.
Today I work in genetics, a lot of the genetic analysis programs are made to run in Linux and nothing else, and I understand why.
For years I have been toying with the idea of moving my work to a Linux environment, but lack of time and disposition had made it difficult. So, at the end of last year I made the jump and decided to install Ubuntu in one of my old laptops and give it a try.
I have been nothing but a point-and-click drone for the last 20 years, my last contact with anything resembling modern programming languages and prompt commands was when I did a Pascal tutorial on my Apple II - and before that, making silly programs using Basic on my TI. This means that I am completely out of shape. Not to worry! Ubuntu makes most things quite easy as most is point and click!

But it hasn't been without problems, just to name a few embarrassing moments:
I didn't know I didn't have any video drivers installed, until a few days ago.
I mistakenly installed several flash players and then I wondered why I was able to crash Ubuntu at least once every 2 hours.
I installed a package in some random download directory and then I wondered why I couldn't run it from the command prompt in the home directory.

Anyway, then the challenging part came. 
WHY OH WHY!
WHY DOES EVERYTHING HAVE TO BE SO CUMBERSOME!
I know I am getting older and the learning curves are not as smooth as when I was in my 20's but this is friggin ridiculous!
Once you walk into the dark side of tools for genetics and statistics, you are on your own.

Help documentation and forums are written for Linux experts.
I read the stuff and its like a foreign language.
WTH! Iconify! Run in Windowed mode! (those were easy, but nonetheless as I was unsure of the meaning, I had to look it up, and it takes TIME).

When I search on Google for a solution to my problem and I find some other poor noob's question that is similar to mine, the answer is:

Then there is the so-called 'tutorials' which is just a list of code to make the program do 'neato' stuff which you have no idea what it is, because there is no explanation of what the commands mean. But at the very very end you get to look at pretty graphs you just created! WoOOhOOO!

This is hard and frustrating, and I wish I had a buddy in my office who knew all this stuff and had the time to help me with it. But there is no one else, except the interwebz and the helpful help forums. and the tutorials. and the documentation.

As a quick hint - when you're looking for information, try and track down a "Community Document" They are very well written and most come with Screen Shots.

Getting used to how things install can also be a headache, where ever possible use Synaptics Package Manager - That way the installed programmes / packages will go and live where Ubuntu is expecting them to. That saves an awful lot of grief !!

If you have installed something and are not sure where it went...

```
locate *package*
```

Is quite a cool command :p

Alien is very good at translating Klingon (aka rpm packages) into something that Ubuntu can understand.

### Post by yeats on 2010-02-17
> I had the hunch that the bin folder was a special place, but one of the things that prompted me to write this rant is that today I installed ncbi's genome workbench and it went to live in a folder it created called 'opt' in root or whatever that nameless place is called.

/opt is where you store "optional" programs, perhaps "out-of-system" installations - it's not normally where a pre-packaged .deb would install it, but that's not unheard of either.

/ *is* called the "root" directory (which is different than /root, which is the root *user's* home directory).
