### Post by pytheas22 on 2008-05-07
There are already plenty of threads on the forum regarding individual reasons for using Linux.  Most of the answers seem to focus on practical considerations: e.g. Linux is perceived as more secure, faster, prettier, more stable and so on than proprietary alternatives.  In many of the threads, the issue of software freedom (where freedom refers to liberty, not price) is rarely cited as a primary factor in the decision to use Linux.

I am interested in gauging the extent to which philosophical concerns influence Linux usage, especially for followers of a mainstream, user-friendly, desktop-oriented distribution like Ubuntu.  So my question is: how important is the notion of software freedom in your decision to use Ubuntu?  Would you use Ubuntu if it were not free software (or it were closed-source but didn't cost money)?  Would you use Windows if it were free software (note: I am not trying to start a Windows-bashing thread; please give an honest and civil answer)?  Or is the abstract concept of freedom not as much of an issue as practical considerations like price, security and stability?

Moreover, it would be interesting to note how long you've been using Linux, and how the importance of software freedom has changed for you over that time, as well as whether or not you connect software freedom to larger social or political convictions.

Thanks in advance for responses, and I apologize if this thread is redundant, but I don't think there are any others that focus on this particular aspect of the Linux-motivation question.

### Post by heartburnkid on 2008-05-07
I think that practicality is the big issue for me.  Of course, I'm a relatively recent convert to Linux (going on 2 months now, as far as being a serious Linux user), but my main reason was seeking an upgrade path that wasn't Vista.  Linux was an obvious option to try, since it's free, and Ubuntu is constantly highlighted and hyped on one of my favorite blogs (Lifehacker).  And I loved it.

If Linux wasn't free?  If it was still free-as-in-beer, I'd probably have still tried it, and probably have moved to it, but I doubt I'd be as in love with it as I am.  Even if it wasn't free-as-in-beer, as much as it pains me to say so, I'd have probably pirated it just to see what all the fuss is about, and may or may not have bought a license later.  On a purely intellectual level, I love the open-source philosophy, but in practice, I'm a pragmatist.  Give me something that works well, and I'll overlook the smaller details.

I have nothing against proprietary software.  In fact, there's quite a bit of it that I like, and that I've paid for (mostly games).  But I do prefer free, open-source solutions over proprietary solutions whenever possible.   Though, this, too, is more a practical consideration for me (I have to budget carefully, and using open-source over free-as-in-beer is a good way to avoid spyware, as well as avoiding the maker of your favorite program deciding to end development abruptly).  Again, I appreciate the open-source philosophy, and it does make me feel better about the software I use, but pragmatic considerations are king for me.

I will say this, though; my short time as a Linux user has made me see more of the pragmatic side of open source.  It's amazing how most of the bugs I've experienced have been directly tied to the "binary blobs" on my system (namely, Adobe Flash).  The more I use Ubuntu, the more I become an open-source believer.  In many ways, the philosophical considerations become practical considerations, once you immerse yourself in the environment.

Since then, however, software freedom has become more important to me.  I've read some of the manifestos of Stallman et al., and have been convinced that there's something fundamentally wrong with surrendering control over my computer and data to proprietary software.  Also, as other posters have mentioned, I think that free software makes more sense from a practical perspective as well, since open code can be improved more rapidly, and no one has to waste his time reinventing things that have already been done by others; instead, he can do something truly useful and advance their work.
