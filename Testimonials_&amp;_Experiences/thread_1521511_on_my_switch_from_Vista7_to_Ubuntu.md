### Post by HelloIndustries on 2010-06-30
**Before we get started,** let me make at least *one*  qualifying statement about the writings below: This isn’t meant to be any kind of review or critique. Just an average user’s opinion on switching from Windows to Linux. I don’t consider myself any kind of super-user, nor am i clueless about computers. I can build and maintain my own system, and i know how to use google.

Previous to trying Ubuntu, i really was of the mis-informed opinion that i could be spending hours compiling software and drivers. I didn’t want the hassle. I just want to get on with using my PC. That’s mostly what kept me on Windows for so many years. But, times change, and my Windows experience was only getting more annoying.

**The first thing i noticed about Ubuntu vs Windows; It’s faster.** Much faster. Especially on start-up Ubuntu really wins over Vista and even Win7. But; Until i’d used Ubuntu, i thought that was just how PCs were, but no. Vista was to blame for all the sluggish performance. With Ubuntu, my system sits at about 600MB of 4GB, and a good chunk of that is down to Exaile and my music collection or FireFox. Both utilisations of system memory are acceptable.

**To serve as a quick, purely observational example;** Ubuntu boots in 25 seconds to login, whereas Windows Vista takes at least twice as long. Then there’s the time it takes to ‘settle down’ after login. No prizes for guessing which OS wins there, too.

As for an example of memory usage: Vista idles at about 1.4 to 1.6GB, whereas Ubuntu idles around 400MB and i’ve never seen it go over 1GB during normal usage.

**Software and OS updates:**
Windows update is reasonable, and i was also using FileHippo update checker to catch all the other applications. But, even with all that it was a fair bit of hassle to keep my system up to date. Ubuntu does away with the effort, and i love that. With Windows, i’d have the annoyance of the slowness of Windows update, and with all my other apps, well, that’d be quite a number of clicks to download them all and get them installed / past security dialogues. With Ubuntu; It’s one click to get it going, then just a password to authorise the lot. Practically no bother at all.
[B]
Security:[/B]
I always hated Vista’s annoying attempt at security, with it’s constant pop-ups and warnings. I can’t say i blame people for just clicking through and ignoring them. Obviously, Ubuntu is quite the opposite. There’s also a lot to be said for “security in obscurity” and Ubuntu has that in buckets. Even Mac OSX is relatively safe because of this, and it has almost 10% of the market, a figure i doubt Linux/Ubuntu will ever have in terms of home-user installs.
I love the feeling this gives me. Not that i’m complacent, no. I’m careful about what i install, and cautious on-line. It’s more that there aren’t thousands of viruses out there lurking in applications or on web-pages just waiting for my 5-seconds-out-of-date system to haplessly wander by.

**Praise aside, there are things i still like about Windows;** I miss the ease of install. Just double click the .exe and we’re off… I miss that. I know that i can use .deb install files with Ubuntu, but those are somewhat rare, and i have to open up the terminal to add a PPA and such like, but I’m not too comfortable using it. Though; it’s not like i’m just mashing away to see what happens, i follow instructions to get the results i want. The core of my issue with it is that i know just how much damage i *could* do. Besides, that defeats the point of the software centre and centralised updates.

Having said that, obviously, there’s the issue of crap/mal-ware installs tagging on for the ride. Such things are much less frequent with Linux, largely down to the moderated software library, and the overall approach users and contributors have to the system and -much as i hate to use such phrases- The ‘core ethic’ of Linux/Ubuntu vs Windows.
[B]
Software support is a bit of a gripe for me,[/B] i know this isn’t the fault of Ubuntu, but software vendors. Still, it affects my user experience, so it goes on the list. There are but a few bits of software i still need Windows for, mostly i have either native Linux versions, or perfectly usable Free-ware alternatives. High on this list would be: iTunes (for my iPhone), MP3tag and ToDoList. The last being very useful to me as i work from home.

I know i can get an older version of iTunes working via Wine, but it’s not compatible with iOS4.
As for MP3tag; there are a lot of great tag editors for Linux, but i prefer MP3tag. I can get it working just fine under Linux via Wine, but where it lets me down is in previewing files so that i can determine the genre. ToDoList; Portable as it is, i can’t get it to work via Wine, I should probably find a cross-platform alternative….

**Games. Yeah, this was bound to come up.**
I don’t play all that many games on my PC any more, as it’s almost 4 years old, and was only a pretty good mid-range system back when it was built. Thankfully, this will be somewhat resolved when Steam begins to support Linux, and eventually, a least a good chunk of the games i have on there should work. At least i’ll get to play Portal 2 / HL2:ep3 etc….

**Overall:**
I now use Ubuntu as my main OS, and much prefer it over Windows, The community support is great, as is the effort which goes into some of the software.
Ubuntu really feels like a solid OS to me. Here's hoping that it can gain even more market share.

**For reference, the basics of my system and the OS’s i’ve written about:**
PC: Dual core Athlon 64 5000+ OC’d to 3.01GHZ / 4GB (4x 1024MB) 800MHZ DDRII / nVidia GeForce 9600GT-512
Windows OS: Vista Home Premium 64bit (which the pc was built for almost 4 years ago)
Linux: Ubuntu 10.04 32bit
