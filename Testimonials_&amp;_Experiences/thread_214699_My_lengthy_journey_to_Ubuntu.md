### Post by cptnapalm on 2006-07-12
Many, many years ago, when dinosaurs roamed the earth and I had a girlfriend, I had a 386.  20Mhz CPU and a massive 80 MB hard drive.  The thing came with DOS and Windows 3.0.  I hated Windows.  Not knowing that it was supposed to be something more than just another program, I deleted.  Thing ate up too much hard drive space and did nothing I wanted it to do.  Then the 386 became functionless due to a keyboard controler that went bad.

In 1997, I played Quake on a friends computer.  I needed a PC.  NEEDED!  QUAKE!!!!!  So I built a PC with an AMD K6 233 and 128 MB of RAM (which was a stupidly massive amount at that time.)  And I got Windows 95.

Blue Screen Blue Screen Blue Screen

I got sick of that.

One day, hanging out on EFNET #quake, I mentioned my hate for Windows.  Someone piped in with "Use Linux."  Linux?  What was that, I asked.  A different operating system, more stable than anything Microsoft ever dreamed of making.  No blue screen?  Nope, no blue screen.

$49 later, I had a copy of Red Hat Linux (4.2, I believe).  Oh wow... this was a pain...  The directory structure was way different.  Where the hell was my C: drive?  Getting graphics up and running was one of the most trying things I had yet endured that wasn't a girlfriend (I still could get one back then.)  There were things that didn't work quite right.  Compile a kernel?  That's... I can do that?  Yep.

After using it here and there, I split my time between Windows 98 (a for pay bug fix for 95), Windows NT and Redhat Linux.  I even got a friend to give it a try; he was migrating to a PC from a Mac.  Yellow Dog for the old Mac and SuSe for his PC.

One day he said he had switched to Debian.

I would NOT be out geeked!  So I too went the Debian way.  Sometimes I would be exclusively Linux, sometimes I would dual boot.  Debian unstable was far more stable than any version of Windows.  Apt-get was the greatest thing in the history of operating systems.  I loved it, quirks and all until...

Sometime not too long after someone (I do not remember who) stepped down as the head or president or what have you of Debian, it appeared to me that the quality of the unstable branch became much more like its namesake.  One apt-get upgrade was to the networking; it was obvious it had never been tested at all because it completely broke all networking.  Had the maintainer actually tested it, he or she would never been able to upload it.

The problems began to increase.  Everytime there was an X update, Debian would automatically overwrite my xfree86 configuration file.  So I had to redo it by hand, almost everytime.  Sometimes it would even trash my backup.  Trying to get some help was frustrating and painful; Debian's gurus were, to understate, not friendly.  Still dual booting, I began to spend more time playing games in the new Windows XP.  XP was okay.  It was certainly better than previous Microsoft efforts.  At somepoint, after yet another apt-get fiasco, I ceased to use Linux at all.  This was just as well, as my machine, an AMD 2000+ blew up so badly that the metal on the opposite side of the panel to which the motherboard was secured was scorched.  No PC.

It would be a year and a half or so before I would get a new one.  This time, I shopped for and bought a laptop by Toshiba.  Good performance for a good price.  That is what I am typing on as I write this.

There was no thought of Linux when I bought it.  I just got the laptop with XP and Elder Scrolls IV.  I played the hell out of Elder Scrolls.  Finishing that up, I poked around my laptop seeing what applications were on it, what I could use if for.  The version of Word was a 60 day trial version.  Kiss my butt, I am NOT paying hundreds of dollars for something which I really don't have much use for.  Aside from some downloaded apps, I mostly just used the machine for web browsing.

While doing so one day, Linux came to mind.  "I wonder how things are going these days in Linux land," I wondered.  So I took a look.  There were the old standbys of course.  Redhat and SuSe, Debian and Slackware.  There were some new ones too, Gentoo and PCLinuxOS.  And something called Ubuntu.

Not interested in dealing with .rpms and burned  too badly by Debian, Ubuntu sounded the most interesting of the bunch.  While based on Debian (thus the wonder that was apt-get) it was geared towards desktop users.  Sounded good.  So I downloaded a DVD iso.  A week or two later, I finally got around to giving it a go.

The Live CD (or in my case, DVD) was a stroke of genius.  Test running a new OS without having to install it was just great.  I played with it for a couple of hours and decided to give it a go.  Of my 100 GB hard drive, I figured 15 GB would not get in the way.  So the install was on.

It installed without a problem, but the first time it ran on its own, there was one.  X would not start.  Oh dear God, not more X nonsense...

Reboot to Windows and check forums.  After some digging (not too much though) I found a solution: the ATI x600 mobility was just being recognized as a desktop model so inserting a chip id into xorg's (what the hell is xorg? I wondered) configuration file and all would be well.  And it was.

Things were, for the most part, as I remembered them.  Top panel for applications and bottom for the task bar.  I rather liked this setup, which I had forgotten about.  I found Synaptic.  Something *easier* than apt-get?  Wow.  Got Pan for newsgroups, as it is still the best newsreader, in my opinion.  Toyed a bit and went back to Windows XP for some gaming.

While in Microsoft's pride and joy, I noticed that I was not liking some things.  I did not really like the interface.  Windows explorer crashed an awful lot when there were a lot of files to thumbnail, a feature I recalled that I was using with Nautilus before XP came out.  For the hundred or so dollars I knew I paid for this operating system, I was not particularly impressed.

A few weeks later, I noticed that my Ubuntu usage had become such that the Linux partion was running out of room, while there was still tons of space for Windows.  So I repartitioned.  Half for one and half for the other.

So I'm going along happily.  And then, something occurred to me.  I had not booted into Windows for a month, but it was still eating up half my hard drive.  Unbidden, a thought drifted through my head: so junk Windows.

Initially, I retorted to myself, "what about games?"  "What games do you want to play?"  Um.... I still like Quake 3.  Nothing else really cries out to be played, in my view though.  "You can do that in Linux, just as you did before."  Yeah...  "So what are you waiting for?"

Finally I realized that I just didn't like Windows.  I liked using Ubuntu, even with some frustrations like the whole WAP wireless thing, more than the blah-ness Microsoft's product inspired.  And I booted up the new Dapper DVD, took a deep breath and wiped my Windows partition out, after saving the relatively little that I had under it which I wanted to keep.

So I've been nothing but Linux for the last six weeks or so.  Nothing but Ubuntu.  And I'm perfectly happy, even with the occasional oddity or problem.  X, I am STILL looking at you.

It wasn't ideology or the freeness of Linux that got me to walk away from Windows.  It was the Ubuntu experience, the operating system and the really great people here on these forums.

Just thought I would share.
