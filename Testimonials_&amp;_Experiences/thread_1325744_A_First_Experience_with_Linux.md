### Post by BarfBag on 2009-11-13
Duns Scotus,

First of all, welcome to Ubuntu Forums!  Welcome to the world of free software as well!  It's a shame that your first impressions weren't as good as they could have been.

I'll address your specific points of concern in a minute, but first, there is something that you seem to be missing.  It's something that many people who venture onto this forum miss at first.  Linux is not Windows.  In every way imaginable, it is different.  It's an entirely different world.  If you feel the need to switch to Linux, you have to throw most things you think you know about computers out the window and start at square one.  I'm sure some people on this forum will disagree with me, but in your situation, relearning everything may not be practical.  As a matter of fact, if you're fairly satisfied with Windows, I would recommend sticking with what works for you.

> I have a computer which has Vista pre-installed. It is quite slow but, reliable. It is an interesting idea: Just use it for as long as you want to, everyday. As long as one does not try to fiddle with it, it is a no-brainer.

You do have to fiddle with Vista.  Vista requires regular maintenance to keep up to speed, as does every operating system.  You have to defragment, scan for viruses, scan for spyware, etc.  You don't have to do those three things in Linux.  There are, however, other things that should (arguably) be done weekly in Linux.  Installing updates and scanning for rootkits, for example.

> Ubuntu Jaunty cannot be installed on this computer without several errors. They flash by on the screen as a list, then disappear forever, unsresolved into the system on reboot. But as booting starts, there is a very quick message stating that certain things cannot be found.

Do you have specifics on this?  It's possible that the CD you burned may have skipped or something, so if it's impossible to get specifics, I would recommend burning a fresh CD.

> I bought a server and converted it into a pc. Not very difficult. Just take a side panel off and insert a graphics card and a sound card. Ubuntu Jaunty did not like the sound card. Download and install drivers, a breeze in Vista.

What a complicated rigmarole installing the drivers was. Checked online. Do not worry, Karmic has the drivers required. Therefore, Karmic has been installed, yes the sound worked, Hurrah.

Why not try a little Wine for those software experiences of yesteryear with Microsoft.

Yes, yes yes!

"Sudo aptitude install wine".

And then:

The sound disappeared.

Tried the sound card on an actual Windows computer, installed the drivers, works perfectly every time.



What kind of sound card is this we're dealing with here?  WINE's a bit tricky for new Linux users, so unless you have programs you absolutely need to run, I would recommend staying away from it for a little while.  In fixing your sound, the first thing you should do is reboot (if you haven't already).  If this doesn't work, you may be able to fix your sound by double clicking on the speaker in the upper right hand corner, next to the clock, clicking on the drop down list in the window that comes up, and selecting a different sound driver.  If it's ALSA, select OSS.  If it's OSS, select ALSA.  If it's nothing, select ALSA.  After that, if sound still doesn't work, try rebooting again.

I think you may need to reexplore your reasons for wanting to run Linux.  If you want a Windows clone, then I would highly recommend sticking with Windows.  If you want a speedy, virus-free environment with a large collection of free software at your finger tips, then Linux may just work for you.

### Post by Anton32828 on 2009-11-13
I have seen this topic over and over again as I search these forums. 

My take on it is that Duns Scotus is right...but so are all the "Linux is not Windows" folks.

The problem is that the Ubuntu mission statement (or Canonical's if you prefer) is geared towards satisfying the needs of a non-technical end-user. I am very happy with the results thus far, but of course I'm more of a "power user" technical type. From what I can see in forum comments, the vast majority of new people who are attracted by the Ubuntu marketing campaign find their first introduction to the command-line to be a very unpleasant experience. But by comparison, when you download or buy a Suse or RedHat distribution, their respective websites leave you with no illusions about needing to be a bit technical. So folks like Duns Scotus tend to feel mislead by Ubuntu's grand ambitions, especially if they are unfamiliar with anything but Windows or Mac OS.

I can sympathize with how irritating the "why isn't it like Windows?" questions are to long-term users. Gnu/Linux was built from the ground up with a hacker's mentality, in the best sense of that term. Once you get accustomed to the culture (and understand the huge amount of work that has been done to get this far), the newbies seem just a tad ungrateful as well as uninformed.

I'm not sure there's a solution. The more I learn about Ubuntu, the more the entire project seems like an attempt to herd cats. I'm amazed it's worked so well thus far.
