### Post by Adamaeus on 2007-10-06
I'd heard of Ubuntu around the time of 6.06's release; I downloaded it and burned the iso, but never really experimented with it. I just happened to have a free CD available and thought, why not put it to some use. 

I experimented with Ubuntu on an old computer that had belonged to my aunt. It was running Millennium Edition of Windows, and it was awful--slow, buggy, crashed a lot...it wasn't useful. Then I remembered I had that disc somewhere, and installed Ubuntu 6.06 on it. 

And even though it's an old computer, it still ran pretty fast. Usably fast. And it did what I told it to do, when I told it to do it. There was some tinkering needed, but it wasn't nearly as difficult as with Windows. And there are so many rich programs available for Ubuntu--and the terminal, oh, the terminal! 

```

sudo apt-get install

```

That right there? That's my best friend. And the thing of it is, Ubuntu has such a wonderful, glorious community that if you have a problem, there's always a ready answer. See, the thing is, Windows is commercial. Nobody is enthusiastic about a money-maker. But Ubuntu is done out of love, and a desire to give people options, and my God, but do I love that! 

See, I'm not what may be called a sophisticated computer user. I surf the web, play the occasional Flash game, watch some YouTube, scour Wikipedia, and chat online via AIM/MSN/Yahoo. 

Every single thing that I do with Windows, Ubuntu lets me do--and much, much more stably so. I am presently on that old box of my aunt's, running 7.04, and anticipating 7.10. 

I also have a laptop with nearly a gig of ram, a good processor, and Vista. 

Why, you may ask, do I keep Windows? Because my family needs it; but at present I am scrapping together the cash to assemble a decent box strictly so I can install Ubuntu on it and have a system that'll last me for years. Seriously, one of the most impressive aspects of the OS (after coming off of Windows Vista, which is a total resource hog), is that it's so darn easy on memory. I like GNOME, I enjoy the hassle-free nature of using Ubuntu. It's like Canonical stripped everything down to what the most basic of computer users (like myself) need, but have kept their options open so that more advanced users can tweak the OS to their needs.

And I love that, I really do. 

I never, ever, ever want to go back to a Windowed world.

You should try a so-called Live CD to see if all your hardware is recognized or not. Currently, almost all installations come as a live CD. So, you may toss the CD into your drive, reboot the computer and try the linux distribution in question without touching a single byte on your current operating system. You should check especially whether your modem and printer / scanner model works under Linux.

Then you can install it when you are satisfied that every (or almost every) hardware has been recognized by Linux and haappily install the system to your computer. Speaking of which, latest Linux distributions are very adept on identifying and correctly configuring your hardware, even more-so than Windows, where you need to go fetch a driver from the web for each extra piece of hardware you attach to the computer.

For the distribution, I can recommend Ubuntu "Gutsy Gibbon" (when it is out) or Linux Mint "Celena" if you like GNOME environment better, or Kubuntu or PC Linux OS if you like KDE better. See for yourself which desktop better suits to your taste by trying out their Live CD's. 

When choosing the right distribution, the Linux Mint and PC Linux OS come a step further in terms of user-friendliness, because non-free, proprietary audio/video codecs and DVD playing are installed by deafult. However, installation of proprietary audio/video codecs has become very easy with the latest versions of Ubuntu/Kubuntu, so they are also one click away if you install Ubuntu (Gnome) or Kubuntu (KDE).
