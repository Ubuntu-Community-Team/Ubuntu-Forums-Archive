### Post by Tylerofl on 2007-11-16
**Personal history:**
My first experience with Linux was four or five years ago, my sophomore year of high school, using Mandrake Linux with KDE, attempting to connect with a Microsoft wireless card. Blegh, talk about a nightmare! To make a long story short, I gave up and went to a new pirated copy of XP.

A few years later, fed up (again) with Windows, I tried Ubuntu 5.04, if I remember correctly. It was better than Mandrake by a LONGSHOT (for some reason my Mandrake experience made me actively dislike KDE), but I still sold out again when I was too lazy to learn to dual boot XP and games like FarCry and Elder Scrolls: Oblivion hit the shelves.

Two years ago, on a fateful day in June, WGA locked me out of Windows! What nerve, that evil corporation, preventing me from using the software I stole! I heard from a friend that Ubuntu made a lot of headway since 5.04, and since I was pretty satisfied with my last experience, I went in again. *Why deal with pirating an operating system when you can use one legally for the best price: free?*

Of course, my pirate juices got flowing when Vista was leaked to the software underworld, and I believed I should always have some small partition set aside for Windows, just in case. I got Vista. I work at Geek Squad and sometimes I like to infect my own systems just to look at ways to fix them.

Days of not using the partition turned into weeks, which turned into months. I saw my hard drive was getting full, so without hesitation, I opened up gparted, deleted my NTFS partition, and resized my ext3 to fill the gap. *There's no going back now, and I couldn't be happier.*

**Current experience:**
There has never been a better time to join the open source community. You can burn CDs and DVDs, use comprehensive office and image editing software, use a completely free email client, scan and print, connect to the internet wirelessly, all for zero dollars, without having to steal thousands of dollars worth of software, or use ad-ridden layouts. It sounds like one of those cheesy commercials with a scam, but it isn't.

The only catch is that you'll have to let computer gaming take a back seat. Yeah, yeah, I know Wine is making a lot of progress, but getting games that were made two-three years ago running with "minor glitches" after doing hours of tweaking just doesn't compare to being able to run *new and old games* out of the box on Windows, with minimal tweaking. I realize that this isn't Linux's fault, and that vendors are the ones not developing games for operating systems that will give them little to no payback, but it doesn't change the fact that if you switch to Linux, *you will not be able to play games NEARLY as well as you could on Windows*. But gaming is a small price to pay for a free, legal, ad-free, and downright FUN operating system.

I have to deal with a lot of frustrated old folks at my job, because they think their computers are broken, when they really just don't know how to use them. A Windows user who expects to pick up a Linux operating system for the first time with everything working the way they want can be easily compared to confused elderly people. We were all raised into Windows, just like they weren't raised into computers at all. We don't remember being frustrated learning, because we picked it up while we were learning everything else in the world. *Learning a new operating system will give you some growing pains, but that doesn't mean the operating system is flawed*.

I've been lurking on the edges these forums for two years now, looking up how-to's and asking questions, but now I think I know enough to start giving help. I'm very much looking forward to the Ubuntu changes in the future. I'm never defiling my hard drive with Windows again. I've been using just Ubuntu for two years, and love it.

### Post by Yoooder on 2007-11-16
Preach it, brotha!

I had my little "Linux Moment" a couple months ago, I've been using Linux explicitly at home for a year and have used it on/off as my main desktop since the days of kernel 2.4.0.

A couple months ago I was building a new machine for a client, and loaded Ubuntu to test that I didn't get any dead components, and to do some burn-in tests.  Ubuntu gladly worked with all components and was overall a very good experience.  So upon delivery of the machine I had to install XP from my client's personal disk set.

XP's installer BSOD'd at the SATA controller, so I had to track down a floppy and a working windows machine to get the SATA driver onto a 3.5.  Then after the looong install of XP it booted up with a list of essentially every device in the system as "Unknown."  This isn't a surprise to me, I was a PC tech before a programmer and I have dealt with it many times.  However, after spending literally nearly 2 hours *just setting up the basic drivers* and then another whole afternoon of downloading/installing updates, rebooting, and doing it again and again, the realization hit--**Windows is harder to install/configure than Linux**

It really was a revelation, to be able to laugh at the old stigma that Linux is difficult to setup--and Canonical really does deserve a lot of the applause for their very slick LiveCD/installer setup.

After spending nearly a full day to just get to an updated stable base windows system I still had another day and a half of tracking down applications, installing Office, and other setup work before it was done.  It took me very near two days in XP to do what I managed in Ubuntu in a matter of only a couple hours.
