### Post by Old *ix Geek on 2007-09-11
Keep in mind that I started using *nix back--waaaaaaaaay back, in the mid-'80s--when everything was command-line driven, there was no such thing as a GUI, installing anything usually meant compiling it yourself, and tweaking kernel parameters was a common occurrence.

The first system I was solely responsible for setting up used SCO Xenix, had a 230MB drive, and came with 1MB of RAM (yes, I really do mean ONE) which I upgraded to 3MB right off the bat.  Putting that system together, including Wyse 60 and 150 terminals, a few printers, a couple of 9600 baud modems, and a few short-haul modems cost right around $25,000.  It was an amazing system which allowed dozens of users, from three different physical locations, to do their work quickly and easily.  I used **extensive** shell scripting in conjunction with database programming to run the entire business (a furniture store chain).

Over the years I, at times, had to cross over into the dark side, and was SO SHOCKED at how lacking, rigid, limited--and limiting--windoze was.  To say nothing of slow, bloated, and crash-prone! ](*,)

I started trying Linux back in 1991 with the very first release (which I still have around here somewhere--on diskettes).  I also tried other *nixes, such as Coherent (which I really liked), and eventually tried a lot of other Linux distros, including Red Hat, Slackware, Caldera, and even Lindows (Linspire when it was still called Lindows) because my father-in-law had just started using it and I needed to be able to support him.  They all had their pros and cons, but the one thing that was consistent (in MY experience) was that it took a LOT of work to get them configured and, especially, to get them working properly with various hardware/peripherals.

Fast forward to today.  In my never-ending quest to try newer and better Linux distros, I jumped on the Ubuntu bandwagon in 2005 when I installed Hoary on two of my computers, a desktop and a laptop.  I was very, very impressed, but there were still a lot of things I never could get working (such as recognizing my Kodak DX3900 camera via its USB connection).

In April I bought another laptop, and this was the first one I'd had that came with a wireless card; I put Feisty on it as soon as I wiped vista off of it.  It's an HP dv6000 and--as some of my posts prove!--I had the worst time getting a **fast** connection via wireless.  But thanks to the help found on these forums, I did get it working, and it's amazing.  I was also pleasantly surprised to find that after some work I got it to recognize my DX3900 camera.

A few weeks ago I bought a[nother] desktop, a Compaq SR5110NX and immediately put Feisty on it.  I have to say that I am BLOWN AWAY at the ease by which I've gotten everything I've thrown at it to work! That Kodak camera? Absolutely NO effort on my part.  I just plugged it in and it was recognized and ready to use.  Wireless? No problem, and no tweaking.  It just worked.  Printing to a networked printer--hooked up to a windoze box--no problem.  USB thumb drive? Recognized and ready to go.  And on and on and...

Two days ago I disassembled an old desktop (7 years old, running windoze 98--it was used solely as file storage for the rest of the network) that I had decided to retire, but took its hard drive out to continue using.  So yesterday--after first wasting 30 minutes opening the new desktop, disassembling the hard drive cage, etc., only to find that my legacy IDE drive can't connect to its cables--I bought an external hard drive enclosure to put it in.  Of course the box made no mention of Linux and, of course, it came with a CD for windoze, but I figured I'd get it working regardless.  I popped the old drive into it, put it back together, plugged its USB cable into the new Compaq and, guess what?  IT WORKED.  It just worked.  No drivers needed, no tweaking needed, no CD needed...it just recognized the old windoze 98 drive and I was immediately able to use it locally and via the network.

What else can I say?!  I am so impressed with the improvements made thus far with Ubuntu.  It's truly amazing to me, as someone who's been at this since the old days of CLI only, to see things work with such ease.  I want to give a big shout out to the Ubuntu team because this is really wonderful.  :mrgreen:

### Post by Old *ix Geek on 2007-09-11
Now I have even more to add: I just installed an HP Deskjet F4100 printer and...drumroll please...it JUST WORKS.  Locally and over the network.  (It's an all-in-one model--scanner, copier, and printer--and so far I've only tried printing since that was most important to me.)  I plugged it in via a USB cable and within seconds it had printed its own test page. (It's supposed to allow additional features, such as printer maintenance.)

### Post by Old *ix Geek on 2007-09-18
I thought I'd post another comment about how it keeps getting better, so here it is!

Right now I'm installing Feisty on a box that--don't laugh!--still had Hoary on it.  (And it was working just fine, but I finally felt like upgrading it.)  I timed when I started the installation process (i.e., from the moment I selected 'install' after booting from the live CD), which included formatting the partitions.  From the moment I started the installation until the moment the box was up, running (at a higher screen resolution than I've ever used on that box before), and with full network & Internet connectivity? 13 minutes.

I was so confident that everything had been detected, and was working, that I didn't even bother checking for connectivity before firing up Synaptic and choosing the KDE components; once I hit 'apply' it started cranking away, downloading 370-something files.

So that's where I am now.  There was ZERO effort involved on my part, other than the most cursory of input (country, time zone, keyboard, etc.).  That was it.  The install proceeded fully unattended and was completed in a total of 13 minutes.

Like my thread title says...it just keeps getting better and better. :lol:
