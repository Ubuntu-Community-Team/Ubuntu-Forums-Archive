### Post by MountainX on 2008-01-29
A while back I read an article my a major figure in the computing industry who is Linux-friendly. He concluded that Linux had no hope of becoming a widely adopted desktop OS. 

After having used Ubuntu 7.10 for a couple weeks, I believe he was wrong. Ubuntu could easily become as widely adopted as MS Windows is today if a few key things fell into place. The operating system itself seems very, very close to being ready for prime time. 

The main obstacles for people who want to use Ubuntu (or any LInux) seem to be hardware vendor support (drivers) and software application availability. 

The biggest challenges for reaching beyond the group of "early adopters" are probably commercial vendor agreements and the entrenchment of Windows among casual computer users, educational institutions, etc.

However, I believe Ubuntu has a chance to displace MS WIndows as the OS on the majority of computers in the world.

### Post by jbor7755 on 2008-01-29
I thought I would reply to this thread because I think the title sums it up pretty well. 

Yes, Ubuntu can do it.... you just have to work a little for it.

I originally installed 6.06 LTS version and while I thought the idea was admirable, many things such as productivity programs, network connectivity, plug and play and other drivers lagged a long way behind commercially available systems (at least as far as user friendliness goes).
Now with 7.10 a lot of this has been fixed for the likes of me and I am almost ready to ditch XP

However when I got pissed off I remembered one thing.  This is an operating system that is provided for FREE!

Firstly.  Why should I, or anyone else for that matter, expect a magic carpet ride through the learning process?  Why should it be easy to do everything? It's free.  That usually means that you need to do some of the legwork yourself.  A bit like cooking your own meals.  Anything is possible with this operating system.  But you just have to find out the methods which work for you.  This is something which gets better literally by the day.  How to's pop up all over the place.  If there was one thing I would suggest, it would be a section of reviews of the available printed manuals so that the newcomer might be able to pick just one.

Secondly you can't expect the experience to be the same as using windows.  Its a different system and has different strengths and weaknesses.  If it were the same as windows there would be little incentive to use it.

You also can't expect ubuntu to be as flashy.  Computer users do not NEED a desktop environment to look "good" (whatever that means).  Computers are supposed to do tasks. If I wanted something that looks good I would go a get a Van Gough.  The extra "flashiness" of windows doesn't make it more user friendly, it just sucks up the available memory.

In short Ubuntu is different but certainly no less capable than anything else.  It just requires a bit of patience and application.  Remember: It's FREE!

### Post by bobbybobington on 2008-01-30
I think Ubuntu CAN do it. First part is to see where ubuntu fits in against Windows and Apple. Windows is basically the "generic" os. It is predictable, it is familiar, not too exceptional, but it works. Apple is the opposite, it is bold, strives to surprise with the next big thing, sells an identity of uniqueness. 

So, where do we fit in? I think ubuntu's biggest product isn't the software, it's the community. It applies the aspects of the internet, such as involvement and community, to the world of software. This has already been done by countless other distros before, what's the difference? Openness. Ubuntu is open and kind to everyone, through support and the software itself.

I think ubuntu's philosophy positions itself to go where no distro has gone before. As computers become an increasingly important part of our lives, we become more invested in the os we use. Don't believe me? Look at all the senseless flamewars between windows and apple users. Ubuntu instead, takes that energy and emotion we put into OSes, and focuses it to do something positive.

So, do we need big advertising to catch up to our competitors? No. There is no need. Distribution is essentially free, economies of scale are irrelevant. Word of mouth is the most powerful and effective way for the community Preinstalling Companies will have to though, they a selling physical computer. So although microsoft and apple may be big and powerful, Ubuntu is a powerful philosophy with a community to be reckoned with. The software will follow the other philosophy and community.
