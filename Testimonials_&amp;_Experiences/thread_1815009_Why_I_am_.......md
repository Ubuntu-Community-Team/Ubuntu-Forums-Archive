### Post by georgemc on 2011-07-31
Bleeding edge GUI's currently, IMHO, are Gnome3 and Unity.  They do require a high minimum of hardware performance that many older systems just do not have.  They mark a distinct change in the accustomed GUI's.  If G3 or Unity will not work then there are many others that do, for example X|L Ubuntu.  Both, G3 and Unity, need one or two release cycles to mature and full fill the promise of a new and innovative GUI with good work flow and appeal.
 

 “The first impression” thing.  Through the years I have always stuck with “factory defaults”, used very little to no customization  on desktops etc.  The reason behind this is that when I re-install the system I want it up and running ASAP without a bunch of fussing with customization.  So yes my XP installation still has the “rolling green hills, cloudy blue sky” background, and my U10.04 still has the purple background with the default theme.
 

 Yes, I know it is all about choice, however, good, solid, and appealing defaults for any system is what a new user will see first and have to deal with.  So, for any of the developers who may wander across this post; Make sure the defaults work and are appealing.  Nothing like the first impression.
 

 I believe one of the most confusing part for new users of open source products in general is the massive about of choices one has these days.  Should I use the LTS version or go with the latest release?  What distribution should I use?  I see many posts asking for guidance on this and other forums, and I see as many opinions as there are responses.  Confusing and daunting for new users I must say.
 

 I haven't put any kind of numbers together on why new users leave or if they just don’t like a certain implementation, however, I feel that most post and leave because some thing fundamentally doesn't work for them.  If you can't make it work, you can't use it.  So back to what works.
 

 Thank you for your kind responses.
 

 George
