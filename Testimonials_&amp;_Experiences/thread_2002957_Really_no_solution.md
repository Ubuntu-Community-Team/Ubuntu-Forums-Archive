### Post by eyeofliberty on 2012-06-13
> **aykoola said:**
> No, Arch, Slack and some others are not right for us...and never will be because of the complexity. But Ubuntu? Linux for human beings?

Well, I would personally argue the opposite here. I primarily use Slackware, and aside from the initial setup and configuration (the same with Arch), it's really not that complex. If you learn to use the tools available to you, and learn how to set configurations by editing text files (it's really not that hard), then you truly harness the power of Linux, and empower yourself at the same time. Things break more with distros like Ubuntu, because of short release cycles, the tendency to configure everything through a GUI interface, and patches to the core tools and apps. This often creates buggy software which breaks in one way or another, causing user frustration. When something breaks in a distro such as Arch, Slackware, or Gentoo, it's usually your fault, because you did something stupid (haha), and because you have a pretty good knowledge of Linux from using such a distro (hopefully), you have a pretty good idea of how to fix it. Or you know how to ask the right questions to other users in the community forums.

Having said this, persistence is key. Seriously, consider what I said in my previous post in this thread: install 11.10, and then do an upgrade. It worked for me, when the 12.04 disk installation wouldn't.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2012-06-16
Check your RAM. On my partner's machine I had trouble even booting the live CD, and when it did the installer would crash. I took one stick of RAM out, and the machine has been as solid as a rock ever since.

Your RAM might be part of a faulty batch; if you take out one of the RAM sticks and it has the brand-name "Kingston" anywhere, then it's part of a faulty batch :-)  No but seriously, I've had a 50% failure rate with Kingston memory.
