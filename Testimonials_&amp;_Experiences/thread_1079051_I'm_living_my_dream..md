### Post by jbysmith on 2009-02-24
> **Vince4Amy said:**
> When I install Ubuntu for customers I try and stick with the LTS releases so they don't have to upgrade as often.

Ditto this, especially if they just need the basics or just first-timers in general.  Some of the "more important" apps still get updated via backports and from the security team.  

Get used to how the OS works and the general procedures before you introduce a new element of complexity from dealing with the problems that bleeding edge typically delivers.  Jumping into a borked X configuration for example when you don't even know how to edit the config file would be extremely aggravating to say the least, and just leads to the inevitable "Linux sucks" posts because they didn't know what they were doing.  Of course XP won't have these problems; security patches aside it really hasn't introduced anything new in *years*.

Aside from the risk of breakage every six months, there's typically bugs introduced, some from the various packages, some from Ubuntu itself.  (I like the stuff Canonical adds, looking forward to 9.04's notification system for example.. is it based on Mumbles or something new?)

I know some people who instead of sticking with the LTS releases they just stay one version behind; by the time they upgrade the bugs are typically gone.

For my own desktop, I run Arch and like living on the bleeding edge, but I certainly wouldn't recommend that to a client or newcomer.  I used to do tech support for Windows, and I've heard a *lot* of silly questions, everything from blaming the OS because their monitor wasn't plugged in to their "cup holder" (DVD drive) being broken.  (I kid you not.)  I shudder to think of supporting Linux with the same type of questions.
