### Post by sprezzatura on 2009-04-05
This is my first experience with Ubuntu. Definitely a step up from Lindows/Linspire! The appearance is gorgeous, the color scheme warm & rich. The interface is simple and consistent, better than Windows. The installation was very straightforward.

I am particularly delighted that Aardvark runs on Firefox. Aardvark is what's going to make Firefox overtake Internet Explorer.

I am a Windows developer. I've been writing code since I was 16 in 1965 (not on Windows obviously...). I've made a good living and supported five dependants by writing programs on dozens of different platforms, O/S and languages. I've had a great time writing C++ on PCs since 1995, and now C#.

I just spent about eight hours trying to get an Intel graphics card to display higher than 640x480 on Ubuntu 9.04 (see the [_sordid saga_]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?p=7015602#post7015602")). I combed through all the FAQs and support forums. I stumbled on the solution by accident, right before I was about to install 8.10 on top of Jaunty. Keep in mind this is a plain vanilla HP Pavillion (2.5 GHz), salvaged from the scrap heap for $100 because its Windows had become irretrievably infected with malware.

I want to thank those who took the time and effort to post solutions. I admire and respect their skill. I also realize in life you don't get what you don't pay for.

However, to expect the average user to wade through these complex steps is utterly unrealistic. 'nano /etc/X11/xorg.conf'? Are you kidding me? I support about 200K to 300K users, typical consumers, and I have a fairly good idea of what they are capable of, and what they aren't...

On Windows, this screen resolution problem would never have occurred. Why weren't the Intel drivers integrated in Ubuntu?

Anyway, I still think there is a solid future for Ubuntu: safe surfing.

One of my Windows XP PCs just got completely trashed by random, drive-by malware. It was so bad the only solution was to install a new hard drive, re-install Windows, copy the data, and quarantine the original drive.

I expect we're going to see more and more of this as hapless users connect their PCs directly to a broadband modem without routers or firewalls, and wonder in frustration why their Windows PCs are getting slower and slower, and connect to strange places... Malware writers are becoming increasingly deadly, as they probe and penetrate every corner and weakness of Windows. Microsoft can't compete with thousands of clever, jobless malicious programmers with time on their hands, and Mob money in their pockets.

So I continue to use Windows for my accounting and program development, because that's where the market is. I've disconnected all my XP PCs from the Internet. And I encourage my local computer dealer to put together a 'safe surfing' package: used generic PC, Ubuntu, second-hand monitor, presto! No more malware! Everybody can use a second PC.

An idea whose time has come (TM).

If I ever become independently wealthy, I'd love to write in Python for Linux. Meanwhile, I got bills and a mortgage to pay...

### Post by jbysmith on 2009-04-05
Well I do *somewhat* agree with the OP, there are the occasional glitches that can really throw a curve-ball at a person who doesn't understand how the OS works under the hood.  This isn't specific to Ubuntu; some come upstream from various developers, some are the hardware vendors, etc etc.  (nVidia's drivers for example are still going through growing pains.. I've had my fair share of wrestling with them from time to time too.. especially under a KDE4 environment, they didn't get along so well for a while.)

But that's definitely not unique to Linux.  Any Windows novice who has gotten into trouble with malware or a random application breaking something in the registry can attest to this.  Boot into safe mode, delete this file, edit such and such registry entries.. no way a novice would know this stuff without pouring over Google either.  

Also try to avoid the "I had problems with Ubuntu so Linux sucks" mindset which I see wayyyyyy too often.  No one distro is going to work for every single person perfectly out of the box.  Ubuntu is definitely getting there, but not quite yet.  If it doesn't work for you, there's plenty of other distributions to try that probably will.  Some come with proprietary drivers, some don't.  They all have their own philosophies and goals when it comes to their particular build.

That's not unique to Linux either.  One of my test systems is an older Athlon processor based on the nForce2 chipset.  Still has plenty of life in it, but it runs Vista quite poorly just because the nForce2 chipset isn't supported.  XP runs perfectly, and Ubuntu 9.04 runs like a dream on it.

Or if you want to throw the "need to upgrade" quote at me, fine.  I have another gaming rig.  Intel Core2Duo E8400 Wolfsdale running at 4.2GHz with 8GB of memory and a high end high definition nVidia PCI-E 16 video card.. yes I take my game time seriously. It also doesn't work well with Vista or Windows 7 because of the VIA chipset being used, buggy drivers...hasn't been addressed in the past couple of years.  Not surprisingly, I had Linux going the first time with zero issues, running Arch.  No fooling around with configuration files.. just installed the nVidia drivers, ran the config tool and off I went.  By that rationale I can say the exact opposite.. Windows isn't ready for prime time because Linux worked right out of the box.

An operating system isn't ready for Joe Six-Pack only if Joe doesn't bother to check for hardware compatibility issues before installing it.  Cant just assume that since I just bought the hardware in question, it *must* work with everything.  That applies to *any* operating system... Windows, Linux, Hackintosh (OSX on a PC.. not a Mac joke), whatever.  

Do your homework, and regardless you need to be prepared to learn new things. If you're new to the operating system in question, all those years of experience are going to mean absolutely jack.  Completely different ways of doing things.  Don't be afraid of the console either; the amount of power it gives you is downright absurd, and is actually a huge time saver once you get the hang of it.  I only wish I had something similar in Windows.. the command prompt is an absolute joke and PowerShell isn't much better.
