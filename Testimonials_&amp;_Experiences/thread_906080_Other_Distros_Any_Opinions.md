**Zenwalk ** another xfce based distro .. wh i liked for its simplicity and lightness .. it worked out of the box and i didnt have any problem with it .. it also looked simple and although i didnt explore it much .. i look forward to running it on my home desktop when i have the time .. also i think its more of a source based distro .. but am not soo sure

**pclinux OS** i had soo much expection for pclinux .. having hear a lot of cool things about it .. i downloaded pclinux os 2007 ..and was hugely disappointed ..it really sucked .. nothing worked for me .. it didnt even detect my screen resolution .. ( and i installed it on an intel GMA 945 based laptop) wireless and too didnt work .. did i talk about the installation process .. gosh it was a pain ..i spent hours trying to figure out how the partition editor works .. when i tried to register to the forum to seek help .. i was told to wait for a mod to approve my account .. that was where i lost it .. i was a noobie in need of urgent help  the best i get is to wait for hours maybe days for me to be approved .. i just lost it formatted the drive and installed 
**mandriva spring 2008** it was another pain to install .. cus it uses the same partitioning tool as pclinux .. but after installation was quite cool .. i loved the kde implementation so much that right now my work computer uses kde (sumtin i couldnt have imagined) wireless worked and even the ATI card worked out of the box with compiz .. everything was smooth and dandy till i logged out .. then it started .. i logged out into a black hole .. and i had to shut down forcefuklly to get out of it .. a lil googling showed it was a age ol bug that mandriva has with KDM .. i tried all fixes but non worked .. so i bailed and installed 
**gOS** Which to me is simiply awesome .. i was stunned at what i saw .. yeah it closly resembles a Mac .. but its also cool .. in fact the coolest linux i have ever seen .. the Enlightment desktop was smooth and beautiful .. the down side was its not meant for advance users .. most config tools are hidden or not installed .. so although it would be cool on my girlfriends laptop it just wasnt for me .. 

**Mint  **It was to be the best decision i have ever made since switching to Linux . Some have called it Ubuntu made right. and its not had to see why,must of the things that i have personally wanted to have on Ubuntu out of the box came out of the box with MInt. Multimedia Codecs,Thunderbird mail clients,and a cool mint menu which some  legend says vista ripped off Mint . other cool things about mint is its simplicity for example i love the fact that you could uninstall a program just by right clicking it on the menu and choose uninstall . ndiswrapper is installed by default for those whose wireless card depend on it to work . that is one less thing to worry about.

Overall Mint is still very much like Ubuntu its 100% compatible with any application that works with Ubuntu and because mint is based on Ubuntu .every mint release is based on the most recent Ubuntu release . the latest version is Elyssa which is based on Ubuntu Hardy Heron . and yes it uses Ubuntu repository in combination with Mint’s own repository . i personally would recommend Mints to new users who wants to try Linux for the first time. it would be a pleasant experience

Hi Carlos, I am a big Openbox fan (typing on it right now) and it's become very popular lately on the forums. I am all for doing it yourself from a minimal install, but I also think you can learn a lot by studying existing distros. Crunchbang is an outstanding Ubuntu+Openbox build (in fact it was built up from the Mini ISO just like you're thinking of doing). Boxbuntu is a brand new project that is quite young but shows promise; it's the closest Openbox distro to Ubuntu in terms of user interface and theme. Then there is SliTaz; it's a complete Openbox distro that uses less ram than Ubuntu CLI! (a minuscule 25mb if installed to the hard drive) It uses LXDE, which is Openbox+menus+panels, if you're into that sort of thing (I like my Openbox rather plain). Then, there are probably others I haven't tried yet. My point is, burn some Live CDs of these distros, and you should get some good ideas for your project. :)

You can test out all of your ISOs on virtual machines without even burning them to CD. It's great for comparing distros because (ram permitting) you can run them side-by-side on identical-spec vm's.
