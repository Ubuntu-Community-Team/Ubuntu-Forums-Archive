### Post by TWhyman on 2012-02-13
We are at a very interesting stage in the development of computing platforms. The humble PC is not alone and is joined by smart phones, tablets and Internet enabled TVs, and probably more. However, these later entrants are all content consumer devices by and large. The PC and especially the Desktop PC will remain the platform of choice for the content creator, if only because it alone can provide the rich and diverse environments needed for content creation across a broad range of content types.

To me the Unity Desktop makes sense in the context of the content consumer. It works well with imprecise pointing devices, such as fingers and gestures. It suits users that run a limited and usually constant range of apps, and are happy with the slower Icon view rather the faster list view. And it looks good - mostly.

On the other hand, I do not view Unity as the interface for a content creator. Take my own setup. Ubuntu 10.4, two graphics cards, two screens, one X server. I'll have EMail and Firefox open on the left hand screen plus reference documents, while on a busy day I can be using four desktops on the right hand screen for Open Office, Anjuta (C++), Eclipse(PHP) and Lazarus, together with a virtual machine running a server under test. I might even have Windows running in another VM because the client wants the documentation 100% MS Word compatible. And there are often umpteem consoles, text edit windows and file browsers open as I flick between the various environments during an integration test, plus the odd Gimp or Inkscape session if there are graphics that need tweaking.

Gnome 2 handles this beautifully well. I could draw up a list of improvements if you asked, but these will all be small things. Unity just isn't targeted at this market and as for Gnome 3 - I have only one question - why? It's a downgrade. The Gnome 3 developers really should have understood the truth behind "if it ain't broke don't fix it". 

In my opinion, Canonical are right to push ahead with Unity for the Content Consumer platforms. This is a big and important market and Linux and Ubuntu should be there.

However, in this rush to meet a new market Canonical would be wise not to forget the content creators. We are the evangelists for Ubuntu. We are those who tell others to use it and develop the apps that they use it for. If Unity is the only future, then we cannot continue with Ubuntu - and is that really what Canonical wants to see.

I believe that there should be a divergence in Ubuntu into "Consumer", "Professional" and "Server" platforms. Unity is the UI for the "Consumer" platform while Gnome 2/Mate is the UI for the "Professional" Platform. Forget Gnome 3. Perhaps some will argue that xfce should be the UI for the "Professional" Platform. I could live with that as xfce is not that far behind Gnome 2 - but Gnome 2 exists, it looks like it will continue to exist through Mate and/or its many supporters, so why shouldn't Gnome 2 continue as the UI of choice for Ubuntu Content Creators.

### Post by snowpine on 2012-02-13
You are confusing "workflow" with "task." 

People can perform the task of "creating content" with Windows, Mac, Linux, Unity, Gnome, KDE, Xfce, etc.

You personally prefer the "workflow" of Gnome 2, and I suspect you would prefer it whether you are creating content, playing games, surfing the web, or writing the next great novel. I respect that.

Understand however that Ubuntu has always been a fast moving, latest-and-greatest, cutting edge, "look at me!" distro. Ubuntu is not a distro that clings to tradition and outdated software. Ubuntu is not the "Gnome 2 Historical Preservation Society."

That being said, Ubuntu is supporting Gnome 2 through April 2013. That should be plenty of time to evaluate your workflow and decide whether to continue with future Ubuntu releases. (If you decide to jump ship, I recommend Red Hat, which will support Gnome 2 through 2020, for a price.)
