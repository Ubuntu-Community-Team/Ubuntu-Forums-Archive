### Post by mr72 on 2009-04-24
Well, I am sorry to say, but I had to switch back to Windows Vista.

I tried to make Ubuntu work on my Acer 5515 laptop, but there were just too many headaches, unsolvable problems, and flat-out road blocks to make it work. When it was working, or in the cases where it worked, it was great.

What worked great was web browsing (firefox), email (thunderbird), and apps like Openoffice and Gimp. A decade ago I would have said that this should be enough for any typical user but unfortunately it falls short on one important front (I'll get to that in a minute). I also love using gnome, it looks great and with the right theme and launcher panel it had usability right in there with OSX. I am a long-time linux user (nearly 20 years now) and really wanted to make it work for my daily PC needs but it just was not going to happen.

The biggest, #1, supremo deal-breaker for me was decent support for my iPod. The iPod failed to mount correctly half of the time, and even after trying all of the apps out there that are supposed to manage your iPod, none of them are even remotely close to the brilliantly simple integration of an iPod with iTunes. I wanted to just plug in the iPod and have it sync with new music automatically. How hard is that? Too hard for Linux. I think this one limitation would likely ruin the applicability of Linux for the majority of casual users.

Then the #2 deal-breaker is lacking support for Windows apps that I have to use for work: Visio and CorelDRAW. The Corel installer crashes under Wine, and I could not get either Visio 2003 or 2007 to install or run after a week's worth of trying. The fact that there is no Linux app out there that will read/write the file formats for either of these Windows programs made this a no-go and forced me back to Windows. The Wine folks blame a bug in the Corel installer but of course it works fine on Windows so no matter who is to blame, it's Linux users who get the short end of the stick. I didn't even try to install another program I have to use for work, Finale. Reaper would run and play files but I never tried to tweak the kernel and sound drivers to get it to work nearly as efficiently as it works under Windows native.

Other niggles had to do with iffy USB media. My external HD (NTFS) would routinely unmount uncleanly and leave some flag set that necessitated a command-line mount with "force" to get it to mount again. My Sony-Ericsson phone would not mount the memory stick unless I plugged it in prior to booting. And documents stored on my thumb drive wound up corrupted so frequently as to make that means of storage unusable, since everything had to have a redundant backup stored somewhere (I even lost 51 pages of a document that I will never get back). Of course all of these things work seamlessly in Windows, never a hitch.

So as much as I love to hate Windows, it is running super stable, fast, and slick on my 5515. A decade ago I tried the experiment of running Linux on my family's home PC and determined that it was close, but no cigar. Unfortunately, it is in that same state now, at least for what I would consider the majority of normal users. No decent iPod support is the death blow.

There's a lot to love about Ubuntu and it has come a long way. The installer for Ubuntu is slick and effective. The update manager is great. Synaptics package manager is brilliant. I spent a month or more trying to make it work out because I just really wanted to believe it would work. It's kind of like the middle aged guy trying to make his sports car work for a daily driver (BTW, I also have a sports car) but finally figures out that a mini-van is the only rational choice.
