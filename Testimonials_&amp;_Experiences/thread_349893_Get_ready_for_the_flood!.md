### Post by psyne on 2007-01-31
I have to say that for myself one of the biggest decisions to change was the DRM (read anti-consumer rights) features of Vista. One of the most annoying issues with Windows Media player is the fact that you can't take your media with you between computers. Unfortunately I have to agree with Meng the average user is probably not going to install Linux. The vast majority of people perceive Linux as inaccessible, perhaps this is matter of propaganda or perhaps this is just perception. 

Your average user wants something shiny that works out the box and is easy to setup. Ironically these are the things that I have found Linux and specifically Ubuntu to be. I told my friends and coworkers that I made the switch and they started telling me all the things that I couldn't do with Linux and how hard it is to use. They felt that because of my technical background I would have no problem switching but the average user could not. I showed them how easy and accessible Ubuntu is and after a while a couple where considering trying it out. 

Unfortunately the average shopper that walks into Best Buy or Comp-USA is not going to get that perspective. They will receive the selling points of the new OS from Microsoft and pay for it with their new machine. Until Linux can get into the retail channel (which it has with limited success) it will be a continual uphill battle. However the DRM and increasing system requirements would be excellent talking points for the Linux community to highlight in encourage people to switch.

### Post by Phrawm48 on 2007-01-31
I've had Ubuntu running for just under a week now.  I, too, owe a lot to this forums and its contributors for the many positive, helpful responses I received to my questions.

Note, however, that like many new users with moderate levels of technical competence, I stumbled over and struggled with the same basic system configuration problems.  My experiences (they're still ongoing), my conversations with other new Linux users, and my travels through various Linux help forums, invariably include the following grumbles.
[B][U]
Network connections and operation --[/U][/B]
Networking!  (networking!  networking!  networking!).  Seems everyone encounters one or more problems getting a network connection up and running. Unfortunately, in too many instances neither Gnome's nor KDE's GUI based tools enable users to get a network connection working "out of the box".  When this happens, "fixing" the networking problem requires the new user to (a) use multiple Linux text commands to go spelunking for their networking hardware's operating characteristics,  and (b) experimentally enter cryptic representations of those characteristics into one or more text files. Users find the latter requirement especially daunting since entering the wrong character in a text file only exacerbates the problem.  Abandoning Linux is usually the next step.

_** Drivers -- **_
I was lucky: It only took me three days(!) to figure out how to get a  proper graphics adaptor for my GeForce FX 5500.  At least I finally succeeded (thanks again to this forum and the Envy script tool).  Let's not even talk about getting the value-added features of  multi-button pointing devices and keyboards working properly.  Long story short, many users crankily abandon Linux when  their system devices can't readily be made to work to their full capabilities.

_** Attached devices --**_ 
It's too tricky to get attached devices. especially USB storage devices, to function as a truly integrated parts of the Linux user experience.   For example, it requires a fair amount of knowledge, and multiple, sophisticated commands,  to be able to list ("ls") files on a USB drive that Gnome / KDE has mounted and which is clearly visible and accessible via the GUI. This problem in turn leads quickly to the new-for-most-users concept of managing owners and groups for these devices and their files.  Many new Linux users find this bewildering and, after a few tries and getting around the obstacles, abandon Linux for the "more rational" behavior of Windows.

The essential point is that  somewhere, somehow, there needs to the sorts of GUI-based, easily usable  tools that will enable moderately skilled users to  begin enjoying Linux so that they can and will incrementally and iteratively acquire more comprehensive knowledge of Linux's powers and benefits.

The alternative is that many users who very much want to use something other than Windows will experiment briefly with Linux and then abandon it as too hard to use...

Cheers & hope this helps,
Ric
