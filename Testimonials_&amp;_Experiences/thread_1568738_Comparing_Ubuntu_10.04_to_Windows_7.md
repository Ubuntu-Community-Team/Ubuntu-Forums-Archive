### Post by arvevans on 2010-09-05
One has to realize that Linux is not Windows.  Linux, including Ubuntu, is the evolution of much older and well thought out operating systems based on UNIX, which came about as a derivative of the Multics operating system written by McGill University for early IBM mainframes.  Linux is a multi-user OS, where Microsoft originally was a single-user-at-a-time system.  Can Microsoft do multiple users at the same time by acting as an OS server?  I don't know because I have not used it for several years.

Any comparison between Ubuntu Linux and Microsoft would be a comparison of apples versus oranges, and not really demonstrating anything meaningful.  They are very different operating systems, with very different goals and requirements.  In addition, you MUST take time to learn any new OS before claiming to be expert enough to do meaningful comparisons.  This typically takes several years of in-dept involvement in all aspects of the target system.

Same situation goes for comparison of Windows to Macintosh OS, which is again UNIX based from the BSD-UNIX source.  Macintosh and Windows are very different OS, with different goals and different requirements.

When you purchase hardware that is specifically advertised as being for Windows, it is somewhat unreasonable to expect it to operate on other operating systems, even though it might plug into your motherboard.  There are hardware compatibility lists available on-line that show which hardware has been tested and verified to work on various versions of Linux.
