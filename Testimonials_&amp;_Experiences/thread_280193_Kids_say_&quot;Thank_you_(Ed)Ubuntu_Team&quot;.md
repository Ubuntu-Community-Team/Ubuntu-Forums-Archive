### Post by ecomoney on 2006-10-20
Wow! How many words can a picture say? Id be happy to let you know a bit more about the project, and give credit to the others involved in it locally and internationally as well.

Were a small [Social Enterprise]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_enterprise") (more social than enterprise) who primarily write open-source software for managing a type of community regeneration scheme online. Weve got one set up in our local area as a live test. The computers were donated and set up by volunteers that have been recruited through the [Local Scheme.]("http://www.scunnymoney.com")

We sourced the P.C.s through our [local Linux User Group]("http://www.scundog.org/") (Specifically the company which sponsors them, thanks Shaun! :D ). Though we had set up old p.c.'s for charities, community groups and volunteers before with Linux (of the [Small Puppy Variety]("http://www.puppylinux.org/user/viewpage.php?page_id=1")), this was the first time we had used Edubuntu. I must confess I dont know very much about (ed)ubuntu on a technical level - but I didnt have too! I quickly read the Edubuntu website, downloaded, burned and booted the live cd as a quick evaluation. I was immedietly impressed with how it came already set up for the purpose with everything included (especially [Gcompris]("http://gcompris.net/-en-") which is supurb as a computer skills learning tool, heads up to the package writers too). 

As im usually very busy with other projects I gave the computers to [a local volunteer]("http://profile.myspace.com/index.cfm?fuseaction=user.viewprofile&friendid=43578867") to set up. He is a semi-skilled Windows user with no formal IT qualifications, and had not installed Linux before. When I asked him how the install had gone he said perfectly without a hitch. He did not need to install any drivers or extra software, Everything was pretty self explanitory. This is really important as many people do not have the luxury of time to learn complex set up routines if they are from poorer backgrounds, as they must also take the time to keep a roof over their heads (incidently, this volunteer actually used the "volunteer credits" that he "earned" through the scheme for setting up these p.c.'s towards paying his rent!).

We took the photos with the help of another volunteer photographer from the scheme who doesnt want to be named. We are hoping that the local newspaper will cover the story with a request for more P.C.'s, as the children argue over them terribly! The volunteers, nursurys and community groups in the area are desperate for 2nd user computers to use as Linux PC's. Linux is particularly good for charities and community groups for many reasons, we have found that:-

- Because the licience is free this allows them to save lots of money for other projects (such as childrens meals in the case of the nursury). 

- It will run on very cheap or free hardware, saving them even more money (and reducing the problem of disposing of what is essentially toxic waste).

- Thy do not need to track complex liciences or licience keys to keep within the Law.

- They know they will work reliably and consistantly and will not require expensive skilled work to keep them up to date and virus free.

- Spyware with innappropriate advertising will not install itself and slow the computers down wasting their time.

- The security is also very important to them as many deal with difficult cases and vulnerable people. They have a responsibility to these people to keep their information safe. 

Im not sure that the Linux community as a whole, many of which only use their computers at home, fully realise the potential of what they have created to help increase productivity of community, voluntary and educational groups. With so many new competing "uses" for government money around the world at the moment many of these groups are struggling to equip themselves to do their very important and much-needed job. Please people, if you have spare time or equipment, go down to the nearest drop in centre, nursury, school or charity and offer to help them out. Feed back your findings both technical and anecdotal to the programmers who create the supurb thing that is GNU/Linux - so it can be made even better!!!!

Anyway, thanks again for your supurb work, here are some more cute pictures of some of the results or your labours and expertise.

**The Wrawstock Festival**
This festival was organised and managed using admin provided entirely by open source. The posters and flyers were designed in Openoffice and the GIMP. Thunderbird, VOIP and AMSN was used to communicate between the bands, the venue, and the organisers. It was also advertised on the local musicians Website that runs APACHE server and MySQL. The accounting was done using OpenOffice and Gnumeric.

Around £800 was raised towards much-needed diagnostic equipment for the local hospital intensive care unit. This wouldnt have been possible without the "rock"-solid stability of open source.;) 

[IMG]http://www.scunnymoney.com/userpix/2_NM270806wrawstock28_1.jpg[/IMG]

[IMG]http://www.scunnymoney.com/userpix/2_NM270806wrawstock24_1.jpg[/IMG]

Theres more pics and a write up of the event [here]("http://www.scunnymoney.com/viewtopic.php?t=427")

**Riddings Drop in Centre Cybercafe**

[IMG]http://www.ecomoney.co.uk/files/images/cybercafe_0.jpg[/IMG]

This is a free, open access cybercafe in the middle of a poor estate in Scunthorpe. There is access to the net via Firefox, cheap SIP phone calls, word processing for C.V.'s with OpenOffice, AbiWord and Gnumeric. The internet connection is shared securely between the cybercafe and the Voluntary workers admin machines using an old Pentimum 1 computer running [Smoothwall]("http://www.smoothwall.org/").

Crucially it provides access to the Job Centre Database (the main jobcentre is several miles away). Currently there is one PC running Edubuntu for the Creche here too, but we plan to install it on the other four P.C.'s  and have them dual boot Puppy Linux/Edubuntu. This will be used for the parentcraft and kids sessions. When we can source another four P.C.'s we will be running open source computing classes in the evening. We also have a wireless community LAN project in development from here, made possible by Linux router software from [OLSR.org]("http://olsr.org/").

### Post by ecomoney on 2006-12-17
Greetings popoi/snuffkin

All linux's share a common thread and the principles are the same. Installing Edubuntu is very easy (the computers in the picture were installed by a teenager with only a basic working knowledge of windows. They had only ever installed Red Hat twice two years previously). You do not necessisarly people that have specialised in it. There is lots of excellent support available online, especially to organisations like yourselves. Having had experience of supporting both, you will not need anything like the same level of support for Linux as you do ..other.. products.

As for the Edupup (its not going to be called Edupuppy), it is a very early stage spin off of puppy Linux it is very easy to remaster a new setup using latest puppy with inbuilt wizards to make a new self-installing live-cd). There are no official translations, and it is currently undergoing a major re-work. We have volunteered to do the field testing of it only. There is a brazilian portugese version of puppy, and there are people in the puppy team who have stated they will do spanish versions. [http://www.murga-linux.com/puppy/viewtopic.php?t=667&start=30](http://www.murga-linux.com/puppy/viewtopic.php?t=667&start=30)

To make the spanish version of edupuppy it would be a matter of copying some language files into it. GioveLUG (an italian Linux User Group) I believe do an italian transaltion, and they would be able to help.

Overall I think that the language support in edubuntu is better, and it is my belief that puppy runs very fast on older hardware (please let me know if you have more experience comparing the two). Which distro you use should depend on what hardware you have, how fast you need the computers to go and whether you need native language support.
