### Post by emarkay on 2006-10-19
Thank you for assisting me in making my transition from Window$.  I am still learning, but have now spent 2 full days without having to get back into the Winworld.  

***(This is sooo cool!)***

The kindness of those who have assisted in getting me up to speed is sincerely appreciated!  

While I can't ofer any help to other 'noobs' really, at this point, I can share some information I have found that has helped me:



First, if you are running the CD, remember , while you can print and 'surf' the WWW, you really can't configure anything - you are not using a Hard Disk, so nothing gets saved.  But this is a FANTASTIC way to get familiar with Ubuntu before actually installing it!

Installing and dual booting was pretty easy, just map out what you want to do (what discs and volumes and all that) BEFOREHAND!  

If you have a number of volumes, that you don't want to show in Ubuntu on your desktop, it's easy to unmount them - this also speeds up the booting process.

Editing GRUB to customise your boot menu is easy, plus there are about 10 recent posts here that detail it.  ;)

Removing Firefox will NOT "break your system", though you will lose the "ADD/REMOVE" software feature in "Applications" and be forced to use the Synaptic Package Manager.  Then, you can also removing the bloat of games and other things unneded. Users of Pentuim class and above PC's can get the "686" version of Ubuntu there, too for better performance.

Do "Google" the websites of some of the packages you may want to install beforehand, for more information and other things, just to be safe - Bookmark them for future reference, too.

Toolbars can be modified so they "Star Trek" door slide to one side and dissapear (someone's GOT to make a sound like that!) giving more vertical screen space.

The GIMP image editor is a great and usable image editor - I have just begun to play with it!

The Open Office really does what is says - emulates M$ Office!

Do make backups of you configurations and files - it's easy to forget to do so when things seem so smooth in Linux.  There are a number of posts here that show how to.

While not all hardware is natively supported, getting good with Google (along with this forum and other Linux forums) can enable one to get just about anything working in Ubuntu.  It may not have ALL the features you are used to, but in reality does anyone really use all those features anyway?  :)  Also the developers of those drivers are generally responsive to suggestions, and updated seem to be frequent, too!

Finally DON'T BE AFRAID to ask for help here!  If possible, first do type in your topic in the search box, or use the search feature; this will save you time, possibly provide multiple answers, and help those who are helping us in not having to waste their time.  If possible when posting make sure you have what version Ubuntu you are using stated somewhere, and try to be specific, if it's about something specific.  :)
