### Post by sunfromhere on 2012-07-10
Just my 2 cents to the newbie department...

So my father's been using my computer every now and then, when the need arose (he usually works on Windows XP). He converted some xlsx files to xls, searched for Russian war ships on Google Earth, killed me with a gallery of photos, sent mails, watched movies... basically everything one uses a computer for.

Yesterday I just clicked "shut down" and the computer screen showed "Ubuntu" before it shut down.

He said: Ubuntu? :confused:
I said: Ubuntu!

He had never before seen (or heard of) Ubuntu in his life and has been using it as normal because he expected "something different" as my computer is newer (operating systems do get different over time). So he adapted.

Unity, Gnome, Windows... If it works, it works. If it doesn't - hammer time!

### Post by 3Miro on 2012-07-11
People have hard time migrating to a new system only when they are well accustomed to an old one. Windows "power users" will have very hard time moving over to Linux and many of them believe that if they are having hard time, then regular users would have even harder time. Truth is, people with low computer skills find one system just the same as another, for a person who hasn't made an investment into a system or interface, Linux works just as well as Mac or Windows.

### Post by QIII on 2012-07-11
"Intuitive" is just another word for "familiar". Remove the expectation of familiarity and replace it with a sense of adventure and change becomes fun.  Fun begets use.  Use begets familiarity.  Familiarity begets "intuitiveness".

The expectaion of familiarity is what puts many would-be Linux users off.

On the other hand, if something works for you why bother changing if you don't want adventure?
