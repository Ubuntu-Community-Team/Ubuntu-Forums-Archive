### Post by dan1701a on 2008-10-09
Well, I'll throw my two cents in regarding Ubuntu.

I discovered Ubuntu waaay back during the version 6 days. At the time, my laptop was running Windows XP SP1. I decided to repartition my meager 30gb hard drive and install Ubuntu on the new partition. I played with it for a little while, but didn't really understand how Gnome worked, where my control panel was, how to connect to the Internet (turns out I couldn't), etc. At some point, my MBR got corrupted and instead of trying to rebuild it (this was before I discovered all sorts of nifty rescue utilities), I reinstalled XP. No backups. Live and learn.

Since then I've tried a lot of different distros, nearly all on live CDs (except for PCLinuxOS, which I installed on an external USB HDD and was using it just fine until the HDD went belly-up - and I couldn't do wi-fi on PCLOS, either).

I ultimately purchased an Acer Aspire 4520 laptop with a 64-bit processor, and was itching to run it to its fullest, but it only came with Windows Vista Home Premium (the 32-bit version). When Ubuntu Hardy Heron came along, I was intrigued by the fact it was available in both 32-bit and 64-bit versions, and the Wubi installer (which, I assume, was a cutesy way of saying "Windows Ubuntu Installer"). I was actually able to install Ubuntu within Windows, and dual-boot, without worrying about a corrupted MBR or GRUB or repartitioning or anything like that. The cool factor shot way up for me.

This is actually my second attempt at 8.04. The first time, the wireless functionality was non-existent (probably due to some error on my part), and I had issues with the hard drive, so I uninstalled it for a short time. But it's back now, and I must say, I feel like it's on a par with Windows Vista - and uses a whole lot less space. I only allocated 10gb of my 50gb D:\ partition, and it doesn't act like it's getting full at all. It runs at least as fast as Vista, and maybe faster (because it's native 64-bit) - and that's even under the Wubi environment, which states that "performance may suffer". Not as far as I'm concerned.

The other neat thing (some may find it neat; others may have a different view) is that I was actually able to make my desktop about 95% Mac OSX-ish. I am using Cairo-Dock (far superior to Avant Window Navigator, in my opinion), gdesklets with the widget layer turned on (hit F9 and the widgets come up just like on the Mac OS Dashboard), and the Mac4Lin project's Emerald window decorations under Compiz-Fusion (which is flawless). The reason for this is that we're switching over to Macs at work, so I wanted to get a feel for it before I plunged in headlong. I know Ubuntu Linux is not exactly like Mac OSX (not even close, actually) but at least being able to practice closing windows on the left instead of the right is a plus.

This is a long-winded way of saying I like Ubuntu. I have used other distros that I liked, but with Ubuntu I feel like I can accomplish most (if not all) of my basic computing tasks as well as on Windows Vista. There are still some flaws - Flash doesn't work flawlessly, but then again it doesn't always like Firefox on Windows, either. Mostly, I'm pleased with Ubuntu, and the fact that wi-fi works (especially with WPA-PSK) is a huge benefit. Thanks to the Ubuntu developers for such a fine product!

### Post by L815 on 2008-10-11
Drivers on Linux and Windows is a double edged sword.

Most hardware for Windows have drivers developed from the company that makes that hardware. Sometimes it's harder to find, other times it's easy.
The problem with this is sometimes it takes time to find them, and install them all manually.

Linux on the other hand, has everything working out of the box (for the most part).
The problem with this is that the driver quality sometimes is not up to par as it would be on Windows; unless of course the hardware manufacturer also makes drivers for Linux.

One example is the Intel GM965 video driver. It works fine in Linux except for tearing in video/flash. In Windows, the driver works flawlessly.

In Linux, my wireless works out of the box and is super fast, compared to the driver for Windows which seems to be hindered a bit in speed for some reason.
