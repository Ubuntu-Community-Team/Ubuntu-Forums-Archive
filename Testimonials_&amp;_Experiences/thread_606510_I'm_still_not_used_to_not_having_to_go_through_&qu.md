### Post by SomeGuyDude on 2007-11-08
People have talked about how Linux does this over Windows/OSX or this function and that function.

I suddenly realized tonight that there's one HUGE thing that I was always doing in Windows that I haven't had to do here: maintenance.

I had the following little "task list" of things I had to do every so often just to make sure I was running right.

1) Check for updates. Windows only installed crucial updates itself, I had to manually check once in a while.

2) Run antivirus/antispyware. I had this automated, which either meant my computer was "in motion" perpetually (running those while I'm asleep) or at some point in my day the whole thing's slow as molasses.

3) De-fragging.

4) Registry cleaning.

5) Clear out unneeded files.

6) Pull crap out of my start-up menu that for some reason put itself back in there even after I get rid of it (I'm looking at you, iTunes and Ares), or from stuff I just installed but wasn't aware got lodged in there.

7) Probably a reboot just to "clear out" all the junk that clogged up the system over the past few weeks (this was rarer in Vista than XP)

And there's probably a few more. I had the following software to help with these tasks:

1) NOD32 for viruses and a firewall

2) Windows Defender for the spyware

3) Diskkeeper for the defragging

4) Ace Utilities for the various cleaning and ease of startup-menu editing

In the past few days, I've twice checked for updates because I am still refusing to believe that I can go for a week without having to perform "general maintenance" on my machine. There were no new updates, I don't have any defragging or registry editing tools. Obviously no malware apps. And if I want something to start up with the machine, I have to tell it to because nothing does it on its own.

I feel like the episode of The Simpsons where they move to the nice town and Marge doesn't know what to do since the house cleans itself. I'm so used to always having something I can do to clear things out a bit that not needing to is almost foreign to me.

On #6 of problems, has anyone else ever logged onto someone else's Windows computer and watched as roughly 70 programs all opened upon startup, leaving you to sit there for a half hour while HP imaging monitor, iTunes, Adobe, Norton, AIM, Java, and every other piece of software they own auto-loads just to sit in the task bar?

Hell, I remember having a "RAM Optimizer" with XP that would "free up" some RAM every 10 minutes or so because the OS itself had such a penchant for swallowing it slowly over the course of the day.

### Post by CarpKing on 2007-11-08
That's one thing I love about using Linux.  All the updates I need (for all programs) come through one central system (if I want a newer version of something, it isn't usually too hard to find).  Whenever I use Windows now I'm always surprised at the sheer amount of items in the tray, a mixture of security programs, programs tied to the drivers of various hardware, and programs begging to be manually updated.  All those little programs for "maintenance," the installation of which I used to think was an essential part of setting up a computer, just aren't necessary.  People complain about Linux lacking drivers, but the drivers it does have are just so much nicer than Windows.  On Windows when I want to use my graphics card, printer, etc., I have to put in a CD and install the drivers from it, along with the vendors own suite of "helpful" applications.  On Linux I just install the operating system, and most of the time the drivers are already there.
