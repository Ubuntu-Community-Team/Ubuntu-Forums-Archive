### Post by linuxlizard on 2008-05-05
A little background-
I've been a linux user since about 2001 or 2002 (don't remember exact date for sure). I started around the time of redhat 7 or 8.

Things have come a long way fast, and for the most part I'm happy with all the improvements. I went from redhat to fedora core, but became frustrated with Fedora core after a few releases because one release most things would work great, the next I would have lots of things that didn't- on the same computer. Then I found Ubuntu- I think around the time of breezy. Everything on my computer with ubuntu just worked. It was great. And it's been great, with the exception of my usb dvd burner which works most releases but on one (now 2) has not.

Until Hardy.

Hardy's got all kinds of little ugly pockmarks where things aren't working as they should. Things lock up, compiz or nvidia isn't working right on a card that's worked properly for years now, my dvd burner isn't working, dvd playback craps out after a few minutes to an hour and has jolts and jerks here and there.

The thing that drives me nuts about all this is it's the same hardware that I've used for years on ubuntu over several releases, and not had problems.

Why do developers have to freakin break stuff when they have a new release when that stuff worked absolutely wonderfully before?!? You have to break stuff to make progress? Are developers just crazy about having to tinker with stuff that works fine, is that their problem?

That part about linux really, really gets old after a while. I thought ubuntu was different from other distros in this regard, but hardy shows me they clearly are not. 

The worst of it is how I had built up ubuntu with our county public school system's itrts here, and how they were waiting for hardy along with me in anticipation of trying it out. 

Forget that. If their computers have as many ugly quirks as mine, they'll run not walk back to the arms of good old reliable windows xp.

I can't imagine the potential nightmare this could cause for a serious business or school system that had to have things working properly. It would cost a lot of time and money to troubleshoot crap like I've been dealing with on my computer, or they could have partly working systems like mine currently is.

What a joke.

Back when I started linux, the big thing was it's not as bloated as windows, and it's more reliable than windows. Well, Sad to say, ubuntu is becoming more bloated than windows requiring more system resources to run (I crack up every time I see people on here posting "add 512 more ram"- do they realize how terrible that would have been to a linux user 5 years ago?) and new releases are like tossing a coin as far as reliability gos. Maybe things will work, maybe they won't. Now *that's* not my definition of reliable.

There are some great things about hardy- finally bootup time and running speed is comparable to other linux distros- it doesn't feel bloated or heavy anymore on my hardware compared to the last few releases, though maybe it is as I recently read a post that says it requires 400-500 mb to run properly.

But stuff not working that worked great before isn't progress.

Please don't give another release like hardy.

### Post by keykero on 2008-05-05
I'm glad someone brought this up because this is what I see as almost a design flaw in repository-based distros.  On any other system, if you want the latest binary of a particular app, you can  usually download the lastest binary (eg. Windows .exe or Mac OSX .dmg) from the vendor.  If your repo-based distro was just released then you must wait for the next distribution release to get that latest binary OR compile it yourself, which doesn't always go well because typically there are significant changes or differences within the libraries themselves.  Thus you risk serious amounts of breakage.  It is not like this at all on Mac OSX or Windows -- sure, you do occasionally see OSX binaries that might *require* Tiger or Leopard, or require SP2, but typically the per-application update process is much simpler.
