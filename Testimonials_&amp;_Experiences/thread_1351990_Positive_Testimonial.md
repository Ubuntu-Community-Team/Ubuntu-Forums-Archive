### Post by sisyphus1978 on 2009-12-11
My experience of Ubuntu:

I got my first computer some 20 years ago (an amiga 500+). As a young adult, I begrudgingly moved my way onto the PC, working through 95, 98(se) and settling on XP as the operating system of choice. And it served me well over the years. I do remember purchasing a Linux book some 10 years ago, but I gave up because it was just too difficult. I am not a programmer but I do consider myself an able computer user. Able to find out why things are not working and hopefully rectify it.

Introductions over, thank XBMC. Thank you, XBMC. Necessity is the mother of invention and wifey wants a nice media centre so...Long story short, about 2 years ago I ditched the DIVX player and invested in an xbox. Up until 6 months ago, the xbox and the CRT were fine, but I felt it was time to update.

Now, I'm not saying it was easy. I purchased a dedicated media PC (asrock 330) and took to tackling Linux again. What with the live CDs and the extensive forum/wiki help, getting a very impressive media centre up and running was not too difficult. Yes it involved a great deal of research and thinking (taking into account the different technologies involved, this is unavoidable) but the current state of the project is very good (and certainly my partner doesn't notice anything other than the "ooooo that's pretty" and then she's away 
watching Desperate Housewives (shudder).

But the HTPC is not the only computer in the house. Oh no. With the media centre sorted, my next plan was to develop a better system for backup. Case in point: over the last few years I must have reinstalled windows tens of times (over 50). What with girlfriends hosing their systems and OS updates, I decided it was time to learn about PXE, Raid and minimal Ubuntu. Before starting the project the most I had done with Linux was set up XBMC. With a little reading around and testing I built my PXE server from an old PC and added in 2 120GB HDD that were just sitting around. I'm not going to lie and say that it didn't take me some time to get it working. It did. Even when it was working, a small error forced me to rebuild the thing from scratch (my opinion is, that it is better to understand why it didn't work, than just be happy as it is). Finally then I have a PXE clonezilla server which can back up any of the other computers on my home LAN, directly over the LAN. This is brilliant not only because I don't really like physical drives (and thus don't have any CDRW etc in the machines) but because I can keep a couple of current working OS backups saved and in the event of a major breakdown, can return to a working system in minutes. More than any other thing that I have ever done, THIS has saved me hours of my life. Thanks be to clonezilla, DRBL and Linux.

With my working clonezilla server and my working media centre, I decided to remove the last bastion of the old M$ regime. My main desktop computer holds the media (music, movies, documents) and I was kind of wary about removing XP from it. I have tried to swap OSs over the last couple of years (XP to Vista, back to XP, tried out 7, back to XP) but it was always things like Remote Desktop and newsbin pro which kept me hooked on M$. Finally a self-inflicted virus (damned enticing exe) sent me to wiping XP once and for all, and since Karmic had just been released, I decided to install the latest greatest offering on my desktop and learn to deal with it. And learn I have. Again, I am not going to say that there haven't been problems. I completely changed the partition types on my HDD (3 TB over 4 HDD) to GPT and found that after GRUB2 changes it often wouldn't load again. But we learn and no data was lost (thank god). I learnt about file-systems, apt-get, hellanzb, w3m and nanorc (to name but a few). And today, now, I am very happy with the config and running of my Karmic desktop.

Okay, nearly there. Last was my trusty eeepc 701. Hark, I hear you cry, but the eeepc already comes with a version of Linux. Yeah well that was wiped ages ago, and replaced with that old friend, XP. Seeing as I was rapidly turning the house into a Linux household, I thought that the best thing to do was test a variety of distributions and see how they ran. Funnily enough, it was the UNR (9.10) which not only worked the best (but required the least tinkering). Fine with me, since all my computers are running a version of Ubuntu, it should make it easier for me to manage and administer them. And so it is. 

I'm not finished tinkering (although I have ssh set up nicely and am currently writing this in nano on my HDTV from my wireless keyboard in an ssh session on my computer in my study upstairs. XBMC is nearing perfection (I think I have to learn about modelines, resolutions and xorg) but I think that's for 2010.

My testimonial in a nutshell: stay calm and try. Back up important things and you'll be okay. And since it works great for some people, perhaps you should go back and try something else.
