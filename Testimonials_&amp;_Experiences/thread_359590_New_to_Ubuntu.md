### Post by Gitfinger on 2007-02-12
This weekend I installed Ubuntu 6.10 as I'd had about as much of Windows XP as I can stand. Don't get me wrong, XP was okay. It was stable (it has never bluescreened) and did its job fairly well. The problem is that first it's boring, hopelessly propriatary, technologically out of date and over all just not suitable for my purposes.

I've ran Linux before, I'd wrestled with Redhat 6, 7.3 and 8 before giving up. Redhat just required too many fiddles, too much effort to accomplish even basic things. Things like Flash and Java in web sites just became a fight to get Mozilla to use it. Installing extra fonts was hard. All these silly little things eventually became overwhelming, I expect to be able to just browse a web site, not spend two hours fighting at the terminal to get it all set up correctly. Writing to CDs was agony. The sad thing is that on Redhat 8 they were really close to success but by then I'd had enough. My new PC had Windows XP on it, did most ly what it said on the tin and that was that.

More recently I started to think more closely about computing in general. Firstly with Windows Vista I realised that Microsoft is a company in decline. They have took 7 years to write the OS which means its successor will take more than a decade. This is just not a realistic proposition. I also started to think about how cosmetic changes to the OS were not worth the pain of upgrading, the core of how Windows works just seems so centrally outdated.

I also started to think about vendor lock-in. Of all things it was Apple iTunes that made me realise that propriatary, locked in formats were just bad. I've bought 10-20 tracks on iTunes and I decided it was time to jump ship before I'd spent a lot of money on music that couldn't be moved to anything but iTunes. The only way forward was to trust an operating system and way of thinking that said everything should be published as an open standard and was interoperable. Next the upgrade to Office 2007 was another pointless exercise that has no purpose other than filling the coffers of Microsoft's cash-cow. OpenOffice 2.1 does everything I want from an Office package. I do a lot of LaTeX documents and Linux supports this out of the box so if I need really pretty documents I'll use that.

I'll also started to think about what is my platform these days? Most of it is through Firefox which is just the same on Linux as it is on Windows. Same with Thunderbird.

So it seemed from all the positive feedback Ubuntu was the obvious choice. A simple download and burning to CD gave me a complete OS image. The only thing I needed to replace was my network card which cost me the princely some of £6.99 for a Linux compatible one. Everything else was delected automatically. The whole Ubuntu experience is unbelievably good and their is so much to reccomend it. Not least the ability to escape from the mediocrity of Microsoft and Apple.
