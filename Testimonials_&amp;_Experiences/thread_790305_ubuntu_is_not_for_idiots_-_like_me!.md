### Post by metiosarius on 2008-05-11
> **stefanm said:**
> There's absolutely no point telling me to go into terminal and do anything there, I'm terrified. I don't know what any of the technical terms I have come across mean like deb, wine, jack, edgy, gdm, grub, ext14, compiz, snap-to-pop, gui-tool and so on. 

First, dont fear the terminal :) It can be very useful, and if someone on the forum is giving you step by step instructions, it's really difficult to actually mess anything up.

As far as terminology, it's an 'acquired' thing - if you need help with it, just ask :) Some of what you listed i've outlined below:

**deb** - Installation packages for Ubuntu. (Similar to "setup.exe's" in Windows.)
**wine** - Windows Emulator - It allows you to run *some* windows software on Linux.
**jack** - Just the name for the sound system.
**edgy** - The 'codename' for Ubuntu Version 6.10
**gdm** - "Gnome Display Manager" - This controls everything you see on the screen.
**Grub** - "grand unified boot loader" - This is the program that boots the operating system when you first turn on your computer.
**compiz** - A program that gives linux all kinds of cool desktop effects.
**GUI** - "Graphic User Interface" - this is pretty much anything on your screen that isn't the old "command prompt" style.


Always remember that Google, and these forums, are your friend :) I'm sure that most of your problems can be solved if you are willing to follow instructions - just make sure that you ask for detailed step by step instructions :)

Even if you're going back to windows, at least keep checking back here for helpful posts - perhaps someone knows of a program that will meet your audio needs.

### Post by nikkiana on 2008-05-13
First of all, you're NOT an idiot. With some patience and willingness to learn, you CAN make this transition if you really put your mind to it... and there very well may be support out there for you in real life if you know how to look for it. 

That said, I want to apologize. One of the things that frusterates me a great deal about many Ubuntu enthusaists is how excited they'll get about how reliable and easy to use things are, all the while forgetting that the friend that they're telling this to doesn't quite have the same level of computer know-how or same interest level in how things work. Unfortunately, I've seen what's happened to you many many times... Excited friend tells you how stable, secure, easy to use, etc. Ubuntu is and then hands you a disk or gives you the URL of where to download it, or maybe even is kind enough to do a base install for you... and then runs for the hills before everything you need to do what you do is set up. It wasn't right or fair of your friend to tell you to install Ubuntu and then not help support you through the transition. 

The reality of things is this... Ubuntu Linux is different than Windows, and I think it's unrealistic to expect that anybody is automatically going to know what to do the first time they sit down in front of it. That doesn't make you an idiot, it just makes you a little bit confused... and there's no shame in being confused... It's okay, you're new to it and there's a lot of confusing new terms and it's hard to know what ones are relevant to you and what ones aren't. (Here's a secret, I've been using Ubuntu for two years now and I don't know what half of the terms you listed not knowing about are ;)) 

Since things that are new are often confusing and take time to learn, my suggestion would be to revert anything business related back to Windows for the time being... and then tackle learning Ubuntu in your free time at home. Over time, you can build up your confidance and your knowledge... and you can start exploring what solutions are available for the things you do in your business and start testing them out before making a hardcore commitment to things. (I know what you're trying to do is possible... It's just that personally I don't know enough to be able to advise the best course of action). 

A great way of learning is to make an effort to meet other Linux users in your area. I'd check out the Ubuntu LoCo group list: [https://wiki.ubuntu.com/LoCoTeamList](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/LoCoTeamList) to see if there is a LoCo group in your area. You could also, try posting on your area's LoCo forum: [http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=183](http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=183) to see if there might me anyone who lives near you that would be willing to help you in real life. Alternatively, checking out a LUG (Linux Users Group) which are typically a little more distribution generalized might help you out too, you can find a list here: [http://www.linux.org/groups/](http://www.linux.org/groups/).  I'd also try convincing that programmer friend of yours to help you, too.  

Good luck!

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-05-14
You're not an idiot. All your computer experience has been with Windows, so when you switched to something different you had to relearn everything.

Firstly, your Audacity error message is probably due to another program using the sound input or output. Firefox, Pidgin; these could be using the sound output. Go into System > Preferences > Sound and turn off "Software Sound Mixing" before starting Audacity. This will almost certainly fix that problem.

To play Flash in your browser, you need the package "flashplugin-nonfree". Go into Synaptic Package Manager and install it. You might need to enable an extra repository (a server that holds software for you to download). You can do this by going to System > Administration > Software Sources and checking all the boxes in the first tab there. After this is done, flashplugin-nonfree can be installed.

I know it's a cliched, impolite expression; but "Google is your friend". You wouldn't believe the number of problems that are actually easy to solve once you've done a search on Google for the exact error message or situation.

And finally, do you know how to minimise windows? A couple of months ago, I taught a workmate how to minimise a window instead of just closind it. I installed Ubuntu for her and set it up with all the programs she wants; and she's not having any problems. So Linux definitely does work for semi-computer-literate people, you're just going down the "install and setup yourself" route.
