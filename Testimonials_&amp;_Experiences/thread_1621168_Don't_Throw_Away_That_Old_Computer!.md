### Post by aa4bb on 2010-11-13
My daughter was moving recently and I was helping her decide what should go, what should stay. She pointed to her husband's old, old laptop, a Compaq 1200 circa Y2K or earlier. "He doesn't want it any more. Should we just put it in the electronics recycle?", she asked.

I decided to bring it home with me and see what I could do with Linux. There are lots of old computers that still work and could be some kid's first computer, if they were able to run a modern OS and applications. I booted it, and sure enough, up came Win98, so the hardware seemed fine.  

I looked at the system list. AMD K6 processor, 500 MHz, 90 MB memory, 6 GB hard drive, USB port but no Ethernet. Fortunately, I had a USB  Ethernet adapter that I could try.

Then I went to see which distros could work within those constraints. Regular Ubuntu? Not even close. I had used Xubuntu with an old desktop HP and did pretty well. However, recent reviews were not encouraging for using it on small systems. 

I settled on Damn Small Linux and Slitaz for starters. Both were straightforward enough and did well for what they did. One big hurdle was cleared. The USB Ethernet adapter worked fine. However, the applications were pretty lean and I hoped it might be possible to do better.

A bit more study and every place I looked kept sending me to Lubuntu. I discovered I'd need a bit more memory. I checked the user manual for the 1200 and found it could take a memory upgrade. A search on eBay quickly found a purveyor of compatible memory. Two days later I was up to 180 MB and ready to give Lubuntu a shot.

I had a couple of false starts. I downloaded Lubuntu 10.10, then saw in the fine print that it did not support the AMD K6. For that, I needed 10.04. Another several hour download, I burned the .iso to CD and did a self-test on the LiveCD. "One file has an error". I burned a second disk at slower speed. Same message from the self-test. (I see from my research that others have had this problem, so it's probably just a bad file or MD5, not a bad disk)

In the meantime, more fine print got my attention. It was recommended to use mini.iso instead of the LiveCD with as little memory as my system had. Mini.iso comes in at 12 MB, then downloads the various modules as it needs them during the install. More time spent waiting for download...

Things started looking up after that. The system finished installing, and booted from the hard drive. I began to familiarize myself with the Chromium web browser and other light-weight applications that come with Lubuntu. Not bad! Certainly quite usable for someone who didn't need all the bells and whistles of Firefox, Open Office and so on.

How about WiFi, almost essential these days? As luck would have it, I had bought a PCMCIA wireless G card that I was going to use in an old laptop until it bit the dust. The card was just lying around, gathering dust. Maybe that sucker would get automagically recognized and the right software installed to make it work. 

I had been through the wireless wild goose chase several times with different ones of my modern laptops - all using Broadcom that required the proprietary drivers. I was prepared for jumping through extra hoops, so went to Hardware Drivers to have it search for and activate the Broadcom driver. Even so, I couldn't find the Network Manager icon anywhere. Finally, I right-clicked on the panel, found and installed the network icon. I was then able to get to the wireless tab and enter the password for my router. Hooray, my PCMCIA wireless card worked!

For my two days of searching and installing, I now have a decent little laptop with terrific applications that will surely find its niche somewhere. Furthermore, I'm poised to help others who have old computers that otherwise would find their way into scrap recycling that can now be repurposed to a better outcome.

### Post by Pantera116 on 2010-12-19
A few months ago i bought off ebay a sony vaio laptop (p4 2400 1gb ram 60gb hd 16" screen).It is a bit of a desktop replacement rather than a portable laptop.

Having bought the vaio i had to decide what to do with my Toshiba portege 7200 laptop (P3 600 192 mb ram 20gb 13"screen). I had had Xubuntu on this and had added the LXDE desktop to make it more responsive. I had a vodaphone 3 g mobile broadband stick working with this machine.

I decided to install Lubuntu 10.04 after having first bought a 8gb ssd (Kingfast brand MLC type cost £35) i also upgraded the ram to 320mb. This laptop would be used only for surfing via 3 g and Wifi (Edimax ew7100g PCMCIA ralink based card works perfectly out the box).

I should mention that there are a few tricks to reduce the number of writes to the ssd, you should be able to google this. The install was done with the laptop on its dock which has dvd and Ethernet. No particular issues except the default 800 by 600 graphics on the 1024 by 768 panel. An edit of the Xorg.conf to provide refresh rates cures this,
Lubuntu by default is playing it safe. I find the desktop very clean and modern and the icon artwork is nicely done. Application shortcuts are easy to put on the desktop and suspend works just by closing up the laptop lid.

Using this laptop to surf at work at lunch time i am connected to the net  via 3g about 2 minutes after pressing the power button. Google chrome is a little different from firefox but launches nice and fast unlike firefox. The combination of the improved hardware and Lubuntu works really well. This laptop dates from 1999 and is still a useful PC.

I decided to check for updates via synaptic (via wifi at home) the other day. There was about 135mb to download new kernels etc. After it was done i instantly noticed a speed improvement during boot up. I am now connected to the net via 3g in 90 seconds. Login screen is up after 35 seconds and desktop fully up in under a minute. This is very impressive given the hardware, what is also impressive is the improvements with the latest updates.

All in all Lubuntu is a very good distro for my application on this hardware.
