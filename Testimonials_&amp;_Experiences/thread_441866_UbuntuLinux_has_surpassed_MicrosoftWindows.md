### Post by WhiteHorse on 2007-05-13
Wow, thanks to all the replies I am editing this post:
First of all I am moving this to the top-
Linux and open source is a body of work which shows what people can do collectively when they put their minds to it. It represents an overthrow of oppressive corporations and slave-driving software companies that seek to use computers to entrap people into a cycle of continual payment. By using Linux you're helping to bring down their stocks, and hopefully, one day, those of us that held on to the dream will see it take on a life of it's own.

As an expert on Ubuntu/Linux and Micro$oft/Windows, I would like to say plainly that Linux has surpassed Micro$oft on all fronts except OEM rollouts. 

First, let me qualify what I'm saying by informing the reader that I have the equivalent of a PhD in Computer Systems Engineering. You can check out my qualifications in detail at [My Resume Site]("http://www.digitalnetworking.biz/quals.html"). I have set up almost every system imaginable including freeBSD, Linux, OS 9, OSX, All variants of windows and DOS, and Solaris. I have personally used all of these for my home and business, as well as for other companies from small businesses to fortune 500 companies. I currently use Linux and freeBSD exclusively for all my business and personal needs(except that one game, f#ckers).

With the advent of sourceforge, linux development has accelerated, and propelled open source at least 20 years ahead of any proprietary system. In th last 6 months alone, at least 15 major enterprise ready systems have come online through open source initiatives. Not the least of which is Ubuntu.

I was absolutely shocked and appalled when Ubuntu 7.04 worked... out of the box... for 99.99% of my hardware. In fact, I didn't have to download any drivers!! No post-install configuration!! It's unbelievable. What will blow you away even more is that I run a 64bit laptop with USB mouse, DVD drive, Wireless, Bluetooth+MotorolaRAZRV3, Firewire, Compact Flash, etc, etc. Combined with Automatix and a little Flash workaround, I now have the equivalent of a $7,000 Microsoft Development laptop. Only about $4000 of that is what you'd pay for the developers suite I have plus GIMP(Photoshop),  Openoffice(M$Office),  MySQL(SQL Server), Apache(IIS), DVD/CD Ripper/Editor/Burner(Roxio or comparable), GnuCash(Quicken/M$Money), Frontpage(Nvu) and about 1000 other useful programs. The other $3000 is the what you'd pay for hardware to run all that bloatware from Microsoft. So my $7,000 equivalent cost me $1,200. 

Although I do admit it should be $1,300 because I need to upgrade my ram to 1GB from 512MB because Eclipse is a pig(it compiles in 15+language), I must admit, this it a good deal. Also add $.10 for the CD I had to use to burn the ISO.

So most people ask me how long it took me to learn all this. What is the amount of time you invested as opposed to Micr$oft? To tell the truth, I've actually spent less time on Linux than I have on Microsoft. No seriously. I just completely set up a laptop in under 3 hours. Including installing drivers and running updates and restoring previous config. I could do it faster if I had to repeat it. I could do it in 10min if I chose to automate for rollout of an enterprise with PXE boot.

I know this doesn't answer the question... The real thing you want to know is how much time I invested to become as proficient in Linux as I am in Windows. Well the truth is it took me about a year, but with the help of Ubuntu and the new 7.04, I could see someone doing it in 3 months. I'm talking the full conversion to where you never use any Microsoft products again. There is a learning curve to go from Microsoft apps to Linux apps but guess what? The information you learn in Linux carries to the next version... In Microsoft, you have to learn it all over. So the real truth is the learning curve for linux is at least a factor of 10 less than Micro$oft. Over an entire lifetime you will spend 10% of the time you spent on Micro$oft for learning.  

Ubuntu as a desktop OS is the best available. It has more support than Micro$oft, it's more stable, and it requires less hardware. In the long run, switching to Linux will save you a lot of hassles and Ubuntu is the easiest route for a new person. 

Edit: After dabbling in Vista I've noticed not one happy user and every computer person I know thinks it is a poor decision to use Vista. I have an inside view because I worked for a DRM(Digital Rights management) company. Vista requires more computing power because it is reporting your keystrokes to Micro$oft, checking all your files for licenses, and reporting to the RIAA(Recording Indistry A$$holes of America) if you have any copyrighted material. It will actually prevent you from using illegal software, music, and viewing unauthorized content on the internet.  How ya like them apples?
Oh, additionally, your printers won't work, your blackberry won't work, and all the expensive devices you bought with software will have compatibility problems.

[SIZE="2"]**Why Ubuntu is better than Vista and XP**[/SIZE]

[INDENT]Ubuntu requires less hardware in terms of disk space, RAM, and CPU; A full install takes 6GB disk space(10GB for XP and 15+ for Vista), 512MB RAM for java developers/256MB for power-users, 128MB for users, and even less for office computers(4/2GB for vista and 1GB/256MB for XP) ; the CPU rarely hits 100% on a 1GHz processor unless you compile firefox from source(try 3GHz for Vista and 2GHz for XP).

If you plan to upgrade to Vista, you will hit a 2GB RAM limit on your old motherboard and need to replace it to not have a slow machine(as well as buy 4GB RAM). Instead of upgrading your hardware, just upgrade your operating system to Linux. It's also been shown that games run 25% slower on Vista than XP(which is about the same performance hit if you run your game in an emulator on Linux).

Compatibility: Linux is more compatible with everything. It runs on every processor, recognizes every type of hardware, and uses it more effectively than other operating systems. Many problems in the past were caused by hardware vendors who didn't release Linux drivers for their products. They were too ashamed to admit that their script-kiddie-India-computer-whores, fresh out of tech college, didn't know how to make Linux drivers(aka kernel modules). So guess what? The unemployed experts made the drivers anyway and gave them away for free.[/INDENT]

*Multimedia:* I watch wmv9 files, streaming media, and can encode/decode any format known to man.

*Finance:* Gnucash runs my business and downloads my bank statement and autopopulates the register. Double-entry accounting? hehe My accountant switched to Linux so she could run Gnucash.

*Games:* Linux has better games and more of them. Although they're not all free, and not all games have Linux versions, this has changed dramatically in the last 6 months. Don't blow out your windows partition just yet though. I do have a sneaky suspicion that the next big game will be cross-platform and run better on Linux. Check out World-Forge for a preview. In the mean-time, just nag the developers of your favorite game until they make a linux version.

*Office:*Openoffice is far superior to Micro$oft Ofiice in many ways. First of all it can open all MSOffice documents. Second, it interfaces to every data source on the planet. Third, it does way more. Fourth, it's easier to learn. Fifth, it's saved files are smaller.

*Internet:*Open source software on Linux is superior to anything else. Firefox for web browsing, Gyach for Video chat to your lover on Yahoo, GAIM/Penguin? for all other IMs, too many to list. It's unbelievable what you can do on the internet with open source software. Heck, I even interface my computer to my asterisk PBX with Ekiga. ### Post by TrashmanL on 2008-07-14
Well, I feel like adding my two cents. I agree with most of the original post, though I think there are a couple small exaggerations. I don't care though, so I'm not commenting on them.

Though I can't say I haven't had a trouble free installation, I have had no more trouble than with Windoze. When XP first came out I heard about all the bugs, and I was building my own system at the time. I knew ME was a total POS from friends whose computers were crippled by it, so I chose 98SE as my OS at the time (2000, though I thought was the best, was too expensive).

It wasn't terrible. It had its bugs. In retrospect, I might have taken XP, because it's not a bad OS after SP2 came out and other bug fixes. It started implement DRM, that I didn't like, but as it became clear the DRM was buggy, Microsoft offered pretty easy ways around it.

As 98SE became outdated, I started looking into dual-booting. I started with RedHat 7.something and though I initially liked it, I found it almost as bad a resource hog as Windoze. After RedHat dropped their end user support (before Fedora came out) I abandoned it and attempted to use Slackware. Despite being a pretty high level computer user (at least in the opinion of my friends and family), I found Slackware a bit beyond my expertise. I could get basic functionality, but I was having all kinds of trouble compiling sources for it. I was attempting to upgrade my gnome version at the time, and having troubles, and I downloaded the Gnome 2.14 LiveCD which featured Dapper Drake. I loved it. It was the only Linux I had found that could actually use my Tuner card. I couldn't get it to install right, though, then I downloaded a Feisty Install CD and installed it. It's been great since. I have a few small issues, but only annoyances really. When I decided I needed a new laptop, I had a hard time finding non-Vista laptops, and even the XP laptops were really expensive. I knew from some friends experiences that some laptops are crippled by Vista, and you can't downgrade them to XP, because there aren't even XP drivers! So I went to linux Certified and got myself a Linux Laptop. They goofed up, I asked for Gutsy and they gave me Hardy, but it's been working great despite the complaints I've heard. There's still a few 64-bit bugs to be worked out (but then again, all 64 bit OSes have been having problems). The Wi-Fi worked right out of the box, I bought a $5 bluetooth adapter that worked as soon as I plugged it in. I installed Kubuntu, and though took a little while, I got it working good. I couldn't get Amarok working right on my Fiesty install, but now that I have it working on my laptop, I love Amarok, and finally have a music player I like more than Winamp 2.9. 

Recently, my Win98 install on my desktop machine got fried by something and I just might wipe it out and go all the way with Ubuntu. I'll probably use my WIN partition to install Gutsy or Hardy to, then remove my Feisty install when I have all the packages I need.

Also, I think Ubuntu's Add/Remove programs is great, far superior to Windoze. Anything you want is just a few clicks away. And so far, I haven't had much trouble running my Windoze games in Wine, though I haven't tried them all yet. I may even start recommending it to my newbie friends. It is definitely superior to Vista in every way, shape and form. The final straw that convinced me to switch, by the way, is Microsoft's push to phase out XP faster than previous OSes and push this POS called Vista on everyone. I hope many more switch because of it.
