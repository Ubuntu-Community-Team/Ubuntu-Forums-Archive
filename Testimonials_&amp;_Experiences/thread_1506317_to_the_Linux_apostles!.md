Well, I don't even use Ubuntu, and the distribution of linux I use is far from "ready out of the box" (you have to compile it from source code).  So I don't expect it to "just work".  In fact, I don't _want_ it to "just work".  I want it to do exactly what I told it -- period, the end.

But it's too bad that people are broadcasting the message that Ubuntu is a 100% trouble-free out-of-the-box experience, because I think that's not true for all users.  Many people have hardware that's not handled properly or have some other unique requirement.  Also, Ubuntu (and Desktop Linux in general) has been evolving so rapidly that there are many "growing pains".  (Kind of like Microsoft had transitioning from XP to what is now available, via Longhorn, er. Vista (how many years of cluster-****ing and pain?).

Many people install Ubuntu and it "just works".  Other have problems with this or that (e.g. wireless drivers, which are undergoing a complete re-architecting within the Linux kernel at the moment, or video drivers -- same issue, or their touch-pad, etc.).  Or many not everything works right in 64-bit (same problem in Windows-land, though).  Still more, being new to Linux, break their system in short order doing something stupid like trying to "install software" they downloaded off the internet, and then don't know how to fix it.

As a developer, maybe you'd enjoy a more personal experience that doesn't promise to "just work" without your help.  Maybe you should try Arch or Gentoo or Slackware.
