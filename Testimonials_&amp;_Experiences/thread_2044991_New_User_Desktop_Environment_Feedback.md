### Post by clockworkangel on 2012-08-20
[FONT=Arial]Hello everyone!

I'm not sure if this is the right subforum for such a post.  Please forgive me if it isn't.  I just wanted to drop by and provide feedback on my Desktop Environment experience as a brand new Ubuntu user.

To begin with, I am not entirely new to Linux in general.  My first experience with Linux began roughly 10 years ago, when as an intern for a software company I was given a PC with RedHat on it.  I believe it was the old KDE DE that I was using.  I enjoyed it from the start.  A few years later, and I installed Fedora as a dual boot with Windows on my home PC.  On the recommendation of a friend, I decided to try out Gnome 2, and I fell in love.  If it hadn't been for the video games and my inexperience with getting things to work with Wine, I might have wiped Windows entirely.    Eventually, however, I grew tired of maintaining two operating systems.  I had to choose, and I made the agonizing decision to stick with Windows.

Fast forward a few years later, and I've decided I've been without Linux for far too long.  This time, I thought I'd try Ubuntu, as Fedora seems to use its users as guinea pigs, and I wanted something more stable and hopefully more user friendly.  I am a software engineer, and I do like to tinker, but most times when I'm at home I'm too tired to tinker and just want to get things done without any fuss.  I was hoping Ubuntu would do the trick.

I was very happy to discover I could use Wubi to try it out.  It was a very no-fuss installation--the easiest OS installation I've ever done in my entire life.  Kudos!

I rebooted into Ubuntu, and lo and behold, the new Unity DE greets me.  I'll give the Ubuntu developers kudos for this much:  I could find my way around easily enough.  Still, I was most perplexed that I couldn't make desktop shortcuts, or that normal desktop shortcuts for, say, the trash bin were missing.  I was delighted that I could search so well with the HUD.  However, it wasn't easily apparent that I could simply browse by category for things.  What if I just wanted to look around to see what was installed?  I felt I had to know what was there in the first place.  My other gripe with Unity was that I couldn't seem to move the dock (the one on the left with the big application icons) to where I wanted it.  I've been trained like a monkey since my youth to right click for a context menu.  None appeared on the dock when I tried.  Dragging the panel with the mouse didn't work, either.  Finally, I had trouble at first finding the menu bars of windows that were maximized.  It took me a while to figure out that it was in the top panel.  I found that to be rather jarring.

Due to Unity's shortcomings, I decided to try out Gnome 3.  I've never been more confused in my life as to how to use something.  I'm having a hard time deciding which DE is worse--Gnome 3 or Unity.  It was really annoying to accidentally move my mouse too far to the upper left and have the workspaces pop up.  I did like that I could sort of browse for applications via the Application menu.  Still, the icons were rather cluttered rather than having a neater, more cleanly grouped layout.  The missing dock on the left was also annoying.  This is where Unity has 1 up on Gnome 3.  Still, after a few tries, I finally figured out how to use Gnome 3, as well as how to install extensions to customize it.  What was sad is that I had to actually go find the extensions.  Having the most popular included already would have been a boon to new users who just want to get started.

I did try Gnome Classic.  It definitley doesn't hold a candle to Gnome 2.  I decided sticking with it would only bring disappointment, since an Internet search revealed that Gnome developers aren't likely to maintain it once they figure out a fallback (non-video card acceleration) for the full Gnome 3.

I then tried Kubuntu, Xubuntu, Xfce, and even Cinnamon.  Xfce/Xubuntu was the easiest to return to.  Still, I was disappointed in it.  Why can't I have something that can look familiar and yet have the application search capability of the new DEs?  Yes, Xfce has an app search, but it's not nearly as sophisticated.  It felt like taking a step backward, rather than making more progress on ease of use.  With Xfce, I was settling for less.

Finally, nearly all these interfaces (Xfce being the largest exception) suffered from lack of accessibility to those who don't have the best vision.  On a high resolution desktop, it's getting harder and harder to see these teeny tiny panels with these teeny tiny icons and fonts.  Sure, you can increase font size, but that doesn't help with these teeny tiny icons and slim panels.  Gnome Classic and Cinnamon were the worst offenders.  I'll give Unity and Gnome 3 a hat tip for at least having big icons.  Granted, not everyone wants those, either.  Customizability in this department is a must, so that users can get what is most comfortable for their eyes.

In the end, I just felt frustrated.  I found myself spending too much time tinkering to get a familiar interface that suited my needs.  If this is how just using the interface is going to be, I can't imagine the hell I'll go through getting my iPod and video games working.  Not to mention all the other little things I haven't thought of yet.

My thoughts, as a new user, are to give people some familiar layouts to choose from upon installation.  I'm sure many folks have tweaked their Gnome 3/Unity desktops into all sorts of exciting arrangements.  Why not make a few of those available as layout themes, so that users can get something familiar up and running quickly?  For example, if you want your desktop to look like a Mac, here's a Mac layout.  And so on.  I could see an entire database of these layout themes where users share their configurations becoming quite popular.  Best of all, it's user friendly.

Anyway, there's my feedback.  I might try Linux again in a few years.  I'm just burnt out already.  Hopefully these newer DEs will mature in a few years to be more configurable out of the box, as well as more user friendly up front.  Until then, it's back to Windows 7.[/FONT]

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2012-08-25
Once you've been working with Unity for a while, and you know where things are, you will absolutely love it and won't like Windows at all.

The design decisions that have been made with Unity are there for a purpose (well, except immovable Launcher; but it makes sense for it to be on one side, and it really doesn't take much time at all to get used to it there).

I'd gently suggest to stop trying to change the user interface to match what you're familiar with; and instead try and work to the user interface's strengths.

Followup to my original post.

Okay, so I decided to take time off from Ubuntu, so I could come back to it with my expectations reset, and ready to try something new.

I've given GNOME 3 a second try.  I must say, it's beginning to grow on me.  I decided to keep most of the vanilla settings, with the major exception being the extension to keep the dash visible at all times (as it is in Unity).

I'm beginning to see the advantages of not having my desktop cluttered with icons.  If I don't use my desktop for storage, then I never have to minimize my windows to find a file or application launcher.  It's kind of nice.  Perhaps my family won't complain about what a mess my desktop looks like whenever they're borrowing my PC.  (Now they're complaining the opposite:  Where's "My Computer"?  Where's the "Recycle Bin"?  Muhahaha!  Serves them right!)

I feel GNOME 3 could still use a few enhancements to be more user friendly.  For example, a GUI-based means for registering a custom application so I can add it to the Applications search, as well as to the dash.  Some of the extensions at gnome.org should come stock vanilla with GNOME 3, with options to enable/disable as one pleases from an easily-accessible menu.

I'll probably give Unity another test drive, but so far I'm finding GNOME 3 meets my needs.  I do feel both could be less resource hogs.  I have 12GB memory on my laptop, with an i7 core and nVidia card with 2GB.  (ASUS Republic of Gamers laptop, only roughly 6 months old).  I don't feel either of these DEs should be having the delays I'm experiencing with my laptop.  Hopefully, these issues will become optimized over time.

I was pleased to get Lord of the Rings Online working under Wine with little fuss.  That's really the only video game I play (and barely), and I doubt I'll ever pick up another game.

That leaves only getting Banshee to stop freezing whenever I insert my iPod nano G5.  But that's an issue for another thread.  (Yes, I may have to use Rhythmbox.  At least it sees my iPod.  Still need to perform a sync test.)

I think I might make a full switch from Windows, once I can confirm that everything on my must-have list will work on Ubuntu.

Thanks, everyone!
