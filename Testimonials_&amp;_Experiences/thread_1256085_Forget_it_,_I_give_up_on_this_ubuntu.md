Wow. It took me two and a half hours to install Ubuntu from scratch and configure it with all the codecs (slow internet connection, that's why it took so long). You, ma'am, must be a perfectionist for screen settings.

> that was reason i tried ubuntu...tried the gimp (THE RAT as i now call it) and it cant compare even with photoshop

Shock! Horror! Professional-level program with 18 years of development behind it, that has hundreds of fully-paid developers and sells for a thousand dollars, has more features than a young open-source program with no paid developers and a handful of permanent developers! Who'd have thought it?

> ....couldnt get my tablet working...my bf even found an "explanation" on the www about how to get a tablet working but again it requires typing a bunch of bs into some text file...too damn confusing....sorry im not a computer person i am a tool person.....

I'm assuming that everything else on your new operating system has worked out well out-of-the-box, because you haven't mentioned anything else being wrong. So why didn't you just copy and paste the stuff into the text file? Takes about ten seconds. You don't need to understand it, just paste it in.

You see, hardware manufacturers rarely release driver software or specifications to Linux developers. Although the developers made sure that your sound, networking, keyboard, mouse, optical drives, SATA, IDE etc work out-of-the-box, some specialist devices don't have built-in support until the developers can reverse-engineer it. This takes time, and sometimes requires some slightly odd things like pasting commands into the terminal, running a script, or pasting stuff into a text file.

The good news is, it's generally not hard, and you generally don't have to do it for more than one or two releases or until support for your device is rolled into the Linux kernel.

There is a lot of excellent software on Linux. The Gimp is excellent considering that it's free software. Why not take the opportunity to try out some more free software? (btw you didn't need to try Ubuntu to try out The Gimp; it's available for Windows)

### Post by Methuselah on 2009-09-02
I have a wacom tablet working with Ubuntu and yes, I had to configure xorg.conf.
That wasn't a big deal to me but might come as a huge culture shock to others.

Most peripheral makers try to make their devices easy to install on windows.
However, many devices are made to work on linux with minimal manufacturer support so the installation/configuration process might not always be 'point and click'.

So I think it's still true that if you have 'unusual' needs you'd best be somewhat computer savvy, willing to learn, know a techie, or stick to windows or MacOS.

I find Ubuntu flexible enough to do anything I need but the road there might not be one those with technophobia will embark on.
However, people with simple needs and common hardware will often discover that Ubuntu does all they need right out of the box.
