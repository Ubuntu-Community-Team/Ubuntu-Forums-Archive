### Post by redrider on 2009-01-21
Linux of any flavour is not trouble-free, that's guaranteed. Even when everything on your system is working, you're only one upgrade of anything for something to suddenly stop working. Or you're running swimmingly for weeks, but the time comes when you need to use a peripheral you hadn't thought of, you had better still have Windows on your computer. 

It's best for people who like to tinker. If you just want to get work done, but you still like opensource for whatever reason, you can pretty much do anything you want on windows without buying a single thing. You can even easily make it into a unix-like experience by using Cygwin. Cygwin is a lot of fun to tinker with, just like Linux, and you're in both worlds at the same time.

I'm not an Linux expert, but I certainly have been tinkering with it for over a decade. I'm able to solve problems I encounter, but it does take up a lot of "hobby" time, and some thinking... because it rarely happens that you find a perfect solution already written up that works without some further adjustment of some kind. It's not always productive time, that's for sure. But this doesn't mean you have to ditch Ubuntu or whatever. It doesn't have to be all or nothing. Just keep both Win and Nix. It's not like years ago when drives were small and you weren't inclined to leave stuff you don't use on them.

### Post by mid_life_crisis on 2009-01-21
Answering a few of the questions posed, in no particular order:

The main reason I was giving up was because I couldn't get the ethernet connection working, and the graphic interfaces for trying to get it working were essentially useless.  The reason the ethernet connection made it seem overwhelming was that I had to log off Ubuntu, log on to Windows, search, write things down, save to a disk, log off Windows, log on to Ubuntu, ad nauseum trying to find a fix that worked.  It didn't help that the dual boot install failed and now the only option I get offered for installing Ubuntu is to take the entire disk.  When the computer boots, Ubuntu is in the list, but it doesn't start.

I just don't define a friendly interface as one that requires you to learn an extensive command line language to be able to deal with just about any problem that comes up, and I think this is probably why most people that give up on it do so.  

Anybody trying a variation of Linux has to be aware going in that at this point in time it cannot hope to come close to matching the sheer volume of apps and games available for Windows, so I hope that isn't why others are dropping it.


I finally decided that I am not going to quit just yet, but instead will try running Ubuntu as a VM.  If I can stop the constant log off/log on foolishness, maybe I'll find the patience to muddle through until I get it working.

I have to say that I am pleased that none of the responses were harsh.  I half expected to be called a few names indicating a lack of masculinity or intelligence.  Nice group of people here. :)
