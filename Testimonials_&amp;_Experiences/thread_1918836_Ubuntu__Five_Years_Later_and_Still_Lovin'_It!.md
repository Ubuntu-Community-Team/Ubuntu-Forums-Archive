### Post by LillyDragon on 2012-02-01
Just figured this testimonial would make a decent first post! I've been using Ubuntu Linux as my primary operating system for close to five years, (Since 8.04 LTS) and believe me, it's almost tempting to dump Windows altogether at times; this is why.

Since I was old enough to read, I have used the Windows operating system exclusively and nearly all of its subsequent versions since Windows 95. Despite how much I liked the OS, the constant drama of anti-spyware scans, overnight defragmentation sessions, infrequent registry cleanings, and the occasional BSOD from bad drivers forced me to squander more time than I would have liked on just maintaining the system itself. Half of my time on the desktop was spent not even getting actual computing or work done! I'm not saying I had a horrible experience with Windows, but all the pointless micromanagement really dragged me down.

When I was seventeen, I decided to look into Linux as a hobby, and checked out a few good books on the subject from my local library. (Linux Administration for Dummies and a book on RedHat were some of them.) Initially, I actually thought Linux was still an antiquated command-line OS without even so much as USB support, but I was overwhelmed with excitement to discover not only was that the exact opposite, but the choices of Window Managers, Distros, Applications, Live-CDs, Open-Source file formats and so much more blew me away! The fact that all linux-centered partition formats like ext2 didn't even fragment, and the OS's better security practices (When compared to Windows, obviously.) from the start quickly attracted me that much more to Linux. I immediately started looking through distros for the one Linux-based system that fit my needs.

 Unfortunately, I was still too chicken to install Linux natively at the risk of harming my Vista installation, due to its backwards swap-file always being stuck at the end of its partition preventing me from shrinking it! The distro choices were overwhelming as well, and a lot of them weren't as frequently maintained as I would have liked. I did try and like PCLinux2007 in a virtual machine, but the fact that installing RPM packages outside the official repository would brake the system broke my heart. Most of my favorite programs, or at least the most up-to-date versions weren't in said repository. It quickly became nothing more than a novelty that I couldn't put to serious use.

Then I found Ubuntu Hardy Heron, and it's newly integrated Wubi installation option; I nearly jumped out of my chair and went for a blank CD right away! After getting my first true taste of the OS, it blew me away with all its functionality. I immediately fell in love with how easily customizable Gnome 2 was, the overall greater depth of the Linux desktop in general when compared to Windows, and the wealth of open-source programs that bested and replaced nearly all of my overpriced-propriety software. And if there wasn't a Linux-equivalent to a Windows program I needed/liked, the WINE application-layer ran it right off the bat with few workarounds; even most of my games worked! The only thing I still can't do in Linux is play Halo, (Intel graphics is a joke when it comes to game performance anyway, even on Windows.) but that could be fixed by upgrading to a better supported graphics card/chipset brand, like nVidea or ATI.

I did bump into a few hardware problems at first, of course, but with minimal research, all I had to do was revert to Advanced Power Management and disable IRQ Polling in GRUB's boot parameters. (Intel-related issues, obviously.) Everything was peaches and cream after that! Even my webcam and portable scanner worked without needing additional drivers. When Linux just works, it does so beautifully; can't say the same for Windows half the time. Linux is so close to maintenance-free, (Once again, when compared to Windows.) for once, I felt like I could actually focus on just using my computer, not holding back the floodgates against backdoor trojans, a blatantly flawed partition format, and an annoyingly messy registry structure. The threat of viruses is still there, like any other computing platform, but jeez Windows suffers from a plethora of malicious crap in every direction, it's not even funny. I feel like I can finally breathe while doing a typical search on Google.

Later versions of Ubuntu ran even smoother, not even requiring me to implement workarounds. Then I finally got Vista's partition to shrink so I could install Ubuntu natively...oh how I nearly didn't go through with a dual-boot setup! But I figured, "ya never know when you might need Windows for SOMETHING", if not to play a mid-end PC game on this inferior Intel chipset or fiddle with a new device that didn't have Linux-native drivers.

And now here I am, so proud of my new Ubuntu 10.04 LTS install after getting the wireless card to behave. Thanks to Realtek already providing the needed drivers, all I had to do was follow the compile instructions in its readme document to the letter. (Initially, I thought I needed to include more verbose parameters, and the terminal obviously spat out a ton of error messages!) Thankfully, Windows 7 has proven itself to be the best thing since Windows XP, even better actually. So I'm sticking with a dual-boot setup at the moment, and absolutely happy with both OSs sitting side-by-side on my laptop. Every last contributor and developer that has made Ubuntu Linux into the amazing, polished and absolutely beautiful product that it is today deserve a lot of respect for what they do, and are still doing! It's plenty evident by this OS's outstanding quality they truly care about delivering a product designed "for human beings". It is my operating system of choice to this day, and would gladly use it exclusively on all of my machines if I wasn't so tethered to software for Windows that I can't replace.


As a bit of forewarning, I'm about to criticize Unity below, (Though not harshly; I don't hate it with every fiber of my being.) so if you're done reading my little testimony about Ubuntu Linux, you can go ahead and skip/ignore the paragraphs below if you want.

I did try 11.04 through Wubi recently, out of curiousity. Unity is honestly a beautiful solution for anything with a touchscreen, it really is, but it's by no means optimized for every other kind of computing machine out there. It was the most awkward, alien, unnatural, bulky, and obstructive environment I've ever tried to control with a mouse or touchpad. Unity got in my way every chance it got, and the lack of customizable options made it even worse. After I fought with it for about half an hour, I just rebooted into Windows and uninstalled that crazed, rabid creature from my machine. It was the most embarrassing and awkward experience I ever had with a desktop environment in Linux. Would have been the exact opposite experience on a touchscreen, but I don't have one, so Unity's Ipad-centric UI design pretty much screwed me over.

Yes, I'm well aware of the fact that all non-LTS releases are more of a test platform for new features to be included in later LTS compilations than anything else, but who made the decision to make Unity the default DE instead of Gnome? Would have made more sense if Unity was a secondary option, and maybe even encouraged more people to try the new environment simply for novelty's sake.

And before you mention it, yes I did try Gnome 3. Most of its classic features were flat-out broken, even after implementing all the workarounds I found in my Google search. Didn't really like it that much anyway. It ticked me off enough when the Gnome team took out transparent windows and customizable login screens from their DE in later 2.x.x versions, but that disappointment was the last straw. I'll more than likely give 3.0 another shot once the classic options are fixed, though. In the meantime, I keep hearing more good things about XFCE, (Tried it once and loved it to pieces, but customizing XFCE wasn't as convenient as Gnome was at the time.) might take it for a spin sometime to see what's changed since I last played with it.
