### Post by solwic on 2009-02-24
This community has played a large part of my life over the past couple of months, and I've made a few new friends here with which I've carried on communications beyond this forum.

I also love Ubuntu.  It'll be my #1 OS as long as it's around.  I love the Linux structure and ideals and I couldn't go back to MS even if I wanted to.  This OS is the best on Earth.  

My goal for my own participation in this community had always been to encourage and support.  I don't know enough to be of any use in the help forums, save for the simplest of matters that are answered long before I could tend to them.  But I am moderately good with words (I write for a living), and so I thought I might find my place here, in the Testimonials section.  I could, as I said, encourage and support, helping those frustrated folks who post an anti-Ubuntu rant stick with it and work through the beginner issues that everybody deals with eventually.  

That was my goal, my purpose.  It was how I thought I could give back to the community.  But in the end, I let myself be caught up in the negative things that go on here.  The endless harping over pointless topics like which desktop environment sucks worse, or why Ubuntu isn't ready for the masses, or why Windows is better, and on and on until what began as a noble goal transformed into something unrecognizable.

I do not fault the community for this transformation; the responsibility for that is solely my own.  I knew better.  I knew coming into this section that repetition was the rule, rather than the exception, and that the nature of personal experiences are - well - personal, meaning that ten different people could experience the same thing and have ten different views about it.  The very nature of this subforum is liquid, fluid, ever changing.  

But my goal has been compromised, my focus lost, and I find myself yammering endlessly on, blending in to the very conversations it had been my hope to stave off.  I have 1,150 posts, and I can't honestly remember any of them, save this one.  To think of how much time it took to write 1,150 posts, and to realize that I have used that time to say *nothing* of value or importance....  As I said, I am a writer, and I know the value of words.  The words I have written here, that had the noblest of intentions, could have been spent so much more wisely elsewhere.

So this is my experience.  I love Ubuntu.  I love everything about it, including this community.  I hope the mods leave this post here, for my experience with this community has truly shaped my experience with the software.  Were it not for the people here, I'd have long ago gone back to Windows.  The encouragement I received here - and that I wished to give back - kept me focused long enough to see what was right in front of my eyes.

Thank you for that.  You all have given me a great gift.  Would that I could return the favor.

Thank you for your time and your effort, and for the wonderful software we all use, as well as the wonderful support found within these very pages.

Thank you.

### Post by stchman on 2009-02-24
You have probably benn more help than you think.

I remember when I first started Linux a little over 2 years ago I knew very little.

I set out a few tasks to become a better Linux user.  I will admit that my experience with Windows installs have helped as I was pretty familiar with computers.

1.  Learn to not be afraid of the command line.  I still am not a huge fan of it, but I can use it.

2.  I had to re-train myself on how to do things on PCs.  I was so used to Windows thatit was pretty foreign to me.

3.  When you figure something out write out the steps with which you did the process.

Right after I started using Ubuntu I created my website.  At first it was for me, but after I see what problems others have I decided to give to others.

I have noticed that when I read others' HOWTOs they are usually written for the person that is fairly experiences in Linux.  I aimed to make my website site more towards the beginner.

I also notice that there are too many hardcore Linux guys out there that want to edit config files with vi rather than install the GUI tools (ndisgtk is a nice GUI frontend for ndiswrapper).  I aimed to make more of a point to use the GUI tools.  The Linux newcomer is easily scared when you give daunting commands but if greeted by using a user friendly GUI tool they will feel more comfortable.

If you want to make what you feel is a better contribution to the forum then go into the Absolute Beginner forum and start answering questions that you can handle.  You can then move up to the other help topics.

I usually come to the Testimonials area to put Ubuntu bashers in their place.  If someone bashes Ubuntu and knows what they are talking about, great.  Too many people bash it when they do not know what they are talking about.

So keep your head up.
