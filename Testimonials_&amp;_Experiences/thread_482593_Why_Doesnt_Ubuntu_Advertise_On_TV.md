### Post by bigboy_pdb on 2007-06-24
I agree that giving money to help support open source projects is something that people should do. I find it incredibly frustrating that a number of people who use Linux are willing to spend money on various forms of useless entertainment and services, but then they claim that they don't want to pay to help advance Linux and that it should always be free (as in beer). If enough money were to be put toward open source projects so that programmers and computer scientists could work on them as a profession instead of as a past-time then there'd be more free professional software on Linux.  

However, regarding the point about airing an Ubuntu commercial, I don't think that would necessarily be a good idea at this point in time. Many people who use Windows have certain expectations and they don't want something different. When they find out that Ubuntu's not the same it could cause Ubuntu to get negative media attention, especially since many people are going to complain that their wireless cards/printers/scanners or other devices didn't work (and many of them won't put in the effort to try to get them working). This might actually cause more people to turn away from Linux in the end.

I think the best way to get people to use Ubuntu is to become good at using it yourself and then to try to teach others to use it. You can always explain to people with outdated versions of Windows that their computers are no longer secure, their files are not safe, and that there's a good chance that someone could be using their computer as a zombie ([http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zombie_computer](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zombie_computer)). If they feel confident in your abilities then they may try Ubuntu.

You could also try to set up sessions to get people to bring their computers to you and where you help them to install and configure Ubuntu. For example, if you're a Scout leader then you could have a weekend session where people in your troop bring in their computers and you help them to set up and use Ubuntu (and this work could go toward earning the computer badge). Personally, I'm going to see if I can get something like that going in my Church for next year.

I'd say that one of the things that gives Ubuntu an edge is the fact that there are so many people out there that use it who are willing to help others learn to use it.
