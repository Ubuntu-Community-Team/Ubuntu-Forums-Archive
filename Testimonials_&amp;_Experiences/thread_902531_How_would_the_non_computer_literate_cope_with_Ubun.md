### Post by Yellow Stevens on 2008-08-27
That is question that I might have asked myself if I had ever heard of Ubuntu, or Linux or indeed anything other than Windows.

I didn't because in all honesty I don't know anything about computers. My job is about as far away from computers as you can get. 

I'm a Vicar. Some people call me a Minister or a member of the clergy. 

I am based at a University Chaplaincy in the UK. 

One of the students one day told me about Linux and I was intrigued but when I looked into it, it became clear to me that most people felt that open source was only for people who knew what they were doing. However because I liked the idea of community based software I decided to let an electronics undergrad put it on my machine as a dual boot. 

It was quick and simple to use and unlike Windows it did not take half a hour to load nor did it crash on me at least once a day. In fact 6 months later it has only crashed once! After a few months I noticed that I never booted my laptop into vista so I decided to put Ubuntu on my home desktop. 

I have found it easy to use, flexible and whenever I have had a problem the solution is always there on the internet easy to find.

I did have some problems going from 7.10 to 8.04, it took me a little while to get *everything* working (such as a digital projector) and I found it a bit fiddly to set up Compiz fusion with Emerald Theme manager.

But on the whole I have found Ubuntu to be the best piece of software I have ever used and people who think it is just for techies are wrong.

So there you go that is my long winded testimonial.  

I'd love to get more involved in the community, but actually there doesn't seem to be a lot that a non computer literate person can do. If anyone can think of any obvious needs a vicar can meet. Let me know :P

### Post by estyles on 2008-08-28
Here's the thing - Linux kind of *isn't* for the non-computer literate.  Because installing an OS in general isn't for the non-computer literate.  People buy computers with the OS pre-installed, and for most, pre-installed Linux isn't an option.  Most people think that Linux is only for techies because most people that use Linux have to install it themselves.  Installing your own OS is hard.

Sell a person a computer with Linux pre-installed and have them put Windows on it...  good luck!  The first thing you'll hear is "my internet doesn't work" (you didn't install the motherboard drivers that enable the onboard ethernet), then "my video is bad quality" (sorry, you need to install your video drivers too), then "I can't even open a document that my friend sent me" (oh, what, you didn't pay extra to have MS Office installed?  You'll either need to buy it or download Open Office.).  If the person in question actually *used* the pre-installed Linux for any length of time, you might hear "man, Internet Explorer doesn't work well... it's not like Firefox at all", or "I want to install a program, but the Add/Remove programs dialog only lets me remove programs... what the hell?  Do I have to search the internet and download stuff just to burn CD's (listen to music, monitor my system, etc...)?  What a bunch of crap!".

That's because installing an OS, and getting it working with all kinds of crazy hardware is not a beginner task.  It's definitely possible for someone who's willing to learn (which the OP definitely seems willing to do), but for the vast majority who want it to work with no fiddling required, you need an expert to install your OS and make sure it plays nice with everything.  If you don't have a very good friend who's a linux geek, that means a pre-installed, pre-tested OS, which unfortunately means Windows.  I don't like this, but it's pretty much true.  That's why I don't recommend Linux to too many of my friends and family - I have limited time for tech support.  We need more Linux pre-loaded machines, which seems unlikely in the short term, and more Linux geeks willing to play tech-support (which seems very likely - Linux users are very willing evangelists.  Kudos on that to all who take part).

I have no real point here.  But that when people say "linux is just for geeks", they kind of have a point.  Most people just aren't willing to learn, and Windows is already there for most of them.
