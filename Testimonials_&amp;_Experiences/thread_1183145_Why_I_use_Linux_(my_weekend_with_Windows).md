### Post by mdsmedia on 2009-06-09
I will freely admit that Linux is not perfect. I've been using Linux almost exclusively) at least on my home (notebook) PC for nearly 4 years, and I still consider myself a newbie.

For the record, I'm an accountant, I've used Windows (and DOS before it) since 3.1. I once asked a client who had a Mac, "when are you going to get a real computer?" (ie. Windows). I'm not an IT pro. Just an interested bystander.

About 4 years ago (at about the same time I joined the forums), I had a 6 month old HP laptop, running XP. After 6 months I was really annoyed with how slow my Windows system had become. Yes, it may have simply been some of the software I'd installed, but having removed that software it was still as slow as a wet week.

I'd been considering (and had tinkered in the past with) Linux for about 5-6 years, but had never used it terribly much. I was subscribed to mailing lists from my local LUG (Linux User Group), and I asked about installing dual boot. Ubuntu was suggested, so I downloaded and tried the LiveCD. I chatted with a friend in XChat and expressed how fast it seemed (YES on the LiveCD!!). I found a How To and installed it, dual boot, with XP. I don't know if I expected Linux to become my default OS, but from that moment on Ubuntu was my home. 

That was 5.04 (Hoary). I later upgraded to Dapper and had Dapper installed 'til long after Hardy was released. Not having much experience with, or confidence in, backing up and ensuring I wasn't going to lose anything, I always procrastinated about upgrading, especially by clean install. My upgrade from Dapper to Hardy, via the Update system, was a revelation. Hardy gave my system new life!

I have a desktop PC which was dual booting XP and Ubuntu, but I was hardly using it, so I decided to use it as a play machine. I used the Update system to update Ubuntu to Edgy, then Feisty. The Update system didn't seem to like my desktop. Eventually I wiped the hard drive and installed Mandriva. It gave me a chance to try something new. It now has Debian Testing running on it. I might document my experience with Debian later. But I installed Jaunty Alpha on the desktop and it ran like a charm, so when Jaunty was released, and I needed a clean install on my notebook, I thought "what the heck".

Anyhow, a friend of mine uses XP. A few years ago he bought a new PC with XP, has never been happy with it, since he was used to '98, but he's a habitual downloader of gimmicky software. Needless to say, his XP system was dragging its heals....10 minutes to boot to "readiness". I say readiness, but it really was a chore to do anything. Opening anything took ages. Numerous support calls (to me, from him) took ages, as we waited for something to open, or to reboot. I decided that it was time to re-install Windows, and maybe even persuade him to dual-boot Ubuntu.

As last weekend was a long weekend here in Melbourne, I called and organised to go there on Saturday morning and re-install Windows. I knew we'd be messing around backing up, making sure we had all the necessary license details for the software he'd bought, even a paid-for email client, IncrediMail. By Saturday night we'd re-installed Windows and I called it quits, arranging to come back the next morning to try to get his email back in IncrediMail, to reinstall anti-malware etc. Sunday night we'd downloaded a trial version of a program to restore his IncrediMail, paid for the license, and managed to restore his mail at least to folders on his hard drive. Monday morning, 3 hours more on the phone and his system was almost where he wanted it. He was still looking for the license for his world-clock, but at least he was happy at how much more quickly his system was running, and booting.

Tools used to partition, backup his system, restore his files:

 Ubuntu 9.04 (LiveCD was too slow on his system, so we dual-booted and    it ran like a charm);

 GParted, to partition my external HDD to enable us to backup his  Documents & Settings folder;

 GRsync, to copy his files to the external drive;

I wanted to use Ubuntu because I knew the tools available to partition, backup, restore. I didn't know what I would use in Windows and I knew it would take some stuffing around to find them, and I probably wouldn't have found anything suitable anyway.
