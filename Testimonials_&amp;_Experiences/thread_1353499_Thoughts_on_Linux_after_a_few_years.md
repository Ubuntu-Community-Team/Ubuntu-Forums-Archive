### Post by jbrown96 on 2009-12-12
This is the big 1000 (posts) here for me. I've been using Linux (Ubuntu
primarily) for three years (anniversary at the end of December). I've really
learned a lot, technically and politically/socially. I thought I'd take a little
time to post some relflections on my Linux experience. Hopefully, you will find
them insightful.

A little history of my Linux experience. I started using Linux during my
Christmas break in my Freshman year of university. I don't really recall what
got me interested, but I was hooked immediately. I was spending time at a family
residence in rural Canada. They had very slow dial-up internet, so I couldn't
download any distros; I ended up doing a lot of reading. I was experienced with
computers, but did have much technical ability; I'm a CS student now, so I'd say
I'm in a top percentile for computer expertise, but it wasn't like that starting
out. When I got back to the states, I immediately downloaded a Mepis. I was able
to set up the partitioning, but couldn't figure out how to install software
(emabarassing now). I quickly ditched Mepis and quickly moved through several
other distros. I remember trying Mandriva, but I can't remember the others. I
finally discovered Ubuntu 6.10. It was actually easy to use, but more
importantly I could actually find information. The forums were great when I
first started (and still are!), and I think that was by far the most important
reason I switched to Ubuntu. When I returned to school, I promptly wiped my
laptop (XP) and installed Ubuntu; I decided that I wouldn't switch if I tried a
dualboot. 
From there I learned a lot. There were many frustrastions starting out. Beryl
(now Compiz) on my ATI card was a nightmare; anyone remember configuring AIGLX?
My university's wireless didn't work (802.1x). The list goes on, but it was
frustrating; yet, I never considered switching. My external hard drive deserves
a lot of credit for saving me; I'd reinstall at least once a week. It was very
rough (both Ubuntu and my use of it) at that time. 
That's a brief history of my introduction to Linux. Now that you know where I
began, I'm going to talk about some of my conclusions about the community,
Linux, and the future. 

1) Linux develops very rapidly, and you can't make any assumptions based on what
you knew even a few months ago. 
It's very difficult to stay current on Linux; everything seems to be in start of
constant change. Distros are constantly changing the software and versions that
are available. APIs and ABIs are constantly changing; notice the trouble that
ATI has releasing drivers for current kernels and distros other than Ubuntu.
When I first started using Linux, I got one of those "Linux for Dummies" books;
it was sort of useful, but it was outdated just a year from release. I answered
a question for a user recently, trying to use a similar book a couple days ago.
They couldn't figure out why the commands from their book weren't working. It's
disappointing when you buy a book, and it doesn't give good info. Documentation
tends to be a little better (it isn't static like a book), but it's frequently
poorly maintained. For example, the info on Blu-ray playback and iphone syncing
(official community docs) are out of date and just don't work. 
Rapid development is a boon and curse. Desktop Linux is not nearly as good as
server Linux (look at usage statistics: Linux has significant server marketshare
and next to nothing on the desktop). That's makes rapid development great;
there's a lot to fix, so newer versions fix at least some of the stuff. As I
just mentioned, rapid development is bad for knowledge. Good documentation takes
time and just doesn't get done a lot; furthermore, even if it is done, the old
documentation doesn't get removed frequently (users consultanting blogs/forum
posts  that are out of date, in particular).

2) New users are difficult. There's so much to Linux that it's hard for new
users to know what to do. How many posts do you get like this "X doesn't work on
my computer. I have a Dell $MODEL. Please help"? People who read posts like this
(I primarily try to answer threads with 0 posts, so I see this a lot) are forced
to make a reply that basically doesn't help fix the problem at all. I almost
always have to request that they run some sort of command to get me more info
(fdisk -l, lspci, ifconfig, etc.). This is difficult in a forum because many
hours can lapse between posts. It really does become a problem when people just
want things to work. Since I can't work directly on their computer, then I have
to explain things that they don't understand. This gets really old; there's an
instinctive urge to tell them to RTFM, but I resist.

3) New users are great. I can't bash new users; they are essential to the
continued use of Linux, and we were all there at one point. Every once in while
someone will post a really rude reply to a new user. This really
bothers me, and I always try to say something to the offender. One of the
consistent things that I hear about soem *BSD communities is that they are very
hostile; I  don't want any part of the Linux community, especially a distro
that's "Linux for Human beings," to be like that. 

4) Us vs. the world (Linux vs. Microsoft) will always be a losing battle.
I really hate to break it to all the idealists, but Linux is not going to take
over the world. I imagine that the marketshare will increase, but it won't be
on the majority of computers. I used to be a fanatic that thought Linux was the
best thing since sliced bread. I think that I've matured since then. People who
call Microsoft M$ and other derogatory terms are not doing Linux any good.
Let's say that you are trying to convert someone; how persuasive is the
argument that the OS that they are using (Windows) and probably always have
used is the worst, evil thing ever? When you criticize something that people
use, they implicity feel some of that crticizism directed towards them. Linux
has its place, and I sincerely believe that its place will expand, but there
isn't going to be some glorious overthrow of Microsoft. 

5) Linux is the perfect OS for me, but that doesn't mean it's perfect. 
Just a few things that I love about Linux: great for software development, very
 poowerful terminal, software repositories, customizable, and automation.
Besides the software repositories (for easy software installation), I don't
think 95%+ of computer users would identify with any of those things. How many
computers have default desktop wallpapers? I'm a huge nerd, and I'm glad that
other nerds have developed an OS for nerds. I do have problems, but they seem
to be declining. I was thinking that Linux is less fun than it used to be; I
used to have to tweak so much stuff just to get my system working. I used to
have to compile ALSA for a couple versions of Ubuntu; I just don't have to do
stuff like that. My computer has become more of a tool; I use it for a purpose,
instead of fixing it constantly. It's very nice that it just works, but its
definitely a change from how I have used Linux the past few years. 
There are certainly still problems. Suspend doesn't work reliably anymore, but
I don't really care enough to switch back to 9.04. There's also a ton of
features that I wish Linux had, especially features (especially APIs) that
other operating systems have. In the end, the complaints I have are
peripherial, and the features I really depend on are not available anywhere
else. 

6) Community is very important to Ubuntu. 
Ubuntu has certainly makes the best distro available. There just isn't another
distro (mainstream at least) that rivals Ubunutu for easy of use and setup.
However, several distros have made great strides in recent releases. I think
that Fedora and OpenSUSE (especially opensuse) have quality to rival Ubuntu.
The difference if the community. If you search Google trying to find a fix for
a problem, and don't include "Ubuntu" in the search, almost all of the results
relate to Ubuntu. If you are a new user, why wouldn't you choose the distro
that is most likely to work, and has the most people willing to help you fix
any problems? I really enjoy giving back to Ubuntu. My way of giving back is to
reply to new threads. I think that its very important to get a response as
quickly as possible. I've had serious problems where I post a question, and I
almost constantly refresh the page until there is an answer (when I was new). I
don't usually like to get into really long running threads. I find that
frequently no solution is found, the OP has abandoned the thread, or some
argument starts between posters. Without the community (blogs and forums), I
don't think that Ubuntu could have reached this point.

7) The community can be unreliable and contradictory
I think that one of the largest problems with this forum is the lack of
consistency. There's often 10 different ways to do something in Linux, and it's
frustrating for users seeking help to try multiple solution fragments. Everyone
has their own way that they would solve problem X. I really wish that we could
decide that you fix X by doing Y and not Z. But I find that someone will post
with problem X, then the second post is solution Y, and the third is Z. I wish
people would refrain from posting alternate solutions, until the OP reports
whether it was successful. If you are going to propose Z, then at least give an
explanation on why Y is not recommended. Rember, you are not just convincing
the OP but also the person that proposed Y (if Z is better, then Y should not
be recommended in the future). I wish that the forum staff would institute some
sort of training seminar to standardize support. Give people that pass it some
special avatar or badge, so it's clear that they use standardized support
procedures. Another point on the same topic, I really wish people wouldn't hide
their post count. I know that post count is not necessarily an indicator of
expertise, but it is certainly useful when there are multiple solutions
provided. If I need help, I'm unlikely to listen to a poster with 5 posts
rather than one that has 3000, but I can't tell with a lot of people.

Those are just a few thoughts that I've had.
