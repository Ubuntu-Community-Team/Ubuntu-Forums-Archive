### Post by lpa53 on 2008-12-25
I'm home alone for the holidays with time on my hands and an IBM T41 castoff from the office that has to be scrubbed of its contents, including XP. I hated to just toss it out so decided to venture into the Linux world, downloaded Ubuntu, ran it off the CD as a test, thought it looked intriguing, and did the load that removes Windows.

I was pleasantly surprised at how quickly and easily the process went.  And even more surprised when just about everything worked, including thumb drives, CD, sound and printer, and the wireless connection to the web. Not bad at all.

I did have to change the wallpaper though.  The OOTB image is very depressing - a real downer that could discourage users, thinking they're getting into some weird cult or something!

I had a time learning how to uninstall the bundled version of OpenOffice and install the new one but with help from OO forums I got it done.

NOW the Question:  What do I do now?

Outside of getting a "free" OS, I now have to decide what I'm going to use it for.  I work with AutoCAD at work and need Windows for that.  I haven't yet tried out my M-Audio device with Audacity but I don't think the laptop's powerful enough anyway.

What is the primary reason people use Ubuntu?  Hatred of Microsoft?  A desire to be different?  Maybe my reason only has to be just to say I've used it and know a little about it - just a new world to explore. That could be enough.  A challenge.  

But can I get my wife to use it???

### Post by solwic on 2008-12-26
> **mdsmedia said:**
> An interesting thing about Windows and Windows users. Any little problem in Linux is a major reason to use Windows. Any major problem in Windows is overlooked as being par for the course.


Which says something, I think, about the goodness of Ubuntu.  Problems in Ubuntu are seen as an oddity; people don't expect them.  In Windows, I imagine people are thrilled to only have little problems like sound or ethernet; they could be dealing with BSOD's, or Virii, or Malware.  

As for running back to Windows, you have to understand that Windows is what they know.  It's their safety net.  People fear what they don't understand, and fear can make people do strange things.  

All we can do is be here to help and be - at the very least - touched that they think so highly of Ubuntu that one little problem is enough to shock them.  

Besides, if one problem would send them packing in fear, then they will always be happier with Windows/Mac.  Ubuntu isn't free, you just don't spend money on it.  You've got to learn, you've got to roll up your sleeves and get your hands dirty...and some people would rather pay to have others do their thinking for them.

Acceptance is the key.  Accept that they are scared and frustrated, and accept that they can only find their way to Ubuntu the same as we all did - on our own, and in our own time, and with help from the community along the way.  :)
