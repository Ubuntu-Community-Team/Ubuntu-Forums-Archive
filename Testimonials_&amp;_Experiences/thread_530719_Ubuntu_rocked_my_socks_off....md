### Post by DrumScum on 2007-08-20
Since a month or two, my computer is solely running Ubuntu Feisty Fawn. At last - after a hate/love relationship that lasted a little over 4 years - Linux just worked for me!

My first encounter with Linux was way back in 2003, when I downloaded - just out of curiousity - a Mandrake 9.0 iso. Before that time I used Win98SE (it came with my first ever PC, which I bought in 2001 - it was a little Fujitsu/Siemens Celvin box), closely followed by an (illegal!) copy of Win2000. So I only used Windows about two years before my first Linux encounter.

Anyway, I was pretty amazing about how easy MDK9 installed. It was as least as easy as a Win2000 install. At first I was a little overwhelmed by terms such as Windows Manager, RPM, KDE and Gnome to mention just a few, but along the way I learned to understand most of them.

However, Mandrake couldn't give me what I wanted. I used to do some audio work under Windows, for which I used e.g. CoolEdit, amongst others. MDK also offered some audio apps, but all of them were pretty basic and very "beta". So most of the time I still used Win2000, with Linux just being something to fool around with, because also other important programs such as Firefox, OpenOffice and Gaim were still in their early stages of development.

I also couldn't get along with the package management, which very often took me right to Dependency Hell, as it was called on the net.

After trying 9.1, 9.2 and 10.0, I kind of gave up on Mandrake. It stil sat on my PC, but I rarely used it.

But persistent as I am I started researching Linux again in 2005 and stumbled - through Knoppix - upon Debian. Hey, a complete different kind of packages, and it seems to work a lot better than those RPM thingies. So I tried that for a while, but only to find that Debian was rather hard to configure due to the lack of graphical configuration tools, like Mandrake did have. So it still was something to fool around with. Sometimes I managed to get things to work, sometimes I didn't, and sometimes things I did get to work suddenly stopped working for some obscure reason. Some examples are media playback (mp3, divx, windows media...) and browser plug-ins (such as Flash and video plugins). So I was till using Win2000 most of the time, and I was quite happy with it, although I found it had its shortcomings (driver issues, ugly...).

A bit later I stumbled - during one of my find-a-solution-for-a-debian-issue prowls - upon a quite new distro called Ubuntu, which claimed it was very easy to use. It must have been late 2005.

So I downloaded and installed it (it must have been 5.10, can't quite remember though), and yes, it looked quite promising. It was based on Debian, so luckily it had .debs instead of RPM's. Always a good point. But still it had quite some issues. Pretty difficult to get flash working, hard time getting support for non-free media formats (I even had to search on how to enable mp3 playback!), plus a lot of other minor glitches. But it was very workable and it seemed to have pretty recent and stable versions of OpenOffice, Firefox, Audacity and Gaim, which I started to use on Windows in the meantime. I also fell in love with Gnome, while in the past - on Mandrake and Debian - I preferred KDE. So I started using Ubuntu more and more, but still I was in Windows most of my time. I didn't really see an improvement in the 6.x version I installed subsequently.

And then came Feisty Fawn. I was expecting the same issues as with my previous Ubuntu installs, but hell, was I wrong. After installation I had a pretty cool OS, loaded with all kinds of very useful software, but still without a lot of media support. i knocked on wood when I opened the "Add/Remove...", but was very surprised at what I found there: packages to install flash, java, restricted media support... I selected all those interesting options, clicked apply, and lo and behold: in less then one hour my PC was more complete than it ever was with Windows. I now could even watch trailers on apple.com without a glitch. And I never had to use the terminal even once. Big applause.

And after some more (GUI only!) configuration steps I added some repos from trevino and the mediabuntu repo, so I could install some cool audio apps (Ardour just ROCKS) and 3D desktop thingies to play with. Also without using the terminal once!

So this time I found myself not booting in Win2000 anymore, so now I got myself 6 GiB extra discspace :)

Just to say that Ubuntu 7.04 rocks my socks off :guitar:
