### Post by SeePU on 2010-07-25
I had to install a flavor of Linux on my Pentium 3 computer so I chose Ubuntu 10.04.  It was the first CD I came to so I thought I would use that one.

But, scrolling web pages or pdf docs was agonizingly slow.  Some subtle freezes here and there.  The experience was not pleasant.

It was not usable so I installed the other disc I had at hand, Lubuntu.  It installed okay (it seems about 40 mins with U maybe slightly longer) and the computer is running way more smoothly.

I had 9.04 and maybe 9.10(?) on one of these computers before.

But, 10.04 just bogs it down.  

I recall reading an article from Linus about how Linux was getting bloated.  I think it's time to promote the smaller footprint DEs.  Linux was able to boast, once, that it could preserve older hardware by providing a base OS that could make it run faster.   But, now KDE and Gnome are getting fatter and it's no secret that xfce in Xubuntu is said to not take advantage of the lighter resource DE.  Ubuntu doesn't seem to be dedicated to preserving an efficient DE.  Lubuntu worked but I haven't compared it (performance-wise) to other distros using LXDE.

This is a very valid complaint and concern regarding Ubuntu.  I think it is one that should be taken seriously.  If a user is going to take Ubuntu seriously, the devs have to tackle this problem instead of always relying on the marketing, PR and 'pretty looks' of the externals and concentrate a bit more on the internals! ;)

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2010-07-25
> **SeePU said:**
>  
I recall reading an article from Linus about how Linux was getting bloated.  

This quote gets taken out of context a lot.  Linus develops the kernel -- it's his life and his world, basically.  When he says "Linux" he isn't talking about distros or desktop environments. He's talking about the kernel -- or more to the point, the kernel codebase.

The kernel codebase *is* getting bloated, mostly due to the massive amount of device driver code in it.  That's a totally separate issue from GNOME bogging down your system.  It also doesn't mean that you can't make a linux kernel extremely small and fast by removing driver code or other subsystems you don't want -- take tinycore linux for example: a distro with a GUI and package management that's only 10MB.

What's bloating every OS  out there is the fact that people increasing expect computers to do more and they expect it to happen without them having to know how it works.  This isn't unreasonable, but it takes code to accomplish.  Functionality isn't free, in this sense.

### Post by SeePU on 2010-07-25
I think there's a debate going on these days between various Linux users regarding DE and 'bloat' or how resource heavy they are seems to be a topic of discussion often.  I'm sure many of you know of some KDE users who discuss KDE 4 (v.s. KDE 3.5.x) and whether KDE is getting a bit 'too bloated.'  In the 'Gnome or KDE'discussions, it's just one part of it being the developments of both/either and whether the requirement of resources is getting excessive.

I like the idea of a lower resource DE and trying to maximize efficiency while pursuing the best compromise between feature-rich performance and lower resource options.  That's why I started looking at XFCE and LXDE since they're a bit more advanced than other DEs that really apply to the most basic of computers (aka ancient or primitive computers! :D  ).  Also, XFCE and LXDE are becoming more standard as DE for distro derivatives so that I can try one distro and easily switch to another using the same DE.  It's convenient when the devs have pre-configured everything including the default DE (or left it as XFCE/LXDE DE default options) because then there's not a lot of 'outside/additional' files added using up disk space.   I guess if the drive is large enough, it's not an issue.  

Also, for e.g., I am leaning more away from KDE (formerly, my fav. DE) since it tends to have a lot of 'stuff'running that may slow things down depending on what you're doing.  I just like the concept of trying to keep things 'light' so that either 1)older computers can still seem fast and 2) more powerful tasks may still be able to continue if things/functions/processes are kept as efficient and 'streamlined' as possible.  Just a thought.
