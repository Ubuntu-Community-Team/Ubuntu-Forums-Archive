### Post by GOfree on 2008-05-14
Hello,

My name is Geoffrey, and I am a Ubuntu/Linux newbie. I started out with Ubuntu 7.10 "Gutsy Gibbon" in March 2008 and quickly moved on to 8.04 "Hardy Heron" in April.

I am so pleased with Ubuntu that I want to spread the word. As a newbie, I cannot compare "Hardy Heron" to previous versions of Ubuntu, or Ubuntu to other Linux distributions; but I have been a casual PC user for the last fifteen years, and I am glad to say that the Ubuntu/Gnome/Gnu/Linux experience over the last two months has been the most gratifying computing experience of my life. Thanks to Ubuntu, I have become interested in computing again.

When I started using Microsoft Windows in 1993, I was fascinated with computers and wanted to learn everything about them. Fifteen years later, before finding Ubuntu, I had not learned very much more about computers and had lost almost all intention of ever doing so. Computers had become nothing more than appliances to me--despite the advent of the internet and the advances in multimedia software. 

I had become accustomed to the limits and limitations of computers. If they would do what I wanted, they were great. If not, I could only give up in bitter frustration. There was always a feeling of being held back--a sense of paternalistic control behind the technology. All enthusiasm for computing was gone, replaced by frustration and resentment.

Ubuntu has changed everything about computers for me. My enthusiasm for computing has been revived and I can once again see the world-changing potentials of computer technologies. I feel empowered and limited only by my technical know-how and imagination. The sense of corporate oppression has lifted and been replaced by a sense of faith and trust in the Ubuntu community.

Setting up my computer with Ubuntu has been a joy--a labour of love. Ubuntu does not pretend to do everything for you, but is so relatively easy that a newbie like me can quickly figure things out. Instead of having to rely on the software to do everything for me (for good or ill), I have had to get my hands dirty and do things for myself--with the helping hands of fellow human beings. In the process, I have learned so much about my computer (BIOS, boot order, command lines, file system, hardware, etc.). It has been a very gratifying experience--educational, inspiring, and empowering.

I am doing so many new things with my computer, and Ubuntu has opened up a whole new view of the possibilities of computers. In the last two months, I have been setting up 3-D desktops, installing all kinds of new software, learning to work with databases, using bittorrents, setting up dual boots, and even using a virtual machine to run an alternate operating system in Ubuntu. Before Ubuntu, I did not know I could do these things and probably would not have even tried. Now with Ubuntu, I feel confident to push my personal computing abilities and am excited about the possibilities I have yet to discover.

I love Ubuntu. I want to thank everyone who is making it possible--developers and community alike. I want to let everyone who has not yet found Ubuntu know what an incredible experience it can be. Ubuntu is so much more than an operating system. For me, Ubuntu has not only revived my interest in computing, it has changed my whole outlook on life.

### Post by GOfree on 2008-05-15
Thanks for the replies, everybody! :)

Just one last word to other newbies to Ubuntu/Linux...

Be patient and remember the big picture. While Ubuntu distributions mostly work perfectly "out of the box", no computer or user is exactly the same. You may have to do some tinkering to make it work the way you want it to. And there are some bugs still to be worked out. 

But this is all part of the new philosophy. Ubuntu has a Do-It-Yourself component to it that may be unfamiliar to you. If you stick with it, you may find that it is just this ability to do things for yourself (with a little help from your friends) that makes Ubuntu such an enriching experience.

Can we always afford to just throw money at people to do everything for us? Isn't this where Ubuntu fits in with the whole world-view of sustainability--learning to do more with less?

Besides nearly every other day we get regular updates, so we can rest assured that someone is already working on the problems. The reason perhaps that Gutsy worked so great at the point Hardy came out was that it had six months of updates behind it. (There were some 150 updates at the point I installed it.) I am sure that Hardy Heron will work just as well, if not better, six months from now.

Reading some of the comments in the forum, I see some people are getting hung up on a few bugs.

The bugs can be fixed easily enough. It is the technological dependency/alienation that really needs to be cured.

See you all around!
