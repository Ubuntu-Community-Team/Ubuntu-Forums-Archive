### Post by Therion on 2008-09-17
I have read your post and I understand your point of view.

In point of fact I believe the core issue here is based in differing viewpoints.  I'm going to call these points of view the "*Windows Way of Computing*" and the "*Linux Way of Computing*".  Furthermore each 'Way comes with it's own complete and unique philosophy.  

It appears to me that the vast majority of current computer users have been "raised" with computers that run Windows, and thus, by default, have learned to do things using The Windows Way.  Nothing inherently wrong with this.  The Windows Way gets things done.  No one here will argue with that.  The Linux Way of computing does as well.  No one who knows what they are talking about will argue that.

The real difference, and the core of the issue for switching from one Way to the other, is not, for instance, the presence (or need for) the Command Line Interface (CLI); but rather that it presents a different process than what the Windows Way presents.  Is the CLI easier?  Is it faster... Better... More powerful?  These are not questions that have "Yes" or "No" answers.  

The Windows Way, as I see it, was/is designed to *conceal* as much from the end-user as possible.  The Linux Way is present as much as possible to the end user, albeit in a controlled and usable fashion.  Is this better?  I don't think of that as a "Yes" or "No" question, it's a difference of how you want to think about computing.  

There will always be those who want to jump into their car, put the key in the ignition, punch the accelerator and "just go".  There will always be cars made with them in mind.  There will also always be people who, like me, prefer to drive a manual transmission. Who want to exercise a little more control over what happens and when.  Who want to pop the hood and take a look. Who don't mind asking "What's this do?  Can you explain that again, I don't understand..." There will always be cars built for us as well. 

Some of us would RATHER use a CLI because we can control what our PC is doing instead of blindly clicking a “shortcut”.  The CLI is satisfying to us because WE tell the PC what to do inch by freaking inch and not one bit further.  Is this better, faster, more powerful?  That’s not the issue.  The issue is a mindset: The mindset you have about computing and how that gets accomplished.  We want to control the PC, not be controlled by it.  We want our machine to do our bidding like little whipped schoolgirls.  When we say “jump” our PC had better prompt us with a snappy “How High” dialog box so that we can dictate not only HOW high, but also total hang time and angular trajectory based on prevailing weather conditions and overall sunspot activity.

It’s not about a better way.  It’s about a different way.

### Post by lukjad on 2008-09-17
@greenleaf
You say that the terminal is harder. I say that you only *think* that it is harder, so it is. You just never learnt how to "read" a command. Take this command:

```
sudo apt-get install supertux-stable
```

It looks scary if you don't know what it means. *sudo* is a warning that you are about to do a system level task. What you do in *sudo* can and will affect your computer. After you insert a command with sudo you will be asked for your password. One you enter it, you are acting as an admin. This is where the term *sudo* came from: **S**witch **U**ser **DO**. You are now, in that terminal "switched" from a normal user to an administrator. (There is also gksudo, which is for opening something graphical from the command line. For example, if you wish to edit a file deep in the system or add a few different repositories. There is also something called kdesudo for when you are using Kubuntu. It replaces gksudo.)

Below are three rules to use before you do anything with *sudo*:

```
    #1) Respect the privacy of others. (Assuming you are on a shared computer.)
    #2) Think before you type. (Always a good idea.)
    #3) With great power comes great responsibility. (Or, in other words, BE CAREFUL!)
```

Now you know about *sudo* you now have only have three more parts left to learn to understand a command line.

I will focus on the next: *apt-get*.

*apt-get* is a tool used by APT (**A**dvanced **P**ackage **T**ool). It is the program that installs the software you want (in this case *supertux-stable*). Now you know apt-get, you can forget about it. It is like Synaptic.

*install* is the next phrase. This is simple, and easy to figure out. It is telling *apt-get* what to do with *supertux-stable*. If it is installed, you can remove it by changing *install* to *remove*.

*supertux-stable* is the name of the file you want to install. If you remember a game and you just remember that it is called *super* something, you can hit the tab key and apt-get will give you a list of all the programs that begin with *super*.

There are many more things you can do with APT and the terminal, but I just listed a few. For further reading and some of my sources:

[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/UsingTheTerminal](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/UsingTheTerminal) (A must read!)

[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/AptGet/Howto?action=show&redirect=AptGetHowto](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/AptGet/Howto?action=show&redirect=AptGetHowto) (Very interesting.)

[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudo](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudo)

[http://www.linux.com/feature/40745](http://www.linux.com/feature/40745)

[https://help.ubuntu.com/6.06/ubuntu/serverguide/C/apt-get.html](https://help.ubuntu.com/6.06/ubuntu/serverguide/C/apt-get.html)

Please note that I have done my best to explain things in a simple, clear and most importantly, correct way. If I made any mistakes, please tell me so that I can correct this post and learn.

I ask you one question (not as a challenge but i really want to know. please don't be offended... you're someone senior to me and been around for >3yr with this community....)
Why then do you think Mac OS is eating away at Windows marketshare lately & not linux? Is it purely because of clever marketing? Or is it something else? It's a combination of factors.

1. **Apple understands the value of having hardware and software designed to go together.** (Of course, they take it one step further and say "You *must* use our hardware," which I don't agree with.) My wife and I [had a nightmare setting up a Mac Mini for my in-laws](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntucat/macs-are-just-computers-not-magic/), since the only thing Apple has control of in that situation is the computer itself (not the monitor or printer or keyboard or mouse). But the laptop solutions work extremely well, for the most part (except that my wife's Macbook Pro can't connect to WPA, but that's another story).

2. **They create an entire integrated experience.** If you buy all Apple products, they all work together. The iPhones will sync with the Macbooks (iTunes, address book, etc.). The Macbooks are built to work with .Mac accounts. You also have Apple TVs and iWork software.

3. **They have a physical store**. I bought an Eee PC on faith and a lot of reviews (I read literally hundreds), but there wasn't any way for me to try it out myself. No store near me stocks Linux-preinstalled and preconfigured computers. And any Linux-installed computers I've seen were basically designed for Windows (and even have the Windows key on the keyboard still) and just had Linux slapped on top.

Many long-time Windows users still have preconceived notions about Macs, and being able to walk into an Apple Store and try out various Apple computers is a great way to have those notions dealt with.

Can a long-time Windows user do the same for Linux? No. Instead, she has to download a live CD and then find out that the various Linux-unfriendly components in her Windows computer don't work with Linux, and then she'll conclude Linux is for geeks only.

Apple doesn't make you download a CD of Mac OS X and try it on some random hardware. They make sure the hardware will work for the operating system and vice versa.

I actually wrote up a little proposal that Ubuntu should open a [Canonical store](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntucat/ubuntu-the-open-source-apple-challenger/), similar to the Apple store, except favoring open source solutions instead of proprietary ones, and selling recommended preinstalled computers instead of forcing people to use only certain preinstalled models.

4. **Clever marketing**. Since you mentioned it, yes, Apple is great at making things seem cool and hip, even if they're mundane or unoriginal. *Spaces!* Yeah, Spaces is just workspaces, which Linux has had for over a decade. iPods not having radios or voice recording abilities? No sweat. That's a feature. 

Clever marketing can help if you have those other three points as well. As it stands now, Linux in general and Ubuntu specifically lack all of the above, and that's what we need for market penetration. We need preinstalled options and hardware designed to work with the software and vice versa. We need physical stores where people can try out Ubuntu is ideal working conditions. We need an integrated experience (Ubuntu-friendly player that syncs with Rhythmbox and is marketed as such, access to commercial music through a Rhythmbox plugin, an open source phone that works well and takes your address book from Gnome). We need good advertising.

There's also a fifth problem Apple doesn't face that Linux does.

5. **No software freedom ideals**. Apple isn't afraid of DRM, big business, closed source, or proprietary solutions. In fact, it embraces all of the above. Which means you can get commercial software and commercial videos and music without half the user base complaining about it. With Linux, you'll always have half the user base saying "Yay! Such and such got ported to Linux!" and then the other half saying "Who cares? You think I want that proprietary crap on my computer?"

6. **Looks**. Looks are subjective, of course, but the only people I know of who think Macs are ugly post on these forums. Everyone I know in person loves the look of Macs. Even though I don't like the way Macs behave, I like the look of Macs. That said, I do think Ubuntu gets overly abused by self-appointed "brown is ugly" aesthetic gurus here (who also seem to think Mac's Aqua is ugly). My wife, who is a graphic designer by trade, thinks Windows is ugly, loves her Mac, and ooh'ed and ah'ed at Ubuntu Hardy Heron's default look (it was mainly the wallpaper that got her).

Frankly, I think Apple realized that all of the above were far more important than have an intuitive or easy-to-use interface. Mac OS X is one of the most difficult user interfaces I've ever encountered. People don't mind it, though, for all the reasons I've mentioned above. And difficulty/ease of use always gets trumped by familiarity. After you get used to using Macs, you just don't care about what's difficult or not, and you actually start to like doing things that way. Same with Linux. Same with Windows. Ease of use and intuitiveness are great things to have, but people always prefer familiarity.
