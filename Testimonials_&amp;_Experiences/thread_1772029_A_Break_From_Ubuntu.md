### Post by ausmuso on 2011-05-31
I have seen the rise and fall of many distros since I first started using Linux in 1997. I have been with Ubuntu since Dapper Drake and, on the whole, Ubuntu has served me well.

In the heated discussions about the respective merits of distros we sometimes tend to forget that a distro is merely a convenient means to get Linux installed on our desktops or laptops. Once our Linux is up and running it hardly matters which distro we used to install it. Linux is Linux and it's a great OS.

Unfortunately Ubuntu's insistence on a half-yearly release cycle can become an irritant to someone like me who uses his desktop for a lot of day-to-day work. Why bother installing a new release when I know it's going to be  outdated in half a year's time?

Also, real software development progresses at snail's pace so every now and again Mark Shuttleworth throws in something from left field to create a sense of real innovation since the previous release half a year ago. GRUB2 was a prime example. It was alphaware when first released and even today it hasn't matured sufficiently to be embraced by most other distros. Who needs GRUB2 anyway? A bootloader should be just a humble, simple but robust piece of software to get your system up and running. GRUB1 did - and still does - an excellent job.

Now we are being faced with the Unity desktop, a one-size-fits-all Swiss Armyknife of a desktop. One a modern desktop the icons are far too large and navigation feels slow and intricate if you have a lot of applications you regularly use. At the other end of the scale, Unity's CPU load is beyond the meager crunchpower of my humble 7" EeePC netbook. Desktop and netbook's demands are so different that one desktop design simple doesn't fit all.

All in all I feel it's time for me to get off the half-yearly release treadmill for a while. That's why I'm now running the 16 bit version of Linux Mint's Debian edition. Installation - although maybe just a tad less newbyfriendly than Ubuntu - was still a breeze and everything seems to work fine. No need to install a new version every half year, small and frequent updates will keep the system up to date. Best of all, I now tap directly into the motherlode of Debian repositories.

I'll be running the system intensively for a while. Meanwhile, wait and see, I may be back with Ubuntu yet!

### Post by TBABill on 2011-06-01
All the valid points have been covered already, but I'd have to question why anyone doing real work (meaning business relies on that machine being both up and operational at critical times) would ever stay on the 6 month release cycle? If you use your machine in the performance of your work, or even in support of your work, then stability should always be top priority. To that end, every new release has bugs to squash, which is great for using a LTS version because they overlap by one year. So this give you 2 years on a LTS and then a new LTS is released. Stay on that older LTS a few more months or up to a year, then upgrade to the next. Add some TRUSTED PPA's (for office software, browsers, multimedia and other critical apps) but keep them on the LTS for better stability.
 
It has been mentioned that LTS does not necessarily mean more stable. Yes, the acronym letter "S" is support, not stability. However, the LTS is based off of a snapshot of Debian Testing whereas normal releases are based on Sid (unstable). THAT is the key. Testing is the playground for the next stable release of Debian, which means it's where the packages get to be stable enough to be considered for release as stable. Snapshot that, tweak it, add Ubuntu's additions and there's your LTS. 
 
They all have bugs. I agree with that wholeheartedly. However, it takes far less to squash LTS major bugs than any others and 10.04 is now rock solid. If I relied on Ubuntu as my distro for work, it would be LTS all the way. Forget about the release cycle...to me those are great for purposes that are not mission critical. I like seeing the changes every 6 months, but I have a machine I do NOT count on to play with and break it on purpose.
 
Sorry to get on a soapbox, but the release cycle happening so often is to bring out newer technologies and features at a rapid rate while evolving the distro. Stability is not found in doing so. Yes, they do become stable over time, but much of the 18 month support window gets chewed up getting there. For some they seem very stable right from the start, but for others there are show stoppers that take forever to get fixed. I've been lucky on each release, but many have to revert back to a working version till bug fixes are sufficient to stabilize their system.
 
Good luck, but the argument posed by the OP is a bit swayed if that's a work machine. I'd never trust work to anything but a proven stable release.
