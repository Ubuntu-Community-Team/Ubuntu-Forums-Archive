### Post by Sillouette on 2009-06-06
/rant on

Alright Linux (Ubuntu); WTF? -or- Why I do not have a hard-on for terminals and config files.

This is now the second time Nix has pulled this ****. Though for the sake of honesty, the first time it wore a "Mandrake" hat.

Now don't get me wrong, conceptually it does rock. From the non-fragmenting file system, and the inherently solid security, to package managers / repositories, it's very existence is a solid kick to Microsoft's sack. Bravo.

I have been wanting to dump MS for almost a decade now, and I have tried, seriously. Twice.

But like a cat at bath time, It's almost like Nix works to prevent this. Painfully.
The first time was a touch over seven years ago with then Mandrake. Now I will concede that times were different then, but any Desktop OS that needs to have it's Kernel recompiled by the user just to get a gForce card functioning needs to have it's *** kicked on principal. No excuse for that ****. At all. And after a few weeks of recompiling kernels, editing config files (so many f%cking configs), hunting for nonexistent drivers, and watching myself and others with "tough" questions go ignored in support forums, I threw in the towel and reinstalled Windows.

I almost welcomed the return of the BSOD.

So I watched and waited. I kept a close eye on the many incarnations and developments of various distros, waiting for one to come of age. I know, I know. That sounds like "waiting for Linux to be more like Windows, just better". And too many in the Nix communities have convinced themselves that is what we Win converts want from a Nix ditro. And subsequently dismiss us out of hand. But nothing could be further from the truth.

Were that the case, we all would have migrated to Mac in droves years ago.

Fast forward to present day, and to the new Disto dejoure; Ubuntu.
I won't even ask what a "jaunty jackalope" is.

After a few years of nagging from my Nix friends, I decided it was time to try again. After all, it had been over seven years, surely vast improvements have been made.
Yet there was this nagging voice in my head that seemed to be saying two things; 1) Dual Boot. and 2) "Round two...FIGHT!"  It scares me a little that the nagging voice sounded a lot like the Mortal Combat announcer guy.

Ubuntu is very impressive. Sleek, responsive, secure. The install went without a hitch. The differences and improvements between then Mandrake and today's Ubuntu are too many to list. I was honestly impressed. "This is it" I thought.
Then it kicked me in the nuts. Hard. 

Two days total to get wifi to work. A fair amount of blame rests on my shoulders for that one, as I guess I should somehow just "know" what lib's, dependencies, and repositories need to be satisfied before ndiswrapper will even function. Though props to whom ever came up with ndiswrapper...neat trick.

So that worked, or works now anyway. Though no thanks to anyone in the Ubuntu forums. Seems those *** hats are still picking the "low hanging fruit" when it comes to offering solutions to problems. 
Seriously guys, when you do the Superman pose after helping someone figure out how to change the desk top background, then ignore the hard ones, you just end up looking like d-bags. Like many of us, I've worked the phones in tech support centers too. I am a hardware geek to the core. I've seen all the tricks to ditching customers that have issues you can't rattle off a platitude to solve. From on-hold loops, to crumpling-paper-static-opps-disconnected tricks, I've seen them. But the ones who stand out the most aren't the uber brilliant ones, its those that have the balls to say "I don't know, but let me see if I can get that answer for you. Though it may take some time."

To Ubuntu's credit, there was no Kernel recompiling to get the video card working this time, and I acknowledge the efforts of both the OS developers, the FOSS community, and manufactures for Nix driver support. Steps have been taken and improvements have been made, but understand that when someone with "fresh eyes" points out that there is a ways to go yet, it is not a personal attack on any distro or community. As good as Ubuntu is, if the hardware you run isn't natively supported, and you do not posses prior admin-level knowledge of the OS, get ready to give the "cat a bath". There Will Be Blood. That much has not changed in seven plus years. And that is the sad part.

Also disappointing is basic quality. While giving a potential convert an "out", dual booting also allows for easy comparison between two OS's on identical hardware. Once all components were up and running on Ubuntu, I was disappointed that sound and video playback just didn't cut it. Rather than picking a different OS to boot to, I felt like I had downgraded my hardware when I wasn't looking. The differences were not slight, my ten year old actually remarked "wow, whats wrong with the movie? Don't look or sound right anymore Dad". 

At this point I have to say that while I have every intention to keep and maintain my Ubuntu partition(s) this time, it really feels, well... not done yet. As stated, improvements have been made, but the quality and usability are just not there yet. What is great is I can actually roll up my selves, get dirty and contribute to the cause along with the community once I learn enough. Now that I am older I see the value in that. But there is also equal value in having fresh eyes, as constructive criticism is necessary, and does not equate to personal attacks.

What it comes down to in the end is that there really are thousands upon thousands of us who are here, wanting to make this work. Permanently, and not just as an experiment. But while we struggle to learn and adapt, all too often the OS fights the process, and at times the "support" community kicks those who are down rather than providing clarification. Though not always, but often enough to make a difference.
"Suck it up, NOOB!" is never a good answer.

Finish Him!

/rant off

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2009-06-06
> **Sillouette said:**
> Ubuntu is really nice, but look folks, if your hardware isn't nativity supported, it could be painfull.

That's true. Hardware support is continually improving, and the pace of improvement is accelerating. With certain types of devices, it's hard to buy something that's NOT Linux-compatible!

I like to think of it thus:

1x Microsoft Windows Vista Home Premium OEM: $199

1x Mac Mini: $799

1x Linux-supported USB wireless device: $19

Those are your choices: Get a copy of Windows and reinstall it, buy a Macintosh, or buy a decent wireless card. Your wallet can make the decision for you! (incidentally, I just bought a D-link DWL-G122 off eBay, works great out-of-the-box, cost me AUD $19 including postage)

Eventually, hardware manufacturers will come together and form a standard for devices, so that only ONE driver will be needed in order to run all the devices of a particular class. You know, like what happened with USB hard disks and optical drives, and like what is happening with webcams and TV tuners. This means zero software development costs for hardware manufacturers, zero software incompatibilities for Windows users, and 100% hardware compatibility out-of-the-box on all platforms.

Until then, Linux's hardware compatibility will continue to get better.

Just my two bits:
  I started using Linux back with SuSE 7.3 Personal Edition because I got sick of Windows and it's incessant crashes and driver updates, not to mention the never-adequate anti-virus software.
