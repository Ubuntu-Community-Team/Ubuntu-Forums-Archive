### Post by Steve S. on 2007-01-30
First of all, I want to thank Ubuntu, yet again, for giving me SO much learning experience.  I have been with Ubuntu from Hoary now up to Edgy.  Very, very pleased and love this system...it has encouraged me to do nothing but spread the word that, by the way, there are other options besides Microsoft and yes, many are much better, including Ubuntu (in my opinion).

That has relevance, I swear.

I put together older computers into running systems, for fun. Yesterday was a pentium III system that, although it did have a cd player, also still had a 5" floppy drive.  10G system.  Not the oldest system I've played with, but certainly getting on in years.

Anyway, I came across a simple, Linux-yet-windows-ish livecd called BeatrIX.  Great little cd, installs very easily (20 minutes).  Very impressed.  Quick loading livecd.  I had it installed on the system and up and running with very little effort.  Didn't even have to take this computer apart and just put BeatrIX on it and now it is a great little working system.

Well, the more I looked, it turns out that BeatrIX is just a modified version of Hoary, and all the repo's and etc.  After using Ubuntu for the time I had, I could modify it easily and run through all I needed.

Just thanks again to the Ubuntu community and all those that have helped me over the years with all the support 'cause it has made stuff like this even easier.:D

### Post by K.Mandla on 2007-01-30
You can run Ubuntu on anything down to a Pentium I and not sacrifice too much in usability. You have to be tricky and you have to be patient, but it can be done. 

[Damn Small Linux]("http://www.damnsmalllinux.org") is a great alternative for older machines, and for my money, [Fluxbuntu]("http://www.fluxbuntu.org") is every bit as speedy as DSL and you get the Ubuntu framework as a bonus. It's in beta until Feisty, I believe.

If you know the guts of your machine and you want to milk as much speed as you can out of it, you can tinker with [Arch]("http://www.archlinux.org") Linux or [Gentoo]("http://www.gentoo.org") or even something like [Linux From Scratch]("http://www.linuxfromscratch.org/"). 

By the way, I'm typing this from a 300Mhz Pentium II running Arch linux -- boots in 37 seconds and on only 18Mb of memory out of the 256Mb installed. :biggrin: (Sorry, couldn't help bragging. 8-[ )

### Post by K.Mandla on 2007-01-30
It's [custom-made]("http://www.archlinux.org/about/"), for what I understand. It's an independent distro that I believe had roots in Crux Linux.

It's a speed-tweaker's dream. Everything is precompiled with i686 optimizations (there are some other versions for other architectures), which means it's kind of like Gentoo without the wait ... if I can be so bold. :roll:

You start with almost nothing and you build up from there. There is no unwanted baggage and no excess weight. It has a great package manager and the documentation (the wiki in particular) is top-notch.

The only downside is that you really, *really* have to know your hardware. It's not going to configure itself, which means you can't be afraid of the command line either.

Of course, if you've tinkered with Gentoo, Arch won't be intimidating at all. :D

### Post by wykedengel on 2007-01-31
yes, BFX (final) is alive and well (I am using it as we speak). The distro, while a hybrid of debian, ubuntu and a splash of knoppix, has stability and speed as it's strongest points. using the breezy repos (as well as their own), a wide variety of software is available to give 99% of users what they seek.

another strong point of bfx is that it is newbie friendly. not bombarding you with 1000 choices per application ensures that the average user had everything needed to begin her computing journey.

while the community is small, it is rapidly growing. however, i don't know may distros where you can get detailed help from the person who built the program; however, you get that and more.

not wanting to get into a "what's the better distro" debate, I will say that for my computing need (office functions and desktop publishing) bfx is better equip to keep me working without slowdown. (example: when dapper starts on my laptop, 138 mb of ram are already in use before applications...on bfx 64 mb.)

while the software from the breezy repo is a little dated, the bfx repo keeps the important apps (like OOo and evolution) current. these "homebrews" work with the distro to provide a productive experience for users.

while not a 'bleeding edge' distro by any means (no 3d desktop...but that's not my thing anyway), it will take care of 99% of your computing needs with speed and grace. btw, in speed comparisons on my computer, bfx is faster than fluxbuntu.
