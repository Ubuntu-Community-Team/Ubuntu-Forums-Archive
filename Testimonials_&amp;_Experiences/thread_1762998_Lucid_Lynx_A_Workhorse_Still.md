### Post by ventrical on 2011-05-20
I like the interoperability of all the ubuntu products. I am fortunate to be able to benchmark Ubuntu on several PC laptops and desktops, so , I have the resources to experiment with multiple boots and also legacy systems. For example I can move files and folders from a filesystem mount of Maveric to a running OS of  Natty, Fedora, Mint or any other system. So all I have to do is  just update *one* pendrive and migrate settings and other files to the new system.

The unique thing is that even though *support* may be officially no longer provided for an older version it can still be worked in with the new system and it is also backward compatible.

However , Lucid 10.04 LTS is still a remarkable achievment and I would venture to say that the devs are using  that particular framework as a placeholder while they continue to iron out the bugs with Unity and Gnome 3. And  this is another thing - the diversity of Ubuntu and Linux in general.

  This is to say that if a newer version of  Ubuntu crashes or develops problems for end_users that are having difficulties with the newer learning curves they can always recover their data using a USB to IDE/SATA  ATI Bridge(Cable) or just by having their HOME directory backed up and then take a backstep from, say , Natty to Lucid and just migrate from there by simply dragging the home directory of the crashed install to the new backstepped install.

  This is a kind of failsafe for noobs who are having a difficult time getting orientated from *other* proprietary OS packages that they may have been running for several years to the new Linux/Ubuntu OS distros.

  So, as I see it in this light - Lucid is still a workhorse for noobs (like me too) :)

### Post by ventrical on 2011-05-23
> **rvchari said:**
> no doubt lucid is the best till date. i evolved from jaunty > lucid and now maverick. but lucid live cd is still kept handy as i know that someday i may need to fall back if need arises and lucid will never let us down !

 I mean, Lucid is probably the closest thing to a *system restore* for Linux OSes in general (IMO). Any work done on a new install of a *new* distro such as Natty can always be _moved_ to a stable of Lucid.

ie; If an end_user makes a partition of about 10GBs on a larger drive and installs Lucid on that drive and then they are able to run newer distros (or VBoxes ,etc) on the rest of the hardrive space. 10GB is very large. I have a copy of Lucid on an 8GB pendrive that works just great. 

 Anyways- to the topic- after an new distro is installed on a side by side, and it then subsequently crashes, any and all work can be gleaned from that crashed distro by booting into the Lucid distro from GRUB, dragged to a backup folder and then the end_ user can then wipe the buggy distro, replace it with another and move along. This of course all being done after the questionable distro crash has been mounted while running under the Lucid partition.

 Ubuntu (IMO) and the way it is structured provides end_users with the option of using older, obsoleted harddrives, in unison with pendrives and other removable storage devices, So legacy and cutting edge can work together in *unity* (not the desktop interface:)., but I would presume that Unity will soon be finely intergrated with the Compiz App and all will be well.

 I know there are many tools to explore in the Ubuntu Store that can probably do this a lot more efficiently but to be able to do this manually in the way I described (and for noobs also), it gives any and all a better understanding of the inner workings of Linux/Ubuntu and , personally, I can appreciate it more.

thanks ! :)
