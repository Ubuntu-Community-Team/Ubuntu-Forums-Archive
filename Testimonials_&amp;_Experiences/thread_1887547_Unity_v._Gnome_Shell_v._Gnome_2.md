### Post by photodan on 2011-11-27
I admit it......I'm an old guy (74) who doesn't like change for change's sake. My career was as an auto engineer for GM and, too often, I watched the boys down in marketing destroy a good thing merely for "change." I'm assuming you all know how things are going at GM? They refused to change the important things, you know, like quality! Rather, they always changed the "bling."

I went to Linux about five years ago and started with KDE and, frankly, never liked it (What's with that Plazma desktop thing? No one's ever explained it's purpose, including the folks over at KDE.) So I went to Ubuntu, back around version 8, and I loved it and the Gnome desktop! Finally, a desktop easily negotiated and customizable enough to completely personalize it. By the time I installed Maverick I was converting my friends over from MS to Linux every chance I got. I mean I loved it and most of them did too!

Then, last year, Ubuntu (and the good folks at the Gnome project) made the decision that major changes were in order. They (and many devotees) decided Gnome was getting "old", "tired", "falling behind." Thus, Gnome 3 was born, a desktop designed for modern times. I immediately installed it, dual-booted alongside Maverick. 

What a disappointment! Unity was Microsoft in the extreme, offering no customization and a handsome but inefficient workspace. I hated it! So, I installed Gnome Shell, and found it to be just as off-putting as Unity. Still, I kept 11.10 installed to give it a fair chance. In the past month I've forced myself to stay on the 11.10 side of the dual-boot and, after much soul-searching, settled on Gnome Shell. Why? Because extensions are flying out from those inventive fellows out there who always seem to make a good system better (another reason I love Linux.) With extensions I've just about gotten Gnome Shell customized enough to make me happy. Unity, on the other hand, seems to be lying still, with very little work by the creative "aftermarket" boys. 

Then I went back over to the Maverick side and very quickly found it was still better than Gnome Shell in moving around quickly, with minimum fussing with the mouse or keyboard. I had been missing my Compiz Cube and the ability to flip desktops quickly, simply by using the button on the mouse wheel, allowing me to jup from one open app to another almost instantly. I can find no way to move so quickly in either Gnome Shell or Unity. For me Global Menus are ridiculous. I can see no way it's faster to go from an application all the way up to panel to open and click on the global menus. Silly and time consuming. By the way, I know they can be disabled and I have done so. The disappearing scroll bar is another item that annoyed me and I again found it can be removed, but the ability to make these changes is well-hidden.

Still, these features, if you want to call them that, are symptomatic of what's happening in Gnome. Change for change's sake is wrong (bone-headed in fact.) Hey! You've only to look at my old employer, GM. If Gnome and Ubuntu's decision is to spread the desktop to mobile devices perhaps their marketing stretegy will work. On the other hand, in so doing they're losing a huge base of loyal users, the desktop crowd.

My bottom line is, I'm happily back with Maverick and, in the coming years (whatever years I have left), I'll find other Gnome 2 desktops or, hopefully, Gnome 3 will give users the ability to better customize things. As always, the folks outside of the major projects will do most of the heavy lifting. In the end though, I can't help but think of Gnome and Ubuntu as being frighteningly like Microsoft. You know, "Just do as we tell you. We know what's best for you." Good grief! That sounds just like the boys down in the marketing department at GM!

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-11-28
Ubuntu is changing for new market tastes. To extend your car analogy, they're making the bodywork curvier and adding synchromesh gears, because people like a sleeker looking car and for it to be easier to drive.

Ubuntu has always been a distribution of change, and the computing industry has always been an industry of change.
