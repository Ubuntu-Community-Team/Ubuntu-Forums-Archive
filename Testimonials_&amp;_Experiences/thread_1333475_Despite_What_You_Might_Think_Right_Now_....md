### Post by oldefoxx on 2009-11-21
Yes, despite what you might think right now, Ubuntu can be a wonderful experience for even beginning users to enjoy.  And it is recognized as one of the very best Linux Distributions available today, very user friendly and not too different from Windows, the operating system most PC users have already learned to use to some extent.

But to deal with Ubuntu, or any other operating system that seeks to go beyond Windows, you are faced with getting it, giving it a try, and then deciding to install it.  And the installation of a new operating system can be a bit of a challenge.  So maybe the first thing is to just get Ubuntu and give it a try.  If you are not comfortable with making a CD yourself, someone might be willing to do it for you, and you can get a CD here for just a few dollars, and it will be shipped to you.

Now if you already know how to create your own CDs, then you can just download one of the .ISO images, burn it to a CD, then have your PC boot from that CD.  The option to boot from a CD is usually a setting in the BIOS, which means getting into the Setup mode when your PC is first booting up.  If you are not familiar with this, then maybe some help from someone who is familiar with it would help at this point.

Why would you want to go to the trouble?  Because it represents a whole new wqy to learn to enjoy and use your PC.  Many things that can be done under Windows can also be done under Ubuntu, but Ubuntu gives you many new ways to do things as well, and this is all for free.  Also, Ubuntu makes it easy to bring new life to an older PC and make it useful again.  I don't mean a PC that is broken down, just a PC that is, you know, somewhat old or underpowered.  Ubuntu makes better use of the existing PC, and that can mean being able to use that PC for a longer period of time before having to replace it.  Or, if it has already been replaced, just add new life so that someone else can have and use it.

Most people got their PCs with Windows and other software already installed.  That made it a no-brainer when it came go getting much of the software that now exists on the PC, and the struggle to get that software on your PC is usually handled by the manufacturer before you bought it.  So dealing with that as well is what puts a lot of people off.  But learning to install software is a fairly fundamental task, and the advantage of Ubuntu is that really, it is rather easy.  You only have to make a few choices, and the installer program handles everything else.

I think the real question might be which flavor of Ubungt to use.  There are several flavors of Ubuntu, such as Kubuntu and Xubuntu, and these effect the appearance and maybe some of the functions found in Ubuntu.  This is not a real critical choice, and you can change your mind later if you want.  So maybe just decide to go with Ubuntu as the starting point.

But, each flavor is also marked by release numbers and neat little names.
The nigher the number, the more recent the release.  Now dispite the fact that the most recent release is heavily promoted here and elsewhere, it is also likely still being subject to bug detection and additional fixes.
The slightly older (lower numbered) releases can be more stable and a better choice for beginners.  There is so much involved in creating a release, that it can take months, even a year or more, to get the newest release to the same level of stability.

As an example, the newest releases offered right now are numbered 9.10 and 10.04.  Both still have issues to be resolved.  On this bases, it would probably be better to step back to one of these releases instead:
8.04, 8.10. or 9.04.  Now the method of numbering may seem odd, but the idea is that the .04 marks a significant redesign being introduced, By the time the .10 release comes out, the .04 has been rendered as stable, and the .10 includes all those features and adds a commitment to support it for a perioed of time - 3 years for the individual version, and 5 years for the server version.  Now the difference between the ,04 and the .10 versions is very minor, if the .04 version has been brought up to date with the Update Manager, so many with the .04 version just stay with it rather than try to move up.  It is the individual's choice.  But also note that the .10 will continue to get more updates after the .04 version is effectively discontinued.

How to chose which one?  The answer is, that it is not that critical.  But you can check the forums and see what some of the differencess are, and how people feel about the different releases.  Many people still like and plan to stay with 8.10 for the full 3 years, then think about which one to move on to at that time.  You do not have to keep updating in an effort to keep pace with a product line that is undergoing continual improvement and growth.

What improvements can you expect?  An incredible number over time. I've seen an average of 50 patches and updates come out weekly, because with workers around the world trying to make it all better, there is always something going on.  Now compare that to the very few patches and updates to come out for Windows, where you generally have to wait years, then pay to get a newer version tp replace the old one. It is altogether a different way of doing things, and like all Open Source projects, meant to do more for the people who use it at no real cost to them.

You don't have to be super smart to see how this might work for you, and might even work for both big and small companies.  But you have to get involved in order to learn what is really involved.  I hope you do.  I speak from personal experience when I say this is a great way to go.

Now this is bad news.  I don't have Vista available separately, though I do have Windows 2000 Pro and XP Pro. After that, it is no biggie, but if you are stuck somewhere before that, then trying to deal with Windows shortcomings and limitations can be really frustrating.  Of course you could always just take the PC to a shop somewhere and let them deal with it, or have someone in the know on hand to help you out when you need it.  Almost every flavor of Linux is better in this regard, because they come on LiveCDs, and will just run from the CD, meaning that they are equipped to do many things beyond the reach of Windows.

Second, if you are dealing with Windows, then you will most likely need the install disk and other CDs to deal with many of the types of problems that can come up.  With Linux, just the LiveCD will do, and if you do not have it in hand, you can just download it and burn another CD with it, or tell someone else how to do it.  Get it up and running, and you have the advantage, because the Windows' CD is there in case you have to reinstall or attempt a repair of the operating system.  The other CDs might then be needed to reinstall some of the applications that run under Windows.

If you ever get to the point of having to deal with Windows on this level, and then find out how something similar can be done under Linux, but in an easier manner, you begin to see why some of us could care less if there is a Windows 7 out there, or whether support for XP is dropped or not.  We can manage, and do what we want to do anyway.  All thanks to the presence and choice of Linux, and our willingness to undergo some change as a way of getting free of the rest of it.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2009-12-01
Your description of the version numbers is a little inaccurate.

When a new version of Ubuntu is released, it's the latest stable release. 9.10 is the latest stable release, not 9.04. It's important to note that all versions of Ubuntu have issues that have not been resolved; yes, even the Long Term Support releases, and even 9.04. For instance, the Intel graphics issue in 9.04 will NEVER be resolved in that release.

Also, the ".04" and the ".10" refer to the month of release. Every release that has the letters "LTS" in the name is supported for 3 years on the desktop and 5 years on the server. All others are supported for 18 months.

The current LTS release is 8.04, not 8.10. It's always the first Ubuntu of an even-numbered year (6.06 LTS, 8.04 LTS and 10.04 LTS).
