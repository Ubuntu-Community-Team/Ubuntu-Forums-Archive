### Post by clruwe on 2010-07-27
I am definitely not what some refer to as a "super-user", but I'm also not the "average" user. I am that guy that most of my friends ask me about problems with their computers i.e. I know what a processor is and the difference of that and RAM. I also know about OS and where most things are.... and if not I know how to Google the solution fast and efficient. I couldn't build a computer but I'm usually pretty good at detecting where the problem is e.g memory, hard-drive, etc.  When I was a child, over 20 years ago, my father believed that in the future everyone would be able to PROGRAM their own computers (of course this was before the arrival of windows). Me and my sister use to play with a Radio Shack computer that had no hard-drive, floppy or anything, and anything we programmed had to be stored in tapes (cassette). My father used to refuse to buy us the games we wanted and instead would buy us a manual that would TEACH us how to program the game we wanted, so that we would learn the language.
Just as an anecdote, my family and I used to travel a lot and changed hemispheres more often than I can remember. In one of these changes from 110-120v to 220-240v I managed to burn our infamous radio shack. In fear of my fathers punishment I spent the next 10 years without a computer.... This was unjustified, he laughed when I finally confessed (in my late 20s).

My first experience with a modern computer was in my late teens when I went to visit my father at his office, and since he was busy in a meeting, his secretary said I could play with the computer for a while. This is something that lot of people of my generation should be able to relate to: The black screen came up and I typed the usual "basic" or "binary" stuff that I could still remember... Nothing worked! Then the secretary realized what I was doing and said "no, no... you have to type in win" or something... I was shocked at what happened next! Windows and color showing up all over the place... What the hell happened to telling the computer what to do???
Unfortunately I had to wait until I got married (when I was 30) to get my first 'modern' computer (OS WIn XP), but as a good ex-nerd I took pride in learning everything I could about it (only to find out that this was obviously the work of hundreds of people and many years, thus beyond my grasp).

To cut a long story short: eventually I heard about Linux and its philosophy... being a Pareconist I found that this suited me beautifully, so I gave it a shot.

**The positive:**

I had this horrible Advent laptop with windows vista and decided to sacrifice it for the experiment. I was shocked and impressed at the result: The crap laptop now worked fast and stable and reliable, etc. Of course this was out of the box i.e. as it came. Unfortunately I couldn't get any of the usual software, I need for work, to function on it (Sibelius, Adobe acrobat, sampletank, etc.). I thought it was such a wonderful OS I recommended it to all my friends (That did not require "specialized" software) to change since they would see a MASSIVE improvement on their computers speed/capabilities. I also loved the fact that you could make it look however you wanted. I mean come on. If I have to spend 8 hours in front of this thing at least I need it to suit my eyes, not be imposed by some nerd that knows nothing about design or the rights contrast of colors for long work hours.

**The negative:**

About a year ago I decided to buy an extra hard-drive for my desktop and give Ubuntu another try, I thought to myself: maybe I could have windows for my work and linux for myself i.e. email, movies, music, etc.

For the past year I've read every forum, every trouble-shooting, every how-to I could find. I made a point of not posting simplistic problems and REALLY try to figure out and learn how it works (after all I am the computer-guy amongst my friends). BTW this is my first post ever in Linux forums. I thought if I could learn windows (which is rubbish) I could most certainly learn Linux (which is created by the people for the people).
OK, so accuse me of being dim! Or maybe I was pampered by Windows for too long! I do not understand 99% of the forums I visit... What language are they speaking??? When friends and acquaintances would say to me "But isn't Linux for computer nerds?" I would say "I don't think so, is still a windows based system, thus for the normal user". Man was I wrong... Let's say you want a simple thing like like installing a media player, so you Google: best media player for Ubuntu. Then you get VLC, but the VLC you install via Software-center is out of date and full of bugs! To get the latest version I personally spent 3 hours googling and reading forums. When I finally got back to my friends about it I found out that they had (sadly) returned to Windows.

This is just one example of many. Another very common problem, that I have experienced myself, is the slow transfer of large files to external USB hard-drives, never solved it by the way, read everything but I just could not understand what they meant. Fortunately I have come across forums of people like me that are getting advice by super-users and you can really sense their frustration when they don't get it. Things like: "just sudo apt-get....!" How in god's name would anybody understand that that means: go to Accessories>Terminal type in the command, give your password, etc. Most people are freaked out by just the word "command".

I started by reading everything I could from the "absolute beginners talk" most of it is OK, but then again there's so much that is just beyond anybody that doesn't work with computers e.g. the normal user = 99.5% of computer users.

**In conclusion:**

The Linux philosophy is a beautiful one, I personally support it and encourage it. But in order to reach the masses a big change of "language" needs to take place. I recently did a complete overhaul of all my business computer systems (I am the guy that does all that in my company which is devoted to writing music, not software): as before every computer has dual boot and we have upgraded to Ubuntu 10.04 64 bit and Windows 7 64 bit. Windows went down like a charm! We used Windows "Easy Transfer" and was surprised how easy and how much data was transmitted to the new computer. And we used tar cvpzf backup.tgz for the Ubuntu systems. I'll say: It was a nightmare!!! We had all sorts of problems: 1- the computer would not reboot once the restore had taken place. 2.- We tried about 6 times with different file exceptions (.dev, .etc, .media, none of them worked! Eventually what worked was a manual transfer of all files and settings (I did bother to read every forum and how-to out there) which took about three days of man-time and lost work hours, whereas windows took a few hours with no human intervention (a few minutes to click on the right buttons).

**In closing:**

Now please understand, I am an absolute supporter of this system. But as it stand I cannot recommend it to anyone. Windows wins not because of its propaganda or marketing strategies, in fact around this area (northeast of England) when you go buy a computer they do give you the option if you want Linux.  It wins because its support language is aimed at the most ignorant, inept and therefore AVERAGE user out-there, in the sens of: "go there and click this, you'll see a thing that looks like so and so... click on that".
I love computers and I love how human-kind has been able to create such amazing machines that are able to do so many impressive tasks, but Linux developers and supporters must understand that most of humanity is not like me.... They need something and they need it to work now and in the right way! They do not share our sense of awe...
In the word of C.K. Lewis "this is the most amazing time to be alive but it's wasted on the worst generation ever!" [http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rOtEQB-9tvk](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rOtEQB-9tvk)
Please learn to speak English to us and not computerglish....
