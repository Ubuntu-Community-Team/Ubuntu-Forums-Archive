### Post by Alterax on 2007-06-11
One of the biggest complaints that I have heard about Ubuntu usually is that the one doing the complaining asserts that he or she "has work to do".  This is demeaning, if you really think about it.  This assertion gives the impression that Ubuntu users have no jobs, and if so, nothing so complex as to actually require that the computer work dependably.

I am not one of those mythical users with tons of free time.  I work full-time for one company, own my own business on the side as a systems consultant, and on top of that, I am a full time student at the local University.  My own Linux experiences started roughly a year ago.

My reasons:  My father dared me to.  I was teasing him about his Mac laptop; I was pretty much a Windows-only guy at the time.  His response:  That there was a lot more out there than just Windows.  I could make a great living doing that, but I would be limiting myself.

So I took his advice:  Got a mac, loved it.  Got very proficient, and told him about it.  His response?  Now try Linux!

So I did.  I went for about three months with a Mandriva 2007 install using the KDE desktop.  Then my mate had gotten the bug and tried Ubuntu.  After watching for a bit (and fighting with dependencies and looking for RPMs,) I took the plunge with Ubuntu Edgy.

Oddly enough, it didn't detract from my skills as a Windows technician.  But what it did do was grant me a better understanding of what is going on behind the surface of the Windows systems I administer, because it gave me a new perspective on what exactly an OS consists of.

The transition was quick; I was still able to do most of my college work right off the bat.  Modifications to my Ubuntu (programs, et cetera) now makes it 100% compatible with my schoolwork.  

Then there is my own work:  Most of my clients for my own business are NFPOs (Not For Profit Organizations).  These usually work with quite a limited budget, but their needs are just as demanding as your standard small to mid-size businesses.  Linux, to them, meant being able to extend the life of their computer hardware, it meant inexpensive and reliable fileservers, establishing VPN connections between various sites, and stable, dependable workstations with everything needed to get their jobs done.  It also meant a more secure environment, with less downtime due to viruses and "user indulgences".

The transition at the company I work full-time with happened first when my work PC crashed.  I asked my boss if I could attempt using Linux instead.  He laughed and said to be his guest, XP can be installed via RIS if I changed my mind.    

Since that day, they've seen changes too, just like I showed for the NFPOs I contract out to.  Redundant systems reduce the risk of lost business.  No software licensing fees, with the exception of one program we currently have to use with WINE API.  I ended up deploying a proxy server and a NIDS (Network Intrusion Detection System) using leftover hardware destined for the trashbin--cutting down on bandwidth consumption (Current statistics are 35% less than baseline taken the month before implementation; average is sitting at twenty-six percent less) and having an indispensable tool for detecting access attempts and malware activity.  In reducing the hardware costs (and upkeep even further), we've started using LTSP with thin clients--making our production floor nearly 100% paperless, and MUCH cheaper than putting out Windows boxes both in immediate deployment AND in maintenance (lower TCO- Total Cost of Ownership)  Adding to this, we've started using Moodle for e-training, and are moving legacy databases from old MS Access 2.0 formats (always trouble) into custom LAMP applications.   So has taking the time to learn Linux cut from my productivity?  I think not--and I know for a fact there is an undiscovered country of software out there that can take our company even closer to the cutting edge, just waiting to be found.

Does it "Just work?"  In all fairness, sometimes.  Likewise, Windows systems seem to have the same trouble.  Both systems require a far greater amount of work to prepare them for the end user, but the Linux systems seem to require a lot less once you get them up and going.  And Ubuntu has made this even easier by having everything within easy reach, including a forum full of great people jumping at the chance to help out.

So there's my Ubuntu testimonial.  I have work to do:  As a full-time student, an entrepreneur, and as a full-time MIS, I definitely have more than enough to deal with.  Ubuntu just made it much easier to do it!

--Alterax
