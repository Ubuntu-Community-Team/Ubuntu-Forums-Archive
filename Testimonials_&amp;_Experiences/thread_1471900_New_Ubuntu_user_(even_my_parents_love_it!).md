### Post by Blacksheep01 on 2010-05-03
Hi all, I had never installed a Linux distro in my life until last week. I've been building my own PCs (always Windows based of course) and repairing other people's PCs for the last 7 years so I'm PC savvy to a degree. I was always curious about Linux but because I'm a gamer, never made the plunge myself, but I'm incredibly impressed with Ubuntu after just one week of use and here is how I got there!

Last week my parents got a trojan on their XP Pro box so severe that I really struggled to root it out. HiJack this and ComboFix actually failed to root it out on two passes and all my other tricks proved fruitless. I later discovered it was a trojan disguising itself as firefox files, but this being about the 20th severe virus they had gotten, I decided it was probably time for a reformat anyway as that hard drive had taken a beating. My first thought was to buy a copy of Windows 7, hope that it had better security and load it with system clogging anti-malware to protect them. However, later that night, something struck me. I would check to see which Linux distros appeared to be most usable/compatible and install it on their system, hoping that they would like and I could set it up.

At the time, just three days before 10.04, Karmic is what I installed, oblivious to 10.04 being around the corner. It formatted and loaded on their hard drive in 20-30 minutes, something that impressed me incredibly. The next thing that impressed me is that all the internal hardware just worked. I didn't have to do anything, it found everything, asked me to install some extra drivers for the graphics card etc. and I was off running.

I got their firefox up, thunderbird and I even installed Wine to see if I could get my dad's beloved Poker games (Full Tilt & Poker Stars) working. At first no luck, but then I got Poker Stars up and running without a hitch in Wine. When 10.04 came out, I upgraded, but wasn't happy with all the mistakes I made messing with Karmic, so I reformatted again and was even more amazed to see 10.04 loaded so fast the loader screen no longer appears! lol.

After scanning the forums here I got their printer up and that sealed the deal for them, they are using it on their own without calling me, a first, because in Windows they always did! Full Tilt still doesn't work right in Wine, but I'm working on it

Anyway, as a lifelong Windows user, I am amazed at how well Ubuntu works and looks. It found everything by itself, no mobo driver disks, no Ethernet adapter drivers, no separate graphic card drivers to search the net for, it was all there. Installing packages of necessary updates also impressed the hell out of me. I loaded their .mp3s and .mp4s and Ubuntu detected them, asked me if I wanted the codecs and auto loaded them. Windows has never done that, I always need to install Community Codec Pack! lol

The look of Ubuntu is also amazing. I have never thought a desktop GUI something beautiful to look at but with Ubuntu 9.10 and 10.04 I have literally just stared at it, scrolling through the menus, clicking on the software acquisition tool, swapping workspaces, it is a work of tremendous beauty in both form and function.

The only thing stopping me from completely ditching Windows for my own PCs is that I am a lifelong PC gamer and Linux just cannot play all of my games. I know Wine handles some as well as that paid version of Wine, but I would be at a loss without my games. I will be dual booting Linux though. My only hope is that in the future, developers make more games that work in Linux, maybe that rumored Steam Client will lead the way. 

In my opinion, the lack of gaming is the only thing holding Ubuntu (or other Linux distros) back, everything else, IMO, is a near flawless piece of work. I can't say enough how impressed both me and my parents are with this OS. The best thing ever was when my mom asked me if she still had to delete every single e-mail, even from friends, that had an attachment for fear of a virus and I responded"no, you aren't going to get a virus in Linux, ever." :D She was so happy with that response. You guys are life changers here, keep it up.

### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-05-05
Thanks for your comments- well written, I'm glad you finally see just how amazing open source really is. Steam will be here soon, things will get better, vendor support is always growing more and more, rarely do vendors withdraw support after initially giving it. Linux is a growing market, and worth getting your foot in the door with.

I must agree on the comment that gaming is the only real thing holding it back. Wine is great, but it's not perfect JUST YET. It will probably be seamless and fully compatible some day, but not today.

However, for most newer computers, running applications like Adobe CS and Microsoft Office in VirtualBox (in seamless mode, for example) isn't a big deal and doesn't have a noticeable slowdown. Even computers with only  1 GB of RAM seem to run Photoshop CS 3 well in VirtualBox.

So really, the only thing that people won't sacrifice a Virtual Machine for is games, because speed and effects are all that count. So if it's not native, or it's slower in Wine (many games are faster, but again, this isn't the case for all of them), then your only real option is Windows.

However, I would encourage you to use Wine, since the tweaking isn't always extensive, and many programs don't require any tweaking. Even if you can only play 3 of your favorite games in Linux, that's more time you don't have to boot into Windows.

Dual-booting is rarely a bad idea, since you get a wide variety of software from both worlds. If you don't want to support Microsoft at all, that's a different subject, and 20 percent of Windows users aren't supporting Microsoft with their cash, anyway. But I assume you aren't letting the morals get ahead of the functionality, here.

Like I said, you don't have to cut Microsoft out of your life, but you don't have to use Windows exclusively, either. Getting on Windows could be just like getting on your PS3.

Also, if all you use your Windows partition for is gaming, and those games don't require a network connection, then you're pretty much set with security- just disable it. Even if you are playing a networked game, the passages for you to potentially be invaded by malware in Windows will be greatly decreased, and you can streamline your Windows partition just for that purpose.

So you see, even when Linux doesn't give you all your Windows-exclusive games, it gives you more options for how to use that partition more efficiently. I'll repeat myself; dual-booting, with enough disk space, is never a bad idea.
