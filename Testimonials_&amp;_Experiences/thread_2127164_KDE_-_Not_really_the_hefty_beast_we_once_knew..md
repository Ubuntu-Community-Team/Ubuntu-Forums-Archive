### Post by Roasted on 2013-03-19
Recent frustrations and/or recent curiosity has sparked interest in trying out different desktop environments. I spent two weeks solid on all of the major desktop environments in an effort to find one besides Unity that stuck with me. While there are a tremendous amount of excellent environments out there (seriously, we Linux users are spoiled), I found myself more at home with KDE. KDE felt like a good place because it has a ton of configurability. With very little effort I can make KDE act more like Gnome Shell, Unity, Gnome 2.X, etc. There are a ton of ways to manipulate the interface to what you want to see. I actually have mine laid out much like Unity with a touch of Gnome Shell. Bar on the side, Homerun launcher in the upper left (resembles the Unity dash), along with a hot corner to present all windows to me like in Gnome 3, although my hot corner is the upper right. Ahh, the freedom of choice. On top of that, the act of configuring everything is extremely easy. Some other environments require third party software, an array of extensions, or even extensive terminal work to do some of the simplest things.

In the past I've always matched up the appropriate desktop environment with the hardware in that particular system. My least powered system is actually a CR48 netbook from the initial ChromeOS trial, which we all know is a single core Intel Atom N455 with 2GB of RAM and a 16GB SSD, which some have referenced as a "super slow disk" due to the fact this SSD is no where in the same ball park in terms of speed as your average SSD is today. I have tried every major environment on it, but each one choked. Some were marginally "okay" to use, while others were struggling over the simplest thing. I ended up putting LXDE on it as a last resort and still grew frustrated of the lack of speed I got out of that system. This is a laptop that will choke even on full screen YouTube videos. I ended up putting it on the shelf with no intention of using it again.

On a weird hunch I decided to try Kubuntu 12.10 and KDE 4.10. I decided to give it a shot since I noticed a pretty substantial speed improvement with KDE 4.10 over KDE 4.9 with the other systems I tried. To my surprise, this CR48 is actually a very usable system. Now, to be fair, it's a single core Atom, so it's hardly a powerhouse to handle video encoding while simultaneously Gimp'ing a massive array of professionally shot photographs. But I do have to say, KDE 4.10's speed on this laptop is actually... quite good. Even my desktop effects work, which was a decent surprise.

We live in a world with countless environments and we should all be thankful for that instead of being cutthroat against one or the other. Each environment is a tool, and you have to use the right tool for the job. I feel confident that dropping KDE in front of most anybody out there will prove to be a wise move for users on all levels, as you'll be able to run with it as-is or configure it to a layout you'd much rather have with relative ease. The fact it can run rather decently on this laptop leads me to believe that the KDE devs have been working some massive overtime, because no other OS/distro I've tried has worked worth a damn on this laptop. This is a sweet deal to me because I have no other personal laptop that I own (only have work laptops). With our first on the way and budgets tight, it's nice to breathe new life into this laptop since buying a new laptop right now isn't in the cards. 

Considering that even recent threads I read have suggested that to run KDE you need a mid-range gaming computer I felt compelled to share my experience. If you have the time, give 4.10 a chance. You may be as pleasantly surprised as I was.

Well done, devs.

### Post by Roasted on 2013-03-21
> **MadmanRB said:**
> I suggest using the kubuntu backports PPA, as its much safer in  the long run.

That's what I did with KDE when I tested it for this thread. My KDE testing was done on Kubuntu 12.10 64 bit, with the backports PPA bringing KDE to version 4.10 . 

KDE 4.10 is undoubtedly the best KDE I've ever used, but there's one gotcha from the thread author... me. I don't want to turn this thread down a this vs that road but I found myself spending more time in Gnome 3.6 lately. I found myself spending countless amounts of time customizing KDE, which is great, don't get me wrong, but I never did hit that sweet spot, where on the other hand I felt like Gnome brought that polished sweet spot to the table immediately. But hey, looks aren't everything, which brings me to the other night... when I was working on my Kubuntu powered work laptop and I had a project to get done with for the morning, but I ran into a series of plasma crashes when tweaking sound settings and a few microphone-related show stoppers. The issues were the same on different systems so I knew it was software related. Ultimately I was running on fumes with 2 hours of sleep and fired up Gnome to get the job done in minutes. This caused me to reconsider time in Gnome 3 land which has proven to sync with my work flow quite nicely while still running decently on my considerably low powered gear.

At the end of the day we have to use what works best for you. I spent 2 week chunks testing out various desktop environments and Gnome hooked me back in for multiple reasons. Most importantly I think we're all in a very interesting time now in the Linux/*buntu world. At one point we had three pretty dang new environments. KDE 4, Unity in 11.04, and Gnome 3.0. All three were terrible. Now we're spoiled with those same three environments maturing and they're all very solid. The latest Unity coming in 13.04 is pretty dang fast, KDE 4.10 is quite a pleasure to use, and Gnome 3.6 is lean and beautiful. As far as KDE in particular goes, 4.10 is undoubtedly the best KDE so far. 

I encourage everybody out there to try all of them out. I have found each one to have pros, cons, good, bad, etc. I spent a considerable amount of time doing my tests to come to these conclusions. I recommend everybody else do the same.
