### Post by Aquabuntu on 2007-05-07
Warning: This is pretty long. Read it, don't read it...

I've always been keen to go OSS, the dev model is more collaborative and as a dev you know full well the world is watching. Not the average user world either, some highly intelligent experts will be grading your work. Takes a lot of guts to do that and I take my hat off to everyone involved. :cool: 

Also, I like the philosophy, dislike that other OS and the ideology behind the whole proprietary software machine. Basically a whole buncha reasons.

But always been too brainwashed and nervous to try. Then, a while back I got hold of 4.10 CD and was so chuffed because I could have a peek without committing. Booted live, hardware support issues :( 
But, it kept on bugging me and I kept on browsing sites and articles. But, I always left bewildered at the amount of choice and the learning curve intimidated me. Then last week I downloaded Feisty and decided to give it a bash (pun intended) over the weekend.

Always thought I was pretty tech savvy, or rather savvy enough for my needs. Well, I have had what I can only describe as an awakening this weekend!

First, I used a tutorial I had found and successfully slipsteamed XP install CD with SP2 and then customised it to load my SATA drivers direct from the CD and to perform an unattended install. Admit it was tricky, and I have 2 new shiny coasters, BUT, I did it!!! Then I went ahead to try and install Ubuntu on a dual boot, was too hasty with my partitions and had to delete one which then broke Ubuntu and had to reinstall. Then was trying to mount one of the logic partitions in my ext part for /home and managed to change the disk label not realising what I was doing ](*,) Back to the beginning again. But I persevered and now have a working dual boot \\:D/ 

During all this I made a few posts on the forums for help and then after some reading realised what a typical, lazy Windows user I had become because all the info is already there. You just need to look. As a result I now properly understand how hard drive partitioning works and I only had a vague idea before. I also felt embarassed that I had just waded in without taking the time to read the stickies BEFORE posting and realised how I must have looked to the regulars on the forums.

This really got me going and I have been hatching plans in my head about setting up my previous PC for my son, also with a dual boot, so that he can learn from the beginning how to use both OS's. Then I started to think about how I can use my first PC (Pentium III) which was just rotting, and install Ubuntu server for internet connection for his and my PC's because it would be perfectly adequate for that purpose using Ubuntu.

Then today, I read this excellent article ( [http://pcmech.com/article/windows-to-ubuntu-transition-guide/](http://pcmech.com/article/windows-to-ubuntu-transition-guide/) ) which explains how to setup a web server on your box to share files with the world at large and also how to setup your own music to stream over the net so I can listen to my own music collection anywhere in the world. I started to realise the possibilities available to me that weren't before.

I haven't been this excited about my computer for such a long time!!! I realise that despite my best intentions I had become brainwashed and lazy. Used to being fed whatever someone else wanted me to eat. Over 1 weekend all of that has changed!!!!

Then I got disappointed because I only have a net connection on my work notebook using a 3G PCMCIA card and according to one of the articles in the forum it will be hard to do much without one. But, now I'm just thinking that this will force me to do things the hard way until I do get my ADSL connection and I'll probably learn a lot more than doing it the normal way.

It's really hard to describe how I feel. It's like I had become jaded about one of the biggest passions in my life and all the old excitement and thrill has just been restored in the space of 2 days.

I'm also so proud that one of my fellow South Africans had a part to play by founding Ubuntu and now this is being shared to add to all the hard work already done by the rest of the world in making Linux evolve from strength to strength. I also can't wait to get stuck in so that I can become experienced enough to help other new users from around the world.

I'm really chuffed to finally be part of a community that I have admired from afar for so long!
