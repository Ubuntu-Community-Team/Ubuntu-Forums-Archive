### Post by Xero Xenith on 2011-05-05
I recently made the switch from a hardcore Ubuntu preacher to a more reasonable human being, and these are my thoughts :P

If you find there are considerable annoyances with Windows, by all means switch.
If you think you have the time and interest to try something new, by all means try a Wubi install or dual-boot for a month or so and see what happens.
If neither of these apply, don't bother.

I use Ubuntu because everything I do day-to-day works just fine, and it's improving as we speak.

My favourite features - the ones that keep my on Ubuntu - are the fully integrated update systems, the app store, the lack of viruses, the beauty and the cleanliness. On my Ubuntu screen (default install), I have very few items and only one tiny toolbar which integrates with the window bar. On Windows there is much more.

On Windows I have to deal with every piece of software trying to update itself - Ubuntu does this for me.
On Windows I have to deal with antivirus software (which can be more annoying than the viruses) - Ubuntu doesn't need that.
Windows is nice, but doesn't look as nice and isn't as customisable as my Ubuntu install.

And if there's something I want to do in Windows, I can just start it in a virtual machine and be there within 5 seconds!

That's my experiences anyway. It's worth the switch, but only if you have the time and interest. :)

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-05-06
Windows XP is truly ancient; it represents the pinnacle of what they could do with computers in 2001. It's also decidedly complex to deal with when you are administering the system.

Ubuntu is a modern operating system. On some Windows control panels, you can get into three nested Properties windows; on Ubuntu the control panels are designed so you might have one or two uncluttered settings windows. Ubuntu is made to do what people want to do with their computers TODAY, rather than what they used to do in a less-connected world. For instance, you can search for and install software right from your desktop, without having to go trawling all over the internet to find what you need.

Ubuntu has sane defaults, and is simplicity itself to keep running. The UI allows for quick and easy searching of installed programs, and orders your files by recently-used. It's also a lot better looking than Windows, and there is a bigger range of free software to do what you want.

Ubuntu changes very quickly, it's quite exciting in this way. There's new things and new features every six months. If you'd like to be excited about new features that you can get for free, then Ubuntu is for you. If you want to stay with exactly the same EVERYTHING for years and years, then Windows XP is for you.

In short, you'll find yourself doing MORE with your computer with Ubuntu, and you'll probably enjoy your computing more.

One final point: Ubuntu's more modern nature does mean that it has a tighter security system than Windows XP. When you're setting up your system or installing software, you WILL be asked for your user password. Get used to this; not only does this help make Ubuntu one of the more secure desktop OSes you can have on your computer, but all other operating systems are moving towards the Ubuntu-style model of security. It's all part of change, and in computing you really have to embrace change.

### Post by aphatak on 2011-05-06
The reason *I* switched over to Ubuntu from Windows was partly money, and partly a feeling of being taken for granted.  Way back in 2007, I had seven computers in my home, all running Windows XP Pro.  I was being asked $1,259.93 for upgrading to Windows Vista - to say nothing of the money I'd have to spend on upgrading my software - such as MS Office - and beefing up my hardware.  And this would have lasted for two years - Microsoft was already gushing about Windows 7, so I would have to fork over another similar sum of money again.  And all this while I'd be paying my annual tax - er, subscriptions, for anti-virus software.

What I was getting for all this money didn't look very attractive to me.  Transparent windows?  Windows that fade?  Widgets and sidebars that encroach on my screen real-estate?  Colored *ribbons* (for my software, not for my pigtails)?

I didn't like the idea of being *told* to upgrade, and having to jump to it.  I tried Fedora, Ubuntu and OpenSUSE as a replacement for Windows.  And it worked for me.  I found all the software I needed under GPL, and I didn't have to convert any data.  Ubuntu can read and write Windows NTFS partitions, and OpenOffice could work with MS Office files.  In fact, if I edited a Word, Excel, or Powerpoint file and sent it to my colleagues who used Microsoft Office, they wouldn't notice any difference.  On top of that, some people who had Office 2000 could not open *.docx* and *.pptx* files, which OpenOffice could without as much as a pause!  And I didn't have to pay for and install anti-virus software.

Finally I settled on Ubuntu, mainly because Fedora's security was almost paranoid, and a pain in the place that daylight never reaches.

And there is a bonus.  If there is a feature you want, or a bug that is biting you, imagine what would happen with Windows.  You enter your request in a bug-tracking system.  Periodically, a committee walks through the bugs and request database, and then prioritizes - decide which bugs would get fixed and which features would get added in the next update.  There is quite a good chance that my request would be 'deferred to the next major upgrade' (which is the current euphemism for '*forget it, buddy*').  In the Linux world, say a hundred thousand people are using the application, of which a thousand get bitten by the bug.  Now, because the source code is freely available, there is a pretty good chance that at least one of this crowd (a) is irritated enough to want to do something about it, (b) has the knowledge, and (c) actually works out a fix and publishes it - usually there are several.  What I found was, nine times out of ten, the bug that was bugging me was already fixed by someone, and all I had to do was download a patch and apply it.

Finally, there are forums like this.  I have been lucky enough to be able to help some folks solve their problems, and have had significant help from other forum members.  There is obviously no money in it, but the feeling of satisfaction one gets out of solving a problem for someone halfway across the world is more than enough of a reward.

I still sometimes use Windows - for example, my employer's intranet site checks for (and works *only if*) I am accessing it from a specific version of Internet Explorer.  I have no smoldering hate for Windows or Microsoft - in fact I admire Bill Gates for his vision and smartness, to say nothing of his philanthropy, and I respect Microsoft for developing some nifty software.  But when it comes to my computers, they run Ubuntu.

That was quite a speech, now, wasn't it?  Well, that's *my* reason to use Ubuntu.
