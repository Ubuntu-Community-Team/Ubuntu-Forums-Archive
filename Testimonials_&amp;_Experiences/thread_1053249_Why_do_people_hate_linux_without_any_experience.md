### Post by Alterax on 2009-01-29
Going back to the original topic, I've been gauging people's reactions to Linux in my area.  What I've noticed is that children seem to have no issue with using Linux (my nephew and nieces absolutely love my mother's Ubuntu system--and it's not even Internet-enabled), Mac OS, or for that matter Windows.  I also get the same reaction from older adults that don't use computers regularly.

So where do we get the knee-jerk reaction from the remaining people?  Most have never heard of Linux.  Most have dealt only with Windows, and they have come to believe that is what a computer is "supposed" to run.  It's the people that have already had to go through the hazing of being a "noob" and learning the ins and outs of a particular environment that tend to polarize against anything else.  Deep-down, they feel that they will be made out to be idiots if they can't be immediately as proficient with a new OS as they are with an existing one.

On top of this, there's also the matter of the status symbol.  Having the latest and [allegedly] greatest is usually sought after by people that want to give others the impression that they can afford the latest and greatest.  Linux throws that a real monkey wrench in that line of thinking; I may not be able to shell out almost a thousand bucks to get the new MS Office on Vista Ultimate, but when it comes to the bulk of Linux distributions, there's no socioeconomic barrier that prevents the mail clerk from running the exact same software that the CEO in the corner office uses.  As illogical as it may be, there is a general consensus that if something is free, it is inferior.  (Note that this doesn't seem to apply to stolen software, such as pirated copies of Windows and MS Office, which from the consumer's perspective ARE free.)

Another reason is that although we have had GNOME, KDE, and XFCE (among many others) for a very long time, Linux has very recently come to a point that puts it on equal footing with Windows when it comes to an intuitive GUI, not to mention hardware support.  

And the final reason is that unlike Windows, we don't seem to have nearly as much interaction with the non-IT world.  We get very little press (except for perhaps through the news covering uninformed teachers taking students' install disks as contraband, or whiny laptop-buyers that didn't do their research and got upset that they got exactly what they purchased).  We simply do not have the visibility, and THAT is what is keeping the public demand down at this point.
