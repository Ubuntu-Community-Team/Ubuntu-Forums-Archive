### Post by UnrealMiniMe on 2011-08-05
> **FormatSeize said:**
> But Ubuntu is as easy as it gets. It doesn't seem as if you have a good grasp of what difficult actually is as it applies to GNU/Linux. 
 
File systems don't corrupt themselves. They have better things to do. If your filesystem becomes corrupt, then something changed, and that change came from user input. There can't possibly be a GUI for every possible human error to be made in GNU/Linux, because there are far too many. Probably millions. 
 
Too, being able to download a GUI that can fix the problem for everything is folly. I'm a firm believer that if someone has a system, they should ideally learn their system. That's probably why so many people have problems with viruses. There's something wrong due to user input, or they think something's wrong, so they immediately download the first third-party, homebrew application on the web, and it turns out to just impregnate their system with a virus inside of another virus. But they are attractive because there are buttons to press and dialogue boxes to interact with while they are infecting their system whilst they think they are fixing it.
 
With a command-line that people seem to cower away from (I don't know why--all you have to do is copy/paste), you are fixing your system at the system level. Isn't that the optimal way to go about it?

The biggest problem with the command line is that to use it, you must either rely on your memory or someone else's...and not everyone has a good memory, let alone a memory good enough for the slew of command line utilities.

You can usually use a -h flag to get a five page list of options (which are important, and which are esoteric?), but that's only if you know which program you're looking for in the first place.  Even if you know the utility you need, you still need to remember that utility's strange-*** abbreviated name.  ifup?  ifdown?  ifupdownaroundthecorner?  Oh, wait, "if" stands for "interface," when it's usually used as a conditional.  I'd MUCH rather use something like Network Manager, which does all the dirty work behind the scenes and lets me switch networks at the press of a button.  It keeps me from remembering if I left out some command or made a mistake that will take hours to research the fix for.

The nice thing about GUI apps is not that they have a big clickable "OK, GO!  WHEE!" button.  The nice thing about GUI apps is that they allow for user exploration:  You may not know how to do something, but if you have a GUI at your disposal with a bunch of menus, you can navigate through them and figure things out...and you can often get visual feedback about the results of your action.  **Unlike the command line, GUI's help you help yourself.**  I'd MUCH rather use GParted to reorganize my partitions than research command line options and pray I didn't miss something that will corrupt all of my data.  Unfortunately, that's not quite an option, since GParted can't deal with LUKS/dmcrypt.

If you use a particular command line application a lot, or if you want to batch process something, then yes, command lines are FTW.  I'd never even consider using a GUI for video encoding; x264's CLI is too good for that.  It required a bit of time to learn the options and remember them though...and for someone doing a task infrequently, the time investment is not always worth it.

I understand the sentiment that most people do not know or understand the "rules of computer common sense."  For instance, you'd think that most people would know better than to open up unexpected email attachments by now.  However, it is completely unreasonable to expect everyone to know how their computer works as a system:  **To most, computers are tools, not a hobby.**  For most people, their hobbies are elsewhere...and if messing with computers is not your hobby or profession, and your time is valuable to you, you're not going to spend it learning how to fix problems with the command line.

**Could you imagine if someone told you that if you use a car or microwave or TV, it's your responsibility to know the machine inside and out and understand everything about how it works?**  I have a vague idea about how they work, but I wouldn't have the slightest clue about "debugging" them!  That's the domain of other people, with other hobbies, interests, and professions.  I just want to use them to accomplish something, which is exactly how most people feel about computers.

Anyone coming here comparing Ubuntu to Windows in a disfavorable light is asking for trouble, but the truth is that they do expect it to work without a fuss, and they SHOULD.  No, they didn't pay a dime for it, but Canonical is nevertheless marketing Ubuntu as an easy-to-use Linux distribution that's perfect for converting Windows users.  Hype creates expectations.  When the marketing hype seems to oversell the product, can you really blame disappointed users for grumbling?  Nobody's paying money for Ubuntu, but it's not marketed as "buyer beware;" it's marketed as fun and easy, and people DO invest their time based on the promise of a world where everyone can be free of Windows...not just computer geeks who know Linux inside and out.

Don't get me wrong; I love Ubuntu, and I love Linux (sorry, Stallman; if you had chosen a name that didn't start with two butt-ugly consonants strung together without an intervening vowel, I might be more tempted to say GNU more often ;)).  I had a rough start a few years back with getting my wireless to work (that's the WORST, when your only way of connecting to the Internet - and outside help - is broken), and I've had other technical issues and hiccups in the past, but on the whole I'm so glad to use something other than Windows (most of the time).  Getting Ubuntu up and running on my hardware has been easy for many releases now, so the kind of things I grumble about are a lot less serious (like the fact that my Firefox is FUBAR this week, but I don't want to upgrade to 11.04 due to fix it due to power usage issues and Unity, and I'd really love a true rolling release...blah.  It's probably my extensions anyway, but back to the rolling release:  I used Debian Testing some time ago, but the freeze was annoying, and it sucks when bugfixes take forever to get included because another bug was fixed within 10 days.  Maybe when Debian Rolling comes around, they'll experiment a bit more with that policy and/or overrides.  I'd use aptosid, but I hate the idea of dropping to a full-screen terminal for frequent upgrades, and I'd panic if I couldn't get into X.  Anyway, with all of that ranting, note that I didn't once consider going back to Windows. :p)

So, I love Linux.  I do keep a terminal open almost all the time for the random things I do know how to do...but I'm more of a hobbyist than most people, and I STILL have no clue what to do if something goes wrong (besides come here ;)).  I feel impotent looking at lines of abbreviated commands with abbreviated options flags.  Messing with basic computer settings with a command line doesn't come naturally to me (which changes are temporary, which are permanent, and which will I need the CLI again tomorrow to micromanage and fiddle with again?).  I greatly prefer navigability and effortless visual feedback (instead of thinking, "Did it work?  Ah, crap, what went wrong?").  GUI's are a godsend for basic troubleshooting and system management, and Ubuntu has helped push the Linux desktop forward in that regard.  It's just a shame that we still have some catching up to do.

Having a problem with the CLI is NOT just a matter of, *"It's different, and I don't know it already, so I don't like it.  Boo, hoo, hoo, I'm butthurt that I'm a noob now."* ;)  It's a matter of, *"Entering magic text commands into a blank screen is something I cannot possibly learn by myself without outside help or a reference manual."*  The CLI has tremendous inherent strengths for batching and automation, but it is inherently more unforgiving, difficult to learn, and high-maintenance.  This is the fundamental nature of the interface, completely divorced from past user experience.

I didn't become semi-competent at Windows by memorizing commands from a manual or copy/pasting from the Internet (I didn't even HAVE an Internet connection).  I became semi-competent at Windows by finding the IRQ settings in a GUI when I was 11 and screwing everything up royally just to make a joystick work...and I ended up figuring out how to do everything on my own, without outside help, thanks to ubiquitous GUI's...even when my mouse of all things was no longer working.  You simply cannot "learn through exploration" like that with a CLI interface, and that is my problem with it.  The CLI is great when you already know what to do, but lacking a GUI is crippling when you don't.

That's not to say a GUI even works for every problem domain, but when it does, it's a much better "learn to help yourself" option...and if all it takes for a monkey to manage a Windows system is learning three graphical apps, the same kind of GUI's would work wonders for letting monkeys manage a Linux system without help as well. ;)

I'm not saying, *"Let's make things like Windows, because I'm familiar with Windows."*  Windows has lots of problems both internally and in terms of user interface, and Ubuntu is far better on the whole.  Whenever I have to use Windows, I'm shocked by how much HARDER most things are to do in Windows, and I'm shocked by how intrusive and bothersome the OS is.  That doesn't mean we have to pretend that memorizing CLI commands is as easy or intuitive as navigating a GUI.

The CLI vs. GUI debate is becoming more and more academic, now that using a GUI is an option for almost everything in Linux distributions.  This is a clear improvement over years past...but it's not thanks to CLI diehards who insist the CLI should be good enough for everyone.  It's thanks to all the "whiners" who prefer inherently learnable GUI interfaces (both users and developers).  There are only a few gaps left, but there's nothing wrong with pointing them out (or justifying past improvements).  You don't become the best or most user-friendly by focusing on your strengths and rationalizing your shortcomings; you become the best by addressing your shortcomings head on.

> **beew said:**
> How do you fix registry or internet problems by putzing around with the gui?

What kind of registry problems are you talking about?  They're generally not the kinds of problems that seriously inhibit the functioning of your computer anyway, so if you're looking for the name of a key to correct some variable, you can typically afford to trawl the net for it.  If you really want to though, you can always just browse regedit, unless you need to add a key (and know the magic text name).  If you need to clean things up, RegSupreme (and similar tools) are nice for that.  Still, I've never absolutely needed to edit the registry just to get some basic functionality to work.

Internet problems are more like the problems I'm talking about.  If you can't connect to the Internet to get help, you'd better have a GUI handy or know the magic shell commands by heart.  Whenever those problems are software-based, they usually come down to, "My wireless isn't working."  Like Ubuntu, Windows has a built-in GUI for messing with that, in addition to third party GUI's that come with wireless drivers.  Without that GUI, we'd be stuck with stone-age ifup/ifdown micromanagement (along with all the other diagnostic network utilities that would help us figure out what to do, if only we knew the magic commands for using those too).

That said, I never said that everything is fixable by GUI, but most common problems are.  As wolfen69 said though, a GUI still isn't the be all, end all.  For instance, what if the problem is due to uninstalled (or non-working) drivers or firmware?  The driver CD for your wireless adaptor will work for Windows, and you can install them with a series of familiar GUI prompts.  The GUI helps there, but it may not help in Linux:  In the past, it was common for wireless adaptors to fail due to missing proprietary firmware.  That wasn't anyone's fault but the device manufacturers, and either way, you'd need to connect to the net with another computer to download the appropriate firmware.  From there, you might as well learn about a command line utility anyway (like bcm43xx-fwcutter, which was not-so-helpfully missing from Ubuntu CD's when it was needed most :rolleyes: ).

Let's look at another example though:  When you want to set up a local network, what do you do?  It's nice when it works out of the box, but what about when it doesn't?  (Usually it doesn't, because Ubuntu's Samba defaults are broken...and you still have to know to install Samba in the first place for full functionality.  "Samba" is named after the SMB protocol, so it isn't exactly an intuitive name for, "set up a home network for me plz ok thx bai!")  Anyway, a simple diagnostic and config editing GUI would be a ton of help...as in, much better than having to search the net for the name and location of the config file, the right lines to change and add [from thin air], the syntax, and the magic text options that the config reader will recognize.  Config editing isn't exactly the CLI, but it's still freehand text entry.  The same basic principle applies that if you don't speak the language already, this interface provides little help for you to figure out what to do on your own.  I think there is a GUI available for this in the repositories, but I'm not sure how well it works.  In any case, it's not the default interface, unless the network setup wizards on Windows.

The point is the same as always:  GUI's won't cook bed in breakfast for you, but they can show you the way and help you help yourself much more than text-based interfaces.
