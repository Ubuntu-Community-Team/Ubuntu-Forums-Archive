### Post by johnjust on 2007-10-09
Just wanted to post my testimonial for the Ubuntu people to see.  To anyone wondering why I installed Ubuntu, there's a couple main reasons:

1. I'm not one of those people who think Linux is the end-all, be-all of Operating System, but I DO see it as a very viable replacement to Windows (I'm sure if I installed it on my desktop rather than a laptop, I would have had less problems, since I wouldn't have wireless on the desktop.  Problem solved.).  Linux does practically everything Windows can do (apparently, with the exception of FFXI and other "high-end" games, which I realize is a vendor issue, not Linux-related), and offers much more customization.

2. Customization: It's HUGE in my book.  The ability to turn something that everyone uses into something that only you use is the whole reason I became involved in computers years ago.  After WinXP wouldn't even let me alter the color of the taskbar, I became uneasy.  Practically everything I own is customized (I'm talking small, but recognizable - ex. Vinyl skin on Xbox, Custom paint job on Desktop chassis, etc...), so why can't I even change the color of WinXP from that putrid olive green to something much more eye-catching, like Forest Green?  Something that simple makes a world of difference to me.  How many people out of everyone you know end up using one of the three WinXP skins that it comes with?  The answer (barring the use of StyleXP, WinCustomize, or other similar programs) is practically everyone.  Using KDE, it's as easy as KMenu -> System Settings -> Appearance, and making some simple adjustments to the color scheme.

3. Terminal/Konsole/whatever...  I started with Windows 95, no lies there, but then when Windows 98 came out, I decided to go backwards, into DOS and UNIX.  I wasn't too keen on the terminal at first, but when I learned that some things don't require a GUI to function, I changed my mind.  I actually tried out something I saw on TV a couple years ago called the "OS on a floppy", which was a bare-bones DOS/UNIX console with a simple GUI on top of it.  And yes, it really was only about 1.3 MB.  Point being, having the terminal there makes me feel better about times when something goes wrong in the GUI.

4. Synaptic/Adept.  Wow, I had no idea prior to using Ubuntu that this would ever be a reality.  Windows "Automatic Updates" doesn't come close to the elegance of Adept.  You can obviously google whatever you're looking for, and get it almost immediately.  If I had the choice to do that or use Adept, I choose Adept.  "One program to house them all" should be the tagline here.  It's excellent.  Well done.

5. Personal accomplishment.  Linux is not for everyone, enough said.  The fact that I installed, configured, troubleshot, and finally came out with a working install is a great feeling.  I feel comfortable with my knowledge of computers as is, but being able to take advantage of what Linux offers takes that to a whole new level.

6. Docking.  As the list goes on, the points get less and less important.  I remember using Winamp or Trillian in Windows, and saying "Hey, they dock to the sides.  Why doesn't EVERY program do it?"  Well, having OCD isn't a blessing by any means, but it certainly helps in keeping a neat, organized, clean workspace.  Your desktop (I'm talking about the Computer's Desktop here) should be no exception.  Docking is actually probably higher up in my list, I just don't want to admit it to myself...

To anyone who thinks it can't be done, you're very, very wrong.  I think the main thing here is patience, something an old cliche states "is found in a woman, but never in a man."  So true...  I almost gave up a bunch of times, but perseverance pays off.  If anyone gets anything out of this post, know this: use the forums, use google, use your head.  The solution is out there, you just need to find it.

So, in the end, I sit here, eagerly awaiting Gutsy's official release, and asking myself the question: "So...  do I really want to upgrade and possibly go through all this madness again?"

Simply put: yes.

Bring it on!  In closing, I just want to say thank you to the Ubuntu staff, and all related parties (as I use KDE over GNOME), and especially Linus Torvalds.  I hated your OS when I had to take a Linux class in college.  I hated it more than anything in the world.  I wanted to reach into the computer, pull Linux out, hit it in the head with a rolled-up newspaper and say "No.  Just...  no."  There's still times I want to do it, except now I look at the final outcome and I tell myself "You can fix it.  You have the technology."

Eagerly awaiting Gutsy (and Dolphin for us KDE'ers),
- Justin J.

### Post by johnjust on 2007-10-10
I forgot to post this in my last reply, so I'll do it now, since your quote "one mate that didn't know how to burn an ISO so I'm not to sure if Ubuntu is for him" reminded me to post this experience.

I have a friend that lives down the street from me, and he is probably the most clueless person I know when it comes to computers.  He didn't know what anti-virus software was, he's constantly calling me to fix his problems (which I don't mind, since he's literally seconds away from my house - I sometimes charge people to work on stuff if it's anything of a drive ^^).  Well, last week, when his computer crashed and he lost EVERYTHING (running Windows XP Pro, he prolly had a crapload of viruses and stuff on it...), he asked me about Linux, since he's been hearing a lot about it recently, with the NYTimes article on Ubuntu, and Ubuntu on Digg at least 20 times per hour, etc...  At the time, last week, I was having my problems with Kubuntu, so I told him it DEFINITELY wasn't for him.  He seemed a bit offended, so I offered to reformat his computer for him.  But, he didn't have a windows disc, so I met up with him after work last night and showed him my laptop, now running a working Kubuntu install.  He said it looked cool, and a lot like windows, and I explained to him that it was free and has the same basic functionality as Windows.  I explained that he didn't need to use the Terminal/Konsole, and I showed him some quick things on my laptop.

He said he'd try it out, so I installed it (with my Canonical Kubuntu CD that now has a permanent home in my lappy case), did some quick configuring (since I knew he wouldn't be able to do it himself), and told him to give me a call and let me know what's up.

I'm at work, he has today off, so he called me about a half hour ago, and told me he loves it.  He has had 0 problems, I got his DVDs and DivX codecs working, and his internet (wired, not wireless, lol) worked without any problems.  He also told me he's been looking around the net for some resources, because he wants to be, and I quote, "1337."

I laughed and pointed him to these forums, Linux-Questions.org, and a few other resources on the net.

So, in the end, it is very possible for a clueless techy person to run Ubuntu.  Of course, I did the major configuring for him, but hey...  it's a start.

Next convert: My dad's old Gateway from the stone age.  It's a Win 2000 Pro, 500mhz P3, with 256 RAM and an 80gb HD.  I'm thinking Xubuntu, although I want to show him KDE, since, in my experience, it acts the most like Windows (but I've never used XFCE, so I may try it out first).  But, you want to talk about being clueless with computers...

My dad asked me how to burn a CD.  There's NOTHING hard about that, so I'm on the line here.  To Ubuntu, or not to Ubuntu?  **_THAT_** is the question.
