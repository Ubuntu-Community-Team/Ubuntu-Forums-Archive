### Post by ugm6hr on 2008-12-22
> **jrock612 said:**
> And personally (and I know I'll be flamed for this one) I think if Ubuntu is going to presume to be a functional Operating System, they DO have a responsibility to make as much work as they possibly can, no matter whose "fault" it is that a specific piece of hardware doesn't work.

There are 3 potential solutions to this problem for Ubuntu that I can see:

1. Apply business / economic pressure on manufacturers to support Linux (the Microsoft way. i.e. support our OS or lose sales).

2. Have a "hardware division" that licenses or rebrands hardware that is compatible (the Apple way. i.e. let us rebrand / repackage and sell your hardware at a premium to our OS users, and not support any unofficial hardware).

3. Reverse engineer hardware to create working drivers without manufacturer support (the Linux way. i.e. feel free to ignore us and we'll try and get your hardware to work with our OS without your help).

Ubuntu obviously follows method 3 predominantly.  Unfortunately, it requires the most effort on the part of the software engineers; hence some drivers "suck".

There is an [Ubuntu hardware division]("http://www.ubuntu.com/partners/hardwareprogramme"), but it is only available for complete systems rather than individual components.  Whether the expense of ensuring compatibility with Ubuntu makes business sense is obviously dependent on a company's (potential increase in) customer base.

Ubuntu potentially has the economic clout to get more manufacturer Linux support, but not to the same extent as MS.

Hope this helps reveal the truth in people's responses.

As a user, you can help with the pressure on manufacturers...  Dell is a good example.
