### Post by kanikilu on 2009-03-09
I'm sorry your experiences with Linux have not been favorable. I remember my first attempts at getting Red Hat 5.2 to work about 10 years ago. I recall staying up to 3 in the morning reading and taking notes about cryptic (at the time) commands only to find out my modem was unsupported and would *never* work, no matter how much I tried.

The lesson is, not all hardware plays nicely with Linux. This is more of a driver problem than a fault of Linux. Windows doesn't "just work", it just happens to be the most popular, so the driver base is enormous.

Having said that though, I've done literally hundreds (thousands?) of Windows 98 through Vista installs as a systems administrator, and I can tell you that a modern Linux distro (e.g. Ubuntu) has at least as good, if not better out-of-the-box hardware support than XP.

When I install XP, generally I have to get at least one (usually more) of the following drivers (display adapter, audio, wired network, and wireless network).

The main difference, and the source of a lot of frustration (including my old struggle with a so-called "winmodem"), is that with Windows, if it's not supported out of the box, you can generally find the driver, provided you know your hardware and have access to another computer. Occasionally with Linux, however, you either have to do a lot of technical work to get hardware operational, or you may be unlucky enough to have something that is completely unsupported.

For instance I have a web cam that I've almost never used that is not supported. I don't use it much, so I can live with out it. I know that's obviously not the case with everyone. If you can't get fundamental devices like wireless on a laptop or audio to work, then I can certainly understand your frustration.

// sorry, ending Windows vs Linux rant...

Regarding your complaint about "technospeak", not everyone is at the same experience level, so it's hard sometimes to craft a one-size-fits-all response. Just last week I remember a thread here with a very technical, "low level" kernel question, here in "absolute beginner talk" (although I think it was subsequently moved). On the other hand, some people have needed to ask how to run basic commands or how to even get to the terminal. So you can see that there are a huge variety of questions asked.

Try to remember that people here are *choosing* to help you (and others) on their free time, and that a huge number of questions have already been answered (or documented elsewhere), so at times it can be frustrating for people trying to help too. Please just be patient with people trying to help you, give as much information and detail as possible, and just ask (or google it) if you don't know what a particular term, phrase or command means.

### Post by lovinglinux on 2009-03-09
I would like to add an experience I had a couple of days ago. I recently released a new extension for Firefox and I have provided a detailed online user guide, to help people understand how it works. 

Since Firefox is the default browser in Ubuntu and it is very popular, I presumed that Linux users would be very familiar with it, so I skipped some information about how to access it after installation (need to add the extension icon/button to the toolbar). Usually when I install an extension I go directly to the toolbar customization to add it's icon/button, because they are usually there. But not everyone does this when they can't find a way to access the extension. So, after a while a received some support requests about this. 

One person was very creative and copied the extension address from the user guide screen shots (which also works), but others weren't able to access the extension at all. So, as you can see, different people have not only different experiences but also different thinking process when trying to solve a problem. 

The point is, unless you tell people who are trying to help that you don't understand the terminology or procedures, they will assume you understand. But if you tell, I'm sure they will help you with step-by-step instructions or will give you links with basic tutorials.

Whenever I try to help people here in this forum, I assume they have some basic knowledge, even for an "absolute beginners talk". So I don't give them very detailed step-by-step explanations unless they ask for, otherwise I would spend a lot of time writing a lot of details for people that doesn't need them.

BTW, I was a Windows user for 15 years, until 6 months ago. If I didn't had the amazing support from the community here I would never switched to Linux. Obviously that I had to do my part, researching stuff and experimenting with different solutions. Some things were hard to learn than others, but in the end I was able to solve all hardware issues (not that there were many). Now I'm very satisfied with Ubuntu and I regret not switching a long time ago.

Just my 2 cents...
