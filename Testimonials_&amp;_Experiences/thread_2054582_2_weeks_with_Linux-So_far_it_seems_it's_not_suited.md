### Post by 777funk on 2012-09-07
So far I like Linux, lots of good programs for getting through the day (office documents, pdfs, browsing the net, music/video/etc/some graphics editing). GIMP is a nice piece of software for instance.  

But for music production, no ***real*** hardware is supported and the software seems VERY limited. 

I use CAD/CAM for my work and that's also pretty weak in the Linux world it seems. I found some CAD solutions that seem ok (Draftsight) but what about CAM. If you can draw something up but can't quickly and easily program the CNC GCode toolpaths, that's a pretty big shortcoming. There are some workarounds with certain software that *could* get a person by but most of these are not efficient or built with production and streamlined ease of use/functionality in mind. 

I'm a semi professional photographer (part of the marketing end my job), and though there are programs that deal with Post Processing and RAW formats, none seem to be as powerful or easy to use as say Adobe's Lightroom. 


So... at least for me, what I've found is that Linux isn't useful for doing any real work. Sort of a gripe... and I guess you can't complain about FREE. It's good for simple things. 

But am I right in my overview as far as Linux not really being up to par for the user who needs real tools? I just mentioned CAM, Photography, Music/Recording Production but I'd guess it's similar in other areas. These are just the ones I'm involved with and found it to be lacking in as far as content.

Not a complaint, more just an observation. Obviously you really can't complain about free. But in a way it's still sort of a disappointment. Linux seems to be a great OS and I'm sure the horsepower is there to do all of these things and more if only the software and drivers existed.

### Post by 777funk on 2012-09-12
Just tried editing my customer photos today in a few Linux programs and though I can see them working for everyday use, they're just not up to par with Adobe Lightroom for intensive RAW editing. There are times where I color has got to be dead on accurate. This is simple in Lightroom. Use a Color Checker or Gray Card with the eyedropper and it's pretty close, from there it can be dialed in if any minor tweaks are needed. And for each color there's a fine tuning slider. Say the Reds in the picture are not rendering right... grab the Red Adjustment slider and tweak ONLY the reds. Bam it's fixed. I spent a few hours with Linux trying to edit the photos and I think it proved to be a waste of time. I know Lightroom well enough and it's power with RAW images, that I think I'd be hurting myself productivity wise to use other software. There are pieces missing or difficult to navigate etc. Seems like Lightroom is well worth the $300 they charge. I can see why pros will spend it. But this is what I'm saying. Tools like this that are geared for the professional just don't seem to be there in the Linux world.

In other areas, open source blows MS out of the water. I much prefer Mozilla's products vs say Outlook/Mail or IE. I like the Office programs. Really I like the OS in general. Just those missing tools in the box are hard to live without (CAD/CAM, Lightroom, and Audio Mixing software that works with most major hardware).

EDIT: I do have to give Shotwell credit at being a nice bare bones (the important ones) program. It's not entirely how I'd like it (don't like that I can't see my folder names) but it's good at what it can do and it's simple and neatly laid out. I appreciate that. Still when it comes to needing a power tool (especially for RAW files) there's not much I've found that can do what Lightroom is doing. I think I prefer Shotwell to Picassa (better simple editing). I still miss the easy searching by folder names in Picassa though.
