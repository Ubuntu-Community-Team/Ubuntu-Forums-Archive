### Post by Mazza558 on 2008-04-29
I've been a part of the community since 2005, which is pretty long compared to a lot of users here. I've seen that some of the newer users have started complaining about Hardy, only to have their criticisms shouted down by people who don't seem to want to near any negativity regarding the new release.

I was active in the development of Hardy, upgrading at the release of the first beta. As was expected, there were a few minor issues, for example, a broken ndiswrapper, extremely laggy and poor quality ATi drivers, but, after all, it was a beta.

As the RC was released, I started to investigate the ATi driver further - adding some tweaks to the Xorg.conf to speed things up a bit. Alas, I couldn't seem to fix some glaring problems with 2D rendering. Even when the RC released, I was absolutely sure that Hardy was, on the whole, slower than Gutsy. It didn't seem to be a graphics issue either - even Vesa felt slower.

Of course, I would have thought that this was entirely the fault of ATi for releasing such slow drivers, but it makes me wonder why the Ubuntu developers even included the latest and "greatest", rather than opting for the old and fast Xgl driver (which was missing some features but, at least for me, was fast). However, the devs have to accommodate a massive variety of hardware, meaning that the ATi driver obviously fixed more stuff than it broke for most people. 

ATi aside, the second problem I had was the fact that trying to access Windows network shares through nautilus was broken. This had never happened before, but was probably due to a change in network filesystems. I did a quick search and confirmed it was broken. Why is this broken on release? It's a massive step back for everyone!

Finally, I loved Firefox 3 Beta, but when closing tabs began to take upwards of 10 seconds and I couldn't even type properly, I began to get very concerned about whether Hardy was ready.

Hardy's release was a massive event on the internet, with a far bigger hype than even Gutsy reached. It was unfortunate that I had to conclude earlier today that Hardy simply isn't ready for me. Carrying a raft of issues with no current fixes, and often breaking more than it fixed, I gave it up.

I reinstalled Gutsy, and the speed difference was dramatic. I will, of course, follow Hardy's development into a usable OS (probably by June's 8.04.1 release), but for now, the Gibbon is the king of Ubuntu.

P.S - Yes, Gutsy also had issues on release, but never anything in terms of actual speed.

### Post by mivo on 2008-05-02
> **philipuknovice said:**
> Linux needs to sort itself out. Theres over 300 distros under linux. This confuses the hell out of trying to find out about linux, if your a company trying to switch.

Sometimes I feel that way, too, but then I realise that it's my own "consumer"-oriented thinking that was shaped by some sixteen or seventeen years of using Windows (it wasn't my first OS). There are some undeniable advantages of having only "one" version of an OS, or at least all versions of an OS from the "same" development team. But there are also many disadvantages, especially in regard to choice. If there were only one Linux distro, what would you do if the developers added something that your computer disagrees with, or that you disagree with? That's how it is for Windows users. If they want to use that OS, they can basically choose between XP and Vista, and is bound to become obsolete because MS is a for-profit company.

As for the 300 distros, I think if we look at fully supported and maintained distros that are viable for the desktop user with a sizable community (important for support), we'll probably find around twenty alternatives. Still a lot, but nonetheless a number that allows people to download Live CDs and try them out. It costs nothing but some time and a few blank disks.

In terms of popularity, you could narrow down the list even further: Ubuntu, Fedora, OpenSuse, Debian. Those are the **big players** in the desktop area (Debian not limited to it). Perhaps Mandrake too, though the distro's popularity has shrunken somewhat. All four, or five, distros approach the Linux desktop from different angles, so everyone should be able to find a popular and large distro that meets their preferences. If one or two seem fine, but still not exactly what they want, there are often spin-offs like Mint, Sabayon, etc. that change (relatively) small aspects and may suit.

There are plenty of other distros, many of them are for more expert users and special interests. But there is nothing wrong with that, because in the end it only means that there is more choice. A lot of people think they want choice, but they don't really desire it -- because choice means making decisions and making decisions bears the risk of making the wrong decision, which may then result in confusion. Windows is safer that way and removes the hassle of decision-making, but then you let others make decisions for you.

It's a trade-off, really.
