### Post by bbqsandwich on 2008-02-14
For a while, I dual-booted Vista and Ubuntu. Now I only use Ubuntu.

When I made the dual boot, I was curious about Linux and had heard many good things about Ubuntu. I wanted to learn a new OS.

But I also knew my home computer had to be able to do a number of things I required, and I could not completely get rid of Windows unless Ubuntu could do all of those things.

Those things were things I think many home users need: Web browsing, importing pictures from several digital cameras, playing music, editing photos and connecting to my company's corporate network and more.

I knew going into it that there was a wealth of free software available for Ubuntu that rivaled costly commercial software. Most people here know the obvious parallels: For most purposes, GIMP is as good as Photoshop. OpenOffice is as good or better than Microsoft Office.

(Photoshop Elements + MS Office Home & Student together are $250; Photoshop CS and Office 2007 Ultimate together are $1,330; I think you can get more expensive than that, too, but that is beside the main point of this post).

But when it came to simple matters of functionality, I was pleasantly surprised to find that not only was Ubuntu able to do all of the things Windows Vista could, it actually did more.

I will detail these below for anyone who is interested and also to show an example of how Ubuntu is a better operating system for the home user, at least in my case.

Here were the surprises:

1. I have a Flip video camera. It's a basic, $100 camera that records 30 minutes of video. It's very easy to use and is great for family footage. Well, Vista doesn't recognize it -- at all. The manufacturer told me to install an MS hotfix; I did and still nothing. Ubuntu, however, recognizes it fine. As a bonus, the codec to watch the the video is free, whereas in Windows I would have had to pay for the latest version.

2. When I connected to my company network in Windows, I used the Cisco VPN Client. It often took three to four minutes just to connect, and many times it would inexplicably restart the connection process or simply not connect at all. Using vpnc in Ubuntu, however, I can connect nearly instantaneously. This is a real timesaver when I just want to grab a file quickly.

3. My company e-mail uses a Microsoft Exchange Server. To get my e-mail in Outlook 2007, I had to be connected via VPN, which, as is noted in the item above, was a real pain in the butt. Even then it was slow. Evolution, however, retrieves my mail regardless of whether I am using VPN or not, and it does it quickly.

4. I have an 80 GB hard drive. Under Windows Vista, I had about 35 GB free (before I set up the dual boot). Now that I have gone Ubuntu-only, I have 55 GB free. 

5. My laptop came with 512 MB RAM. I had to upgrade it to 1 GB just to make Vista tolerable. Underneath my desk right now is sitting another 2 GB I had ordered to make Vista not just tolerable but pleasant. I can now return that 2 GB and get my $50 back because Ubuntu flies on 1 GB.

Some people switch to Ubuntu, I think, because it is cheaper than Windows, or because they are impressed by the eye candy of Compiz/Emerald.

These things are true for me, too, but I have also found that Ubuntu is simply a better operating system that meets more of my needs than Windows. It is more functional and faster.

And, of course, I can't wrap up this post without mentioning the great support available in these forums.

Sure, there have been some hiccups (a simple search for my username in these forums will prove that) but they have all been valuable learning experiences.

When I started this Ubuntu hobby, I thought it would be a matter of "Can Ubuntu do everything Windows can do?" Now I know that it is actually "Windows cannot do everything Ubuntu can do."
