### Post by Testrum on 2009-03-01
To get thing's started I have been using Ubuntu 8.10 "Intrepid Ibex" for roughly a month give or take. I heard about Ubuntu when it first came out from a PC magazine or online source I can't really remember exactly.

I had always considered using it but was dead set that Linux was the devil and that Windows was the best OS ever (how wrong I was :shock: )

I made the switch at the end of January when I purchased my Toshiba Satellite L305. I had the failure of an OS called Vista :-& was horrified when I actually used it for a week.

I downloaded the Intrepid Ibex ISO burned it on a disc installed it and completely eviscerated my Vista partition with the intentions of never going back.

I was a little iffy when I booted up Ubuntu since I didn't know what I was getting myself into and had completely erased my Vista partition. After reading and asking a lot of questions on these forums ranging from how to get my wireless card to work to mounting an external hard drive with a command I didn't understand.

I have now had my Ubuntu install for a month and I'm absolutely loving it. Every time I visit my Mom and see that she still has Vista on her laptop I laugh and show her the superiority of Ubuntu along with the elegant simple design of Gnome.

I am now 100% proud to call myself a Ubuntu-ian and Linux user.

~Testrum

### Post by jbysmith on 2009-03-01
> **neu2buntu said:**
> reply to "davidryder".... about spending 1 to 3 hours editing config files:... "that is half the fun of ubuntu(linux) when you solve a hardware problem through the command line or by editing a file that just makes things work kinda gives you a sense achievement!"

Personally, they just make a whole lot more sense than a massive registry file.  

With the registry, there's a (somewhat) set of standards where stuff is supposed to go.  Does every application follow it? Hell no.  You can dump settings any where you please as long as the account has access to that section. Chasing down some obscure class ID is especially un-fun.  Plus, if you make a mistake, the potential for a major disaster is quite real.  99% of the time an application uninstallation leaves a lot of junk behind too.

Linux makes it a whole lot simpler.  Typically (not always but usually..) you'll find the appropriate configuration file in the /etc directory.  One file for one application.  Usually pretty easy to figure out what the settings do just by looking at them, and if you mess up, you probably won't smash your system in the process.  (Welllllll there's a few that'll make your system un-bootable.. but easily fixable with a live CD.)
