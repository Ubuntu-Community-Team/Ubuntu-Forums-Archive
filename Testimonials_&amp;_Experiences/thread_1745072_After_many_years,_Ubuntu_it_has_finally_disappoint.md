### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-04-30
I have 3 grandchildren living with me. I have been able to provide them with 3 computers to use. They are old computers; however, Ubuntu has kept them running. I tried Ubuntu 11.04 on one of the old computers. I was disappointed to learn that Unity wouldn't run, and even with the classic GNOME it was extremely slow. It was so slow they could never use it. With a 6 month release cycle I would expect some indication that I need new hardware; however, this is a sudden change in the hardware requirements. 

I may be able to get 11.04 running on the older computers using classic GNOME; however, Unity will not work, and the Classic GNOME is not going to be supported in future versions from what I have been able to discern. 

I use several distros; however, Ubuntu has made it easy for me to provide something for my grandchildren. It has been easy for them to use, and the menus work for them. It looks like they will be using Debain, CentOS, or some other distro in about 2 years when 10.04 is no longer supported.

I never could have afforded to give my grandchildren the 3 computers and applications in a world without FOSS. Having Ubuntu exclude old hardware suddenly like this is very disappointing. Fortunately there are other distros I can choose from.

I know, I need to upgrade the hardware; however, this requirement came suddenly. 

I still like Ubuntu; however, I feel disappointed with Canonical for this decision.

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-05-01
I've read the replies, and I appreciate that no one flamed me for what I said. I was just disappointed and needed to express that disappointment.

I have been a long time Linux user. I know of other choices in both DEs and distros. I have tried a lot of them, and I use several of them at home and at work.

I will continue to use Ubuntu when it is the right distro for my purpose; however, the machines I have supplied for my grandchildren to use aren't going to work with Ubuntu Unity. It appears that GNOME isn't going to be offered in future versions.

I thought to myself, if I am going to hack Ubuntu to get it to work, I might as well do a bottoms up hack. I downloaded Debian Testing onto one of the old computers, installed the extra stuff I needed, and it is working very very well. 

When 10.04 is no longer supported, I know that I have something I can install on their computers that will work similar to what they have now. That is assuming they are still using the same old computers.

I also know I have a couple of years left on 10.04 before I need to upgrade, and Canonical may fix the problems I have with Unity by then.
