### Post by k2t0f12d on 2008-02-07
The first computer I used very heavily was the Intel 8088 with MS-DOS.  It was the family's computer, used mostly by my father, and hacked on by me in his absence.  I didn't have any build tools in those days, and wasn't even aware what build tools were initially.  I picked up somewhere that to create an ".exe" executable I'd need a compiler, but not understanding what a binary was, nor where to obtain a compiler, the biggest impediment to me taking to computers fully early on was this absence of tools (thanks Microsoft, you useless bunch of whopping %^*&ers).  But I could write interpreted software with GW-Basic, which is what I did for awhile, before taking on to drawing, painting, and the studio arts, which I subsequently studied full time.

I used Windows for years, which is not to say that I selected it from a pool of choices.  At that time, Microsoft Windows, having taken care to lock-in the vendors to suffocate the competition (e.g. DR_DOS), *was* computers.  All the tools I had to use ran on that platform, and Apple was *the other thing* that I had used but was never ubiquitous enough on which to catch.

Years and years after having all but forgotten my youthful attachment to computers, and my Dad's 8088, I finally broke down and bought my first P4, with which I am typing this message no less.  I got the entire setup from a workmate for a song, and although its gone on the antiquated side now, it is still a very viable workstation I use frequently.

I do not recall where I *first* heard the name Linux or UNIX.  I remember another workmate of mine having mentioned Linux as being "an operating system for programmers".  This sparked my interest, and I kept it under my hat for awhile.  I began seeing the name all over the internet, even occasionally seeing support for it with software that I had been using on Windows.  I mentioned it to an IRC channel buddy once, who directed me to the Debian distribution.  Having had at the time no experience with GNU/Linux distribution methods, I downloaded the three DVD binary set for Debian `Sarge' 3.1rc1, even though I could have just as easily downloaded just the first CD image of the distribution for installation.  I burned them to DVD-R and put them in a wallet for a few months.

Not so long ago, I went back to school for computer science.  Unfortunately for me I wasted all the money I put into that school, who not only teach Windows programming exclusively, but also distribute inaccurate facts about GNU/Linux [one Prof said that GNU/Linux has no GUI] and do not allow the use of free software formats.

Meanwhile I had decided to give the Debian distribution I had downloaded a try.  Installation was not very difficult, and I got a minimally functional desktop at the end of the day.  My biggest problem with Sarge was being able to get X to use my video card.  Since I was dual booting with Windows at the time, I toyed with it on and off for a couple weeks learning how to interact with the system.  I do not now recall whether I ever had my video card going with Sarge.  I think I had, but the method in doing so prevented me from doing some other things and was just a general hassle. A *fun* hassle, but a hassle nonetheless.  Enter Ubuntu 6.10 `Edgy' Eft.

After two weeks of Sarge, I had learned enough to realize that Debian stable at that time was more then a little out of date, which is the usual case with *any* Debian stable.  So, after a little solicitation I was pointed toward Ubuntu and I downloaded the Live CD (the copy I burned is still in my mission-critical media wallet ;) ).  Compared to Sarge, Edgy Eft was a dream to work with.  All my devices worked with very little extra effort, and I got used to using it much more frequently then Windows for its robustness.  There is not a job I ever found that I cannot do with software available for GNU/Linux.

I used Edgy for about two months.  After Edgy came Feisty, with which I had some trouble (particularly the Live CD partitioner, which was incredibly easy with Edgy and hell on toast with Feisty).  Meanwhile, Debian Etch had either been released or was close to it, so instead of continuing with the Ubuntu releases, I went back to Debian.  Since then I have reorganized by sources.list to maintain a Debian `Testing' system, and keep packages from that version for my architeture mirrored locally.  Somewhere along the way the partition that had once held Windows XP Pro got deleted to make room for something or another.  By the time I realized I needed that space for something else, deleting it was trivial since I hadn't used it in so long I didn't even remember what it looked like.

These days, I spend my time with artwork, playing guitars, and hacking on GNU/Linux.  Since I began noticing that I was replacing a lot of .deb's with locally compiled versions, I have also been developing a build system with which to build my own GNU/Linux exclusively from source code.  I continue to follow the Ubuntu releases, even installing and testing new versions, but am not currently persuaded to run it exclusively.  I do certainly would recommend it to others, however.  Besides, running Debian `Testing' is sufficiently similar that the experiences of one are valuable to the other in every way that is meaningful.

So, kudos to you all for playing your part in my formative period with GNU/Linux.  Free as in *freedom*, not as in beer.

Cheers!
