### Post by mrmark24 on 2009-09-14
Hola, everyone :)

I've been using Ubuntu since 7.04---it sure doesn't seem or "feel" like I've been using Linux for that long!

I wasn't too sure about this whole "non-Windows" OS at first; probably would've given up all-too-quick if not for the fact my brother uses Linux, too.

7.04 was the first, and not an "easy" first---at first! Once I began figuring things out (like awesome programs to install to enable the same tasks once carried out on XP), however, there hasn't been much looking back since :guitar:

I was quite excited for 8.04 Long Term Support (LTS) and even more surprised by its improved features over the two prior versions I'd used, as all upgrades I've used have had tremendous "pros" and few, if any, "cons".

When 8.10 was available, I just upgraded from 8.04.  It was better in many ways and it was well-used until 9.04 became available...

9.04, for me (a not-too-advanced PC user), had many pros: faster boot time; some improvements in the Preferences and Administration menus; and a few other new bells and whistles.  However, for some unknown reason, the first time I plugged-in my USB-connection camera, I received an error---something specific (I think it was "Error 60: camera not [something!]"... I wrote-in to the appropriate Ubuntu Forum and discovered another user had a similar problem with a camera and had submitted it to LaunchPad for help; no new news on that yet.  In the mean time (while Ubuntu was going to be "bookey"), I used a different, KDE distro with the camera connection working.

I thought the camera was an isolated incident (only cameras) until trying to plug in, via USB, a digital music player I came across, which produced the same "error" and even reported the "camera not..." error, though it should have been recognized as a USB Mass Storage Device...

So, to wrap things up here, I reinstalled 8.04.2 (the LTS's big update, I believe) on my laptop and, BOOM, both my camera (again) and the music player were recognized and now work A-OK!

I apologize if this is in the wrong part of the forum.  My intent is to praise Ubuntu (it's been so great I've went back to using it 99.9% of the time; that other 1 tenth is my KDE distro) and share an experience about it.  A question remains, however: are the Long Term Support versions better to use for a "mainstream" computer user?  Because of their longer support, it seems they have a bit more thought put into them and, also, have more time to correct any bugs that may be in the OS.

Cheers,
mrmark24
