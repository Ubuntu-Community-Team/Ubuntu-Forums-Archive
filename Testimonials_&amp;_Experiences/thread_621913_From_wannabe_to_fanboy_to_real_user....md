### Post by Sarteck on 2007-11-24
Long message, might be too boring for you to read.




It was the early nineties.  The Internet was beginning to get popular, particularly with businesses.  A few pretty wealthy people had it for home use, too.  The new Windows for Workgroups 3.11 made it so much easier to connect computers with other computers and printers.

I was fascinated by these as a young teenager.  In my earlier years, before my family was required to travel weekly in order to earn a living, I had an old Atari 400 that I learned to program BASIC on.  Nothing astounding, nothing that I ever got to save (especially considering I had no disk drive to save it to)...  I think the largest program that I ever personally wrote myself (i.e., not from the books) was only 500 lines or so.  Still, that's quite an accomplishment for an eight-year old (or it was back then, anyway).

Now the year is 1994.  Windows for Workgroups 3.11 has been out for about a year, and PCs with Windows pre-installed on them have been coming out for just a bit longer.  My family...  Well, the closest thing you could compare us to, in these days, were gypsies.  We went from Pow Wow to Pow Wow, somewhere different every week, and made our living by singing, dancing, and selling our wares.  Our home was a 1972 Winnebago, and the tipi we carted around in it (with the poles on top, of course).  Naturally, even if we had the money for it, there's no way I'd be able to have a computer, and even less of a way for me to convince my parents why I'd need one.  At some Pow Wows, we were in a big city, and I could sneak away, go to a library (particularly college libraries, who for some reason never saw it as odd that a 13-year old was putzing around on their computers).  But by and large, I was computerless.

In the winter, we stayed down in the southeast US, usually in Florida with some friends.  (There aren't as many PowWows in the winter, you see, and most of those are down south.)  These friends DID have a computer, though.  As much as I begged, they'd never let me on it, heh.  One time, though... it was the Winter of '95-'96, if I remember right, they were having trouble with their flatbed scanner.  Just from what I heard, I was able to diagnose the problem and suggest a solution to it, so they reconsidered.  I was told that I could get on the computer if and ONLY if I first read every single help file on the computer.  So I did, and for the next few nights I couldn't sleep, so I'd sneak into the house and read more (we were living off to the side of their house, in the Winnebago).

In the years to come, I was hailed as the Family Computer Geek, and I prided myself on my large amount of knowledge concerning computers.  (Mind you, as far as I knew at the time, ALL computers were Windows--I even thought that Macintoshes ran some kind of Windows, and didn't understand the difference until the late nineties!)  My obsession with computers inevitably lead to me wanting to be recognized as a "hacker."  I began looking into such things when we finally got our own computer in late 1996, and finally our own Internet connection in early 1997 (only after I'd agreed that I'd pay for the bill, of course).

That year, we settled down into a shop in northern Georgia, here in the US.  Even though I was done with my home-schooling, and could have received a diploma from them, I decided to go one year at a real school, since it would look better for the program I was trying to get into in the Navy.  It was during this time that I really began to be a wannabe hacker in earnest.  I was part of a very large community called WBS (webchat broadcasting system) where I often went to roleplay online in chatrooms.  I also learned how to use "leet" things like WinNuke and other wannabe hacker programs designed for screwing with people.  Since I knew a bit of HTML, people that knew a lot less than me often believed I *was* some sort of hacker!  I also didn't meet many people that knew more than me.

In the Winter of 1998, I enlisted in the Navy.  It wasn't all I thought it was going to be, so I found a way to get myself out after only a year and a half.  It was during THAT time, however, that I began to be less of a wannabe and more of a fanboy.  You see, I had found this site called "Cyberarmy," which was mainly a bunch of wannabes like myself wanting to become something more.

Cyberarmy was set up with ranks and simulated hacking challenges.  Once you beat a challenge, you were admitted to the next rank, which allowed you more access to the server and more rights on the forums (including moderation rights at the higher ranks).  It was all quite a bit of work to figure out for myself, though, so I cheated.  I made a new account, acted as if I was a female, and socially engineered those of higher ranks to tell me how to pass the challenges.  XD  I worked my way up to the "Kernel" rank in that fashion.

As I was doing this, though, I actually did learn a few things, and one of those things was "real hackers use Linux."  Well, I wanted to be a real hacker, didn't I?  So I head to learn to use Linux.  I found a Mandrake CD being sold at my local WAL-MART for $7.00, so I figured I'd buy it and try it out.  I honestly didn't see how it was much different from Windows except in where things were placed, and I had trouble getting a lot of things to work.  Finally, I gave up, and re-installed Windows 98.

Several years passed.  I was still a Linux Fanboy, but a Windows user (go figure).  I would tout how Microsoft is so evil, and how Linux was so much better.  When cornered on the *why*'s and *how*'s, though, I'd try to slide out of an answer by giving a bunch of technical jargon I hoped those askin wouldn't understand (mainly because I didn't understand it myself).  I blindly hated Windows, and blindly loved Linux, just because it was cool.  Thus, I was a fanboy.

Finally, the blind hatred gave way to real hatred.  I was sick of Windows.  I was sick of seeing the Blue Screen of Death, I was sick of not being able to have decent software without shelling out money I didn't have, I was sick of having to keep a CD full of a bunch of drivers that I'd have to eventually re-install when I had to re-install Windows after it got too corrupted.  Every little modification I made to Windows to make it work more like I wanted it to just made it go berserk, until it spiraled down into endless BSODs.  Yes, Finally, I installed Linus for the *right* reasons.

My hardest choice was what distribution I would try.  I think that, really, is the hardest choice for anyone making the switch.  The only experience I had was with Mandrake, but it was so little experience as to be negligible.  After reading at several forums, I chose OpenSUSE.  I actually really liked it a lot, but there were times when it, too, would give me trouble, particularly with video and audio.  I moved on to Debian... and only once got it to install correctly.  However, I had no idea how to make it access the Internet, so that soon gave way to Fedora Core.  Now *that* distro was much more to my liking, and I stayed with it for my longest amount of time so far--four or five months.  Still, I was using Windows much more often just to get simple tasks done that I didn't know how to accomplish in Linux.  I was tweaking something in Fedora, though, and evidently screwed something up bigtime.  I probably could have fixed it, looking back, but I decided I'd just try another distro.

Along came Ubuntu.  Being a KDE fan from my OpenSuSE experience, I decided I'd try Kubuntu, and downloaded the CD image for it.

Mind you, all during this time, my boyish fascination with Linux and wanting to be recognized as a "hacker" kind of faded away.  Sure, I still wanted to be a "hacker," but in the traditional sense of the word, meaning "hacking away at the keyboard all night."  I was teaching myself JavaScript and PHP at the time, hoping to make something of myself instead of trying to prove to others that I was something.

Well, Kubuntu was installed on my computer with no problem.  There were a few post-installation problems I had with it, but those were solved quickly and efficiently with a Google Search, which most often landed me right here on these very forums.  My old computer was pretty much a clunker when it came to running Windows, and wasn't much better at sluggishly trying to run KDE... but it -was- better.  So much, in fact, that I often forgot I had Windows on a separate partition.  I only booted into Windows now to play Diablo II with my girlfriend and to go to GaiaOnline with her (at the time, one of the little games there required SHockwave).

Working with Kubuntu, I feel that I now could probably go back to those distrobutions I'd tried earlier and actually make them work how I want them to (though since Kubuntu can do everything I need, I don't see a reason for it).  It's taught me a lot, is a great environment for me (learning programming, playing old console game emulators, watching anime, etc.), is totally free, and has an *outstanding* community support.  Everyone at these forums are usually so willing to help everybody else.  Now, all this doesn't even bring into the equation the Open Source philosophy, which I wholeheartedly agree with and support.

This isn't it, though...  Now even my parents--in their fifties, and more used to Windows than anything else--like Kubuntu better than Windows!  My Dad the other day was cussing about "Microsoft sack of sh*t", and Mom loves that downloading her Mahjongg and card games is so much easier that in Windows.  She also loves that I'm not constantly on her *** about downloading executables that she doesn't know where they came from, since Linux is virus-free.  My Dad, who's kind of a jack-of-all-trades (all of my family is, really, and if you've read this far, you might understand why, heh) likes the fact that he doesn't have to buy some extremely expensive and complicated program to map out wiring schematics.

Not only that, but the only thing they've had trouble with is the file system.  It hasn't sunk in that disks and CDs and such go in the /media folder rather than being assigned a drive letter... but I'm sure they'll get it eventually.  Other than that, they say "to hell with Windows."

Therefore, most of my family (including myself) are actual Linux users.  I guess I'm a bit of an administrator myself, really.



My girlfriend, on the other hand, will defend Windows to the death for the simple fact that she loves her Sims 2. XD

### Post by navaburo on 2007-12-01
> **Sarteck said:**
>   She also loves that I'm not constantly on her *** about downloading executables that she doesn't know where they came from, since Linux is virus-free.  


Unfortunately the "virus-free" nature of Linux is really a myth. Sure the Linux software generally has better security as far as keeping out internet worms, you are still just as vulnerable to trojans as windows users. The only reason that Linux users get fewer viruses is because there are fewer script kiddies at this point in time.

Even the user/root distinction does very little. For example:
```
alias sudo=ls
```
The above has a cute effect that can be removed by doing ```
unalias sudo
```. But by the same technique a program could have stolen your root password next time you sudo.


Feel free to disagree, but I believe that it is just the sad truth.

What surprises me actually is that more open source projects haven't been attacked by people inserting malicious code into the source. Sure, the author reviews submitted patches, but you would think people would find ways to take advantage of the system.
