### Post by Crashmaxx on 2007-10-23
Its been 2 years with Ubuntu for me and stuff still comes along and blows me away.

Like MythTV, such an amazing bit of kit. I had a TiVo, I say that because as cool as it was, MythTV does so much more. For a little more then $100, I put a tuner card in what is a pretty average desktop. Now I can record 2 shows at once, watch another I have already recorded, have torrents running at 100KB, and full Compiz craziness with me rotating the cube, all with no slow down. No more $12 a month bill, no ads, it even skips ads it detects, a ton more storage, and I can compress the shows, transfer them to any other computer, or even stream them over the web. Without a separate device even, and I could easily run it on a separate TV and have a normal remote if I wanted.

But even smaller stuff is great. The college I go to doesn't support Linux, but they do run a Sun OS server cluster and gave us all SSH access and some drive space. With a very small amount of searching and a few commands, I have it mounted as another drive on my machine. So I can just save them to it as if it was my local drive, and later if I am in a lab on campus and need something, its already in "My Documents" and I can just open it. Totally awesome, and much nicer then a flash drive. I wanted to do the same on my one Windows install, forget it. At best I have to pay $50 for some crappy software that _might_ work and definitely takes up more resources. Otherwise, the only way to use it on that is to either do SFTP, or use the web interface and download each file I need, and then upload them with done. Fine for most stuff, but a pain for stuff like C++ projects with many files, and just not anywhere near as cool.

The upgrade process still blows my mind. I just did the upgrade to Gutsy on the laptop, and man, it was cake. All my settings and themes, even stuff like Pidgin which was upgraded from GAIM, were kept perfectly. And now with just a few clicks I had Compiz running perfect even though it has a "poorly" supported ATi card. I remember 2 years ago I tried that, pretty much always just ended up with a crashed X. Mind you, knowing Linux basics I know now, that is really not even a crash, though at the time I needed to (or at least preferred to) do a fresh install to fix it. Which I really didn't mind as it was a breeze to do. In half an hour I could be back to where I was.

Open source is a truly amazing beast. The advantages of working together on projects openly, and sharing solutions and standards are just massive. Ubuntu is a great example of what can be done with it.
