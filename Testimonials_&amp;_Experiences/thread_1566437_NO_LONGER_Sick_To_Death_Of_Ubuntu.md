### Post by OldHarry on 2010-09-02
Dear All,

At the end of a very long and taxing weekend, I began a thread called "Sick To Death Of Ubuntu 10.04". People understandably replied in support of Ubuntu, their favourite operating system. Eventually, the thread was closed due to "degenerating into name calling", which I thought was a harsh thing to do. The feeling of some was that I was being a troll. Others wanted technical details in an effort to help resolve the problems we were experiencing. To my regret, I wasn't able to reply at the time, being occupied with the pressing daily grind of life. However, I wanted to set the record straight with some explanation so that everyone can get some closure on the issue.

Most importantly, I was not being a troll in writing the things that I did. I understand that trolls write much more basic things (like "yah-boo-sucks") before running away. Although it did at the time appear as though I had run away, what I wrote was much longer and very considered. When I wrote my post it did occur that I would be flamed silly by the entire ubuntu/linux community. To your credit, I don't believe that happened. However, I was partly driven by the need to vent a lot of frustration and steam at something that was not working when it should have been. A system that randomly breaks down is not a system at all. The other reason for posting was to share an experience rather than request technical advice. The chances were that other people had undergone a similar experience themselves and would continue to do so in the future.

In any event, with fingers and legs crossed, we managed to sort the problem out in the end, or, more precisely my Wife came up with the winning idea which was to load the more advanced version seeing as it was available. To sound a bit like a doctor, for those who are afflicted with a persistent, random crash of Ubuntu (or a blank screen, barcode or barcoding during boot-up, suspended loading or hang, to mention a few search terms), following a new installation I would recommend the following non-technical procedure:

**1. Confirm that your hardware is good enough - **We started with our secondary PC, an old laptop, because this was an experiment. We didn't want to lose this PC but it would be a smaller loss than the main PC if things went wrong. We initially regarded the hardware as good enough, but the later problems made us wonder if we had encountered the supposed nVidia/Video Card problem, for which there would have been no real solution given that we were working on a laptop. Seeing as our Ubuntu PC now works, either the problem doesn't exist or we don't suffer from it.

**2. Confirm that you loaded the correct file for your PC - **We used the 32-bit desktop i386 version. I have no proof, but I'm guessing that this is the same one that most people will use for a home PC or laptop. It took us less than an hour to download and most of the time it said it still had an hour to go. I can't remember what the 64-bit file was for, but I don't think its intended for most people. There's no harm in checking several times to be sure.

**3. Using alternate means of loading the system - **We used a USB stick instead of a CD, but only because that was our preferred means. We created a folder with the Ubuntu iso file, the Universal USB Installer, and the Ubuntu Pocket Guide in PDF format. There are probably lots of other guides that might prove just as useful.

**4. Check that you have selected the correct file, then check again with your finger and read the list - **Its silly and childish but it works. Its really easy when you are tired and bored for your eye to unwittingly glance off the page and grab-hold of the first thing that looks like what we wanted. There is always a possibility that this is what we did when we originally loaded the system, but I am only guessing on the basis of uncertainty. There had to be a reason for the crashes with one system and none when the system was restored with a later version. When using the Universal USB Installer there is a big long list of file types that can be loaded, a lot of which look exactly the same. We thought that the lack of dividers in the pull-down list made it very easy to accidentally select (amongst others) Kubuntu 32-bit desktop i386 instead of Ubuntu 32-bit desktop i386.

**5. Remove Mozilla Firefox and replace with Chromium - **That Ubuntu, Firefox, and an X-named system are adversely interacting with each other is probably another one of those myths, as some might have suggested. Still, we prefer the Google Chrome internet browser, anyway, so we saw no harm in replacing Firefox. Search for terms like Mozilla and Firefox in the Software Centre, to ensure that you have removed everything. Google Chrome is listed as Chromium Web Browser.

**6. Load a later version of Ubuntu - **This was the main point of our effort with this procedure. We did try to load an earlier version, Karmic Koala, but without success. We were just blocked and brought up short quite quickly. After posting our original thread, we re-thought everything that we attempted and decided on re-loading with Ubuntu 10.10 Maverick Meerkat. We wouldn't ordinarily use something ahead of an official release, in case we become the worlds first for encountering a significant problem, but we couldn't think of anything else that would solve the situation. Regrettably the system does not come with a cartoon picture of a Maverick Meerkat.

So far, there have only been two "hiccups" since we carried out this procedure when the system momentarily failed but then righted itself. It is more likely this was due to us messing about with the system and loading new programs into it. There has been nothing like the earlier problems, either in frequency or type. We think that the new system will easily reach one week without any further crashes or breakdowns, which means that it is functioning properly. If it carries on like this, then we will load Maverick Meerkat onto the main PC after October.

In answer to the many comments in response to my original post, I can only offer the following few replies:

**To Old *ix Geek - **I thought 1.5 hours was pretty good for loading and configuring Windows, Office and some other essential applications. I agree that the problems we were having might not be Ubuntu's fault, but what conclusion would you have drawn if you had spent an entire weekend trying to load-up an alien system such as, in your case, Windows. I think you would be saying a lot more than its dreadful and would have insisted on returning to Ubuntu. Funny you should mention it, but we did look into restoring the driver as well as the graphics issues as possible causes. Although we found the old files on the internet, they made no difference. I'm afraid neither of us can remember how we were stopped, but we think it carried on crashing immediately on boot-up. Because the laptop only had 40gb of space, and for reasons of personal preferences, we selected "occupy entire drive" rather than fiddle with partitions. In for a penny, in for a pound.

**To motorcity909 - **"Quote: we didn't realise we would have to write the operating system ourselves" Can you expand on that please? - I haven't had to play with coding for 15 years, not since the days when I used MS-DOS, Unix and Xenix. A lot of the time, in our researches, I was really having to cast my mind back to a completely different mindset. Its a very different thing with Windows and its "Make It So" way of doing things. It proved the most frustrating part of the whole exercise, like trying to read a foreign language years after leaving school. You know you should understand it, that once upon a time there wouldn't have been a problem, but now you are flicking through a book trying not to feel silly.

**To Jazzy_Jeff - **To use your analogy, yes, but at least I know what the essentials mean and what to look for. At least I know what the steering wheel is for which is a flying start compared to some. If I have driven one vehicle, then I should be able to do it with another even if I might not do it very well. So long as my expectations are not too high, I can learn the rest as I go along. At least until the Police catch up with me and demand to see my license.

**To Mark Phelps - **Wow, that is much more experienced than I am. I remember Cobol with punch cards, although I am too young to have ever used them. I hear what you say and I believe you have a very strong point. I do think, though, that what I was trying to communicate is still valid because, to use a bad analogy, there are several million people out there who feel that just because they can't speak in French or Latin doesn't mean they have nothing to say. By this I mean, the majority of ordinary users are Windows-based, not *nix. A lot of them are the kind of advanced users like me, that are such a headache to IT departments because IT literacy has extended into general use. I am experienced enough to know that with only a marginal amount of persistence I will hopefully have a better system than I did with Windows. Still, I will readily concede the point that we hit a really big snag and didn't have the knowledge to easily overcome it. We seem to have managed in the end but it took a lot of thinking and attempts before we were successful. Yes, we did use the Ubuntu Trial from the memory stick to see if it worked and there were no problems apart from an understandable sluggishness. We actually liked the trial version and thought it was a good idea. It was one of the reasons why we thought we were safe in dispensing with Windows and going the whole hog.

**To DogMatix, Shippou - **The post was intended because I had to do something. I, personally, had been working through countless suggestions, not all of which I actually understood but we tried them anyway. In the end it becomes not about specs and resources but about just saying something even if all you can do is just express how you feel. So that's what I did - just breathe out and speak your mind. Lo and behold, a solution presented itself and it worked. Maybe the Gods of the Internet had finished with us for a bit. That's the second time in the last week I have heard the term FUD when previously I had never heard it before or knew what it meant. Its one of those weird coincidences. But to answer the point, no I am not a FUD (Fear Uncertainty and Doubt) Activist. I am not sure we could manage without our computers for a week. No telly, no communication, no control. How would we pay the bills, entertain ourselves, or work. Even playing solitaire would become painfully slow and difficult.

Anyway, I think that's enough of all that. I hope that we can agree that things worked out OK after all, which is always the preferred option. My wife is very pleased with the new system and I will probably transfer over after October, certainly by December, provided my Sister gives back the external HDD I loaned her. I would like to say thanks to all those who responded, mostly because you were quite reasonable and fair-minded. One thing that I would suggest is that someone create a list of Windows equivalents for new users, to look like the menu's from the desktop, so that they understand that Write = Word, for instance. It would have been useful to us, but I suppose there are copyright problems or something.

To confirm, for people's peace of mind, I now agree that I was quite wrong in saying that Ubuntu was "frankly dreadful". In actual fact it has been working quite well and has provided a number of pleasant surprises such as Tetris, err... Quadrapassel. In time, this whole experience may become a humorous memory, but, for now, its part of the learning curve. Maybe one day, we will discover why and how we had these problems and now we don't.

Many Thanks.

Paul.
