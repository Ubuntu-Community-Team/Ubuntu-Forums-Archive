### Post by Shay Stephens on 2006-12-31
Last year I began my quest to learn how to use linux with the goal of dumping windows and ending my reliance on it forever.  I had been MCSE certified and knew it through and through.  But my happy factor started to die when XP was announced and I learned of the activation.  "don't worry" they said "it's easy and quick to activate".  Problem was I didn't want to give any outside entity that power over my computer.

So I finally caved in after about two years and got XP.  I liked it ok, but I was no longer free to do what I wanted with my computer.  I had to call Microsoft within a month to ask for continued permission to use it since it seems I reinstalled one too many times for its comfort.

Years went by with me hesitant to change hardware on my own computer.  Then I hear the details about vista.  "That's it" I said, I have to get off this out-of-control bus.  I had tried to use RedHat as a server in the past, so I thought I would look into linux to see if I could use it now.  I didn't want to use Apple, because they are just another Microsoft waiting to spread their wings (in my view).  

So December of 2005 I started reading about linux, downloaded a bunch of distributions and tried them out.  Nothing really worked for me 100%.  I finally found Ubuntu and it worked on my computer.  Great! I said, and so around the first of the year I installed it as a dual boot system with windows.  I didn't know much, but I was determined to learn.

I did what I could in ubuntu, and would dual boot into windows to do what I couldn't yet do in ubuntu.  Yes it was a pain, and I wanted it to be that way.  It is a nice incentive to find a solution to work on ubuntu so I wouldn't have to rebbot to windows.

Over the course of 2006 I slowly migrated things over to ubuntu.  First it was email, web, and bluefish, that was easy.  I converted my website from asp to php.  But I also use my computer for work, I am a photographer, so there are a few things I just gotta have.  But little by little I would try something that worked.  K3b here, bibble there, but I ran into a wall with dvd authoring and batch processing of photos.

It wasn't until this month that I finally learned enough to crack the last and final barriers.  I learned enough python to be able to write the gimp scripts I need to batch process photo galleries for uploading to my website, and I finally learned enough to get qdvdauthor working for me, and the Lacie lightscribe labeler program came out for linux.  I now have a system setup that allows me to work 100% without a microsoft OS installed.  I still rely on some application written for windows, like Photoshop 7 and memories on tv, but they run well enough to use in wine.  A decent compromise for now, and a compromise that is well worth the extra freedom it affords me.

So that's it!  I am free!  The windows hard drive no longer exists.  In a little over an hour, I begin 2007 free of any chains holding me in the grip of redmond.  I would like to thank all the fine contributers in the forum who have helped me learn what I have up till now.  Thank you to the Ubuntu staff.  And I hope I can start to provide as much help as I have received over the last year :-)

It feels good to be free \\:D/

### Post by Shay Stephens on 2007-01-01
Thank you all :-)

And a message to anyone trying to do the same.  Don't feel bad if you need to use windows for something.  You have to be able to get what you need done.  So that takes priority.  But having that goal of eventually getting rid of windows does help one to explore alternatives.

One example I had.  I need to print on DVD disks.  I have a thermal printer that does a very nice job.  Problem is it is so tied to windows that the dumb thing has to have internet explorer in order to even install.  And no amount of coaxing in wine would work.  So I was finally able to get it working in vmware with a windows 2000 os.

That allowed me the luxury of time to wait for an alternative to show up.  That happened when the lacie lightscribe labeler came out a month or so ago.  After doing some testing and burning through a spool of disks testing different graphic layouts that would look good and compliment the lightscribe layout, I had a linux replacement that actually looked better than the windows solution.  It was different to be sure, but it was as good or better.

You gotta be open to changes.  Open to new approaches and ways of looking at things.  And that can take time.  So give yourself that time.  In the end, the payoff is well worth the trouble and effort :-)

I think the last push, the final effort that made December the month of change, was that I had installed a sound card, and XP complained about that and demanded I reactivate windows.  I was naturally PO'd and so I backed up everything on the XP drive in the event it would not reactivate.  I moved the data onto ubuntu, shut down Photoshop CS2, copied all my actions, and basically prepared it for oblivion.  I am familiar enough with bibble now that I don't need photoshop CS2 for raw editing.

Now I still need some automation that Photoshop provided.  I have been kicking python around here and there, so I set to work to figure out python-fu in gimp.  After a few days of full time effort, I had successfully written my first script that batch processes a folder of photos (resizing, sharpening, watermark, B&W, sepia, thumbnails).  My next script was pumped out in a day, others were done faster.  Still no where near as fast as just recording an action in photoshop, but now I had my core functionality replicated in gimp.

I then realized, I had not booted into windows in weeks.  I had not used photoshop cs2, I finally didn't need them.  So I told windows to take it's activation and shove it.  

I still have photoshop 7 for text layout and correction layers and such, but it gave me a start to being able to use gimp on a regular basis.  And my hope is that with time and experience, and gimp being improved, I will one day be able to stop using photoshop in wine, which is my next goal on this journey.  The first goal was getting rid of the windows os, my next goal is getting rid of windows applications.  I don't have a timeline for that.  And I don't necessarily need one as all the software I now rely on does not use activation.  I will never use software that uses activation again.
