### Post by stoneage on 2010-06-29
It depends what you want to do. Running Windows games will be difficult as they were not written to run on linux. Wine and Crossover can help with this, but there are also games for linux - just not as many, perhaps. Post up what you want, there may be a linux alternative.

Flash is usually pretty straightforward, you just run the installer or install from Synaptic. Post the problem and any error messages for this, and someone will know how to fix it.

The main thing to remember is that Ubuntu is not better or worse, but it is different, so things that seem simple in Windows may seem complicated for linux, but not once you get to know it.

I don't know if you are aware of it, but for the record, things like Flash and some other packages, are proprietary software. Microsoft pays licence fees to ship them with Windows. Ubuntu cannot do that and distribute the OS for free, so some things you need to do yourself. 

The situation for drivers is similar. Manufacturers do not write drivers for linux, so someone has to take the time to figure out or reverse engineer them for linux. You can't really blame Ubuntu for ATI(or whoever) not writing drivers for linux(...or making code available so someone else can do it).

Once you have your Ubuntu set up it is pretty stable, and you may find it runs faster than Windows, so the time you spend setting up, you will get back by not having to wait. I think over a year you will definitely win with linux.

A sensible recommendation is to dual boot while you figure out how to run and troubleshoot with linux. That way you really can't lose........

### Post by Loctrice on 2010-06-29
Ubuntu software, admittedly, is not as good as mac or windows.... it's better!

What kind of stuff are you tinkering with to break that? I have wireless keyboard and mouse, plug in wireless whenever I feel like, use a kvm, and watch vlc videos.

Regarding the video , if you dont' reformat it you need to get codecs. You are in control of your computer. Plug and play can be difficult, but then again I have just recently re-installed the beloved xp on a clients computer because it page faulted (just like the windows demo's show) when he plugged in his mp3 player.

It's a simple learning curve to learn how to actually use a computer. They made it easy on you by building gui's that you can get. They simply don't pound you with lots of trial software for you to buy, and presume to know what all you will need on install. 

If you want to get vlc, and you can't watch a wmv, then get the win32 codecs. If you want to load in a new piece of hardware and it doesnt auto load on boot (which ubuntu is really very good at) then google which driver to modprobe, or download the source and compile/install it. If you want to format with codecs get ffmpeg, if you want video get kdenlive. Seriously, there's plenty of stuff out there. It just doesn't auto load on clicky like windozers love.

You come to the community forums and find answers, or meet other linux users that can help you figure this out. The community is great, the os pwns windoze (especially if you are a developer). 

Games are another issue entirely. Wine does great, there are other apps you can buy. You can thank the programmers for that, using microshaft system objects without knowing what they do, or considering the os. Then you can thank the linux community (like WINE) for allowing them to port for us.

Everthing has plenty of documentation, and it's not confusing after you get over the hump. The difference, other then most of it being free and well supported, is you may need to spend a few minutes learning how to use a program you just downloaded from a trusted source that doesnt inject you with a virus.

by the way, you can take about 3 classes at the local college to learn how to install, administrate, use, and manage network in linux. You can take about 3 semesters worth of microshaft courses to get the same abilities.... yeah, seems simple to me.
