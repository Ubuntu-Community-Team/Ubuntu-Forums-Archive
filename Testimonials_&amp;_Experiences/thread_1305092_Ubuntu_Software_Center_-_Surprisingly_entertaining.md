### Post by soelk on 2009-10-29
Let me first say that I'm one of those people who have been excited about the idea of an iTunes-like application to access the huge library of free software that exists for Linux. I'm partial to the concept.

So I've been following the development of the software center by reading about it on the net (see for example [https://wiki.ubuntu.com/SoftwareCenter#Ubuntu%20Software%20Center%202.0]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/SoftwareCenter#Ubuntu%20Software%20Center%202.0")). The thing that struck me as strange is that it is released without the ability to review or grade applications or to see popular applications. That part is planned to ship in October 2010, a whole year from now.

While I have huge expectations for the idea itself, my expectations for this release were low because of the lack of features. However, I was pleasantly surprised by the experience. It lacks functions, but everything that is there works as advertised and it seems to be rather foolproof. I have not been able to break anything so far. :)

By using the Software Center during the last few days I have found many applications that I have never seen before, as well as some that I have used and enjoyed in the past but forgotten about until they showed up in the Center. I would describe the experience as more entertaining than useful. The ratio of just-for-fun apps to actual useful-to-my-daily-work-apps is rather high. That is probably how it should be. If you really need an app, you often know someone (like a coworker) who will tell you that you need that app, so you don't need a software center for that.

The most disappointing part of the experience is that I can't find some of the apps that I know I want. I know how to install them from other sources, but it is still disappointing considering how easy it is to install stuff using the Software Center.

One trait of a good app is that it gives you plenty of ideas about how to improve it. To me, this is one of those apps. I find myself wishing I could go to the Graphics Department of the software center and then click to see the most popular apps. To click to see the hottest apps the last few weeks. To click to see the most talked-about apps on free software websites and communities. To click to get recommendations based on what software other people like me (however you define that) have installed on their computers. To get links to support forums and wikis for a newly installed application. The possibilities are quite exciting.

I also look forward to the idea of selling free (freedom) software in the Center as well as the ability to donate money for an application, in the same fasion as with that Radiohead album.

To sum up: The Ubuntu Software Center is still very lacking in features, but it is a fun work in progress, one that has occupied me for quite a few hours. The best feature is it's ability to find apps that you don't need, but that can keep you entertained and widen your horizons a bit (although I would recommend that you take your laptop outdoors for that :D). What it lacks, to my untrained eye, are community and Web 2.0-like functions. I don't think that I will continue to use Ubuntu Software Center as much as I have done these last few days, but I think that I will use it from time to time to browse and stumble over applications, and I look forward to using the full product in Ubuntu 10.10.

Do you also get excited about the Software Center idea? What's right/wrong about it so far? What other similar apps are being developed at the moment and who is funding the development? It seems to me that low-cost netbook makers would benefit from funding such a project in order to get it out more quickly and thus sell more laptops without Windows licenses, but I don't understand the economics of that industry.

By the way: I had a lot of fun with the old Add/Remove application a couple of years ago. The biggest improvements since then is that Ubuntu Software Center does not feel as dumbed-down and that there is a roadmap for improvement. The biggest drawback is that the ratings that you could see are gone altogether. They were a bit useful, even if the 1-5 scale was incredibly dumbed-down. (How did people pick movies before IMDB's 0-10 with decimals?)
