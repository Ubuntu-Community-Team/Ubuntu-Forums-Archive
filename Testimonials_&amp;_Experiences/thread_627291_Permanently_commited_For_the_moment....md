### Post by mister_lister on 2007-11-29
I have noticed something with Linux in general - a lag between a new peice of hardware being distributed and adoption by those who provide device drivers and support in Linux. It really varies between distrobutions of Linux. Perhaps the draw back of Open Source Development is the fact that the real world of computing is privatized, windows centric, and not very Open Source friendly. This goes with both software and hardware. People like to make money from their inventions. Open Source sort of thwarts that.

Don't get me wrong, I think everything except hardware should be free for computers. I am fully for Open Source software and drivers. I think it is the future of computing. RIGHT NOW, however, there a a huge lag between a peice of hardwares availability and drivers and support in Linux distributions. 

Take for example my computer. It is at least 10 year old technology.

*500 mhz AMD K6\2 processor
*PCI GeForce 128mb Video Card
* Creative Sound Blaster awe 64 sound card
* Realtek Network Card

It is only recently, with Ubuntu 6.06 that I found ALL of the above supported fully by a Linux Distribution. I had tried many distros over the years, and only had spartan support. I had to compile drivers and hack **** to get even a little functionality out of a linux distribution (mandrake, SuSe, Redhat).

I now completely use my Ubuntu 6.06 distro. I have abandoned windows, only using it for college assignments ( I am studying Network Administration). I am not your typical computer user. I am hovering between tech and power user. I still do not know enough about Linux, but I definitely get by with support.

In a nut shell, I reallly, really, believe in Linux, but I see it's faults and draw backs. I would like to buy a super fast and cutting edge computer, but I am afraid I will spend countless hours just getting it up and running. What happens if a peice of hardware fails or there is a power outage, do I have to go through all the compiling of drivers again? 

For Linux to really work, it has to be as hyper as the market. Cutting edge, Full Support for new hardware. That requires industry support and contact between distro developers and industry insiders. For Linux to ever truely compete with Windows or Mac it needs to develop a strong connection with hardware vendors. 

Another thing, software is just getting there. Only now are we seing a browser, email client, and office productivity suites that can compete. We all really need to support those products and provide feed back.

Linux is wonderful, but it is not perfect. I will tell you, I will go with the technology that suites me best. At the moment Linux fullfills all my needs. Tomorrow may be a different story. I just hope Linux can keep up with my SLOW pace of change and go WAY beyond it. 

It will have to if it ever will truly compete with windows or Mac. Lets work to gether to see it happen.

Windows is addictive for a reason. You can walk into any store and buy hardware, bring it home and it works with Windows almost immediately, just point and click "setup" fiiles and you are almost done. Sure there are hiccups with some stuff, but nothing in comparison with Linux. Like I said, It took ten years for my set up to be fully recognized and supported. Dare I go to another distribution or upgrade my current linux OS... hell no. I'm staying here until 6.06 support runs out. Hopefully by then there will be a supported hardware list and I can hand choose my next rig to fit Ubuntu.

This distribution has a lot of work cut out for it. I just hopes it can keep up.
