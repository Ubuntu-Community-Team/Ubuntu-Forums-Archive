### Post by phlembob on 2008-04-29
Today there is a new kid on the block. Ubuntu is a Debian Linux/GNU base Operating System.  Since this is a public creation, it will only cost you what you're willing to put into it.  Free is such a relative term, but if cash is the first thing that jumps into your mind --- you will need no cash to get a copy of Ubuntu 8.04 LTS if you have a computer with an Internet connection.  Ubuntu 8.04 LTS (Long Term Support) will win over many Windows users.  Of course, there will be many branded minds that will not change, but you owe it to yourself to investigate and graded it.  I suggest you grade it by its merit, and not its name.  Like anything new, there will be some fumbling for a while, but Ubuntu isn't really new.  You can type Ubuntu into your search engine and find it quickly.  An open computer operating system, which allows you to participate in the next generation Ubuntu OS.  The Linux/GNU Operating Systems have been around since the day of mainframe computers with less power than your desk-top computer.  :guitar:

I setup 7.10 with all the codec, got my belkin wireless working with Ndiswrapper and wicd.

The real question is whether companies want to switch now, or wait 5-10 year for better hardware support in linux. Companies can install Kubuntu and have the kde bar, which is reletively similar to XP, so the switch wouldnt (theoretically) be that hard, its just getting used to the new names for apps, such as OpenOffice Writer instead of MS Word, etc.

But as Ive said its the hardware issue. Companies dont want to re-invest in hardware to make the OS work. Theyve already purchased their hardware. And as we should all know, so IT hardware in companies, is worked hard untill it falls to bits. Companies want to get the full monies worth out of hardware, and then some. Hardware is a depreciating asset, where it has no resale value. In fact if you had a monitor or old printer, you would have to pay a recycling company to take it away, and their going to get away with paying you peanuts. You would have to read up on the accounting concept of depreciation, there are two major ways of doing this, that I know of. And being a wanna-be-smart-butt I sort of know how they think.
If Ive just purchased 20 new all-in-one-jobs back in Dec, and someone says "Reduce your IT budget by going over to Linux. But yourall-in-one-jobs will only be able to print. Oh its a lexmark, sorry its not supported by linux" Then do you really think a company is going to switch ? Nah. Hence why Linux in the UK would only take off if your eally, really lucky, and every piece of hardware you have is supported. 

But another major issue is "In-House" software, which is also keeping companies with XP. Just my pennies worth, long I know.
