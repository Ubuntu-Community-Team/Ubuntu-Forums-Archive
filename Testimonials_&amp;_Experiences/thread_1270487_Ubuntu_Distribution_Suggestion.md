### Post by ZetaZeta on 2009-09-19
Hi, I'm a new poster here, but I've been using the forums for support for the past year.
I had a suggestion and my friend totally thought I should post it on the Ubuntu forums. I've never posted here so I'm not sure this is the right place for this, move if necessary.

This is part suggestion and part asking about the basics of marketing in general, as I'm not a marketing major or know much about marketing. I do know, however, what I look for and what friends look for as consumers.

Ubuntu, or more specifically Linux in general, does not have a presence at the consumer level. When I step into Best Buy, Wal-mart/Target's tech section, or any other mainstream consumer electronics store, I do not see Linux.
Sure, they sell Dell machines, and you can get Ubuntu on Dell machines through Dell's web site. However, not in store demo is running Ubuntu. The section with OS or Desktop software will have copies of Windows and Office, but no copies of Ubuntu. (Granted I have seen an eeePC version of Linux running on an eeePC demo - that is no longer at my Best Buy).

Why?

For one, Ubuntu is free. It should remain free. What is there to package? Would Best Buy stock a box that contains a free OS? (Or $4.99 or whatever to cover the cost of the disc and shipping)? Has this been done/tried before? If I saw this as a consumer, I would be (as the software enthusiast) interested, and probably buy it at such an impulse price, stick in the LiveCD, etc. I could probably figure it out, like it, and install it. My sister can't even install iTunes on Windows, and I have a friend who would complain about the simplest things and not know hwo to accurately Google the answers (despite it being easy). I'm not arguing any kind of lack of usability of Ubuntu, it's fantastic. However, you offer Starter support for a reason.

At the $0.00-$4.99 range, an impulse purchase of the software is just that - there's little dedication to the software. I find $2.99 used PS2 titles unfinished stacked next to my bed. When I drop $50-60 on a game I consider it for at least a minute, read a review, etc. before buying it. When I head into Blockbuster and see the 99 cent rentals or head out to the library, I'll pick up 5 or 6 titles. Some might not even get watched, some stopped partway through if they're not exactly what I wanted. Another friend does the same. If we're dropping money on a DVD, we're planning to watch it again and again, or if we're paying to see a movie in a theatre, it's a premeditated purchase.

Sure, price can act as a barrier. I'm not suggesting charging for a copy of Ubuntu itself.
Instead offer something like this:
"Ubuntu Desktop 9.04 with 1 year Starter Support - $49.99"
Inside the box can be a way to activate/receive your support you could otherwise get from store.canonical.com, as well as a Live CD of Ubuntu 9.04 (or whatever the current version is) and basic installation and default GUI instruction, if not an entire printed version of an online Ubuntu manual/guide/wiki etc.
Price can act as a barrier, but for others it can act as a sort of gateway. For some (I can only cite specific examples, I don't know if there's a trend or relation) "something that's free can't possibly as good as something that costs money" or "if something is more expensive, it must be better" etc. I've heard these arguments before. When the Game Boy Micro was introduced by Nintendo for $20 more than the SP, despite lacking Game Boy Color compatibility, some of the same peripheral hardware, and in spite of the addition of the backlit screen to the SP not too long after, I read that the higher price was to "appeal" to consumers who were looking for a premium product. Although sales were eclipsed by the DS, it still sold almost 3 million units. Most consumers don't mind paying a premium for a great product, and some will think a product is better based on price and appearance. Also, what goes into Microsoft products? Not just the product itself, but support as well. Whenever I would use support.microsoft.com, the support options would be labeled "free of charge." The support is included, and I definitely believe most products should have support included in the price. Some users are skeptical of a free product. At least I'm one of the few who has never even touched an AOL CD on checkout line endcaps, and my dad has had trouble comprehending running free software.
Packaging support with Ubuntu gives the product a nonzero price tag, quality software, quality support. A fine deal.

Sure, if you offer the OS only for free, you'll get a number of serious buyers and a number of impulse buyers who will like it. However, if you offered both a support package (of 1 year, or various lengths or types) *and* a free version of the OS right next to it, you would satisfy both.

I think I can revise my post to a few final points/questions -

Is Ubuntu+Support a viable product that could sit on a store's shelves?
When do you think it will be? There's got to be a point some day. Will it only aim for OEM distribution, and hope the word of mouth will aim prospective users into buying a Linux machine or point them to ubuntu.com?
If previous distribution failed, one would look at why it failed, not simply brush it off with "maybe it's not ready." Packaging is pretty important.
