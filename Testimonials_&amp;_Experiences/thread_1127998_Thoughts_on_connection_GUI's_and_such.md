### Post by Digital Cactus on 2009-04-17
Let me start by saying that I am absolutely new to Linux and Ubuntu, and I wanted to offer the perspective of someone who has never played in that environment before.  I think that this perspective might be useful, as we sometimes get so involved with something that we can't see the forest for the trees.  I also want to add that so far I really really dig Ubuntu, its community, and its inherent philosophy and that any criticism expressed here is merely toward a constructive end more than anything else.  Open source, copy-left, and the like are things I find infinitely interesting and hopeful, and my wish is that they will continue to grow and expand.  Also, I would like to say that I am no fan of Windows, its philosophy (or Microsoft's rather), or how it runs—I find it cumbersome, open to attack, buggy, and highly inconvenient (every time I change my damn motherboard I have to jump through flaming hoops to be able to access MY files—its infuriating).

That all being said, and in light of the community atmosphere of Ubuntu, there are things, albeit minor, that I thought might be good to mention on the boards.  Recently I loaded Ubuntu on a secondary machine and sat there wondering: “How the hell to I connect to the internet?”  Luckily my primary machine is connected (XP) and I was able to do some searching to find out.  I learned about the terminal and pppoeconf this and sudo that and couldn't help but think what someone would do with either no internet connection (I admit that I didn't look through help very thoroughly) or  little to no computer knowledge.  

I imagine that Ubuntu/Linux wants to reach as many users as possible, and perhaps supplant Windows as the premier OS in the world.  I just can't for the life of me picture Martha in Iowa figuring that she needs to open up a damned command line and type sudo pppoeconf to get her internet working.  It seems that the rest of the OS is super-slick,though—I love the look and all the open source programs that come bundled, the way it works with separate work spaces etc.  In this arena Ubuntu is aeons passed Windows. So, why not apply some of that kick *** ingenuity and add some sort of GUI connection wizard that walks you through the process?  It can't be that hard for the super savvy guys that program this ****.  I mean, as bulky and annoying as Windows is, it sure as hell sets up a connection quickly—Type of connection?  Username?  Password?  Done.  It doesn't require another OS sitting idly by in the corner waiting to give answers on how to connect.  This goes double for networking.  Samba?  Please.  Might as well teach calculus to a monkey.  Again, with XP: Name of network?  Connected?  Bam.  Done.

When looking through the boards to solve the connection problem I saw that I wasn't the only person having trouble, but more interesting, I noticed that people were connecting and searching with their Windows machines to get answers.  Linux boasts the there are several million users—however, my deep suspicion is that many of those users are dual platform—and while this is cool and all, it doesn't really indicate that Windows is threatened in any way by the numbers—they're misleading at best, minuscule at worst.

It just seems that these are gateway issues—fix them and it opens the OS to the average user—millions of them, in fact.  Keep them as they are and I think it will scare millions away.  Thoughts?

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2009-04-17
Samba? Difficult? There's a GUI included in Ubuntu that allows you to get the workgroup and some very basic settings. Then you right-click the folder you want to share and choose "Share This Folder". That's it, sharing enabled. If you want more control such as hidden shares, single-user-only shares and password protection, there is another GUI you can install to do this.

As for pppoe, it's rather rare these days to see one of those sorts of connections. Most of the time, you have a modem/router that automatically connects for you; this is definitely the way most people connect now as games consoles and other embedded computing devices can't access the internet through pppoe connections.
