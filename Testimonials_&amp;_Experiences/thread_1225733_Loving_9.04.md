### Post by privatejarhead on 2009-07-28
My Linux story thus far:


I seriously considered using Linux right around March/April 2009. Before that, I didn't think that it'd be worth the trouble and only a little before that, I barely knew anything about Linux other than how it began. The only reason that I finally considered using this new OS was because I was looking for a new laptop to buy for myself and I didn't care much for Vista. Over the course of a few weeks/months, I would read articles from these very forums about how Ubuntu works, as well as other Linux distros (particularly Fedora and Arch). Since Ubuntu seemed to be the easiest distro to try out, I went to Ubuntu's ShipIt site and ordered myself a copy of 9.04. I waited for it to ship to me all the way from Holland to South Carolina, which was said to take a month or two. I was surprised when it came to me three weeks from the day i ordered it, so that made me happy because it showed that the company behind Ubuntu wasn't like other 'get this for free!' places I've used (which either took forever or the product was half-***; the LiveCD I got was far from that). In the meantime, I continued to read these forums and see what everyone thinks about 9.04, plus to see what hardware worked best with it (I learned about the many problems about Ubuntu and ATi, for example). Along with that, I would talk about it with the few friends at school that actually knew a thing or two about computers. One thought of Linux as a computer OS like the ones you see in 1980s movies: old, clunky, and CLI-only. Another thought of Ubuntu as pointless, considering that Windows is a good OS for anything a common person would need a computer for. He wasn't completely ignorant though because he did try out a few OSes, including Ubuntu (he paid $20 for it too, which made him a bit mad when I told him about ShipIt), but he still prefers Windows anyways. It didn't bother me much because I still like and use Windows to this very day (game support, TI Connect, and as of now for Java-based programs). When my CD came, I played around with it on my old eMachines T6412 just to get a feel for how it's like.

Skip a few months ahead to July 2009. After looking at various laptops and netbooks, I finally found the perfect computer for my needs. There was a sale at the local OfficeDepot for a Toshiba Satellite L505 (3GB RAM, 320 HDD, Intel GMA 4500) for $430 (ToshibaDirect.com sells it for $550-$600), so I jumped on that offer and purchased it at the store. Problem was, I had to wait until Tuesday (I bought on a Sunday) for my laptop to arrive since they didn't have any at stock at that moment, so that bummed me out a bit. I went back on Tuesday to pick it up, as well as some accessories needed for laptop use (laptop bag, cheap optical mouse, usb hub since it only comes with 2 built-in, and a Netgear wireless-g router that was on sale as well). I used Vista to get my wireless working, which was a complete pain in that ***, but that was because of Netgear's rubbish setup guide and the fact that all the support lines wanted to charge me in order to ask a few questions. I only got the router to work after completely winging it. Once done configuring Vista to the way I want it, the moment of truth came when I took the plunge and installed Ubuntu on my laptop. Because I'll be getting Windows 7 as a free upgrade when it comes out, I didn't want to fool around with the hard drive just yet, so currently I'm using a Wubi install. When I install 7 and configure it, then I'll save my configured 'version' of 9.04 to my flashdrive and partition the drive for dual-booting. 

Anyways, now to talk about Ubuntu. The very first time I used it, I checked to see if my most important function would work: wireless. I've heard many horror stories of setting up wireless under Ubuntu, and I didn't exactly have a 'normal' problem. The first time in Ubuntu, it recognized my wireless perfectly and I was able to surf the web and such. Then later, I restarted and logged into Vista to make sure nothing happened to it during the Wubi install. Everything was fine, except Ubuntu somehow broke my wireless on Vista... I had one hell of a time getting Vista to connect: I could see my network (and others, all strong signals), it even asked me for my network password so I could sign in, but it would never connect. After about an hour and a half of messing around, I somehow got it to work (I don't know what I did...:confused:). Later on, I logged back into Ubuntu and I learned that somehow, Vista broke Ubuntu's ability to connect! I found it both heartbreaking, think I could never fix this, and endlessly funny, joking about how the two OSes were sabotaging each other to make itself look good :lolflag:. Again, I could see a strong signal, it asked for a password, and no connect. Again, I still have no clue how I managed to get it working. But after about 12 hours, I was able to get both of them to behave with each other (for the most part, Vista breaks from time to time, but not too much though :)). With a stable connection, I proceeded to trying out the Add/Remove program and seeing how that worked. Coming from the land of the only way to install something was by either CD or a link on the web, the centralized downloading program was new to me, but I learned quickly. To test it out,the first program I downloaded was KBattleship, which is a fun game to mess around with actually ;). Afterwards, I went to customizing Ubuntu for my pleasure, installing the SlicknesS-black theme, Firefox add-ons, AWN, and the like. The only problems I have with Ubuntu are minor: no matter what I do, Java will not work under Ubuntu; Flash was slow in loading up Flash-based games (solved now), I'm having problems getting installed cursor themes to work, and YouTube videos are choppy in full-screen. Overall though, I love using Ubuntu: it does everything I want, support is amazing, it's faster than Vista, it's sexier than Vista 8), and it's completely free (as in beer and freedom), which for a rising college student is extremely important. Maybe I'll get around to figuring out said problems, but besides that Ubuntu is a blast to use and I look forward to installing it on every other computer I'll buy in the future and maybe even converting a few friends to using it (or at least giving it a try) :popcorn:. To all those in UbuntuForums, you're advice has been nothing but gold and the welcoming community just makes the Ubuntu experience that much better. Thank you all.
