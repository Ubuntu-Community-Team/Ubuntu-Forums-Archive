### Post by noobicon on 2009-04-28
In a previous life as a web developer I had lots of experience working with Linux remotely, but my experience was generally confined to LAMP setup and other administrative tasks, most of my development still took place on an XP machine.

Over the past year using XP began to become more and more painful and more and more depressing.  XP is still the high-bar in terms of user-friendliness, but I began to find staring at the same boring taskbar, coupled with annoyances such as hidden updates installing without my permission, creeping slowness over time, randomly disappearing settings and the general feeling of lack of control were too much to bear.  Just after Christmas 08, as I started shutting down my machine XP started installing another round of hidden updates despite my settings to the contrary, it was here that I decided enough was enough.  Booting back into windows, I headed to ubuntu.com and downloaded 8.10 to make the switch.

Booted into the LiveCD, awesome, a bit of tinkering and I almost felt like I had a usable system there and then.  With delight I clicked the install button, answered a few simple questions and let the installer do its thing.

Once done the system restarted.  Splash screen, very nice, flickering screen, flickering screen, white screen.  Bah, what a load of crap!  F**k Ubuntu I thought, and reached for the familiar and warm friend that was my XP install CD.  As the XP reinstall proceeded I heard a voice in my head - "you've been down this road before, you know where it leads".  Pain crawled across my scalp as my hand reached tentatively for the reset button.  For what seemed an eternity my hand hovered over the reset, and I saw the image of Bill Gates in my head, laughing maniacally.  At this point I passed out.

I awoke with a start.  After a moments disorientation I noted my machine was rebooting.  I also noted the XP CD lying in fragments on the floor nearby.  A multitude of virtual scars adorned my body, obviously it had been some battle.  No going back now I thought, I must make it work!

At this point I noted several options at the bottom of the boot screen, one of them indicating graphics modes.  I selected the safe graphics option and once more entered into the LiveCD environment.  All too soon the install was complete, and with great trepidation I once more rebooted.

Ding!  I had reached level 2!  I was greeted with a fairly plain looking desktop, with icons reminiscent of those on Solaris when I tinkered with fvwm at university many years ago.  Bit drab, I thought, let's see what I can break.  After clicking through the menus I started playing around with the Appearance settings.  The Visual Effects tab caught my eye, I clicked this, which prompted an autoinstall of a 3D graphics driver.  Rebooting again brought me to a black screen, garrghhh.  Undaunted this time, I set about fixing the problem.  Booted from the LiveCD again intending to reinstall, but this time I decided to research the issue via Firefox.  Within 10 minutes I was up and running again on a fully 3D accelerated desktop, I had reached level 3!  It was at this point I realised I had no sound.  After banging my head against the wall trying to get my Creative Xi-Fi card working I finally just removed it and enabled the onboard sound.  Voila, I had reached level 4 :o))

Things proceeded briskly after this point.  Within another hour I had all the bells and whistles running.  Compiz cube, new cursor themes, fonts, codecs, wallpaper, skydome, new splash screen, awn, gnome-do.  So much to play with!

Digging deeper, I was able to completely remove all trace of NTFS from my drives, by slowly shrinking the NTFS partition through gparted on the LiveCD and copying batches of files over; the whole process made a lot less painful by the fact I had a full environment running from the LiveCD and could browse the web comfortably while my drives chugged away in the background.

Three months on and I'm still here.  Everything is pretty well customised to my liking now and I feel more comfortable working with Ubuntu than I ever did with Windows.  I've tried Gnome, KDE, Xfce and Enlightenment.  Stuck with Gnome for now, but it's nice to know how easy it will be to change should I desire it.  I updated to Jaunty as soon as it became available as a release candidate; this broke my graphics again, as well as the evolution calendar and a few other bits and pieces.  By this point I felt more than equipped to deal with these issues and that's exactly what I did.  Better still, things that didn't quite work for me in Intrepid (notably the TV out resolution which was slightly borked, bluetooth desktop control and phone media transfer) now work just fine. Getting lmsensors up and running along with fancontrol was truly gratifying, and something that never worked for me on XP.  I've even set up the latest Virtualbox with OpenGL acceleration and tried out as many other distros as I could find, as well as a few BSD variants.  All nice in their own way, but I just found Ubuntu more satisfying overall.

There are still many cracks into which unsuspecting new users can fall, a side-effect of the ability to truly change anything and everything to your liking (and also a side-effect of the problems with proprietary drivers).  I've fallen into a few of these myself (mainly ATI related nonsense) and I'm more tech savvy than most; but am finding more and more that a little bit of knowledge helps fill those cracks nicely.  I've been showing it off to my friends and they're all very impressed.  Sadly many of them are locked into Windows because of Adobe's offerings, plus the fact that Linux is still just on the wrong side of intimidating for the less technical amongst us, but the desire to change is definitely there. Speaking for myself, there has been a definite positive  psychological impact as a result of going through this change, akin to chipping away layers of crust from ones brain.  My one real gripe is the lack of truly compelling games.  Half Life 2 through Wine has been a highlight, but in general I've found gaming to be unsatisfactory from the point of view of a game lover.  On the plus side, I did play too many games before when I was using XP, I'm happy now just getting regularly spanked off gnuchess.

Based on a comparison to my last attempt to switch (around 2 years ago, an unmitigated disaster which left one of my hard drives bricked) it really has come on in leaps and bounds.  I have a computer that is now more usable, more fun and not a little challenging.  I have to think more about certain things, but only because I now have a lot more options open to me.  Thank you to everyone who has contributed to Ubuntu and Linux in general, an amazing effort that finally seems to be coming into its own.

### Post by noobicon on 2009-04-29
I agree in principle with the comments regarding install, the average person would struggle with a clean install regardless of which OS (though I personally think Linux wins this just because of the lack of need for product keys and activation).  One of the key advantages Windows (unfairly) holds, however, is the fact that for many users it comes with their kit and so no install or post-install config is generally required, this will always be quicker and easier for an average user than running an install no matter how quick or effective said installer is.  Once this situation changes (i.e. Linux has at least a fair share of the pre-installed market) then undoubtedly Linux would shine and the faint-hearted would be presented with no obstacle to tasty OS goodness.  Another key factor is the ongoing problem with proprietary driver bias, again working in favour of Windows (click, next, next, reboot) vs Linux (click, next, next, edit some config, run some commands, potentially reboot).  As I said in my original post the whole process is a lot better than when I tried a few years ago, and I think and hope that it is just a matter of time before Linux gets the widespread acceptance it truly deserves, from the brave and faint of heart alike.
