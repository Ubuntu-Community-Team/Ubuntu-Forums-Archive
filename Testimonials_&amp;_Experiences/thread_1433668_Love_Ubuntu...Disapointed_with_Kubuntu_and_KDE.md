### Post by craig10x on 2010-03-19
I have been using Ubuntu for about a year now...and really love it...had an 8.10 Wubi...then a 9.04 hard install and now 9.10 (and got rid of windows also).....It's easy to use, understand works great, etc...

But i took a look at KDE...it has such a nice look...and interesting programs in it....I tried Kubuntu and was totally disapointed....

1) Seemed to have a lot of bugs in it...you'd click on a setting (say to have the display dim in an hour) and hit "apply"  Did it work?  Nope (display would still dim at 15 min default...lol)

2) Where are the screen savers? showed 2 selections and neither one worked when i applied...

3) Difficult with codecs...the Kubuntu Restricted Extras Package does not install streaming radio codecs...leaving you to try to figure out what you need to get (and i never could..gstream plug ins don't seem to work with Kubuntu like they do in Ubuntu)...

4) K Package Kit...the software installer...was very user unfriendly and didn't even work for me at all...had to end up installing Package Manager which isn't even there by default..
   
   Ubuntu's Restricted Extras give you everything except the Encrypted DVD

It was also overly complicated and convoluted to use..can't see why a beginner would like KDE over Gnome...everything has a million setting and adjustments and it's not easy to find everything you are looking for (like in Ubuntu and Gnome)...

I tried some other KDEs also..such as PC Linux Os and had similar problems with those as well (though at least that had codecs installed already....lol)

It's a shame the attractiveness of KDE and some of it's programs and the simplicty and easy of use and reliability of Gnome (and Ubuntu) can't be combined into one system..

Ubuntu seems to put a lot of "love" into the Gnome side but not in the KDE side..really a shame i must say....bear in mind that Ubuntu and other Gnome systems have worked flawlessly on my laptop..which is a one year old Toshiba which seems to be very "Linux Compatible" for sure...The Kdes i tried all had bugs, problems and weren't that "user friendly"...

I guess i'll have to stay with Ubuntu and Gnome...really wanted to like Kubuntu and kde but it wasn't "liking me back".....

I have said it before and will say it again: I think canonical should concentrate all of its resources into making Ubuntu even better, instead of create other flavors that don't necessarily look or work that good.

There are many distros that do a better job at implementing KDE.  Mandriva and Fedora are at the top of my list, OpenSUSE is also better than Ubuntu.  Arch seems to be very good as well.  I haven't tried PCLinuxOS, but it also looks good.  I would recommend you give either Fedora or Mandriva a try.  Mandriva specifically is very good for starters, almost up there with Ubuntu in terms of ease of use.

Sometimes it is hard to realise what is to blame on the distro and what on the window manager, so I tried several different distros before I built an opinion on KDE.  These are some of the things I dislike about it (Note I have not tried KDE SC4.4 yet!):

1.- In my opinion, it concentrates on looks and making every aspect of the GUI customizable, but some very simple things seem to be missing.  

For example, I love how easily you can change themes and icons in GNOME.  Just switching to a new theme updates controls, window borders, menus, etc., and icons are consistently changed everywhere.  KDE is a bit of a nightmare when it comes to this.  Changing icon themes often proves to only work partially (updates the menu but not folders, for example).  

Controls and window borders all look old and low definition IMHO.  I always end up using Emerald instead of the Kwin themes available.  Controls look bad to me as well, they lack the definition and crispness that you can find in many GNOME themes.  When using KDE, I always end up using clearlooks, as I hate Oxygen  or other themes buttons.

2.- I think KDE manages to make very simple things very complex.  For example, I love how you can very easily create custom keyboard shortcuts in GNOME.  KDE has many predefined combinations, but I still have not found how to create my own!  I guess there may be a way to do it, but the fact that I am struggling to find it, even googling about it, shows that it is not very intuitive.

Another thing that is really clear and simple in GNOME that I find cumbersome in KDE is creating panel shortcuts.  GNOME allows you to create shortcuts straight from menu icons, using drag and drop, etc.  KDE requires the addition of a panel launcher, and their functionality is often very limited.  Just trying to change the launcher icon can prove quite a trip!

3.- GNOME is usually most widely used, so many applications are developed under GTK and either are not available under KDE, or simply look terrible on it.  Dropbox is quite a must for me, but it is not available in KDE.  Firefox, Skype, GIMP, Geany and many other GTK applications simply look bad under KDE.

4.- I like how GNOME manages opened docs history correctly, which KDE does not.  For example, If I open a session as USER1 and from that session I open a doc as USER2, GNOME will only show USER1 opened docs under USER1 session.  KDE seems to show all opened docs in the session, regardless of who opened them.  This is no biggie, but just feels incorrect for me.

5.- In my experience, Compiz manager allows for less options in KDE.  I have had dual boot setups with Ubuntu and Mandriva on the same box, and compiz config displayed many more features under Ubuntu.  Initially I thought that was a Mandriva limitation, but Fedora and OpenSUSE had the same, so I am assuming it is KDE specific.

6.- Applications have heavily improved lately, so I no longer feel this is a drawback for me, but it used to be.  Ark, Kget, Amarok, Kate, Kwrite, Ktorrent, K3B, Kdenlive, Kdevelop...  They are all very decent applications, some times better than their GNOME counterparts.  K3B, for example, is awesome.  I have installed it in some of my Ubuntu boxes because it does what brasero sometimes cannot.  

In summary, I actually think GNOME can look better than KDE with enough skills and taste, while keeping functionality, performance and ease of use right up there.  

On the other hand, I must say KDE development seems to be more aggressive, effective and efficient.  I like where the future of KDE is going, but can't really say the same about GNOME3.
