### Post by burnsmicro on 2012-01-23
If you are a first time Linux user contemplating Ubuntu 11.10...

            [SIZE=3]** ...Don't. **[/SIZE]

Ubuntu 11.10 requires considerable luck and/or experience to set up adequately. Instead, consider Ubuntu 10.04 or a totally different distribution, or, heaven forbid, Winows.

I am sure Ubuntu 11.10 is very good ..once it has been set up properly ..by someone who is intimately familiar with Linux.

Ubuntu 11.10 works nicely off the LiveCD. But the version you install may not be as friendly. When you first set up Ubuntu 11.10, there is a very good chance your 11.10-PC will boot up to a black screen with a terse message "**Cannot display this video mode". **Getting help from the good folks at ubuntuforums.org will require considerable Linux expertise to understand. You will be advised to hit the [Shift] key to interrupt the bootloader. Bbut, by default, this feature is disabled and you will be directed to enable by editing /etc/default/grub, using a command line user-hostile editor, VI. Obvious to a Linux head. Intimidating to a new user.

If you want to network your 11.10-PC with Windows PC's and a printer, you will have to go again to ubuntuforums.org for help and you will require considerable Linux expertise to make sense of the help. I have used Ubuntu 11.10 for almost 2 months and I still cannot network with the Windows PCs or access the printer attached to one of them. In the process I am learning a lot about Linux ..But I am not sure I really wanted to learn all this.

If you want to view YouTube videos, you will have to install Flash. Not difficult, but far from obvious. You have to google for How-To's.

I have spent about 70 hours searching forums, googling and experimenting, and I still do not have a fully functional Ubuntu 11.10-PC. What does work, however, is quite fast and responsive, but not without a few very annoying "features" like:
 
[LIST]
[*]Alt-Tab switching from say,     Thunderbird, with a couple of windows open to LibreOffice with a     couple of windows open is an exercise in frustration. You can see     the window you want to open, but you cannot click on it to select     it. You must tab through all your open apps...
[*]Files, the Folder/File explorer, provides little or no     information about the selected folder or file. You have to open it     and go to "properties" to, for instance, find the date of     modification.
[/LIST]
 So, why am I sticking with Ubuntu 11.10? Hard to explain. With 70 hrs at, say, $10/hr, I could have bought about 5 copies of Windows. Then there is the time of all the wonderful volunteers who tried to help me, which would be equivalent to many more copies of Windows. So, it is certainly not the "free ride" that appeals to me.


 Part of it is I got used to waking my PC with the tap of a key, entering my passwords and, voila, my PC is exactly how I had left it. I do not have to hang around while my [Windows] PC whirs away before asking for a password, then whirs away some more to eventually restore my sessions.

Perhaps the most powerful reason for sticking with Ubuntu 11.10 is because of the great community supporting it and the thought that, perhaps, I may some day contribute back.
Aside from the practical matter of getting my Ubuntu 11.10 PC to network and display correctly, my biggest concern is that Linux is loosing it. Market penetration is slipping, whereas I would like to see it growing. And I hate to say it, but Ubuntu 11.10 is contributing to this erosion by inoculating new users against Linux. The only way to reverse this trend would be a new-user-friendly version of Linux.

### Post by leth on 2012-01-23
Maybe you just got unlucky with your hardware, because I've booted Ubuntu 11.10 on 4 computers (3 desktops, 1 laptop) and never had that error.  The laptop gave me video issues that were fixed by upgrading the BIOS.  The only desktop to give me problems is a z68 chipset and it wouldn't install with the onboard video, but after getting Ubuntu installed using a PCI-E card, it works just fine with the onboard video.  I suspect I could have got it to install using the "nomodeset" boot option, but I didn't bother going back to try that.

Printing has yet to be a problem.  I did have to search through the forums to figure out how to install my Brother network multi-function printer, but the steps worked and the process didn't give me any trouble.  Just this past weekend I was at my girlfriend's place and needed to print.  I plugged in the USB cable for her HP Deskjet printer and Ubuntu recognized it and installed drivers without me touching it.  It worked flawlessly.

I didn't have to Google for how-to's for Flash, I just used the Software Center.

I have never in my life thought to use the mouse when using Alt-Tab (e.g. in Windows.)  It just seems un-natural to go from using a keyboard shortcut to a mouse click.  Have you tried Super-W instead of Alt-Tab?  I like it better than Alt-Tab.

Did you try using List view?  Press Ctrl-2 or go to View->List.  You can customized the columns with View->Visible Columns.   Note that it will make the width of the Name column wide enough to fit your longest file name, so you may have to scroll to the right or make the window very wide before you will see the other columns.  Or just resize them by dragging the bar between the column headers.

I find Ubuntu runs a lot faster than Windows on all my hardware, and it's just refreshing to use something other than Windows after being stuck on it all day at work.  I like that it's free and there's this sort of joy I get from not being forced to use Microsoft's crap and still be productive.

I'm sorry to hear you've been had such rough luck getting started with Ubuntu.  It has worked quite flawless for me so far (knock on wood) and I find it fairly user friendly.  In terms of getting things set up, that might not always be the friendliest experience, but neither is Windows (I would know, I am an IT guy by profession and spend a lot of time fixing Windows stuff for people at work and home)

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2012-01-24
Just because you had video troubles on one particular model of computer (there are over a million different models of computer made), does not mean that "there's a good chance" of trouble.

Networking is easy; if you're finding it hard, you might just be doing it wrong. Nautilus can browse your existing network for shares. If that fails, you can put in the IP address directly of the share you want to access. As for printers, as long as the Windows computer is sharing it, the Printing control panel on Ubuntu can see it too.

Creating a file share, and creating a shared printer, are also easy. Even if you don't know how to do it, you can have it done in 10 minutes, including Googling time.

The Files lense in Unity is not meant as a full-featured file browser; but as new users are treating it as though it should be, there must be something done about it. Some way of making it 'search-only' or adding features to it; either way should do the trick.

Ubuntu 11.10 IS new-user-friendly. It has some ways to go, but there are no more regressions than usual.

### Post by VoodooLoveDog on 2012-01-24
First, let me say that I'm sorry you are frustrated. It happens, I've been there. I've been installing OSs onto hardware for the better part of 20 years and I still run into problems that you describe above. I'm currently running into an issue with Ubuntu 11.10 that will not gracefully restart on a Dell Optiplex 390. I haven't yet figured out why. 

One thing you must understand is that due to the variance in hardware, no one OS will be able to work perfectly with every single piece of hardware out there. It is just not practical or possible. If that is the PC experience you desire, I suggest purchasing an Apple desktop or laptop. The beauty of the Apple products is that the OS is custom tailored for the hardware it is installed on. Unfortunately, that customization/brand comes at a cost. 

Your next option is trying one of the BSDs. The port system that those family of distributions employ better lends itself to hardware variance. However, in terms of difficulty, I think any of the BSDs will be more challenging to install than Ubuntu. 

Your position is quite unfair. You lacked the expertise or the knowledge set to install the software to the hardware so you attacked the software comparing it to Windows. Windows has just as many problems during installation as Ubuntu. Try installing XP to a machine that has the Intel Matrix Raid functionality or is using ACPI. If you do not understand the hoops you must jump through to install, you will end up just as frustrated as above. I just ran into a problem with Windows 7 the other day. I couldn't for the life of me get the manufacturer supplied drivers to install correctly for a serial add-in card. (/Shrug)

In my experience, Ubuntu OS is vastly superior to Windows when it comes to installing the correct driver set on OS install. Just so you know, it's your inexperience that is driving the frustration, not the OS. Linux is not Windows.
