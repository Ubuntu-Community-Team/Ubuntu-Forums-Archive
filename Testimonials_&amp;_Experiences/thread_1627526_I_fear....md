### Post by trinitydan on 2010-11-30
I guess.  I think the game selection is not bad.  There is a variety of emulators available from the Ubuntu software center, from ATARI to N64.  You may not be able to play windows games but the selection of games that will work in Ubuntu including online games has improved a lot over the last few years.  I love how Ubuntu 10.10 has xbox 360 controller drivers built in too!  It's a little tricky getting it configured to the different shape of the N64 controller the games were made to work with, but most N64 roms play fine.  Nexuiz is available from the software center, as is Alien Arena, if you're into online first person shooter style games.  Torcs and VDrift are both quality racing games.  I know if a person has their mind set on one particular thing there can be no swaying them, but to me there are plenty of possibilities for gaming with Ubuntu.  Me and my little brother do battle with 2 xbox 360 controllers on super tux cart.  I'm not trying to convince you to change your mind or anything, just pointing out my different experience and perspective on gaming with Ubuntu.  

Then again, Mupen64, Torcs, VDrift, Alien Arena, Nexuiz, Urban Terror, etc. are cool and all, but Windows comes with Minesweeper.  And solitaire.  You don't want to walk away from that...:D

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2010-11-30
[> 29732]("http://ubuntuforums.org/member.php?u=1088707")

I've always been shocked that Ubuntu/Linux isn't a Windows clone. One would  think that with Windows driving the computing world, no one would dare  contest them. :rolleyes:

If Windows games and apps. fits your needs than logic might suggest you  remain in that environment. It's been my experience that some  individuals (I know) expect Ubuntu/Linux to work just like Windows and  be totally compatible. Upon finding that it isn't, they reject Ubuntu  because it comes with a learning curve and doesn't run their Windows  apps. Since it's free to try & use - there's no harm or foul.

As indicated by others, one possible solution is to notify the apps and  request they port to Linux. I seriously doubt they will, but it's always  worth a try. Or try Wine. One thing to remember, Linux offers choice.  The alternative is no or limited choice. Also, keep in mind that  Ubuntu/Linux and most of the associated FOSS apps are free. Opening the  world of computing to all, not just those who can afford to pay the  price.

Good Luck with your OS of choice. My only suggestion, is you not invest  time worrying about any software that doesn't meet you  needs/expectations (including OS's). :wink:

Just $0.02
