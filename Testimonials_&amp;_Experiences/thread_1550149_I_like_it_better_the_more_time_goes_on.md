### Post by Thryn on 2010-08-10
I bought a new laptop a little more than a month and a half ago, about six months after my old iBook died (RIP). I put a significant amount of thought into what kind to get, and settled on a small, lightweight notebook. I didn't have the money for another Apple, but with all the issues I've had with Vista, I don't trust Windows. Still, I knew I might need it for school (it's a very Windows-centric department) so with some trepidation I settled on a dual-boot.

A few days after I got it I installed Ubuntu. The installation process itself was easy, very similar to installing OS X, but I had some minor issues at first: my wacom tablet didn't work, the screen flickered, and the image from my webcam was upside-down. It took me a while to fix these issues, and I'm not happy that I have to fix the wacom one for each new kernel, but since they were common problems, there's a lot of information on how to get them fixed. And frankly, I'd rather be dealing with that than with viruses - it's a whole lot easier and less scary.

I've tried Kubuntu and Lubuntu as well, but so far I like regular Ubuntu the best, in part because I have the easiest time getting things to work (though if there's a silver and blue theme similar to those two, I would love to use it). And recently I installed 64-bit (so that now I am triple-booting) and it works so nicely that I might stick with it instead of 32-bit. It was also much easier the second time, because I knew what I was doing. I also found a thread on these forums with many power-saving tips for my laptop (an ASUS UL30 series). I've gotten hooked on customizing things, and every time I have to use Windows I miss having multiple desktops, which are very useful on my smallish screen. I'm also glad that Ubuntu is comparatively lighter (it uses way less RAM than Windows 7). I haven't run into problems with using too much RAM yet, but it could happen, so it's nice to have the leeway. And with the powersaving tips, I don't have to clamp down on the settings all that much to get good battery life (for instance, I can make the screen pretty bright). I also like that it's easy to switch languages - something that OS X is also good for, but that Windows isn't. I grant you that it's rough with more unusual languages, but many programs are available in French versions, which is enough for me (obviously I use the English version when that fails).

I'm also a bit of a gamer, and today I was overjoyed to find out that there's a Linux version of my favorite scrolling shooter from my iBook days, Gunroar (it's even in the repositories!). I usually can't justify the price of commercial games, and I'm mostly a casual gamer, so I'm very happy with being able to get simple games for free, and installing them easily without worrying about malware.

I considered myself pretty tech-savvy before, but I had no Linux experience and only a little with Unix, so I'm learning new things all the time (which I love). I thought I might have to use Windows pretty frequently, but at the moment I mostly boot into it to make sure it stays updated. We'll see if I really do need it when school starts up.

ETA: I forgot to mention that Ubuntu boots more than twice as fast as Windows 7 for me.
