In that application, MS is trying to take some market share, not so much defend a big share they already had.  This is the closest thing I can see to direct competition.

2) Home computing:  90% of "typical" home users are afraid of Linux because they think it is still a GUI-less console app, or just want to use MS Word, Outlook Express and buy crap on Amazon.  There really is no need for them to switch OS's and MS knows it.

Maybe a picture of their kids as the background, and an 800 number to call Dell when their computer (inevitably) breaks.

3) Business / Engineering: There are 2 sides to this.  A lot of researchers use Unix/Linux as it is, and MS never had that market.  They never will, and since it is relatively small, probably don't care to.  Now, every single MCAD program I have ever used is intended for Windows.  Some can run fine in Linux, but most companies do not want to hassle with emulator issues and database access for multiple OS's.  I worked at Cisco about a year ago, and certainly Unix shells were all over for monitoring the commercial routers I was involved with.  Windows was on every workstation I saw though, since the MCAD work I and others did required it.  Maybe someday Linux will be able to get in on that part of the market more, but for now it does not look too likely.

_The Cliff's:_
MS and Linux are not really competitors IMO.  Perhaps in the future they will be, but MS is currently a $60B company and Linux for the most part is a volunteer effort.  I by no means am trying to belittle Linux as I love it and Windows itself drove me to hop on this bandwagon.  Still, such a large percentage of consumers just want something familiar, that Linux would be a very hard sell (well, it IS).  I am working on getting my mom & GF on it right now, and i probably will succeed.  However, there are not enough enthusiasts out there willing to hold hands for people who do not use forums and Google to solve problems.

Hopefully this will not incite too many flames.  I know how personally a lot of folks take the OS "battle" but this really is how I am seeing it based upon working in a technical field.  Comments / corrections welcome (afterall, I AM still pretty new to the Linux side of life!).  Windows has its place, and Linux has its place.  Each has its benefits and drawbacks (although I am so sick of Windows that I did not even consider dual-booting lol).  I would really LIKE to see Linux become a market competitor as competition drives innovation.  However, that could be tough since profit drives competition, and Linux is all about the free-love (in the home-user/enthusiast sense anyway).
