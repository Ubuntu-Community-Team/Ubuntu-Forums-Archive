### Post by rsimmons619 on 2011-05-18
I used to be a full time Mandrake user about ten years ago, flipping between Gnome and KDE as they leap frogged each other. But the job made me switch back to Windows because of certain software that had to be used. 
 
Anyway, I was putzing around one day and decided to see what's been going on in the community and ran across Ubuntu. At work I use an HP T3500 Xeon with 12 gigs of ram and Win7 (great machine). Anyway, I tried a Live CD and really liked what I saw. As an experienced computer user and former technician, I found it very easy to get around and I understood immediately where Ubuntu wanted to go for ease of use. It also occurred to me that this would really tick off Linux power users. It's not for them. And if I was still in the game in that fashion I might have felt the same. But since Linux comes in so many flavors and you can fall back to any desktop you want, no biggie in my book. I also downloaded a PPC version and tried it on my iBook G4 with a gig of ram. It wouldn't run unity, but the classic version ran very well. The lcd screen looked brighter than usual, which was a plus.
 
So, I had a an HP Mini netbook at home that sucked horribly with WinXP, has 512 meg of ram, an eight gig hdd with a 2 gig removable storage stick. It was pretty unuseable in it's current state, so I decided to wipe it out and install Ubuntu using a USB stick. It installed flawlessly and ran Unity by default without a hitch. Even on a 7" screen, it looked pretty good. The only thing that didn't work right away was the web cam, but installing Cheese took care of that. Other than that, everything was pretty perfect. 
 
Now the real test. I gave the Mini to my teenage daughter to use. She's comfortable with Windows and Mac and uses both. She took to it like a fish in water. Her only question was understanding the file system and where her files where going. She plugged in her Nikon Coolpix camera, it got recognized right away, Shotwell opened, she transferred her photos and videos and starting dropping things on Facebook. She plugged in her iPhone and it got recognized as both a music player and storage device with no assistance from her. She's totally happy with it. I also gave her an external USB powered HDD for extra storage. The only thing I haven't gotten around to doing is setting her up to the wireless printer in the house. 
 
For me, I had an old IBM Netvista (small form factor) laying around that I thought I'd try with the CD install. 1ghz speed, 386 meg of ram, 40 gig hdd, 1 meg video (yes one meg). Runs great. It doesn't run Unity, obviously, but the classic version runs just fine. So did LibreOffice. Cool.
 
So I grabbed my old workstation I built back in '02 I think. AMD 1ghz, 1 gig of ram, 200gig hdd, ATI 9800 video card. This I had problems with. Took forever to boot, then on install it would flake out. Tried Kubuntu, same thing. Tried Ubuntu Studio, same thing. Over and over. Finally, got Ubuntu Studio to load. Nice. Messing around, installed xfce from terminal, lxde and KDE as well. I like KDE's new interface, but it still leaves me kind of blah and it runs much slower. xfce and lxde ran very well on the machine. Then something happened, not sure what I did and now can't remember what I was doing and I screwed the install up. So tried reloading from cd again. Install problems again. Made a new cd with a slow burn, same problem. Hmmm. removed front usb's, removed floppy. Machine has a usb card with 5 usbs and a firewire with 3 ports as well as a soundblaster live card, and 2 ethernet jacks. The problem? The stupid DVD Rom drive. Changed it out for another unit, install went without a hitch. It's always something right in front of your face.
 
Anyway, I see the possibilities. I grabbed another machine from church. A Dell Optiplex P4 with a gig of ram. I'm going to turn it into a multi-track recorder and see how LMMS and Ardour work in a real environment. I'll run it off some auxiliaries until the bugs are worked out, but I think it will be good. We also run a program with kids for after school and the summer, so I'm looking for some donated computers if anyone knows where to get some. I want to use Linux to keep costs down but still have a robust system. Eubuntu looks like it will fill the gap nicely. 
 
Finally, I've read the frustrations of others on this forum and I understand where they are coming from, but I do think Ubuntu is moving in the right direction to bring computing to the masses. I see the vision and I get it. You'll still have to pry my Mac from my cold dead fingers (for now), but I really like the way things have grown in the community and looking forward to things in the future.
