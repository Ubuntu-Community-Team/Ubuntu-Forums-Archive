### Post by phaed on 2009-02-28
I've been using Ubuntu for almost four years.  You can look at my profile, I registered on Ubuntu Forums in May of 2005, which is around the time that I started using Ubuntu.  A few days ago I realized that as a student at my university, I could download a free (as in money) copy of Vista -- and not some watered down "student" version, but Vista Ultimate.  I thought, well, I haven't had a need for Windows in years, but why turn down a $200-300 piece of software?  Maybe in the future I'll need to install some special Windows-only software (that doesn't work well in WINE), so it would be good to have it around.

The package came as a disc image which I downloaded onto an XP machine at school (the reason for this is that the university site for downloading the software was only compatible with IE, go figure).  Of course, IE first downloads the file to a temporary folder and then transfers it to the destination folder, which in this case was the Desktop.  It took *13 minutes* to transfer the 2.4 GB iso file.  In Ubuntu, I can copy 4 GB of data from a DVD in 2-3 minutes, and it's even faster when copying from one place in the hard drive to another.

The only software available on that XP box to burn the image was the Roxio CD/DVD Creator.  I tried several times, but after giving me several errors in a row that there was an "undefined" error while attempting to burn to the DVD, I gave up.  I tried installing the ISO Recorder application, but it gave me an error that I was "out of system resources."  So, I booted an Ubuntu Live CD. :)  Luckily the computer had two DVD drives, so I booted from one and used Nautilus to burn the image onto a DVD in the other.  Went off without a hitch.

I was still nervous that there might be data corruption on such a large file (Nautilus has had problems in the past), but I installed Vista just fine on my home computer.  Of course, a word needs to be said about that.  I can install Ubuntu in 15-20 minutes and only one reboot is required.  Vista took some 45 minutes and rebooted the computer three times for unknown reasons.  It gave me zero information about what it was doing.

But that's water under the bridge.  I finally logged into Vista only to discover that it didn't recognize my network adapter.  After going through a few help features, it suggested that I download the necessary drivers off the web.  Well, you can't do that if you don't have internet access!  If there's one piece of hardware that Vista should be universally compatible with, it's the network adapter, since that is the gateway to everything else. 

My solution?  Well, I was planning to reinstall Ubuntu anyway, providing ~50 GB for Vista and ~200 GB for Ubuntu on my 250 GB hard drive, so I went ahead and installed Ubuntu, which DID recognize my network adapter along with everything else.  Then I went to the Dell web site and downloaded the necessary Vista driver and saved it in the Vista partition.  Then I rebooted into Vista and installed it.  So Ubuntu saved Vista's *** in that situation.

My impression of Vista?  Everything is needlessly complicated.  Winding your way through the various dialog boxes is onerous.  Everything in GNOME is a few clicks away.  Also, non-native applications look bad.  Default Firefox looks like crap in Vista.  I changed the theme to Camino immediately.  Still, I don't much like the look of it.  The panel and the transparent window border is nice, but the widgets aren't.  

Also, installing any third party applications requires searching the wild, wild web, where you don't know what you're getting.  I much prefer the repos.

My conclusion?  Vista will stay on my computer for those unpredictable just-in-case moments, but I am firmly commited to the usability and simplicity of Ubuntu.

### Post by phaed on 2009-02-28
Also, one last difference in usability.  I have an Nvidia graphics card on this machine, and in both cases, installing Ubuntu or Vista, before the drivers were loaded, I was forced into 800x600 resolution.  With Vista I had to pop in the CD to install the drivers.  Vista made no mention that drivers were needed or available.  It was happy to let me stay in 800x600 mode. Obviously I knew the difference, but some people with little computer experience could suffer in that resolution forever.  As soon as I booted Ubuntu it made me aware that proprietary drivers were available and easily installed them (version 177 in Intrepid, in this case, although I am aware that 180 is available by default in Jaunty).

The long and the short of it is that from the perspective of a naive computer user, Ubuntu is more compatible and easier to use than Vista.  The only places where Vista wins are where roadblocks are engineered into the system, like by hardware manufacturers who only provide drivers for Vista / OS X.  Ubuntu takes care of the user much better than Vista along the way, from installation to recognizing different partitions/file systems to recognizing the need for drivers, to its usability.  Experienced Windows users may find Ubuntu harder to use because they already have a set of expectations about where things should be.  That's not good.  Just look at how KDE users responded to KDE 4, even though naive users have said that KDE 4's kickstart menu is easier than KDE 3.5.  But the learning curve is much lower in Ubuntu / GNOME than in Vista for naive users.

The ONLY barriers that remain, in my opinion, are games and drivers, which are both engineered barriers by outside entities.  Linux / GNOME / Ubuntu developers have done what they could, and have succeeded marvellously compared to Vista.
