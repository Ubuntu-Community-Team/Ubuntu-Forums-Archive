### Post by rs3 on 2006-12-20
scouring the forums for other testimonials has indicated that i've very little to add, but...

i started off with computers at five years of age, inheriting my grandfather's apple ][+ and learning to throw together programs of little consequence in AppleSoft BASIC (with occasional ML routines).  i had both a DOS 3.3 user's guide and an AppleSoft BASIC programming guide, and i read both cover-to-cover with utter fascination--everything was laid bare for me to discover, understand, and apply, and though my capacity for such was quite limited with my age, i felt a sense of ownership that, until ubuntu, i've long since lost.

i had a IIgs (marvelous computer, imo) and enjoyed it for a spell, but i was soon thrust into the de facto world of pc clones, windows 3.1, and the like.  gone were the joys of casual programming and the marvels of true ownership; instead, i was subject to rampant reboots (windows 3.x crashed like a mother, as i'm sure many can attest) and an entrapment of the greatest severity.  i had limited control of my operating environment and limited understanding of my hardware and its capabilities.

i have struggled to reclaim some sense of ownership over the years, insisting on building my own computers rather than buying into prefabricated systems wherever possible; even so, i am no stranger to the scarily insular world of modern computing via windows.  as such, i have struggled, also, to take the plunge and go gnu/linux since '99, but to very limited avail--until ubuntu.  this distribution (and this philosophy) gives the linux world a warmth and a humanity unseen in other such operating systems and, for me, it welcomes me back to that very sense of ownership.

when something puzzles me, the ubuntu community almost unfailingly points me in the right direction (though i am, by and large, a notorious lurker, i do witness countless instances of people helping people, and the archives of such serve to benefit me equally).  moreover, i am **invited** to know my computer intimately, to know my operating system and my applications, and i'm free to modify any of it.  the same obviously cannot be said of windows, and while it is sometimes a struggle to achieve comparable functionality in any distribution of linux, the struggle is minimal with ubuntu and worthwhile for the freedom it yields.

i once again own my computer.  i'm having fun now. :D

i've branded both of my systems appropriately (with much thanks to system76 and their free stickers!) and took the plunge with total commitment.  while it's not always easy, it's always worth it--and i encourage others to own their computers.
