### Post by endafy on 2008-07-22
January, 14 2008
    I work at a huge building in New York City which houses several businesses. I work in IT and on a friend's laptop he brought in what looked closely to a Macintosh but it was a Dell. My natural reaction was "Cool theme, where did you pick it up at?" He snidely said KDE. Of course I never heard of it.

January, 15 2008
    I went home and discovered over Google that there was a way, illegally, to install OSX onto a computer, OSX86. Next day I went in flustered at the fact he would jeopardize his and my job with such BS. Don't bring that crap to work. If you want me to try it simply bring in a DVD and let me install it on a computer which I know will never backfire on me. He was like no it is Linux you ***, KDE. We didn't talk for quite some time as I had offended him.

     Now I have heard of Linux and played around with it a few times but never to an extent as we use HP-UX on the servers and XP on the workstations. All of my PC's at home ran XP. We never use X11 on the servers as it is pretty useless. When I did play with Linux it was with Gnome or Fluxbox. Fluxbox as I can run it in Windows. I never dove into it as I always had issues with sound, printers, graphics cards, you name it.

May, 09 2008
     One day he came in and the server room was pretty silent and we had to fix a Solaris blade and over small talk was forgiven. He then handed me a Kubuntu 8.04 LTS with KDE 3.5.9 pressed and everything from the get a free Ubuntu CD program you all run. I then proceeded to install it and then later realized how much KDE better suited me and this specific distribution. The default theme was pretty but better suited for my friend Carl. I mean no offense by this he is my best friend and he uses this now too. He actually showed me that I can change my themes and all. I quickly found KDE was much more flexible and better working than Gnome. Every computer in my house now runs Kubuntu. I later asked I don't remember the date if he always carried a Linux CD around hoping to give it away and he laughed.

Monday July, 21 2008
     I went into a board meeting and apparently the businesses needed to save money. Of course with my big mouth when questioned on how to cut with the computing budget, instead of Vista Ubuntu is free saving 100 dollars per license or more. Mind you this is Monday, no coffee yet, 6:30 AM. They are now talking about installing K/X/Ubuntu on over 25,000 workstations at work within 6 months and we are to train ourselves in it. A good 80 percent now uses Sun Open Office on XP. I learned Ubuntu in under a month which took me years with Windows. This is saving the IT department over $4 MILLION in Microsoft Licenses. We are taking the money to invest $175 thousand in training. We will finish this fiscal year well ahead. If this works I have job security and every workstation in the building will have an Ubuntu variant on it, if not I am canned and back out looking for another job. That is a cool million computers over easy. If this works it will save the companies here close to $300 million minus training costs combined. If this works it will spread to where the companies have spread to in smaller cities and towns. Bank and law firms and other various records departments.

     Lets see where this goes I am off to work now to begin rolling it out onto the machines.

### Post by CarlosNYB on 2008-07-27
Re: hospital records, data processing, mail, etc., yes, better security on linux is a MAJOR advantage, though of course configuring group mail can be an issue whenever changing a system.

Re: stray programs/files...  In most places I've worked, there are people with specialized macros in MSOffice, converting them can be a REAL pain, and as an IT person I hated it when people had downloaded CRAP and written bad macros and then cried to IT for help... some people really do want to have easily accessible macros, however, and it's easy to make them in MSOffice, so some power-secretaries and the like may chafe at having to learn macros in OO if they aren't used to it.  It's a good thing many people are already using OO in your system!

Re: Photoshop, people who do professional destop publishing/printing, etc.  You just can't expect artists and the like to switch from solid Adobe software on PC or Mac (usually Mac).  It's insane and unprofessional to expect them to do it, so don't even try.  Gimp won't be accepted.  Professional Graphics is done on Mac and on Silicon Graphics workstations.

Re: Law applications, and some accounting, etc.  Sometimes people have a system in place, millions of dollars, and it requires some OS, it's just the way it is, and conversion is a nightmare, very costly.

So yeah, focus on the people who rely mostly on Open Office and Firefox, and the people whos major systems are already Unix based, and you should be fine.
