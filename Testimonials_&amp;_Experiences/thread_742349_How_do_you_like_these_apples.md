### Post by warbread on 2008-04-01
1.8ghz P4
1.5gb pc 2700
GeForce 4600
Breezy Badger

This was my first lasting Linux experience.  I was fresh out of high school and bored to tears with community college.  My CS major friend knew I had a slight interest in what Linux is, and so he pointed me to Ubuntu.  We had both tried a few distros before Ubuntu became popular, but were frustrated with the install process.  It was at this point that I decided I should try this "dual boot" thing.  I had many gigs of music, most of which was from CDs that had been stolen from me, as well as videos, save games, bookmarks, assignments, etc.  Essentially, all of my life from the beginning of high school on was on that Windows box.  Guess what?

I lost every bit of it in a partitioning error on my part.  

Was I unhappy?  Yup.  Did I blame Linux and give up?  Hell no.  I kept going.  Instead of dual booting, I dove in head first and partitioned the whole drive for my first real Linux experience. My goals were fairly lofty, too.  With only the experience of a young Windows power user, I wanted to not only learn how to use the terminal, but get Twinview working, which was not a trivial matter back in 5.04.  I not only did that, but also got my sound working and playing MP3s, all in one night.  Of course, this would only take me a matter of minutes now, but boy was I ecstatic!  From then on, I've been hooked, and have gotten Linux to work on a variety of machines:

2.8ghz AMD Athlon
512mb ram
GeForce FX 5700 LE

400mhz Pentium II
64mb ram
Video Card?

1.7ghz Pentium III
256 mb Ram
32mb TnT 2

500mhz Pentium III
128mb ram
32mb TnT 2

1.6ghz HP Laptop
512mb 
ATI x200m

2.4ghz Core 2 duo Sager NP2090
2gb ram
GeForce 8600m

I've played with hardware ranging from the worst to among the best, and with every version of Ubuntu from 5.04 on and many other distros.  I now have a dedicated Ubuntu studio rig that I use, quite successfully and without much trouble, to record, mix, master and produce music and my best laptop which is dual booting Windows XP (for games) and Ubuntu for everything else.  I watch DVDs, play all of my music, watch videos from Guitar World and Computer Music magazine as well as type all of my homework, (in 4 different languages - the only thing I've found significantly easier to do in Windows) synthesize and analyze speech in Praat and edit my photos, all in Ubuntu.  It works.  Not perfect, but it works very, very well.

My point is actually quite simple.  I have a message to those who are new to Ubuntu and finding themselves struggling:  keep going.  The more you put into this, the more you will get out of it.  Linux is getting easier to use, but it's still not perfect.  You WILL, however, learn much, much more about computers and technology.  Don't give up just because it's a little tough.

My message to the whiney nay-sayers:  for almost every problem, there is a solution.  If you don't want to take the time to find it, don't blame the software.  For every problem you've got with Linux, I've got with Windows or Mac OS.  If you don't like free software, go back to your $300 malware bundle.  

The end.
