GEEK:
<fun story>
My Linux experience relates back to history. The fun, and most educating part comes here: I was at working practice in a company that made some robotics and other cool stuff. I quickly learned that they were all Linux guys. First thing in the IT room that caught my eye was a cup of tea with Tux on it. The cool industry class robots were run by Linux servers (according to manager, with 7 years uptime) and python. I even saw their source code,Whoa! I'm serious, they didn't allow me to bring camera to the factory part, corporation secrets, funny guys.
</fun story>

### Post by JA2 on 2007-01-01
I had a similar situation with my younger sister and I sharing my home PC when she stays over at my house some days each week. 

I dual-boot between Windows (games) and Linux (games & everything else), but didn't want her installing any applications & or unknown malware from the Net that I wouldn't know about until after the computer developed a seizure or crashed out with a BSOD. So I gave her an Ubuntu account, customized the desktop themes & icons to resemble what she was used to at work. 

Of course, there were the inevitable questions... "What's Nautilus?", "Where is Internet Explorer?", "What's this funny orange circle thingy?", "What is Firefox??".  

Over time though, she got used to the tabs feature while browsing (no more window-hunting on the taskbar), got very acquainted with my music library & the artist/lyrics lookup features in Amarok & Listen Player (about to wean her onto Exaile), changes her desktop themes & backgrounds on a regular basis to suit her mood (I haven't enabled Beryl/Compiz yet...soon) and now the gripes are minimal-to-nonexistent - except when I do my bi-annual distribution upgrades and things...... change.  

But she's proof to me that anyone can get accustomed to the Linux desktop over a short time, as long as they can do & access the same things they are used to in an alternate environment.  There's still a bit further to go in making it seamless. Whether it's Gnome, KDE, XFCE or any other popular UI, I think a simplified interface is what the new user should see first, with the options to turn on any/all other interface features just a couple of clicks away. And automated help should be gratuitous... lots of wizards & pop-ups. Maybe there should be a "Newbie" desktop mode for the beginners...
