### Post by sffvba[e0rt on 2011-04-15
Hi all...

I had a few days off and decided to remove Natty from my laptop.  I had been running it since Alpha 3 and the last week it just crashed on me once too often and I was fed up.

4 days later, 4 different distro's and I am now on Natty Beta 2 (which btw has been uber stable for me)... but why?  

Simple answer, I missed the interface and shortcuts and got frustrated using Gnome 2.x and KDE which I have been using for years and missed several things I had gotten used to in Natty... So much so that I am back.

For any of the nay-sayers and doom and gloom users out there, if you have used the Unity interface for a month or two (preferably after the official release of Natty)... then, and only then feel free to spew your venom on-line... I do however suspect that after this period, you might be inclined to sing a different song.

The Unity interface has been ways to speed up your work and in general give a more pleasent computing experience... Try it, don't just believe what you read on the internet and simply jump on the band-wagon...



Happy computing :)

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-04-15
I started using Unity on my netbook a while back - I thought "Well, this is going to be the new default on Ubuntu, and it looks nice enough, so I'd better get used to it".

It took a little while to get used to, but it kept on improving. When I read on OMG! Ubuntu! that you can now drag and drop files to the launcher icons, and that the icons dim or brighten depending on whether they can accept the drop, I thought "Wow, this is just like what I used to have set up on Mac OS 9".

I kept Ubuntu 10.10 on my desktop, but found I wanted to use my netbook because I liked using Unity. Eventually I installed 11.04 on my desktop, and I can confirm that Unity works BETTER on desktops than it does on small screens. I even submitted a feature request and got it implemented within days.

I find it odd that Ubuntu's so-called fans complain loudly at every single change. Network Manager was the devil in 2007. Pulseaudio was the devil in 2008. The X-org kill key combination change was the devil in 2009, as was the new notify-osd. In 2010, people were prepared to mutiny and change distributions over the order of the window buttons! The reality is, Linux changes so often that you have to **expect change**.

My advice to the Unity complainers is: Use whatever works best for you, but some day Gnome 2 will no longer be around. While you're busy putting your panel applets back in the correct order after a screen resolution change, I'll be using jumplists and searching with Zeitgeist and lenses. Why don't you cut the cord now and experience what the Linux desktop of the future will be like?

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2011-04-15

Thank you for sharing your opinion.

I'm certain users will decide for themselves. With all the DE and distro  options available, the users I know realize that Gnome (Ubuntu) is  being phased out and are considering their options. Since it's still  available in 11.04, there's no hurry.

IMHO, one of the true benefits of Linux is all the available  alternatives. At present my family (5 Ubuntu only systems) has expressed  a total dissatisfaction with unity and are testing [_xfce_]("http://www.webupd8.org/2011/01/xfce-48-ubuntu-1004-and-1010-ppas.html"). So far their responses have been positive. Thanks once again to wolfen69's reminder.

I don't buy into the "some users can't accept change" philosophy. As any  user that's been around Ubuntu for more than a year have seen and  accepted multiple changes. It's all about personal choice and suggesting  that those who refuse to accept a change or dislikes it are some how  flawed - is ridiculous. Especially, considering those users that have  continued to use an OS that is constantly in a state of flux. Attempting  to negate the opinions of others by insinuating that they are resistant  to change, fails miserably if said individuals have continued using an  OS which changes every six months.

IMHO logic suggests users make their own decisions and not rely on the  opinions of others. As opinions are like bellybuttons, most everyone has  one. Should any member of my family decide they wish to use unity on their system, I will gladly assist them. But, I sincerely doubt it will ever again see mine. Amazingly, I'm capable of deciding for myself - I suggest others do the same. ;)

Just my $0.02
