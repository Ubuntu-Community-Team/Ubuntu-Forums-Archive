### Post by carusoswi on 2009-09-19
I have a really modern and fast computer at my office - runs XP really well (I wouldn't want to have touched Vista at all), and, except that it's at my office (where the atmoshpere is mostly tense in these trying times), I have to admit that XP continues to be solid as it always has.

My home machine (a Shuttle small form factor PC) used to be modern, runs at 3.0 gHz, 2gb RAM, updated HD's give me 1tb of onboard storage, and I dual boot Ubuntu and XP.

I really can't comnplain about XP on that machine, either, I guess, and, while I've always leaned towards Ubuntu as my favoriate of the two OS's, I used to have some definite complaints about Ubuntu.  Earlier versions would crash as I browsed the 'net or worked in MSAccess through Crossover.  I don't know if the problem was Ubuntu/Linux related or a result of the applications deployed, but I do know that I wasn't the only one complaining.

Thankfully, while it is in my nature to complain a bit, I also tend to have patience and tenacity, and have kept Ubuntu on my machine over the years (mainly because, if nothing else, browsing the 'net from Ubuntu is totally more satisfyingly safe than doing so from within XP).

Installing 9.04 seems to have totally put me over the top in terms of stability and Linux satisfaction - that and finding a couple of solutions to long-time problems (solutions which,themselves, are likely afforded through the passage of time and persistence/tenacity of developers devoted to the cause of making proprietary non-linux things work on Linux.

My ability to print photos is no longer defeated or compromised in Ubuntu, and the other day, I found a site that provided full functionality to my flatbed scanner.  What a joy.

9.04 has not crashed on me once since I installed it, and, the other day,I installed Ubuntu Studio, and it has made most of my sound card configuration problems vanish (don't ask me how, it just did).

I have had some bios boot up problems occurring (this is before I get to that point where I select which OS I will run), and, in the process of sorting them out, had to boot between OS's several time.

I hadn't run this home machine in XP in over six-months.

Update issues aside (can't blame XP for wanting to update itself), I simply had forgotten how long it takes before all the stuff that Windows needs to keep itself safe takes to load.  I've been spoiled by Ubuntu.  You boot it up, and you are there.

So, here's to all who have worked on Ubuntu to make it what it is, today.  One non-hoggish, sprightly, yet stable operating system.  I'm certain even better days are yet to come.  Keep up the good work.

Caruso
