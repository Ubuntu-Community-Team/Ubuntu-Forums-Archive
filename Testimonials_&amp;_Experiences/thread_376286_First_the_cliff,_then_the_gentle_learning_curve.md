### Post by MrVisible on 2007-03-04
I was wandering along the upgrade path for a long time, winding my way from DOS to Windows to 95 to 98 to XP, and I was getting less and less happy about where the path was leading me. When I saw Vista looming in the distance, a dark and dreary colossus feeding on huge piles of cycles and belching DRM smoke, I knew I was on the wrong path. I guess I'd known for a while.

I'd heard about Ubuntu, and even tried an install a while ago, but I couldn't get my wireless card working, so I gave up. I decided to try branching out again.

I cleared off a hard drive, and set up Ubuntu in a dual-boot configuration, and armed with my trusty laptop as a source of files and how-to pages, I started the process of configuring my wireless card.

It was a cheap Airlink101 using an RA73 chipset, and it took a while and a lot of help from threads on these forums, but by three in the morning I had it running. Connecting to the internet. Using WPA. And losing connectivity every half an hour or so.

It was enough to get a few looks at how things would work if I had everything fixed, though, and I was able to play Frozen Bubble for a little while. Actually, for a few hours. I was hooked. I kept trying.

Now, I know my limitations. And while I could probably have done the troubleshooting necessary to fix the problem with the Airlink, it would have taken me forever, and I don't have that kind of time right now. And as I wasn't really attached to that card in the first place (even the Windows drivers were kind of screwy) I took it back, and picked up another card. A TrendNet, I think it was, a 423 rev. b1.1r. It looked pretty, too.

I got it running by noon that day using ndiswrapper, got it connected to the web, got it running WPA. And then the system started locking up. I could use it for about ten minutes, and then it would freeze. The mouse would work, but it was just teasing me with the illusion of functionality.

I found out that it wasn't an uncommon problem with certain wireless cards. I realized again that I was out of my depths in terms of troubleshooting, and took that card back as well.

And then I stopped a moment and thought. And I wondered why I was using a wireless card on my home computer anyway?

I remembered then that a couple of years ago our dog developed a taste for CAT5 cables. She wouldn't touch electrical cords, she wouldn't chew on anything but her toys for the most part, but run a network cable under a rug and she had to savage the thing. I think she may have been trying to get on the web. Perhaps she even succeeded. Whatever the case, I got tired of replacing cables and got a wireless card.

Of course, a year later we decided to try kenneling her when we were out of the house, and found out that she was much happier. She'd been having trouble guarding the whole house when we were out, and the kennel makes her feel much more secure.

Which means I no longer need a wireless card.

I spent twenty bucks on a fifty-foot cable, took ten minutes to run it to my office, and a half a minute getting the network card activated.

And now I'm blazing away in Ubuntu, happily reveling in all the cool goodies and games the system has to offer, marveling at how fast and friendly this OS really is.

Thanks for being a great community. It's nice being back on the right track again.

### Post by skale on 2007-03-04
I have a TrendNet 423PI in my box, and it seems to work without any problems.  But, then, ndiswrapper is a pain until you use it a few times.   You might want to use the latest ndiswrapper source (and compile it yourself); I cannot remember how up to date the repo one is.  Full instructions are in the ndiswrapper's own wiki.  Cross check it with Ubuntu's ndiswrapper page for distro-specific stuff.  

Glad you like linux/Ubuntu/GNU.  Wait till you try f-spot.  It's digital camera stuff.  I think it's the best-designed application around.
