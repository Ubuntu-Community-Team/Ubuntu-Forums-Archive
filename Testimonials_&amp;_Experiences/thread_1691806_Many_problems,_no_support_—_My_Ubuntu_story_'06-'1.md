### Post by LIJI32 on 2011-02-20
The last time I installed Ubuntu on real hardware and it worked straight for a reasonable amount of time was in 2006. Then I got another machine, and other ones too, and whenever I gave Ubuntu another chance, it would disappoint me with stupidly annoying problems. Problems not only I wouldn't get on Windows, I would also never get them on any other Linux OS I tried.

Initially I had problems with my ATI GPU, no one in the support forums could help. I tried other Linux distros, like Fedora and Mandriva. In fact, they had more bugs. However, unlike Ubuntu, they both had much better support, so I could actually solved the problems I had. I later switched to nVidia so I could give Ubuntu another chance, but all I got was more problems, they were even more bizarre and silly. After 3 months I didn't use Ubuntu (Can't remember why exactly, probably other problems I forgot about, it's hard remembering all of them), I tried booting up. It got stuck in the terminal, apparently "apt" was magically uninstalled, and it asked me to "apt-get install apt", which obviously didn't work. Support forum didn't help at all, I eventually solved it myself.

Not sure why, I eventually removed Ubuntu, can't remember why it stopped working again, probably because I tried updating it (Via the obvious an so-called friendly automatic software updater) to the next major release. I still wanted to leave Windows. I tried installed OS X on my machine, and in VMWare. I loved the system. It wasn't very stable on my hardware however. Eventually, I bought an iMac. Sure, it's closed source, expensive, but it used a very good operating system, amazing hardware, and both software and hardware were very polished.

However, there are more machines in my house. There is an older machine where Windows XP was originally installed. It became very slow even when we reinstalled Windows, so a few months ago I gave Ubuntu yet another chance. This is probably my first success story with Ubuntu since 2006. The machine became much more faster and stable, and it even can use Compiz Fusion with quite decent settings.

Last month we bought an HTC. It was obvious that it will use some kind of Linux, as I wanted to customize every little bit in it. I installed Ubuntu and XBMC. Everything was smooth, I had a very problems with XBMC but I've managed to solve them. Took many days to tweak and configure the system to my needs, and then I realized Ubuntu is **still** buggy and problematic. The machine wouldn't shutdown, restart, nor go to sleep! Sleep and restart will show the Ubuntu shutdown/boot up screen for hours, [sleep mode gets suck in a terminal-like mode]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1682982"). I have never ever seen a single operating system that couldn't shutdown or get to sleep mode! Why should bugs be in such a simple function in the first place?

I posted a topic in the forums, no reply. Tried IRC, took me 20 minutes to actually get someone to read my question, he suggest to disable ACPI, I had to Google how to do it. Didn't work. A day passed, I bumped my topic. Almost two weeks passed, not a single reply. I bumped again, tried IRC again. Nothing helpful, just trigger-happy users who like shooting these silly "pre-recorded" messages, ironically under the excuse that the way I ask my question "make the chat room unreadable". I hoped the support got better, it only got worse. Last time I asked I got wrong answers, now I get no answers at all.

For years whenever I need a Linux environment I stupidly automatically chose Ubuntu. I contribute to open source projects so I sometimes need tools that work better under Linux, and sometimes I need Linux to solved problems with other machines, and sometimes I just need a computer to run Linux. Every time a new Ubuntu release is out it claims to be ready for the real world. It claims to be stable and have decent support. And every time it has to remind me it doesn't. I will probably have to redo all the work I've done on the HTC on a decent OS. It took me days to tweak all the software I installed for my needs. At least I can thank you for reminding me again that Ubuntu isn't, and will probably never be ready for real world use.
