### Post by bwsmith25 on 2010-02-23
I guess the best place to begin would be the beginning, because beginning in the middle or at the end would only make things more confusing. (Poor attempt at humor - sorry.)

I am one of those people who can say that I had been a Windows user for my entire computing life. I started way back when Windows 3.1 was the new gig in town, and went through the usual course of 95, 98, XP, and Vista - and I can't say that I absolutely hated the experience, but I just wanted something more - something *better*.

So, in December 2008, I decided to give Linux a try. I had always heard of this "open source OS" but had never thought about using it. I started researching and found that Ubuntu would be the most user-friendly for me to try, so I downloaded the 8.10 Intrepid ISO to a CD and popped it in my laptop. 

I looked around on the live cd for a while and realized that I liked it, so I decided to install it onto my hard drive.

I set up a dual-boot XP/Ubuntu system on my laptop (just to be sure that I could use Ubuntu and wouldn't be without something to fall back on just in case it didn't work out for me), and while I enjoyed the Ubuntu desktop, my laptop had problems with the screen resolution that I couldn't resolve after weeks of frustrating Google searches and forum searches - so I finally gave up and decided that it just wasn't meant to be for me at the time. (For some reason I didn't have these problems when looking at the live cd, it only happened after I installed it. I later learned that it was the graphics card on my laptop, but we'll get to that later.)

A few months later I had saved enough money to buy a new laptop (which was good because the one I was using eventually died), but the laptop came preloaded with Windows Vista. Since I already had Vista on my desktop, I didn't mind because I was familiar enough with the OS to use it on my laptop as well.

Once Windows 7 came out I was faced with the decision to stay with Vista or purchase the upgrade for Windows 7 (and I would have to purchase the 3 PC edition, since I had 2 computers), and I almost bought it - but fortunately for me, the company I work for had to replace the main computer in my office and it came equipped with Windows 7. I was able to test drive it while working and realized that it wasn't as spectacular as I thought it would be, so I learned that I didn't need to spend the money. 

My wife and my son both have laptops with Windows 7 on them, but I didn't want to spend the money to upgrade mine to have an OS that I really didn't like in the first place. And, since we don't use the desktop that often anyway, I found it a waste of money to buy the OS to put on a "hardly-used" computer.

Enter Ubuntu into the picture. I remembered my experience with my previous laptop and remembered that it wasn't the OS's fault that my laptop had an inferior graphics card, so I decided that I would give Ubuntu a second chance. I downloaded the 9.10 Karmic ISO to a CD and put it in, hoping for better results. 

I played around on the live cd for a day or so, and realized that I was ready to install. To be safe, I set up a dual-boot with Ubuntu and Vista because I didn't want to risk losing my OS if something went wrong. (I have been accused of being too safe, but I just didn't want to take the risk.) 

After a few weeks of using Ubuntu, I realized that I was booting into Vista less and less frequently, until finally I wasn't even using it at all. It was then that I realized I was beginning to fall in love with the little open source OS, and was finally breaking away from my Windows dependency that had had a grasp on me for my entire computing life.

But I didn't want to completely remove Windows, because I had yet to find out if everything was compatible with Ubuntu. (I still needed to test my printer, for example.) After trying to print I learned that it wasn't compatible, and that was a major strike against Ubuntu because printing is one of the things I can't do without. (I can live without games because I'm not a gamer - but I can't go without a printer.)

Since I couldn't get my printer to work, I decided that I would spend a little time and see if I could fix it before completely giving up on Ubuntu, because I liked it too much to just quit. So, I started two threads here on the forums to see if anyone had similar problems, and to see if maybe someone could help me figure out what was wrong with my printer or Ubuntu setup. (I learned that printer compatibility is a common issue with Ubuntu.)

After two weeks of reading and responding on the forums and trying various methods to get the blasted printer to print a page, I finally got my printer to work (thanks to the help of the great forum members!) - and I realized that I was the reason my printer wouldn't work because I didn't have the repositories set up properly. There was nothing wrong with my printer, it was just one setting that I didn't have set properly - after fixing that, I was printing in no time!

With the printer problem solved I had no reason to go back to Windows - now that everything important to me worked with Ubuntu, I didn't see any reason to keep the old Windows partition on my hard drive. But, I decided that I wanted to purchase a larger hard drive for my laptop anyway (just so I could have more space to store files). I went with a 250GB instead of the default 160GB, and when I got it and installed it in my laptop - the only OS that was installed was - you guessed it, UBUNTU! (I kept the old hard drive formatted with the dual-boot Ubuntu/Vista setup, so if for some reason I ever need it, it's still there.)

That was a week ago. I must say that I haven't had as much fun customizing, reading, learning, and playing on my computer in a long time! (The 3-D rotating cube desktop is awesome!) I have also created a USB disk with Ubuntu 9.10 on it to keep with me in case anyone I know wants to learn what Ubuntu is all about. I use it on our family desktop (I can't remove Windows from it because my wife and son still use it from time to time and they are still Windows users - but hopefully that will change in the future.) 

I wouldn't have enjoyed my computer as much had I not taken the time to learn what Ubuntu could do for me, and I wouldn't have stuck with it when I had a minor hiccup with getting my printer to work if not for the great people in these forums. Thank you Ubuntu for making a wonderful product, and thank you for bringing the joy of computing back to someone who had lost it after many years of Windows setbacks.

And thank you to everyone in these forums for making it easier to solve problems when they arise. It is because of you that Ubuntu can add one more convert to the list.
