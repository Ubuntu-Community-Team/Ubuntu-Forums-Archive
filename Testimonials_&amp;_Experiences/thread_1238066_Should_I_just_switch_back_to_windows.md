In the last year Intel's created a lot of regressions in their Linux driver and until they get ironed out, I can only imagine it will cause a painful experience. That said, you don't happen to know what Intel graphics adapter you have and what screen res your computer is? 

In general you should have flash and compiz working at least okay, even with intel graphics. Though not FPS games at any playable quality.

The concept that everyone is capable of installing an operating system is somewhat naive in my opinion. Many people purchase systems that have OS's pre-installed and may never have installed an OS. If an individual isn't comfortable installing an OS or has significant issues while attempting the procedure, it's only logical they seek assistance. I believe that when installing any OS, all efforts to learn and have some fundamental understanding of the software - is mandatory.

For anyone to think that by burning a disk and placing it into a drive, an OS will automatically install and be 100% fully functional on all systems - is somewhat unrealistic. Without any pre-existing knowledge of the OS and no effort made to obtain such knowledge prior to installation, isn't one expecting the OS to emulate an OS their already accustom to? Should an issue arise, aren't they at a disadvantage?

I've assisted >30 individuals at installing Ubuntu on various systems and have found at least one common denominator. If historically Windows was their OS of choice, their expectations are that Ubuntu will emulate Windows. I refuse to assist anyone until they have invested time learning about Ubuntu and made some effort to understand the differences. Plus using the live cd for at least 3 to 5 days.

It's been my experience this helps to minimize the Windows clone questions and expectations. This also provides the new user with the opportunity of assessing Ubuntu to assure it meets their requirements on their own system. To many times I experienced individuals (after installing Ubuntu) being dissatisfied with Ubuntu not being a Windows clone and returning to the comfort of Windows.

For that very reason, I believe that Ubuntu isn't for everyone. Not all are comfortable leaving the environment of Windows, nor do they have any desire of investing time in learning a new OS.

Frankly, I don't care and spend no time recruiting. Should someone ask for my assistance, I gladly assist them. if they meet the requirements of investing time prior to installation learning about the new OS and using the live cd.

If you have assisted installing Ubuntu for >30 people and haven't experienced those that have no desire to learn the new OS - I'll be impressed. Presently, over 90% of those I've helped, are still using Ubuntu. They do contact me periodically and ask for additional assistance, usually with regards to hardware replacement and/or failure.
