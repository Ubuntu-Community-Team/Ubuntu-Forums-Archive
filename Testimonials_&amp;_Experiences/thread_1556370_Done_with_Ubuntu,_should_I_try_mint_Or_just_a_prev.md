However, be aware that this stability is achieved by using older software that is very well tested. So in the end, you might decide it's worth dealing with a couple of bugs in exchange for having up-to-date software. :)

I always recommend to newbies that they always remain **one release behind the newest release**. Karmic is supported for long enough to shake most of (if not all) the bugs out of Lucid.

Also, another thing to keep in mind:

**Choose a distro - and you're also choosing a repository!**

While PCLinuxOS has a very good repo with all the stuff most people need, it can't compare with the vast treasury of software in Debian or Ubuntu's repos. They added a new package on request though, in a matter of just days. That's a plus for sure.

The other thing I think is a big, big issue with PCLinuxOS: The all-or-nothing way they have of keeping your system up to date. Yeah, it's "rolling release" and that's great, but the *only* way to keep your system up to date is to open Synaptic, **R**efresh, **M**ark _All_ Upgrades, and **A**pply. All or nothing. And some software is just better left alone when it's working fine. A newer version is not always better! Give me a choice in the matter, please! Not all-or-nothing.

With Linux Mint, you kinda don't have the choice either. The "Mark All Upgrades" feature is disabled in Mint's version of Synaptic! I'm sure they have their reasons, but again, it should be the *user's* choice.

Just some stuff to think about. Hope it's helpful.

### Post by TBABill on 2010-08-20
dixiedancer, you were correct on most things about PCLinuxOS, particularly Phoenix, but a couple I'd like to address just to clarify for the OP:
 
1. The repo speed test is used to determine which of their long list of servers is fastest so you are always using the fastest. Yes, if connectivity were an issue you'd not be able to update, but that would be the case with any distro. Their mirrors are spread around the world and the only time they are normally down is when they are being sync'd with the main server (syncing the new updates), but that is not ever all at once that I have experienced. I'm sure there have been instances where access to any is not possible, but I'd think that extremely rare.
 
2. When you Mark all updates, but before you click apply, is your time to deselect items you do not want updated. And you can mark items to never update. Their 3 step update process is so simple any user can manage it. So if you get that particular application just right, mark it in synaptic to never update and it should run forever as you installed it. And you only update to a later kernel by manually doing so. You must search for "kernel" or something similar to get the list, choose the kernel you want (bfs, a64, generic, etc), mark for installation and click apply. Then reboot to begin using the newly installed kernel.
 
I have found it to be extremely easy to maintain an up to date system, but only when I want those new apps. Before you click Apply (after marking items to update), all you have to do is scroll through the list of apps to see what will be upgraded (if you didn't verify them on the popup list that makes it a lot easier to review).
 
Hopefully that helps clarify. I use PCLinuxOS heavily on 2 machines and didn't want the OP to have any confusion. I just put Peppermint (Ubuntu based) on one of mine last night and it is sweet so far!! So many great choices out there to try. Mint is great too for me and is on one of my laptops. I'm not stuck on any particular one...just love using the ones that work great on my hardware and are fast.

### Post by WorMzy on 2010-08-21
I'm going to recommend Arch Linux. It's not as friendly as other distros to begin with (You just get a CLI, no DE), but it's just that little bit more customisable than most other distros. You can cherry pick packages that you want installed to make the system exactly how you want it. Arch is a much richer learning experience than Ubuntu, but you have to put in a lot more effort to get everything up and running.
