### Post by nickleboyblue on 2011-03-15
I found an article from one of the various Linux bloggers that I read which said that Ubuntu alpha 11.04 was unstable and buggy, much more so than the alpha from fedora 15.  I have long been an Ubuntu fanboy, but decided that I might give fedora a shot.  Well, it doesn't work.  Seriously, it wouldn't update, it wouldn't boot after attempting to update, it froze every few seconds when the Gnome 3.0 actions menu (or whatever it's called) was open, and it was an overall disaster.  Just a waste of half my day.  I had begun to wonder why I like Ubuntu so much, and was reminded of the reason I began using it in the first place.

After purchasing my first laptop, I was looking like mad for something, anything to replace M$ Virus with.  I started with openSuse, but they didn't have very good support for my wireless drivers, and the whole system just seemed to need a little more attention than I was willing to give it.  I heard about Fedora, Mandriva, and several others, but I got the same exact vibe from them as well.  Finally, I discovered Ubuntu.  Upon discovering that it worked with my wireless card, it promptly became the sole inhabitant of my hard drive, and I have never looked back since.

I read in another blog a few days ago that Ubuntu was considered the best OS for beginners.  I beg to differ.  Not that I think Ubuntu is not the best distro for beginners.  It is, no doubt about it.  But when they say that in blogs and magazine articles, they usually do it in much the same way as middle school graduation ceremonies.  Everyone needs a medal, and since Ubuntu is unquestionably the best distro for beginners, that's the medal it gets.  They then go on to list other medals, like "best distro for developers" or "best for experts."  What that says to me is that if you are not a Linux expert and you try one of them, you'll invariably spend the rest of your life trying to figure out why your printer won't work even though you have the right drivers, and in my opinion, that is NOT a feature, that is a bug.  Therefore, Ubuntu has taken the only "medal" that suggests a lack of bugs, as bugs are things that new users are not going to want to fix on their own time.  So, if you are planning on writing a blog that gives that sort of medal away simply as a way to include all of the Linux distros, be forewarned: I can see right through that ploy, and I recognize that Ubuntu should probably be the top distro in more than just the noob category.

I do like the Gnome 3.0 interface, though there are some aspects of it that I seriously despise.  If I understand it correctly, Cannonical offered to help out with the development of gnome shell.  When key features were refused, Cannonical split and chose to go with Unity.  As time goes by, I am seeing more and more why this was the best decision they could have made.

Unity Places is the one feature of Unity that I think makes the decision worth it, and I'll tell you why: Unity places have the potential, one day, to be custom-made by end-users, and they could even be linked to home automation functions, giving the user complete control over their home as long as they have an internet connection, without having to install an alternative desktop environment.

In all, I have really learned nothing from my short escapade into the world of other Linux distributions.  I already knew that Ubuntu worked better than the other available distributions out there out of the box.  I just... well, solidified that knowledge.  I am actually going to school with the obscure hope that I might some day be hired on as a developer.  Looks like Ubuntu will have a faithful user in me for at least a few more years to come.

### Post by nickleboyblue on 2011-03-15
Wow.  Didn't expect to generate that many responses... Anyway, I do realize that alpha versions are generally buggy.  I've seen what is going on with 11.04, and the blog I read stated that the current alpha version of fedora was in better shape.  It wasn't.

Though fedora did show some promise, it had so far to go that it was impossible to get it to do half the stuff that the blogger said it would do, probably because the blogger ran it on a virtual machine.  The same blog stated that fedora gets the "medal" (I'm the one calling it a medal, the blog just stated it) for the most supported hardware, so I thought that my new laptop would be perfect to test it out on, and no, it wasn't through a virtual machine.  It was native install, and like I stated earlier, it was the worst user experience I've ever had, with an alpha that was supposed to work better than the mostly-functional (according to my experience) natty alpha.

By the way, every one of the features that I liked about gnome 3.0 are in Unity, but Unity has a few added perks that, though they have not come to full maturity yet, make it the desktop I'll stick with.  I especially like the availability of the desktop switcher, the presence of Unity Places, and the reduction of used-up vertical space by putting application menu bars in the panel.  With keyboard shortcuts, it will easily become the fastest user interface to navigate.
