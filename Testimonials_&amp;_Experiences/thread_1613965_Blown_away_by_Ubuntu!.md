### Post by travissparks1307 on 2010-11-05
I have been a computer hobbyist since I was 15, (I'm 27 now). When I first started I thought Windows 98 was THE best. But it didn't take long for me to realize it was just plain not. Then I heard about this wonderful OS called BeOS. It was super fast, super easy, never crashed. Fed up with Microsoft I took the plunge and ended up with OS religion. I loved BeOS, and I still do, I sure miss it. (I wish I could get Haiku running but I never have any luck, but that's another forum). What BeOS really did for me was open my eyes to a new way. I didn't HAVE TO use microsoft anything! 

Well, BeOS was starting to go the way of the Dodo. I didn't know where to go. Then other BeOS users told me about, Linux. I switched. It was hard. Wow was it hard at first, but I stuck with it. I'm glad I did. Over the years I used a bunch of different distributions. RedHat, Mandrake, SuSe, I ended up finding Slackware and stuck with it for quite awhile. I still think it's a good distro for advanced users. 

However, cracks started showing for me. Infrequent updates and lack of a standard package manager left me high and dry numerous times. I started searching for a new distro. Again. 

In my searching, one name kept popping up over and over. There seemed to be a huge following. Companies like Google were supporting it, and everyone seemed to be raving about how this one distro seemed to have brought Linux into "primetime". That distro's name? You guessed it, Ubuntu.

I was skeptical. But I was also intrigued. I downloaded it. Ubuntu 10.04 Lucid Lynx. Yes I'm a fairly new user. But...

Wow dude. It was the easiest install I had ever seen on a Linux distro. Seriously. It booted up lightning fast. Everything just worked. Getting new software was a breeze. Automatic updates? Great and mighty Zeus! The more I started using it, the more I liked it. Then I started reading up on it.

Back in BeOS's heyday, there was such an enthusiasm for it within the company, and in their online community. Jean-Louis Gassee the man who started Be Inc., was committed to what he was doing, he BELIEVED in it. The people working in the company seemed to LOVE what they were doing and it showed in their work. I haven't seen anything like it in years. Until recently.

Canonical and Ubuntu remind me of that. Mark Shuttleworth seems hellbent for leather, determined in his vision. The people working at Canonical share his enthusiasm and it shows in their work. The community behind Ubuntu take such pride and joy in their, I'm sorry, **_OUR_** distro. The more I read about it, the more they remind of ol' Be.

But there's a difference. BeOS was proprietary. Closed source, except for a few things here and there. Be Inc. was out to make money. They talked about their OS's superiority over the likes of Apple and Microsoft, and then inexplicably went down the road of internet appliances where they died a quick, yet merciless, death.

Canonical seems to have taken all that was good about Be (as a company that loved what it was doing), and then did away with the stupidity and arrogance. Clearly defined goals as opposed to vague ones. 

Mark Shuttleworth states unabashedly that he wants Ubuntu to be the #1 OS in the world. Jean-Louis Gassee didn't seem to care if BeOS was.

Thanks to Canonical and Ubuntu, I feel like I have someone and something to get behind. Sure I've only been using it a few months now, but I LOVE it. I have a Linux distro that I can hold up proudly. Before, when I tried to show people Linux I had to jump through hoops and I always seemed to be explaining "Once it's setup right it's great." I couldn't give them an "out-of-the-box" demo and they turned away from Linux. "It's too hard." "You have to do *what* to get it working?" All of that has changed. Now when I want to show off Linux, I show off Ubuntu. And jaws drop. "No viruses? No malware?" "You mean it's *that* easy to install?" "It hasn't crashed yet!" "This thing is *so* easy!" 

I have been thoroughly impressed with Ubuntu and what Canonical is doing. I find myself handing out Ubuntu CDs quite frequently. 
I get people to switch at the drop of a hat. 

Ubuntu is easily the BEST distribution I have ever used. 

Thank You for Ubuntu.
