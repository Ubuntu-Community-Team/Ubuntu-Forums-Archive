Greetings to all who are Linux;

Part of this will be a vent and I'll say that up front. The other part will try to describe my problem(s). Forgive the vent, but I've been working on this problem now for three days with no end in sight. For a tiny bit of background, I've been a Windows administrator for nearly 25 years. I have only 'toyed' with Linux and know the very very rudimentary elements. For example: I understand the concept of packages being nothing more than software geared for installation. Much like a Wise installer does in Windows. However, in Windows I know I can get or download a 'package' and its ready to go - double click and it installs. Granted, not always correctly, but at least it does most of the work. 

I've found that in the Linux world, nothing is easy - and it is exceptionally irritating; frustrating; and down right maddening to learn. Nothing is easy! I have been trying for days to resolve my stupid xorg-intel video problem, and it just creates problem after problem for me. I've had to blow out my Ubuntu 10.4 installation three times as a result ... and start over. I have "easily" found the xserver-xorg-intel drivers, but god forbid you people put it into a ready-to-go package for use. Nooooo, you have to make the end user "build" the dang things. Why don't you just put everything in a .h file and make us all C++ programmers in order to use this thing? I think if you made that statement up front, you'd have less users coming over to the 'dark side'. 

Alas I am trying like h-e-double-hockey-sticks to learn this and make it work. I have done nothing but frakking reading for the last three days and got no where useful. I've been in the open IRC #ubuntu channels asking for help. At first, I thought folks were able to help me. Boy was I wrong. Ended up trashing my dang installation and starting over - again. I've been all over the freedesktop.org site, gentoo, and tons of other sites. No where does it cater to the novice. No where. We are expected to know how to "build" packages, make diffs, know what the heck diffs are and how they apply, and of course we are supposed to be conf file editing wizards for every god blessed tweak needed. Let us not forget that we are also expected to be hardware wizards capable of extracting the minutest of details on our hardware at the drop of a dime. 

As an administrator of 25 years, I fully understand the open versatility of everyones machines and how no single installation is the same across the board. I understand the need for customized installations, customized solutions and customized drivers. What I don't understand is how Linux has gotten such a huge following when the end user is _forced_ to become a bloody programmer to operate the dang thing. Now mind you I also have experience on QNX POSIX systems in the past, and even THOSE were easier to use than today's modern Linux. Every time I find something that claims to be a "fix", it ends up looking like a document for editing a windows registry x 100! If you want Linux to appeal to the masses, remove the dependancy on the end user becoming a programmer to either use or FIX anything in it. The common user wants to do nothing more than double-click and have it work. I'm good with it even meeting me half way, but this is ridiculous.

With warmest animosity and regard,   *wicked grin*
TheOctagon911

For your case, what you could do is investigate why sound does not work when your computer comes out of hibernation. Find the problem, and then report it to the Alsa project so that it is fixed to the source, probably the Linux kernel.
The proper way is that you do not need to deal with the sound. It should just work.

Many members fix issues with workarounds which do not make it upstream to the source projects, and then you get many people with the same problem asking again and again. We get this here with sound quite a lot.

At this point I appear to start ranting myself. :popcorn:

For your sound issues, you would need to 
1. Try to find if there is a BIOS update that could fix your ACPI issues. Sound may not come back after suspend if the ACPI tables are messed up. It's more common than you think. To figure out the extend of the mess, follow the steps at [http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1036051](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1036051) where you extract and try to recompile your DSDT tables. If you get plenty of errors, your laptop manufacturer is to blame. In that thread there is a friendly person that can try to fix your DSDT, in case you want to go to that direction.
2. There is some chance it's an Alsa issue. You need to output the alsa-info.sh information for your hardware. Then, search the Internets for hints on this issue, others with the same problem, etc.

As a hint of motivation, see this testimonial, [http://simos.info/blog/archives/984](http://simos.info/blog/archives/984)
