### Post by Tamlynmac on 2009-07-21
> HappyFeet
Unless you do hardware diagnostics, you will never know for sure.

+1

Been involved in component level diagnostics for decades and must agree with your statement. Too often hardware of any sort is overlooked until it fails at which time it can be catastrophic. It can result in other hardware failure that's associated to the hardware in question (power supply, etc.).  It's been my experience that three factors are often directly involved in component failure. Heat, fluctuating power (noise, etc.) and mfg quality. All three can be disastrous to digital components. Individual uncalibrated sensors may not reflect the heat issue until failure has already occurred. Specific circuits in the digital components may fail and only be recognized when a certain function is required or requested by command.

Detailed troubleshooting of specific digital components is generally not realistic without quality test equipment and/or software plus the knowledge required to use it. The average user may never know that a failure is imminent or has already occurred until the sub-system completely fails. At which time the entire system may be compromised.

For example, a past power supply failure may have damaged multiple other sub-systems and components, that only show up when certain commands are requested. Modern troubleshooting tends to overlook component issues and focuses primarily on entire sub-systems (board level). Exchanging boards and assuming the system is fully functional based on a few fundamental commands. The cost of a full system diagnostic is generally an undesired expense for the average home PC.

Many users are sitting on a time bomb and have no idea that failure is lurking and/or imminent. They continue using their systems until the failure manifests into a visible issue. Many refuse to use power noise reduction (stability) devices that may minimize power issues and some cases are not well ventilated, creating localized heat issues. It's been my experience that to often component failures result due to a lack of maintenance. Dust collection inside the case can and often does act as an insulator, keeping heatsinks clean and checking fans on a regular basis helps to minimize failure. But even with quality maintenance, device failure may occur strictly due to a defect in a component itself. Most components are classified based on requirements. Of course, if it's military associated the requirements are substantially greater than whats require for a home PC.

I believe HappyFeet is right on target when he states "I trust any OS before I would trust hardware manufacturers. Mistakes in the fabrication process are actually fairly common." My experience with consumer hardware mfgs. has proven they don't always use high quality components, especially for low end devices. Keep in mind that consumer hardware mass production relies heavily on purchasing of components from various component mfgs. Genreally, based on low bid. After all, profit margins must be maintained or stock holders bail.

Just my $0.02
