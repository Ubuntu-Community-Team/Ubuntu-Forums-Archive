### Post by VitaminG on 2008-05-05

### Post by spacefreak86 on 2008-05-06
Not quite, as it still has some bugs to work out. Maybe in about a year or so from now, then yes. Oh, yeah, and we need to get the software vendors online with Ubuntu as well.

I think one of the reasons that Windows and now Mac is so popular is because of the large amount of software available for it. I hate to say this (believe me, I really do), but I remember hearing Balmer screaming "Developers, Developers, Developers." Sadly, my programming experience extends to one semester of C++, but in trying to keep an open mind, while Synaptic / Adept have over 21,000 software packages in them, the vast majority of those are support libraries, not physical programs themselves. Of the ones that are physical programs, a fewer amount have a stable, bug-free GUI interface (I myself today came across a BSOD in Kaffeine of all programs). 

I realize that Windows and Mac have bugs in them as well, but because of the financial resources of not just the operating system manufacturers, but also the software manufactures, Windows and Mac will be able to continue growing and maintaining market share. 

In my small time with Linux, one thing I noticed while visiting various software vendors is that commercial Linux software is really hard to come by. The two explanations I had for this was that the market isn't big enough for the average retailer to be selling commercial Linux software, and that because of the various types of basic architecture of the distros, there is no single thread to build on top of. It costs them money to have to port a Windows program to Mac, as it would a Mac program to Windows. That's just to one operating system. Now if they tried to port to the wonderful world of Linux for desktops, they would have to have a program for Debian based, Gentoo based, RPM-based, Slackware-based, etc etc. While it is good to have a wide-variety of operating systems available, perhaps most notably for security and if there were a Linux-virus outbreak, virus-protection, it also cuts into the ability of mainstream manufacturers to port their software packages to Linux. 

This should not be seen as saying that independent, individual developers do not produce good code, as there are many great free (as in freedom and beer) programs that have been produced, but there are still many areas that software has not been able to cover. Until all areas of the software market are covered with stable, reliable programs by independent developers, the average person that we are trying to gain market with will not be as willing to make the switch.

While it seems the variety of distros for Linux is one of its greatest strengths, it could also be our Archille's heal.
