### Post by yetanotherproblem on 2007-11-29
I understand that a lot of people have trouble with Ubuntu (most people, probably), and that a lot of people complain about how Linux and/or Ubuntu failed them.  They log on to the forums and vent, and everybody here (who are, for the most part, avid users and supporters of Linux and Ubuntu) gets defensive and it starts a big argument.

I don't want to do that, but I did want to write this post, because I think that you guys, as a Linux/Ubuntu community, need to hear it.  Probably you've heard it before, and probably you'll hear it again, but perhaps if enough people say it, it will affect change, which is the whole point of posts like mine.  Most of the rants are probably just disguised pleas for change, too (or so I believe).

I first installed Ubuntu when Drake was new, and after a few weeks of problems (video, typically, and sound and gaming), I switched back to XP.  When Vista came out, I upgraded, used it for about six months, and then switched back to XP.  I was goofing off online when I ran across a mention of Linux, and remembering that Ubuntu updates every six months or so, I decided to check the latest version out.

I'll admit that it was attractive.  The restricted drivers looked promising, and you guys now even have a GUI installer (most people with ATI cards can't use it, but you have it all the same).  The media problems were supposedly fixed (avi, mpeg, dvd playback), as well as the audio problems (mp3 playback).  Compiz looked like the very thing Linux needed to bring it at least within shouting distance of the  league that Microsoft is in visually (Vista, for all its flaws, is aesthetically stunning).

Sadly, Linux missed the mark on all counts.  I ended up with the text installer because of lackluster (if not nonexistent) support for ATI video cards (ATI cards are so much better than Nvidia that there's really no legitimate comparison, but that's just my opinion).  Even then I had to log in to recovery mode and reconfigure xorg just to get basic graphics support.  Once I could actually see my desktop, the restricted drivers fixed most issues (no 3d rendering).

But video playback showed blue tinted videos and jerky playback when maximized.  After an hour and a half on the forums, I got that bug fixed, and then looked to install Compiz. It installed relatively easy, but once I rebooted the system lagged horribly, and the video playback was once again blue tinted and jumpy.  I couldn't fix it, so I decided to turn Compiz off.  I found many posts on the forum that suggested turning Compiz off, but only one that actually told me how to do it.  Finding that post took almost an hour and three Google searches.  

Then I tried to install Guild Wars via Wine, which according to WineHQ is possible, but it only froze on me.  Opera Web browser wouldn't install properly, nor would VLC media player.  

It dawned on me then that I was spending most of my computer time trying to fix Linux instead of enjoying the benefits Linux claims to offer (speed, stability, security, etc.).  

I'm not out to put down Linux.  What I'm out to do is say that the Linux community, and Ubuntu in particular, has a long, LONG way to go if they are serious about being a threat to Microsoft or Apple, or even a viable alternative.  I encountered problems from the time I installed Ubuntu until this minute, writing this post (from Windows XP).  And yes, I understand about proprietary drivers and codecs and how hard it is for the Linux community to find suitable replacements for those restricted things.  

Even still, the only true improvement I saw (and I'm just the average user, not a programmer, but the average user should be your target audience) between Dapper and Gutsy is the improvement with reading/writing to an NTFS drive.

Needless to say, it wasn't enough to make me stick with Linux.  I appreciate the work you guys do, I know you don't get paid for it, and I totally support the development of Linux and the Open Source movement.  But it's not compatible with my needs at the moment, and so I must go back to Microsoft.  I don't like it, but I've seen nothing in Linux to make me change my mind, other than the philosophy behind it.  And that's just not enough.  The time for words is done.  Now is the time for action.

I hope you guys see that, and do something about it.

Good luck!
