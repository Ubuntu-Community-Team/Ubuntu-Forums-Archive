### Post by Hieronymus Josch on 2009-12-16
I am a 4 week noob to linux.  In that 4 weeks I have re-installed fresh copies of ubuntu 9 times.  I have spent countless hours figuring out how to manually compile and install drivers for my pci wireless-g card and antiquated gpu.  I, with help from members of this community, was able to solve my wifi problems but was unable to get my graphics card to work properly with the nvidia legacy drivers.  Today, I spent $75 on a wireless-n card and GeForce 6200 gpu and  both work flawlessly...natively.  What could potentially help lots of noobs is some simple honesty. I, like many, enjoy the challenge of solving a hardware issue and running a cuss load of terminal code was a great way to get acquainted with the world of Linux but sometimes, enough is enough.  Lots of people give up on their Linux installs because of compatibility issues, I have couple of friends that have.  My point is simply this, if someone is struggling with a real bear of an issue with one of their key system components, i.e. sound, display or web, it's worth making the suggestion they swap out there hardware if they can afford to.

A lot of us run older machines, older hardware and have an earnest desire to migrate away from Microsoft-dependent computing but we need honest direction.  Time spent diagnosing image-artifacting on a 4-year old integrated gpu or connecting to the web with a Linksys Wireless-G PCI-card could be better spent teaching a noob how to migrate his media or transition his daily tasks from proprietary, Windows-based programs to free, Linux-based suites.

Try installing.  If one of your components doesn't work properly, you'll find lots of help here at the forums.  Troubleshoot to your hearts content but don't get frustrated, some hardware just doesn't want to play nice with ubuntu and sometimes the other way around.  If you, like me, have old hardware and have tried every work around under the sun to no avail, consider buying new gear.  Once you're sitting in front of fast, sleak KDE, you won't miss the clams.

Hi Hieronymus Josch,

I'm also a great believer in having the right hardware for the job. People in general are too used to buying $5 webcams and $20 printers, and loading their windows machines with a million different drivers ... all seems to work, until one day it all blows up in their face. Can anyone say "fdisk, format, reinstall" ?

I've been using Ubuntu since 6.06, and I can say I've been quite fortunate with my hardware. Perhaps this is because I use decent stuff - not top end expensive stuff, but decent. And now that I'm an Ubuntu-only user, I do my research before purchasing any new gear.

I must say I've had some bad luck with scanners and webcams, and perhaps some trouble finding Wireless adaptors that are (1) supported and (2) actually sold anywhere.

And nothing annoys me more than people that don't listen. My father for instance - I TOLD HIM to buy a HP printer, as HP has an open source division, and HPLIP is included with Ubuntu. He went out and bought a Brother instead. The printer works, but the scanner doesn't. So yes I think there is some user education needed - it's the reverse of Windows really ... instead of installing software to make your OS compatible with your hardware, you have to buy the hardware that your OS supports.

Anyway, enough rant from me - I love Ubuntu, I love everything about it, and now my family are using it my life is easier without all the "I've got another Virus" phone calls. My family's machines are virtually maintenance free. I love the community spirit, and the community support.

Well done for raising the points you did about spending a few dollars to get your machine up to spec!

Cheers
Callan
