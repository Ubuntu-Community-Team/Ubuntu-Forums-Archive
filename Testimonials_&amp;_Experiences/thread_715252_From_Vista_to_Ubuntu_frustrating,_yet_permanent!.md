### Post by bben on 2008-03-04
Hi, I'd like to share some of my first impressions of Ubuntu with the community.
First off, let me tell you a little about my digital expierience (not impressive, but maybe relevant). I got my first computer around the age of 13, it ran DOS ofcourse. I assumed it was the only option available. Afterwards I installed Win 3,11, 95, 98, 98ME, back to 98, XP and last month I started using Vista. Around the same time I switched to Win 98 for the first time, I heard about Linux and how it was free and supposedly much more stable than Windows, so I looked around for more information and ended up installing Mandrake Linux. It was great, but I had no idea what to expect, so the lack of hardware support and the completely different filesystem threw me off and sent me back to Windows.

Anyway, since installing XP which was a lot more stable than previous versions of Windows (still crap, but oh well) I never really thought about Linux anymore except that it looked cool but seemed incompatible with most every piece of hardware I owned. Actually installing it didn't really come to mind. After a long time without BsoD's Vista showed up, looked really nice and I figured that M$ would have improved stability even more, I installed it, booted, updated, installed Firefox and there it was, the almost forgotten BsoD... Just once, but that was one time too many. According to Windows it was my video card (I installed a driver that came with Windows update!)
Inbetween the times that my system wasn't locking up randomly, I was browsing youtube and came across a video that compared Vista Aero to Ubuntu Beryl ([http://youtube.com/watch?v=xC5uEe5OzNQ](http://youtube.com/watch?v=xC5uEe5OzNQ))
About ten minutes after watching it, I had downloaded the CD-version and was burning it to a CD.
After booting Ubuntu, I went back to Vista, copied my media and personal files to a spare disk and installed it to my harddisk. Knowing somewhat what to expect from Linux, it was much less painfull than my first attempt at Linux.

Now, here are my (your average Windows junkie) first impressions.
Note that these are mostly just thoughts that came to mind rather than actual questions (although I would like to see some of them answered)

**[COLOR="blue"]Free[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: AWESOME!!!
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: Well, ok... But, who pays the guys who code it? If it's a funded project, how can I be sure the company behind the funding won't shut it down when they're fed up with it? Why are they funding it? And if it's not funded, then who is the man behind the OS who has that much time on his hands to do this for free, he must be filthy rich, why is he not on a cruiseship?
It's hard to imagine thousands of people all working more or less togather to build this in their spare time and not making a single penny off it...
And more: if it's free, that means I can't make money off it either... right?  If you'd make enough sharewares for Windows, it will eventually give you some extra income (if you're any good at programming...)

**[COLOR="blue"]Flexibility / customization[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: I was impressed by the options you got 'out-of-the-box' for the taskbar and theme of the desktop. You can change the color, orientation, size, make it auto-hide, slide it to the side, heck... You can even completely delete it if you'd want.
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: Well, the customization isn't endless. Right-click the panel/taskbar and change the color... that's where I was rather dissapointed, several buttons didn't change to the color I wanted and just remained gray. Then I saw this awesome theme in a desktop-pic topic on this forum, it was extremely minimalist third or fourth one in that topic) and had just a transparent taskbar and very thin window decorations.That was a dream come true, I wanted something like that and went looking for more info on it. Again dissapointed, he had changed his desktop to openbox, I had hoped it was just Gnome modified. But I was able to easily install openbox, so problem solved. Then I went looking for the taskbar he used, Tiny Task Manager it seemed to be called. Still no problem, I was able to find and install TTM, but couldn't activate it. So there went my desktop. All in all this is just a minor frustration and nothing big, but I had hoped for FULL customization of any part of the desktop, which seemed to be a bit of a wrong impression I got.

**[COLOR="blue"]Support[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: Astonishing, truely amazing. Just this forum is enough to learn anything you need to know about Ubuntu and if you really can't find it, you can always just ask. Perfect!
Compared that to the Windows 'problem solver'... Need I say more?
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: My default installation didn't seem to have the Help file included OR I lost it... oh well.

**[COLOR="blue"]Look 'n Feel[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: Well, to be perfectly honest... I feel good about using Ubuntu. I don't know why &#8211; is it the freedom? The refreshing look? The perfect stability? Knowing that the risk of virusses and hackers is almost non-existant? Or is it just the speed of my system now that it's running a real OS &#8211; but it's a whole new way of working.
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: Being used to Windows for so long, it's a big step... Maybe a welcome-window with a step by step guide of the basics of Linux would be nice. Why not even an option to have the taskbars and buttons of the desktop set up to look more like a fresh Windows install?

**[COLOR="blue"]Installation[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: No problems, fast, smooth, easy. Perfect
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: N/A

**[COLOR="blue"]Compatibility[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: It detected every single piece of hardware (except for one) during the installation or on first boot. I wasn't even aware that it had already detected and installed everything. The way it should be.
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: It didn't find my printer (because stupid Lexmark doesn't have Linux driver for that model, so it actually doesn't have anything to do with Ubuntu)

**[COLOR="blue"]Setting up the desktop to my likings[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: SOOOOOO many options!!!! Give me more please!
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: SOOOOOO many options!!!! I need better explanations for every option please!
[COLOR="orange"]NEUTRAL[/COLOR]: There's a lot to learn for a first time user and yet I seem to be missed that fully customisable desktop. I would love to be able to alter Gnome Desktop and panel to the extent that I can have every button and feature detached from the panel or even replace the button graphics with my own. Hell, why not even the ability to configure the taskbar with custom scripts?

**[COLOR="blue"]Out-of-the-box Software quantity[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]Ubuntu[/COLOR]: WOW
[COLOR="red"]Windows[/COLOR]: LOL

**[COLOR="blue"]Out-of-the-box Software quality[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: It sure it a complete package! Most of the software fits in perfectly with the look of the desktop environment, it ALL does what it's supposed to do without many (any?) bugs.
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: Some of the software seems like it doesn't belong to be standard for an OS like Ubuntu.
I'm a die-hard Winamp fan, I knew about XMMS so it was one of the first things I installed. It looks great, work great and then I right-clicked the player to get to the menu and though WTF? That is just ugly... I have the desktop at a resolution of 1400x900 and the menu that opened in XMMS is just HUGE, the font is ugly and that makes it hard to navigate. Then there was this other piece of software called ... umm... little wizard or something? It's a click 'n point programming environment for children, or well, that's what it claims to be. So I installed it for my kid, I didn't expect too much of it, but was very dissapointed when I opened it the first and only time. The first button I clicked didn't even do anything and it looks like some software they dug up from a 1996 freeware collection CD... What is it even doing in the synaptic list? I know it's all free, but free isn't a synonym for crap, is it?

**[COLOR="blue"]Compatiblity with windows software[/COLOR]**:
[COLOR="green"]THE GOOD[/COLOR]: It's possible.
[COLOR="red"]THE BAD[/COLOR]: It's too hard sometimes. If I'd want to run Ventrilo, I have to copy files from a windows install to get the sound to work and even then the sound quality is very poor. Windows software in general should be supported by Linux itself. I know why this isn't possible and I know that it will not be possible any time soon. I'm just saying that you can't blame software devellopers for trying to reach a larger audience by making their software for windows and not even bothering with Linux (probably because they don't know how to make it work in Linux.)
Btw: Is there a version of Direct-x for Linux or a substitute?

[COLOR="green"]Conclusion[/COLOR]:
Well, I'm having a hard time adjusting to Linux and I probably just need a pat on the back and someone telling me to hang in there. But whatever happens, I'm never going back to M$. EVER!


PS: If you notice a lot of spelling errors, English is not my native language, I hope it's not to bad.
PPS: I hope I didn't step on any toes with this. I get the impression that the devellopers don't really know what's bugging people who are new to Linux and I was just hoping to help out a bit...

I believe there is a backup fund that should last a while. Also the devs are not paid. It is foss.

Kde is perhaps more overwhelming than GNOME but is ok with customization.

The help file should be like a life ring at the top panel or under the middle menu.

Installation is fast. Suse took me almost 3 hours. However that has to do with the package management.

You will get advanced desktop effects and a more customizable Window Border. Remember you need the Restricted Graphics Drivers for this.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-03-04
To answer another one of your questions, you can make money off open-source software even though it's free to use.

Linux users don't tend to be shareware-fee-paying types, but you can make money off your own software by selling services related to it. For instance, Ubuntu's two main streams of income are through support and customisation. Businesses using Linux will often take out support contracts with their distributor, so they know that if something goes wrong there will be a company who will solve the problem and keep the IT side of the business running smoothly. Some companies want specific modifications done to Ubuntu for specific purposes, and Canonical can do these modifications for a fee.

Sourceforge is a website that provides free open-source project hosting. They have introduced a marketplace for project developers to provide support services to anyone using the code. My own project is tiny and for a limited number of desktop users (not enterprise) so I've never had anyone request paid services :-(  But the option is there!
