### Post by jsg24 on 2010-12-15
Hello everyone.

I have no idea where I could post this. So I decided to choose this section.

This whole post is basically one hell of a long rant and mumbling about my computer history using Ubuntu, thoughts, ideas and just plain madness.

If you really are going to read all this. Bravo. I know I honestly would get bored and give up reading by the second paragraph. So for those of you who will read this whole thing. I take my hat off to you. :)

Where to begin...

I had been a Windows person for mostly all my life. The gaming was great for starters. It was only in 2007 that I actually first started using Ubuntu on a box-PC. It was Ubuntu 7.04. My previous experiences of Linux were that it was a complicated scary OS, great for server people and large corporations, but not for a guy who wanted to check his email and use Facebook. Seeing how surprisingly user-friendly 7.04 was, I thought: “Hey Linux might actually be worth trying out.”

So, duel booting Windows for gaming use (Can’t ignore Doom 3 and Half Life 2), I started using Ubuntu for two years. Right from 7.04 all the way to 9.10 in 2009. I even installed it on my first ever laptop (Sony VAIO VGN-T150).

I experienced many problems along the way. Biggest one ever (and still is) is hardware support. And with each new update I seemed to run into even more problems while others luckily were solved either through the new upgrades or my own researching forums and finding help.
Problems ranging from lack of audio and video codecs (trying to play videos from phones like Sony Ericsson's and Nokia’s, that tricky .3gp format), internal microphone’s not working (Skype itself could use a nice makeover for Linux I think.), and many other problems I’ve forgotten about. It’s been such a long time.

While I really suffered through all these problems, and I’d say I was “forced” rather than “seduced” into learning a bit about the Linux shell and systems, (I am no computer techie or systems administrator) I thought: “Hey, for what I’m paying for this... nothing... this is a really cool system. It’s a cool thought that anyone could rig a computer with one of these and get a fully functioning (mostly) OS. Think of poor people who can’t afford Windows. Think of business who could save money with this.” I got a really cool feeling using this software. I am naturally a sucker for anything convenient and free.

But through those two years, I eventually got the feeling that I don’t want to go trudging through command lines to solve problems, writing scripts. I wanted everything to just-work. Absolutely everything. I envied Windows and Mac people. (That does not include Vista.) Their hardware vendors made drivers for their laptops, and so many applications were made for their systems. They never, ever had to see a command prompt. Everything was through a nice GUI.

I really appreciated (and still do) Ubuntu’s attempt at making everything super user-friendly and the use of GUI’s for everything.
I loved how I never had to worry about viruses, malware and pop-up ads. I could take a USB-stick from a friend using Windows and physically remove the virus and malware files because they were toothless in a Linux environment.

But towards the end of 2009, I was fed up. There were just too many problems with Skype, Facebook, laptop-sleeping and others I now can’t remember. So one day I just had enough. I sold my Sony laptop and bought a Samsung NC10 at the end of 2009. Rated the best netbook of 2008 by TrustedReviews.com. It came by default with Windows XP.

Having stopped serious gaming about a year back before this, it was strange using the XP environment every day. But in no time whatsoever I got used to it. I also “open sourced” it. 

Ubuntu had given me a liking for things open sourced. On Windows XP they were always free, were easy to uninstall and were basically a guarantee of no malware or spam. I used everything. VLC media player for music and video. Peazip for archive extractions. Sumatra PDF viewer. Open Office. ClamAV. Gimp. Firefox. Pidgin. If it was open source (or similar, GTorrent - MIT licence) and worked on XP I got it.

I stupidly never backed up my OS on an image. But I was happy with everything. As long as I made my weekly virus checks (about the only thing I missed from Ubuntu) I was happy. Everything just worked.

Eventually though, I became lack with the virus checking and browsing and surely enough the irritating malware came back. A few months ago I was thinking of upgrading (wiping out in this case) Windows XP to Windows 7. 

However, I thought, Ubuntu’s come a long way since I last used it. I’m gonna wipe off XP and use 10.10 and if I don’t like it, I can always wipe it off and get Windows 7.

So that’s where I am now. And I am writing this from an Ubuntu 10.10 OS on my Samsung NC10.

Ubuntu has come a long way indeed. I love the new look and feel of it. (Don’t be shy Canonical, go for the Mac look by all means. You have at least one person cheering for you.) I loved how so much more just worked with my netbook. And that in the installation phase, you could install all the restricted codecs like mp3 and .3gp (remember that one?) and such. I was very impressed with it.

However, it didn’t come problem free. But I’ll come to that in a bit.

I fully support the way Canonical is taking Ubuntu forward. I’m very exited about the new Unity look, merging the Netbook and Desktops into one with 11.04, Ubuntu One and the ability to purchase things in the Ubuntu Software Centre and Rythmbox (soon to be Banshee). 

I am not a proponent as such of open source specifically or free software. What I am strongly a proponent for is user-friendlyness (as in the average computer user in the world, more than 70% of which use Windows) and convinience (as in just works and if it’s free, all the better).
If Canonical can make Ubuntu more of this, as most surely their aim is, I am all for it, wish them the best of luck and guidance and eagerly await their future releases.

As for the lack of problem-free I have with this 10.10 I am currently using......
The flash has improved so much, it is great, but not entirely as smooth as I would like it to be. There is no brightness options in 10.10. I can not adjust the brightness of the screen (the physical screen because of age and damage makes the whole screen white sometimes when the lcd screen gets too bright) and the default laptop buttons for that do not work, unlike the sound ones which do (was very impressed with that). I can not upload multiple pictures to my Facebook albums using Java with either Firefox or Google Chrome. I have to resort to either the simple-Facebook-uploader or using Shotwell to upload albums (very happy with Shotwell since I can’t remember the same functionality in the previous Ubuntu versions I used). And lastly, the two-finger scroll is disabled in the GNOME mouse GUI options. It would be really nice to use it. (I have to resort to using the shell to activate the two-finger scroll, which deactivates everytime I put the netbook to sleep).

And while I know I can hack my way around most of these problems. I don’t want to. I don’t them to be there in the first place and secondly, I want to use GUI’s for everything. I don’t even want to know of the shells existence.

Besides these problems, this is wishful thinking on my part, I wish Canonical would choose Google Chrome as Ubuntu’s default browser, but meh. That’s me.

If all my current Ubuntu problems were solved, I would be utterly contented. I literally could not criticize it. It would be my perfect little free open source machine. Almost  like my own baby. A perfect system for me. And yes, I know I can purchase a Dell where it would be exactly that, but dammit, I want my NC10.

For myself, personally, it is not 100% assured that I won’t decide to switch to Windows 7 at some point. If I had my perfect system, (ie this Ubuntu without my current problems) I wouldn’t need to switch back. What would be the point? This prevents me from playing games anyway and I’m simply more productive on this.

So yeah. If you’ve read all this, wow. Bravo again. I’d pat you on the back if I could. I would never read something this long. :P
This is mainly like a public tech diary that everyone can read. Glad to get all this off my chest.

Well here I am. Using Ubuntu 10.10 and looking forward to installing afresh, Ubuntu 11.04 and loving everything I’ve heard about it.

Um... Thank you very much for reading all this.
