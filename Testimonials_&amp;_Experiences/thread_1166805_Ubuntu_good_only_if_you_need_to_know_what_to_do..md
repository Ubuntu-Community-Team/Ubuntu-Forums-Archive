### Post by Jestersage on 2009-05-22
First off, I am not saying that Ubuntu is a bad system. In fact, after the time I spent trying to resurrect the XP/Vista machines of my firends, I gain new love for the CLI of Ubuntu.

That said, I cannot help but to wonder if it's seriously ready for desktop. Simply put, Ubuntu does not behave like Windows, which is what most lay person expect. They want it to behave either like Windows or Mac, and if it differentiate even a bit, they will be crying for help. Here's some example: I have a friend who asked me how to read chinese characters in Office 2007, when I easily googled it.

There are plenty more habits that are not good habit, but they are common habit. Many of the people just do not like Vista not just due to performance issue, but the fact that they have to "run as admin" all the time. Also, I notices that in GNOME ubuntu, there are no option for password less entry for regular accounts, somethign that my mother complained until I loaded up KDE on it (after trying it out on my virtual box). While I realized that it's best to change some habit, I think it's about time we take a technical support approach: bend the linux to users will.

The biggest problem is that Ubuntu is also the distribution used in the Freegeek program, which is a non-profit program/organization dedicated to reuse old computers. While Ubuntu is the best in that it maximize the performance, it is also probably one of the least user-friendly, due to the reliance on CLI for many tasks. End result is that I have many friends and people that I help, aside from asking for help, also complaining about the operating system being difficult.

I have heard that SuSE and Linux Mint actually improved of such aspect, in addition to throwing in non-free repo, but I have yet to try them out and confirm such rumor. Biggest problem I can see is that Ubuntu, as a Desktop, does not behave like Windows, which is actually a reason to for people not to adopt Linux.

At the very least, we MAY have two distribution that seems to be willing to include non-free stuff. However, as we are users who decide to stick with Ubuntu, I propose that we should also do something too – by trying to create more front end that is more user friendly, and by helping new users– not by making them to adopt our "good" way, but to acommodate their easier but non-secure problematic way, by teaching them methods that does not require sudoing.


Sidenote: I think professors should let students a choice to use what kind of server-software for an IS project. working in a large enterprise make me realize that Microsoft is still very strong in the server market.
