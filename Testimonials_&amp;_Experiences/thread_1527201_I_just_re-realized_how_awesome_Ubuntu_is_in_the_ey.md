### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-07-09
I always knew Linux was awesome since I started using it in 2008, and I've helped over 20 people move to it already, and many people converted just after seeing me using it, how pretty and fast it was. Of course, Linux has many benefits beyond this, but for many people that's enough.

So, someone saw me running Garry's Mod on Ubuntu and got curious. I explained to them most of the benefits over Windows (many) and warned them about Wine's non-completeness. We checked the AppDB and all of his apps were there, and I told him he can dual boot or use something like Wubi to take it easy and see how he likes it.

I'm pretty good at making Ubuntu appealing and resolving users' fears. If they had an Ubuntu store, I'd work there.

Anyway, point is he was really excited, and suddenly a worried look got on his face. He asked with a nervous gulp, "how much do I have to pay to get Linux?"

So I went to the Ubuntu website (which is really impressive to newbies, by the way) and clicked on 'how is it free?'. I explained what open source is and what it means, and that Ubuntu is free. I also told him that if he learned how to program or knows someone who does, he can add functionality to his favorite software.

He totally flipped out and told me to install it right then!

To be honest, even though word of mouth and little glances aren't the best forms of advertisement out there, they're extremely effective in getting people who would like Ubuntu into it. That ends up being people from all corners of life.

In fact, I've only helped convert about two people who you could call nerds. The rest are just normal people who didn't want to have crashes, slowness, viruses, and thought it'd be fun to try something new. They're all very happy with it, since it gives them so many options. It's like a breathe of fresh air.

I think if we had a 20 second ad for Ubuntu on TV for just a week, showing the Facebook integration, music applications, maybe a little KDE or customization, Ubuntu One, maybe a little compiz- you know, the stuff that most visibly sets us apart- and mention that it's free, people would go crazy.

Anyway, long story short, seeing the look on that newbie's face kinda' reawakened the realization of what powerful, interesting, and beautiful software I have in my hands, and the principles behind it. It's kind of invigorating. XD

### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-07-09
No, I told him all about Windows applications. See the part where I mentioned us going to AppDB and making sure his programs worked, and telling him the options to keep Windows or use a virtual machine? I demonstrated this all to him and gave him fair warning.

Also, Flash even on a computer with 384 MB of RAM runs fine, at least with all the machines I've installed it on. I think maybe the problem isn't so bad as you think it is, although Flash could munch a tiny bit less CPU. I really don't hear any complaints about that.

So yeah, I learned early on that you need to inform them and research what they use their computer for so they're fully satisfied. It's all just common sense.

### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-07-09
Ah, it's true- the most long-term and persuasive kind of advertising is by word of mouth and demonstration.

However, as they will show their family members and get others interested, there is also the downside. Whereas an ad can be seen and remembered distinctly by millions of viewers, a few dozen million users of open source aren't all likely to be the kind that share it with those around them like I am. Many are too bashful, don't know enough to feel comfortable 'selling' the idea, or think it will make them look too geeky even though they just popped in a CD and waited fifteen minutes for it to install.

So I think we really have done a great job spreading it the old fashioned way, but it wouldn't hurt to make people more aware. All I'm saying is that, most people who have heard me talk about its benefits, even without seeing it, start to really want it, and evaluate their attachment to their current OS.

If people saw how good it was AND heard its benefits, and it was portrayed to them on television, where everything feels more traditional, official and 'canonical', then I think there would be a lot faster rate of adoption.

Of course, you probably agree. Does anyone think there's a negative side to advertising Ubuntu on TV, other than negative funds in someone's pockets? Hah, I believe I just highjacked my own threat with my ranting. XD
