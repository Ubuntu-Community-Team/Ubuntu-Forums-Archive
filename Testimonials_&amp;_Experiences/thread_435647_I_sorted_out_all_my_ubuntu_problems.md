### Post by nuser101 on 2007-05-07
I replaced my PC with a Mac Plus in the early eighties, and switched back to the dark side of Windoze in the mid-nineties because I needed it in the business community. I hated having to do that, and still do. The Mac is a much better OS, IMO. However, with the advent of OSX, Apple made the conscious decision to assume, as does Microsoft, that the end user is a thief. Gone are the days when disaster recovery simply meant you had your files backed up. Now, the system state is monitored, and just dragging files to their original locations does not restore the usability of a crashed system. Because of this, I began experimenting with dual boot Windoze/Linux systems a couple of years ago, but was unhappy with the device driver situation. I'm now on Ubuntu 7.04, as a dual boot with Windows, and this release supports all of my hardware to an acceptable degree, although I have admittedly old stuff:
Deskjet 4160
Scanjet 4370
Sound Blaster Audigy Platinum ex (the level of support for this card is very good)
All in Wonder 7500 (have to use xawtv, not the best player, but the tv in works, and that's all I need)
ASUS P4B266 motherboard
Pentium IV, 1.6 GHz

You can see that this is an old, home built computer, but the point is that everything works. I had to spend about three hours figuring out how to configure the hardware. Contrast this with the days I spent recovering from a crashed disk in Windoze, and I see economy in switching. Yes, there is a frustrating learning curve, but it is nowhere near as frustrating as having Microsoft Backup's ASR system tell you that you're not allowed to restore your files from you backup medium because the hardware configuration has changed since the backup occurred. This "feature" is only documented at microsoft.com. You don't know about it until you've been bitten, and the bite is extraordinarily painful. Not only that, but when you recover the asr.inf files, assuming you can still get the backup to run, ASR will cheerfully reformat your hard drive to include a partition that is exactly the same size as that of the original system, even though you may have purchased a larger drive after your crash. The rest of the new drive will remain unformatted. Finally, because of the hardware configuration change, it also assumes that you are a different user, and you have to re-establish file associations. The "recovery" process takes days, not hours. You will face the music on the day your system crashes (and nearly all disks do at some point in time). At that point, he may consider a Linux distribution. I know, because I just went through all of this, hopefully for the last time.

Because of my history in the corporate world, I do have a mild memory (senility is approaching), but unix wasn't that bad, and Linux seems to make about as much sense as that did. It's a pain to have to relearn terminal command syntax, but it's better than thowing money away on systems that can't be recovered without an even steeper learning curve.
