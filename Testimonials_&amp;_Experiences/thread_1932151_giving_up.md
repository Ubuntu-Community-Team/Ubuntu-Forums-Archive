### Post by doublemeat on 2012-02-29
You could have titled your post the same thing I just did (I also have spent six years trying to switch).

I feel your pain. I mean I've experienced, and feel it. I've gone through the same things.

Well all except the losing data due to Ubuntu part. Yeah I've lost lots and lots of data over the years, but not BECAUSE of any one OS. And trust me, you can lose data in Mac OS X in a REALLY big hurry! (E.g.: Just try "merging" two folders in a Finder window. Oops!)

I'm surprised you didn't lose even more than that due to hardware failure. I lose dozens of HDDs a year. Just last night my boot drive vaporized on a desktop PC. (Literally. The magnetic coating got scraped off the platters and blown all over the inner platter compartment.)

If you don't back up, you WILL be screwed. No matter the OS. No ifs, ands, or buts. Reading into your post a little more than you wrote, it sounds as if you absolutely, positively would have lost all that stuff eventually, with or without Linux. So yeah, FLOSS - Linux distros especially - are often pains in the butt. But it is unfair, unrealistic, and inaccurate to blame it for losing your data. YOU lost your data. (That would be like blaming your boss for firing you, after showing up to work drunk every day.)

Personally, I use CrashPlan+ on all my computers. Solaris server, Windows desktops and notebook, Mac OS X (on the same notebook), and even Ubuntu VMs. The server is the main thing; Crashplan on everything else is mainly for transient data and configuration files. Crashplan is super easy to configure, but rediculously-powerful for the power-user (e.g. me). I reviewed and tested all of the major cloud-based backup services. CrashPlan+ came out so far ahead it was rediculous.

Don't rely on Apple's Time Machine or whatever their wireless backup thingy is called. It won't protect you from fire, theft, or the police getting the wrong address for a drug bust. If you aren't backing up off-site in near-real-time, and you also value you data (e.g. priceless and irreplaceable baby videos), then you are just playing Russian Roulette. Sure there are many cons to backing up to a third party. (I'd love to additionally backup to a remote machine that I own.) But it is orders of magnitude less risk than backing up to your own stuff in the same location...much less not backing up at all!

### Post by flemur13013 on 2012-02-29
- Keep data (e.g. music files) in a separate partition from the OS.

- Keep backups on a physically separate medium - USB drive, DVD - not just in another partition on the same drive.  Even keeping a backup copy "offsite" isn't a bad idea every once in a while: DVDs are cheap.

- DON'T have your backup gizmo connected when you install a new OS or upgrades. (In fact, don't connect or mount unless you need to).

- I don't know about Mac, but observe the large number of posts about backup problems and failures on [www.sevenforums.com](www.sevenforums.com). MS puts users' data in the OS partition by default, and the MS Win7 backup software is really awful.
