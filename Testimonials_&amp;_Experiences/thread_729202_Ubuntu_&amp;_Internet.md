Reading between the lines, I think part of the issue might be the valid complaint that, for various reasons, Linux is sometimes still not for Joe Sixpack -- i.e., users who don't want to have to know about the technical details of getting these extremly complicated bits of hardware and software to talk to each other.  Having read the thread where he posted the USB manufacturer's response, it's pretty clear that you'd have to have some idea of what you're doing to get it up and running.  That combined with his post here in this thread leads me to think his issue is that he wants something simpler (at least as far as the end user is concerned).  

Various distros are aiming for such ease of use, with varying success, but as I noted above, hardware manufacturers need to step up to the plate somewhat and either provide specs or functioning (and maintained!) drivers for Linux -- as they do now for Windows -- before many of these issues can be resolved.  However, since a lot of consumer electronics often includes more functionality on the hardware level than is enabled at the software level, with more functional products sold for higher prices (this allows manufacturers to use the same chipset to produce multiple product lines -- chips with marginally more defects are used for the lower-end products -- I used to work for a semiconductor production equipment maker), it's highly unlikely that they will be quick to release control over the software side of things.  

Anyway, ultimately, it sounds like he's unhappy with Ubuntu (and Linux in general) because it isn't as plug-in-and-run easy as Windows.  And that's partly Microsoft's doing.  Oh well.

Part of the trouble here seems to be an assumption based on a miscomprehension -- there can be no such "universal inputs and outputs" unless everyone (i.e. the manufacturers of the various hardware devices) agrees to build to some such standard -- which folks clearly are not doing.  As others have tangentially come close to pointing out, Mac works so well with its hardware precisely because Apple very severely limits the number of hardware devices they use.  As I've tried to point out, Windows works so well with its hardware precisely because Microsoft has abusively consolidated its marketing position into an effective monopoly -- but even so faces numerous problems with drivers and hardware compatibility, brought into sharper focus through the whole Vista debacle that continues to unfold (c.f. SP1 and numerous Vista SP0 drivers that will not work now).  

So ultimately, what you have run into is what is known in financial terms as **[COLOR="Purple"]a structural defect of the market[/COLOR]**.  Linux can "grow up" all it likes (and frankly has advanced in leaps and bounds while Longhorn rumbled along and finally spat out Vista), but the maturity of Linux **is not** the issue at hand.  Rather unfortunately so, as this means the Linux community cannot control its own fate in this area.  :-|

You describe "devices that are out there being used _in the world at large_", and with that qualifier, Linux does a pretty good job.  Unfortunately for your case, rburgan, it seems that "the world at large" no longer includes modems, in part because, as 3rdalbum/Chris notes (emphasis mine):

> **3rdalbum said:**
> Dialup modems were standard for some time, and then **manufacturers decided to cut costs and make them all substandard**... 

I wouldn't class "dialup modems" as the preferred kit for connecting to the internet. Naturally, there will be more programming effort going toward wireless cards than softmodems. 

Given Microsoft's abusive but overwhelming market presence, manufacturers making such sub- or non-standard dialup modems are going to release drivers primarily for the Windows platform.  And since the modems are not standard, and since most computer peripheral hardware manufacturers are extremely close about their specs (partly for reasons I mentioned earlier), the Linux community has to jump through a lot of hoops to get a working driver.  And sadly, as the number of users of any particular type of hardware decreases, so too does the pool of possible Linux driver developers and the overall motivation to put in the toil and struggle to create a driver.

> **rburgan said:**

### Post by bonzodog on 2008-03-25
I think people have overlooked something fundamental here -- dial-up interet connections are 'old tech' and proper support for USB dial-up was never implemented from the kernel level outwards. The only hope for using dial-up with linux is to use a serial modem. When connected to a serial hardware modem, linux just talks to /dev/ttyS0.

Linux is primarily a broadband OS, that relies on good internet connections. Linux will always go to your ethernet port for networking as it really does need that. What needs to be done where-ever you are is to get onto broadband. Dial-up and Linux have never gotten along that well. I actually went to the bother of getting ISDN in 1996, before the advent of broadband in the UK, as linux just did not get along with dial-up.
