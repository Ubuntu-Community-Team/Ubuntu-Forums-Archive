### Post by JewelledDragon13 on 2008-04-22
I have moderate computer experience; I am running dual boot on a homemade machine so I can't call myself computer-illiterate, but when I run into problems I nearly always need to ask for help.

I think opinions from people with less computer skill are especially important right now because Ubuntu is just about ready to break into the general usership... but it can't do so unless it appeals to--and is usable by--the general public.

I switched to Ubuntu last spring after a horrendous experience with my computer, Dellorean*.  My motherboard died (the computer was just 2 years old).  Upon discovering that I needed to pay $40 just to have the problem identified, I decided to just salvage the non-proprietary parts and build a whole new machine.  Thus, Dionysus was born.

Of course, that meant that the key to my Dell copy of XP no longer worked.  After about my 4th phone call to Dell and/or Microsoft, I decided it was time to switch.  I installed Feisty.  I kept a dual boot for games and what not, but I've only used it under great duress. 

On the whole, my Feisty experience was very good.  Installing was only a minor rigamarole.  I was immediately impressed by the eye candy; the ease of installs; standard settings that actually made sense; and the way the packages interact as an integrated whole, rather than a bunch of random software that all happens to be on the same machine.  Feisty worked smoothly for me in general with only a few lockups.
However, as an inexperienced user, I found a number of frustrations.  Installing some things that involve downloading tarballs seemed overly tricky.  The lack of an easy equivalent to ctrl-alt-del made the few lockups much more frustrating.  I got my dual-monitor display working with Twinview, but I had to manually configure it every time I booted up (and Twinview is kind of annoying anyway).  Twinview refused to play nicely with Beryl, making me lose much of my shiny.  And a few things I just never got working: my 5-button mouse, video/audio streaming over the internet, and my flash drive.
Satisfied with my system and not willing to go through another rigamarole to upgrade, I never switched to Gutsy.

However, about a month ago, Dionysus began running quite slowly and I discovered that it was using up all its RAM.  I decided a fresh install was in order as soon as Hardy came out in beta.  This, however, turned out to be a large rigamarole.  Feisty wouldn't upgrade to Gutsy, so I did a fresh install.  I messed up my partitions in the process.  While my Feisty dual boot worked by the book, this time I spent a long time being able to make either XP or Ubuntu boot, but not both.  A friend fixed this.  I also needed him to get my dual monitors working.  Now, though, I finally have the dual display I wanted all along.  My 5-button mouse also works perfectly.
Obviously I haven't had much time to mess with Hardy, but it looks promising.  I love the lack of the wanton change you find in Windows (redesigning things just so they'll be new); Hardy seems to change what needed fixing and keep the same the things that were good to begin with.  I won't complain about any of the minor problems until they've had some time to get fixed.  The bad news: it's still using all my RAM.  Perhaps it's just time for me to upgrade, though.
Firefox 3 is sweet so far too, though that's only tangentially related.

Conclusion: It's a bit of a rigamarole.  For a beginner, it takes some dedication to decide to use Ubuntu; however many annoyances Windows has, it's easy to make it run, at least.  However, I know what I want my machines to do and am willing to put in any work necessary to make them do it, and nearly always, Ubuntu will do it.  And once it's running properly, the interface is so useable it's almost uncanny.  It's taken a lot of patience (and a few good Linux friends), but the payoff is more than worth it.

Come to think of it, a truly pedestrian computer user would find everything at least as straightforward as Windows, since the places I got hung up were with the boot and the display.  The real trick is convincing people to learn a new OS when they're already familiar with one, and if they won't, that's no fault of Ubuntu.

Oh, and two other things I should mention:
1.  I would never consider switching back to Windows.
2.  Whatever problems I've encountered, they've been solved--or at least a lot of useful advice given--by friendly, intelligent, English-speaking people.  For free.  With no wait time.  You guys are great.  (And also especially my computer friends here at college.)




FYI, my specs:
Gigabyte 965P-S3
P4 3.2 GHz (from Dellorean)
1 Gb RAM (from Dellorean)
GeForce 6800
120 Gb hard drive (from Dellorean)

*Caveat: I owned a Dell because my parents offered to buy a computer for me on the condition that it was premade.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-04-22
After seeing my workmate rip DVDs, check her e-mail and surf the web in Ubuntu with almost no instruction on my part, I believe that the general population can use Ubuntu with no hassles, if it is preinstalled and preconfigured like Windows is.

There's a reason why tarballs are tricky to install. It's because you're not actually just installing them, you're compiling the source code. The reason why developers provide source code rather than just precompiled binaries is because Linux runs on all sorts of processors, and a binary made for your Pentium 4 will not run on a PowerPC-based machine; nor the other way around. Windows doesn't have this problem, because it's only really made for x86 processors like your P4.

(If you ever tried running Windows on an Itanium-based computer, you'd definately have a greater appreciation for compiling source code, lol).

It's actually cooler to compile from source code, because you can select all sorts of things to compile-in; like bleeding-edge features, for instance. Having said that, I very rarely go outside Ubuntu's extensive repositories.
