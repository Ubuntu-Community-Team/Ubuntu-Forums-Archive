### Post by mivo on 2008-01-14
> **Dr-KDE said:**
> I am sure Gnome is faster but it lacks a great deal in customisation which is for most users a must.

Actually, Gnome is very customizable, but this is mostly "under the hat". KDE is easier to tweak and customize, but to a point where it confuses many users and appears cluttered. You can make KDE and Gnome look and behave almost identically.

In my experience (which is very subjective), KDE is less stable than Gnome, and there are apparently more programmers that develop with GTK in mind. Not saying that KDE is bad, I run it on another box, but I keep coming back to Gnome for the machine I work on most of the time. KDE3 also uses some unfortunate architecture designs, but KDE4 promises to fix this. 4.0 was very underwhelming for me, but then again, "zero releases" of KDE have always been that way. 4.1 will be the "real" version 4.

But it's all about choice. Use whichever you prefer. :)

Edit: As for the other sub-topic ... well, home computing was booming in the 80s before MS really entered the picture. Yes, there was DOS, but this wasn't what the average, non-office/professional user used. Many people believe that MS introduced GUIs, mice, etc... and that just isn't so. I used a GUI-driven OS and a mouse in the mid 80s already (TOS/GEM), years before Windows, and Apple had this sooner even. Online networks, like FidoNet, Zerberus, etc and "internet-like" communication systems like the Usenet, all boomed and grew without Microsoft being in the picture. I think it was really the large online companies such as AOL (as much as they are disliked), CompuServe, GEnie, and others that brought "online" to the masses and are responsible for the breakthrough (at least to a good degree). The "web" was just the next step. Yes, Windows did have an impact on the computer market, but they didn't create the market -- they just gained dominance over it with bundle contracts and various other rather successful (but not necessarily ethical) measures. There is no doubt in my mind though that the internet and the "web" would be around today even if we had never heard of Microsoft. We would probably have more e-pluralism and more choice. Just like we did before Microsoft monopolized the computer world.

Netscape, by the way, was the first company that really embraced open source on a cooperate level by releasing the source of their browser. "Open source" is as old as computing, though. In fact, it was there before "closed source". There has always been "public domain" software, publicly availavle source code ("listings"), and so on. As a teen, I would type in source code from magazines. :) Computing, online communication for "normal people"  (outside of military/educational faculties), open source, they all didn't start with Microsoft or Windows, nor with Linux.
