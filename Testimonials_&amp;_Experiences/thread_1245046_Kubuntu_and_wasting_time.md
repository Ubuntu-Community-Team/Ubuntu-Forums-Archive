### Post by warreno on 2009-08-20
I tried Kubuntu on another system and found it chugged rather badly; it was sluggish and buggy and prone to die for no readily understandable reason. Frankly, it still needs some work before it's ready to go on the level of Ubuntu with GDE. It's not that they don't *care*. It's just a work in progress.

Sorry you had to find out the hard way.

I guess the canned response would be: *Well, it's Linux, it's all technically beta-release software*. I know that doesn't really help. But some betas are more stable than others, and KDE, while it looks very pretty, isn't quite there yet, I think.

On the good side, you haven't actually wasted time; you've learned something. It was a painful lesson, but it's not one you're likely to forget soon.

Our species is a lot like crows. We're attracted to colorful, shiny things. Shiny, however, doesn't always equal *good*. This is one reason I still don't have an iPhone.

Glad I could help, to the extent that I did. Sometimes we all need to vent and just express frustration. (And the formatting changes actually are an improvement, so well done.)

I started out really wanting to like KDE, but as I used it I began to realize it really wasn't as collected, slick and user-ready as GDE. On the back end everything is still Ubuntu, of course, but up front the difference between KDE and GNOME became apparent fairly quickly. GDE feels faster, more spry and responsive, and generally less full of quirks or bugs.

I have K tools on my system, but they don't use the K interface any more, and I think my experience using Ubuntu has improved because of that.

That's not intended to belittle the efforts going into KDE, which I know are huge. But for end-user systems — especially for those still fairly new to Linux — I think the GDE package installed by default with Ubuntu is probably still the best choice.
