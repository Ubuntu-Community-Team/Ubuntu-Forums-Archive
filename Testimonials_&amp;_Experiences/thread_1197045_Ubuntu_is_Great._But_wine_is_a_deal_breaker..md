### Post by DeusExM1 on 2009-06-25
I recently installed ubuntu 9.04 on my friends computer. He has great specs (quad core processor and 9800GT for gaming). Well we tried to get some of his games to work, and they are not even that extraordinary:
CS:S and Day of Defeat Source. 

These games have been around for a while and they are on the APPDB list on the main wine website. CSS is ranked silver i believe, while DOD is platinum. Since ubuntu is the most common distro i figured that it would be compatible. 

So we tried CSS. Game crashes after 5 seconds in game, fonts are unreadable. Tried several work arounds (turn off in-game friends, turn off pulse audio, blah blah blah). Too much time to get a single app to work which has been around for forever. Got the fonts to work, but couldnt fix the crash so Gave up on that.

DOD. what can i say. This thing starts, but thats about the only positive thing. With 9800GT , the FPS is around 15. Tragic performance. Although it runs, its anything but playable. 

The conclusion is pretty simple, my friend just asked me to get Vista back on his PC. And the silly part is that he liked Ubuntu and said that if it wasnt for gaming he would never go back to Windows. I realized that i myself am waiting for a few new games to come out (Deus Ex 3), and seeing how long its taking to get even the older games to work, i must say im unwilling to wait 10 years to play a game that is about to come out. 

Ubuntu is by far the best distro i ever tried, and as a stand alone there is no match for it. But something needs to be done about the gaming market. And i dont think that wine is the answer. Emulation is never as good as the real thing (loss of features, and worst of all loss of performance). Games need to natively run in linux - the gamer market is too big to be ignored. 

So i decided to install Vista back on my friends and my computers. Im definitely going to keep looking if games ever get implemented better. At the moment, its just not good enough. I would say id try dual booting, but i feel thats too much wasted HDD space. Windows imo is a piece of **** software, but MS got all the games, so what can you do...

### Post by bazmonkey on 2009-07-10
*To the OP*:  Why did your friend want to use Linux in the first place?  I've found that people who end up liking and regularly using Linux on their desktops do so because they have a personally-compelling reason.  Ya gotta **want** it.

Unfortunately, everything works for Windows because everything comes with Windows, and Linux doesn't really come with much of anything in the consumer world outside of a few netbooks.  Linux will always be playing catch-up in this respect, and there isn't really a way to do anything about that besides help play catch-up.  Since you do have to willingly install the OS (most people never, ever install Windows themselves), and you do have to tweak it to get your system to work (Windows only wins here because the manufacturers of your system paid good money to get it to work), someone who's merely been persuaded by their geek-inclined compádre isn't going to be too impressed with the experience of doing all of this just to end up having to learn to do what you already knew how to do with your computer.

I'm not saying that's the case.  I'm just sayin'...

*To the rest of us*:  I personally doubt the free-software movement is going to make inroads into the gaming market.  It takes a LOT of effort to make a game worth showing itself in the gaming world.  How many not-100%-working applications do we have in Linux that are still useful?  Half-finished programs are still able to do something.  Half-finished games are broken games.  Combine that with the fact that coding a game isn't exactly more exciting than coding anything else (if that), and viola, the FOSS world is nearly void of truly good games.

Proprietary games on Linux is another story.  I hope that happens.  Short of one of us actually doing something about it, however, I doubt anyone will just up and decide to write teh über FPS, for free, on Linux, and actually succeed.

We hear time and again about how they're all just waiting "for good games" on Linux, and in a way, that's exactly why it's not here.
