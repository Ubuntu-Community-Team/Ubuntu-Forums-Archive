### Post by N00b-un-2 on 2008-09-14
I've been using Ubuntu Hardy Heron for a little over a month now as my primary operating system.  Due to my frustration with Microsoft in general, but in particular it's massive security iniquities--even when using a firewall, an antivirus program and anti-spy/mal/adware--I've been fed up with the whole Windows system.  After having both of my computers go down due to viruses within a month of each other despite using the aforementioned software (which I paid good money for, mind you), coupled with Microsoft's impending mass exodus to Vista coinciding with it's termination of XP support starting next year has forced me to look into other avenues for my computing needs.

So far I would say that my experience with Ubuntu has been wholly positive.  Although I'm brand new to Linux, I would consider myself an advanced computer user, and I find that despite the operating system's inherent "learning curve" for the more in depth administrative functions, I am quickly able to adapt to the new system and easily learn how to work in Linux.  That being said, as far as the more basic functions and applications that the average computer user looks for--it's all there.  Simple, easy to use programs are available for free to do virtually anything you could think of, from organizing photos and music, to graphic editing or even recording audio and writing music.  OpenOffice works every bit as well as microsoft office, but it doesn't come with the premium price that Microsoft Office carries.  For what one would pay for the new Vista operating system and Office alone (which does not come with windows...) you could go out and buy a brand new computer!  It's ridiculous.

Now, don't get me wrong.  Ubuntu is not perfect by any means.  I've had to do my fair share of troubleshooting when it comes to getting my various unsupported components to operate properly.  ie; my nVidia graphics card, my Atheros WiFi card, and my Lexmark printer to name a few.  I've just about gotten all the bugs worked out of my systems.  But the fact that I've been using Ubuntu for over a month now and I'm just now approaching the level of functionality that I have right off the bat with Microsoft says that Linux isn't quite ready for the masses.  The average computer user would be lost at sea when it comes to trouble shooting any of the problems that I've encountered since switching to Ubuntu.

But on the other hand, Microsoft isn't perfect either.  My brand new Laptop computer came with Windows Vista installed.  I can't stand trying to use Vista.  It's far too buggy, and considering how long it's been on the market it's lack of software and hardware support makes Vista no better than any other system out there.  Vista is the worst product that the Microsoft corporation has put out since Windows ME, which is notably one of the biggest failures of the modern digital age.  I don't like vista's interface, it's frustrating to use, and it's lack of backward compatibility with XP is a bunch of crap.  So I decided to downgrade my laptop from Vista to XP, only to find that Windows XP on it's own does not contain SATA drivers, and I was forced to reprogram my install disk with SATA support just so I could install XP.  And then once I was able to install the OS, I was greeted with a system crippled by an acute lack of drivers.  After several days worth of hunting, I was able to find all the drivers I needed to get my computer up and running mostly on other laptop manufacturer's websites.  I'm pretty irritated by the fact that Toshiba didn't offer the drivers I needed through their website.  They only had the Vista drivers, which for the most part are not compatible with XP.

So in my case it's a "you're damned if you do, damned if you don't" situation.  Windows XP is not supported out the box on my computer, Windows Vista is a garbage system, and Ubuntu has virtually the same problems as XP.  Only difference is, Linux is a good stable platform without the security issues that Windows has.

All I can say about my experience with trying to get my non supported hardware to work in Ubuntu is thank God for Redhat.  Redhat is pretty much one step away from Microsoft, but if it weren't for at least one Linux distro whoring itself out to the corporate world, users of the freeware open-source linux variants wouldn't have readily available software and drivers to scavenge from.

I'm pretty satisfied with the way my computers are running now.  I can do pretty much everything I want to with Linux.  But I'm still not ready to format that other partition on my HD just yet.  Linux's support of games is not quite up to par just yet, and try as I may, I STILL can't for the life of me get my web cam and mic to work properly with any chat programs and it's not for lack of knowledge or trying.  Nothing short of further software development for Linux is going to fix those problems.  It's so close that I can almost taste it, but the fact of the matter is, Linux just isn't ready for the mainstream yet.

The future of Linux is in preinstalls. It's where everything is headed and it's so much easier for everyone involved. As for your webcam, better do as the Mac users do and find one that's Linux-compatible.
