### Post by Pragmatist on 2007-06-10
> **Eoghanalbar said:**
> Ubuntu is not all bad...But it doesn't "just work"....
I hear this sort of complaint all the time and I think it is founded on a big misconception.  Windows does not "just work", nor do most things.  There is always some learning curve.  Of course, if you spend years using the Windows OS everywhere (work, home, library, university, friend's house, kiosk, etc...etc...), then you make that your definition of "just works".  Now it is true that there is a notion of ease-of-use.  There are whole fields devoted to ergonomics and human-computer interaction.  Microsoft has borrowed heavily from Apple in this regard.  Yet many people who are used to windows, find the macintosh awkward to use!  Does this mean that Microsoft has improved on Apple?  I don't think so.  I think that many people are used to using Windows, and want to spend the least amount of time possible learning a new OS.  They are frustrated with the "blue screen of death" and viruses, because they require time to fix.  The same concept: people don't want to spend time getting their computers to work!  This is natural.  So, what I'm trying to say is that there is a certain xenophobia and aversion to the learning curve that frustrates people when it comes to new things like Linux.

Yes, but does Linux require more work to learn than Windows (i.e. assuming I'm from another planet and I've used neither)?  I would honestly have to say yes!  A little more time.  Why?  Because Windows controls something like 90% of the home computer market.  This provides a lot of advantages such as hardware manufacturers that design drivers specifically for Windows since it is in their economic interest to do so.  Or, multimedia software companies that provide you with proprietary decoders--for a price!

So, why bother with Linux at all?  Partly this does have to do with ideology, but there are also a great deal of practical benefits such as: A more secure and stable system (say goodbye to viruses), much more control over your system, a huge array of excellent and free software, and near-infinite flexibility in creating just the system you want.

There is a general concept that with greater flexibility comes greater complexity.  This make a lot of sense as you have more choices.  If you have a toolbox with two tools, you have to learn how to use two tools; if you have one with twenty tools, you have to learn how to use twenty tools!

> **Eoghanalbar said:**
> I've wasted huge amounts of time, and gotten NOTHING in return.

The problems you've mentioned normally have simple solutions.  There are a few important things to learn that will help you solve a large array of different problems.  Read the thread linked in my signature, for instance.  The general principle is: "don't reinvent the wheel".  There is almost always someone who has had the EXACT problem(s) your having.  Look them up on the forums, in the wiki, using search engines, using man pages, using synaptic and the repositories, etc...etc...  Then, if none of that works, create a thread and post your problem here.  Problems like getting your printer to work, usually only need to be resolved ONCE.  You set it up and then you never touch it again.

> **Eoghanalbar said:**
> 'm stubborn, and I've been trying to find a way to fix these problems and it's getting me nowhere, and even if I did, I'm sure I'd just run into more.

I think your initial comment about frustration explains this one.  If you think about it, you would realize that you have no basis to believe that if you fixed your current problems you would run into new ones.  However, given my explanations above regarding the learning curve, and the necessity to adjust your attitude that you are learning something new, you might think you are running into "problems", when in fact you are just running into situations where you need to learn something about your new OS.

I> **Eoghanalbar said:**
> Is there any reason that I shouldn't just give up?

I don't know, is there?  You came to Linux for some reasons, what were they?  Perhaps you had false expectations that Linux was a panacea.  GNU/Linux is a wonderful OS for the right person in the right situation.  Personally, I don't believe it is for everyone.  I know several people who aren't interested, because they don't want to invest any more time in learning how to use their computer.  If their current OS works for them, then it makes no sense to change.  Only you can know your situation and what is best for you.

to fix your browsing problem, you'll just need to disable ipv6 like so:
```
sudo gedit /etc/modprobe.d/blacklist
```
and add this line to the end of the file:
```
blacklist ipv6
```
save the file, reboot your computer, and you shouldn't have any more problems with browsing.

I'm a SysAdmin with degrees on the wall and letters behind my name, but it's all related to Microsoft and Cisco.  Granted the Cisco training helped, if not pushed, me towards the transition to Linux, it was still a little more difficult... different than I had anticipated.

I started messing around with Linux, seriously anyways, just about a week ago. It blew my mind at how hard it was to do something as simple as install an application you just downloaded or to turn on your NIC.

What do you mean I can't just click an .exe? I have to do what? Compile the source code? Jeez don't the programmers do that? Terminal? What do you mean I can't just create a folder there? Sudo who? All I wanted to do was turn on my NIC.
 
However it's nothing gnu. Raise your hand if you remember those fun filled baby punching days in front of a Win95 machine the first time you had to reinstall the drivers for a Creative modem or sound card that you lost the disk to and Creative refused to make the drivers available to the public without purchase. Oh yeah, that was worth the wad of cash I dropped on that  hardware, OS bundle of joy. And that is the glaringly obvious difference. We were paying for the honor to beta test Microsoft products, with Linux it's all free and all out in the open. 

So is it worth it? I would have to answer with a a most definite, yes. And I'm really enjoying being part of what feels like a grass roots movement to expect the right to only have on your PC what you want on your PC and be able to view the source behind the curtain if so desired. 

But nobody is denying there are going to be transition pains. Sure, I may have spent all day just trying to make [these]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=13576&highlight=ipw2200") directions work, and resisting the urge to drop my laptop in the fish tank, and punch a penguin. But when it all clicked together, it was better than pepperoni and pineapples on pizza, and my network is more secure for the effort. And as a bonus I walked away with a gnu understanding and respect of an old friend I thought I already knew. 

-Disclaimer- No babies or penguins were punched in the moments that led to this post.
