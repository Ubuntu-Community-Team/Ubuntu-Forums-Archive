### Post by buster2209 on 2012-05-11
I've been using Ubuntu since 8.04 with the gnome desktop and loved it.  I'm an electrical engineer and part of my job is writing software so I'm well versed in programming and by no means a stranger to computers.

When Unity first came out, I was appalled.  It just didn't work.  It was an beta UI and we were all guinea pigs.

Fast forward to 12.04 and now it feels like a UI should.  It's impressive... but it's also a resource hog.  I have a Revo 3700 and 720p playback has a low FPS rate.  And yes, I have installed all the correct drivers and whatnot but the FPS is still low.  Booting into 2d is a totally different experience.  Clearly, not much time was spent on this.  After digging about, it looks like it isn't anything to do with Unity 3d, but merely a skin to look like it.  Anyone who has an older or low powered laptop has to deal the miserable experience that is Unity 2d.

I tried Gnome3 and it's pretty and works well, but is completely unintuitive to getting any work done.  What it is good at though, is going on my little Revo and being the base UI to play XBMC, download torrents, and move files around.  It's nice to use when looking at a large screen that's about 10 foot a way.

Moving onto my laptop, I have 12.04LTS on there running Unity and, I must admit, I like it but struggle to get work done efficiently with it.  To that end, I found the Cinnamon UI, which is a fork of the Gnome3 project, and this works for me.  It seems the cinnamon guys have listened to what people want and have integrated the best aspects of Unity, Gnome3, and Gnome2 together to create a UI that appeals to power users.

One thing that has to be mentioned is that the Gnome3 shell does have a classic mode which goes back to gnome2.  This is a good thing to be bundled in the 12.04 release as people who are wanting to stick with Ubuntu can safely update to 12.04 from 10.04 and get an extra 4 years of updates.

Whilst I admire Gnome3 and Unity, they need to become more mature in order to be used by a power user to actually get things done outside of casual computer use.  I mean, I have a tablet running the Android OS for when I want to casual web surf or e-mail.

Given time, I believe Unity and Gnome3 could be fantastic UIs, but as it stands, they just don't have enough ease of customisation to satisfy this user.

I look forward to the future.  I think both Canonical and Gnome have rock solid UIs.  I just hope they mature their UIs quick enough that the die hard Linux users are still around to care.
