### Post by Iandefor on 2005-11-20
Well. I've been a part of this community for a couple months, and I've had a good time with it; I'll give a synopsis of how I got to Ubuntu and why I'm sticking with it as testament to the quality of Ubuntu.

**Ichiban**: About five years ago, I was an irritating little young'n. I thought I knew how to use computers well because I could make Windows do what I wanted sometimes. I had a lot (I mean a *lot*) to learn about using computers. At this time, we'd just gotten a new computer (Which is what I'm still using today, quite literally. It's never been upgraded. It takes money to upgrade...) which had Windows ME preinstalled. Yeah, we dealt with that piece of crap OS for about 4 years. Slowly, the population of my household dwindled after we got it. Two of our users left; My mom and my sister (My mom got separated from my dad and then my sister graduated and is now attending college), leaving my dad & I. My dad likes to fart around on the internet, but we didn't have internet on this computer for a period of about 3 years, so he didn't use it. I was the sole user of the computer at that point, and even then, I didn't use it for much because it tended to crash for no reason (I imagine the spyware that we collected didn't help lol). It collected dust in the corner for a period of two years...

**Niban**: Well... It was near the end of 2004, and I wanted a working computer. I looked sidelong at my dear old computer, and grinned. I thought to myself, "Maybe I could get that piece of crap to work again..." and thus did my quest begin. It started with Windows (I'd experimented with Linux at this point, but didn't quite get that Linux != Windows); I figured I could probably get 2k working on it. Then I thought about Linux... I might give it a try. It could hardly hurt the poor old machine. So, I farted around and found Xandros Linux, a fine OS for people who are just migrating from Windows to Linux and don't really want to screw with the OS as much as they just want to use it. However, it wasn't entirely satisfactory (It felt a little rough around the edges; IMHO, that's because it was based off of KDE). Soo... after about four months, I started looking idlely 'round for a different distro. I bumped into Ubuntu and decided I'd give it a shot.

**Sanban**: My Shipit CD's arrived late in June, but I didn't actually install it up until late August. When I did, I found Ubuntu to be a pleasing distro. 
 Let me tell you *why* I found Ubuntu as attractive as I did... When I got it installed, I was greeted with a calm, subdued, and quiet login screen. I logged in, and it got better from there... The computer took forever to log in, but when I finally saw my desktop, I saw beautiful, subtly curved brown panels, an attractive desktop background, and pleasant, functional icons for all the drives on my desktop (I believe, at around the time, I had my Jumpdrive and my external CD-RW/DVD drive plugged in, and they showed up without difficulty as well as my internal HD), along with a set of applications for just about anything I needed to do (As well as access to up to 16,000 extraneous packages on the repositories). My only problem with it was it's intolerable slowness because of the age of my computer. Even then, I was able to remedy that by installing a lightweight desktop environment instead of GNOME, and it still looked good and worked fine. I was able to use everything I needed to. 

**Shiban**: Now, after 5 years of working with this computer, which has put forth an admirable effort to put up with everything I've thrown at it (Compositing with all the features like dropshadows, fading menus, etc. enabled) and suffered under the dominion of 3 different OS's (Windows ME, Xandros, and Ubuntu) I'm going to be buying a new computer... this next one is going to have Ubuntu on it from the outset, I'm so pleased with it. Ubuntu, for me, has worked like a charm, and has been able to adapt to my needs without complaint from installation to today. I'm converted, as it were, and will never ever go back to Windows for serious work*. I'm all Ubuntu. 


*To be totally accurate, when I get my new computer set up with a serious graphics card (which'll be later than when I get it, I assure you), I'll install Windows onto a separate partition so I can play games with minimal complaint from the computer. I don't really wanna screw with Cedega or WINE just to play some games.

### Post by Iandefor on 2005-11-20
> Nice . So just how did you learn about these two particular distributions (Xandros&Ubuntu)? And what made you try them instead of others? Also, how much did you learn in Xandros? How? What about Ubuntu? And just what is **ichiban, shiban, sanban and niban!?** 
lol, I'll answer your last question first, since it was so much more adamant than the other questions. If I got my Japanese right, Ichiban basically means 1st, Niban means 2nd, Sanban means 3rd, and Shiban means 4th. Probably, I've done something wrong formulating those (Japanese has two names for every number and I can't remember which ones you use for which situations). 

I learned about Xandros via [Linux.org]("http://www.linux.org/")'s list of distro's. I'm not entirely certain why I chose Xandros. I guess I saw it was being pitched as a good distro for people who were migrating from Windows, and I thought to myself "Hey, that's Me!"
I learned a bunch from Xandros; primarily, how to use the CLI to my advantage and how to script to my needs, as well as the value of RTFM, because, at that time, this computer was *sans internet*, and thus my only resource was the Fine Manual ;). When I needed to do something, my only resource *was* the manual or an online guide I could print out at school and take home. I aso learned a lot about how Linux differs from Windows (Mounting a filesystem as a directory, for example). 

Around May, I started hearing a lot about Ubuntu and I figured I'd give it a shot; I was tiring of Xandros at that point because I was really learning about Linux and Xandros really wasn't as good as it could have been and should have been. I'd heard a lot about other distros at that point, but I'd heard the most good things about Ubuntu.
In addition, I could just order some free CD's from ShipIt and not have to download the ISO over my school's internet connection, a risky enterprise at best, because there was so little bandwidth available that I'd be halfway done when I had to go home and we weren't strictly allowed to download things anyway. 

I'm not at all certain I'd be able to enumerate the things I've learned from Ubuntu, but I feel like I'm a lot more competent using Linux now than I was when I first installed it.
