### Post by toupeiro on 2007-07-25
I've been using computers since I was seven years old.  I cut my teeth on Amiga A500 running workbench 1.3.  I didn't realize how completely innovative the Amiga was until my family got our first 386 running PC-GEOS (an OS based on MS-DOS 3.3 and Windows 286)  Through the years we've "upgraded" through various MS-DOS versions up to 6.22 and Ran versions of windows, Windows for Workgroups, and eventually Windows 95 and NT 4.0 Workstation.  Truth is, I think I've tried every variant of windows to come out, including releases like Nashville (the unreleased windows 96) and other code name releases.

I've never felt like I had the control over any Operating System I ever ran from Microsoft.  There was always some black magic going on.  As I came into my career in IT, I supported many large IT environments where I was hoping I could learn more about this black magic.  I figured if I knew how it worked, I could make windows do what I want it do.  Well I am here to tell you after doing huge upgrades moving corporations from Novell 4.11 to Active Directory 2003 and Exchange 5.5 to Exchange 2003 that most of the time Microsoft doesn't even understand their black magic most of the time.  Anyone who has watched their "best practices" flip flop over the years will know what I mean.

So jaded over windows, I began to focus more attention to the other operating systems I've supported over the years.  The UNIX Flavors, the Linux flavors.  DG/UX, Solaris, Red Hat, HP/UX.  My first exposure to Linux was Red Hat 5.2.  While I was not entirely impressed at its hardware support at the time, the operating system was very appealing to me.  Perhaps because I saw then that "this isn't put together well now, but there is absolutely nothing stopping it from being put together to run on anything."

I think Ubuntu is the final outcome of that thought I had years back.  I've thrown ubuntu on all kinds of hardware imaginable.  I plan on trying to get it to work on a  SunFire V880 at work at some point.  Ubuntu has done a great job of supporting hardware off the getgo, and the linux kernel has developed so much since 2.2 when I started, and documentation and help has grown so much that you truly can make the operating system do what YOU want it to do, and it is only getting better as time goes by.

Ubuntu has brought the innovation, power, and promise of linux to the end user better than any other release has to date, and I look forward to what they will bring in the future.  It's all I run, and I have no need for windows, which is something I've been dying to say for a long time.

So, from a happy techie, Thank you!
