The problem is that when the tab bar is on the side of the screen, all of the logos and text describing what each tab represents goes, unless you hover over it with the mouse.

Absolutely!  Move your bottom panel to the side of your wide screen and right-click on it.  Select Properties (second choice from the top on my version 8.04) and widen the size from perhaps 24 pixels to 100 or more.

The words begin to appear.

Edit:
Be careful about moving the top panel:  If it has a lot of icons on it, when you move it to the shorter dimensioned side of the screen, there is no way to grab hold of an empty panel pixel to move it; instead you end up copying icons to the desktop.  A solution is to create another panel by right-clicking the bottom panel and moving  some of your icons to it to clear a space that you can grab to move the side panel.  Then move the icons back and then right-click on the new, empty panel to delete it.

Edit number 2:
Another idea just occurred to me, that if you have a wide screen (I don't):  Create a new panel, move all your top panel shortcuts to it and move both panels to the side.  The menu titles will run vertical instead of horizontal, so I don't see much benefit in widening these panels.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2009-10-26
> **linux4life88 said:**
> I didn't know where else to put this so I put it here.

Suggestions

I basically have one problem with Ubuntu and that is the out of the box factor. Driver support seems to be a major problem that I have every time I use Ubuntu (mainly graphic and wireless chip support). I hope to see driver support improve in the future.

Wireless support is actually getting to be good now; if it's not right out-of-the-box it can usually be installed through Hardware Drivers. I have three wireless devices and they all work out-of-the-box with Ubuntu 9.04, although one of them didn't work out-of-the-box on Intrepid.

Graphics card support: Proprietary ATI and Nvidia drivers will never be installed by default due to licensing reasons, but they are available to install from Hardware Drivers. VIA and SiS chipsets are much more difficult because either the drivers for those things suck, or they are not available. It's not so much of an issue down in Australia as it's rare to find a computer with one of those. Poulsbo (new Intel integrated chipset) is also a compile-it-yourself job on Ubuntu, but hopefully the issues surrounding that will be resolved in time for 10.04.

Ubuntu's out-of-the-box support is much superior to Windows Vista's (and probably Windows 7's too). Things continue to get better in this area, so stay tuned.
