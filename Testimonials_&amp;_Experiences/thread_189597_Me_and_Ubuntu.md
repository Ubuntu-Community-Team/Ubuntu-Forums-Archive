### Post by gr0kzer0 on 2006-06-05
The laptop I'm using here and now is the first computer I've owned since I was a kid and my folks bought me a ZX Spectrum (for those who don't know what the hell I'm talking about, the Spectrum was one of the first color microcomputers, and had a whopping 48K of RAM - this was in the 1980s, and believe me, 48K was damn good back then).

Anyway, I left school, went through a period of crap jobs, and computers sort of fell by the wayside - it was a toss-up between computers or food, and I'm afraid the fear of starvation won.

So, for 20 years or so, I had very little to do with computers.  I went to university, and used the PCs there to write essays, but that was about as much contact I has with computers.

Then I became ill - I won't bore you with the details of my illness, suffice to say I couldn't work any more.  When you have nothing to do, and all day to do it in, you get bored pretty damn quickly.  I started going to the library - to read books.  But they had a bunch of computers there too, and I started to mess about on them.

My computer skills were very poor.  I'd learnt the rudimentaries of word processing, spreadsheets, databases and the like at uni, so I wasn't a complete n00b.  But what I did mostly at the library was surfing the web.  Point and click, with Internet Explorer, that's just about all I could do really.

My online explorations, and books I borrowed, sparked an interest in this thing called "Linux".  Of course, the boxes at the library (and the community centre, which I discovered also had PCs) all ran Windows.  But my curiosity as to Linux and Unix was piqued.  I got myself a free shell account (at [http://sdf.lonestar.org](http://sdf.lonestar.org)) and that was it - I just *had* to get a computer of my own.

Luckily, the place where my brother worked were upgrading their equipment, and he got me this laptop.  It's nothing fancy - 450 MHz processor, 156 MB RAM, 4 GB HDD - but it was perfect for me.  I'd planned ahead for the day I'd have a box of my own, and had acquired a few CDs with Linux OSes on them.  I had OpenSUSE, SimplyMEPIS, and, courtesy of shipit, Ubuntu Breezy Badger.

For some reason OpenSUSE wouldn't work for me - it kept complaining of dependency problems.  SimplyMEPIS loaded okay, but I just didn't get along with it.  Dunno why, it just wasn't right for me.

But Ubuntu... man oh man, I fell in love with Breezy as soon as it installed.  Of course, Gnome ran pretty slow on my old laptop, but I installed the xubuntu-desktop, and now it fairly zips along.  I surf the net, I use Abiword to write all kinds of junk, I ssh into my shell account... it's given me a new lease of life.  My illness had left me feeling pretty low - not able to work, feeling like I was on the scrapheap, my life over at age 35 - but now I'm revitalised, chatting with people from all round the world, getting into debates and flame-fests on Usenet - it sounds pretty melodramatic, but Ubuntu has changed... has *saved* my life!  I feel alive again.

Now I'm waiting for shipit to send me Dapper CDs (unfortunately my internet connection is through using my mobile phone as a modem, which is far too slow to download something the size of an OS).  And I'm so looking forward to it.  Breezy is damn cool... and judging by what I've read about Dapper, it's gonna blow my socks off.

I've done a bit of Linux advocacy, in my own limited way.  I passed my extra Breezy CDs to computer-literate friends.  Unfortunately, they're all Windows users, and can't see why I'm so Linux-crazy.  Maybe Dapper will change their minds.  But anyway, Ubuntu has given me a new lease of life.  Some days I'm house-bound with this damn illness of mine... but with Ubuntu, my laptop and my mobile phone, the world can come to me!

Thanks, Ubuntu!
