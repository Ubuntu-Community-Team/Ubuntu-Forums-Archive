### Post by bill516 on 2008-10-29
I installed the latest release candidate for 8.10 last evening.  My initial reactions can be found at:

[http://discuss.extremetech.com/forums/thread/1004424765.aspx](http://discuss.extremetech.com/forums/thread/1004424765.aspx)

I'll not repeat what I said there.  Several other observations since then include:

The "Control Center" apparently has been removed.  Granted it duplicated other menu items, but it was convenient.

Cruft Remover appears to be convenient.

System/Preferences/Screen Resolution now gives aspect ratios for each resolution choice.  Thank you!

Suspend appears to work!  This is a first, going back to the 7.10 release, which was my first.

Hibernate worked when I tried it, although the shut-down did not appear to be clean.  More testing is in order.  If suspend/hibernate do work that is a huge step forward for lap-top users such as myself.

The only device on this system not yet tested is the camera in the lid.  When I download and install skype that may permit me to test it, since there seems no other included application that accesses it.

My thanks to everyone who has worked on this release.  It looks to be quite good.

### Post by bill516 on 2008-11-01
Here is the text, which apparently is not easily readable as originally posted.

An Intrepid Encounter

So here I am, happy as can be with my stable, always-works Ubuntu 8.04.  So far as I am concerned, 8.10 is a non-event and I'll have fun watching everybody bring down the Canonical servers in a few days.

And then my wireless connection went south for no apparent reason.  Way south.  We switched routers, switched network managers and carefully checked and double-checked the ESSID and WPA keys.  Along the way we verified the proposition that if a person persists in the same stupid behavior they tend to get the same stupid results.

Slowly the realization dawns that we have some corrupted files someplace.  We might look for them but we do not know where to look or what the baddies will look like when we find them.  (What's going on?  Hard to tell because the GUI net mangers give so little feedback.  I suspect that for some reason the connection is being made but an IP address is not being provided, which means some sort of DHCP problem.  Just a guess.)

Hmmmm, is this the time for The Big One?  Do we pick up the red telephone and end Linux as we know it?  Not hardly, that means going back to Windows, which was very good to me in the past but does not seem to be the future path.  

Well then, what other 'nixes do we have around here with a reliable wireless capability?  As it turns out, just two, one of which is a surprise.  We look at Knoppix, DSL, and Mandriva on LiveCD.  The new Mandriva is especially attractive but maddening.  It will tell me I have a wireless connection but the browser says I do not, "Address not found" is getting really tiresome.

Ah yes, little Puppy [Linux 4.10] wags its tail and says we can play.  Amazing when I think about how simple it is and how small the development team is compared to the big players.  But it works every time and I am sorely tempted.  So why not?  Because even if I set up a firewall, which I do, I am out there on the net as Root and I just cannot get comfortable with that idea.  Then it hits me; An Ubuntu 8.10 "release candidate" made its appearance last week and maybe that is worth a try?

Bingo!  The Gnome Network Manager is now version 0.7.0.  I've no idea what they did, but it works every time on the LiveCD.  OK, we decide on a clean install to clear out whatever debris has infiltrated my system.  So there is a bit of work saving documents, photos, music and browser favorites.  We do not save the entire user home directory because we want to eliminate any chance of carrying over whatever gremlins are playing with the gnomes.

As an aside, this is all happening on an Asus Z96J laptop, which runs an ATI 1600 graphics circuit (no Compiz for you, young man!) and an Intel 3945ABG wireless card, normally reliable -- until recently.

So here we go.  Roughly 35 minutes later we are at an Intrepid Desktop.  Doing the straightforward installation (a lovely example of how well these things now work) proves to be simple.  Make a few choices, tell the installer where to put the new system, which in this case means over-writing the 8.04 system and go brew some tea.  On return we invest another 15 - 18 minutes getting immediately specified upgrades, which means another tea.  This is all done wirelessly and it is a very nice two-tea job.  If we are doing points we give Canonical perfect marks here.

And let me point out that Wubi remains available and it is a very clever installation alternative.  Essentially users would install 8.10 in a folder within their Windows system, which means no need to dual-boot, learn GRUB intricacies, etc.  Bye-bye VMware and virtuality, if I may coin a word.  (Why not, everybody else does.)  It is clever and people who want to do a low-risk installation in order to explore Linux will find it attractive.  Wubi is down-home smart and it will make a lot of friends for Ubuntu.  Listen up development teams.

Then there is the desktop.  This is Gnome 2.24.1 (with a 10/24/2008 build-date, which certainly makes it fresh) but it remains familiar and a person likes it or not.  No KDE 4.xx glitz to be seen here.  And no Kthis 'n Kthat kwhich kis kfine kwith kme.  The default resolution on this machine came up at 1680 x 1050 at 60 cycles.

But oh golly is this one ugly desk.  The wallpaper is simply brown splotches representing - what?  One hopes that the final product will be spiffied, but there is a lot of spiffing to be done.  Good grief people, borrow somebody from Mint or Mandriva or - anybody.  Please?  If the Ubuntuns got perfect marks on installation they all failed their art studio finals.  Admittedly this is a subjective area but I would grade this effort as only slightly above wretched.

But aesthetics aside, 8.10 arrives with a well equipped toolbox.  Open Office, Firefox and all the usual suspects are here.  Under Applications/Accessories Manage Print Jobs comes off as a clone of the well-known Windows utility.  Applications/Games offers 17 simple but effective ways to have fun and kill time.  No shooters here, but then again this is a socially responsible distribution you understand.

The new things are not boat-rocking but they are nice.  For example System/Administration Create a USB Start-Up Disk is a terrific idea and one you will want to investigate.

Applications/Graphics presents F-Spot, the Gimp and Xsane, which recognized my HP-4070 scanner when I lashed it up. (My simple HP D1520 printer also was no problem.)

Applications/Sound & Video includes Brasero, Totem and Rhythm Box.  As always with Ubuntu proprietary closed software codecs are not loaded by default.  I have found, however, that going to the Medibuntu repository and installing them is both simple and quick.  There should be no great problem if you need them.

Finally Applications/Universal Access contains the Orca Screen Reader and Magnifier, which should be a god-send for people with vision or hearing problems.  Nicely done, Canonical.

So you can load up and go to work and there is one benefit to the no-glitz desktop if you cannot Cube; this is a nicely responsive system and it should make older machines feel youthful again.  New ones will have their after-burners engaged, I suspect.

What's to do once all of my Docs are back on?  I will get me to Medibuntu and I'll also install Abiword and Gnumeric.  In fact I like those enough that Open Office may come off.  I just do not do heavy lifting in document production these days.  (Evolution is enough like Outlook to make me shiver.)  K3b probably goes on because I prefer it to Brasero.  The Terminal needs an icon on the top bar (whatever they call that thing) and the kill-switch for misbehaving applications also has to be there.  I still need to find out if build-essentials came on board with gcc this time.

So should you join the frenzied 8.10 downloaders?  Not if you have a stable system doing what you want it to do.  Stay there and remember that an operating system is for housekeeping.  The fun is in the applications and the Debian/Ubuntu repositories are chock-full of stuff.  If you have problems or are just curious then you need to at least look at both Mandriva and Mint.  They are attractively done  and I suspect they will work very well indeed for a great many people.  Keep Puppy Linux with you when you travel.  It is a terrific lightweight system and for growing numbers of people it is their preferred system.

Bill Julian
Oct. 28, 2008

As uploaded to ExtremeTech Forum

### Post by rfarmer on 2008-11-02
This is my first post and let me start off by thanking all those that have posted useful answers to questions I so needed answered.

I am like many here, mainly a Winlows user but interested in linux. I first started playing with ubuntu 7.04, seemed ok but was not real impressed. 7.10 was not bad, did most of the things I wanted to. But was really only something I played on. These were both on a Gateway MT 6840 laptop, There were resolution and wireless driver issues, but not that big a deal once I figured out how to configure them.

First version I tried on my desktop was 8.04, once updated to 8.04.1 was pretty good. But I have dual monitors on a nVidia GT8600 card and was having performance issues with seperate X screen and Visual Effects. I did an exhaustive search for answers, and even though I probably missed it, seemed to come down to poor driver support from nVidia. Lot of little things that nagged at me, gdesklet would not work for 64 bit, knotify crashed everytime I tried to run k3b. Nothing major, but enough to keep me running back to Windows.

Along comes 8.10 and I think why not, might as well give it a go. Installation is quick and smooth, log in and download the nVidia driver. Reboot and configure seperate X screens. Open Visual Effects and cross my fingers. Extra Effects working with no lag. gdesklets working on Amd64. And so far no issues with Knotify.

All I can say is I am impressed, been running for 3 days without rebooting to Windows. Everything so far has been flawless. A big thank you to the entire ubuntu community.

Wanted to add I am using ubuntu studio 8.0.4.1 on my laptop and it works beautifully, so I have felt no need to upgrade there.
