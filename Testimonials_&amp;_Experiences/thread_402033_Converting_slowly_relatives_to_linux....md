### Post by bmartin on 2007-04-05
In a sense, converting people "from the bottom up" is the best way most individuals can spread Linux, provided that's what they (the people spreading Linux) want. Some people could care less: as long as Linux works for them, why should they care if anyone else uses it?

The people I help make the move are all friends and relatives, or people that come to me complaining about Windows. My 11-year-old brother has been using Ubuntu now for several months. I installed Kubuntu's desktop and let him choose between Gnome and KDE, asking "which one do you like better?" His response: "I don't see a difference." Priceless. He had been using XP ever since it came out. He liked it so much on the computer I had built for his birthday that when his HD and CD drive on his old computer came out, I slapped Ubuntu on there, along with Beryl, so he has something fun to do with his GeForce FX 5700.

My girlfriend has moved over to Ubuntu from Fedora, formerly XP. With a little hand-holding, we got through the tough times. It was very easy to orient her to Fedora; Ubuntu was a little tougher. My experience was the same, but the fact that both of us used Fedora first might explain the bias. She now updates her own graphics drivers and can edit her xorg.conf file by hand, where necessary. We're hoping some day XGL or ATI's drivers will improve to the point where she can play games and use Beryl from the same desktop, as switching sessions is annoying, and direct rendering is necessary for some of the games we play.

I plan on moving my virus-stricken parents and performance-obsessed uncle over to Ubuntu when DSL arrives where they live. I've converted a couple of my friends to Ubuntu users and another one is on the way: he's sick of the Windows Genuine Advantage tool causing him grief and he's abandoning his past history of software piracy.

I haven't had to show off Beryl or bribe any of these people to change. A lot of people are interested in trying something "new", and most of the people who put down Linux either are intimidated or didn't have luck with it in years past. Personally, I had to install Linux 3 times several years apart before I got a distribution working smoothly. It takes a while to get used to it, but all the people I've converted agree on one thing: the time investment is worth it.

### Post by bmartin on 2007-04-06
> **Kearin said:**
> My elder brother is my hardest target. He has been using Windows for years and could probably be considered a power user, and he is a gamer. I have not given up, but he is very stubborn. My parents WOULD be easy to convert, but my elder brother is the official system administrator.
Anyway, good luck on getting everybody else to start using a REAL operating system.

It saddens me that some people don't consider Windows to be a "REAL" operating system. It's both as real and as much of an operating system as any other. My biggest gripe with Windows is that you can't modify it to suit your needs: it is illegal to do so. Simply put, there are things I wish I could do with Windows, such as use BASH, bind whatever keyboard combinations I want, etc. Fluxbox, on the other hand, does this very nicely.

I can understand your brother being a hard target. You don't have to install Ubuntu for your parents to see what it's like; set them up with a Live CD. I plan on having Fiesty shipped to my parents' house as soon as the CD is available, as they're on dial up and they have an ancient laptop running XP (600 MHz, 512MB RAM... I don't understand it, either). If your parents like/prefer Ubuntu, who's to say they must use Windows? If they don't like it, you wouldn't want to force them to use it. Besides, assuming they have internet access, you can perform a lot of maintenance from a remote location; this is ideal for me, as I live in a different state than my parents do, and I'm moving much further away (25 hour drive).

Go to people that might benefit from Linux. Trying to convert people who don't want to switch or wouldn't benefit from Linux is a waste of your time unless you convince them that Linux has something to offer. Power users are the hardest people to convert; I converted one only because we live together and he was amazed at the things I could do and how fast my computer runs.

Does Ubuntu have something to offer to your parents? Does it do something better than Windows does? You should focus on these aspects of your sales pitch. I "sold" Ubuntu to my brother without having to show it to him or explain it: it runs all his games; he doesn't play anything really new, mostly stuff on emulators and old DOS games; he's a true classic gamer. "Virus-free" was enough to sell my parents on it. "DRM" scared my girlfriend to Linux (she reads digg.com). Windows Genuine Advantage convinced my friend, John, to make the move. Microsoft calling my house and sending me postal mail scared me away (It was a long time ago; did they threaten to subpoena my ISP?). It's a little different for everyone.
