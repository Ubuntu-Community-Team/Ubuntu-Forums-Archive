### Post by khruska on 2007-07-23
After spending a fair amount of time reading about all the coolness in Ubuntu, I decided to dive into the pool and see just how warm the water really was.

Man was I impressed!

Recently, my son and I decided to build a digital picture frame for my wife for her birthday.  

We looked long and hard at just buying a pre-configured one, but decided to man up and build it from scratch.

We had an old Dell Inspiron laying around and we felt it could work really well as the engine for our picture frame project.  It didn't have a lot of CPU power or RAM, but we felt that just for displaying pictures, it should be fine.

Our goal was pretty simple.  Build the PC out with 20GBs of pictures and configure the system to quickly boot up and start displaying pictures without any interaction on my wife's part.  

After looking at our options, the gotcha was finding a solid OS that wouldn't cost an arm and a leg to purchase and load, and that could scale to meet the needs of the project.  

The laptop had XP on it, but even with virtually everything but the basics unloaded, the PC was still sluggish and just didn't do what we wanted it to do.

I decided to download Ubuntu and install it on the laptop to see what it could do.  I didn't have a lot of expectations for Ubuntu.  I've played with various flavors of Linux for the last 10 years, and I was yet to find something that I thought was easy enough to use to be a real threat to the great "Windows" empire.

Right from the start I was blown away.

The install was simple, straightforward, and took less than 20 minutes to get Ubuntu installed.  The flexibility of the OS was downright scary.  Don't want a feature?  Don't install it.  Want to change the desktop to meet a certain technical focus -- Done!  

In less than an hour we had configured Ubuntu into a scaled-down, free OS that booted up and started running a picture gallery in less than 45 seconds.  No user interaction, no fuss, no hassle.

We mounted the laptop in a large shadow box and cut access areas so she could attach power and add more pictures to the hard drive with a USB drive if she wanted to.

Needless to say my wife was in love with the gift.  She got a 12" display that held literally thousands of pictures compared to most digital picture frames that have 5" screens and can only hold a couple hundred megabytes of pictures on an SD card.

Since our first try using Ubuntu, we've gone on to 'recover' two additional systems that had been in the PC graveyard in our basement and turned them into viable PCs on our family network.
