### Post by Master-of-None on 2008-04-27
Thats the question someone asked me when I said I want choices.

Thats a good question. Why should we care about preventing computer operating systems from being a choice?

I'll explain why I care. Linux, to me, represents the ability to have something no one else does. A choice. Most people don't really have a choice, in spite of really having one, you see. No novice computer user has the knowledge of getting a Linux distribution. No average user knows how to use Linux to it's potential, leaving them frustrated. That leaves those groups unable to choose, or choose to stay where they are.

But why do I want a choice? Why should I stick to Linux as if Windows isn't an option? Windows is mainstream. Almost everything works for that. Not many commercial companies don't support it, some don't even know of Linux. This means I can't get my favorite game(s) or my favorite software.

My friend, who isn't necessarily a Windows fanatic, tells me that I shouldn't have to worry about choices with computers, as one IS one. Windows is there, so I shouldn't have to put myself through the frustration of being a full Linux user.

So why in the hell am I still here telling you about why I SHOULDN'T use Linux?

Because I like it better than Windows, obviously. I'm not a computer master, so I should have quit Linux long ago, but thats far from the case. I don't know programming, I suck at command-line, and I don't know how to compile anything, so I got more frustrations than many users here, but Ubuntu is really a far better experience than that of any Windows I've ever used.

So why is my friend saying I made a mistake with Linux? Why would Linux be considered a mistake? Do you share my views?

### Post by jdrodrig on 2008-04-28
> **Master-of-None said:**
> 
But why do I want a choice? Why should I stick to Linux as if Windows isn't an option? Windows is mainstream. Almost everything works for that. 

All decisions involve trade-offs. Money-wise, there are free distributions of linux as oppose to having to pay for Windows. But the main point is the *mainstream* term you used. Windows world is mostly driven by closed-source software where companies try to innovate aiming at the average user (for obvious business reasons).

In open-source world the innovation can come from anywhere, from the average or equally likely from the non-average user in mind. Also given the licenses used in open-source such innovation process cannot be monopolized by any single company. 

Equally important I think, is the self-selection of users that opt for Ubuntu or Linux in general. I would claim that the average linux user is more demanding in terms of customization and flexibility so that application development for linux, devotes more resources into those features.
