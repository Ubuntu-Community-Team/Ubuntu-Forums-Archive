### Post by prodigy_ on 2010-07-27
> **dforionstar said:**
> Perhaps someday all developers may work together on a single distro like Ubuntu
I don't think it's going to happen. Different people, different points of view.

Debian team prefers to keep their disto clean from any copyrighted material (they even rebrand their builds of Firefox). Ubuntu team has nothing against proprietary drivers that most distros refuse to include. And RedHat actually wants to sell their Enterprise Linux, not to give it for free.

Under such circumstances, it would be a great step forward for the community if we could agree at least about some **basic** things. For example a common set of networking scripts and a strong convention about how **exactly** they should work, would really help.

But far now, even such small and reasonable manifestations of unity are just a dream.

### Post by Rapture2k4 on 2010-07-28
As a network technician for the military, I get daily hands on experience with Cisco equipment. All of it is unix based command line. I setup a training lab using CentOS as a tftp server and a couple other basic functions. When I sat down to write some code for a simple, clean, and efficient GUI to challenge co-workers, I found Linux to be too complicated for a newbie to program in.

The things I liked were how simple it was to get the basic tools I needed up and running. The enormous amount of networking tools at my disposal (heck, even old school Solaris has a built in sniffer!).

The many many things that I didn't like is a bit extensive. I've been actively "playing" with Ubuntu since 8.04. Recently I decided to make it my primary OS for my daily internet usage, video editing, and home network management for my lab. I've pretty much given up on the hardware compatibility and just waited for a stable release that had no (or minimal) issues with my current hardware. I've spent MONTHS reading forums, sorting through google's ever increasing garbage search results, and attempting command line fixes. As a novice, I find it really pathetic that the most basic hardware doesn't work due to companies ignoring hardware support across linux (yet they provide Mac support!?!?!) and how difficult it is to get a simple answer without getting the typical "learn to google" response on IRC.

It seems advanced linux users exude the elitist attitude and have no sympathy for those who cannot find the answers on their own. Granted, this is my experience and is probably due to frustration when I could not find the answer I needed. Call me blinded/jaded.

I MUST learn linux for my career field. So, I've spent many many many many more hours figuring out how to do things on my own and expecting no help from anyone. It has been a very difficult road and has made me a bit annoyed with it. To this day, I still have no clue what the basis of Ubuntu is. I mean, Microsoft has forced it down my throat for years "It just works! Everything is so easy. Point and click." Don't get me wrong, when things don't work in Windows, it probably never will until the manufacturers of hardware step in. MS provides no support via forums, IRC, etc. Granted, I have enough experience to work through it on my own. I do applaud the Debian based dev teams out there for being actively involved with the community.

Just my toll for the boat man.
