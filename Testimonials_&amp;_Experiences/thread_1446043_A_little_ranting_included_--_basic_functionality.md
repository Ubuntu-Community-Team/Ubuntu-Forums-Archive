### Post by Aacron on 2010-04-03
Hey,

  First off, I'll admit that I'm not the *best* at Linux, more an intermediate user with a good strong background in how computers work.

  The other day I had a friend come in and hand me his laptop.  Even though it was under warranty, Dell and Best Buy (where he originally purchased the laptop), refused to resolve an issue with his Windows OS not booting.  I think he managed to acquire a nasty virus and it ate up a good bit of his hard-drive.  Anyhow, being a proponent of free software that works, I showed him my Ubuntu install and he loved the compiz effects, and all that -- so I installed Ubuntu, did a few basic configurations to get the things I knew he would want, and he went home with a working computer.

  He called me up this morning because he was at his favorite site, Yahoo Games, and it was not allowing him to play chess.  After a a minute and basic troubleshooting, I remembered that I had a similar issue with Flash not functioning properly, so I did a Google search, and found exactly what was needed to resolve the issue.  He had to download and install the official Adobe Flash player from the website to get input working inside flash.

  I understand that Ubuntu and most linux distros don't want to put in software that isn't open source.  I also understand that the repo maintainers can't possibly keep up with every single proprietary release.

  Thing is, for an distro that wants to 'just work' for the average non-technical user, things like this seriously inhibit Ubuntu and Linux in general from becoming the 'Ultimate Experience'.  For all of the security, and all of the freedom, that Linux has, and is, it seriously needs to get around to 'usability for average Joe'.

  Average Joe shouldn't have to do a Google search, or call a tech-head, to get the functionality that s/he needs.  Especially not with basic web-surfing and use.

  Maybe Ubuntu needs to make a repo for these users, that includes both open-source and proprietary software, is kept up to date enough to be usable, and 'Just Works'.  Flash is everywhere on the 'net.  Google videos, game sites, even sites providing drivers (nVidia/AMD), etc all use flash.  Why doesn't installing the Flash Player from the Ubuntu 'non-open-source' repos just work out of the box, the way that it is intended to?

  This, along with the sometimes overly-technical necessities of Linux, are what seem to hold it back the most.  I'm a technician, know my way around a computer, and there are things that I get frustrated with, and end up switching back to Windows for.

---End of rant, now a suggestion:
Make a repo that is for 'Average Joe'.  Suggested description, "Software in this repository may not be open source, nor is the software maintained by Canonical or any other well-known Linux distribution.  But the software in this repo will work without searching over half of the 'net, and without giving you a headache."
