### Post by detroit/zero on 2009-01-11
I'm not usually one to complain. I love Ubuntu, and I've been using it for two years now. It suits most all of my needs, and the support here at the forums is a first rate user-driven community.

Having said all that, on to the complaining (which I hardly ever do):

The wife's Acer laptop has been dragging a** lately with WinXP. She sees my computer running all the time, is rather enamored with the functionality I have, and says she's ready to take the plunge and install Ubuntu Hardy Heron on her computer.

She's quite a trooper, and plugging right along. She know how to install programs from the terminal using apt-get, knows how to use commands like lsusb and lspci to get her hardware info, knows how to use synaptic and add/remove to find software.. And only a couple of weeks into her Linux experience! I'm mighty proud of her. Plus, whenever she gets stuck or needs a hand with something, I can always SSH into her laptop and take care of business.

Anyway, circumstance has me leaving for the UK tomorrow morning for a job training session that could last as long as four weeks. I'll of course want to be in touch with my special little lady as much as possible, so aside from an IM program and emails, she decides to install Skype on her Ubuntu system so we can talk and see each other. I have it on my system already;  it's how I keep in touch with my family back in the States.

She managed to find, download and install the right Skype package for her system. Not too bad for a beginner. Unfortunately, that was the last thing that went right.

First thing that was off: Sound. The sound on her PC speakers has always been sub-par when using Linux - it's high pitched, staticky, and cuts in & out sometimes. For some reason, when she plugged her headphones in, not only would the laptop speakers not turn off, but there was no sound in the headphones. Coming from three versions of Ubuntu running on the same Toshiba laptop, I've dealt with my fair share of sound problems. After several hours of fiddling, I can get her laptop to recognize when the headphones are plugged in and kill the PC speakers, but no amount of trying or doing will get the sound to come through the headphones, and the PC speakers are still horrible sounding. The microphone works now though, and you can make calls with a mic and some PC speakers. However horrible it will sound, it's still the love of my life on the other end and that makes it all seem not-so-bad.

Second thing: Webcam. Turns out the webcam that comes built-in to Acer (and other laptops) has poor support in Ubuntu, and Linux in general. Support for the Acer Orbitech camera (a Logitech device) has been lacking for close to two years now. It's not some mythical beast like a phoenix or unicorn - it's a standard Logitech USB webcam that just happens to be reside in some molded plastic on top of the laptop display. Another hours worth of googling; reading forum posts and instruction pages; downloading, compiling, and installing drivers to give me a camera that works for about five and a half seconds before it cuts out and dies. Another hour worth of tinkering, combing through bug reports, more tinkering, and trying everything I can think of or find to make this camera work yields nothing. I get 5.5 seconds of video at the beginning of a Skype call, and that's it. Period.

Third thing: Skype itself. Compared to the other versions available for various other platforms, the Skype that installs in Ubuntu is stripped-down, bare-bones, and lacking in many features.

After spending a total of four hours messing with her computer and coming up with zilch, I rebooted both our computers into our respective Windows versions (Vista on mine, XP on hers). I downloaded and installed skype on both machines. I signed in to our respective accounts, and on both machines all hardware was instantly detected, recognized, set up with drivers, and in-use as fast as I could plug in my webcam and push the Call button. No tinkering, no settings, no drivers to download, no compiling, no headache. The skype version that runs on Windows is feature-rich, hassle-free, and you could probably train chimps to run the thing.

What that says about me, I don't know. It just sucks that I had to kill four or five hours wrestling with something I shoudln't have to the night before I leave for up to a month, so i figured I'd kill another ten minutes to write this rant and let everybody know about it.

That's all. Thanks for listening.

### Post by Keyper7 on 2009-01-11
That's an awful amount of bad luck... :(

But unfortunately it can happen. Sometimes there's simply no satisfactory workarounds. When I buy a new computer or try to install Ubuntu in another person's computer, I usually try to minimize the amount of frustration:

1 - Search the launchpad and forums for any horrible showstopper bug related to the hardware.
2 - Fiddle as much as possible with the LiveCD: in respect to hardware testing, there's very little you can't do with the LiveCD and an internet connection (or pen drive).
3 - If something didn't work with the LiveCD, might work in a full install but you're not sure, try Wubi.
4 - If Wubi is not enough, try a temporary small partition just for tests.

In your case, I believe Skype wouldn't already work in the LiveCD.

### Post by detroit/zero on 2009-01-12
Well, all is well for now. We have our temporary solution, and it'll get us by for the month or so I am gone.

I've been using Skype on Hardy (and Gutsy before it, and Feisty before that..) to keep in touch with my family back in the states. Aside from the sound and video quality not being what I'd like it to be (as compared to my other, less-free, less-open, less-awesome OS) it's never failed me.

I'm not doing the standard rant about how linux sucks and will never kill windows, so I'm just switching back right now and anyone else who doesn't is an idiot for not doing it. Far from it.

I'll blame her computer just as much as anything. It's two years old. It's an Acer. It has a lot of its own problems that have nothing to do with any of this..

It was just a little disappointing to have all those three things fail to work all at once and cost me several hours of valuable time. The icing on the cake was doing it all in Vista/XP and having it all work the first time with zero configuration and zero hassle. Never having used Skype on any MS OS version, even I was surprised at how easy it was.

I'll stick with Ubuntu for everything I do, no two ways about it. But when the time comes to run Skype and make a call, I guess I'll have to turn to the dark side for the time being.
