### Post by thasatelliteguy on 2012-12-16
Sorry LinComm, (Linux Community)

Once again I have tried, and once again I have failed. Well not so much failed, as decided it's not worth my time. Linux just isn't there, and after analyzing it this time, I don't think it ever will be. I have never been able to put my finger on what makes Linux not viable for public consumption before, but I understand now, and I'm out. Because it's never going to get better. 

People say it's hardware support. They are wrong - mostly. I did find most of the machines came up and ran. I tried 4. One was 64bit, and I had the most problems with it and USB WiFi. I put in a PCIe and it worked at bootup. Zero effort. I am also aware that MAC is of common ancestry, and therefor I would think that once MAC supports it, it wouldn't take too much effort to report something to Linux. I'm assuming there, I could be wrong. I can say for sure that one of the two reasons Linux will die is the command prompt. When you google for a program to perform a task, and the installation instructions are 5 and six screens long of what appears to be the lost text missing from the Mayan's doomsday tablet, even someone who started at 7yrs old, in Dos 2.1, on 5 1/4 floppies, says WTF? YOU DO NOT NEED THE COMMAND LINE. YOU DO NOT NEED TERMINAL. There's a REASON Billyboy got rid of the command prompt back in Win98. Now it's there as a tool, but you can accomplish anything you need without it. Why would you use the command line to copy files, and rename, make directories and such? Just so you look smart to the other people you work with?? That's the only reason I can think of, because, otherwise, it's TOTALLY UNNECESSARY. And why on god's earth would you have to learn and use command line interface to download and install programs IN THE GUI?? I could understand using it to configure the machine and install the GUI, (like Win3.1 or Win95) But why do you think you need it after that? You don't. I point to MAC as the example. It's very similar, and has no use for command line. 

But the primary reason Linux will never be main stream:
It's not written in English, or any other spoken/written language either for that matter. It's written in a dialect of Ancient Geek that no one has heard spoken aloud in 30 yrs. You people that develop for Linux, need to stop making up ridiculous names and acronyms for simple things. It's hard enough to learn a new OS, without also having to learn to translate everything in it into English first. 

GIMP = Graphic Image Manipulation Program? Maybe? I think...??
Why not something simple someone could intuitively figure out, like, oh I dont know, PAINT or PHOTOSHOP??

GNU = Still have no clue. I know it has to do with open source licensing.

Konquerer = Tells me squat  Internet Explorer = Tells me everything I need to know.

Just found a program called Tor. I now know what it does, but even after reading the installation page on the web, I STILL can't figure out what the hell 'Tor' stands for! I think the r is relay, based on what it does, but that's as far as I get. 

You can look at ANY aspect of Linux, and you will find it TOTALLY counter-intuitive to try and learn what things do. And then to top it off, when you get online and ask for help, you are given guides that don't apply anymore. Or don't apply to YOUR DISTRO, or don't apply to whatever desktop environment you happened to have loaded. Half the instructions are wrong and refer to menus that no longer exist.

In the end, for you guys that have used it since it was Linux vs DOS, it makes perfect sense, I guess. But to the rest of us, you know, the ones out here in the REAL WORLD, with sunshine and grass and sht, Linux is a total clusterfk. I have learned programming languages, like Basic, Vbasic, C, C++, and most recently Simpl Windows by Crestron. Which, by the way, I SWEAR Crestron made things more difficult to understand and learn so that they could keep the weekend warrior from learning it so the programmers can remain more elite. I could immerse myself in Linux for several weeks and learn it too. But that's my point. It should not take as much effort to learn to use my computer, as it takes me to learn to write the software that runs on it. It should just WORK. And for what doesn't, it should be obvious why the hell not. It shouldn't take 3 days to figure out why I cant load a wireless driver... on three different devices that everyone swears work OOB. (Oh ya, theres another one.... OOB) See what I mean... GUI, OS, OOB, I'm getting the hell outta here before I catch this crap too!
