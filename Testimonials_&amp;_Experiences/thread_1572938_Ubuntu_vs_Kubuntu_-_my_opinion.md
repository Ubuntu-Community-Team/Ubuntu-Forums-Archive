### Post by perspectoff on 2010-09-23
I'm exactly the opposite to those who like the Gnome desktop better. I like the KDE desktop better.

I must say that the Kubuntu LiveCD is slower than the Ubuntu LiveCD for some reason, but once they are installed on my hard drive the Kubuntu runs faster.

Don't ask me why.

After all this talk and seeing that Ubuntu is still the most popular distro, this week I again did a full runthrough of both OS (using Lucid) side-by-side.

I think doing a side-by-side comparison of both desktops in one installation is the wrong way to do the comparison. They really don't work that well side-by-side (despite claims to the contrary). Each works better when installed cleanly in its own partition.

I can't say I liked Ubuntu better. In fact, I still like Kubuntu much better.

Now, I see the effort to make the Ubuntu Gnome desktop resemble the Mac OS X desktop. 

The KDE desktop resembles the Windows desktop more.

I have been used to the Windows desktop over the years, so that probably influences my decision. I have never really been that big of a fan of the Mac desktop, since I am mostly a business user and not a "Gee- whiz!, look how pretty!" type of user.

Yes, I do use a bunch of Gnome apps in Kubuntu, and they work fine. But for apps which exist for both KDE and Gnome, I always prefer the KDE apps. I like Amarok over Rhythmbox, for example, and K3b over Brasero. I like k9copy. I like Konversation (for IRC) and Kopete (for IM). I prefer Kaffeine to Totem (although I use VLC and MPlayer in both). I like KRDC for remote connections, but Gnome has a nice remote connection center as well (even though I find it somewhat thick to configure). Almost all apps like these can be used in either desktop, though, so it really isn't a big deciding factor.

Further, for Groupware, KDE has far, far superior apps to Gnome. Kolab groupware, for example, works directly with the KDE apps (Kontact, KMail, etc.)

And yes, KOffice has kept pace. I am nervous about the fate of OpenOffice now that Oracle has bought it along with the rest of Sun, and KOffice is a good replacement for OpenOffice (if necessary). It is a good first choice for routine usage as well (although I must say I like AbiWord as a leaner word processor). 

The KDE browser Konqueror is a good alternative to Firefox. It allows most of the same add-ons as Firefox and has integrated the most important security (script control and ad-blocking). Firefox has done a lot of weird things in the past few versions, is getting slow and bloated, and has a lot of integrated internal scripting that I really don't like. I am getting closer to moving over to Konqueror if Firefox keeps going this way.

(Yeah, yeah, please don't suggest Chromium to me as a browser. It's not integrated into any desktop, as Konqueror is, and to my knowledge doesn't use all the add-ons from Firefox/Gecko that are actually important to me).

Now, I do customize my desktop to make it appealing to me, as I imagine everyone does. I have found my ability to customize in KDE is far better than than in Gnome. I can get a cleaner, prettier desktop in KDE (I hate to be distracted by extraneous nonsense). I have one menu for everything, instead of 3 or 4, as in Gnome.

There are lots and lots of widgets that are integrated into KDE as an integral part of the desktop, not add-ons as in Gnome.

As to fonts, that is a silly objection. Fonts are customizable in both desktops. (Heck, you can use Microsoft core fonts, if you want.) You can customize themes, the toolbar, whatever. That is true of both desktops. A judgment based on what comes pre-installed is pretty superficial. The question is how easy it is to customize, and I'd say for customizing superficial elements, the desktops are pretty equivalent. The big difference is the menuing system, which I prefer in KDE (well, the "Classic" menu system, anyway). It is just easier to find programs and start them in KDE.

I also happen to like the Dolphin file manager in KDE quite a bit. I prefer it to Gnome's Nautilus (and even prefer it to using KDE's Konqueror as a file manager). This is probably just my personal preference, though, and not a major deciding factor.

Lastly, KDE underwent a lot of pain for the first few versions after the transition to KDE 4. Gnome is about to make a similar transition to a new structure as well, and I expect there will be a similar amount of pain for a few upcoming versions of Gnome. 

Sound problems with Pulse Audio occurred several versions ago in both Ubuntu and Kubuntu, but I have not had the problems of the previous user in recent versions. In fact, the current version of PulseAudio is the only one that allows me to stream to my Airport Express and to use HDMI TV output (which I do from Kubuntu).

Although I have not had problems with KPackageKit in Karmic or Lucid, this is the one place I have to concede Ubuntu has a better app. There is nothing better than the Synaptic Package Manager. For that reason, I have always installed Synaptic Package Manager alongside KPackageKit in Kubuntu. Big deal. It takes all of 30 seconds to install and it works fine in Kubuntu. That isn't enough to make me want to switch to Gnome.

Startup for me is slower in Lucid than Karmic for both Ubuntu and Kubuntu, but that is the fault of the Plymouth splashscreen and graphics, which is a problem shared by both desktops.

So, after my bi-yearly side-by-side comparison of Ubuntu and Kubuntu, I am sticking with Kubuntu.

### Post by 3Miro on 2010-09-23
Gnome vs KDE is different from Ubuntu vs Kubuntu. Ubuntu is very Gnome centric and as such, all non Gnome variants are having big or small issues of some sort. I have used Kubuntu 8.10, 9.10 and 10.04. I liked KDE, but something was lacking. I recently installed Gentoo with KDE 4.4 and that was a huge improvement (granted, it took me 2 weeks to install it, but it was a big "wow" at the end).

In the past couple of months I have been experimenting with different distros and DE. Variety is the strength of Linux, however, it also raises one's expectations to an unreasonable level. Here is my view on all four major DE.

KDE: after the bumpy start of 4.0 and 4.1, KDE is converging to something very nice. My issue with it is that it is big and it feels big. KDE has a somewhat high set of system requirements. It works on my laptop, but it has a tow on my productivity. I also don't like the somewhat monochrome color scheme. More KDE art is coming along to fix that hopefully, but right now GTK based stuff can be so much more colorful.

Gnome: It has been a major DE for a long time and it is currently the most robust DE. I wish they had a few extra features in Metacity, but it works well with Openbox too. However, I don't like the direction of Gnome-shell. I have tried it on couple of occasions and while I don't want to judge it before it has been released, it looks like a very dysfunctional piece of software. (I know that many people like Gnome-shell and it is possible that I just have not been able to figure it out yet, so can someone post a youtube video that shows how to use this thing in a functional way; and I don't mean "look shiny windows" or "look cool zoom", I mean have a number of terminals, browsers, text editors and/or IDEs and then quickly switch between them, copy paste text, start and close new ones, and so on)

XFCE: If it only had a few extra features ... Right now I think it is the best DE. The problem is that most of the GTK apps assume Gnome and that leaves some issues with integration. Going XFCE you often end up running Gnome with different panel and menu.

LXDE: Too new and it doesn't have all the features of the other DE yet. It is headed in a good direction, but if you want something more complete, you should look into the other ones first.
