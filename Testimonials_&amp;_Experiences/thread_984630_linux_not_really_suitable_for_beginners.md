### Post by jflaker on 2008-11-16
From a one time Linux Newbie:

**_"The install process is damn complex"_**
It can be, if you are trying to dual boot where you need to resize partitions and keep your other OS intact.  I will agree that the documentation, while not secret, is not really saying enough in the installer process to guide someone who is just a user and doesn't know the terminology......many people have installed over their OS because they didn't understand enough

**_"The OS just works, but it's not flawless"_**
Well, Ubuntu is a pulling together of many pieces of Open Source applications and applets (the login screen as an example, is an applet).  While the linux kernel (the core of the operating system) is very stable, some of the programs and applications may not be.  It is open source and you have the opportunity to report any problems so they get fixed, unlike windows which doesn't really give you the opportunity for anything other than an error report.

**_"It's impossibile to have my wireless lan card working" _**
Yeah, HARDWARE, IN GENERAL is a thorn in the side of Open Source.  Not many manufacturers are willing or able to assist the open source community in developing drivers that actually work with their specific hardware.  Again, there are some user friendliness pieces that can be improved and you can report such user interface problems if you wish to help the developers.

**_"overall complexity: everytime u have to write un-comprehensible code lines in an old-fashioned terminal window"_**
Almost everything CAN be done from a terminal command, but not EVERYTHING MUST BE DONE from the terminal.  If you are talking about installing software, there the package manager which allows you to install as well as off of the applications menu.  I think this is misinformation where you probably weren't aware of ways of doing things.  Also, if you asked a question, you were probably asked to enter a command, which, from the standpoint of quickness and ease of instruction, is what most of us will tell others to do.  It is easier than explaining the navigation of the menus and such

**_"I think that linux - ubuntu is not a mature system for end users since it requires u to know a lot about how it works and programming"_**
Again, I think you may be misinformed or you came to linux with the preconceived perception that this knowledge was required....My 4, 14 and 16 year olds are using Ubuntu Linux without any issues.......I don't quite understand your ending statement.

In reality, you are not being realistic about Linux for the business...It may not be for every business.

The reality is that a few users, (those who will not be a business impact should Linux not work out) would be chosen for initial testing.  Then if all goes well, your IT department will install and configure all the systems to company standards and give them to the users (this is commonly called a rollout).  The users will be limited in what they could actually do at their desktop, just like in windows, so that they don't damage the system settings/setup...

Linux has worked for many large companies, such as Home Depot and has caused a significant reduction in the IT budget.......When properly rolled out, Linux can be as transparent as Windows.

### Post by blackened on 2008-11-16
I'm not taking issue with the fact that linux has some serious headway to make as far as hardware support goes, but I feel inclined to point out why it is you're forced into feeling the way you do.

You need to realize that Windows is not much easier to install and configure than Linux is. The difference is that, since most machines come with Windows pre-installed, you weren't the one that had to work out the kinks.

If you were the one to install Windows, then you would quickly come to realize that it is the exact same: for unsupported hardware you will be required to go to the manufacturers website and download drivers for the problem hardware. If this hardware is your wireless or ethernet adapter, then you are in the same boat as you are with Linux.

The one saving grace in a business environment is that machines are usually rolled-out in groups that have identical hardware, so once you find a fix for a particular problem, then you can institute that fix en masse. This process is almost never, whether on Linux or Windows, dealt with by the end-user as most businesses employ some form of IT personnel. 

Most people are only comfortable with Windows because they weren't the ones to do the initial installation. This is not a personal shortcoming for the end-user who often doesn't care about the things you mentioned. For people of that ilk Windows and Mac are better (mainly because of market-share leverage on hardware manufacturers), but are certainly not ideal.

Probably the majority of computer uses share the OP's attitude that they just don't care about how the computer works, or any of the technical details. They just want it to work. Its an attitude inevitably fostered by a consumerist society.

There is no way that ANY OS (at least one designed to run on heterogenous hardware) can hope to meet the needs of such people if they have to install it first. There are too many drivers, hardware quirks, etc that have to be worked around. Some people will always have issues. In fact virtually every install I've ever done (be it of linux or windows) has had irritating things that have taken days if not weeks to iron out.

Anybody who wants to treats computers as consumer devices simply shouldn't try installing the OS. Have someone who knows what they are doing sort that out...and if necessary pay them to do it. Unfortunately Ubuntu's high profile, user friendly image and Window's current difficulties mean we are attracting a lot of people who want to try an alternative, but don't really have the knowledge to handle installing an OS.

Once the OS is properly configured, Ubuntu needs barely any more knowledge than windows to use, there are differences that take time to learn, but that's all they are, differences.

In the main the CLI is not necessary, there are a few places where it is unavoidable, and a lot of places where it is the fastest and most efficient tool to solve a problem, but it is rarely the only way it can be done.

If you want a proper test, get someone with experience to install Ubuntu, leave your windows mind set at the door, and play with the box for at least 1 or 2 weeks. Then make up your mind.

Hope that helps
