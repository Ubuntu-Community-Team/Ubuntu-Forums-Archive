Nothing to be apologetic about. You have made a considerable investment in Windows applications to fulfill your functional computing requirements, therefore you are committed to keeping a working MS Windows environment. You are, in a sense, trapped.

Many of us are in a very similar situation. I use GNU/Linux a la Ubuntu for all purposes except Flight Simulation where I have been an MS Flight Simulator enthusiast since 1986. The situation that we flight simmers are in is that MS have abandoned MSFS. The last two versions are broken in Windows 7; because they were both developed in the W2K and XP environments and MS have made changes in the underlying operating system which wreck their functionality. So I have to keep an XP box on the road for evermore, or stop flight simming. This is already proving challenging - I am having real fun maintaining device driver configuration to satisfy the needs of changing hard wares with unchanging application requirements.

For the long term your best strategy may well be to migrate your functional needs, as you can and in your own time, towards GNU/Linux native applications - will not cost you any more than your time and in the long term will give you a more stable and robust home computing requirement.

Hope this helps

### Post by rg4w on 2011-03-09
> **eduard879 said:**
> I don't see the possibility of ever switching over  completely to Linux because I have SO much Windows software that I paid a  lot for and Linux won't run.
I wholeheartedly agree. IMO your observation was very politely worded and not all the controversial:  there's simply a lot more software available for Windows, and even Mac OS, than there is for Linux.

A lack of applications is often cited as a critical factor for not adopting Linux, and it's a tricky catch-22:  more apps are sorely needed, but many devs can't afford to port to Linux until the audience is larger.

Ubuntu's focus on usability is bringing Linux to new audiences, but it'll be a while before it grows enough to viably support specialized software like Electric Quilt.  My hope is that the Ubuntu team will also give as much priority to ease-of-development as they do for ease-of-use, so we can see the cost of supporting Linux ports drop to more affordable levels.

In the meantime, I took a lot at the web site for EQ and it looks like an excellent package.  Unfortunately I don't know anyone in the community of devs I work with who's making anything like it, and the product seems sufficiently feature-rich that it may be cost-prohibitive to try to replicate it in a new Linux app.

Your best bet with apps like that may be WINE.  I don't know if EQ works well with WINE, and it may be necessary to try it out to see for yourself.

If not, you may have to keep using Windows for a while, but FWIW I find it's sometimes helpful to ask a software publisher if they would consider porting to Linux.  That's how I got started with Ubuntu, I had  few customers who asked for a Linux version of my products.  In most cases a single-platform app is an unlikely candidate for such a port, but with devs using cross-platform frameworks sometimes a Linux port is easy enough that all they need is a little encouragement to consider it.

There are many, many more *published* applications available for GNU/Linux than Windows+Mac put together. The trick, of course, is sourcing the particular GNU/linux app you need to meet your specific functional requirement. The Ubuntu user gets a pretty good deal from the Ubuntu Software in being able to capture and install a huge array of apps, each of which has been tested and assured by Canonical and most of which are automatically maintained and updated via Update Manager. 

Windows and Mac purchasers get no such deal  from their OS vendors. Microsoft, particularly, has built up a very nasty reputation for obsoleting their own native applications by changes to the later versions of their OS. Hence Flight Sim is broken in Windows 7.
