### Post by bigbearomaha on 2006-09-11
Hello,

I was trained as a network technician on Novell back in 1996 or so.

I have installed Novell, MS from DOS al the way through XP. I have installed Mandrake/iva, Red Hat, Suse and now Ubuntu.

My favorite has been Mandriva... Until now.

Ubuntu shows great attention to detail and user expectation. They have come very close to so called "perfection" in Linux. as it stands.
There are obviously some areas in which Linux needs to develop and mature yet which is quite understandable given it's dramatic rapid development in such a short time.

Much of the driver disparity is caused by tensions due to legal woes involving SCO, IBM and Novell. I believe that when IBM wins the day, it will signal an all-clear bell to hardware manufacturers and software makers that they can safely port to Linux without fear.

The biggest detriment Linux currently has is the abundance of some, certainly not all, users of Linux who behave in a boorish, arrogant manner which tends to turn away the so called "average user" because no one likes to be treated in such a poor, snobbish manner.Unfortunately, they will project and assume this is how most people who use Linux will behave and give up on a bunch of "nerds"

I do my best as well as the so many others I see here in the forums to prevent that image from propogating. Enthusiastically and with great interest in their desire to experiment I help them all I can, which is woefully little as I am still learning myself.

I have encountered problems in every OS I have installed at some point.  There is no such thing as a "bug free" OS.

In a way, my beginnings with Novell which were based on command line, and of course good old DOS,have helped me. I am not bothered by the command line in Linux, just a matter of learning which commands do what really.

I keep a dual boot system with XP due to the few apps that do not have Linux ports and do not work in wine.  I must admit to not liking the use of an emulator to run an app.  

I firmly believe that the market will open up an many "professional" apps will become common on linux , likely at a price but that is the great thing about a free and open market.  If the people are willing to pay for something that demonstrates excellence over that which lacks , then it will thrive or die on it's own.

Open and free communication is really what drives me to Linux.  It actively demonstrates that when all people have equal and free access to information and the tools to get and use that information,  people can change their lives.



Long Live Ubuntu.
Big Bear
