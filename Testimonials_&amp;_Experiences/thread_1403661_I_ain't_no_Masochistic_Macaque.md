### Post by Useless Urchin on 2010-02-10
Oh, I didn't know there was a section where to say goodbye, and much less that there was something called "Criticism FAQ"... an interesting "Criticism FAQ".

I also read the other sticky here, where it says, "It is well known that computers can be frustrating and learning anything new on them can make them even more so."

I wish, though, that people who had the good will to waste precious time and try the thing, such as myself, would not be patronized. Computers are exciting and beautiful and there's nothing inherently *frustrating *about them. In fact, it's substandard operating systems, rather than "learning something new", that will make computers "even more so" frustrating. 

In short, don't call me Witless Wallaby just because Ubuntu can't support hardware properly, can't play sound through my headphones, has no half-decent image editor, or because in some Ubuntu bug threads you watch people solving their own problems while those supposed to help them post nonsense. 

Do not confuse working hard to fix mistakes on a half-baked OS with that cheery feeling of "learning something new". 

Frankly, I'd rather be cleaning Fedora's garbage, pardon, "learning something new" :) If that's what it takes nowadays to be a Linux desktop user. At least there's no mushy "philosophy" attached to it.

It seems to me... where I could be terribly wrong, but it seems to me that Ubuntu is, when it's all said and done, actually giving Linux a bad name. It's advertised as the "best and easiest", and it's neither, but many people will actually believe it, try Ubuntu and be put off Linux forever. 

Hype can only get anything so far. 

And yet, I hope things get much better. I don't think it's inconceivable, although I do think it's unlikely. Nevertheless I'll be very interested to try the new version when it comes out and very happy to come back and eat humble pie if it proves me wrong. 

Live long and prosper, and 

Have a good day.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2010-02-11
> **Useless Urchin said:**
> In short, don't call me Witless Wallaby just because Ubuntu can't... play sound through my headphones

A lot of people have not yet made themselves familiar with the changes introduced by Pulseaudio in semi-recent versions of Ubuntu.

First, plug in your headphones. Now go to the volume control and right-click it. Go to Sound Preferences and hit the Output tab. Switch the output to your headphones.

If you just want to switch one program to headphones, then that's easy - run the program, make it play some sound, and then use the Applications tab of Sound Preferences to switch the input or output to your headset.

Too many people expect it to work the exact same way that it used to back in 2007, find that it doesn't, assume that Pulseaudio is "broken" and then attempt to remove it.

> has no half-decent image editor

I'm afraid that's nonsense. The Gimp is an excellent image editor, very powerful. A lot of people complain about The Gimp because it uses a multi-window interface rather than the single-window interface that people are familiar with from their pirated copies of Adobe Photoshop ;-)

> or because in some Ubuntu bug threads you watch people solving their own problems while those supposed to help them post nonsense.

Well that's often true. Some people don't read the original post properly, or try to reply on topics that they know almost nothing about. It's regrettable, but you can't really do much about it - those people might run into and overcome a problem that you'll ask about some day, and you can't discourage people from trying to help.

If the advice is really terrible, (such as using 'sudo chmod' on large amounts of the filesystem so the user account can modify root-owned directories) I'll often PM the person who gave the advice and gently let them know that their solution isn't appropriate, and why.

Before erasing your hard drive for a different distribution, I'd appreciate it if you could give "Lucid Lynx" (Ubuntu 10.04) a try. It hasn't yet reached the third alpha, but it may solve some of your issues with 9.10. And the Ubuntu developers could use some up-to-date bug reports. Install the Alpha 2 image and then run all system updates.
