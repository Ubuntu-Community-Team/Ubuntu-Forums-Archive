### Post by 1roxtar on 2009-06-20
I am 38 years old and have been using Windows since 3.1 and through Vista (Ugh!).  I have only been actively looking into Linux for just about six months now.  I read about the different distro's and tried out several LiveCDs.  When I came to Ubuntu, I was instantly hooked. 

 My intro to Ubuntu was 8.10.  Through the awesome community, I have experimented with and learned about dual-boots with partitions and with Wubi.  I quickly and easily learned about the repositories, using the Synaptic Package Manager and even using the terminal has been so damn fun.  I now have 9.04 Jaunty installed on my laptop and my netbook.  I am a complete Linux and most specifically Ubuntu convert.  

I have completely done away with my dual boots, removed Windows and fully installed Ubuntu on my machines.  I am getting excited about 9.10 coming soon.  I can care less about Windows 7.  With Ubuntu, I love it's security...I don't care about having to use my password to change things.  It's better than seeing my antivirus program flashing on my Windows screen every few minutes about trojans, adware or spyware.

I love that I can tweak everything on Ubuntu to suit my tastes.  I can now honestly say that my machine is an extension of who I am as an individual.  Linux truly creates a personalised and creative environment for users.  I am happy with the open-source alternatives to my Windows programs.  It's funny how some people always boast about Microsoft's billions of dollars and vast array of programmers, but I have seem a lot more talent in the Linux and FOSS communities.

I'm sick of reading how Linux/Ubuntu is too hard to learn and how it's not ready for the mainstream.  It's all a load of crap, in my opinion.  I have only known about Linux and Ubuntu for about six months and I can say that I feel confident about using it on my machines and recommending it to my friends.  Even without official customer support, the community is so amazing that I learned everything I had to learn from our forums.  Thank you guys!!!

Honestly, IT JUST WORKS!!!!  Yes I had to add a few things and tweak a couple of things, but this was mainly for personalisation.  The time I spent just made me love the system so much more.  Ironically, with Windows I spent more time REMOVING the bloatware, just to make my machine work decently.  Tweaking Windows was like, Ugh!  Tweaking Ubuntu is like WOW!  I can do this?????

So it's official, Goodbye Windows!!!  ):P

> iRun
Well, let's be fair.  You guys have a lot of time on your hands in those "retirement communities". 

That's just a cheap shot LOL. :roll:

I don't live in a retirement community. I must admit the winters here can turn the area (around us) into what appears to be a retirement community. :lol:

None of the individuals I mentioned are geniuses. What they all have in common is me and I'm certainly not a genius. I informed them prior to trying Ubuntu that it's the same - but different. They understand that by interacting with the computer, they can figure it out and of course I lock down and backup everything that is essential. It's been my experience in a fairly brief period of time one can learn how to _**USE**_ Ubuntu. I'm not saying they are proficient at the intricacies, only that they can use the OS as they would Windows.

Perhaps that's the key. Teach them how to use it, then experience will assist them in learning the details. We all know that Windows comes pre-installed on the majority of computers sold. Thus, all people have to do is learn how to use it. Most Windows users (I know) have no idea how to install it, but they certainly know how to use it. Most I know, learned by tutorial or repetition, they repeat the procedure until it accomplishes what they intended and then apply that to other apps. They have no idea why or how it works, only that it works for them. Converting them to a different OS requires they relearn basic repetitive processes and most I know had enough trouble learning them in the first place. With all the help programs available for Windows and associated apps, one must consider:
If Ubuntu/Linux is to succeed in competing (assuming that's the goal) then it must find a way to attract new users by providing them with training and help support (from the time they push the ON button) that is aimed at **"USERS"** - not installers or computer savvy people.

This type of support must focus on people who have no desire to learn how or why their computer works, only need to know the basics of how to use the OS and associated initally installed apps. This creates another issue - how to get Ubuntu installed in the first place. The only solution to that problem IMO is thru sales. Canonical must continue convincing computer vendors the value of installing Ubuntu on their PC's. In a MS$$ dominated environment that alone is one heck of a challenge. Then they need to enhance the OS by providing an intuitive educational start program designed specifically to walk new users thru the basics. Again another monumental task. I think Ubuntu is a long away from being considered as the OS of the future and I believe many of us know that. A project to write and implement a start program for the education of new users is in my opinion a necessity. Without any help - most everyone can follow the bouncing ball, fewer can figure out how to control the bouncing ball and even fewer can figure out why it bounces.

Just my $0.02
