### Post by jon_herr on 2007-12-17
I'm a gutsy fan...  have eagerly updated to whatever the latest release was ever since Dapper.  

Dapper, Feisty, Gutsy...  all good for me.

For What It Is Worth:
I run Ubuntu Gutsy on 7 machines
* main home machine (multiboot windows xp, windows vista, ubuntu-gutsy)
* laptop (multiboot windows media center, ubuntu-gutsy)
* work machine (multiboot windows 2k, ubuntu-gutsy)
* home file server (gutsy - amd 64, raid 1)
* my car (amd geode mobile, plays music and movies)
* my mom's pc - yes, my mom runs linux (ubuntu - gutsy)
* my living room media pc - ubuntu-gutsy

What I like about gutsy?  
* Wireless works out of the box - WPA encryption - on my laptop and one of the mobile machines.  Works in car using ndiswrapper
* Nvidia proprietary drivers work out of the box
* Security, easy of use, rivaling even windows for installable applications
* CD / DVD burning etc..  using K3b
* Virtual machines using VMWare Player and Virtual Box
* I spend most of my time in Ubuntu now, not windows.  Windows XP is my 'main' OS where I check and keep my POP email...  but that might change soon - ubuntu is just too good - if that is possible!

* I am not a kubuntu fan so much though, many things that I can easily work with in Ubuntu are 'broken' or more difficult in Kubuntu - requiring more manual configuration.  If kubuntu has given you trouble - try Ubuntu.

Great work, Ubuntu Developers!

### Post by ret on 2007-12-18
I'm going to back you up here instead of starting a whole new thread. I, too, am a Gutsy fan. I'm brand spankin' new to a *nix environment. I was, until recently, an old Windows/DOS guy with telnet access to the occasional unix shell so I was decently familiar with the structure of *nix and commands. As far as switching directories, running programs, and su'ing in I, at most, almost understood a little. About a week ago I ran into huge problems with WinXP, product keys, virii, etc and decided that was it. It was time to install and learn a linux OS. Here is my short story of the install process:

  I did a little research and talking. A friend of mine told me I should try Ubuntu so I downloaded the LiveCD iso, burned it, and gave it a shot on my laptop. What do you know, when I loaded it my wireless features weren't enabled. Over the next few days I'd go on to learn that this is a common problem with a hundred different fixes but at the time I didn't know any better. I guessed Ubuntu just wasn't for me so I went back into XP. That lasted about an hour. Hey, what can I say? I'm just one of those guys that knows what he wants and I refused to accept failure.

I found some video tutorials on youtube about setting up your wireless stuff in another distro that I will keep to myself as to not turn this into a which distro is better thread. We'll call it "distro B" for reference. I downloaded the distro B iso, which was HUGE. I laid on my stomach on a hardwood floor, hard wired into the router, for quite some time during the process. It was okay, though, at least I had a video coach taking me through the process. After installation was over it was time to download their close to a thousand update packages. Wow. That took quite some time but at least I knew my box was up to date. Now on to the black hole of enabling the integrated wireless...

Luckily this guy had a video for this, too, and I followed it step-by-step from the initial "lspci" to installing ndiswrapper to getting the correct SYS/INF drivers. His worked, mine did not. I must have tried at least 10 configurations of blacklist, a modprobe alias, drivers (bcmwl5, bcmwl5a, etc). I got tired and set it back to how I had it originally, rebooted, and while it was rebooting I headed downstairs to grab my Windows XP CD to install. I was only going to do this so I could find a set of drivers that I would know, for a fact, worked to enable my BCMW chipset. Afterwards I would remove Windows XP. I was sure that loading XP after distro B, XP would screw something up but it was a risk I was willing to take. As I headed upstairs and laid back down on my already sore ribs I saw the most glorious blue light in my life coming from the corner of my laptop. The process of getting it working had taken me roughly 8 hours. I'll be damned if somehow the wireless stuff started working after that last reboot! Oh glory be, it was wonderful! Over the next day or so I followed those tutorials to the letter and actually accidently started downloading the 750 programs this guy uses personally! 3 gigs to go. Oh well, at least I'd never have to install anything, haha! After all this was done it was time to move on to the desktop effects. Didn't work (integrated ATI Radeon 200m btw). Now, after the 800-some packages and 752 programs, whaddya know, my laptop was running pathetically slow. I stumbled into the original friend that suggested Ubuntu. He told me, again, wipe and clean unstall Gutsy. "Okay, dude, but I swear don't bail on me. Help me set up my wireless crap after it's installed!" He agreed. Turns out this guy had the same Broadcom wireless chipset AND the ATI Radeon 200m. If anybody could help me through setting up wirelessly and compiz, it was him.

I stuck the CD in, rebooted, and clicked install. I was off and running, hard wired into the internet this time, The installation process was a breeze. Instead of 800-some updates I only had to worry about 142. Some restricted drivers thing popped up with 3 drivers in view: ATI Radeon, BCM43xx Wireless Firmware, and some dial-up modem stuff that I didn't bother to enable. The first two, however, I enabled. I rebooted and smoked a cigarette as I knew it would be a loooonngg night from my experience with distro B. When I came back I'll be DAMNED if there wasn't that blue light again! It actually worked during the install process!! I was BLOWN AWAY to say the least. Remember it took me roughly three days to get distro B up and running... Ubuntu did it on its own! My friend, who had NOT bailed on me (lol), found a quick HOWTO: on setting up compiz with ATI cards. The section for fresh install users was only 3 or 4 steps and worked like a CHARM. 600-ish FPS with glxgears but it would do. It was kind of an old laptop anyway.

I started messing with the laptop and over the next hour or so I fell so in love with it that I dragged my desktop upstairs, plugged into the router, and installed it here too. Sadly I'm still dual partitioned w/ win XP. I'm a typical "desktop producer" -- I write songs, play guitar, and sing into a couple windows programs to compile the tunes. As you can imagine audio synch is extremely important. This is the ONLY reason I still have XP. (If speed is a concern, why not run the program in the platform it was designed for in the first place, right?).

I have so far booted into windows ONCE, and that was just to make sure it was still there. I'd like to say thank you, if you've made it this far, for listening to my story. I'd like to also double my thanks to anybody who's ever posted any sort of help for anybody else. You may have just posted "just type lspci" but there's no telling who discovered your post and went on, or will go on, to learn about the distro and post their own help and HOWTO's etc. 

I apologize for the long story but I wanted to convey the weight behind my statements.

To say that I am happy with Ubuntu would be an amazing understatement.
