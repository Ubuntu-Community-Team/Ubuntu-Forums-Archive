### Post by MattDTownsend on 2007-07-08
I always try to steer clear of terms like "general public" and "average user," as they imply there's a large group of people out there whose concerns are all alike.  And I don't believe that to be the case.

Is Linux ready for wide distribution?

Yes.

Is the hardware you'd put it on ready?

No.

See, this is something that's come up here, often:  when something goes wrong in Linux, it's Linux's fault.  When something goes wrong in Windows, it's the hardware, or it's the virus, etc.  And even if you think you don't ascribe to this, you do.  It's ingrained.  You try Linux, end up having to edit some conf files (in my opinion, a great strength of Linux is that you CAN edit conf files), but you find it frustrating.  You still can't get your hardware to work.  WHY?!

Here's why:  the only manufacturer in the world that has decent hardware standards is Apple.  Everything else, and God only knows what chipsets are in use.  I have cursed hardware manufacturers many times for including bizarre chipsets, especially on motherboards.  I recently got a brand new ASUS, and I had to buy an Audigy because the onboard sound support is so dodgey.  Why?  Because ASUS chose RealTek as a chipset maker, and RealTek has a flavour-of-the-week for sound cards.

ALSA can't keep up.  And it shouldn't have to.  In my case, just look at RealTek. . .see how many chipset and codec revisions they've made to SIMPLE SOUNDCARDS.  Bu-but-but, they release Windows drivers!  Well, yeah, but I've heard some of the more unusual revisions don't work in Vista, either.  Even their own programmers can't keep up, it seems.

So, I appreciate being upset about configuring your laptop, but maybe you should take your grievances to Dell, too.  Because what you're asking here is. . .for people who practically have to reverse engineer all hardware support, to do so for every revision of every bizarre chipset ever made.  Very often with no code to work from.  And it just can't be done.

Given this, it's amazing that Linux, especially Ubuntu, works as well as it does.  And that, if you're lucky, editing configuration files can actually fix a compatibility issue at all.  Linux is not so much an alternative as it is a miracle.
