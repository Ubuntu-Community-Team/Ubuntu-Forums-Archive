### Post by jbysmith on 2009-02-25
Heh nice you stuck with it.  

Coming from a pure Windows background, the file system at first is a bit of a shock. I've been there, and I've had my fair share of "where the *hell* is this file at??"  But now that I understand the reasoning, it makes a *lot* of sense, and is just downright brilliant.  

Everything has it's place.  Config files have their directory, man files have a directory, libraries go in another directory, and so on.  Windows on the other hand.. well.. you're at the mercy of the installer which can literally throw stuff any where it damn well pleases, and good luck finding them again when it comes time to uninstall.. which never seem to really remove everything.

I absolutely love symbolic links; junction points in Windows are a weak/poor implementation at best, and frankly are borderline useless.  

And the whole drive letter thing.... that is soooooo 1970's.  I'm still waiting for some real innovation out of Microsoft instead of just polishing the shiny things on the desktop.

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2009-02-25
> WatchingThePain
The first few months were hell and I almost gave up and went back to windows.Odd, for me it was exactly the opposite. I found Ubuntu more intuitive and easier to comprehend. The simplicity was in effect - refreshing.

I started with Windows 3.1 and fortunately found Ubuntu Feisty Fawn after Vista. My migration experience with Ubuntu was in essence, problem free. With just a small amount of effort on my part (learning curve) I was rewarded with a fully functional and progressive free OS.

Having read many posts in this section complaining about Ubuntu's failure to function on their system, I've found two underlining issues that seem to apply (for many).
1. Failure to assess hardware and assure compatibility.
2. Failure to invest some time in the learning curve.

Both of these issues appear repetitively in this section of the forum. You however, chose the alternative route. Hopefully, your efforts resulted in a system that meets with your expectations.

Failure is often (not always) the result of either poor planning or lack of inspiration. I don't worry about failing, but rather what effect failure has on me. Does it inspire me to learn more or simply give up? I have often wonder how others relate to a failed task. If many of the posts I've read in this section are indicative of their reaction - it's not encouraging.

I agree that much of the Ubuntu experience is a "people thing". But it's also philosophy. Choice to follow the mandates of MS and it's profit motivated associations or choose to enjoy the freedoms of FOSS.

This forum not only enlightens (by providing assistance) but also educates by means of individual communications. Thus it plays an important role in the transitioning process from Windows. Success with Ubuntu IMHO can often be linked directly to accessing information, this forum and Google provide the platform for that access.

Good Luck & I Wish You Well
