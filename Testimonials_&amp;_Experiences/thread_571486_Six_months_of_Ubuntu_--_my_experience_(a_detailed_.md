### Post by ticopelp on 2007-10-09
When I joined these forums in April of this year, I was a semi-clueless Linux user, still dual-booting into Windows and only booting into Ubuntu to fool around. Six months later, I've weaned myself entirely off Microsoft products, and use Linux daily. I thought it might be time to make some small contribution by detailing my experience. 

Before Ubuntu came along, my experience with Linux was very limited -- I installed Slackware on an old PC, but it couldn't really do anything. It only had a command line, and I could play Sasteroids on it... that was really about it. I wouldn't touch anything resembling Linux again until I got my first Mac running OS X, and started playing around with the command line, setting up Apache, etc. Again, nothing too complicated, and nothing really that practical. 

My decision to explore Ubuntu came directly as a result of Vista. I was (and am) a pretty big fan of XP, as I think it's by far the best OS that Microsoft's come out with yet -- certainly better than 98, 2000, or God forbid, Me. 

But when my girlfriend got a new laptop with Vista and I had the unenviable task of trying to set it up, I knew I could never put up with it. It was clunky, invasive, counterintuitive, bloated, feature-heavy, and, well, pretty much junk. Not to mention MS seemed intent on forcing upgrades, and severely restricting freedoms once you were locked in to Vista. Macs were more appealing, but out of my price range, and I wasn't into the philosophy of paying tons of money for overpriced apps which were available for free elsewhere. Finally, several friends of mine were into Linux and recommended Ubuntu, so I figured, why not?

So I downloaded the Feisty .iso and played around with the live CD. I liked what I saw, so I bought a second hard drive and installed the OS. To be honest, I was surprised at how smoothly everything went. By way of context, I should say that my level of computer expertise is considerable but not exhaustive -- I've done some very light network administration and some PHP coding, but I'm not a programmer and wouldn't consider myself a "guru." And that's almost all on Windows machines. 

Here's a general breakdown of my experience:

**Installation and stability:** Excellent. I found the install very smooth and intuitive; to my surprise, internet worked out of the box, with no input required from me, and Feisty downloaded new packages from the repos automatically. I was impressed. Dual boot worked pretty much fine for me out of the box, and continued to work until I started tinkering around with stuff -- then it took me some time to get back where I was, but I was (usually) able to salvage things without reinstalling. Granted, I did have to reinstall a couple of times, but that's my fault -- not Ubuntu's.

Ubuntu is also surprisingly stable given the amount of beta software (like Compiz) and other things I slap on it when I get curious. The worst I could say is that GNOME sometimes seems a little slow when opening windows, but in truth, it's still faster than Windows ever was after six months. 

**Hardware Support:** Good. I installed Ubuntu on three machines on my house eventually; my custom-built desktop, my Dell laptop, and my PowerPC Powerbook. The desktop machine and the Dell worked flawlessly; the Dell's wireless didn't even require any tinkering. Everything pretty much worked from the start. 

The PowerPC wireless, on the other hand, took quite a bit of work to get running, and definitely was not ready out of the box. Also, the PowerPC's suspend and hibernate features were (and are) very buggy, with the laptop sometimes just powering down or refusing to come back from suspend. No one really seems to know how to fix this, and since PowerPC support seems to be on the way out, I've resigned myself to the fact that I will probably never have a satisfactory Ubuntu install on my Powerbook. That's too bad, but for the most part, the laptop is functional, and I can live with it. 

The only other disappointment with Ubuntu is my sound card, which won't record sound or accept input from a microphone. Queries on the board about this have mostly met with silence, so I'm just assuming my sound card is not supported. But, I still get sound *out* of it, so it's not a complete loss. Still, it would be nice to be able to record sound or use my mic again. 

**Learning Curve**: Good. Ubuntu is by far the easiest and most painless Linux distro I've ever used. As I said before, I've tinkered with Linux in the past, using Slackware, Caldera, and some other distros on my own. I used Red Hat at an old job of mine, and that was pretty unpleasant. I experimented a little with Knoppix and Mandrake, but none of them were a fraction as friendly as Ubuntu. 

I absolutely adore apt-get... I often see people complaining on these boards that it's "hard to get software" and that you should be able to visit Web sites and download packages. I feel completely the opposite. I hate having to hunt through someone's crappy blog or some ad-crowded Web site to try to find a software download... using apt-get is so much easier and faster. I don't even use Synaptic or the GUI tool that often, because apt-get is so much quicker for me. I know a lot of users complain about it, but I don't mind the command line at all. I think perhaps because I've been computing since the days of DOS and the Commodore Pet. 

**Community:** Excellent. This is one of the most knowledgeable and helpful online communities I've ever encountered. Whenever I had a problem, I could just post here and usually get a response within minutes, and almost always less than an hour. People do their best to help others around here, to the point of going over things step-by-step to solve problems -- it's one of the reasons I get so uptight when I see people come in and bad-mouth Ubuntu or flame the community, because I've seen how people go to a lot of effort, for no pay, to help others. 

To be honest, I doubt I could have learned a fraction as much without the excellent community that supports Ubuntu, not only here in the forums, but on aysiu's web site psychocats.net, which has been really invaluable for me. So, hats off to you, the Ubuntu community.

**Software:** Fair to Good. To be honest, there's still a lot of software that I really miss from Windows. I don't expect Linux to run Windows apps dependably, as I think that's unrealistic, but I do wish there were certain kinds of software that are available on other platforms: 

1) A Linux equivalent for DevonThink, which is a Mac program that organizes and indexes text files. I'm a writer by trade, so having everything in neat folders, easily searchable and labeled and so on, was really a godsend for me. There's nothing really like that for Linux that I've been able to find, although I've managed to compromise with Beagle and just keeping my files organized on my own. 

2) Video editing software. As best I can tell, Kino is completely useless. Cinelerra is promising, but unstable and not very intuitive. I'd love to have something like Premiere or even iMovie for Linux. I don't need anything all that advanced, but as far as I can tell, it's not "there" yet on Linux.

3) Reliable, intuitive DVD authoring. Tovid is a complete mystery to me, and I killed no small amount of time researching it and trying to get it to work. I don't mind the command line, but authoring DVDs from the CLI is not something I'm prepared to tackle. A GUI tool that works would be nice. 

4) Games. I miss my Windows games, and it's the only thing I consider going back to dual-boot for. I don't expect anything in this regard, I'm just saying I miss it. :)

5) A media player I can live with. I was locked into iTunes on Windows, and coming over to Linux, I found I really missed it a lot. I have an enormous music library, and so my music was mostly organized by ratings and divided into many playlists. This is something that is, apparently, nearly impossible to replicate on Linux.

Rhythmbox is stable and functional, but rating tracks is a complicated hardship. There should just be a shortcut or drop-down for this, and I don't understand why there isn't. Exaile shows promise, but has some really clunky, counterintuitive features that keep me from sticking with it. Amarok looks cool, but is unstable on my machine for some reason (kNotify keeps crashing). Songbird looks like what I want, but isn't ready, and it's slow. 

I'm still waiting, essentially, for the right program to come along, or for an updated version to give me what I need. And what I need isn't even that complicated -- an easy way to rate tracks and sort them with smart playlists! That can't be so hard, can it?

In all fairness, though, 90% of my software needs are addressed by Ubuntu, and for the unbeatable price of nothing. I have office software, a media player that's WAY more reliable and easy to use than Windows Media Player ever was, and lots of great, free little apps that do what I want and don't install spyware or ads or harrass me to pay money. I don't need antivirus software, and the OS doesn't treat me like I'm an idiot. Frankly, I love that, and doing a little research and learning new things is a small price to pay for that freedom. 

Overall, I'm extremely pleased with Ubuntu, and don't see myself going back to Windows, ever, if I have any choice about the matter at all. The most I might do is go dual-boot to play my games, but even that's kind of iffy. I don't know if I miss them that much. I've installed Ubuntu for friends (I sold my Dell and put Ubuntu on it) and I recommend it to anyone who's in the market for a new OS, or just expresses dissatisfaction with Vista. While I definitely think there's a learning curve involved, I don't think that's a bad thing. Learning Ubuntu has given me control back over my computer, and I like that.

Thanks for reading my long post!
