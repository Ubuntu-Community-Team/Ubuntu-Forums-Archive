### Post by Lunes en Marte on 2013-01-03
[FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]I am leaving Ubuntu for Mint. 
[/SIZE][/FONT]
[FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]I have read that many other Ubuntu users are saying farewell to Ubuntu after version 12.04 for diverse reasons. I will explain my ones here, in case this may help someone. [/SIZE][/FONT] 

 [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]First, what I do with and what I need from my computer : [/SIZE][/FONT] 
      [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]I am a scientist working in Astronomy and using a lot of Fortran, IDL and scripting programming, editing of documents and presentations with Emacs, LibreOffice, LaTeX, Xournal, as well as image handling and display with IDL  and Mathematica, and of course emails with Thunderbird, videoconfs via TeamViewer and Skype, plus some common applications for videos (VLC). I have a Dell Latitude E4310 at home and a Dell M4600 64bits at work, with >4GB and several cores, and almost identical set ups in both of them thanks to my free 5GB in Dropbox (the free version of U1 is, in my opinion, useless). [/SIZE][/FONT] 
 
 [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]Second, what I dont want: [/SIZE][/FONT] 
       [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]I am not an expertise in computers and Operating Systems nor I want to be. I am a long-time but simple user of them and I want a computer to be my friend and to help me in my daily work. I do not want regular changes in something which already works, and I do not want to spend my time in exploring new desktop experiences. The latest Ubuntu 12.04 is annoying in this sense and is not making my life easier. [/SIZE][/FONT] 
 
 [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]Third, what went wrong with Ubuntu : [/SIZE][/FONT] 
       [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]I discovered Ubuntu at 8.10 Intrepid; I had been using Red Hat Enterprise before that for about 3 years. So, I am not new in Linux but I do not considere myself an advanced user. I have been very comfortable with Ubuntu, its helpful and large user community and its ability to solve issues, and I was specially happy with version 10.04 LTS but since 12.04 I only have several small but annoying problems. The main one is its terribly low speed in my 64bits computers, even with simple things like opening Nautilus to explore a folder. The second problem is that I do not like Unity nor want to adjust to it. I moved to the Gnome Classic version, where I have been for about 6 months so far, but it does not work as fluid as Gnome 2 in 10.04 LTS, perhaps because it is actually a masked Gnome 3. I also tried Xubuntu with not much difference. So, 12.04 + Gnome3 + Unity seems to be going backwards and making ny newests hardware to look old and sluggish. In the last months I have been waiting for updates and improvements in 12.04 as well as for new Linux kernels, but so far these are not changing the situation. Finally I am reading about the latest Canonical moves toward agreements with large business companies like Amazon in order to introduce ads in upcoming Ubuntu versions. [SIZE=2]I understand their moves[/SIZE], b[SIZE=2]ut [/SIZE]saddly, I better leave Ubuntu now. [/SIZE][/FONT] 
 
 [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]Finally, why Mint : [/SIZE][/FONT] 
      [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]That is the q[SIZE=2]uestion! [/SIZE]Well, I was / I am not very sure about where to go after Ubuntu. I thought about Debian 6, which is praised by its stability, but I read some views about Mint being more updated in terms of applications' latest and multimedia codecs, and with Mint I should [SIZE=2]have the benefit of being close to my previous Ubuntu experience[/SIZE].[/SIZE][/FONT]
[FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2] So, I just installed Linux Mint 13 (Maya) [SIZE=2]in my[/SIZE] home computer [SIZE=2]for a t[SIZE=2]est[/SIZE][/SIZE]; although Mint 14 is already out there, Maya is a 5 years LTS (good!!). Of course I am using MATE (which is a kind of Gnome 2) and I am feeling really like at home again. The installation went really smooth, I did not have to change anything in my partition table, I installed Mint in the partition block where Ubuntu 12.04 was before, btw it occupied much less space, and the other two blocks (/home and /mnt) were intact, so I enjoyed all my settings right from start (needless to s[SIZE=2]ay I made a secure backup[SIZE=2] of everything [SIZE=2]beforehand)[/SIZE][/SIZE][/SIZE]. From my very short experience [SIZE=2]in my [/SIZE]home computer with Mint, [SIZE=2]i[/SIZE]t seems to me that [SIZE=2]I have t[/SIZE]he best of Ubuntu but free from the annoying 12.04[/SIZE][/FONT]
 
 [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]I have not installed Mint at work yet, so I am open to comments and suggestions, but unless you[SIZE=2]r advices are really convin[SIZE=2]cing, [/SIZE][/SIZE]I thin[SIZE=2]k I will [SIZE=2]stay with Mint for a while[/SIZE][/SIZE]. 
[/SIZE][/FONT]
 [FONT=Arial, sans-serif][SIZE=2]Sorry for this long post[SIZE=2]. [/SIZE]I hope my views help someone. [/SIZE][/FONT]

### Post by tgalati4 on 2013-01-03
If you only have one (new) computer, and you get used to the Unity interface, then that might be the way to go.  But when you have 2, 3, or 8 machines, a mix of old and new, it's nice to have an OS and desktop environment that runs well on all of them.  That way you don't have to go through the mental gymnastics (relearning) of how to find and do stuff in Unity when you have several machines that can't run it and use those on a daily basis.

For instance on my lab bench I have a few computers I use for testing hardware and programming microcontrollers.  They are older and can't run Unity well.  But Mint14 Mate runs fine on them.  My newest laptop is 2007 vintage and it runs Mate14 well as well.  It runs the latest live version of Ubuntu with Unity, but it's sluggish.  Mint14 Mate runs fast and it's a familiar desktop environment.

If I could purchase an Ubuntu tablet with Unity on it, I would, but I've never seen one.  So I have a few Android tablets floating around the house as well.

Unity makes a lot of sense for what Mark S. and Canonical are trying to accomplish going forward, but in a mixed environment of old and new computers, you need to determine what is important:  new and shiny or compatibility and simplicity.

### Post by montag dp on 2013-01-03
I never had performance issues with Gnome 3-based DEs, even with my 6.5 year-old old laptop, but I can agree with the comments on Unity.  I can also accept that many others may find Unity completely acceptable, and I think I would too if it were the only Linux option available.

But, after using Unity (Ubuntu 12.04) for a few weeks I decided there were a number of things I didn't like about it (in no particular order): 
1) Little customization available 
2) Window and launcher behavior 
  --a) Can't click on the launcher icon to minimize a window
  --b) Launcher aggregates multiple processes of a single program - requires more mouse clicks to maximize/minimze
3) No global menu - I like the quick search ability but I also like having a list available.

I started looking around and settled on Linux Mint as well.  I really like both KDE and Cinnamon.  I've heard good things about MATE as well.

Bottom line: do what works for you.  I think you will like Mint as you continue using it.  Hopefully it stays around for awhile and these other DE projects remain alive and well for a long time.
