### Post by crhylove on 2010-04-30
I have been enjoying the latest Ubuntu: Lucid Lynx all night.

That is to say, there are some astounding and positive changes, and, like previous version of Ubuntu, I'd say that The Lynx is superior to both Apple and Microsoft OSes for nearly all users, and indeed a fantastic Linux distro, but not without it's usual pile of inconceivably unnecessary glitches and mis-steps.

To start with, Ubuntu has long been a solid and stable development platform, and it shows, from stem to stern.  In particular, the .deb package management Ubuntu has inherited from it's upstream Debian roots is bar none, the best way to update and install software on a computer.  Nearly every application or other software you might need is available and ready, easily installable in Synaptic, for even the most Novice of users.

Indeed, I had my machine up and configured faster than I've EVER performed a Windows install, even using custom stripped versions of Windows (which are almost always superior to default Microsoft releases) such as Tiny or MicroXP.

In fact, every driver worked immediately out of the box.  I had sound, video, my mouse and keys worked, and I was online instantly.  No glitch whatsoever in generic immediate usage terms.

On first impression, the default layout is not bad.  There have been complaints on line, but for the record: ANYTHING IS BETTER THAN POOP BROWN.  Unfortunately, the honeymoon ended there.  For one, I liked the purple background, but it didn't match the grayish taskbars, and the orange buttons looked absolutely repulsive.  Spectral opposites work, like green and purple, and near colors work, like blue and purple, but orange and purple is just plain UGLY.  But it gets worse....

What is Canonical thinking by putting the 3 buttons on the top left of the window?  Different isn't always better.  In fact, when usability has been clearly defined for over a decade, in this case, different is definitely STUPID.  Frankly, both Mac AND Windows have a superior Title bar layout that makes infinitely more sense, and also has the backing of OVER A DECADE OF USE!  Keep in mind I am NOT a Mac or Win fanboy, either.  Quite the contrary.

Also, I had some trouble initially getting the restricted drivers to work, which means no compiz, and slow playback on HD video.  Everything was usable, but not as pretty or slick as it could have been.  I determined that for whatever reason, You cannot install restricted drivers while Synaptic is open.  This seems kind of lame, since drivers aren't really in the same category as applications in my mind, and I haven't heard any reports of anyone fixing this seemingly unnecessary glitch.  To Ubuntu's credit though, other superior distros (Like Mint) have the exact same upstream error.  It's a shame nobody has stepped forward to fix this.

Now on to Application selection.

Let's be clear here, for most people, the application selection is very, very good.  Firefox is the world's best browser by a landslide, and the browser is the most important software on a modern OS.  So where it counts, Ubuntu wins, hands down with no room for second place.  Also, the next most commonly used application, the word processor is amply handled by Open Office Writer, which is in my estimation, simply the best document editor in existence.  So for Grandma, who only ever prints the occasional letter and surfs the web, Ubuntu is the perfect choice.

Once again, though, the honeymoon ended rather abruptly and unceremoniously.  In fact, it quickly went from honeymoon to an ugly drunken fight between tweakers drunk on cheap wine.  I'm talking of course, about Movie Player.  Even grandma occasionally watches a documentary or DVD, and this software is completely unusable, broken, and inadequate.  It might make a passing attempt to be less bloated and stable than some UTTER crap, like say Quicktime, iTunes, or Windows Media Player, but there is a video player that absolutely holds all the ones mentioned, and clearly Movie Player too, calmly and solidly under 2 inches of water and laughs as they drown.

I'm not overly prone to profanity, but this must be said:

WHY THE _(*&#%_ WOULD ANYONE EVER USE ANYTHING BESIDES VLC?!?!??!?!

<snip>  Nobody should pick a video player besides VLC ever, for any reason, and frankly, if you want to respond to my review, please don't mention it at all.  You know it's wrong, I know it's wrong, and literally every kid on the street over the age of 7 and with even the slightest modicum of computer literacy knows that VLC is the best video player, and should be the default video player for EVERY OS, and the fact that Ubuntu has failed in this simple and obvious choice speaks volumes to their design committee decision making process, and how unwilling they seem to be to dethrone Microsoft from the average end user desktop.  Disgusting.

OK, now there are some other obvious choices that I feel need to be addressed.

Empathy, though getting better, is not Pidgin.  It can't be Pidgin, and Pidgin is better.  Obvious and stupid choice again.

Brazero is practically unusable.  I know, I know, k3b is not gnome friendly or whatever but..... Brazero is really, really not acceptable.  I actually like infrarecord on Windows better than either bloated and faulty choice, but that's not available for Linux, and this is a shame since burning CDs should be easy and fast functionality that anyone can do reliably and quickly without a tutorial.  I actually dual boot just to burn cds using either infrarecord, or, for the occasional audio CD that needs normalizing, Nero.

Pitivi is lame.  I'm sure many devs have spent years working on this piece of crap, but they should just give up and contribute to OpenShot.  And while they're at it, they should make OpenShot the default video editor for Ubuntu.  This is another no-brainer that the Ubuntu devs miraculously still managed to get completely wrong.

The Game selection in Lynx is a good start.  It's better than it's ever been, but it's still woefully inadequate.  For instance: No Chess!!!  There are about a dozen great Chess programs, and including one should have been a priority.  While you are at it, why not include a great graphical one, like Brutal Chess?  Also, there should at least be a place holder in the Applications menu by default to download Urban Terror.  Urban Terror is one of the most fun, most reliable, easiest to start, and difficult to master FPSes existent.  It's free, it's based on the FOSS Q3 engine, and it works great on Windows OR Linux which means your Ubuntu box would be an instant success at the LAN party.  Never forget that Windows initially succeed for one stupid reason: Solitaire.  Urban Terror and a good 3d Chess alone could help Linux take a lot of Windows market share for the casual gamer.  Also, most of the best games ever made are playable on Linux via Super Nintendo, Wii, and Nintendo 64 emulators.  Having a place holder for these in the Apps menu would be good too.  Imagine having a working Wii emulator on a decent machine right out of the box.  That would easily trump anything Microsoft or Apple has EVER done gaming wise on a default install.  And the Wii emulator Dolphin works great and takes less than 20 mb!!

Lastly, I have a few preferences that are not so vehemently obvious:

Deluge > Transmission
Audacious > Rhythmbox

and one more HUGE GLARING SCREAMING ERROR!!!!

WHERE IS THE GIMP?!?!?  There's an awesome, nearly perfect photoshop replacement that is open source, well developed, and small, easy, and free, and it's not included?!?!?  This is just plain stupid, again.

In conclusion:

This STILL will not be the year of Linux on the Desktop.  Don't get me wrong, Ubuntu, and some of it's superior children like Mint are much better out of the box than any of the commercial OSes.  They provide more functionality, less intensive requirements, better speed, reliability, and are not NEARLY as prone to viruses.  For all users, I would have absolutely have no hesitance in suggesting Linux Mint in particular, except to perhaps hard core gamers that want to run whatever DirectX10 corporate crap ware came out last week.  For all other users, Ubuntu, and it's superior step child Mint offer better security, speed, reliability, ease of use, cost, and functionality directly out of the box and with a very easy setup.  That being said, there's a LONG way to go.  I simply cannot reconcile the decisions not ton include VLC, OpenShot, Pidgin, Gimp, and a less boated audio player like Audacious.  99% of the population would not need to download and install ANY software if these were included from the get go.  In addition, by adding a couple of modest games that nearly all modern computers can handle with ease (Brutal Chess, Urban Terror, Mupen64), Linux could handily trounce Windows in their own market with the casual gamer, and all for FREE.

Once again, however, the greatest strength of Open Source has proven to be it's downfall.  Through the diversity of opinion, freedom of thought, and creative passions of it's developers, obvious choices for the end users have been missed, omitted, or in a mentally challenged way replaced with a pet project or some other catastrophe (I'm looking at YOU Movie Player!!).

I can't WAIT for a captain to come to the helm with some balls and make the right choice for end users.  That will be the day Linux takes over the Desktop, and I feel it is inevitably creeping closer!

PS I REALLY don't understand the buttons on the left.  Even while typing this review, I was irritated no less than 3 times by not being able to minimize and reference something else.  Ludicrous!!!
