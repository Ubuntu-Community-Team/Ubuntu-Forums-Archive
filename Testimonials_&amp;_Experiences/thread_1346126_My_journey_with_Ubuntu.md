### Post by georgemc on 2009-12-04
This is my first post on the Ubuntu forums.
 

 Some of my background: Started professionally with ATT Unix in the mid 80's, used MS Dos since 1.0 and even still have my Windows 2.0 floppy disks.  Been in server farms most of my professional life, where we used 95% of the time some *NIX system, and in the corporate realm we mostly used Windows on our desktops for the day to day applications, e-mail, word processing, project management, spreadsheets, presentations etc.
 

 I have been toying with linux on and off over the last decade or more, and over the years I really took a liking to Sun Solaris. However linux was to much tweaking for me and Sun Solaris hardware to expensive to implement for my personal use.  And then there is the family factor where it just has to be AOL and MS Word and nothing else.
 

 Late last year the hard drive on my aging laptop gave out.  It was loaded with W2000 SP4 and worked well up to this point.  I promptly acquired a new hard drive and a copy of XP Pro and proceeded to load Windows XP.  Needless to say I was not able to get every thing to work properly.  Some drivers were outdated and didn't load or work right, some were just not to be found on the Internet.  Disappointed I reloaded W2000 back on and got most of the devices to work.  What a bummer.
 

 Then earlier this year I stumbled across “Ubuntu” and being unhappy with my laptop I downloaded Ubuntu Jaunty, found some “howto's” about dual booting in the tutorials section and loaded Jaunty on my laptop.  I thought it can't be any worse than what I already have and its time to check out Linux again.
 

 Now you can imagine  my pleasant surprise when the installation completed and my laptop was desperately trying to connect to my wireless network in a very nice GUI desktop.  I clicked through the menus, yes I am one of those types at times that do not read the manuals first, and found that every thing I need to operate was loaded by default.  I have not used Windows on that laptop since and I have the Windows partition mounted in Ubuntu for easy access to my data files.  On my to do list is to recover the Windows partition on that laptop.
 

 For about the last 6 months I have been lurking on these message boards reading about other users issues and sifting through the tutorials, I took inventory of my main stay desktop in my home office and came up with one application I currently rely on that does not work on Linux;  QuickBooks.  There are Linux Accounting applications that I looked into, but I have years of data and do not want to even attempt to convert the data.  Then I read a how to on “virtualization” and sure thing I was able to make a XP guest, using the XP license I had purchased for my laptop,  in Sun VirtualBox and loaded up QuickBooks with all my data.  Problem solved.  The time consuming part of the virtualization installation was registering with Sun, downloading the bits and pieces, and reading the documentation.  Now my home office main stay desktop is Ubuntu 9.04.
 

 I personally always enjoyed working with *NIX and am no stranger to a command line or terminal.  Even though the Ubuntu GUI is nice and easy to use there are many things that can not be accomplished with out the terminal or command line input.  The tools are at your finger tips and many are just a few clicks away to install.
 

 Corporate communications these days rely heavily on MSOffice standards and OpenOffice does give me the tools to comply with those standards.  Granted I do not use Photo Shop and my Power Point slides are simple and run well with OpenOffice Presentation.  I switched from Outlook to Evolution for my e-mail and scheduling needs and find it much easier to deal with.
 

 The Ubuntu tutorials are informative and just plain great.  I appreciate all the hard work from every contributor.  
 

 The “help” forums are highly educational and well worth reading even if you do not have the problem described in the post.  Many times I follow the suggested links and play with the code examples, and these lead me to new and interesting tools and utilities.
 

 The contributors to the Ubuntu help forums are outstanding and helpful, and show the dedication and support of the Ubuntu community.  I enjoy and appreciate the many tips, reviews, and suggestions that all have contributed.
 

 My journey with Ubuntu has just begun.
