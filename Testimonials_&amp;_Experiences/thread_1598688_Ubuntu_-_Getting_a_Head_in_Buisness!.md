### Post by ventrical on 2010-10-16
I accidently booted into Windows earlier this evening.  I thought - "Ok.. see whats up" and the first thing on the schedule was that the Anti Virus software prompted me  (or more or less gave me an ultimatum) that I better uprgade. So I did.

I watched as IE more or less froze up and hung as the AV program took control. Then, I thought about a movie I had seen several years ago called "Getting a Head in Buisness". It was about an advertising executive who become completely controled by his greed and actually grows another head!!

  Then, it hit me. WIndows is actually a Vbox that is allowed to run under the control of Third Party anti-Virus programs.

  And then I am wondering why :
1. My pen-dive thinks it's a harddrive.
2. I have not had one 'malware' or virus or hijacking happen while I have been on line and all I have is that little gufw firewall.

  So the AV - security programs get bigger and bigger - eventually becomming the principle framework of the Windows OS.


  Just awesome . Way to go Linux and Ubuntu.

  Ya know .. actually for several years now I have been proposing just that - and some friends and clients have asked me to develop such a framework. As I see it, though, the LINUX kernel and other security concepts render a strong defense against traditional malware, especially over the internet and some of the simple security layers  take up much less resource while providing evidently a much greater protection.

  There is nothing that dominates an Ubuntu session.  It can be proposed then that a linux_kernel_session is that which what the end_user desires to make it. One can simply change an entire desktop theme and framework and there is not the worry about restrictions or validations or authentications. Of course in many instances some expertise may be required but that is not a bad thing as it encourages end_users to take up a more independent approach to learning and understanding computer security concepts and it saves me hours of downtime having to teach and explain to others how to 'take back' their PCs from the world of malware. If I look at Ubuntu/Linux from this aspect , then , it is not such a hard sell.
