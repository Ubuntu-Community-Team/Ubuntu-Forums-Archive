### Post by lhb1142 on 2011-05-16
For Whatever It's Worth:

Every six months a new version of Ubuntu (as well as the variants Kubuntu, Xubuntu, and, next time, Lubuntu) is released.

I wait one or two days for the servers to settle down from "everybody" upgrading their systems, and then I FIRST download the CD (.iso) file for the variant(s) of choice.

I burn a disc (and create a USB thumb drive) and also copy this file to an external hard drive; that way, no matter what happens, I have the means to effect a clean installation.

I then wait another week or so, reading about any potential 'bugs' which may have appeared, and only then, when I am satisfied, do I perform the network upgrade.

I would certainly recommend this procedure to anyone, especially one who owns only one computer. S/he can burn a CD or create a USB installation stick, preferably both, from the .iso file prior to trying to upgrade.

Should the upgrade fail, even if it 'broke' the previous ability to connect to the internet, s/he could wipe the computer (I use DBAN < [http://www.dban.org/](http://www.dban.org/) >; you can download and create a CD which you can keep for whenever you may need it) and reinstall the (new) OS from the CD or USB stick, plugging the computer into his/her router to connect to the internet (Wi-fi might be inoperable during the installation process).

The installation will then proceed normally and smoothly.

Wiping your computer takes a fair amount of time; I do my wiping overnight while I am sleeping. The installation itself takes about a half-hour. It's the configuration to your tastes which takes the most time; I recommend that you write down (on paper!) the programs of your choice so you can quickly find them (in Synaptic Package Manager) and install them. That's what I do.

In other words, before trying to upgrade your computer, you want to take precautions. To me, this is common sense.

Doing it (upgrading) the way I do it ensures that you will not be completely cut off for any great length of time.

I also recommend that you upgrade (or fresh install) on a Saturday or Sunday; most businesses are off the web during those days and you'll achieve higher downloading speeds.

Just my thoughts ...

### Post by el_koraco on 2011-05-20
You don't need to save the list every time you install something. When you enter the command, before you do a fresh install, you will have a list of all the programs you have installed at the moment.  

If by additional repositories you mean the PPA's, you will need to make a note of those you use. The reason is that you need to add the PPA's again manually after a fresh install, because enabling repositories generates GPG (Gnu privacy guard) keys - which are a means to ensure that you're installing legitimate software.

It's the 

```
sudo apt-add-repository ppa:whatever

```

command when you add ppa's. You just need the ppa:whatever part. These are located in the directory "/etc/apt/sources.list.d" So, once again, it's as simple as doing a 

```
ls /etc/apt/sources.list.d >> ppalist
```

Take into account that before enabling PPA's, you need to make sure that they have been set up for the next version of Ubuntu you're using. Most of the time, though, it's simply a matter of waiting a month after a new release is put out - which is good to do either way. 

If you mean additional repositories as official repositroies, when you reinstall, you just go to the Software center, Edit, software sources, and enable them again. They're found in /etc/apt/sources.list

When you're doing a fresh install, feel free to either open a new thread, or PM me if you have any questions. I'm gonna be hanging out here for a while :D
