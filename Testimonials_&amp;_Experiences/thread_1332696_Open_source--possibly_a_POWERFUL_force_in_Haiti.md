### Post by julianb on 2009-11-20
My friend Gerald, a Haitian who speaks Creole and French, says that the computer I sent to him with Ubuntu-Linux is working well. It doesn't need virus scanners and doesn't get viruses. It's set up as a dual boot machine (with Windows XP) but Gerald wants to avoid using Windows XP since Ubuntu works better.

The philosophy of Linux is relevant here like: people who use Linux can easily teach others to do the same, and people who teach about linux can easily become people who change the way the software works and develop new software. That can make Haitians able to contribute as part of a community; it's something that can make Haitians not just depend on others, because they're contributing. If they do eventually write software, chances are good someone will want to pay them to write more. Community software. 

Creating an expectation that it's possible to buy computers at very low prices can:
--help a lot of people in underdeveloped countries become new computer users, 
--contribute to widespread knowlege in Haiti of e-mail and word processing, and the big possibilities (including fund-raising for community based service-organizations) that are facilitated by the internet.

I sent two computers with Linux, one which was a gift for Xavier. I haven't heard lately how that computer is going for him but I know it's capable of easily using the internet.

I'm inspired the way low cost Linux netbooks can help us.

They are available for about $150 on ebay, and can come with solar electricity for about $190. They require about 10 watts, which can be obtained from a solar panel that fits in a backpack we could bring on a plane to Haiti... a solar panel that costs about $30. These 12 volt solar panels can be used with a $10 converter to change 12V to 9V for the netbook computer, without need for any battery besides the one in the computer.* 

Would you like to join up with other folks in making this possible? Reply here or you can see my contact info at our group's web site [http://bit.ly/8GXBfb](http://bit.ly/8GXBfb)

Besides some computers, we need folks who know how to test the machines and solar panels, and set them up with Linux in French. 

Julian

*We can buy an inverter with expensive batteries, and change 12 volts to 120 volts AC back to 9 volts DC for the computer. But we don't have to, because we can buy a converter that changes 12V directly to 9V.

### Post by julianb on 2009-11-20
Folks talk about how it's easiest to compete with Microsoft in an area where they're not so strong.

My friend Gerald uses Ubuntu in Haiti, where Windows is common and has the advantage of being well-known, but where Windows' on-the-job performance is abysmal.

See, in Haiti folks are dealing with 
-low levels of computer expertise, 
-low levels of English language expertise, 
-the inability to pay for high-end computers,
-inconsistent electricity, whether using generators, solar panels, or grid power
-lack of really good internet connections

Put these all together and what you'll find is, it's a constant battle to keep a Windows machine working smoothly. Windows often is slow because of its high memory usage or because of virus scanners and/or malware. With limited computing time, it's hard to keep Windows and a virus scanner updated, even if you know what you're doing. Most people there don't really understand computer security, so when I was there I saw a lot of out-of-date virus scanners with no valid subscription for virus definition updates. Users don't know what most of the security related popup messages say, because they don't speak English and nobody sells Windows in French in Haiti.

People want to browse the internet, do spreadsheets, and make word processing documents in peace. This is an area where Ubuntu really stands out.
