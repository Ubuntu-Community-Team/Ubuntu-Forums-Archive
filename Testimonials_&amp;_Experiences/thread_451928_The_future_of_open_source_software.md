### Post by arvidkastel on 2007-05-22
I'm relatively new to the linux world and specifically ubuntu, and I have some initial reflections i would like to share. Some positive and some not so positive. 
My first impression was: WOW! so many cool and useful programs! How come i've never looked at these before? And i am free to edit them the way i want them! and they are free! Fantastic...
Ok. I think we all know the benefits of free software, and we all know the bad sides. The good sides are obvious: We don't have to rely on some big company's good will, we make our own software and  there are plenty of programmers that seems to have an infinite amount of spare time to solve our problems.
But let's face it. There are too many beta-stage programs that will never reach the degree of usability a commercial app will.  Of course there are the major applications and suites like openoffice and gimp, that are very good and frequently updated, but do you honestly think that they will reach the same degree of good support and stability to fulfil a modern companys need? Proprietary software have the obvious advantage that there are some programmers that are paid to fix bugs and to push the application that last bit to full usability.
Open source programmers, seems to be very good at proposing fantastic new ideas and to realize them halfways, simply because their reward is to implement some good ideas and not to debug some other persons ideas. This is where money come into the picture. With money you can make someone go all the way. 
I don't say that it has to be a lot of money or that it has to come from a company, just that if you're supposed to do something quite boring and not so inspiring, you would like to get something back. And programming is much that way. A part of it is really great and inspiring, and some parts are just work.
A big problem with ordinary proprietary software is that the marginal cost to start using a program is so large. This means that if you legally want to use a specific software you have to pay for it, even if you don't know if it will suite your needs. The effect of this is that most software companies tend to implement lots of functions into their software and integrate them and lock other software out, thus reducing the marginal cost of new functions for the users. For the computing society as a whole it would be benefitable if software would be based on open standards that would make it possible for different applications to interact and be more module-based. That would make use of the benefits of the free market.
So, is there a solution to this dilemma? I think that there is. My proposal is that the linux community and specifically the ubuntu community would start a fund, addressing these problems. This fund, would pay projects to be finished or further developed, either by paying those already responsible or by hiring external programmers to make the applications more final. Basically these programmers should organize the code, delegate some tasks to the community and complete the non-functioning parts of the programs.
Where should this money come from? From the users. If it is simple enough I am sure that many users would pay some small amount to be able to use good software.
How should this money be divided between different projects? This is a tricky question and i think that it should be a combination of how much a software is used and of the opinions of a strategic committee. If a project is widely used and could be more useful if even better, it should recieve money. If there are strategic interests of having a software with a specific functionality then that project should also recieve funding. 
The ubuntu has a special opportunity of achieving this kind of system, because of the package based architecture. This makes it possible to divide the software in chunks that possibly could recieve money separately, and also to trace what software is widely used and appreciated. 
One could think of a system that says that those projects connected to this system, can only be downloaded and used by those who have payed this fee to become members. People that write good changes of the source code can apply for being members of the project and thereby recieving part of the funds. Projects that support open standards will be economically favored by the committee. 
This would self evidently not be a vista-type of DRM engine. Just something that would make it a little tricky and uncomfortable to copy from your friend.
I think that a system in this direction would combine the best parts of the open-source and the proprietary world. This system would of course keep the main ideas of free software intact, with transparency and open standards as a base. But it also would make a lot of alpha- and beta-stage software go final, simply because of the fact that everyone is used to our market economy. 
A quick calculation at the end of the post: Say that the monthly fee would be 1$ for students and youths, 2$ for regular users and 5$ for companies, and suppose further that this system in the end would have like 5 million users worldwide. This would make a total annual fund of about 100 million dollars. Say that there are 200 interresting project that would come to question of recieving funding. That would make 500 000$ per project a year, and i am pretty sure lots of people would pay 1$ a month to keep their computer up to date. 500 000$ is a lot of money for an open source project. Think about the possibilities: Inkscape surpassing Illustrator, OpenOffice less buggy and better than Microsoft office, ardour comparable with cubase and so on and so forth. Open source has so many obvius advantages, because noone has a reason to bind users to their standards, and to hide anything. Let's not miss this historic opportunity of intellectual freedom just because we won't spend a single dollar on getting it.
I would really much appreciate replies on this one! :p

### Post by ankursethi on 2007-05-24
The open source ideology is simple : software that is free and open, and available/affordable to everyone. I think that much can be understood by the name itself (Free and Open Source Software). FSF was founded on this principle. GNU GPL was written keeping this in mind. Charging for free software would undermine the entire philosophy it was founded upon.

Let us for one moment consider that Canonical begins charging for Ubuntu. Will it be fair? Canonical did not develop the GIMP, neither did they develop GAIM, or Firefox, or Tomboy, or F-Spot, or any other app that is distributed with Ubuntu. Why then should canonical get money?

Let us assume now that Canonical starts a "fund", as you said. That would mean wars between different projects for more funding. Instead of writing code because they like to (and because the believe in the GNU philosophy), people will start writing code for money. This would pollute the OSS environment and lots of developers who code only for money will join in - which would obviously have a detrimental effect on Open Source as a whole.

You want to help Ubuntu? The one and only way is to devote time to fixing bugs, writing new apps, writing docs and producing artwork because that's what the devs need right now - manpower. Many of the people who work on Ubuntu already have jobs and are not working for money, so throwing it at them is no help. Everybody's time is precious, even more precious than money. So devoting your time to a project will do more good than throwing money at it.

PS : Currently many projects do need money. Urgently. My suggestion is that you pick your favorite projects and donate to these projects directly if you are willing to. That would be better than a unified fund.

### Post by arvidkastel on 2007-05-24
Thank you for your responses!
If one looks at the ecomony as a method of producing things that are good for the society, in a more general sense than just trading goods for money, i think it is possible to see why OSS is good and why it is bad. 
It is good because its scope is to produce innovative and good software for the users. The problem of the free market approach is of course that the scope of big software corporations isn't to provide this good software but primarily to gain money for the stock holders. The free market is most often a good thing,  but it seems that when it comes to software it hasn't been that succesful. The support for such things as opens standards for example is quite weak, which would of course be a good thing for the consumers. OSS has provided a solution that is based on that the ones writing the programs should do it because they like it and because they want to contribute to the open source society, thereby solving the dilemma of having a discrepancy between the goals of the corporations (pleasing the stock holders) and the goals of society (good software without surveillance for example). You can see it as  starting a second economy where people trade their time or donations for software. 
This is of course mainly a good thing, but if you would like to solve the more general problem I think you have to rethink certain things. Take a look at plumbing for example. It isn't quite probable that plumbers would work all day, only recieving plumbing as payback. Even if the analogy isn't perfect I think it says something about the OSS-society.
I believe that the reason that OSS has become what it is today, is mainly sociological. Those writing OSS are (at least the stereotype) young men with a great interrest in computing and a lot of time to spend. They find it rewarding to collaborate in order to write software. 
A functioning economy must give reasons for people to work. The OSS economy has implemented this rewarding system with very sublime motivations like contributing to a free and open society. This is good, but I don't think it will work forever. 
This i the problem of motivating the producers. But an economy also has consumers. It isn't likely that everyone will be programmers or documentation writers. In my initial post i proposed that one should pay a small amount of money to recieve good software. That seems to a hot topic to the society. Money is just an ugly word to say. Why should we pay for software when there is no cost of reproducing it only producing it? Noone has to work more just because i make a copy of this software. I think that the solution could be to start an internal "economy", where it is perfectly legal and encouraged to copy and modify the software as long as it is to members of the economy. Inside the economy everythin is free. To be able to gain access to the economy you pay a monthly fee, or if you are a programmer, write good software. If you write a lot of good software you will get paid. This committee I was talking about should provide the frameworks and guidelines for writing good software, and has the opportunity to give resources to important projects.
I think this solves these five problems:
1. Motivation to produce software. You actually get paid to do work.
2. Motivation to buy software. You cant just copy the software if you dont pay or contribute to be inside the economy.
3. Motivation to produce good software. You only get money if you align to the guidelines of the committee.
4. Motivation to copy software. For a member of the economy there is no marginal cost of downloading a program and you are perfectly free to do it.
5. There is need to be a programmer to keep up to par with the society. You actually pay for what you get.
This is of course a quite utopian dream, and may sound socialistic in many ears. And in a way it is. But the economy would internally be market driven, and there could be companies working inside the economy.
It also adresses the more general problem of dealing with non material resources, where the cost of reproducing is effectively zero. There are no motivation of not copying these resources when existent, but someone have to spend resources to make them exist, so the free market principle that you pay for exactly what you get isn't appliable. In this economy you pay for having the opportunity of consuming whatever you want.

### Post by ankursethi on 2007-05-25
Companies like Trolltech are making quite a lot of money mainly because they use some modified version of the GPL, or their own license. It would be quite feasible, with the help of a law expert, to write your own license (provided you are willing to spend money). It could limit the distribution of your software to source form, without any changes.

So only those who are willing to take the trouble to learn to compile software would be able to use your work for free, and this will make up less than 25% of your userbase. Perhaps even less. Since any modifications to the original source package, or any kind of tinkering with the source code, or using any other way to compile the source other than the standard way described within the license or the readme would be prohibited, any script that attempts to autoconfigure and autocompile your software will be in danger of a lawsuit.

This way the OSS community will still get the code, and you will still make (some amount of) money.
