### Post by glynallinson on 2008-08-11
Quite a number of years ago, I like many others bought Windows XP. I was pleased with myself owning my new version.
But after a few years of viruses, spyware and internet product activation, I found out that I didn't own this copy at all, Microsoft do.
Now when I buy something I've always believed that I own it. 
The milk in my coffee, I guess the cows still own that.
The last install I did, I had to phone microsoft to activate my copy and I asked them why I had to activate it of the internet or phone. Their response "You've activate this copy to its limit".
Well that was it, I had to find an alternative. After searching many sites, Linux was always the one that kept popping up.
So what did I have to lose. 
The first distrobution I ever tested was Slax, Wow it was great, no driver installs, it ran from CD and NO PRODUCT ACTIVATION.
That was me semi hooked. After using Slax for sometime. I found Ubuntu purely by chance. So I downloaded the ISO, burned it to CD and restarted.
Will admit the install took sometime, but I didn't care. After the install had completed and the system reboot I thought this is it, a new operating system. After hours of playing around and reading forums, I began to realise that this operating system can do so much more. The hours turned to days and so on.
I've tried many other distrobutions on other less equiped machines and they've worked almost everytime. I doubt I'll ever switch back. 
Ubuntu and many other distrobutions have shown me that theres more to a desktop and laptop than browsing, chatting or playing games.
As for the gaming side, I read many forum posts about Linux Gaming. Me! I just look for an alternative.

### Post by uberdonkey5 on 2008-08-11
A super post. One of my main bug-bears at the moment is upgrading. This is partly because I clicked upgrade for adobe acrobat one day when I was using a vodafone connect pen (1GB max download). When I came back one and a half hours later it was still 'upgrading' and had used up my whole months limit!Unfortunately I had to pay 41 euros to download the work I was SUPPOSED to be doing. I'm new to linux, and have found it fidley at times (I think mainly because it is getting all the drivers to work on a specific computer). Well, with ubuntu I have chosen 'never' to upgrade, although I think occasionally I will check when I have a LAN.

I think ubuntu also has an accesibility that windows doesn't have. 2 examples: I had a film I had recorded on DVD and copied to my computer, but it was in bits. I found software for video editing, installed it, found guide to run it, all within about 15 minutes. Then I set it running to compress 8GB into 700MB, cropped it etc and it worked happily away on my closed laptop as I drove home. You would never even conceive of doing that in a windows environment.

Then, last night, a friend called me on Skype and I realised I hadn't configured the microphone in either operating system yet (I'm dual-boot). I was in ubuntu and thought, damn, windows will be quicker to sort this out. Well, I was wrong, after messing around trying to download additional software etc (that they suggested) but which didn't work I switched back to ubuntu. I did a search, realised it was just some settings within the ALSA mixer and within 2 minutes I was chatting away.

Problem with windows is... we are all used to it. I'm old enough to remember DOS and I realised even then that windows often looked better but was slower to do some very simple things. It IS a struggle to enter the land of linux (esp. with pages full of terminal code) but I am seeing the advantages more each day. I think when more computers come PRE-LOADED with ubuntu (thus all the little hardware problems are sorted) like you can get with windows, MORE people will use it.
