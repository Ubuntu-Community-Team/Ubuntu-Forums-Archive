### Post by enoughsaid05 on 2008-02-13
I don't know whether to laugh or cry at my experience with ubuntu.

Years before I have been reading up on Linux and was lured by its inherent stability and security. My past experiences with Windows hasn't been quite smooth because the system gets reguarly clogged up and my family ended up having to send the computer for repair and reinstalling windows, considering that none of us in the family are good at computing.

So when I made a switchover, I was quite happy at the thought of being out of the some company's monopoly grip and enjoy open source software. Since the start I had a culture shock.

I heard from my friend that when you are using the Windows and something wrong happens, you can blame its company. In Linux, you can only blame yourself, how true!

When I first installed Linux, I didn't have flash and java to start of with. After plenty of reading I finally got both of them barely working, till one day Linux decides not to detect my java. I scrambled to read and do lots of research in the forum but can't find the answer, so I ended up reinstalling the OS.

My friend told me not do that often, but I can't help it. In Windows, everything seems to work smoothly. I can install games, softwares and so on with the help of the installer and everything works fine. In Linux, users have to get their hands on and work their way through to make the software work.

It is not the first time that I have reinstalled the system. It has been a few times and the lastest frustration with the system started when my virtualbox has made my Linux unusable.

I am not good at computing, but nonetheless willing to learn. Hope that through all these falls I would be able to gain some, which I did!

I am more comfortable with the command line interface.

I know how to compile software from source (tar -xvf, make, make install etc) and the first time it worked was with Avant Windows Navigator.

I know how to use GIMP and learned to make fanciful art works with its beautiful brushes.

Still how I wish that Linux could have been more easier to use if it doesn't crash my system at the slightest change (such as installing virtualbox just from the repostories!).

### Post by RawMustard on 2008-02-14
> My friend told me not do that often, but I can't help it. In Windows, everything seems to work smoothly. I can install games, softwares and so on with the help of the installer and everything works fine. In Linux, users have to get their hands on and work their way through to make the software work.

Can't be so smooth if you had to take it back to the shop to have windows re-installed all the time?

Just like Windows, every piece of software you install can potentially damage something on your system.  And just like Windows, you need to research how good an application is before installing it on an important computer.  I feel this is even more important on Linux than windows, because there is so much software out there that is still in development, including software from the repositories.

Having said that, it's really rare to need a re-installation of your system under Linux, as it doesn't have a registry that can get corrupted beyond repair with no one who knows exactly how it all works.
The only good good reason to re-install your Linux system is if you have completely killed it, but even then a Linux system can be bought back from total death if you know what you're doing and are willing to spend the time to learn how to fix it :)

Linux is not harder to use than Windows, it's just different and in my opinion is easier than Windows, it's just that there's so much more you can do and this can easily lead you into trouble.  Just remember to research an application before installing it and see if others have had problems with it.  If you do install something that seems to cause problems, a simple "apt-get --purge remove theproblemapplication" will more than likely bring your system back to it's former state.

Now your problems with Flash and Java are known problems on Linux and they are not the fault of Linux. They are the fault of the makers of those applications not truly supporting the Linux platform, more so of Flash than Java.  With these applications, you should take your complaints directly to the makers and ask them to help you and to support linux better in the future.

Remember, Linux is an operating system that has no barriers to anything, but because of this, there are fewer safety nets also :)
