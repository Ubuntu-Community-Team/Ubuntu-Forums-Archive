### Post by Groucho Marxist on 2010-02-06
This Sunday, February 7th, will be the last showing of a theatrical production I worked on for my college as Sound Designer and audio console operator. What made it a wonderful experience in terms of audio was the fact that, save for recording vocal elements at the radio station I work for, every element was created in Ubuntu. 

Sound effects (SFX) such as a massive avalanche of rocks, or the sound of a neck snapping, sounded crisp and realistic due to the utilization of Audacity. On that note, I actually used Audacity running on my laptop in a live capacity for scenes heavily reliant upon SFX. My reasoning for this was that separate elements needed to be amplified or lowered depending on the performances of the actors. Brasero, the CD-burning program, also proved invaluable for elements such as pre-show music and intermission music.

In the future, I am going to explore using Ubuntu Studio for use in projects such as this; in the meantime, I'm glad I purchased my System76 Ubuntu laptop.

Due to working at a radio station, I have access to an extensive collection of licensed sound clips for use in station imaging and donor spots (it's a non-commercial station; i.e. no "advertisements," per-se). 

So, using a series of professionally-recorded, high-quality uncompressed clips of rocks tumbling, I was able to mix, edit and create the sound of a mountain moving.

Sure!

I downloaded the media pre-production program Celtx, but I am not 100% familiarized with the interface. As of right now, I use OpenOffice 3.1 for pre-production notes and memorandums. I use Audacity 1.3.10-beta under Ubuntu 9.10 64-Bit.

In terms of specs, I have a...

Intel 3.07 GHz Core 2 Duo
8 GB RAM

...System76 Pangolin Performance laptop. The extra RAM helps with editing, as well as when I run virtual operating systems for concurrent tasks.

Now, as a note, 8GB is not needed to effectively edit; I would recommend 4GB for smooth non-linear editing.
