### Post by the8thstar on 2011-08-19
Hello all,

I spent almost five years using Ubuntu, so I can say that I gave it a honest try. It used to be a thrill, with time spent tweaking, sharing and learning with the community. I had high hopes about it and about Linux in general that Ubuntu was THE alternative to Windows.

Then frustration gradually built up, as some problems couldn't be resolved : gnome 2 UI breaking at random times, graphics cards not supported in new releases, battery drains, software regressions, etc.

On top of that, Unity and Gnome Shell happened and the whole new thing lost me - I just couldn't follow the logic, let alone approve the design of either environment.

So my wife and I decided that the Ubuntu experiment had to come to an end. Too much time was being wasted in vain trying to find ways to make our computers work with Ubuntu and we just didn't have the time anymore. After all, family and work are more important than operating systems and indeed they are the true priorities.

So we tried Windows based computers again to see if we could re-adapt to that OS, but the amount of crapware/bloatware they put on PCs these days make them unacceptably slow after only a few days of use. I didn't feel like fiddling with Windows anymore and third party tools to have my PC behave normally, so instead we switched to Apple. It's a pricy choice, but at least it gets the job done and there is a warranty too.

I want to take this opportunity to thank all the people who helped me figure things out for the last five years. I also want to say that if you think that Ubuntu is the way to go, then by all means go for it. 

The community remains the best resource we have to share and learn, so treasure it and contribute !

### Post by the8thstar on 2011-08-20
@ all:

Thanks for your opinions and inputs. I feel that it's important to remind you that we chose Apple over MS or Linux because we felt we were spending too much time tweaking or fixing things to get a desktop running like it should. With Apple, we haven't had to worry about this for the last two months. In the meantime, I was having problem over problem with my Ubuntu laptop (an Asus Z53FJc from 2007).

I'm glad to see that many of you support Linux. I never thought Ubuntu sucks, but at this point in time, the problems I encountered (and some I fixed) are too much of a counterweight vs the benefits of the system (tweaks, free, etc.).

I take it quite badly that some people say that I'm showing off with an Apple product, because I didn't buy my laptop to impress people. I guess it's the underlying marketing and the vanity of some people that rub off badly on tech savvy, more modest, computers users. Personally I am impressed at what the computer can achieve after three months of use, but not at the way it looks in particular. I'm 36 years old, father of three, part of the working middle-class. I'm not your know-it-all, 20-something student that speaks loud while ordering a Frappuccino at Starbucks! 

For the record, all the other laptops sold here in France come with a flavor of Windows 7. I don't trust the system, I don't like how NTFS becomes sluggish with time, and I am sick and tired of hunting for services and programs to remove to get to decent speed. Some netbooks are sold with Linux, but you can't expect an Atom to last you more than 5 years in terms of CPU instructions and power capabilities. Shopping for an all compatible Linux system is no guarantee that software regressions won't happen down the road, so it was a no-go. Plus, most of them are the same price as a laptop with the same specs that comes with Windows pre-installed, so where's the benefit?

One word about command line: yes, it's a power tool which is also a time-saver for routine operations. It can also be very useful to sort out and fix problems you could run into. But eventually it's powerless when the problems lie with software regressions and/or lack of drivers. So the environment is one thing, while the support for this environment is another.

Finally, I have kept a Ubuntu 11.04 VM for two reasons : first, because it's the only way the system will run without problems; second, because I'm curious to see how things evolve in the future, so I can keep using it as a project from time to time (although in a confined environment).

Anyway, thanks for your participation!

After toying with an older PC that has issues, I installed it again on a used Dell Dimension 4600 with an upgraded processor and added GeForce 6600.  The Dell wouldn't be my first choice for a stable and dependable computer, but it will allow me to learn how to use Ubuntu and its command-line interface.  For now, it will suffice.  Eventually, I will look into Ubuntu as a Server, but must become comfortable without a GUI first.

While it is in fact pricey, it has always been dependable, and required little to no command-line input (otherwise requiring knowledge and experience thereof).  Among other reasons, it still allows me the ability to move files around with ease, and I don't have all the reminders and warnings that Windows OSs do.  

Unfortunately, in recent years, Apple has become increasingly controlling of its OS, its contents, and its applications.  And, it is beginning to set a precedence.  I predict in the near future that its desktop/laptop OS will replicate its iOS, which I'm starting to see with 10.7.  Cloud-based services and applications will evolve to the point of, and eventually prevail, and I will no longer "own" my software, but pay a replicating fee to enjoy the privilege of continuing to use it.  We see it now with iTunes, and similar net-based music and movie services.  The way of the CD/DVD, whether it be for entertainment or for commercial software, will eventually fall where enterprise is involved.  This is one reason I decided to start investigating Linux.  

I do wish you well with your Macintosh endeavors.  I agree with you to the effect of command-line interface is good for someone who has mastered it and remains familiar through day-to-day usage.  For someone like me who owns a small business, I currently have two choices: Manage my network and its computers myself, or pay someone to manage it for me.  Without a good and reliable background in CLI, in hind sight, it seems quite obvious which turned out to be the best alternative for me.

@ All: Here is an update on my experience with OS X.

Well, today is the 14th of Jan. 2012 and I've been using my Mac for over 6 months now so I feel I can give an enlightened opinion.

Truth be told, the Mac is rock solid. Apart from rare hangs with some specific programs like VirtualBox, it's been a smooth ride. It boots fast and it's responsive thanks to this :

1. the main HDD is actually an SSD
2. the processor is a workhorse
3. I regularly clean and sweep with Onyx and CCLeaner for Mac

1. the lack of visual customization I could achieve with Linux
2. the fact the VirtualBox and Linux don't fare well together on my OS X host (visual effects are sluggish even with Guest additions), no matter the distribution
3. the lack of a "Del" key on my European keyboard; I have to hit "fn"+"Backspace" to achieve the delete function.

So is a Mac worth YOUR money? In MY opinion, yes.
