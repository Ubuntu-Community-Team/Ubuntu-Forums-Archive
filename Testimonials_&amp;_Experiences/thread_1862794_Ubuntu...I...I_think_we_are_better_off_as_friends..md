### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-10-17
> **TurtleKing said:**
> Your recent decision to remove your icon and window theme customization is what lead me to tell you the following

Ubuntu did not remove the theme customisation. Gnome just never wrote one for Gnome 3. When Ubuntu moved across to Gnome 3, the old Appearance program stopped working.

Don't blame Ubuntu for this. You'll find the same on all Gnome 3 distros.

> 
So nobody seems to mind Ubuntu 11.10's lack of customization? And before everyone starts posting, "install gnome-tweak-tool!"...This requires the help of another program. We use to be able to do this by default folks. I don't see how this is different from Windows forcing programs by default down our throat. Sure you can download extra programs, but the freedom to have it upfront is what I came to love about Ubuntu. 

Let me tell you a story.

Before Ubuntu came around and became popular, all the other distributions came with multiple text editors, multiple image editors, and all sorts of things that end users would find irrelevent such as icon editors and speed synthesis programs.

Ubuntu cut out the clutter and focussed on providing a tight set of default programs. All the icon editors and multiple text editors were still installable, but not installed by default.

Other distributions do something similar now; they have fewer default programs with very little overlap between them.

Ubuntu became popular precisely because it shipped a sparse set of programs so you wouldn't feel overwhelmed with lots of similar programs that had no relevance for you.

You can install themes on Ubuntu, but there aren't many themes available for GTK 3. When you have them installed, you can change between them graphically without Gnome Tweak Tool.

You're drawing too long a bow with your assertion that "Now we don't have x by default, soon we won't even be able to INSTALL x!".

After all, we've still got Gimp in the repositories. There's even F-Spot.

### Post by kaldor on 2011-10-17
Remember that GNOME 3 (subsequently Shell and Unity) is very new. GNOME 3 came out barely 6 months ago, while GNOME 2 has been around since before Ubuntu's existence. 

Though, the lack of customization could be taken positively...

I prefer the ability to change things to better suit me. If my environment does these things by default then I am happy. Largely, GNOME Shell is what I want. I do, however wish I had the ability to tweak more things but I suspect that in the coming years GNOME 3 will end up just like GNOME 2.

But, then you have to see the customization from a UI perspective. These projects are trying to make the perfect and most enjoyable experience. Have you noticed how many Linux desktops look extremely messy and broken? Take away the ability to do that, and the user will possibly have a better experience once they adjust. Here's an example of what I am talking about:

[img]http://ubuntuone.com/p/s16/[/img]

The above has two docks and lots of recurring stuff which is of no use. Does the user really need these things? It's easy to see why customization is not a priority at the time being. 

I'm just looking at it from two different perspectives. It doesn't bother me to an extreme, because overall I enjoy the defaults. I don't want to be locked into doing just one thing, though.

That said I wish the UI team for Unity would realize some very real issues with the usability of Unity as compared to other environments. The concerns for Unity are often just "I hate change! Unity sucks!" but there are some very valid complaints. I use Shell because it doesn't constantly cripple me and get in my way.

Have fun in whatever direction you choose to go :)
