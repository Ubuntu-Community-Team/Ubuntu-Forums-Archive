### Post by akarimco on 2008-02-01
So here I am, for the first time ever, with a fully Linux machine.  Up until now, I've always had a Windows partition somewhere, but not any more.

Be forewarned, I'm feeling chatty, and I'm about to ramble on and on about my usage of Linux over the years... I make no promises of coherence or understandability.  ;)

So it all started in the fall of 1999.  I was a fresh college student learning about dorky things in my computer science classes, and someone went and mentioned Linux.  So, I spent the next week downloading a copy of Caldera Linux (yay for dial-up).  I do believe I was greeted by KDE 1, which I hated.  My modem didn't work, my sound didn't work, my monitor wouldn't go above 800x600... I hated it.

Then, a few years ago, right around the time Mandrake turned into/spun off into/whatever happened into Mandriva, I gave Mandriva a try.  Long story short, once again I didn't care for the experience.  My next foray into Linux was with the release of openSuSE 10.0, and this was officially the first Linux I ever learned to use on a day-to-day basis.  Learning how to use programs like Yast taught me a few things about how Linux works, and once I understood some of what goes on under the hood, it started to become easier.  Unfortunately, I still had nagging hardware problems that kept me from going full-time.

Eventually, I kept hearing about this thing called Ubuntu.  I was honestly put off by the name itself for quite a while, not even curious to know what it was.  Eventually, thanks to a strong following on Digg that continues to this day, I found out that it was an extremely popular Linux distro known for being easy and streamlined.  I thought back to how SuSE had like four web browsers and five media programs, and that was one of the reasons I didn't care for Linux in general was because of the excess of variety.  That much variety needs to get looked at and analyzed before it's just thrown at you, which is what Ubuntu did.  They gave you the basics and then said, "here, now go through this software catalog and get anything you want."  My days of cursing Yast were over!

It was right at the release of Dapper when I jumped on the bandwagon.  I was amazed at how simple everything seemed compared to other Linux distributions.  It took an afternoon to wrap my head around dependencies and repositories for SuSE... in Ubuntu, it just all took care of itself... it was all there already, and eventually when I added more repos, it took about a minute to figure it out.

This was also my first experience at any length with Gnome, which I've fallen in love with.  To me, Gnome just has this simple, polished look, a very intuitive system that's easy to make beautiful.  I never really cared for KDE... I always felt that it had too cartoonish of a look, and to this day, even with 4.0, it still reminds me of those horrible experiences I had with Linux in the beginning... I dunno, it just looks like it was made by a bunch of bad web designers.

Today, I have Gutsy running beautifully on my computer.  Prior to that, I was running Vista, which to my surprise actually ran much faster on this computer than XP did... perhaps some of those who've made a point to avoid Vista should actually try it out... I kinda liked it, myself.  Unfortunately, it's still Windows, and after installing some little program that sent my system into an eternal loop of BSODs (I thought those were supposed to be damn near impossible any more), I said, "screw it!"

The only trace of Windows left is a Win2K VM running in VirtualBox, and that's only because my music software, ModPlug Tracker, won't run through Wine.  Gosh, I can remember trying to use Wine back when I tried Mandriva... it wouldn't run anything I tried.  Then imagine my amazement when years later I tried running Google's Picasa (before the Linux version), and it worked, smooth animations and all.  Now if only they could just get ModPlug to work, I could be totally Windows free!

Of course, I still had a few problems at the start.  My Nvidia graphics card didn't want to work with my TV's 1360x768 resolution, but I no longer have that TV and am using a regular monitor again, and to Ubuntu's credit, I did fix the problem through a solution in the forums, though it did have some weird side effects, like warping the aspect ratio whenever I watched a video full screen.

I know, this is like something I should be posting to my blog, not here, but nobody reads my blog (I like it that way), and I know that if I'd spent hours and hours working hard on something I loved, it would make me feel pretty good to hear that someone else loved it too.  Long-story-short, I love Ubuntu, and I'm proud to call it my one and only OS of choice.  I think there should be a page of nothing but praise from people... perhaps someone should start that in the wiki, or is there one already?
