If you want something more like Windows, install Kubuntu. When you open a media player or a web browser it'll prompt you to install the codecs/plugins suggested for full media playback on the net or in your media player.

It also looks a bit like Windows, just a bit more advanced. Unfortunately, it doesn't have the Ubuntu Software Center to my knowledge, but it's a very simple process to get these things up and going in Gnome (Ubuntu).

I'm quite certain that if you were installing another OS on your computer from scratch, you'd have a lot more issues to deal with than installing the restricted extras (which aren't installed by default because of legal reasons). You can always get an Ubuntu computer so everything is set up for you, just like most people experience Windows computers, but the setup of Ubuntu is usually negligibly simple. It's always a good idea to look up a video or tutorial for your first time, though, since you might not wanna' play around and find out where everything is at first. XD

### Post by QIII on 2009-10-27
First, I would like to welcome you to the forums, and I hope you will not walk away before letting us lend a hand!

Let me elaborate on MightyJoe.

Windows and Linux are like English and German.

You grew up speaking English (or your native language, whatever that may be).  It seems "dummy friendly" to you because it is native.  Speaking Windows in terms of installing programs and such is second nature to you.

To do that in Linux requires the same departure from what is comfortable that learning to speak German does.

Neither English nor German is any better.  They are simply different.

Those of us who "speak" Linux find things to be comfortable.

Please don't leave without giving Linux a good try.

Use the forums to address one issue at a time.  Work that one.  Then move to the next.  That is the best way to learn the new language.  

One thing to understand about Linux distributions (in this case Ubuntu):  What you get is a basic framework for creating a system of your own.  It would be somewhat like a car dealer giving you a basic car, plus all the extra parts and tools for turning it into the baddest hot rod on the block.  For free.

But you will have to learn how to use the tools and how to fit the pieces together.

With Windows, you get what you get and have limited ability to alter your "user experience".  Like Henry "Windows" Ford said:  "Any customer can have a car painted any color that he wants so long as it is black."  In Microsoft's case, that's blue.
