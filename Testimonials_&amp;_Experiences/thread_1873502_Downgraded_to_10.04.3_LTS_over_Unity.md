### Post by 2F4U on 2011-11-01
Well, that depends on what you are looking for. In LTS, software is updated less frequently and you seldom, if ever, get newer versions. What you get are security updates and you get them for a much longer time than for regular releases. That bring stability and predictability. For example, for 12.04, Canonical announced five years of support for the desktop and the server versions and 10.04 has support for three years (server: five years).
So, if your aim is stability and you don't have fancy hardware, you can go for 10.04. If you need the latest bells and whistles, go for a newer version.

### Post by steve8track on 2011-11-01
I really feel the need to vent about Unity and Gnome 3 both.

I switched to linux from windows probably about 4.5-5 years ago.  I was enchanted with compiz fusion (then called beryl before the merger).  In windows, I always thought eye candy was a waste of resources and would turn them all off after getting a computer.  In linux, I would turn all of the eye candy on because it made the desktop feel like I was flowing between my work and I could set keyboard shortcuts easily for anything.

There was a little thing here and there that bothered me in the transition between beryl and compiz, but overall I was a happy camper.

Ubuntu just kept getting better and better, more and more user friendly out of the box, and things would just work.

Ubuntu reached what I feel was it's best moment with 10.10.  It was a perfect 10.

Then I upgraded to 11.04.  Unity wouldn't even render correctly on my computer, with serious refresh problems.  I hated the search driven paradigm as compared to the traditional menu driven way of finding applications.  Searching for applications by name didn't make sense when I don't always know what I've got installed.  I wanted to put myself out there and learn the new stuff and give it a good chance, but it was too buggy and I needed my computer to be usable.  Unfortunately simply booting into the classic desktop wasn't enough to make my computer usable again, as firefox and firebug were unstable and themed for use with Unity.  I had to switch to Chrome (which I love now, but as Firefox was the default, I don't think it should be so unpolished in an official release).

I switched to classic mode.  Then 11.11 came along and the update script didn't reset my window decoration to have the close button on the left.  Since 11.11 by default doesn't have a way to change the theming, I was in a state where maximized windows would have their buttons suddenly move from top right to top left.  I wasn't able to easily add shortcuts, and I couldn't customize the look of unity very much.  I couldn't make icons smaller than ridiculously big for a non-touch-screen desktop.  Compiz was set up so it became more difficult to switch to the cube from the wall to switch desktops.  Everything was in new locations.  Many customization features were removed.  It felt like a touch-screen OS forced on users for a desktop.  I also have always disliked the Mac-like menus at the top of the screen.  It requires you to move the mouse across the screen to get to functionality for the current window, even if that window is in the bottom of the screen.

Then I decided to try Gnome 3.  It was just as ballooned (big touch buttons) as Unity, only without my beloved Compiz.  Both waste valuable realestate in large buttons and especially the bar across the top that is largly blank most of the time, even when Unity puts menus in them.

In addition to this, Ubuntu doesn't feel polished like it had in 2010 when getting a major release.  I've always found some bugs, but this is rediculous.  Gedit had bugs with setting it's custom tools.  Nautilus no longer has an up arrow and would crash when opening more than one window.  Compiz was very unstable with the UI changes when trying to change settings.  Unity took forever to load.  The resources needed were much greater with no added benefits.  I write plugins for gedit, and pyGTK has changed but has little new documentation while Gedit has always lacked valuable documentation (I had to inspect everything in the python console to figure things out).

I develop in Java for a living and I started having problems with java compiling until I figured out that openJDK was installed and needed to be removed for Sun's Java.

I decided I needed some lighter weight environment that followed a more traditional approach.  I swiched to Lubuntu as my desktop environment.  There was still a learning curve and I had to do more with configuring in text files.  I ran into bugs and configuration issues I needed to fix, but overall I've been much more pleased with it than Unity or Gnome 3.

I've been singing Ubuntu's praises for years now, telling all of my friends to switch to it, and even trying to contribute to Linux a little by writing the gedit plugins and trying to answer questions on these forums.  However, lately I have been in some serious discomfort with Ubuntu and it's much harder for me to recommend it to people with how upset I am lately.  I seriously question the direction the UI is taking in linux in general.  I support the idea of making UIs that are apt at touch interfaces, but not for my desktop environment.  I also think Gnome 3 would be fine as a competitor to Gnome 2, but not as a predicessor.  I think demand should have driven people to Gnome 3, not the dropping of support for Gnome 2.  If they had made another desktop environment to compete on equal grounds as Gnome 2 and KDE, then I would say let the best environment win, but instead they wiped out their competition.

I'm hoping that people will switch to LXDE, XFCE, and KDE and increase their support so I'm not stuck with a Gnome 3 or Unity like interface.  Our computers could be so much snappier if we didn't bloat them down unnecessarily.  I understand new features can require higher system specs, but there comes a point when it's just bloat and adds nothing to the experience.  We should look to trim out the fat and refine our tools to give them an edge.

I am very tempted to downgrade to Ubuntu 10.10 (if I can find an iso anymore) or switch distros to Lubuntu, Fedora, or something else.
