### Post by jasontu on 2008-06-24
I have extensively used MS-DOS, Windows 3.1 through XP, and Apple OS 7.5 through X over the past few years both in a professional and a home environment, but Ubuntu 8.04 is my first foray into serious daily use of Linux.

Prior to this I tried SuSE 9 for about a week, Mandrake for about a day, DSLinux only momentarily to debug a laptop, I had Xandros loaded on an old laptop I barely used for long while... All were pretty impressive desktops.  I was frankly impressed with what linux could do for everday web-surfing and office stuff.

The main shortcoming every time though, was the inability to play games and use those off-off brand pieces of hardware I would sometimes find for dirt cheap.  It was disappointing (still is) to see how little the platform was supported.

My discovery of Wine a couple of years ago made me hopeful.  As silly as it is, I honestly think that once Linux can achieve full interoperability with Windows, people will start to port commercial software for it.  I know it's ironic, but you won't get people to switch until they can use their Windows software, and companies won't write Linux software until people have switched.

I recently was given some PC hardware and it cost little to buy the rest of what was needed for a complete system.  

I was a little nervous about what OS to get.  People were acting like Microsoft was going to try to force everyone off XP, and Vista was receiving mixed reviews.  Plus $130 is a lot to drop on an OS I used to be able to get for $15 in college.  

I decided to give Linux a shot with the hope that Wine was developed enough to run some games relatively painlessly.  It was actually a guide on how to run WoW on Linux that mentioned Ubuntu and made me seek it out.

I figured... if it works, great.  If it doesn't, I can always grab Windows later.

Things didn't work perfect at first.  They were good enough to compel me to keep pushing until things were working just right.  Ubuntu is now the only OS on my desktop.  It is the only OS that has ever been on that desktop.  The desktop looks good.  Wine runs WoW almost flawlessly.  OpenOffice works well.  Updates are a snap.

It also helps that orange is my favorite color.

### Post by jasontu on 2008-06-30
I agree that Open Source is definitely the way to go if you're looking for productive software.  There's no reason why an OS, an Office Suite, a web browser, graphic art, website creation, video creation - all shouldn't be open source.  If enough people are willing to develop that for free and do it fast enough to make those free file formats the widely accepted defaults, awesome.  When I talk about companies developing software for the Linux platform, though, I'm mostly thinking about games.  There are a lot of Open source games out there, for sure.  MMOs cost money to run, and some game studios would prefer to actually make some money off of their work.  That's a creative project that I am willing to pay for.  If an Open source game is given to me for free... well thank you.  That's good too.

Sure, Microsoft Windows/Office is a creative endeavor as well, and I don't begrudge Microsoft any innovation that it has brought.  I won't begrudge paying Microsoft for their work.  My main issue with the company is the constant sense of ownership of basically anything that has to do with computers.  They shouldn't have to be so predatory and ridiculous about everything if they were just confident in their work.  

I use Ubuntu because it's an OS that works very well, and it's free.  Sure it takes some tweaking - sometimes hours of tweaking - but I learn a lot along the way.  If Microsoft offered their work at a price that was commeasurate with what they had to offer above the free alternatives, it would make sense for me to buy it.  If Microsfot could honestly come up to me and tell me, "Hey, we have a great product that is secure, has great hardware support, we'll help you work out your computer problems, it will help your system run a little faster, and it has a proven record for stability.  All we ask for this is $50."  Sure.  I'd pay for that.  But that's not what they have to offer.  

Vista isn't terribly stable.  It eats up your resources like crazy.  The driver support isn't acceptable for what supposedly is going to be THE operating system for the next few years.  As for XP - they are trying to pull the plug on supporting it as quickly as possible.  As for support for either of these?  Well... you basically have to either pay for it (let me sell you something that is broken and then you pay me to help you fix it) or you just root around web for a forum that may have the solution to your problem.  All this for the low low price of $200?  

I really hope that Ubuntu can take advantage of this though.  Here's the market break up I'd like to see in the next five years Windows 80%, Apple OSX 15%, Linux 5%.  I think a 5% market share is a reasonable goal and very possible as Ubuntu becomes more polished and makes its way onto more store-bought desktops and workplaces.
