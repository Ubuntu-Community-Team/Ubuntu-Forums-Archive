### Post by Manyette on 2010-05-25
Some months back in response to a "what do you want to see in Lucid", I said that I hoped it would concentrate on being stable, rather than a lot of fixes after release.  Someone assured me that it wouldn't be a problem.  That I just hadn't dealt with an LTS release as yet.

About that same time, a question was asked about whether the fixed release schedule should be kept, or altered.

Well, now that 10.04 is here, and I've had a while to use it, those two questions have been answered; to my mind at least. This release was very obviously rushed at the last minute, fixes appeared within 24 hours of the release, Evolution is broken,  Screen display on boot and shutdown have been screwed up, which was not a problem in Karmic.  MD5 sums weren't posted until hours after the torrent download had completed.  In short, it simply was not ready on release date.  And compared to others comments in the forums, I was rather one of the luckier ones of the lot, since it seems everyone has Evolution problems worse than mine.

In short, I'm about to restore my Karmic backup, and wait for the next LTS with the hope it will be better.  This one has been a disaster.  Just one man's opinion.

It is still my contention that a fixed release date is the wrong way to go, especially on an LTS release.

"Never be the first or last one in the community to adopt a new technology."

I have never seen an operating system of any type that doesn't have bugs upon its initial release. Ever.

Karmic had a few doozies (the system temperature monitor problems, the GRUB2 problems), but Jaunty had the graphics driver problems, Intrepid had a new desktop (at least for KDE) and wireless problems, Hardy didn't have a whole lot of wireless drivers to begin with, and so on as far back as I can remember.

But they all get sorted out in about a month. Plymouth (the bootup/startup screen manager) doesn't work for me on any of my (9) computers in Lucid, and I've had a few minor networking glitches (where some programs can't tell whether the primary adapter is eth0 or wlan0).

All in all, though, I must say that Lucid has had the least number of big glitches (and I only waited 3 weeks after release to install it, this time).

Still, I always advise newbies to never install a new OS right after the new release. 

Altering the release schedule won't make a bit of difference. The Debian and Ubuntu core communities are at best a few thousand people, but there are millions of users all encountering different bugs and limitations. Until a certain number of those users are online with the new release, the bugs simply won't be discovered, no matter how long you wait.
