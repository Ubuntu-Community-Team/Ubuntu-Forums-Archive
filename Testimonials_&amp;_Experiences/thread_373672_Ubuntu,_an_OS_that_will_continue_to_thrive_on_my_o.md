### Post by cubanresourceful on 2007-03-01
Ubuntu, a word I had known for a long time. Linux even longer. This is the testimonial of me, Mario.

It began like this. Two weeks back, more or less, my laptop, poor thing, started lagging. Im talking not normal lag, but some serious, head-breaking, nerve-wrecking lag. Let me give you an example, to start up, from pressing the power button, to the password screen, until I can actually use Windows XP Professional, took from 7 minutes on a lucky day, to 15 minutes on, what I call "bad" days. The time to shut down, took longer than me eating dinner and a shower, more or less. Im talking about more than 20 minutes here, whether it was a lucky day or not. Enough was enough, I couldn't believe my 2001 Pentium 4 2.20 GHz M processor cannot handle simple tasks like turning on and off? Oh, and if I wanted to browse the web, listening to music through MusikCube was NOT an option. My music would skip more than a cd tha was dragged across a street of spikes. That, my friends, is how bad it was.

I heard Linux was the BEST OS to have. It was the thing, but wasn't for people who didn't want to get technical. I remember burning a distro of Debian, the installer only, I think back then Debian didn't have a LiveCD to test on. I popped it in, and was greated by a screen, saying something like 5.01 or something for the version. I hoped it wasn't to far from recent, even though I knew it was a few years old. But, for some reason, the install didn't progress after around 50% of analyzing my system. I then recalled why I never bothered to install it, it was the simple fact that it wouldn't install. I then saw openSUSE, with a windows looking installer. I was intrigued.

So I installed it, had it for a day, and instantly did NOT like it. RPMs were hell, when you were missing dependencies. I went to a forum, and asked if a libpack existed for me, and someone suggested Ubuntu. I asked why, and they say they use the DEB format for packaging. Which automatically resolves dependencies for you. I instantly searched for Ubuntu, and downloaded the DVD LiveCD + Install. As soon as I installed it, I was completely satisfied. I especially preferred the GNOME interface Ubuntu comes with over openSUSE's version, this one was FAR superior.

But, again, as usual, I was running into a brick wall. I got drivers working for my NVIDIA card working easily enough in openSUSE, but in Ubuntu, it wasn't as easy as pie with cheese. Most tutorials told me different methods of installation, most of them telling me to uninstall either the restricted-modules or the nvidia-glx. So, I uninstalled both, but no 3D! I then downloaded ENVY, and had it do it for me. No cigars compadre. Either it would result in a black screen fading into whiteness, (which I can log in blindly, and even hear that I logged in successfully), or it would work, but no 3D rendering. I know if I did or not, because I had a game called Chromium that utilized 3D rendering. I then did something, that ENVY did the opposite. I installed the legacy driver 7824 or something like that with ENVY. As you all know, ENVY uninstalls nvidia-glx-legacy, and I noticed that. So, I was like, what the heck. So, before logging in, I used the command line to get nvidia-glx-legacy and install it. And vista! I had 3D rendering.

Now, I am in Linux heaven. Remember before how my computer would take a long time to turn off and on? Well, here are my times with a stopwatch.
Login-in to usability (Usability determined by opening Firefox, and it fully loading) - 1:34
Turn-off - 1:21

Now, those times are MUCH more normal and acceptable. But it would be cool to load up in a second, right? ;)

Ubuntu does it for me, and I am probably going to be a LONG-TERM user. Windows, I now despise for its slowness. And Mac, well, I don't really like it. Thanks Ubuntu, Community, and Developers! :D

### Post by angryfirelord on 2007-03-01
Glad to see that Ubuntu is working as well for you as it is for me! 

One thing I should clarify. RPMs & DEBs do not resolve dependencies for you. They just help install programs easier. For rpm based system you use yum & on deb-based, you use apt-get (in general). Both programs resolve dependencies well. The advantage that deb & apt-get have is that programs tend to install cleaner and faster than rpms do, and if there's a dependency error, debs tend to be more friendly.

Sit back & enjoy the ubuntu effect! :popcorn:
