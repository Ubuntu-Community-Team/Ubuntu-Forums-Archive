### Post by exploder on 2012-09-03
I have always had some interest in Unity because I liked the idea of Ubuntu developing it's own user interface. I have had more than my share of complaints about Ubuntu in the past but things have changed and in a positive way to me. I have had eye problems my whole life and have had 6 eye operations as a result. I had been using my favorite KDE distro but when my inter-occular implant dislodged and had to be surgically removed I could hardly see to use KDE. I was not able to do much to make KDE usable with 20/300 vision.

I had burned a Ubuntu 12.04 x64 disk before my surgery and it was lucky for me that I did. Ubuntu 12.04's default theme was usable for me for the most part, sure there were some things I could not see but I could get by. Ubuntu has changed it's policies about Firefox and Thunderbird updates and that was something that was always important to me. 12.04 seemed very stable and I found myself in a situation where I just wanted everything to work and having the latest, greatest applications was less important with my sight being what it was at the time.

Unity has come a long ways in a short amount of time. I liked the original netbook remix that Oz Unity used by the way but I really like Unity in 12.04. I read far too many negative posts about Unity and feel that many of them are unfounded. Many are just unwilling to learn new things and do things different. Unity is a work in progress after all and I see new refinements coming in the future. I realize some people are always going to complain no matter what but I think that over time people are going to come around to liking Unity.

My sight is back to normal after a very lengthy recovery but I have decided to keep Ubuntu 12.04 on two of my computers. I may end up having another eye operation to remove scar tissue from my retina and Unity has proven to be very helpful to me. My biggest problem with Ubuntu has always been graphics drivers but the LTS works fine on the two machines I have it installed on and 5 years of support makes it a great choice for me.

I have made other positive posts regarding Unity. People complained in the past that Ubuntu was not doing their own thing and they still complain when they do! Out of all of the various shells that have come into play since the introduction of Gnome 3, Unity is my favorite. I like Cinnamon and there is nothing wrong with people wanting a traditional desktop but I think Unity will help to provide Linux more of a future on the desktop.

New users seem to really take to the Unity interface because it has a Mac like elegance to it. The idea of lenses and scopes really sold me because it is a fresh approach. I have seen the refinements coming to Unity in 12.10 and I like how the interface is receiving some nice refinements. I figure that by the time the next LTS release rolls around Unity is going to surpass the MacOS in elegance and features. 

I do see more people that did not like Unity at first beginning to appreciate it more now and I hope the trend continues. Instead of complaining, why not put some effort into testing and making it better. I really feel that Canonical deserves some recognition these days for what they have accomplished.

### Post by exploder on 2012-09-03
I think that the lenses just give you a different way of doing things. Really, I think lenses just add a little bling so to speak and look how far Apple has gone with a little bling.

The traditional desktop just was not gaining Linux much popularity, not that there was anything wrong with it. Unity is getting people interested in Linux that have never tried it and game developers are starting to take notice. Android has helped to make Linux more popular and Unity is doing the same.

My Dad gave up on Linux a few years ago but he has changed his mind since using my computer with 12.04 installed on it. My Daughter uses a Mac and she was also impressed with Unity. I have installed 12.04 for a few people that have never used Linux and they love it, this tells me that Unity stands a chance of attracting new users. 

I think too that if you give someone a distribution to try with a traditional desktop they expect it to be just like Windows. When I give someone a 12.04 disk to try they seem more receptive to learning something new because it does not resemble Windows in any way. I also find that most people do not want to try something that resembles Windows because they want something completely different.

These are all just my opinions and experiences but I really think that Unity can bring in more users and help to make Linux more popular.

### Post by ojdon on 2012-09-03
One thing I thought about the other day when talking about the Unity Interface to a friend was how it was a "No Fuss" interface for Ubuntu. Yes, I know, some users like to customize the crap out of their Ubuntu DE. But that isn't always unnecessary. For example, if I wanted to just do a quick install of Ubuntu on a laptop, I don't want to then spend hours getting the interface to be productive. This is why Unity is so useful. They've thought about productivity. For example, the launcher on the left hand side of the screen is a great idea. Most computer displays are widescreen so it's making use of that form factor and trying to free as much valuable vertical screen space as possible. 

Talking about the launcher, it's useful that all my most common applications (Chrome, Nautilus, Sublime Text, Eclipse) are all pinned down the left hand side, it means then that I don't have to go through the hassle of searching through a million different application sub menus, they are just a single click away, not hidden what so ever!
