### Post by gshockxc on 2008-11-25
It was the summer of 2007.  Sitting in the office, I overheard a conversation between my company's IT administrator and a co-worker.  They were discussing the never-ending failures and pitfalls of Windows, despite it's widespread use and, (er... um...) popularity.  If something gets used a lot, does that necessarily make it popular?  We all use toothpaste and toilet paper (I hope) but does that make them the hottest things since sliced bread?  But, I digress...  So there I sit, listening, and nodding to the conversation taking place just on the other side of my cubicle, nodding amicably to those criticisms of Windows.  Then my co-worker mentions that his son is deeply involved in a relationship with a linux system, and my attention is sharply focused on all that he has to say.  Unfortunately, the engineering programs that we were using, AutoCAD, 3-D modeling, etc, are not built to be run on a linux system (wait, don't say it).  

My co-worker goes on at length to describe how wonderful linux is, in particular Ubuntu.  While still listening to the conversation, I furiously perform a Google search and start reading all about Ubuntu and the wonders that lay before me.  I purused the Absolute Beginner Talk, and started reading the posts, asking questions, ... and I haven't even downloaded the software yet!

Days go by, and I continue to waste time at work surfing the Ubuntu forum.  Careful not to be too derelict in my duties, I do manage to get my job done, but not before simultaneously downloading the .iso so that I can steal away after work and install it on my machine at home.  Hastily, I rushed home to install the software, only to find out that you can't boot from an image file.  Darn, this foolish Ubuntu!  Back to work the next day to download the Infra Recorder to burn the Image file.  Days later, after finally completing the burn, at last... Success!!!  Ah ha!  Bill Gates, you will have no more control over me!  I will take control of my computer hardware!

Rats!  Foiled again.  I'm running an AMD processor in my machine, and I downloaded the i386 version of Gutsy Gibbon.  Darn, this infernal linux 'thing.'  Why does this have to be so complicated?

Back to work the next day to download the right version of Ubuntu.  But why stop there?  Why not download them ALL?  That's right.  Let me download ALL of the Ubuntu versions and variations.  I'll get the 7.04 for the AMD64, and the Alternative; then the Kubuntu 7.04 for the AMD64, and the alternative; and just for kicks, we'll do the Xubuntu for the AMD64, and the Alternative for that TOO!

After all the posts I've read, and all the successes, this thing has got to be the best thing since sliced bread!  Sure, some people complain, but not me.  They are just too impatient.  They want something that "isn't" like Windows, but really "is."  I'm not like that.  I really want to learn.  I want to get into the guts of the system and sudo -rf this... and rm -rf that, and do all kinds of terminal window keystrokes ... just like they do in the movies.  That's how it really happens, right?  It's just like that.  You just put in the CD, install the software, and suddenly, you're a hacker.  Insta-geek!

After weeks of trying to get the right installation, trying a couple versions on an old Hitachi Visionbook Pro, and finally convincing myself, "This is it.  I'm going to break, up with ol' Bill Gates once and for all.  This time, I really mean it.  I'm through playing around.  I'm going to do a clean install, delete everything, and start from scratch.  Clean slate ... er, except for my music, that is... oh, and my videos too... and, oh, those engineering documents that I created in grad school.  Oh, wait, there's no program to run those in Ubuntu.  Oh well, I'll figure something out... oh, wait, there's that other program that I don't know how to run in linux... and that one too."

Exhaustion.  Frustration.  The inevitable, "Darn, this isn't as easy as I thought it was going to be.  Darn, this infernal linux stuff.  No wonder Bill Gates always wins!  He just makes it too easy to give up.  Oh well, maybe another day, another time, I'll come back around and be better prepared to make the switch."  

And with that, I admitted defeat.  Not that Ubuntu had be beaten me, nor that it even tried.  I had beaten myself.  In my haste for something better, I forgot to ask what it was that I really wanted.  I just wanted not to have Windows anymore.  I didn't know anything more than that.  Wanting change just for the sake of change isn't a good reason to do something (in my opinion).

And so... I put down the Ubuntu CD's and left them in a box until September of this year.  My trusty ole computer system, still with the AMD Athlon64 1.4 GHz processor, still knocking around.  Having convinced myself that I really couldn't do much with the system because the CPU kept over heating (I couldn't run it if the indoor temps were above 65°F), I decided to see if I could resurrect it and just use it for tinkering, teaching myself some computer skills with the full intention that I would break something and have to start from scratch with new hardware.  Despite my conviction of an impending hardware failure, I was certain that it I would learn something important in the process.  

First, I went out and bought a nice, new flat screen monitor.  I hooked up an old keyboard and a mouse, and I flipped the switch.  "I'll be darned.  This thing is still booting up XP!  Wow, what a tank!"  So I tinkered with some Winamp music files, played around with some personal documents, used Open Office, tinkered for a couple of weeks.  "Hmmm, I wonder if I can resolve that overheating issue."  Nah, probably not.  I'll just start from scratch.  I'll just build a new one... :?  I've never built one completely on my own before, but I saw it done once.  I'm sure it's not that hard.  I still remember most of it.  Get the hardware, get it home, ... let it sit.  No time to deal with it now.  Postponed again...

Pick it up again a few months later.  Decide to give it another try.  Finally found the time.  And off I go to solve my first big technical challenge.  I found a hardware forum that I've breezed by on occasion, and started asking some questions: "How does the paste go on the chipset? Oh, you don't call it a chipset?  Oh, it's a CPU or a processor?  Okay, got it.  So how do you put the paste on there?  Should it be a lot?  How much?  Do I need to take the old stuff off?  Is that bad?  Oh, I see."

So I pick up some thermal compound, and reapply the stuff to the silicon die on the chip ... er CPU, and then reapply the heat sink (without removing the OLD thermal compound).  "There's a small rectangle where the die contacted the heat sink from before.  I can just put the heatsink right back on that spot and it will still transfer heat just fine, right?  Because the paste transmits the heat, right?  That's why it's got that silver stuff in it, isn't it?"  

After installing Motherboard Monitor 5, and monitoring the CPU temps, I'm disheartened to find that my first attempt was unsuccessful.  Still, spurred on by the grotesque notion that I'm going to seriously damage some piece of hardware in the learning process, and being completely convinced that nothing more can be done to save this CPU from cooking itself at 150°F regularly, I soldier on to try and fix the problem.  But in reality, I fully expect to have to spend some money to remedy the situation.  "When stuff breaks, you just buy a new piece, plug it in, and it's fixed.  Right?"  Isn't that was Windows teaches us?  

So I make a few more posts on the hardware forum, ask some more questions, slightly more educated than the last, but less informed in some ways... "What CPU's can I get that will fit a socket 939 board?"  Back come the replies, "Well, that's old.  Not much... you can do this, or that, limited choices... etc."
Hmmmm, more questions.  "Wait a minute!  You said that you have motherboard 'Y', but that came with socket A.  You said it was socket 939.  Either you've got the wrong processor, or the wrong board."  Ooops.  My bad.  Socket 939 was the computer I had 4 years ago.  Oh, sorry.  :oops:  More questions.  Post again.  "What about ...?"  Something sparks a thought, "Hey, wait a minute!  Should I clean all that old thermal compund off, clean the silicon die and the heatsink with isopropyl, and reapply a LITTLE thermal compound?"  Yeah, that might work.  What's the worst that can happen?  I could blow the CPU.  I expected that anyway.  The fact that I've been watching DVD's on my computer with the CPU running at 145°F +/- is a testament to itself that it's a pretty durable little processor.  What the heck.  Can't get any worse, right?  

Gently, I remove the heatsink and CPU.  First, I clean the silicon die with the alcohol, then the heatsink.  Hmmm, the old thermal paste seems like gum, and it doesn't want to come off.  "What if I scrape it?"  Careful, don't want to dig up the base.  That would be bad for heat transfer.  Gently, digging at it with a flat-blade screwdriver (razor blades were not available, and probably not recommended).  Slowly, the gum-like substance comes off.  "Easy, a little at a time.  Don't force it.  That's it.  Just take your time.  Almost there.  Oops, tilted the blade too much.  Caught the edge, slight knick.  Darn, I hope that will be okay.  Hmmm, the rest doesn't want to come off.  Well, I've come this far.  I can't just leave it."  

So off I go to the kitchen to get a scouring pad and remove the rest with a little warm water and soap.  Standing over the bathroom sink, I'm thinking, "The folks on the 'forum' would probably shoot me if they knew I was scrubbing my heatsink with a 'Brillo' pad.  I hope no one finds out about this."  Clean it up, check for imperfections.  The base seems smooth.  "Hmmm, maybe that scouring wasn't such a bad thing after all.  Let's hope I got lucky."  Small drop of thermal compound (as I learned to call it).  Spread it out nice and thin, even coat, that's it.  Take your time.  Don't rush it.  You've got all night.  There's no hurry."  Gently, place the CPU into the socket, mount the heat sink, clamp down the assembly... "Ah, there.  Nicely done.  I doubt it will do much good, but I cleaned something and didn't break it!  Score one for me!  Yeah!!!  \\:D/  So what if I scrubbed it with a 'Brillo' pad.  

Connect the cables, plug it in, press the button... hold breath.
"I'll be darned.  It works!  Well, would ya look at that?  Hey, there's the Motherboard Monitor, ... WOW, would you look at those numbers ... 111°!!!  I don't believe it!!  You mean it worked?"  :shock:  "Hey, let's play a DVD and see what happens... WOW... 118°F  It can't be!  Is that for real?"  :shock:

Hmmm.  Maybe I can figure this stuff out after all.  Well, now that I've got my old computer working, maybe I can take my time and figure out how to build my new one.  Yeah, how about that linux thing I was going to do a while back....  This seems like a good time to get going on that.

Get the new case, hardware, one at a time.  Take it easy... not too fast.  There's no rush.  A couple of things here, Power Supply there, remove the brackets, install the fans, "Hmmm, looks pretty cool.  I hope it works when I'm done.  If not, I've got a really cool looking paperweight."  A couple more pieces, a few more things, almost done... oh, shoot.  I wasn't expecting to get that other system working.  Now I don't have a CD or DVD to load the Ubuntu software.  Darn.  Oh, nuts.  No floppy drive, either.  Oh well, back to Newegg.  Spend more money.  "Yeah, but you expected to spend money, so you're still ahead of the game.  You've got one working system, and you're building another."  Hmmm, good point there.  

Find the drives... read reviews... "Hmmm, this one mentions Ubuntu.  Wonder if the hardware has any issues.  Better go back to the forum and find out.  Haven't been there in a while.  Nice to see what's going on.  Kind of miss that place.  "Ah, Ubuntu Forums."  Sign in, check the posts.  "Oh, there it is.  Just like I remember it, **Absolute Beginner Talk**."  Wow, I really miss that place.  Check the SEARCH, and see what's new.  Look for some related posts, throw up a couple of my own. 

"Hmmm, seems like I'm on the right track, but I don't know.  What the heck.  I don't care if I am or not.  I'm just here to learn.  And with that, I started back into, yet, my 3rd attempt, to free myself from the confines of the Windows operating system.  Is Windows bad?  No, probably not.  I'm sure that there are many people out there who are very grateful that they don't have to get into the inner workings of the operating system and try to figure out what's not working, or what needs to be changed.

For me, it became less about being impatient, and getting to the end.  Now, it's more about the journey.  I want to make mistakes, because that's how I learn.  Maybe I do some foolish things.  Maybe I go against the grain, or don't think things through all the way.  Maybe I post a question, and some responses come back, "Well, did you think of...?"  No, I didn't, and right away, I want to kick myself.  Why didn't I think of that?  It's so obvious!  Sure, it is... if you've done this before.  But I haven't...

And that's what it feels like to be a Noob.

p.s. what are "TAGS" used for?  I still haven't been able to figure that one out.

### Post by scorp123 on 2008-11-25
You should have seen Linux back in the mid-90's ... CD-ROM? *.iso file? What's that?? :)

We had to download 10 or so floppy disks and go with that. First insert the "boot" floppy (because that one had the "LILO" boot loader; the precursor to modern GRUB ...), then when booting halts insert the "root" disk (because that's where "/" and /etc were located), then insert the "modules #1" disk (because that's where all the drivers were located; corresponds to /lib/modules/ ...) .... and if you got lucky you'd finally get to a text based shell prompt (no installers back then!) where you could type a few series of commands and try and get the thing installed onto your super-duper big 200 MB (yes, that's megs!) hard drive. A complete Linux installation incl. the GUI would fit just under ~150 MB.

Oh, and "automatic recognition of hardware" ... what the heck is that? You want your hardware recognised? Fine then, the "gcc" compiler and the Linux sources are your friends ... go ahead and compile a new kernel.

As a matter of fact that was the first thing you had to do after an installation or chances were you'd never ever get your hardware fully recognised unless you took the time and compiled the entire kernel again and again and again until you found the right combo that would work ... You see: there were size restrictions too. Make the kernel too big and your machine would not be able to boot any more. Dynamic kernel modules were not available until around 1998/99 or so :)

Package management? "apt-get"?? Nope, didn't exist yet. You want software? ... well, you first have to make your modem work somehow and then you'd dial-up to your provider and then manually download all the libs .... Or you'd dial into a friendly BBS and hope to find the stuff there. And then the usual three-step dance:

su -
./configure
make
make install

... Forgot something? Oh well ... "apt" wasn't invented yet. So you spent another 2 or so days hunting down dependencies and compiling them first. :)

MS-DOS was at version 4.x something, Windows was often enough not even installed on many PC's back then (lack of disk space and too expensive). Windows 3.x was the state of the art ... and it sucked badly.

But on Linux, despite all the roadblocks it put into your way, you'd get a full multi-tasking and multi-user capable OS that would even make use of all your expensive super-duper 4 MB (yes, megs!!!) of RAM because it was not blocked by that stupid 640K barrier Microsoft put in place on MS-DOS ...

That's what made Linux attractive to some of us. 

When I hear present-day noobs talking about "how hard" present-day Linux distros apparently are for them ... I can't help but laugh :lolflag: ... Sorry about that, but that's how I feel about it.

Anyone finding present-day Linux distros "hard" should take a trip back in time and then try their luck with the "oh so wonderful" Microsoft products back then (hint: today's products suck ... so you don't really think Microsoft products were any better back then?? :D ) and also the few early Linux distros .... talk about "unfriendly installation" (which in this case applied to both MS-DOS and Linux of that time period).

I bet such a victim of time travel would come back screaming begging on their knees to be given one of those nice Ubuntu installer disks ... ;)

Sorry for my little rant here :)

### Post by gshockxc on 2008-11-26
After building my new system and making several attempts to install the older versions of Gutsy Gibbon that I had lying around, I ran through all of the amd64 versions and alternates of 7.04 with no luck.  Figuring that I was in for a long haul, and not really too upset about it, I took someone's suggestion to install 8.04 Hardy Heron.  I thought I was having some issues with the floppy not being recognized, and perhaps some unusual conflicts with the new SATA drives that I installed.  

So I went about downloading Ibex and Heron (see, I'm even picking up the lingo) so that I could burn the .iso files.  I did pause to think that maybe I should check it with md5sum.  Honestly, I'm not even sure what that is, but true to Noob form, I just barreled ahead and thought I'd try this first.  I also started to revert back to old habits of trying to download ALL the .iso images for Ubuntu (8.10 & 8.04), Kubuntu, and I even thought about Xubuntu - but later dismissed that.  When my 4GB thumb drive ran out of space, I decided it was more prudent to go with the 8.04 amd64 iso, and have the alternate as a back-up.  But I downloaded both versions of 8.10(amd64 & alternate), just to be safe.  "If I have problems with 8.04, maybe 8.10 will work."  I know 8.04 is LTS, but this is my noob logic at the time.  I have a program on my XP machine called Sunburner than can do .iso files.  It's not the Infrarecorder mentioned in the documentation, but I didn't realize that I had already uninstalled it (some time ago) until I got home last night.  So I set the machine to burning a copy of 8.04 on a CD-RW, at the slowest speed it would handle, 1x, and went off to the gym to get a work out.  

To my delight, the CD seemed to have written correctly, so I swapped the monitor, keyboard, and mouse, and started the new machine with the boot CD.  Sure enough, there's the language screen, and the install menu.  "Hmmm. Looks different than before.  Maybe it's working this time."  A few minutes later, it occurs to me, "No, that was Gutsy, you're installing Hardy.  Get with the 'program.'"  (Did you get the pun there?  Clever, wasn't it?)

So the progress bar bounces back and forth, like the games of Pong we all used to know and love.  Back and forth, back and forth... "Any minute now, it will give me an error.  Just wait, here it comes... Huh, no error.  WOW!  I'll be darned.  It's working.  Well, what do you know!  I might actually be able to get this thing up and running Ubuntu after all."  At this point, I'm actually kind of bummed, because I was sort of looking forward to the challenge of troubleshooting the issues I "thought" I was having.  It was a mixed emotional state for me, because I didn't want to have 'idiosyncrasies', as one forum member put it, but I wanted to struggle through the challenge, and experience that moment of triumph where I overcame the obstacles of running/installing Linux.  "Oh, well.  I'm sure there will be more challenges to work through."

45 minutes later, the installation completed, I restarted the machine, removed the CD, and met Hardy Heron for the first time!  It was a wonderful introduction, somewhat familiar to me from playing with Xubuntu on the Hitatchi Visionbook last year.  "The GUI kind of reminds me of a Mac.  Or is it that the Mac looks like Linux?"  Oh well.  I've got my Ubunutu now, that's all I need.  

And with that, I took my first steps into the Ubuntu world, quite literally sitting there, marveling at how cool this new operating system is.  "Wow, just imagine all the things I can do!  Without the over-confident, premature excitement that I had before, I was quite taken aback by the sum total of everything I had learned up to this point.  I used to think that I couldn't do everything in Linux that I used to do before in windows.  Thanks to some very helpful posts that I read sometime last year, I know that there's a list out there somewhere with an equivalent for just about every Windows program you can think of.  With Google Sketch up, there's no need to worry about those 3-D cad programs anymore, and that's just the one I know.  I'm sure there are others that work just as well.  Forget about Media Player, and Office.  I've got Winamp and Open Office, just to name a couple.  I've got Evolution or Thunderbird for email, FireFox for browsing.  And it's all free!  It's supported and encouraged by some of the nicest, most helpful people on the planet, and it's surrounded by a community of developers, users, forums and members who will lend a hand, and provide all the help needed at a moment's notice.  What more could I ask for?

Sitting there, on my living room floor, looking at my new operating system, marveling, admiring, and in awe all at once, I realized that my journey was only just beginning.  Before, it was just about the 'end,' getting to someplace other than where I was.  Now, it's not about where I go, but how I get there, what the road is like.

I saw a signature from another member recently, Therion -  "Linux: For those who understand, no explanation is necessary. For those who do not, no explanation is sufficient."

From where I sit now, this could never be more true.  I can never explain what it is that I find so appealing about Linux, about Ubuntu, about challenging myself to learn something new all the time, about this new experience being more than just tinkering with a computer system, and playing with some hardware.  I'll never be able to explain why, or what it is that makes it so.  But for Linux users, I don't have to.  For those who aren't, nothing will ever come close.

Today, that's what it's like to be a 'Noob.'  Tomorrow, it will change.  Until then.

### Post by waspbr on 2008-12-03
installing openoffice 3.0 can be very easy

go to your software sources, System>Administration>Software sources

type your password

go to the third party software tab. click on the button add

copy and paste these lines (one at a time)

```
deb http://ppa.launchpad.net/openoffice-pkgs/ubuntu intrepid main
deb-src http://ppa.launchpad.net/openoffice-pkgs/ubuntu intrepid main
```

click ok(it's gonna ask you to reload say ok to that too)

run update manager or synaptic (in synaptic reload and click mark all upgrades then apply)

you should be good to go

[LIST=1]
