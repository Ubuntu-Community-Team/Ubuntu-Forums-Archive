### Post by N00b-un-2 on 2009-11-27
Yes, it is indisputable that the average user who tries out Linux is going to be turned off by one thing or another.  For many, it's the lack of support for Windows or Mac only software/hardware.  For some its the fact that learning a little bit of BASH is a must.  For some, it's simply because it's not "what they're used to" (many Microsoft w**res I know have the same complaint of Vista/Win7 simply because it's not the same as XP)
But at the end of the day, the reason why I truly love Linux, FOSS, GPL, and virtually all that is right and good with the world of computers is that Linux is a transparent, free and open implementation of code, by the hacker for the hacker (subsitute "geek", "programmer", "user", etc for hacker, as the term "hacker tends to have a negative connotation).

The things that I absolutely hate about computers and technology all come from an entities relentless pursuit of the all-mighty dollar.  Take the iPod for instance.  At it's core, an iPod is simply a portable music player, but because of Apple's struggle for ultimate supremacy in a market that they have clearly ALREADY dominated, potential users of their hardware like me are turned off or steer towards other more "open" products.  Why does Apple not have an implementation for iTunes on Linux yet?  They have stolen plenty from the FOSS community over the years.  Isn't it about time they gave something back?  Or better yet, why is it absolutely necessary for Apple to constantly change their firmware and the way that the itunes DB is parsed, solely to make absolutely sure that their hardware can not be run with anything except iTunes properly?  Do they not realize that if they either A ) created an open source implementation of the iTunes/iPod code base or B ) ported iTunes to Linux, even if it were to remain closed source that they would be able to sell even MORE iPods and music on iTunes?

One thing that made me sick when I read it was a preview of the new Google Chrome OS.  I can't quite put my finger on it, but an OS on which you can't actually store your own data without logging into an online service just doesn't sit well with me.  And for the designers of the OS to have total control of what you can and can not do with their software is precisely what I have constantly struggled against with Microsoft products.  And for the Chrome OS to be based on the Linux kernel is simply a slap in the face to the whole community.  Who knows, perhaps with the new Chrome OS, I'm just not part of the "norm" enough to understand what if any virtue there is in having a computer that you can do nothing with that doesn't involve Google in one way or another.  It all seems a bit too "big brother" to me.  Don't get me wrong, I love google and I use it every time I get online, but I can not fathom ever wanting to delete every icon from my desktop and uninstalling every program except for Chromium.

Use Linux and Ubuntu was my idea.

You can be as technically accurate as you like, but a lot also hinges on how you say it.

People who make generalisations and who put labels on people are much more likely to get negative reactions than those who describe their own experience.

E.g. I can say "I'm having trouble with my graphics chip on my laptop and I'm getting really frustrated. I've tried X and Y, they didn't work, and I'm wondering if I want to carry on with this."

Or, I can say, "XXXX is a POS and no one in their right mind could possibly disagree. Only an idiot would want to use it...."

If I don't want to start a flame war, I'll stick to the first way.
