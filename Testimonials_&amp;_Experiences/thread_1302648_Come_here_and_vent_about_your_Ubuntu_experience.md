### Post by Paladiamors on 2009-10-27
I started off with the first EEE PC with Xandros, it worked flawless for me and there it began, the slippery slope into the Linux world. At first I thought it was great, the software was free and there was a whole depository of software that is free and open for people to tinker with.

At last, I thought, I could break away from the hegemony of Microsoft and their pricey, buggy OS. Or so I thought.

I started with this Ubuntu gig since 8.04, I thought it was great. The interface looked modern and right off the start I thought that the multiple desktop thing was cool. 

Then ugly annoying bits started coming out, like really slow HD transfer rates and irratic data transfer speeds. The the usual PC would be able to shuffle data around at 70 MB/sec but instead I got 7 MB/sec at first. It drove me mad.

Then there was the sound skipping problem, (damn you pulse audio!) and as I like to listen to music, I cringe at the idea of having to listen to music that skips. The last time I remember music skipping on a computer was on my old 90 mhz pentium playing an MP3 (when those things were new!). Why now?

I combed through the forums for tweaks, patches and tips that may or may not work, like trying to muck around with the pulseaudio config file, changing some "fragment" size parameters (which I have no idea what they are for) and then restarting pulse over and over again to see if it worked (it didn't!). I tried other tutorials and tweaks but to no avail.

How about some other problems, like why does flash in the 64 bit version have to crash all so often? I downgraded to the 32 bit version to get some more stability but can only access 3.2 GB out of all 4 GB of ram installed on my computer. Then I find posts about changing some compile settings for the kernel to get access to all 4GB but I have to make sure I recompile everytime a new kernel comes out?

Honestly, why does finding ways to solve problems have to be "needle-in-the-haystack" kind of thing? Shouldn't sound work properly when the OS gets installed? Search on the forums is terrible, sometimes you get some pretty out dated posts that refer to the same problem but on an older version of Ubuntu (and for all I know, the solution might not even work on a more recent version). There are times when I want to rip my hair out at.

And then on top of that, even when you do post, sometimes you get no replies... kind of the feeling like when you're asking for help in a busy city where everyone passes you by.

The user experience can be better than this. It needs to be if Linux is to be accepted as something more mainstream. Sure people can point fingers at how it is the fault of the packages themselves instead of Ubuntu that is causing the problem. Well, the thing is that the people here are the ones responsible for selecting the packages and configuring them to work. Things need to be improved.

Look, I'll apologize for sounding like a jerk for blasting at software that is free and developed by people in their spare time. The interface and all nice and everything, but in addition to that, it's gotta work and if there are problems users should be generally aware of them instead of running into them on their own... and perhaps have an easier time of finding solutions.

Because the point of despair is when people spend days trying to fix something using tips that may or may not work. Then they realize that there are more problems that crop up in the same way. 

If there was something I wished from Ubuntu in it's immature state, is just a warning of what might not work. At least so for people to know when to steer clear of trouble.

Anyways... if you got down here, you did a whole lot of reading. 

Thanks for reading, I just needed to vent.

<sigh>

### Post by QIII on 2009-10-27
Let's not jump on the OP for expressing concerns.  That can do nothing but add to the stereotype of the ugly snob.

Although it would best have been in Testimonials, perhaps the OP doesn't realize it exists.

Paladiamors -- There is no need to apologize.  Your concerns are entirely valid, and you should air them.  That is how this thing gets better.
  
  Just keep in mind that people come to forums when they need help with problems, not when they need help with things that are going right.  There are Windows help forums aplenty.  That doesn't mean the either Ubuntu or Windows are particularly bad.  (As a note:  My beef with Microsoft is about their business practices and hegemony, not the relative merits of Windows as an OS.  Freedom of choice is the thing.)
  
 I get HD to HD transfer rates as high as 88 MB/s fairly consistently, which is really above what is expected in theory.  With a few things going on, it's not hard for me to saturate my 100 MB/s router.  Transfer speed drops, of course, when copying files on the same drive.  The read/writes are being done on the same disk, so the two operations swap off and take some time.
 
 I haven't had a problem with sound skipping for the last several distributions.
 
 64 bit flash has been more stable for me than 32 bit.  On that note, the 64 bit OS has been more stable.
 
 I know that getting answers here can sometimes leave you with no replies, but you can bump a thread every day for a few days (don't do it any more frequently than that and don't keep doing it for weeks.  That's a bit unsportsmanlike.)
 
 But I hear you.  We would all like to help.  That's why we are here.
 
 I don't know that Canonical has any illusions about "mainstreaming".  The simple fact is that Windows is the defacto standard.  The hardware and software OEMs bow and scrape to Redmond to get their drivers and software compatible and get the vaunted "Made for Windows" certification.  They are just being pragmatic.  Windows has a choke hold on the world.  Again -- my beef is with business practices, not the OS itself.
 
 The beauty of this all is that you have choices.  I'm not a Ubuntu apologist.  I think you should use what works for you.  If Xandros worked better for you, then use it by all means.  Nobody here has any right to fault you for doing what is best for you.
 
 That said -- No OS gets better if everyone simply gives up when things get hard.  Not Windows, not Ubuntu, not Xandros.
 
 It might do us all well if you used Xandros because it works for you.  Dual boot Ubuntu as a secondary system and help us work out the kinks.  Make sure you work the Xandros forums, too.  That will help Xandros get better.
 
 In either case, you are a member of the team.

### Post by QIII on 2009-10-28
Paladiamors  -- 

It might be helpful for you and anyone else who has similar issues for you to start a new thread with one concern.  Make the title as descriptive as possible, but not too long.

In the body of your post, give some details like the specifications of your machine, what you were doing when you noticed the issue and what you have done so far to try to resolve the issue.  If you have looked at tutorials, provide a link for everyone to look at.  It is possible that someone will tell you that the advice given there is a load of fertilizer.

Also, it is sometimes best to let several people give possible solutions and discuss them a bit before you launch into something that either will not work or will make things worse.  Nobody is an expert on everything.  You won't likely find many malicious posts, but you may very well find misguided or ignorant ones.  (I don't mean "ignorant" as a put-down, but rather as an indication of the poster's specific knowledge and experience.)

Let people help you work that one problem to conclusion.  When you get that one solved, start another thread with the next problem.  

One issue at a time makes it easier for those with expertise in that area to find your post and deal with just that one issue.

It will also help others doing searches to find the end solution.

So I would encourage you to make another post with your most pressing problem and we'll look to help you there.
