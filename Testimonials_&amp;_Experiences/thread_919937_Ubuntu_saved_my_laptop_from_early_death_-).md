### Post by tgyorgyi on 2008-09-14
I am a heavy laptop user. One and a half years into my current laptop (FSC Amilo), I really started to suffer from how long it takes to load Windows, how long it takes for programs to start up, how long it takes for my e-mails to download in Outlook, and that I had to do a clean install every couple of month so that XP does not die on me.

Lucky for my laptop :) at a point where I was often fantasizing about jumping up and down on my laptop with both feet, a friend introduced me to Ubuntu. I am a curious but careful type. Tried WUBI first, then had a couple of months of dual boot where my main OS remained XP and tried to figure out Ubuntu, and the few hardware issues that needed special attention. But even in this phase I noticed that I would spend more and more time doing things from Ubuntu, and becoming more and more reluctant to go back to work in XP and endure the lengthy perios of WAITING for something to happen...

About two months ago I made the full conversion to Ubuntu. XP is still sitting around on my laptop for there is the odd software that I need and for which I have not found a satisfactory Linux alternative yet, but I have not booted XP for over a month now.

And a big thank you to the Ubuntu Forum Community... I have learned a lot from the posts here. Besides the readyness to help others and the great experience that is present here, it cannot be stressed enough that the tone and manner in which conversations are conducted here is very reassuring to somebody who is totally new to Linux/Ubuntu.

Three possible reasons for your boot time being slow are :

1) - You have a lot of programs set to load at boot up, like messenger etc. Plus in the menu you have the folder "Start Up" which can have the ms office entry, etc. Go through the control panel option (Or where ever it is) and stop a lot of un-needed software loading into memory at boot up. This will greatly reduce the time, and give you back memory.

2) - Switch the menu to classic look. Probably wouldnt make that much difference, but it helps.

3) - Defrag your windows registry, especially if you install / uninstall a lot of software. By cleaning it and defragging you will speed up the boot time. Less registry entries = faster boot time.
