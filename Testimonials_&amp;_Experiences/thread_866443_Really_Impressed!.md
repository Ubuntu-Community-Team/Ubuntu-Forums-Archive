### Post by CarlosNYB on 2008-07-21
Ok, I had it with Windows (again) and decided to try out Linux (again)!

The middle of last week I decided to dual-boot Ubuntu from a DVD that came with a Linux Format Starter Pack (Gutsy).  I had downloaded and burned an iso of Ubuntu Studio (Heron based) a week before, but I was a little nervous and procrastinated installing it.  I felt better with the Linux Format Starter Pack magazine that introduced Ubuntu and gave guidance on installation and applications, and I liked the idea that it came on a "live-CD" that I could just pop in and look at and see if my hardware worked.

Well the Gutsy "live-CD" looked nice and worked fine (except it didn't detect my wireless card).  So I installed gutsy.  Tried to get wireless working in various ways, but I wound up just downloading an iso of the normal distribution of Hardy Heron and when it was up and running I use the SYNAPTIC to load ndiswrapper from the normal Heron CD.  Configuring wireless wasn't that bad, but it took some playing.

So all said and done, it was an easier process than installing Windows 2000 Professional and all its drivers on this machine was initially (this is one of those bare bones machines from a number of years ago that I customized).  I mean, all the drivers work well out of the box except for the wireless card which required installing packages and tweaking.  Which isn't unusual for a custom job or for installing an OS, frankly.

When I tried linux some years ago, I put in Red Hat just to see what it looked like and then Mandrake, around the turn of the century.  I was following Mandrake for a while, liking it, dual booting for a little while.  But my life was too busy and chaotic to really enjoy getting into command line stuff, compilation, the tweaking, the learning curve involved, etc.  I saw the promise but I really needed something a bit more friendly as far as installing packages was concerned, and I needed something that could really do music (midi and audio processing) well.

I think I heard about Ubuntu Studio maybe a year or so ago.  Now I wanted to see what it had to offer, because I read about Ardour and I took a look again at Rosegarden and I liked what I saw.  (I hate Vista and though I like Cakewalk Sonar, eventually it will no doubt require Vista.  Plus, I like using quality free tools.  I love Renoise and it has been ported to Linux.)  I had the energy and the desire to make the time to play around and see.  I prefer the normal Hardy Heron desktop distribution for the office applications and look and feel.  I added the Ubuntu Studio Music and ALSA packages.  Been having fun playing.

The fact is, 99% of what I do on a computer involves a web browser, a word processor, and music programs.  If it wasn't for the fact that I had Sonar Cakewalk on my Win2k partition, and an iTunes library, I would just drop Windows right now and it would be great.  As it is, I need to go through a learning curve with Rosegarden and Ardour, to see if I can become as comfortable and efficient as I am in Cakewalk. I hope so, and it seems I may be able to.  Renoise is great on Linux, just as it is on Windows.  Hydrogen, Beast, and the ZynAddSubFX soft synths are extremely cool/powerful.  I'll fiddle with syncing the iPod Nano and work on the iTunes library maybe I'll convert some more things to mp3 and leave Apple behind.

The package manager is really nice.  I was told some years before that the debian package system was really nice.  The way it works in Ubuntu is much smoother than what I was fiddling with some years ago on Red Had and Mandrake.

I'm impressed.  Linux has come a long way in terms of ease of use and in the quality of its music production programs.
