### Post by RobotChubby on 2009-11-29
I'm sitting here with a wonderfully well functioning Hardy Heron desktop, and this thread is really just to say 'Thanks'.

I've been been idly sampling various Linux flavours for 12 months or so, but have not fully committed until now, having been a typically Redmond orientated user for the last 20 years or so.

A few things have encouraged me to fully explore the possibilities of Ubuntu.  For one thing I wanted to build a decent web system for our lounge, keeping the costs as low as possible.  Secondly, my PC mag of choice recently published a good article on getting started with penguin based computing.  Thirdly.... Windows 7.  Consider me unimpressed and slightly bewildered by the overwhelmingly positive response to this new OS.  Sure, it's not bad, but it's really nothing special either, and merely serves to perpetuate revenue for the industry.  That's fine (and, I suppose, necessary) but also boring.

So, I burned a copy of 9.10 and jumped in with both feet.  A mixed result, which is what I'd expected, but frankly I'd rather have an interesting headache with this software and learn something in the process than the alternative.

9.10 is certainly prettier and noticeably quicker than 8.04, and the Audigy card I was using worked perfectly first time.  However, I had a DNS issue which I struggled to resolve, so I decided to go back to Heron, as I knew for sure the Web worked first time with it.

I sit here now with a fully functional system, listening to my MP3 player through the PC (though sadly not through the Audigy, I went back to the bog standard on board audio - it was easier to get working).

My aim was just to get up and running to the standard I wanted as quickly as possible, and to just start experiencing and learning the OS.  This was straightforward, and has posed no major problems whatsoever.

The only real difficulties - being completely objective - are that perhaps some of the user guides tend to assume a certain level of knowledge and can be a little opaque.  But: a) They're very professionally produced; b) The same criticism can probably be applied to many tech user guides; c) I got it working didn't I?!

I don't think age is on my side either.  20 years ago DOS command lines seemed a doddle.  Now the Ubuntu command line is proving more of a challenge.  

But these are early days, and I intend to be around for a good while longer.

Thanks again.

### Post by Methuselah on 2009-11-29
I second the great post comment.

I too found Karmic to be a mercurial fellow so sticking with hardy isn't a bad call.

You'll get used to the terminal and it will seem a lot less cryptic with time.
Remember that the terminal commands come with documentation that you can access anytime with the 'man' command.
Seeing 'sudo' popup in many tutorials and not sure what it does?
Type

```

man sudo

```

and find out more than you'd ever care to know.
Almost every command has an appropriate manpage even man itself (invoke with 'man man').
It a good idea to run man against commands you're told to run that you do not know.
For example, 'man rm' will reveal that the rm command is very powerful indeed.

In addition each command usually accepts a --help switch that provides abridged usage information.
Eg.
```

sudo --help

```
It is less useful than the manpage if you know nothing about the command but can be helpful if you need a quick refresher.

Good luck and enjoy your new installation!

### Post by RobotChubby on 2009-12-01
Advance warning: Long, rambling, shambolic and slightly hungover post follows... albeit with good intentions.  So, er, please don't let me be misunderstood.  

Thanks to all who've replied and sorry about the puns (I read somewhere that it's actually punning that's supposed to be the lowest form of wit, not sarcasm.  Unfortunately I tend to be pretty sarcastic too, so I lose on both counts.  Which is nice.)

Anyway, it's apparent from the outset that there is a really strong community spirit here, and I really enjoy that.  Certainly, my newbie perception is that by definition Linux flavours - like all open source - have developed out of and for the community and certainly in this regard are more successful than 'more established' commercial equivalents, which - again by definition - were insular and non-connected when first devised.  As such, the collaborative - and supportive - approach is something I feel that some of these companies are still learning, whereas you guys are streets ahead.

A few things I've noticed so far seem worth commenting on, but whether they are remotely interesting or not is for you to decide.  (To paraphrase Malcolm Tucker - when you guys need my advice you can give me the special signal.  Which is you being sectioned under the Mental Health Act....;))

After Karmic, I chose Hardy because, a) I already had a disc from last year, and b) It's designated 'Long Term Support'.  It was also my first ever Linux experience, so I have a soft, mushy spot for it.  

I've been using Firefox 3.5 on XP for some time now, so was slightly disappointed that Hardy doesn't apparently support it (I've not looked into this too deeply, but am fairly confident this is the case; also I'm not intending any of these comments to be support calls - I'll go through proper channels if I need that so please don't get irritated with me).  I've also noted that I have an older version of Open Office on here than I'm used too, but haven't looked into that at all yet.

These points do make me wonder whether I've gone with the best Ubuntu choice.  As I may already have said, primarily this will be a rig for web surfing (BBC iPlayer; YouTube various entertainment sites and forums - all of which are working fine).  My Wife, who is very inexperienced with PCs (she likes fast cars and horses.  God knows why she married me.) will be a key user, but at the moment I also imagine that Web and Email will be her main interests.  I wouldn't be surprised if she becomes a Facebookerist... I don't use social networking.  Probably because I'm an anti social *******.

Anyway...... I did wonder if it was worth going with 9.04 or something, but I think for an easy life I'll probably stick with this edition unless you guys convince me otherwise.  I guess Firefox 3.0 is a bit clunkier than later variants, and I imagine that on a Windows desktop it probably presents some security issues, but I don't expect those to be as relevant here.

Once I'm more familiar with the Linux world I think I'm more likely to move away from Ubuntu into something like Fedora for a while, just to get a more rounded experience.  I realise that might be a slightly offensive thing to say in an Ubuntu forum, but this is where I have chosen to properly begin my Linux journey after all.

Talking of which, I wondered if there are any newbie blogs?  I would like to be a more active member of the community if I can, and I wondered about documenting my experiences so that similar novice users have another avenue to explore (also because I like the sound of my own voice.  Or,er, typing.)   I expect that this has already been done though, and probably to a high standard, so if you could point me towards it that would be useful.

On my Windows box I've just got the Ultimate Edition of Office 2007 (through the ultimate steal promotion - if you're a UK student and you don't know about this, PM me because it's quite a deal).   Now, this is my first new version of Office for some time and while:
a) I need to know how to use it for work and courses;
b) I find Open Office a bit unsatisfying;
c) The initial experience has been quite positive;
Despite all that, I find myself drawn back to this side of the room and my Ubuntu box.

I think that this is the key point I take from my general Linux experience so far, the appeal.  Permit me a slightly tortured metaphor if you will (I'll shut up soon, I promise):

Recently a friend of mine was lucky enough to go on a high performance driving day.  He drove, among other things, a Nissan Skyline and a Ferrari 355 and when I spoke to him about this, he insisted that the Skyline was far more enjoyable.  Now, much as I love him, he is palpably wrong, delusional and of deeply questionable manhood.  A Skyline is a great car, principally because it's on-board computer and it's four wheel steering make it comparatively easy to drive fast without dying horribly.   A 355 on the other hand is one of the greatest cars ever made, and - being a Ferrari - it's challenging to drive, requiring determination, physical strength and great big hirsute billiards.   The experience of driving it is pure, but it takes skill to do right.
 

 Computers are a lot like cars.  They both have a chassis for a start, and the more powerful they are the more cooling they need.  The CPU is the engine, the PSU is the fuel, people love to mod them... I won't labour the point.  Now, historically the Apple was the Skyline.  You switched it on and it just worked; no (sunday) drivers to worry about, no compatibility – you just got on with  it.  Even I would struggle to compare a PC to a Ferrari, but the point about knowledge and skill being required to get the best out of them is certainly valid, and in the hands of a competent user a modern Windows 7 PC built to the same quality spec as a Mac is going to be on a very similar level, albeit with less re-sale value.  But for years, MS has been trying to emulate Apple.  All those evolution posters in the 90s obviously touched a nerve. Anyway, with 7 they are getting much closer to Mac in terms of both the experience and ease of use.
 

 Linux, certainly all the distros I've been lucky enough to use, still needs some knowledge, skill and experience if you are going to get it right. But I love that about it, and so presumably do a lot of you.  I don't really care that while typing this my wireless connection has dropped out again and I've had to restart, because I know that once I fix it I'll be filled with the warm geeky glow of a problem solved and a lesson learned.  If that happens when my Wife's using it and I'm not here to sort it – she'll be far from impressed.  But for me it is simply more fun.  I love computers for their own sake.  I love building them, messing about with them and learning more about them.  People like that will probably always love Linux.  But computers have saturated our societies now, and the overwhelming majority of users aren't using them because they love them, but because they need them.  They want the traction control to stay switched on.
 

 So for the foreseeable future I think Linux will remain a deeply loved niche OS.  Chrome OS (which I believe uses some Linux code, but a lot of original stuff as well) has the worrying potential to further marginalise it, but that remains to be seen.  Certainly Linux code seems to have more of a future in the developing mobile model of computers... I think Desktops may be approaching their peak right now.
 

 Ubuntu is great in it's own right.  The fact that it's free as well is punning.  I mean stunning.
 

 If anyone out there is still awake, thanks for reading.
