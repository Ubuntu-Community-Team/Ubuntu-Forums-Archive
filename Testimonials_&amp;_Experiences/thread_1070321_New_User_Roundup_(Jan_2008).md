### Post by Alterax on 2009-02-15
My Ubuntu experience seems to get better with each release, but then again, I'm becoming an old hand.  As part of my business, I dispose of gently-used IT equipment.  What can be reused and refurbished often finds itself forensically wiped, loaded with Ubuntu and a good selection of games, programs, and plugins for the average user, and donated to people that wouldn't normally have access to a computer.

This post is about the experiences of the latest batch of these persons.

The biggest complaint was from a more mature user that had an XP machine that had completely crashed two months before.  Her complaints were that she didn't know how to access the Internet (resolved by showing her Firefox and adding a desktop icon) and cosmetic issues involving the standard GNOME desktop and sound effects.  She has been particularly happy that her computer has been plagued by her curious children, grandchildren, and great-grandchildren, and despite heavy use it remains fast and free of annoying pop-up windows.  She noted that she hadn't had to reboot since she first got it.

The next user was a completely new user, a woman in her early fifties with no previous computer experience.  She is very happy about the games selection (and a Frozen Bubble addict).  She's not entirely happy about the color scheme, but that was fixable.  She recently got Internet access, and was very pleased to discover that adding new software was easy and much more cost-effective than buying programs at her local store.

The next team is a brother-sister team.  They received a computer each, and there were initial issues with the monitors that were given with them, as the resolutions weren't properly autodetected by xorg.  Adding an overwrite into the xorg.conf file took 20 minutes to research and three minutes to actually fix.  The oldest was happy with OpenOffice and took to it immediately, taking homework between school and home on a USB drive.  She was disappointed slightly with a lack of iTunes support, but found that Amazon.com's MP3 store combined with the gtkpod tools was a sufficient workaround.

The youngest could care less; he's only had a couple of issues with some games not having proper documentation.

Finally, there is a twenty-four year old single father who became frustrated when trying to install a printer he purchased with the included CD-ROM.  This frustration quickly was abated when he found the Ubuntu method of adding printers, which correctly detected and added the printer.

None of these people are computer-geeks.  These are standard home users with varying degrees of experience.  The major problems that were encountered were the monitors issue (which occurred with abnormally hi-res monitors), lack of familiar programs, and slightly different methods of accomplishing the tasks that they were accustomed to do.

Overall, the experiences have been highly positive; I'll continue monitoring them to see how the user experience can be improved, and to see what issues may arrive after the "new" wears off.
