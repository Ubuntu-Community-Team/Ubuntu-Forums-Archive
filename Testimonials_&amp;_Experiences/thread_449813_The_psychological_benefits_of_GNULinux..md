### Post by Babbage on 2007-05-20
This is a subject I've not seen talked about much but I'm going to try. I feel a lot more comfortable using Linux (Ubuntu at the moment) than Windows. I think there is an emotional benefit, as well as the performance and cost benefits of using an open source OS. I suppose in particular I'm talking about the psychological factors of using open source software, for example:

1) it's not doing anything "in the background" that I don't know about.
2) Is not designed to check up on anything I'm doing or spying on me.
3) I'm not effectively forced to agree to licence agreements I don't fully understand or haven't read.
4) I no longer need to accept having my computer system scanned by a giant corporation in the US.
5) Leading to a restoration of the trust relationship between me, the software I'm using and the physical hardware I'm using.
6) In turn I feel more autonomous and independent. I also feel more individual as a computer user, as if I'm being respected more.
7) At the same time I feel I have a shared experience with millions of other computer users who've taken a similar decision to use Open Source software.
8.) I feel my computer actually belongs to me again.

In a nutshell there are many positive psychological benefits, for me anyway, of using GNU/Linux and Open Source software. To me using Linux has restored the trust in computing everyday, I feel I'm in charge of my system and helps me feel happy, satisfied and free. :D

### Post by lneely on 2007-05-28
The first time I decided to try GNU/Linux, my parents purchased a copy of RedHat 6.0 for me (as I was on a dial-up connection and furthermore didn't understand that the OS was /really/ available free of charge).  At the time, I was young (around twelve years old) and didn't understand the whole idea behind license restrictions and was an avid software pirate (borrowed games and applications from friends, etc.) out of blissful ignorance. Then again, this was the nineties, nobody cared so overtly back then.

My first experience with the OS itself was an interesting feeling.  As it struck my interest, I read more about GNU/Linux itself, its origins, and its philosophy. it fascinated me that a ragtag group of people from around the world calling themselves GNU would eventually create something that could compete with multi-billion dollar corporations; that eventually, some multi-billion corporations _themselves_ would be adopting their ideals!  Also fascinating was that this OS would be available to all people free of charge and free of restriction and that all Free Software would be available free of restrictions at least.  When that idea really hit home, I was hooked on the idea of FOSS and remain so to this very day.  I've even tried returning to the Devil's OS numerous times to keep my video game skills up to par, but it just isn't the same anymore... It's really unfortunate that Loki Games went down the crapper.

I agree that using GNU/Linux and abiding by its ideals come with their psychological benefits because it is good and it's right.
