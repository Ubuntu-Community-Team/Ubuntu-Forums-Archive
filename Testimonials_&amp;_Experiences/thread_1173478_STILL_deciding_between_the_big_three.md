### Post by Jestersage on 2009-05-29
After using all three distribution, here's my impression
a) Fedora: The minimalist cutting edge. Have plenty of support, but Most of the time I am not even aware of the poackage IS available.
b) openSuSE: Total control: Everything can be done in GUI, KDE feels even more solid, most hardware compatibility, and if anything, the deal with Microsoft actually makes it feel more solid. Also have lots of software, even for special applications (ala Education package). However, compare to Ubuntu, it is either like Fedora (feature is unaware unless you search in its wiki), or is in beta/RC (The openSuSE education is a good example). Also... BIG download. All openSuSE package I find are in DVD, compare to edubuntu, which is a cd.
c) Ubuntu: (actually three distribution, Ubuntu vanilla, ubuntu studio, ubuntu server) Nothign can be compare about Vanilla, and nothing can be talked about much. Ubuntu Studio is good for sound production, and in fact I am wondering why Ubuntu studio kernel is not used for EVERYTHING. Ubuntu server feels... great, that's all I can say; stable.
The thing abotu Ubuntu is that it feels like a pre-packaged computer that are sold by HP or Dell, in that they give you all your required software, and all softwares work out of the box (more or less ) due to tight integration. Hardware not so much, but easily configured for the most part; all these are possible due to the Ubuntu development, which ensure tight integration of software. However, I feel that the Ubuntu delivery cycle does also work against it, in that the repo is frozen every 6 monthes, and so sometimes the integrated software is outdated. Also, too mcuh brown, and not enough KDE.

And thus this is how I feel:
Desktop:
a) Fedora: Honestly, why bother? If you want RedHat go with CentOS or Scientific
b) Ubuntu: want somethign between CentOS and openSuSE, or somethign for new users? Ubuntu works for the most part. As long as users do the basics and is not willing to touch too much stuff. And for OEM, because you will need more configuration to ensure all the hardware is installed; however, it does provide an option for OEM install, which neither 
c) openSuSE: For powerusers (or users who think themselves as powerusers), and for people who want more guarantee on hardware support. Also good if you prefer KDE.

Server:
Take a crackshot. If not because of the support for special server packages, I still feel openSuSE is better, especially with many GUI server tools, like Easy-LTSP. However, since most of the special package is either general package or beta, in terms of stability I may take Ubuntu— which, though prefer command lines, have more software that will guarantee work. Fedora, due to being the oldest, have even more server support (in fact Amahi, the home server package, is for Fedora only for now); users will also have an easier time of transition to CentOS if Fedora based.

So I am still debating, mainly between openSuSE and Ubuntu. I actually like the openSUSE agreement with Microsoft, because ironically that gives Novell more weight to the fact its software is better. However, openSUSE's big download requirement and less tight integration compare to Ubuntu makes me think.
