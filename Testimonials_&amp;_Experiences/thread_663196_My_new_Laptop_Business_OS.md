### Post by jmclein on 2008-01-09
I have been in the IT industry for about 10 years.  I speak fluent Windows, Conversational Mac and not a LICK of Linux.  It had always been something I wanted to try out because I heard that it is stable and lacks a lot of the memory leaks that Windows has (As a desktop OS).  I tried a few distros for about 6 months about a year ago.  Had a lot of problems.  Never got anything working to what I would consider 100 percent.  Closest I got was about 70 percent and that wasn't good for me.

Reading through a few blogs a couple days ago I saw that 7.10 was out so I decided to give it another go.  I had some extra time on my hands so I figured why not?  My experience is as follows.

The install went fine.  Downloaded it, burnt it, installed it.  Thats all there really was to it.  It didn't like the graphics card in my laptop so I had to use the generic video option during the install but that was pretty easy to figure out.  After that it was smooth sailing.

After the install I had 2 "major" issues.  Wireless and graphics.  From the research I had done I knew these were pretty common issues so I wasn't heart broken.  I did a search on the forums and found a guide with step by step instructions that fixed both my problems in about 15 mins.  Wireless was up and working and the graphics issue was fixed.

I spent the next few days downloading and getting used to the  comparable software which I used on a daily basis when I was running windows. A torrent program (Deluge), A VM program (Virtual Box), The movie and music players.  One quick note about virtual box, A totally free VM software?? Rock on!  

Software I needed was easy to find.  Plug-ins and codecs and such all downloaded automatically. Overall, I have found this to be a great business OS.  It does just about everything I think that 90 percent of business users need a OS to do.




Things I love.
-How easy it is to setup keyboard shortcuts.

-Crazy fast load time.  Ubuntu loads from a cold boot faster then windows came out of hibernation.  This is important to me because I use my laptop in the field so the fact that I can turn it off instead of hibernating it means I get a LOT more use out of a single battery

-Responsiveness. I click something, It opens.  I don't find myself waiting around for programs to open.

-Synaptic Package Manager.  What a cool way to install stuff.

-Multiple Desktops.  Whoever thought this idea up should get a cookie.  Seriously, I don't know how I lived without this before.  It makes things so much easier for someone like me that always has tons of windows open.

-Not having to worry about viruses.

Things I hate.
This may make me sound like a linux fanboy but honestly...I havent found anything to dislike about it yet.  My only initial concern was there was a app I needed to run every once in awhile that is windows only.  I simply use Virtual box to run XP and run the app.  I can even keep it on its own separate desktop and use the handy keyboard shortcuts I made to just switch over to it.

I think the reason for my success is that ubuntu fits my needs.  I see a lot of people complaining that gaming is hard on Linux.  Yes, it is.  Linux isn't a good gaming platform.  However, for my needs it works perfect. Before trying Linux people need to look at what their needs are and then do some research.  That would save a LOT of the "Linux sucks, going back to windows!" threads I have seen around here.

I am a gamer.  I know linux isn't the best when it comes to games.  Thats the reason I don't have it on my gaming rig.  I have XP.  It works great for games and I am happy with that.  

I don't run a ton of custom software.  I'm not using it for gaming.  I am using it for work because it is a fast, stable OS that does exactly what I want it to and frankly it does it better then Windows.  I use it to watch movies and listen to music when I'm sitting in a airport or at home on my couch.  As far as I'm concerned it fits my needs perfectly, So, before you install it ask yourself.  What are my needs and will Linux fill them.  If you KNOW that its not good at doing the types of things you want to do then save yourself a SERIOUS headache and just don't install it.  Don't take a piece of software that isn't going to fill your needs and then try to make it fill your needs.
