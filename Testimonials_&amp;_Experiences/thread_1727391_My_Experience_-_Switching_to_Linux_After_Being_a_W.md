### Post by ClientAlive on 2011-04-12
I've been a Windows User for about 15 years. Ever since I started using computers. I've know about Linux for almost 15 years and have wanted to check it out for probably 10. I can't explain why it's taken me so long to get around to actually making the switch except to say I held a lot of common misconceptions: that, in reality, I found out are false.

I was told that Linux is command line - so I assumed that, that was all you had to use the system. It's obviously not.

I was told Linux is simple and inferior, that it doesn't do much - so I assumed running it would be like regressing. It's clearly not inferior; in fact, in my experience so far, it is superior in many ways. Ironically, one of those ways is the command line. Who would've thunk!

I was told, and believed, there is no support for Linux so I would basically be on my own. I find that this is partly true but mostly not. Let me explain. It's not that there is no support for Linux it's just that the support comes in a little different way. Since Linux is run by only a minority of people it may be a challenge to meet people who live in your same town or city who use it but this is made up for by a long shot with this and other Linux forums. The folks here have been really great with me; and, in most cases, I have not had to wait long for a response to my questions. Many times only minutes. Ultimately, I think the support for Linux is better than any I've ever had. I've certainly never had such easy or quick access to support when sitting in que on the phone trying to get through to some tech support department of some manufacturer I'd made a purchase through.

Now I want to concede to something. I mentioned in the previous paragraph: that it may be a challenge to meet people who live in your same town or city who use Linux. I have not made much of an effort to do so thus far - so you should not take me too seriously on that point. It may not be very tough at all and I'm sure there are a lot of variables that make it different for everyone.

Linux is different. More so than I realized from the outside. Learning Linux can be a challenge but it is not without reward. All it really takes is a little determination. I think we all had to have determination when we began using Windows too - didn't we? In the beginning there may be (for some people) issues with configuring hardware or peripherals, configuring software, updating, and the like. These are all things that can be overcome easily enough if you really want to.

For me, it turns out I have probably the worst possible laptop to run Linux on. Yet, so far, I have met every challenge that has come and overcome almost every issue that has come up. (Right now I'm working on getting my graphics card working correctly for 3D rendering with Compiz. I'm sure it won't be long and that will be working fine too). It's all about resolve, willingness to learn something new, and not being afraid to reach out for help. I could not have done any of the things I have without the help of this forum community. I've asked questions all along the way and gotten some really great answers and met some really cool people. I hope to return the favor to others in whatever capacity I can as I continue to learn about Linux.

So earlier I said that so far I had met and overcome every challenge that has come. I want to clarify something about that statement. Saying something like that makes it sound like Linux is so screwed up it has all kinds of problems. This is just not that case (and if you look into this you will find out that what I say is true). It's not Linux, it's the machine I happen to own and most (and I mean the vast majority) of hardware works just fine with Linux. For me its as if the people who put this laptop together sat down together and tried to figure out all the components that would give me trouble and chose to use those when they put the thing together.

Details? Well sure, I'm glad you ask!

I have a wireless chipset in this machine that has been problematic with Linux for years! it's one of only a few but it is one. It's called a Broadcom B43 (for short). In the end, the fix was not difficult and my wireless connection works fine now. It was just a matter of downloading a package and running it. The whole thing took about 2 minutes and worked perfectly. Granted, I was more intimidated by the prospect than I should have been.

I have an all-in-one (an HP J4680) that the manufacturer told me would not work with Linux when I contacted them. Now this did set me on edge for a minute; but, as it turns out, they were wrong. People can be wrong you know (even people from tech support at these big companies). Now, not only were they wrong but setting that printer up with Linux was way easier than it had been under Windows before this. I mean it was really easy. It almost configured itself, I didn't lose any functionality, it's easier to use now, and I don't have to deal with all the extra garbage software that was forced on me when I ran it under Windows.

When I ran this device (my all-in-one) under Windows the drivers from the disc that came with it installed all kinds of crap monitoring programs that the thing ended up being dependent on. When I tried to uninstall them it wouldn't work right and I'd get errors. When I tried to run it with them it bogged my system down and reported information to HP I wasn't happy about. That was under Windows. I don't have to deal with any of that now under Linux.

I have a graphics card, an ATI Radeon 200m, that has always been a problem with Linux. Again, one of only a few but one. This brings us up to the present and I'm sure this too will be solved very soon and I'll be able to use Compiz Fusion in all it's glory and splendor - one of the coolest things I have ever seen!

The rest of it has just been things I needed to learn how to do with Linux. Not a big deal, just takes a little time - just like anything else. Why would anyone put that kind of effort into something like this? The answer is simple - because it's worth it. In the final analysis, I'm discovering that Linux is a very powerful, very functional, very secure operating system and one that I can do things with I would have never imagined with Windows. That's just my experience - that's all.

I'm learning command line now and it's clearly a way of working that is very powerful and fast. I'm just getting started; and, yes, I have had to put some effort into it, but it has been well worth it. I love it! I love using Linux and learning something more about computers I didn't know before. In the end I'll have skills I didn't have before - and that is something that can never be taken from me.

Thanks for reading. I hope this has been of some use to you. Whatever challenges you run into using Linux I encourage you to use the forum. You'll find a community of really awesome people willing to help you. Be smart though and be careful. There is always the odd person here and there who may mean you harm. They are to be found anywhere you go and this forum is no exception - just that I don't think it is the rule, by far, either.

Please let me leave you with one piece of advice that was given to me very early on. It was given to me by a very wise person but is also pretty common sense. Occasionally someone will offer you code to run in your terminal to fix some problem or do something you were asking about. Now the advice is this: 'don't run any code someone gives you before checking it out a little.' Try googling it at least.

Sincerely,
Jake

HP ze2000 running Ubuntu 11.04 Natty Narwhal. Good ol' Natty . . .

### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2011-04-12
Welcome to the wonderful world of Linux!

It's not for everyone of course, nor is Ubuntu a drop-in replacement for Windows for everyone. 

But for this schoolkid, it's everything I need. Everything I did on Windows I can do (faster and easier) on Linux. It's *different*, but not more difficult. Even for a totally non-geeky kid like me.

I just have to share this funny story though:

I have a friend who was my go-to guy whenever I had a problem with my computer. One of my complaints was about "Windows rot," a term used to describe that gradual but inevitable slowdown of everything over time. It happened four times on my WinXP machine and I mentioned that I was thinking about Linux (after the price of Mac scared me away). He said:

> First of all, Windows doesn't 'rot' of it's configured correctly! And second of all, you had **[COLOR="Purple"]better stick with Windows, kiddo, you're not ready for Linux![/COLOR]**

Waitaminute... 

*"Then I guess the folks at Dell didn't configure Windows properly when they installed it... and I guess the Geek Squad at Best Buy didn't configure it properly when they re-installed it (the only cure for Windows rot is a complete re-installation)... and um, I guess [I]you* didn't configure it properly when _you_ re-installed it (twice),"[/I] I said flatly.

> RAWR! I'm not gonna sit here and be insulted by some silly little teenaged brat! Go ahead and try your stupid Linux, then. You'll be back here begging for my help the first day! **[COLOR="Purple"]Stick with Windows. You're not ready for Linux![/COLOR]**

Omygosh, I had offended my friend, and he wouldn't re-install Windows for me. What'll I do?

Total rookie, but desperate, I downloaded a copy of Ubuntu, burned it to a disk, and installed it, completely wiping away Windows on my first try, not even trying to dual-boot, Wubi, or anything like that.

For nearly a year I used it effortlessly, flawlessly, beautifully. I even "distro-hopped" several times, trying out PCLinuxOS, Salix (Slackware-based), Mepis, Crunchbang, U-Lite, Mint, Debian (it took weeks of Googling and trial and error to get that one working, but that's a whole 'nother story), and Xubuntu (my favorite and now "default distro"). I learned to configure and re-install without losing any of my music, bookmarks, e-mail settings, schoolwork, dance choreography, pictures, and all that stuff. It was easy! Even for this silly little technically challenged, mildly autistic kid!

I couldn't help but say so the next time I saw my former computer go-to guy. I told him about no more "rot," none of the expensive bloatware to maintain the OS, and how effortless it had all been. Y'know what he said?!?! Oh, it was priceless!

Wait for it...


> **[COLOR="Purple"]Then stick with Linux, kid. You're not ready for Windows yet![/COLOR]**

Gotta love it!

-Robin
