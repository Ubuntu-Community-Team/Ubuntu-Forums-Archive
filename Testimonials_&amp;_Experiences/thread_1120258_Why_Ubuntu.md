### Post by Afkpuz on 2009-04-08
Well first off, please list the things that are not working properly and we can get to helping you fix those things.  Remember that you did not instantly understand windows.  It took you time to get used to it and to become efficient.  It will take you time to get used to ubuntu and become efficient.  But here are some quick points that might make you like ubuntu more.


1.) Ubuntu will not break unless you make it
Ubuntu has great coast ability.  My windows installs always become bloated and slow.  I've troubleshooted windows for tons of people and their systems are always bloated and slow.  Ubuntu will stay the same speed, as long as **you** don't mess things up.

2.) No viruses
While there technically are a few viruses for linux, the chances of you getting one are ridiculously low.  The risks of destroying linux come from other avenues, but not viruses.

3.) Free and repositories
Ubuntu is free and will always be free.  All the programs for ubuntu are also free and are easy to install.  For example, lets say you want to install mozilla thunderbird to check your email.  Simply search for thunderbird in synaptic package manager, check the box next to it and click apply.  Simple, easy, and wicked fast, considering you're downloading and installing.  Installing windows would mean going to the mozilla website, downloading the install file, waiting through an installation that takes 1-2 mins.  It takes me about 30 seconds from start to finish for me to install thunderbird when I know what I'm doing.

4.) Amazing free programs
Mythtv is a tivo like program which can act as a full-featured media center.  I personally can record 2 analog TV shows and 1 digital and then stream that show to any computer in my house running ubuntu.  Furthermore, I can access that video from any computer (windows and macs included) connected to the internet.  It is also capable of surround sound and is remote control compatible.  All this for free.  There are countless high quality programs on par with mythtv for linux.  

5.)  Updates
Ubuntu is constantly providing updates, including a new version every 6 months. These updates fix glitches and continue to improve preformance

6.)  Customization
You can make ubuntu look however you want.  Don't like the task bar on the bottom?  Then remove it!  You don't even need a taskbar.  You can use gnome-do to access all that you'd ever need.  Don't like the colors of the window controls.  Change em!  If you're ambitious, make your own!  You can do whatever you want with ubuntu.

There are just a few I can think of.  And what kind of trouble are you having with emerald?

### Post by therockindonkey on 2009-04-09
I, too am a new user.  I've been tinkering with both Ubuntu and Mac OS-X Leopard for about the same period of time (approximately 2 weeks).  I find that I actually spend more time using Ubuntu and that I enjoy it much more.  

This is coming from someone who has been using Windows since the spring of 1997.  I have nothing against Windows, but I just have found the whole Ubuntu experience to be by far superior to the Windows experience.  In my opinion, Ubuntu is much more responsive than Windows (no real surprise there).

I've run into a few roadblocks -- I have a great scanner which does not have a Linux driver.  It will be getting replaced in the next year or so when my son is old enough to know what "don't touch that, you might break it" means.  When I do replace it, I will do some research beforehand to make sure that the model I replace it with is supported under Linux.

Most of the roadblocks I've encountered I was able to find solutions for on this forum, either by doing a search, or directly asking.  People here are very helpful (believe me, I really appreciate it).

I will grant you that I am an IT professional, and probably have a bit more patience than the average user when it comes to fixing problems -- sometimes I consider it a challenge.

But I will tell you this:  If you want to get the most out of your computer, regardless of the OS you choose, you need to have some patience and be willing to put some time into learning how to make things work *especially* if you're using Windows, given to all of the vulnerabilities the Microsoft OS has.

I understand that the average user just wants the damned thing to work, with as little effort as possible.  While that's understandable, it's also part of the reason that so many people experience so many problems computing.  Whether it be failure to back up data or simply opening files or attachments without verifying what they are opening.

Anyway, that's my 2 cents on the issue.  Give yourself time to learn the system.  It took me a good 7 months to get to the point with Windows 95 where I knew enough to be dangerous.  Another 4 to 6 months after that, I was installing hardware and tweaking my system to my liking on my own.  Give yourself at least a couple of months and try to be patient.  

Oh, and these forums are indeed great.
