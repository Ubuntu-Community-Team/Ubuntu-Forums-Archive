### Post by steelsnake on 2008-10-27
Just felt like providing a bit of (good) history - my history with Linux that is.

'96 I started my own business. Friend of mine, employee of a big mainframe provider, suggested setting up our servers with Linux. Requirements were easy:
- Safe
- Reliable
- Must offer the usual Internet stuff (email, http, NNTP) on the outside server
- Must provide file sharing on the internal server

Worked like a charm. There were issues - Apache didn't allow virtual hosting back then for example. But all in all it just worked, and for free. He set up Slackware back then, and I still use Slackware for some stuff.

Linux on the desktop however was a PITA (Pain In The...) back then. The apps sucked, the desktop environments sucked and it was all but unusable for business purposes. I tried again and again over the years, but always had to go back to MS Windows or Apple Mac quickly. Various distros, various desktops, you get the idea.

About 5 years ago I decided to take on a challenge again and got hired for a business that desperately needed to upgrade their whole IT infrastructure. They were still working with DOS and modems.

At first, I used a MS Windows desktop. Soon decided that Linux would be better for the job, so I installed trusty Slackware, but hated it for desktop use.

Eventually I stumbled across Ubuntu (v6.something I think). It mostly worked, and required very little fiddling to get it to suit my needs. My needs back then being a multi-monitor setup providing 2 full workstations (each with their own keyboard/mouse) and one status monitor. All running off the same off-the-shelf desktop PC. Beautiful. Rock solid. Productive. Fun to work on (couldn't resist Compiz).

Nowadays, our Linux servers provide everything for the company:
- LAMP stack with fully web based applications (except word processor and spreadsheet, we use OpenOffice for that). This is on stock Slackware.
- Email server with auto-updating (commercial) virus scanners and spam filters. Also Slackware.
- VPN concentrator and firewall for the company WAN. Worldwide. Rock solid, easy to use. Teleworkers love it. Slackware/
- Communication server with jabber, network fax service, Samba, CUPS (all printers are network-attached) and VoIP (video telephony within the company... seen NCIS? Yep, like that) throughout the company. No telephone gateway yet, but eventually that will come too. On Ubuntu HH (8.04)

And guess what? It's all been dirt cheap to implement. Not necessarily easy, but the rewards are well worth it. The company owners are still grinning from ear to ear when they show off the system. And it's easy to maintain.

The future: Each remote location will have its own well-protected server (running Ubuntu), with a bunch of disk- and fanless thin clients (running Linux) attached to it. The first prototype installation is about to go live in a few weeks. I'd expect savings per location of about $1000/year due to reduced maintenance costs. Exciting.

Thinking back to the Linux kernel 0.9x days... Linux and its various application suites have come a long way. I'd still hesitate if my boss would want me to switch all desktops to Linux, but eventually I'd think that's a very real possibility. Thanks, in large parts, to user-friendly distros like Ubuntu in all its variations, the Wine project and as a last resort, VMWare and the like.

Thanks for all the hard work.
