### Post by SeanBlader on 2007-09-21
A few months ago a friend of mine switched his laptop to dual boot with windows. He was enjoying and raving about it so much I thought I'd give it a try. The system I decided to try it on is a Dell Inspiron 8500 Laptop. It's an older laptop, but fairly popular. It came with XP several years ago and at the time was a screaming fast desktop replacement, and my first laptop. I'd tried Vista on it, which I'm sure many won't be surprised to hear brought the system to a crawl. The processor was decent for Vista, but the 64meg nVidia graphics were dated. I'd even upgraded to 2 gigs of ram on the system and Vista was still unfortunate. It's not my primary system anymore, I have a newer Dell XPS600 that runs Vista well and still runs all the other apps I want to use on it. So I felt confident that if something were to go wierd on the laptop it wouldn't affect my daily life and I could search for help online and move stuff to the laptop via sneakernet (USB key) if I needed files on the laptop.

So I went and hung out with my friend and together we walked through the setup of Ubuntu from the Live CD I'd burned earlier that week. Out of the box it just worked, like they say it should. What was missing was Wireless support for the Broadcom (well known and documented), any sleep or hibernate support, and the media buttons on the right side of the keyboard didn't work. Other than that everything was near perfect.

Spent about an hour and got the wireless working through NDISWrapper, which was the main worry of having Ubuntu on the Inspiron. After that it was about getting typically used applications running, Firefox, Thunderbird, and some sort of chat client as the daily use programs.

Distributed with Ubuntu is Firefox 1.5, I had to have 2.0. Easy install with help from the forums here. Same with Thunderbird, repositories all have 1.5 but I had to have the tag's from 2.0 so got that working with little problem too. The cool part there was copying over my Thunderbird profiles folder from windows right into Ubuntu and have all my mail available, brilliant! Pidgin was even easier and supported even more chat protocols than Trillian did, impressive.

During initial setup we did a 2 gig swap partition, after about a week of using Ubuntu and occasionally checking the system monitor, I'm finding that it's NEVER used. The 2 gig's of ram really keep up with any demands that I put to it. 

The fun part is, Ubuntu loads just as fast on the 5 year old Laptop as does Vista on the 1 year old XPS.

But I want to thank all the helpful posters on the forums, some of the how-to's are really great. I got VPN and RDP working last night, so now it's a real business tool, unfortunately. :-) But I still need to find out what needs to happen to get power management and the media player buttons working, but regardless my laptop is microsoft free.
