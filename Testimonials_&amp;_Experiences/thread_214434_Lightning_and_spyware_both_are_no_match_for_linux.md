### Post by Monsuco on 2006-07-12
Well, a few months ago I was repairing an old PC that had been damaged when our house was hit by lightning (not the power lines, our HOUSE was hit). I figured it would never run again, and when I tried booting it up, and it would crash. It's hard drive had apperently been screwed up by the high voltage. Somehow, it turned out that it wasnt damaged, and that the HDD was just corrupted. I considered reinstalling Windows ME onto it, but then it occoured to me that we had three other windows based PCs in our house, so why not try Linux. I had never really done anything with linux before, other than playing around with an old feather linux boot CD. I did some research about linux but heard it was hard to use. I figured, what the heck, I can always format and reinstall Windows if I want to. I never wanted to. I heard that the easiest to use linux distros were Ubuntu, Xandros, Linspire, Mephis, Fedora, and Suse. I also read that Ubuntu was among the easiest of those to use. When I installed Ubuntu, I liked what I saw. A clean desktop interface which could best be described as very practical and functional. It could also be described as being very brown. Boy was it ever brown. I decided to stick with it. Later I would switch over to KDE. True, gnome was more functional, so I kept it installed as well, but KDE was much cooler looking. This was an old 900 Mhz PIII processor with 384 MB of RAM and it ran almost as fast as my Windows XP PC did on a 3 Ghz P4 with 512 MB of RAM (and the windows one had a faster HDD). I was astonished at how well linux detected my hardware and how easy to use the text mode installer was. I was also impressed at how unlike Windows, linux included an office suite and an advanced image editor out of the box. It only took me a few days to become as comfortable with Linux as I was with windows. Sure it took some work to properly configure Wine or to get every last program I wanted on there but all the tutorials and stuff helped me do that easily. Unlike many people transitioning from windows to linux, I fell in love with the command line fairly quickly as it was a quick and standardized way of doing things. 

Later, I would be asked to repair a Windows laptop computer that had been severly screwed up by spyware. It was not pretty. It had been infected severly. My first action was to use portable clamwin on a USB key to kill off the worst of it. Then I installed AVG and killed off more of it. I installed Ad Aware and that removed a large amount of stuff. Then I installed Spybot to remove even more infected crap. I think I then used Knoppix to try to remove more of it, but I dont remember. Anyways, I got rid of this junk and it was back to normal. It no longer put up ads and it let me change the homepage back to what it was. All was well, or so I though. I then opened up Word, it gave me a weird DLL not found error. Then I went to defrag the disk, same error. Then I went to do a disk surface scan, same error. It would crash when I opened most of the programs. This computer did not have an MS product key (it had been lost a while ago) and I knew MS would probably not send a reinstall disk without one. I told the person they could either spend $100 on a new copy of XP or they could try linux for free. I told her it would be much different than Windows. She said to go ahead and install linux as long as she could write word documents, look at pictures, view web sites, and check e-mail that that was all she needed. Well. I managed to install linux onto this laptop. Since it's WiFi card was not supported I got the ndiswrapper thing to work on it. It worked, but it had issues with connecting to encrypted LANs but that didnt take long to fix. I successfully got CPU throttling and auto suspending to work. The only issue was that X seemed to crash with the script that I found and used to handle lid closure suspention. I told her if she wanted to close the lid, just go and hut the suspend button first. That seemed to work flawlessly. It did fairly well, and I guess linux laptop handling isnt so bad once you configure it properly. 

I was also impressed at how it seemed like the high performance ext3 file system never needed defraging and how linux never seemed to crash (though sometimes software crashed, linux could usually kill it sucessfully if it froze). I was also amazed at how well windows software ran on Wine. Wine is commonly described as being hard to use, so I was impressed at how user friendly it wound up being (with the help of WineTools). abou 1/2 of all my Windows stuff ran flawlessly on wine. I was impressed at how much safer sudo was than always being administrative and how securly linux installed new things. I also liked how well linux delt with iPods. I would say linux's support for the iPod with amaroK is better than well, iTunes is with the iPod (amaroK unlike iTunes, lets you take music from your iPod to your PC, not just the other way around). Eventually, I got bored and decided to test linux on both VMWare Player and QEMU (with KQEMU virtualization support). Ubuntu ran at reasonable speeds on those (I think 512 MB of ram wasnt enough for both Linux and Windows though). I then used QParted to multiboot ubuntu and XP on my XP PC. I would say, linux is more "ready for the desktop" than Windows XP. I also think it is funny how KDE (and gnome too) both look better than XP or OSX (and in my opinion, better than Vista) and they both only really need about 128 MB of ram to run while XP needs 256, and Vista needs 512. I have become a huge fan of linux over the past 6 months or so. I am planning to take basic programming (Java) next year as one of my High School courses, and I am glad I learned to use Linux, as GNU Emacs looks like a promising program to learn on. I cant wait.

Thanks Ubuntu comunity for the excelent OS.
