### Post by John Azelvandre on 2007-05-03
Wow, it seems like you've had a particularly bad experience.  As someone who successfullly used Debian sarge for nearly two years before recently adopting Ubuntu, I can say that it usually isn't that bad.

However, I'd like to take a moment to voice my opinion that Ubuntu isn't quite ready for "prime-time," although I really wish it was, given my dislike for Microsoft.  I'm still sticking with Ubuntu anyway, but I hope we as a community can work to really make this distro more accessible to general users.

I'd like to share with everyone my experience starting out fresh with Ubuntu:

Last week I received shipment of a laptop from Linux Certified, Inc.  I had them pre-install Ubuntu 6.10.  Perhaps I should have opted to install it myself, but I figured I could save myself a little time, and also compare the experience to that of getting a Windows Dell or a Mac iBook ready to go.

Initially, it took an unbelievably long time for my computer to boot the first couple of times.  I don't know why.  It has since booted more normally.  Ubuntu doesn't show what's going on the way some distros do, which I would personally prefer.  But the splash-screen would certainly be more appealing to the general user, provided one is not staring at it for 5 minutes.

The following problems were present from the get-go and still haven't been resolved:

1) Some sort of error with the "gnome-settings-daemon" causes login to the gnome desktop to take about 3:30 minutes.  One can afterwards jumpstart the settings daemon, and user settings ARE remembered, so at this point it is mainly an annoyance.  

Keep in mind, in most working environments (for example, in a school, where I work) NO ONE is going to wait 3.5 minutes to log-in.  They will give up and assume the machine is broken, and hit the power button.

2) For some mysterious reason, administrative functions performed through gnome's System/administration menu are buggy.  They sometimes lock-up, sometimes work, in a pattern that I am still trying to map out.  Once again, largely an annoyance factor, because many of the same administrative tasks I can do from the terminal.

3) The add printer dialog in the System/administration menu is broken.  It does not merely fail to recognize my printer, it freezes up.  This may be related to my item number 2 above.  I haven't gotten around to troubleshooting that problem yet.  This is rather a major problem.  I happen to not really need to print anything right now, so it's on the backburner.  Most people would assume Ubuntu was broken and no good if this happened to them.  By 'people' I mean end-users.

4) xsane doesn't work.  I understand from the forums that Ubuntu and scanners don't play nice.  That's unfortunate.  I'm not sure I understand why a scanner is any different than any other piece of hardware, like a camera or a printer or a monitor, but I guess it is.  As an artist, I consider this a major inconvenience.  I am still in the midst of troubleshooting this.  I recall that two years ago it took me a while to get my scanner working on Debian sarge, so I'm hopeful that it will eventually work here.  My question is: why not right away?

5) This a laptop, but power management doesn't work.  Consequently, battery life is compromised.  This isn't going to encourage migration from more energy-smart Macs or PCs.

So, those are the negatives.  I'm working on them, but how many end-users will have the patience to do that?

The positives:
My digital camera worked right away!  
Graphics and display are great, no problems there!
Plug and play recognition of USB devices such as flash memory sticks is flawless!
Gnome desktop is beautiful and functional!
Internet connections, both ethernet and wireless worked out of the box!  Fabulous!
OpenOffice works great (except for a missing form wizard that I hope will turn up ...)
The Gimp is fab!

My point with all of this is: Ubuntu is really not that much easier to set-up than regular Debian, and Debian is not easy to set-up and maintain.  This is still an OS for the determined, the driven, the tech-minded adventurer.  I don't know if I could recommend this laptop to any of my friends (they're not as tech-minded as me) unless I'm willing to commit myself to spending a lot of time troubleshooting for them ...

Just some things to think about ...

Apparently the problem is due to the R31 using the same controller (the i8042) for the keyboard, mouse and power management, and occurs every time something polls the battery. The Wrong Solution is to pass i8042.nomux=1 to the kernel - add it to the #defoptions line in /boot/grub/menu.lst, run "sudo update-grub", and reboot (I can vouch for the fact that this works). The Right Solution is to set this as the default in the next release of the kernel - presumably this is what happened with dapper."

Some alternates:

On CD Live it works to push F6 and add i8042,nomux=1 just after: quiet splash

You can also edit the menu.lst kernel line you're using to add i8042,nomux=1
