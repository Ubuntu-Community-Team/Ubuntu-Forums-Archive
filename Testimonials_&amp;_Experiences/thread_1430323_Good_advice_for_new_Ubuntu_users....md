### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2010-03-16
I think that Ubuntu could save itself a lot of trouble if they had a page like [this one]("http://en.opensuse.org/HCL/Desktops") that OpenSUSE offers to *see if the distro has been tested on your hardware*. You can pick your computer from a list and see in advance if there are issues on that hardware. If Ubuntu offered something like that, it might save people a lot of trouble.

No Linux distro is truly a drop-in replacement for Windows, though many of the big distros make that claim. It simply depends on what you use your computer for. If all you need is a web browser, a word processor, a pdf reader, and a music player, almost any distro will probably work.

The issue with Ubuntu has been, for many users, that it "just works" fine until some kernel update suddenly b0rks the entire system, and they have to identify which update broke their system and then refuse that particular update after a re-install from the LiveCD, or (if they know how) use the older kernel. What Canonical does with the kernel sometimes is just nonsensical when it breaks a working system.

Then there is the issue of including Beta (experimental, unproven, unstable!) software like PulseAudio and Grub2 by default in a distro that is intended for and marketed for the *novice* user. This stuff, when it works, is great. But when it doesn't, it's a headache even experienced Linux users. Throwing it at newbies is just unforgivable.

A number of distros that were once Ubuntu-based have or are switching to a Debian base (Mepis, Crunchbang, others) because of these things.

Ubuntu is simply *not* the be-all and end-all of Linux. And it is becoming less and less suitable for beginners in my opinion, because of these two major issues (experimental kernel updates, Beta software). Newbies are not to be treated as unwitting laboratory rats. 

I send newbies to Mepis or CentOS or OpenSUSE rather than to Ubuntu or anything built on Ubuntu.

If Ubuntu works for you (and it works for me too, with some work), that's great. It's a sweet, easy to use distro when it works (until some update disables the wireless, or the sound, or the video, etc). But **rabid Ubuntu fanaticism** is unfortunate and uncalled for. It doesn't work for *everyone*. And if it is truly *aiming* to work for everyone, that is laudable; but they're not going to achieve that goal on their present course IMO.

-Robin
