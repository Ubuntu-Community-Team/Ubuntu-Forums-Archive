### Post by jerrylamos on 2012-02-14
Having lots of fun here with ubuntu;  This old 2005 IBM Thinkpad T40's running Lucid Lynx and three versions of lubuntu - as upgraded from oneiric ocelot, as installed with generic-pae not supposed to work on the T40, and as installed from mini.iso.  All that plus an archive partition on 40 GB hard drive.

Currently running lubuntu here, I've also run unity-2D works fine, unity-3D on my tower, I have run gnome classic on precise pangolin.

No, I don't ever ever run "update" and never never "partial upgrade".

I do sudo aptitude update, sudo aptitude safe-upgrade.  Now if something important doesn't get installed, I'll run sudo aptitude full-upgrade.

Of course running "unstable development" code expect crashes, bugs, and re-installs.  Actually I do a re-install every so often just looking for bugs.

So if you like command line stuff & reasonable file manager etc. try lubuntu.  Or xubuntu, your choice.  If you like unity try ubuntu unity-2D.  If you like big fuzzy hieroglyphics, foggy borders, lots of side effects, and lots of compiz overhead run unity-3D.  Another "eye candy" choilce is kubuntu, last time I tried it took many more keystrokes and windows than gnome to do the same work.

Oh, when I've screwed up grub (I'm good at that), I've a lucid lynx too.

Jerry

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2012-02-14
> xyzzyman
I'm done with computers and everything, so throwing out my laptop and doing a partial upgrade on life.

I'd be the last person to say that's a bad idea. IMHO many in our  society have become fixated on consumer electronics and have forgotten  to participate in life. Spending huge amounts of time endlessly  chattering into cell phones or playing games, as if life only existed in  the airways or on a printed circuit board. [Lagom]("http://www.wordiq.com/definition/Lagom") (I'm not Swedish, but this word makes sense to me).

I believe that a life change can be an awakening experience. Especially,  if used to improve one's awareness. I wish you the best in your  endeavors and hope you awaken that person inside you that is curious to  those things in the world that must be experienced - not just viewed or  discussed.

Since I became disabled, I've learned how short life really may be.  Living vicariously through a machine or the eyes of others may leave one  empty and discouraged. By investing in ourselves, we learn that  almost anything is achievable - if we're willing to participate. My best  advice: Ignore the opinions of others and enjoy finding that which brings you joy and adds meaning to "your life".

After all, it's just a logical machine, only representing that which is permitted and provided. 

Good Luck
