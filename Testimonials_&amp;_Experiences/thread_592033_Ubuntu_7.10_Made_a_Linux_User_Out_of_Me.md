### Post by mdpalow on 2007-10-26
Hi Everyone,

Have you ever not wanted to spend ~ $300 for Windows and ~ $275 for Office?

Have you ever not wanted to spend upwardly around 4-6 hours installing Windows, all your applications, downloading updates, installing a virus scanner, etc?

I have... many times.

I think I was lucky to just be reading the Ubuntu web site the day before the release of 7.10. I had for a couple of years now wanted to load Linux and see what it was all about. However, at the time - Linspire and SuSE wouldn't work for me and I could NEVER get my wireless network going.

I had actually downloaded the Ubuntu 7.04 the day before 7.10 came out and it didn't work on my laptop. I got a good display and sound worked, but as usual - no wireless. I did however read that 7.10 would have better drivers and was suppose to support Broadcom. Bearing that in mind, I waited a day and then downloaded 7.10.

When I installed 7.10 on to my laptop, I got really excited at the end of installation 'cause I saw that it was about to establish a connection with my broadcom wireless HW. When it was done loading, my wireless light came on and sure enough, I was connecting to the Internet!

Ok, game ON, right!

I'm gonna start off by saying this... it's been since the 18th of October (release of 7.10) and I have not yet booted back into Windows. I can't even find a reason to boot into Windows now.

Ubuntu is an incredible OS and in my opinion is even better looking than Windows. Here are some key points that I love about it:

1. The entire system installs on my computer in 18 minutes and 30 seconds.

2. There are only 9 updates as of this writing and they take all of about 30 seconds to install.

3. Yes, WINE works and I was able to install - rather easily - Photoshop and Dreamweaver. Get this... I like Ubuntu so much, that I actually did another install and this time didn't even load WINE. I'm gonna go with Gimp and Kompozer instead and they come preloaded in the system, so install of those two apps. took all of about 30 seconds and I didn't even have to put the install disk back in the drive! Yes, there will be a learning curve, but I'm willing to try it. BTW, I'm sure Kompozer is NOT as good as Dreamweaver, but I'm not that big into it to really care.

4. I spent more time configuring the way my Desktop looks than I did installing everything.

5. Synaptic Package Manager has hundreds of programs you can install in literally less than a couple minutes. You'll spend more time looking for programs you want to have then it will take to install them. :) You get to Synaptic from your menu and just put a check mark next to the program you want. No Serial #'s and no $. Nice!

6. Evolution E-mail (default e-mail app.) is as good or better than Outlook Pro. Outlook Express doesn't even compare.

7. Backing up your system is CRAZY EASY! With one SIMPLE line of text typed into your terminal, I was able to back up my entire system WHILE it was running. No boot disk, no going into a DOS environment to backup files because while in Windows some files are being used. No additional software needed. NICE! Yes, there are GUI apps you can install from Synaptic, but you won't need them.

8. When you're done installing your OS, you already have the Open Office Suite ready to go along with Gimp. These are compared to MS Office and Photoshop. Open Office is completely compatible with MS Office. That saved me another 30 minutes of installing s/w.

9. Ever want to watch a movie, but don't have the codecs to read it, so you can watch it. I double clicked on a movie and it told me it needed a codec. Asked me if it could search for one. I allowed it and it came up with two to select. I installed them (15 seconds) and the movie started to play.

10. Love your MSN Messenger? Go to Synaptic menu options and search for aMSN. Click it and install it. You'll be up and running on the MSN Messenger network in about 1 minute with all your friends showing as if you were in MSN Messenger.

11. Want to be virus safe? I read recently that in the history of Linux, there have been no more than 50 viruses. Apparently viruses don't work on Linux because of the way permissions work. NICE! Apparently there are 1,000 viruses a month for Windows.

12. The file system in Ubuntu requires a little time to get used to. I'm not used to it yet, but I'm starting to get it.

13. Learning how to install a program that is not in the Synaptic Package Manager can be a little difficult AT FIRST, but if you find one that is done well, it's pretty easy. I simply did a search on "How to install applications in Ubuntu" and found a lot of sites with the method.

14. Was able to install a firewall in all of about 1 minute from Synaptic and had it configured in about 30 seconds; it's that easy.

15. Yes, there is a slight learning curve and you have to be willing to search the Internet for answers to your questions. I did and was always directed back to the Ubuntu documentation. Maybe I should have started there in the first place. :) My questions to get fully going with this OS were: How to install 3rd party apps that weren't in Synaptic (but since re-install, I don't even have any 3rd party apps. I want/need to install), How to use SUDO command, and how to get a graphical file manager to work with root powers. It seemed difficult at first to remember a few text combinations, but now it's ridiculously easy. Just give it a few days at most and you'll get it.

16. Hardest part of install was figuring out how I wanted to partition my HD. If you want to use your entire drive it's easy. However, I wanted to use a spare drive and partition based on some things I learned reading the forum. Learned that you should have four partitions:

1st: /   (allowed 50 gigs for OS. Should have done 20 gigs)
2nd: /home  (used the remaining MINUS 5 gigs for this partition)
3rd: /Windows (used 2gigs (Fat 32) so both OS's could use files)
4th: /swap   (used the remaining 3 gigs for this one)

I used EXT3 file format for 1st, 2nd, and 4th. 

Note: I read SWAP drive should be twice a much as your RAM. I have  1 gig, but I opted to go with 3 gigs just in case. :)

I've never been much of a partitioner, so I spent about 30 minutes in this configuration tool to get it right. Second time around I zipped right through this in about a minute.

17. Ubuntu 7.10 asks you about 5 questions before it begins installing.

  1. What's your language
  2. What time zone are you in
  3. What keyboard do you use
  4. What's your name and what password do you want to use
  5. How do you want to use/partition your HD

Doesn't get much easier than that.

18. Ubuntu 7.10 also recognized my Linksys wireless PCI card and on board ethernet for Internet access.

19. I have an Nvidia FX 5200 video card and on my desktop 7.10 would not be displayed on my LCD monitor. I'm almost positive it was loaded and running, but the monitor wasn't showing it. I rebooted and used the 2nd option in the Live CD boot list. Once installed, I was able to boot into Ubuntu and see the screen with a regular boot up. Ubuntu did recognize I had an Nvidia VC and asked me if I wanted to install some drivers for it. I allowed it and then it rebooted and now I even have desktop effects like in Vista.

20. Both my CD/DVD ROMs work perfectly.

21. USB's work fine to include my Logitech webcam.

Ok, so now a list of what I have loaded and use:

Ubuntu 7.10 obviously
Open Office
aMSN - Messenger
Firefox Web Browser
Evolution E-mail
K3B - similar to Nero
Firestarter - Firewall
Kompozer - Web Design
Gimp - Like Photoshop
Bittorrent
gFTP - File Transfer Protocol for uploading files to ftp site
Movie player
A few music programs to rip, sort, and play music
I don't play games on computer, so none of those

Finally, what is my computer level skill? Well, I wouldn't say that I'm a Windows system manager, but I do know my way around Windows better than the majority of the population. When it comes to Linux I'm a complete newbie and until this past weekend I knew absolutely NOTHING about Linux or Ubuntu. I can search the web though and read/understand instructions on how to do something as long as it doesn't get too Linux complicated.

Assuming your Internet works along with your hardware, you would be remiss to not try Ubuntu. It's that good.

Also, if you download the OS from the Ubuntu site and burn the .iso file to a disk and put it in your cd rom, it'll load into what they call Live CD. That basically runs the OS without installing anything to your current HD. When you take out the disk and reboot your computer - nothing will have been changed and it will be as if you never tried it. Win/Win situation. Also, I was actually able to setup e-mail in Live CD mode to test Evolution and it worked. When I rebooted into Live CD another time (before installing), that setup was gone and I simply did it again. 

Finally, when you run the Live CD, there will be an icon on the desktop that says "Install." Simply double click it and you'll be on your way to installing the OS.

Ok, hope this will prove useful to someone who is considering Ubuntu as an alternative to Windows.
