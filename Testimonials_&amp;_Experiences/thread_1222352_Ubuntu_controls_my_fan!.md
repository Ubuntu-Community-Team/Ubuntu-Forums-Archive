### Post by ratcheer on 2009-07-24
I use a 5 year old Shuttle XPC small form factor PC. It has a high performance (for the day) Pentium IV processor that runs quite hot. When I run Windows, I have to run a Shuttle utility called XPC Tools to control the fan speed to keep the system cool enough. I always thought this would prevent me from using Linux, because no Linux version of the utility is available.

A few weeks ago, I installed Ubuntu, anyway. To my great surprise, the fan is controlled perfectly, with no additional software. I cannot understand why this is so, but I am glad it is.

### Post by Marlonsm on 2009-07-24
Maybe it's your Bios that controls the fan in Ubuntu.
My Bios has the option of under a certain temperature the CPU fan will run at a lower RPM and over that temp, it'll run at a higher RPM, and I can change the RPMs and the threshold temperature.

So maybe it's not the OS.

What might happen for Windows to get hotter is either that it loads the computer more than Ubuntu do or maybe that it overrides the Bios fan controlling.

### Post by ratcheer on 2009-07-25
Replies to several points:

The fan does not seem to be controlled at all in Windows (without XPC Tools set to control it, I mean). The fan just runs at a very low speed, all the time. The CPU will heat up to over 70 C. With XPC Tools running, I can keep it to 55 withhout a load and 62 when it is under a load.

I imagine that it is the BIOS controlling it under Ubuntu and Windows is probably doing something to defeat the BIOS control. In Ubuntu, the fan speeds up and slows down as the load dictates. From my XPC Tools experience (see above), I know that it makes a significant difference.

Finally, it is not that my system is easily stressed, it is that it has one of the early P IV Extreme CPU's that are high performance, but run hot. In fact, this very PC was used at the World Cyber Games in 2004. They sold them at discount prices after the games were over. It was a very hot (no pun intended) machine in its day. I have two of them.
