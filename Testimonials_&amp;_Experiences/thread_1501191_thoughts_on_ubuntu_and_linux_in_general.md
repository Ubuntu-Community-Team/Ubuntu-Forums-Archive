### Post by plutoniumpenguin on 2010-06-04
Hello.

I've used linux for about one year now.  I use two  computers (a very old HP laptop that somehow still operates) and a  custom-built state-of-the-art desktop PC built half a year ago.  My  desktop PC is not in any way optimized for gaming, but is more suited  for heavy mathematical computation.  I've used Ubuntu  (Hardy/Jaunty/Karmic/Lucid), Debian (Lenny/Squeeze), SuSE (whatever was  out last year), Linux Mint (the current version), Fedora (12/13), and  LFS (version doesn't really matter so much).  I also tried Gentoo once,  but gave up on it once I realized that their software repositories do  not really have any software that's more up-to-date than Ubuntu's.   Here's some of my thoughts on each distro:

Ubuntu --- The best  linux I've used, and the one I've used most.  In general it's pretty  buggy, but thankfully not as much as Fedora.  The advantages are that  it's got pretty good software repositories, and that it's the most  widely used (so that it's usually pretty easy to find the necessary  information online when something does go wrong).  The disadvantages, in  addition to general instability, are that the LiveCD choice of software  really blows, and that the "help" documentation is  absolutely/ridiculously terrible (I'm talking about what you get when  you click on the "Help and Support" option in the "System" menu).  The  LiveCD software really is not a valid complaint, but, for example, the  software that I don't ever use includes UbuntuOne, Empathy, Gwibber,  Transmission, Piviti, Evolution, F-Spot, Ubuntu Software Center, Tomboy  Notes, any kind of game, anything to do with scanning, anything to do  with printing, anything to do with remote desktop, anything to do with  IM, etc...  Yep, I understand that it's a simple matter to uninstall  them or just to not use them and ignore their presence, but my point is  that I think it would just be a lot nicer if they kept their LiveCD to  the "bare-minimum", or close to it.  Instead, maybe about 1/4 of it is  filled with junk applications (yeah, I know, probably quite a few people  use the applications that I listed above, but I would be shocked if it  wasn't true that most of them didn't use a couple handfuls of apps).  At  least, this makes the most sense to me, because you can readily  download any package you want anyways, and, further, in the case where  you simply didn't have the internet, well, then you'd be screwed if you  were using Ubuntu and hadn't prepared for it!  Oh, and as far as the  "Help and Support" is concerned --- the man pages and the info pages are  infinitely more valuable, and since the very beginning when I started  using this I quickly made the decision to rely on the internet for help  with problems (which is saying something, because at the time I didn't  have internet at my house, so I would have to walk a couple of blocks to  library to get on!).  

Debian --- I've used this almost as much  as Ubuntu.  Lenny is the most stable linux I've used for any appreciable  length of time.  However, the software is so old, that it won't even  install on my desktop PC (from the CD, although I can install from  USB).  In general, I don't think it's really suitable for the home user,  but perhaps for someone who runs old hardware and won't have any need  to change their system for a long time.  I like their LiveCD better in  the sense that it's pretty basic (i.e. nothing flashy, no over-the-top  attempt at "integrating the experience", and almost no aesthetic appeal  at all).

LinuxMint --- The only differences between this  distribution and Ubuntu are completely trivial.  I'm aware that many  people for some reason prefer it over Ubuntu, but, honestly, it's about  as different from Ubuntu as Barack Obama wearing a business-suit is from  Barack Obama wearing sweatpants.

OpenSUSE --- I hate this  distribution.  It is incredibly bloated, and I just really don't like  it's whole "style" in general.  By FAR the worst.

Fedora --- It's  really pretty good, but practically speaking it's even noticeably less  stable than Ubuntu which makes it difficult to use.  But what makes  Fedora really stand out, in my mind, is their no-nonsense attitude.   Their LiveCD is about as good as you could expect - in the overall same  style as Debian's, but just slightly more polished.  Also, it seems as  though they release updates more frequently than Ubuntu (I guess they'd  have to, though!).  

LFS --- Too much work!  I went through the  "basic" LFS, and then looked at what I had to do to get a desktop  environment up, and got quite discouraged (good learning experience,  though).  It's actually easy enough for a newbie to get this system up  and running (I had only used linux for a few weeks before I did this).   But if problems are encountered, it could be pretty difficult to find a  solution (esp. if you have little to no experience with linux).  

As  for linux in general: I really think it's great, in many respects it is  superior to Windows and Mac OS X.  Anything that's even somewhat  technical is usually much easier to do in linux than in Windows.  In  fact, if you wanted to do anything technical in Windows, whomever you  asked for help would almost certainly say something like "Now, now, why  would you want to do that?  There's plenty of solutions out there!".   I'm referring to tasks such as systematically listing every file on the  hard disk, or determining what files where accessed by some program,  etc...  There's no doubt in my mind that anything you can do in linux  you can also do in Windows, but it's just that many things happen to be a  lot easier in linux.  Also, the fact that there's so much free software  is a gigantic "plus" (although, truthfully, maybe only 15% are of any  real value).  On the other hand, Windows offers unparalleled hardware  support.  I guess linux comes close, but in my mind, that is the most  significant weakness that linux has at the moment.  For example, I use  DVD-RW's.  Oh, they are supported in linux, you say?  Well, sure, but I  can't erase them or even re-write ones that have been erased PROPERLY in  Windows.  So whenever I want to burn a CD, I have to log out of linux,  restart my computer, and use Windows to burn the CD.  What a drag.   Also, the amount of problems linux has with NVIDIA cards is enormous.   And then there's system suspension/hibernation which I haven't ever seen  successfully work - might as well just shut down the system anyways.   Oh, and only maybe half of the stuff on my motherboard and cpu have  functional drivers.  In fact, I honestly think that hardware support is  the major reason why more people aren't using linux.  

Where is  Ubuntu heading?  I don't know, but actually I like it less with each  release.  It seems as though one of their main goals is to get almost  everything done with a "click of the mouse".  Is that a good thing?   Well, look at Windows.  Do you like having pop-up dialog boxes show up  whenever you try to do anything at all?  Do you like having to click on  three or four in succession simply because of 1 error?  I don't.  I  think that's stupid.  Just like how all of the "improvements" in  Internet Explorer 8, for example, or Office 2007, for another, while  they may be of some value, mostly just end up making the process of  using their products more of a chore.  That's what will happen if you  try to put everything into a mouse-click.  

And, just because  it's more funny than anything, the moving of the maximize, minimize, and  close buttons on the windows from the left to the right --- clearly  there wasn't an overwhelming demand for this before it was implemented,  and it just almost seems like somebody was thinking "Well, how can we  make Ubuntu really stand out?  How can Ubuntu be put above the rest, and  make it clear that it's not just another Windows rip-off?  I  know...we'll...move the windows buttons...".  Just like the guy who  tries to get everybody's attention by wearing his pants backwards.

### Post by freebeer on 2010-06-04
Nice post.  I enjoy reading about other distros (and compared to Ubuntu) since I really don't have the time to distro hop and get a feel for alternatives.

With regards to Ubuntu's direction, and in particular its insistence of including the "kitchen sink", I'm personally ok with that.  I look at it in the sense that they've made a decision to position the distro as easy as possible for people to discover the alternative that is Linux.  To do that, they include lots of stuff that they think the average non-techie needs in order to have a positive first impression.  I think the popularity of Ubuntu speaks to their success in that regards.

I think of Ubuntu as "Linux with training wheels".  Sure, it can be annoying for experienced, technically-oriented individuals to have to remove the training wheels.  But it's so simple to do for such an experienced person that I find it a non-issue.  (Obviously your mileage may vary.)

And sometimes I'm happy to be able to flip those training wheels down when I'm traveling over unfamiliar terrain.  :P

With regard to all of the extra packages and features installed with ubuntu-desktop, as mentioned above by **mastablasta**, you can do a minimal install. Personally, I use the [Minimal CD]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Installation/MinimalCD"). After installing the command-line system, I run:

```
sudo apt-get update
sudo apt-get install xorg fluxbox feh
startx

```and build up from there.
