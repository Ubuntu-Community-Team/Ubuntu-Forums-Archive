### Post by venom104 on 2011-12-31
Here is the video I am talking about, [http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5Qj8p-PEwbI&feature=related](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5Qj8p-PEwbI&feature=related). I know many of you have probably seen this video before, but I thought it would be a good starting point for my testimonial on ubuntu.

Most people that use computers are fing retarded and lazy; it takes work to learn something new, and most people are taught how to use windows and get accustomed to being bottlefed by an easy to use operating system. I am a computer science major, and was more than willing to teach myself how to use various unix like operating systems (I've had my share of problems with fedora, mandrake, suse, arch, and linux mint), and I enjoyed the ride. I learned how to compile programs, install packages from source, compile my own kernel, install/add/remove kernel modules from a running kernel, and many other things. Most users don't give a damn when it comes to learning something new. I believe this is where Ubuntu falls short of the typical user experience.

Ubuntu has great scripts and add-ons that do most of the hard stuff for you, a good package manager that resolves dependencies, a graphical program that updates your system for you (unlike in debian where the default package updater is aptitude), and even a "restricted drivers" applet that downloads proprietary drivers, compiles them, and installs them for you. I also enjoy the fact that while most of the hard stuff is abstracted for the average user, detailed guides here on the forums tell hardcore users how to get down and dirty with the terminal, to do things the hard way.

What I'm getting at, is ubuntu is a GREAT operating system, and one I continue to use year after year. I couldn't imagine doing anything different (except for upgrading to GNOME 3.0, I feel that was a horrible mistake); but, Ubuntu will probably never be accepted world-wide because it's DIFFERENT from windows, and there is a learning curve which most people aren't willing to go through.

So to sum up my user experience (and add my own feedback on ubuntu, much the way the youtube video did), I give all versions of Ubuntu up to 10.04 (I've had technical issues going past 10.04 that I hope will be resolved when the next LTS version comes out) an A+.

Thank you Richard Stallman, Linus Torvalds, Dennis Ritchie, Brian Kernighan, Ken Thompson, all the people that make GNU, GNU, and all the people that developed Ubuntu for making my all time favorate operating system. Keep up the good work! (And thank you for letting us easily switch between desktop managers without having to [FONT=monospace]screw with the [/FONT].xinitrc file!
