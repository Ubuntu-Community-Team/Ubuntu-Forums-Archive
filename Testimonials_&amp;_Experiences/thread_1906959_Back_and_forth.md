### Post by GraeW on 2012-01-10
I have been aware of, and interested in the whole Linux platform, since the mid-1990's when a friend showed me RedHat. I tinkered with the installations, but not having the deeper knowledge, it was an interesting experience. I did try to learn some basics about the command line (bash, etc) and even attempted to dabble with Perl and Python. But without serious training, I never got far.
 
RedHat started with a good system, but was a little clunky at start. When they went with the whole "Enterprise" thing, I had already been digging into Mandrake/Mandriva and never looked at Fedora. SuSE was ok, but I had a few similar issues with thier system which reminded me of early days with RedHat. Debian was probably my favorite of the earlier versions, but without more training/understanding/ability with the core items on the command line, I put a lot of time into getting nearly nothing accomplished.
 
About 4 years ago, after all that playing around with the others, I came across Ubuntu. If it weren't for others in the household being too tied to their Windoze, I probably would never go back to that "dark side" again. It gives the best of all things Linux, I think - the Debian base is well worked so everything comes together smoothly during the initial installation, and anything which might need to be added afterwards just seems to WANT to work. This is what most users want out of their systems, and part of the reason Microsoft has such a hold on things (yeah, say what you will, even with the problems and the old BSODs, it still is a point-and-click environment for the average person). I do like the way things are headed for Ubuntu, and hope to see even more improvements as time goes on. (I have also tried Mint, but it just didn't quite "feel" right to me.. I can't really put a definite reason why; it installed well enough, but the minor differences from Ubuntu to Mint just didn't do anything for me).
 
As far as desktop environments and window managers, I have played with most of them. I really liked the looks of AfterStep, but it was so difficult to me to really understand the configurations that I have loaded it, played with it, but in the end it just takes up space. WindowMaker does a little better, things seem a bit more standardized, but activity with that has slowed dramatically as well. Enlightenment similarly so, and at first was a big resource hog. Gnome and KDE seemed to try the best, but each had their own issues. After playing around with some "true Unix" systems, I like the Motif environment, and soon found XFCE. This is probably going to remain my environment/manager of choice, because it just seems to WANT to work.. mostly.. I do have one minor issue (posted in the appropriate area) regarding the WM Dock app 'swallower' for the XFCE panel. If I can't get that to work, it's not really the end of the world - everything else I have set up with Ubuntu 11.10 really just works as I want.
 
I can see the dilemma with using Unity. I think it does have potential, but it does need more work for configurability. Along with many others, I don't really like the bar down the left side like that, and would prefer to be able to move it to the bottom or right. I also am fond of widgets and apps which can show necessary information in the panel - although gkrellm and cronky are nice, I like the smaller and combined footprint of just using the panel/taskbar.
 
Anyhoo.. I may not know as much as some, but I have a fair amount of longevity with Linux. Ubuntu seems to be doing for the general user what really needs to be done to make this a more popular option to Windows and MacOS. I would almost say Ubuntu puts GNU/Linux on par with those two - the only thing truly missing is the high-end gaming with the "latest and greatest" titles.. and even Wine/Cedega are getting better at their magic.
 
..//Grae
