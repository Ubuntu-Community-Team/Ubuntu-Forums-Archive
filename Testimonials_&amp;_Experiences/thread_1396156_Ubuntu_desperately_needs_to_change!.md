### Post by alberthrocks on 2010-02-01
**(Before you read this, please understand that although I'm frustrated, I still love Ubuntu. But things have got to change.)**

Ubuntu 9.04.... what does that bring to mind? Crashes. Intel driver problems. ACPI issues. And the list goes on...

Ubuntu 9.10.... what does that bring to mind? More crashes. Video card driver issues. Kernel crashes. Wireless card problems. And so on...

There's a popular phrase about Ubuntu: with every release, **Ubuntu _takes one step forward and two steps back._** I hate to say it, but I agree.

I started using Ubuntu around 3 years ago. Everything worked. It was a fun distribution. It was actually easy to use and was pretty fun too.

Then came newer ones. Bug reports and forum topics started filling up on all the issues with each release.

Pretty soon, crashes occur. Work is lost. People got frustrated and left Ubuntu.

Now, you might be thinking: why am I mentioning this? Because it's happening to me. 

I upgraded to 9.10 after a good 2 months have passed. I have to say that I'm very disappointed with it. If I can't use wireless internet, or maintain a stable system without having to do some fixes here and there, there is a big problem.

Let's step back a bit to last year. Last year, Ubuntu announced that they will be ditching GIMP in favor of another photo editor. ([http://arstechnica.com/open-source/news/2009/11/giving-up-the-gimp-is-a-sign-of-ubuntus-mainstream-maturity.ars)]("http://arstechnica.com/open-source/news/2009/11/giving-up-the-gimp-is-a-sign-of-ubuntus-mainstream-maturity.ars") 
The reason? Ubuntu wanted to become "mainstream". Unfortunately, running around in a terminal to fix a system is not mainstream. Furthermore, forcing technology down a user's throat (cough Pulseaudio cough) isn't quite mainstream either. 

Let's just say Ubuntu called themselves mainstream, even though it isn't really stable at all.

**Here's what I have to say to the Ubuntu developers:**

First of all, **WHY DID YOU RELEASE SOMETHING THAT WAS BROKEN ENOUGH TO HAVE PEOPLE RUSHING ON TO THE FORUMS WONDERING WHY THEY CAN'T USE THEIR SYSTEM DUE TO VIDEO CARD DRIVERS ISSUES?** Seriously, do you think that's mainstream? If you do, you might as well stop making Ubuntu, because that's certainly not human. The fix is a pretty simple one, yet the developers overlooked it. Remember, NOT EVERYONE HAS A QUAD CORE 8GB MEMORY 2TB HDD ATI Radeon HD 5970 PC! SO what if it works for you? The other 95% will have issues! My suggestion: how about TESTING UBUNTU ON 1000 PCs with different HW configurations before releasing? Maybe that will solve your "mainstream" issues.

Second of all, **DOES RUNNING AROUND IN A TERMINAL TO FIX A WIRELESS DRIVER SOUND "MAINSTREAM" TO YOU?** Seriously, not all of the people in the world are geeks. In fact, because of that, Bug #1 isn't fixed, and Apple gains more marketshare. Can't you test the drivers and make them as good as the proprietary ones? Don't expect the people who use Ubuntu to not be afraid of the terminal. Mention a terminal and when they start to use it, they'll get upset and leave. Yeah, Ubuntu. Really "mainstream".

Finally, **DO YOU THINK THAT HAVING NEW FEATURES BUT NOT BEING STABLE IS MAINSTREAM?** SO what if I have a 10 second bootup? If I get a crashed computer after that 10 second bootup, I'm _**not**_ happy. So what if I have a really new program? It's not really useful if it brings my computer down, or reduces workability. Then after all that new stuff, we must use the terminal. I'm not going to ask my friends to use a terminal, because I know they won't like it. Terminal is for geeks, not amateurs who just want something that works. Nothing works well in Ubuntu, at least not core wise. How can you have a good OS if the foundation is broken? I don't want a 2.6.33-7d3b56ba37a95f1f370f50258ed3954c304c524bgit kernel. I want something that works and is stable for daily use.

**=================================================**
Enough ranting. :)
**It's time for a change.**

Look at the iPad. **It's simple, and easy to use**. 
Personally, I hate it, and despise it very much. However,
I can see it gaining sales. Why? It's stable and easy to use.

Windows 7 got many sales, and positive reviews. Why? It's also
easy to use, and is very stable.

**Macs got sales for obvious reasons - _simplicity_.**

Note that those OSes/devices **don't require a terminal to use** (and fix).
So where's Ubuntu? Most posts in this forum refer to a terminal command to install something. **People don't like the terminal.** We geeks do, but no one else will.

**It's 2010 - another decade. Where are we now?** Still 1% market share, and not much of an increase. Requiring a terminal for fixing/using Ubuntu is certainly not a good idea anymore. 10 years ago, I wouldn't have cared if Linux needed a terminal. Now, I care. Of all those 10 years, I still haven't seen a case where I could just install and never ever touch a terminal again. 

**It's time for a change.** We've sat here long enough, and still nothing big has happened. Most people on the street have never heard of Linux, and if they have, don't know what it is. 

Ubuntu Linux has **MAJOR** potential in grabbing market share. It has innovation (3D FX), and others. We can make a difference. The GUI design is innovative, and makes it very friendly to use. But with all the core (kernel, drivers, etc.) issues, how is Ubuntu ever going to establish an identity?

**_Ubuntu must become stable._** It needs to stop coming from Debian's unstable repository. Occasionally, unstable packages that are well tested and proven to be stable can migrate down. However, all packages (and the system itself) must be stable in order to become mainstream. If not, we'll never see Ubuntu become famous in the general public, nor ever fix bug #1.

**We must start NOW.** This is a new decade. (I've said it again, haven't I?)
This is the future. We can shock the world with something new yet stable.
We can start... **_now._** :D

To get things rolling, here's a list of things that should be fixed/improved/added in Ubuntu. Please contribute general ideas here. This is a community. We should work together to guide the future of Ubuntu.

_**List of proposed changes for the future:**_

**Social/Identity/Workflow**

[LIST]
[*]Move away from being like Windows - if WINE isn't workable in real life, why bother pretending to be Windows? We must design something new. Pretending to be Windows will only prompt a user to buy TurboTax and install it on Ubuntu, only to fail because it can't be installed.
[*]Listen to the people on Ubuntu Brainstorm and implement those ideas. There are great ideas there that are very nice.
[*]Stop focusing on yourself (the devs) and focus on what people want. GNOME specifically has this problem. KDE used to have this problem, but now they've improved and learned their lesson. Ubuntu might be edging towards it, but not there yet - and hopefully will never be there.
[*]Start development of stopped projects. (Like Compiz, etc.)
[/LIST]
 
**Stablility/Kernel**

[LIST]
[*]Use stable packages, and only implement new features/drivers if they have been thoroughly tested.
[*]Drivers shouldn't require terminal use.
[/LIST]
 
**Interface/Usability**

[LIST]
[*]Move away from the terminal. If we have to use the terminal to fix a driver or do something, this will never become mainstream. Make the drivers stable and full featured so that we won't ever need the terminal. At the same time, make fixes not require the terminal. (No "run" dialog either!) Something easy to use and has a GUI is a must.
[*]Allow customization to the fullest, while offering a simple GUI upfront.
[*]Create a new interface, without sacrificing old habits and hardware necessities.
[*]Install Flash and restricted codecs automatically. The rule is not to include them on a CD, but who says you can't install it automatically?
[*]Put the Power button in the login screen - it disappeared in 9.10...
[/LIST]
 Add any categories/ideas you want. This is a community effort.
We will decide our future. If you want to speak, speak! ;)

### Post by kaitwospirit on 2010-02-02
As one of many people who is currently using the Lucid Lynx alpha, I'd just like to let you know that Ubuntu *does* test its OS on thousands of computers before moving into a full release, every time. We are, of course, limited by what computers our alpha and beta testers have: it's done by a community of volunteers, not by purchasing a huge bank of random computers off the market. Just understand that we do work hard to test each release as much as possible - I know I do - and that claiming that we don't at all might make those of us that do this work feel a little miffed. 

You might be more pleased with the plans for Lucid, though. Usually we only have one beta, but for Lucid, there are two planned beta releases before the final release, with software freezes earlier in development than usual so everything can be tested even more thoroughly. Lucid Lynx is going to be an LTS (long-term support) upgrade so everything is being done to make it as stable as possible. 

If your machine has a history of having hardware problems, you can help out so that your problem and others with your hardware don't have as many issues. Even if you have no coding ability (I don't!), submitting bug reports on Launchpad or marking bug reports with "affects me too" and supplying followup information when asked helps the developers to know what's going on and how they can fix your problem.
