### Post by 23meg on 2008-05-29
The [fixed release cycle]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/TimeBasedReleases") is a very important trait of Ubuntu, and release quality can be increased without changing it as well (hint: more eyes, more work done per unit time). In all the forum discussions regarding release cycle periods over the years, I have not seen a single solid argument as to how exactly having a longer release cycle will help release quality. All I keep seeing is the general assumption that having a longer release cycle will automatically result in higher release quality and better QA, with which I strongly disagree.

Such an argument would have to take into account no less than the following:

[list][*]How exactly the resources that are assumed to be freed as a result of the longer release cycle would be mapped to QA and distribution-level bug fixing, and how much of an improvement this would actually bring

[*]What changes would occur in the upstream development and QA interactions, such as packaging, bug forwarding, patching, publishing updates, and policies regarding those 

[*]What changes would have to be introduced into the [development]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/UbuntuDevelopment") and [QA]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/Bugs") procedures, and the impact these changes would have on the work of contributors in these areas 

[*]How the side effects of going out of sync with upstream projects such as GNOME an Linux would be curbed (or if they can be curbed at all)

[*]How the side effects of breaking the Ubuntu promise of predictable biannual releases to potential customers, prospective users and existing derivative distributions would be handled (or if they can be handled at all)

[*]What kind of shift of identity and purpose the whole Ubuntu project would have to undergo as a result of redefining its release policy
[/list]

### Post by Keyper7 on 2008-05-29
Why do some people insist in oversimplifying such an complex issue?

- Yes, several bugs were fixed after release, but the difference between an official release userbase size and a alpha/beta/rc userbase size is huge. Would bugs be fixed so quickly if launchpad depended only on beta testers?

- What is one of Debian's releases most praised features? Stability. What is one of the most frequent complaints against Debian's releases? Obsolete versions of packages.

- Yes, sticking strictly to a release schedule might mean bugs not being fixed on time, but it can also mean (and anyone who follows a bit of Ubuntu's developement knows that's true) that people will really focus on fixing important bugs exactly because there's a schedule involved.

I'm not saying Canonical's method is correct, but it is quite naive to believe that they didn't think about this A LOT before deciding on the six-month schedule.

My point is precisely that there's no such thing as the perfect method. Whatever schedule or policy you propose as an alternative, it will have conceptual and practical flaws.
