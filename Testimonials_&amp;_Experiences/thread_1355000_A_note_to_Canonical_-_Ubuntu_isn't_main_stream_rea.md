### Post by rhardie on 2009-12-14
I have been using Ubuntu for about 2 years and after all this time, I don't see where Ubuntu is ready for the main stream, non-technical user.

A friend said he had heard of Ubuntu from his sister and asked me to load it onto his HP system.  He said he didn't need to do anything other than email, photos and web browsing.  Perfect candidate for Ubuntu!  :-)

I burned a copy of 9.10, loaded it, did the Nvidia driver updates, restored his data back onto his system and did an upgrade.

BOOM!  X server crash and sent me to command line.

The upgrade totally and absolutely destroyed the X server and cleared out the xorg.conf.

I've performed three installs and each has crashed after certain updates.

I'm just technical enough to be able to Google for answers and then implement them in "most" cases and I had to get a resident Linux expert to help find the problem and recommend a re-backup and re-install of the system but to 8.10 LTS.

I spent a good part of Saturday and a good part of Sunday evening working on this for my friend.

I had regular income-producing work to accomplish this past weekend and I lost productive time that costs me...literally thousands of dollars of future business revenue.  But I had my friend's only computer that he and his wife rely on and I could not just put it off unless I wanted to have an adverse impact on a friendship.

I don't have time to find out what caused the crashes and make sure they don't happen again for my friend.

My friend's image of Linux is a little tarnished right now and he may just decide he would rather pay for Windows 7 and not have the headaches.  I'm inclined to agree.

It's one thing to be a geek or a Linux entheusist and it's quite another to be an average, ordinary user who wants the system to load up and just work.

Yes, most people are not too bright and a bit lazy but that is a fact of life we have to live with.  Unfortunately, those same, not-too-bright and somewhat-lazy people get to vote with a) their choice of OS install and b) their pocket books.  This is true for single users to business owners.

I am a business owner and I look at the "cost of ownership" of Linux and it's cheaper to own Windows due to the monetary value of my time to fix my own problems when I should be out generating new business to pay the bills and my people or the actual dollars spent paying someone else to fix the problems.  This is Microsoft's argument against Linux...and sadly, it's true.

The Linux community can make all the remarks and excuses they want, but actual facts are facts.

I continue to love Ubuntu; however, the reality is what it has demonstrated itself to be and the volume of threads on this forum should speak for itself.

Sadly,

rhardie

### Post by the8thstar on 2009-12-14
I believe it takes a significant amount of time to get used to the works of Linux. I'm pretty sure the majority of people using Ubuntu are still using a flavor of Windows too; some are more experienced with Windows than Ubuntu and that's why Ubuntu seems so hard.

Linux is not the Holy Grail of OSes. Personally, I see two advantages :
1. it is comparatively more secure than Windows on a vanilla install
2. the software doesn't cost a cent

However, Linux suffers from its nature: it is very chaotic to the profane. It takes a steep learning curve to grasp the concept and the inner parts of Linux. And in the end you can completely destroy your system if you don't know what you are doing, just like Windows.

To the average Windows user, I would recommend installing Spybot, AVG Antivirus and CCleaner. In most cases that should be enough to get Windows to shine again and be SAFE to use. I would not recommend Linux. It's just too hard for people who can't dedicate the time to learn. To a Windows user, switching to Ubuntu is just as hard as an Ubuntu user trying to set up Gentoo; it is A WASTE OF TIME.

My friends and family use Windows and/or OS X. NO ONE uses Linux apart from my wife and I; I am happy with what I have accomplished in terms of personal knowledge, but I have spent countless frustrating hours trying to figure things out beforehand. 

In the end, Linux in its current state suffers from a lack of support from software manufacturers. Wine, though a great program, is a only a worst-case solution... what Linux needs is more programs that run natively on it, like Apple's, Adobe's or Microsoft's products (I know it's provocative).

Simply put:
[LIST]
[*]Things need to keep getting simpler
[*]We need more and more GUI
[*]We need more support from software manufacturers
[/LIST]

"Linux for Human Beings" is not "Linux for Nerds" or "Linux for the Geniuses Club" or "Linux for the Enlightened". The bar has to be lowered to a level MOST people can understand.
