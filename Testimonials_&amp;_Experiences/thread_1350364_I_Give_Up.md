### Post by gmjs on 2009-12-09
It's very difficult to recommend Linux to someone else.  Linux seems to be something that you have to learn to like for yourself.

There are many 'levels' of customisation.  Linux operating systems are much easier to configure at a much lower level (e.g. you can compile your applications from the source code, having modified it if you're clever enough (and no--I'm not!), and it's legal and all the tools you need are open and available to you at no extra cost).

Higher levels of customisation (such as your photo slide show screensaver) are the focus in other operating systems.  Although building on existing ideas and platforms is a good thing, sometimes it can all get a little too abstract and less 'computer-y' than it used to be.

Linux reminds us how complex the hardware/software interaction really is, allows us to appreciate it and provides an excellent tool for learning how things work.

Sometimes the 'silly' things (e.g. a Microsoft Office PowerPoint slide show only playing being able to play Wave files without opening Windows Media Player, therefore having to convert MP3s into low-quality Wave files instead, or having to open Adobe CS4 Photoshop before you can create a PDF in Acrobat Professional) really do get in the way of 'proper computing' and make it all far too complicated.

Another excellent example (as if I haven't bashed computer companies enough yet;)) is Apple iTunes.  You pay for music, receive no physical proof of the purchase and then can only play your music on up to five computers.  The purchasing, signing in and out, deactivating etc. is unnecessary.  Don't get me started on problems transferring your music if you buy a new iPod or a new computer!

Under Linux, you'd purchase your physical CD, get the best quality audio and could rip the music to OGG or MP3 and drag the files across to a portable music player that supports those file types and just enjoy your music.

Personally, I think open source promotes better interoperability and can make using a computer much easier and more enjoyable.

Also, I work in IT support.  I have to use Windows all day, so the change when I get home is more of a necessity to preserve the little sanity I have left!

When I started at university I hated Linux.  I'd tried Mandrake Linux once but didn't get on with it.  I gave it up, so now everyone else on my course has their degree and I don't!  Ironically, I started using Linux when I left and only use Windows when forced to now (usually for when I have to use files that I can only open with a Windows application--Microsoft Office Publisher for example).  I even run a club at a school where we only use open source software (under Windows, I admit--but only because the sysadmin is a Microsoft bore and won't accommodate a Linux installation).

Whatever OS you choose, using the computer should make things easier, more professional, more reliable or more enjoyable.  The operating system that fits that criterion in your eyes is the one you should use.

Graham
