### Post by Exonimus on 2009-12-29
As a user called '32024' stated a few hours ago in another testimonials thread, 'The indicator of Linux's acceptance to everyone and massive widespread use is the "can my Mom use it by herself" factor'. Having some experience with that, I decided to write a post about my parents' successful conversion to ubuntu. If you don't feel like reading a long story, stop now :)


As of late, I came across several reports about people switching their parents to ubuntu. After having tried ubuntu for several years now, I felt comfortable enough to have them try the switch. My parents have a laptop with windows vista on it, and they use it to do the following things(basic stuff)

-internet, e-mail
-movies/sound
-cd ripping
-frostwire
-office 2003
-torrenting,
-cd burning
-google earth
-my dad is an avid hearts and solitaire player, so that definitely needs to work. Vista has a pretty hearts game included, which I hope ubuntu can match.

Now. Before I continue telling about how it went, you have to know a bit about my parents. My father is pretty computer literate (for his age). That is, he doesn&#8217;t know as much as most younger people these days &#8211;one can&#8217;t blame him, since he didn&#8217;t grow up with computers- but he feels comfortable using the computer and can find out most stuff by himself. He basically was the least of my worries. The rest will therefore mainly be about my mom. My mother is a different story. She almost solely uses the pc for internet/email and some frostwire and is not really comfortable with using other applications. She&#8217;s easily lost, and I&#8217;m the one that gets a call when something doesn&#8217;t work right, which is pretty often.

So, now on to the reasons for switching. There were several of them. Let me first state that while I like ubuntu&#8217;s philosophy, I have nothing against proprietary software. I&#8217;m a practical person who just uses what he likes best. For our two pc&#8217;s downstairs, that&#8217;s currently Windows (7 and Vista) since those are our gaming pc&#8217;s, and one can&#8217;t beat windows in that area. However, upstairs, on my school/work pc, and my laptop, I&#8217;ve been using ubuntu for years now, and it just works the way I want. I have a crapload of applications I can install without any hassle, and there&#8217;s wine for every windows program I still need. On the laptop of our test subjects today, there were several problems with Vista that were the reason to switch:

A: vista booted slowly, while it&#8217;s a C2D pc with several gigs of RAM. Admitted, it&#8217;s been installed for a while, but this was getting annoying. I didn&#8217;t feel like reinstalling every year or so. 

B: Usability. Overall, I&#8217;ve never found any windows version hard to use, and I believe it can be adjusted to one&#8217;s needs. However, it wasn&#8217;t really about windows itself, but some of the apps. E.g. Nero. It&#8217;s pretty easy to use, but it has lots of options. I personally love this, but for my mom, a program with two buttons, &#8220;burn&#8221; and &#8220;exit&#8221; would be ideal. She actually needed to write down which buttons to press for burning a cd. Besides, I&#8217;ve discovered some programs on ubuntu that I found surprisingly user friendly, so I figured ubuntu couldn&#8217;t hurt in that area

C: Wireless connection. The laptop has a wireless NIC, which for some reason just refuses to connect sometimes. It usually works after one or several reboots of the pc, but I can&#8217;t find the reason and it annoys the hell out of me. Besides, rebooting only to get WLAN to work is annoying. I tried an external USB dongle which always worked fine on XP, but vista doesn&#8217;t play dice.

D: viruses. Although they&#8217;ve never been infected (I always took care of that) my parents are always worried about viruses. Added to that, I&#8217;m always annoyed when lazily watching tv in the living room, and hearing &#8220;VIRUS DATABASE HAS BEEN UPDATED&#8221; blasting through the room on full volume every so many hours. It usually scares the hell out of me (Sorry avast :P). Granted, I could&#8217;ve used another program, but I really like avast because of its auto update function. 

E: I just REALLY wanted to find out if ubuntu was user friendly enough for my parents, and I wanted to know if I could get it to do everything they needed.

F: General lazyness. I&#8217;m probably moving to live on my own pretty soon and I just need everything to work so I don&#8217;t get a call with &#8216;could you come over? This and this doesn&#8217;t work&#8217;.

So, with the problems and tasks established, these were the goals I needed to accomplish:
-usability
-office 2003 needs to work
-card games
-easy way to burn cd&#8217;s, rip cd&#8217;s, use a frostwire equivalent and a torrent program
-play media, easy way to &#8216;mount&#8217; the mp4 player and move files
-get ubuntu to play nice with our print server
-wlan WITHOUT any hassle about passwords, keyring, etc.
-a new e-mail client, or outlook 2003 through wine
- a virus free environment(done!)
-google earth
-decent speed

Now, let the installing begin. I took a full day to get everything set up the way my parents could easily use it. This included things like installing office 2003, which installed and works flawlessly. Furthermore, I auto associated .doc files w/ word, this needed a small script. I also installed other software, imported e-mails, enabled wlan and graphics drivers, etc. Afterwards, I let my parents use the pc for a few days and these were the comments (with some explanation) and the problems I/they had


- &#8220;Ubuntu looks &#8216;cleaner&#8217; and I really like it&#8221;

-it&#8217;s easy to find the apps listed by category and apps usually have their purpose stated in the name, e.g. Gnomebaker CD/DVD writer. Whichever person thought of this deserves a huge cake. This is ideal for my mother because this makes it easier to find the right program.

-I installed the only decent hearts game I found (gnome-hearts) , and my father didn&#8217;t like it. The reasons were that &#8220;it doesn&#8217;t look as good as in vista&#8221;, the cards disappear way too quickly, and finally he found that he has to click way more often. He does have some fair points there. My first solution was to use a VM and try to get windows(xp&#8217;s and older) hearts to work. This did work, but it wasn&#8217;t as nice as Vista&#8217;s hearts game. The solution finally came when I found out about the &#8220;Hoyle card games suite&#8221;.

Version 2004 works flawlessly on wine, but I had 2007. This worked on the VM, and I later found out that while the setup failed, I could just copy the folder from my VM to ubuntu and open it with wine and it&#8217;d also just work. This would save 15 seconds of &#8220;xp boot time&#8221; and was ideal. Windows xp&#8217;s solitaire also worked with wine. I tried using some other programs, which were great, but the rules were slightly different (e.g. game over after triple reuse of card deck with Klondike).

Added to this: my dad now had many more card games to play. For extra brownie points, he also found out about gnometris, and because he used to play quite a lot of tetris, it&#8217;s the only game ever where he majorily whoops my behind in and seems to enjoy doing so (oh the horror!) Since these two games, my dad and I usually have one night in the weekend where, when my mom and bro go to bed, we try some new card games, play some tetris and have some father-son time. While I&#8217;m twenty already, I still do enjoy spending some more time with my dad.

-Wireless issues. The NIC also didn&#8217;t like ubuntu too much, just as it hated vista. Sometimes it would, sometimes it would not connect. When I plugged in the usb dongle, it worked without question. Sometimes, this meant that there were two devices enabled at the same time, either one always asking for a password. I solved this by blacklisting the internal NIC&#8217;s driver, and disabling the keyring. It works without any hassle now

-Printing: (had this in windows too) somehow our wireless connection doesn&#8217;t always play nice with our print server, which does WOL, but it didn&#8217;t always work the way I wanted to. This was easily solved by creating a small script (e.g. one button click WOL) and another for &#8220;opening the print server&#8221;, which enabled auto logging in on the pc with tsclient, having the remote hard disk enabled, and printing from the &#8216;print server&#8217; itself. This works flawlessly.

The pc is set to auto hibernate after 5 minutes of less than 5% cpu load, and wakes up in like 10-15 seconds. Admitted, this pc still runs windows for now, but that&#8217;s okay. This means my mother can get into an already familiar environment with two clicks and easily print the file she needs. Added to that, the printer automatically shuts down and automatically powers on. However, that being said, whenever my mom clicks on the remote hard disk, she obviously finds directories like /bin /boot /etc and she basically gets completely lost. This was solved by creating some shortcuts to her &#8216;documents&#8217; folder on A: her own desktop B:the file system, so there&#8217;s always a &#8216;documents&#8217; folder to be found.


-Viruses: My dad had some trouble getting used to the fact he didn&#8217;t need a virus scanner. He seems to like it now, though =)

-cd ripping. After installing sound juicer, ripping cd&#8217;s was as easy for my mom as inserting a cd, opening it with the program, selecting the songs, clicking &#8216;extract&#8217; and it would automatically extract to the Music folder. A logical choice and the first place she looked. Good. However, it extracts to .ogg files. I&#8217;m not sure if her music player likes that. If not, I&#8217;ll need to find a .ogg/.mp3 converter.

-Transferring music to my mom&#8217;s new mp4 player. This was as easy as plugging it in, having it automatically pop up, dragging new songs to the other windows and unplugging. Can&#8217;t get any easier than that. Added bonus for the option to play the music on mouseover! I didn&#8217;t know about that, but that is just genius! I was impressed, and so was my mother.

-wine is sometimes slow to start office 2003 on the first boot. It should really give the user a way to check if it&#8217;s loading. For now, I wrote a small script (thanks to the help on the ubuntu forums) to load a &#8216;loading bar&#8217; image on click, boot office in the background, and close the image when it&#8217;s done. Admitted, this does cost me some extra seconds for loading the image, but that&#8217;s okay. I&#8217;ll keep this setup until I find out how to load wine faster on the first run.

-e-mail. I had some problems importing files to thunderbird, but I found a workaround which meant installing thunderbird on one of my windows pc&#8217;s, importing my moms&#8217; .pst file in outlook, transfer the settings, backup the file then transfer to the ubuntu pc. This could use some work in thunderbird, but isn&#8217;t ubuntu&#8217;s fault, of course. For the dialy use, it worked fine. She had already used it in windows some time ago, so the change was no problem at all. 

-internet: I had set my parents up with google chrome. Now you could say &#8220;but that isn&#8217;t even final yet!&#8221; I know it&#8217;s not. However, I thoroughly tested it, and it doesn&#8217;t have any major flaws when I use it, so I figured I&#8217;d let my parents try. Firefox is still installed in case something doesn&#8217;t work (I told them so) but I've heard no complaints about chrome yet.

-Desktop. After several days, I found out my dad had changed the desktop background. One can say &#8220;that&#8217;s easy&#8221; and yes, it is. But this small thing implies that he has taken the time to find out how to do so, which means that he&#8217;s willing to learn and already learning how to deal with the change. 

-Google earth: works flawlessly, but needed one small command from the terminal to install. No problem for me, of course, but I don&#8217;t think my parents could easily figure out how to install anything besides doing it in the software centre and using .deb files. However, I didn&#8217;t give them the admin pass so they don&#8217;t need to worry about it either. On use, it pops up with a small error message about some directory or file missing of some sort. I&#8217;m unsure how to disable it, but I&#8217;ll find out.

-On shutdown, ubuntu always shows a box with a &#8216;sixty seconds to shutdown option&#8217; thankfully, this was easy to disable.

-Frostwire: let it be said, I hate the program, but my parents did not, and who am I to judge? Anyway, while looking for a replacement, I found out that I didn&#8217;t even need a replacement because it also works on ubuntu. Nice! I imported the songs to the shared folder and they showed up, but didn&#8217;t play. Not nice. After some searching, I found out I was missing a codec. With that installed, it worked as it should.

-Boot time. Better than vista, slower than my P4 pc upstairs, but I still need to disable some services and apply some tweaks. It&#8217;s decent enough though. My mom also said it&#8217;s noticeably faster.

-Tax software. There is special software available from a government website. It basically is a simple tool to calculate some tax related stuff. While it works in wine, I found out it also has a linux and mac version. Bonus points for the considerate  government here. :D

-The only real issue I still have is video editing. My mom uses adobe premiere elements 3 for video editing, but she does so only once a year or so. I told her that if I couldn&#8217;t find a decent equivalent or couldn&#8217;t get it to work, she could use my gaming pc and do it on there. However, from what I&#8217;ve read, one can get it to run on a VM. I still need to find out about the stability and speed of that on a VM, though.


On closing: What happened here is a very successful conversion. When one would ask me: &#8220;is ubuntu ready for main stream&#8221; I&#8217;d probably say &#8220;yes and no&#8221;. &#8220;Yes&#8221; for dialy use, but it depends on the hardware used. I&#8217;m uncertain if there is a big list with working hardware somewhere, but there should be. The &#8220;no&#8221;  is for setup. As long as there&#8217;s someone to properly set it up and answer some beginner questions, it should work fine. That being said, including a 'beginner option' on setup, maybe including a small video that explains some basic things would be nice. In all fairness though, I doubt an average user would be able to install and setup windows, so I might not even need to include that point. Speaking about points: If you've read the whole story up to this point, congratulations, you made it.

Finally: I just want to thank the ubuntu devs and forum members here for a great experience, and for hopefully saving me many headaches in the future!
