### Post by MetalAZ on 2007-09-20
I've finally decided to ditch Windows.  After using Windows since the 3.1 days, and DOS previous to that, issues with Vista have pushed me away from Windows.  I had thought about going back to Windows XP and lose a chunk of my 4GB of RAM or risk driver issues with the 64-bit version, but instead decided to see how Linux has been progressing.  I've used it off and on since early 90's, but never as my main operating system.

A co-worker had given me a Ubuntu CD early last year and I had installed it into a virtual machine to play with.  Remembering how good my experience was back then, I decided to go with it as my desktop OS.

Installation couldn't have been easier and it detected most of my hardware automatically.  It even recognized my HP Deskjet all-in-one printer.  The only two pieces of hardware that weren't recognized were my SoundBlaster X-FI sound card and my Microsoft LifeCam webcam.

I've been using it as my primary home operating system for nearly two weeks now (at least 70 hours of use) and so far my experience has been very positive.  It's actually been so positive that I've been trying to get people that I know to take a look at it, even if they only play with it in a virtual machine on Windows.

I've been enjoying the responsiveness of it and having lots of free memory while having many applications open.  Most of the time I'm using less than 800MB of memory whereas in Vista I was always over 3GB.  One of the coolest things I've encountered is mounting my two spare hard drives within my home folder for my user account.  It's funny seeing free disk space change when entering what looks like a normal folder.  Much cooler than drive letters.

The biggest thing preventing me from switching in the past has been games (I'm not a fan of dual booting).  But since I do most of my gaming now on my XBOX 360 and PS3, this was no longer an issue.  It also helped that WoW runs great under Wine, although I wish Blizzard would release a native version.

My biggest complaint is that most software companies aren't taking Linux serious enough to produce software for it.  For example, I'd like to see Photoshop for Linux (I know there's GIMP) and Yahoo! make a decent version of Yahoo! Messenger for Linux. But I know it's just a matter of time before my favorite Windows apps are native.

So my experience thus far has been positive.  Ubuntu is a very easy operating system to use as long as hardware is already configured.  I'd like to see an easy (GUI) way of handling new hardware like Windows has.  I'm not looking forward to turning off my on-board sound and installing my Audigy 2 card.
