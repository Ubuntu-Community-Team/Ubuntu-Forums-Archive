### Post by jso2897 on 2007-09-08
I had a concrete illustration of this this morning. Having just dealt with setting up a Yahoo mail account, and having gone through all the indignity and frustration that they inflict upon you for doing that, I was already in a bad mood. In addition, I had spent yesterday fixing a sound problem on my MacBuntu box caused by the new kernels.
So, when I went to boot up a different Intel box, and my sound device was not found, I saw red, and resloved to track down whoever had compiled the "16" kernel and throttle them with their own intestines.
Then, as I sat there fuming, my heart rate went down, I chilled a bit, and started to think agian, I decided to just reboot - and it fixed it. Just a hardware glitch, of the sort not uncommon on old boxes, regardless of O/S.
The reason I'm relating this boring story is this: I see far too many people who :
A. Conclude that a problem is insoluble, and:
B. Decide to leave Ubuntu/Linux 
when they are in a state of anger and frustration. This is, in my view, a minor tragedy - a cheating of oneself.
When you find yourself felling that way, shut it off, go away, chill out.
Wait a day or at least however long it would take you to flatten and reload some other O/S. Then come back, and face the problem with patience and a clear mind.
And another tip: ALWAYS have at least one computer (or at least, partition) set up with an O/S that is reliable and stable for you, and use others for experimentation - NEVER put yourself in the position of needing an installation you are experimenting with for something that MUST get done.
You will save yourself a lot of stress, and, very likely, save a member (namely, you) from leaving the community, and the community from losing a member.
:)

### Post by AbredPeytr on 2007-09-08
I'll add a corollary to that:

never delete or modify files when tired. Unless you want the express train to frustration and anger. Much of my being angry and visualizing how to infict pain on writers of software/OSs has occured when I was tweaking the system in the wee hours of the morning. I finally made a commandment for myself "No tweaking/installation after 10pm". It's helped my blood pressure a lot :-)

### Post by por100pre1 on 2007-09-09
A good way to prevent this kind of frustration is to back up often your files on an ext HDD or separate media. Grsync is good for that if using an ext HDD. It gives peace of mind to know that your files won't be lost if something goes wrong. :)

### Post by Irihapeti on 2007-09-11
I'll add another corollary to that.

Never make decisions or take major action when under the influence of any major emotion - depression, despair, self-doubt, anxiety, you name it. Go away and do something else, and come back with a fresh view on the matter.

One thing about computers: they are still there in the same state when we get back.
