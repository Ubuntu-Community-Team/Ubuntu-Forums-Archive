### Post by mlnease on 2011-04-29
Hello All,

Waking up to Unity was quite a shock for me too.  I have an idea as to why some people seem to like it better than others.  The big change in appearance seems to be to an environment that resembles that of an iphone or an ipad.

I don't have an iphone or an ipad or want either.  I don't like Apple.  I don't like touchscreens and I know this isn't terribly uncommon.  Also I live *way* out in the sticks where the combined cost of hardware and telecom contract is in no way worth it.  Even cell phone service is iffy out here.

Gigantic (relatively) icons (the 'Launcher') make sense if you're going to be dragging them around a screen with your fingertips.  On my desktop they only take up valuable screen estate.

Having the fixed, narrow strip at the top where the gnome panel used to be takes up a little more, is un-customizable and--to me--annoying.

I experimented.  I began to get used to the (Launcher) icons, the way they move around and so on, though I still didn't like them.  I missed my gnome panels, their tiny footprint, huge customizability and so on.  And of course their familiarity after many years of constant use of the Gnome desktop environment.

[CENTER]***IMPORTANT:  I'm not an expert so PLEASE don't try this unless you know what you're doing!***

[LEFT]First, I uninstalled Unity. ```
apt-get remove unity
```Then, in Synaptic, I installed Gnome desktop environment and rebooted.  At this point, everything looked pretty copacetic, except that my gnome panels were gone (along, of course, with the gnome menu, applications and so on).  So after a little Googling, I opened a terminal and ran ```
gnome-panel &
```--bingo!  They were back, good as new, till I closed the terminal.

So I reopened them via the terminal, went to System/Preferences/Startup Applications/Add and, in the Command field, added ```
gnome-panel &
``` and now Natty works as well for me as Maverick did.

I suppose this is a temporary fix and welcome any comments or criticisms.  If in the future this workaround is no longer a viable option, I'll probably move to another distro that'll allow me to stick with the GDE.  I'll hate doing it because I've grown very attached to Ubuntu and its community over the years.

This has been a vivid reminder, though, that all the pleasure and practical use I've got out of Ubuntu over the years has been down to the efforts of a bunch of developers who don't owe me a thing.  I've often thanked you guys before and I do so again now, even though we don't seem to see eye to eye on the direction Natty has taken.

I, basically, just want my desktop to continue to look and work like a desktop and not an iphone or an ipad.  This is a matter of taste, of course, and I don't expect anyone to cater to mine.

Best to All,

mike

Maybe this will make some people feel better.

[http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2011/04/gnome3-packages-begin-trickling-into-ubuntu-11-10/](http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2011/04/gnome3-packages-begin-trickling-into-ubuntu-11-10/)

> Due to the high priority of Unity in the Ubuntu 11.04 development cycle, the inclusion of GNOME3 into Ubuntu were postponed until 11.10, and Unity was shipped in Ubuntu 11.04 as a shell for GNOME2 instead.

Not wanting to waste any time, development on 11.10 started yesterday and GNOME3 packages have begun landing in Ubuntu 11.10.

What does this mean for me?
In Ubuntu 11.10 you’ll be able to install GNOME3 directly from the Ubuntu main repositories instead of relying on a PPA (unlike the situation in Natty).

Moreover, since the Unity shell will run on the GNOME3 stack for 11.10, installing GNOME3 with GNOME Shell won’t affect the Unity desktop session and you will have a choice to boot into either Unity or GNOME Shell at the login screen.

And what about the AppIndicators?
Many applications in Ubuntu have been patched to show AppIndicators for consistency in the Unity desktop. But as many Ubuntu 11.04 GNOME3 testers found out, these patches meant that these applications wouldn’t integrate well into GNOME Shell. All that will change in Oneiric.

A smart detection mechanism will be implemented to only show AppIndicators if a Unity desktop is detected. If instead vanilla GNOME is found it will fall back to the default upstream behaviour seamlessly.

### Post by jamesjenner on 2011-05-01
Well it has been an interesting read, I've never utilised these forums other than for searching for help on problems with ubuntu but thought it was about time that I contribute (mainly because I saw someone with a problem under Unity and external drives and I thought I had something to contribute).

Personally I was shocked when I first installed 11.04. The UI was so different and it reminded me of peoples reaction to Windows 7 when they had moved from XP or Vista (the whole single icon to show an application, both to launch and select running, etc).

However I've played with 11.04 for a couple of days now and I had a big breakthrough when I realised that there are a whole heap of keyboard shortcuts to make my life easier. I've found some good references and as I become familiar with them I've finding the whole experience quite enjoyable.

After getting over my initial reaction and reviewing the changes both as a new user and from an ease of use pov, I decided that I like the changes. I like the simplification of the desktop. To me this makes sense because the focus of a GUI should be on applications, not on the desktop. I feel that Unity does this very well.

My one pet peeve would have to be adoption of the Mac approach to application menus. I have a 30" monitor and like to have multiple apps open at the same time. When using tools like Inkscape I tend to be slack and use the mouse to peruse the menus. Dragging the mouse from the bottom right hand corner of a 30" monitor all the way to the top left just to look through the menus is a royal pain in the ***. I really wish they hadn't done this. But to date this is my only real complaint and hopefully they will provide options to make this configurable in the future.

Though to be completely fair, I should really be using keyboard shortcuts for the menu, and this would go a fair way to resolving the issues. I've noticed application design for mature apps have moved away from menus in the application, because in all honesty they're not the most user friendly approach for application interaction.

One thing I have discovered over the years is that people don't like change. It is difficult for people to be objective in their view of change because trying to deal withe the change creates internal conflict. People get into habits and they have to be broken, they're forced to look outside the zone that they feel comfortable in. As such they're uncomfortable and this creates emotional responses, often quite extreme.
