### Post by cowbellemoo on 2008-10-02
Hi.  I've been lurking a little while and felt like sharing.

I'm somewhat of a power user, and have been for years, but only got around to seriously trying ubuntu the beginning of this year.  I didn't have any major usability issues that put me in the mood; neither windows or mac os gave me any reason to trash them that wasn't ideological. My windows xp desktop was easy to maintain, got use of my hardware for cutting edge games, and ran the Adobe creative suite.  My macbook was also a dream, very pretty and easy to use.  But neither was very extensible without paying for software.  I've always wanted to have a proper home/web server, but neither environment gave me the confidence to try -- freeware for them is spotty and I can't afford the software (windows media center) or hardware (apple tv) I had wanted.  So, even though I was fine in day to day usage, I felt discontent with the prospects of watching video on my tv or running a web server or any of the other things I wanted to try.

I had heard that ubuntu was one of the leading distributions for bringing a full-bodied GUI to the open source community, so I gave an old computer a new hard drive and installed ubuntu.  First thing I did was set up samba shares (with help from the wiki) and relieve my desktop of all the videos and mp3s that were sprawled across a few hard drives.  All of my data was finally backed up and organized.  Great!

I wouldn't interact with the machine too much at first, but it did make things easier for me.  Buoyed by this success, I tried ubuntu on my macbook and explored the software available, desktop effects, and started using remote desktopping to explore more about the software architecture.  I also resurrected an even older computer to bridge the gap to my TV with a vga/composite adapter.  Sweet home theater bliss!

My little home network ecosystem thriving, and Hardy Heron nearly arrived, I decided to give my main desktop a total overhaul.  Dual boot ubuntu/xp, with XP only being used for the Adobe CS and my collection of games on Steam.  I would be doing most of my browsing, email, music listening, and tv-watching on my ubuntu install.  That was the plan, and it worked beautifully.  Shifting files around, backing up, installing, and organizing became easier and easier.

Triumphantly, I started to experiment.  I gave my home media server the capabilities of a for-real web server, bit by bit as I learned the configuration of FTP and apache.  I could pull my music from my server to listen to in work.  I started feeling an urge to develop, hopefully one day to help out with actual ubuntu or universe packages.  I've been reading about the ubuntu community and other open source developments.  I've been inspired by Mark Shuttleworth's blog and other news of the development cycle.

So far, I've learned a little python and regular expressions to make my job easier.  I started a project with wordpress and learned enough PHP to implement a functionality I wanted in a menu listing.  I'm experimenting with web APIs and mp3 tagging and I feel confident that I can scratch any itch that I have.  Baby steps, sure, but I feel absolutely unlimited in what I can accomplish with computers now.

The ubuntu art community delights me, the bug fixers make me feel like I compute in a a well-garrisoned fort, the upstream and features developers make me feel like every update is christmas, the documentation and how-to writers make me feel clever, and with all the rest I feel part of something special.

I just keep having moments where I'll be grsync'ing a mirror of my music collection, or playing with stellarium, or browsing through my apache access log and think: "How awesome is this?!"  And Ibex is coming soon and I just feel there's a great momentum behind ubuntu and the rest of the FOSS world.

Gosh.  Anyway, thanks for reading.
