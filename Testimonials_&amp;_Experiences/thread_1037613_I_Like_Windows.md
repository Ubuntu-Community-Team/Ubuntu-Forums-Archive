### Post by jbysmith on 2009-01-12
You can count me along with the Microsoft haters.  *But*, I'm also a realist.  

Under the hood, at the console level, Linux is absolutely brilliant. Sheer genius.

But at the desktop level, it falls apart, and fast.  It's improved a *lot* over the years, but has a loooong way to go before it'll win over the casual "I just want to watch my porn and play games damnit!" users.  

What Windows does, and does absurdly well, is provide a common user interface that is ridiculously easy to use, and is consistent across the board.  99.9% of the time you can look at the window, and immediately figure things out and off you go.  Stuff that works in one program tends to work very similar in others.   No man pages, no Googling, no "am I fixing it or formatting my drives?" console commands, no begging forum users for help.  If the program is built for your particular version of Windows and is bug free, you can pretty much guarantee it'll work.  (Of course, not always the case, but usually....)

In Linux, things are definitely improving, but still a ways off.  And I don't mean "desktop bling", which Linux obviously has in spades, I just mean easy and consistent.  

Once Gnome, KDE and the like, *including* non-WM applications, reach that level of maturity, I'm sure that the size of the Linux user base will skyrocket. 

Once that happens, major publishers will take serious notice and start producing software for it.  There's a few.. Steam for example *is* coming, supposedly Unreal Tournament 3 sometime this century, etc, but it's just a drop in the ocean compared to whats out there for Windows yet.  This also applies to non-games of course.  OpenOffice is great, but there's a ton of software out there that just can't be touched by whats currently available in the open source world, if at all.  Plus once they figure out that just because the operating system is free software, doesn't mean that you can't write a commercial app and make a buck off of it. But that won't happen till the user base really takes off.

But until then, I'm stuck with my XP partition for playing games.  Some non-gaming apps I can get away with in Wine, but it's hit or miss at best; more often than not I still rely on XP in a virtual machine.  (Visual Studio 2008 in Wine?  Not happening any time soon.)  

And besides, if you can live with Windows phoning home to the Evil Microsoft Corporation all the time, it's not that bad if you keep it secure.  The best OS is the one that works for you.  

I still like Windows off and on, in small doses.  Along with my medication in small doses, which probably explains my rambling.
