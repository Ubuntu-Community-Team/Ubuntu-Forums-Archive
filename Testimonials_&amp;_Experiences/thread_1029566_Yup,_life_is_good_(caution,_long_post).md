### Post by thraxy on 2009-01-03
Hi, I'm gonna take a moment to share my Linux experience.

I like to describe myself as one of the Windows Vista casualties. In late 2007 I bought an Acer Aspire pre-loaded with Vista. I found it slow and annoying, mostly due to the fact Microsoft added 5 billion clicks to every action I wanted to perform. But hey, what can you do? After all it's not like there's any alternative, right? Or so I thought. I had already heard about Linux, but I wasn't annoyed quite enough yet to check it out.

It wasn't until I moved back to my home country and set up my wireless that I found out exactly how brutal Vista could be. I could connect to my wireless router, but was refused access to the internet. I spent weeks trying to fix this problem, with little or no information online and no help at all from Microsoft Support (not very supportive for a support center). A friend of mine that worked in network solutions gave me a tip to update my router software, because they had been successful in getting older routers working with Vista doing that. So I tried. Well, my result was a complete lockout from the router settings... lol. So I was doomed to be chained to the router with a 1,5m lan cable for a few more months.

I think it was in February 2008 (please correct me if I'm wrong) that Service Pack 1 came out for Vista. I had really high hopes for it, because the list of issues just seemed to grow the more I tried to use my Vista OS for. I installed the service pack within the first couple of hours of its release. Hmm... none of my issues seemed to be fixed... I guessed I'd just have to live with it until I at least could upgrade my router hardware. That's when I found out! I could now - thanks to Service Pack 1 - no longer use my DVD burner. Thanks Microsoft :D that's exactly what I wanted from service pack 1 :D:D:D ???

I considered that the final straw. Something had to be done, and fast.

As mentioned before, I had seen Linux before. One of my buddies from the Multimedia Design Academy used to run it on a dirt old laptop. He seemed to be compiling something every other day. I didn't really get into that at the time.

I decided to check Linux out. I had no idea what I was getting myself into. I checked youtube first (it's weird, but youtube has become an excellent place to check out software and hardware). My jaw dropped. "IS THAT LINUX?" were among my first thoughts. I had no idea I one could do some of those things on a computer (was of course looking at compiz... lol).

[I]But but but... what's this? When I try to find a download location for Linux I keep on running into weird stuff and never finding exactly what I want... whyyyy? Time to go to wikipedia (yeah, they say wiki isn't 100% accurate, but neither is the random crap I blurt out on a daily basis).

DISTROS!! Now we're getting somewhere. But what is it? Ahh, it's a type! A flavor? Ooh nice :D but which one do I want?[/I]

I found a *distro chooser*. That seemed very handy. Answer some simple questions and the interwebz will tell me what to do! That always works out well, doesn't it? Well, it spit out a few names like: Ubuntu, Kubuntu and OpenSuSE. Without hesitating much I had like a rock/paper/scissors thing and went with Ubuntu (or maybe I just chose it because it was at the top of the list).

I had to uninstall service pack 1 to get the burner working btw.

It wouldn't install at all. Something I now know was most likely because of a corrupt ISO.

Oh well, one down gimme another. I tried OpenSuSE.

The installation was fairly painless, but everything was just so different. No graphics driver... but the wireless was WORKING!!!

After a long read I found out where to get the nvidia driver. But remember, I was a fresh of the bananaboat n00b on Linux. Every time I tried installing the graphics driver my system seemed to crash. I couldn't for the world figure out what was wrong. I was completely gutted :(

I quietly stuck my tail between my legs and humped over and installed XP. That worked pretty well. Got everything working including wireless, which had been my big headache on Vista.

But still... youtube was taunting me. Teasing me by flashing rotating desktops and wobbly windows in my face. I could not let myself be a slave to partial and illegal monopoly. How could I run a stoneage operating system on an almost sparkling new machine? Short answer: I couldn't!

In late April (around my birthday) Ubuntu 8.04 was released. I thought why not give it another go. This time I learned about the wubi installer, so I thought "how wrong can it go?". I dedicated 30GB harddrive space to Ubuntu. It worked! It actually worked! I was running Linux :D

The wubi install only lasted for a few hours though... then I rebooted and did a full install without giving my XP install a second glance. I guess it was just one of those "wtf" moments. All the torture Microsoft had forced upon me flashed before my eyes. I was like "*bliiiip* Microsoft". I felt like William Wallace... FREEDOM!!!

After the full install I was surprised to find exactly how easy it was to get things up and running. Especially due to good support forums and various blog threads. I had the graphics card working within 30 minutes (there's the n00b thing again) and had all the updates and a SWARM of new applications within the next couple of hours.

Now I for one don't really get the people that complain on forums about Linux being hard. Of course it's hard. I remember the first time I turned on a PC. It was back in 1995 and had Windows 3.11. That was hard to get into. I only had experience with Commodore 64 and Amiga 500 before that. It was a radical change.

My point is that you have to adjust to everything new. If you're not willing to adjust and just expect everything to be like what you're used to, you're not gonna learn. Everything today has a learning curve, and sadly you're left behind if you don't take interest by yourself.

If you're like me, then you're very curious about how and why things work. Linux will be a thrill for you with that attitude. If you're not willing to put your time into it, I would suggest you stick to what you know, whether that is Microsof Windows or a rubber chicken on a stick with a notepad attached.

I don't care how many people convert to Linux. While there is some strength in numbers, I truly believe the quality is more important than the quantity. I'd rather have 1 interested and inquisitive person convert than 1000 complainers looking for an instant fix. You get more results with a positive attitude.

All Linux distros make you want to pull out your hair at one point or another, but that's a part of the learning experience.

I've been on a few distros since I started. A few are: OpenSuSE, Sabayon, Mandriva, Fedora, Kubuntu and Linux Mint.

Sabayon is quite interesting. I ran that for about a month, but decided it was not quite my cup of tea. Linux Mint is one of my favorites, but since the release of Felicia I've found that Ibex is more stable for my needs right now.

I think for the ultimate OS experience, you have to let go of the little OS Nazi inside yourself. I suggest using whatever works for you until something better suited comes along, then use that until something else works better for you. Nothing is the very best 100% of the time and something that works for you might be useless to others.

I hope you enjoyed my little rant. If you agree or disagree or just want to tell me how pretty I look in my ninja mask, please leave a reply :)
