### Post by blazercist on 2008-08-13
Basically in a certain type of practice you have a bunch of forms, be they affidavits or motions or form letters etc.  And you have a database where you store each case, the case can contain the file number the assignor/assignee, opposing counsel( name address phone filenumber etc.) details about the case that are usually filed in by the data entry personel.  perhaps scanned documents etc, you get the idea.  then you probably have a directory filled with these forms that i've mentioned like affidavits and motions and formletters (these files would be templates that draw variables from that database that your data entry staff has populated) and merge the variables in the database with the templates... this saves countless man hours customizing each form/letter/motion for the specific purpose and minimized human error as well as editing time.

so basically you need a GUI frontend for this database so that multiple users can create/edit cases in the database and merge variables from the database with the templates, it would also be handy if the frontend allowed users to create custom templates easily.  I have alot of experience with this type of software because of my extensive paralegal work which is mainly generating documents using a system like this.  I have seen MANY such programs/databases some of which were better than others but none that I would call ideal.

I apologize for sounding preachy, but in my opinion Linux is like any other powerful tool -- vast capabilities but some dangers if not used with care.

### Post by Bill Day on 2009-01-31
I work as a lawyer in a small law office that does not use Linux, but I do use Ubuntu (almost) exclusively at home, relying on VMWare on the relatively rare occasions that I need Windows.  In the past, I have also used SuSE, Fedora, and Red Hat.

In my practice, the core software tool is the word processor; we use WordPerfect in the office.  I need a word processor that has 1) easy of formatting, 2) robust spell check, 3) numbered paragraphs, 4) word count, 5) mail merge, and 6) the ability to generate Tables of Contents and Tables of Authorities.  Although I am in many ways a big fan of OpenOffice.org, I have not found that it is as capable of performing all of these tasks as WordPerfect or Word.

In an effort to approach things the "Linux" way, I am thinking of doing some experimenting with LyX, LaTeX, and TeX, but I have only just started to experiment.

As far as luxuries go, I am not aware of any really good, free template/document management programs suitable for legal use, and as far as  I know there are no case management programs al la Lexis-Nexis Total Practice Advantage (Time Matters) or Amicus Attorney, that run on Linux clients. (Some, I believe, can be run from Linux servers but require Windows clients).

As far as I am concerned, in order for Linux to be really useful on the legal workstation, the first thing that lawyers who use Linux need to find each other - threads and forums like this are at least a first step.

Even an embryo of a legal Linux community could accomplish two things: 1) share information about how best to leverage Linux technology for legal purposes, and 2) provide support and encouragement to the wider Linux community to develop software that Linux lawyers need.

Incidentally, Steven A. Reisler has an excellent [article]("http://www.linux-mag.com/id/7187") about using Linux in his law office in the current issue of Linux Magazine.

***********************************

Dear Vorian:

I am writing to propose the addition of a legal professional section to the Ubuntu Forums as an agenda item for the next Ubuntu Forum Council meeting.  Unfortunately, the meeting by my calculation is being held at 4:00 p.m. Eastern Time on Monday, February 16, when I am normally at work.  While I will do my best to logon to #ubuntu-meeting on irc.freenode.net at that time, I may have a conflict.

I am writing to propose the creation of a forum section for legal professionals, as discussed in [http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=888749](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=888749).  At present, Linux appears to be seriously underrepresented in the legal community, and conversely, the legal community is generally underserved.  Of all the major distributions, Ubuntu is in the best position to begin to turn this situation around, since it is the most user-friendly and the most committed to the desktop. 

Making inroads into the legal community is important for reasons beyond simply introducing Ubuntu and Linux to a wider user base among professionals who are heavily and increasingly dependent on computers.  (Most court filings in federal courts in the United States are now electronic.)  More importantly, enforcement and interpretation of the GPL and the legal underpinnings of free and open source software depend upon lawyers and the legal community.  At present, the legal community is highly conservative and very Windows-centric.  Even the notion of using a Mac is regarded as a bit outre, and most lawyers of my acquaintance, particularly older ones, either have never heard of Linux or derisively consider it a "home-made" operating system.  When the core assumptions of the legal community regarding software are that the only "real" software is proprietary, commercial software produced by Microsoft or for Windows, this is not a healthy environment in which Free Software will receive the legal protection it needs to flourish.  We need to bring lawyers and judges into the fold.

In addition, while Linux has the same advantages for the legal professional as for any other user -- including stability, versatility, and power -- there are relatively few applications currently developed specifically for the legal market, and there is a dearth of knowledge in the legal profession regarding ways to adapt existing GNU/Linux technology to the needs of the legal profession.  For example, I suspect that LaTeX could be readily adapted to the complex formatting and strict outlining needs of legal pleadings, motions, and briefs so as to deliver a superior work product, but I am not aware of anyone who has experimented in this area.

While the initial membership of a legal section in the forums might be small, creating a section would offer the following advantages: 1) it would create a highly visible rallying point for the small number of lawyers actively committed to Ubuntu and Linux, 2) it would provide a visible place for curious legal professionals to obtain information about Ubuntu and Linux, 3) it would allow lawyers and legal professionals to discuss and exchange information about adapting Ubuntu and Linux to the needs of the legal profession, 4) it would signal the developer community that lawyers are interested in ways to use Linux to practice law, and 5) it would hopefully contribute raising Ubuntu and Linux's profile among legal professionals.

Especially in the current economic climate, where law firms, like other businesses, are looking for more cost-effective ways to do business, there is a window of opportunity to educate the legal profession about Ubuntu and Linux as never before.  The first step, however, is enabling legal professionals interested in Ubuntu and Linux to organize themselves before they can reach out.  The Ubuntu Forums represent an ideal venue to bring legal professionals together, and I hope that the Forum Council will take advantage of the opportunity.

In the hope that additional lawyers and other users will choose to participate in the next forum discussion, I am posting this email to the above thread and forwarding it to several other lawyers who might also be interested.  Thank you for your consideration.
