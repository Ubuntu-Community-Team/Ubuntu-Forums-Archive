### Post by lariosa42 on 2008-12-02
After ditching Windows six months ago, I have quickly become an Ubuntu enthusiast (which means I am actively learning, but still have a lot to learn), and I have found that I want to share the wealth.*  Many people see me using Ubuntu (my girlfriend, officemates, family members, etc.) and seem to have a vague curiosity (and also a vague fear) about it.  What I want is to be able to sit someone down at my computer when their curiosity strikes, and give them a tour of Ubuntu.

What should this tour accomplish?  My goal is to have someone come away with their fears mildly lowered and their curiosity slightly raised.  I do <i>not</i> expect them to cast away Windows or OS X immediately (although I carry a Live CD just in case).  What I would like from the Ubuntu community is suggestions on how to best organize such a tour.  It must be efficient, since people's attention spans are low, especially for things they do not understand.  It should pique their interest, and most of all, it should be fun.

What are your ideas?  To get discussion started, here are a few questions I have been asking myself.  

    1) What can I show them right off the bat that would really get them interested?  That is, what's a good "hook?"

    2) What can I show them that Linux can do that Windows/ OS X can't do?  (I know there are many discussions on this, but I want something brief and to the point.)

    3) Would it be worth it to show them the command line?  If so, what are one or two very brief but very useful/powerful commands I could show them?

    4) Should I spend much time explaining open source software?  Would I be [selling the steak instead of the sizzle]("http://www.alaskainternettoday.com/experts/connor_sell_the_sizzle.php")?  What is a succinct way of explaining OSS and its benefits? 

    5) Where can I learn more about compatibility, etc. so that I can better answer their questions?


I would like to keep the tour under 15 minutes.  My rough plan so far goes like this:

    -Impress them right away with the boot-up time (currently ~37 sec).
 
    -Let them log into a fresh account.

    -Show them some familiar applications such as Firefox, Open Office, Mines, etc. (the "it's not too scary" factor).

    -Show them some neat feature/applications (the "wow!" factor).

    -Show them the Add/Remove feature (the "so there <i>is</i> software for Linux" factor)

    -Mention the lack of viruses, adware, etc.

    -Answer their compatibility questions.  For example, iPods, wireless, file-transferability (such as .odf --> word), etc.

    -Tell them how to install it, offer my help, and leave the door open.

This plan could certainly stand improvements and expansion.

If you have been an Ubuntu tour guide, please post and let us know about your experience:

    1) What did people like?  

    2) What kinds of questions did they ask?

    3) What (if anything) went wrong?

    4) What would you do differently?

    5) Did they end up installing Linux?  Did they fully ditch Windows?

  Note that this project if fundamentally different from the [Ubuntu-girlfriend experiment]("http://contentconsumer.com/2008/04/27/is-ubuntu-useable-enough-for-my-girlfriend/"), in that it does not seek to diagnose problems with Ubuntu.  Furthermore, the tour is nothing like a quiz--I would help the user if they got stuck after an attempt or two on their own.  

  I will post my experiences in this thread after I give each tour.  I expect to give it a few times in the near future when I head home for Christmas break. I hope to give a tour to my mom (low-level user, mostly e-mail and photo albums, running XP), my girlfriend (medium-level user, needs math/LaTeX software, running OS X), my brother (high-level user, needs specialized audio-mixing software, running Vista), and a few friends and office mates of varying skill/interest levels.  I will try to hone in on the best answers to the above questions as I go.  I'm looking forward to hearing your suggestions!

---------------------------------------------------------------

Technical: I am running Interpid Ibex on a Dell Inspiron laptop, dual core, 1 Gig ram.  This should be speedy enough to at least impress Vista users.

*[SIZE="1"]If you take issue with me feeling the need to "spread the Linux," I understand your reasons.  For me however, the widespread addiction to Windows is like watching people live with chains around their necks, while I have discovered that the lock is easily broken. I am not going to force anyone to remove their chains, but I must tell people how to free themselves.  ([Allegory of the Cave]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allegory_of_the_cave") anyone?)[/SIZE]

### Post by poetstorm on 2008-12-02
Ooh! What an interesting idea. I just moved to Ubunut about a week and a half ago so I’m very interested to hear what others say about this tour so I can try playing around myself a bit more. Here are the things I really liked.

1. The graphical windows explorer-like file manager. I can navigate my directories just like Windows and double click files to open them. For the most part, my files opened up in the right programs automatically which was a blessing after troubled times with RedHat years ago. 
2. Many of the same features as Windows. You can right click a file to get properties. Ctrl-C still copies and Ctrl-V still pastes. Familiarity is good when using Ubuntu as your only OS.
3. When in thumbnail mode viewing the contents of music folders, hovering your mouse over a music file causes it to preview. A small feature but it’s bound to get some ooh’s and aah’s. 
4. The Add/Remove programs menus. It made things SO easy and allayed much of my fears about installing / uninstalling. 
5. WINE – for those programs you just can’t do without from old Windows
6. Ubuntu Forums – show people just how easy it is to get help on a problem.
7. Show them how quickly it starts up and shuts down. No more waiting after logging in for Windows to get its crap together. XD
8. Firefox and Flash a MUST See for internet addicted users like me.

### Post by lariosa42 on 2008-12-06
Well, I gave my first "tour" a little while ago, but I wasn't prepared for this one.  I haven't had time to mull over some of the questions I asked above yet, but a friend came over and started complaining about how slow her (Windows-infected) computer was.  I casually suggested that she install Linux.  

She hadn't heard of Linux before, so I explained a little bit about what it was, how it is free, etc.  After a few minutes of this, she said "what's the catch?"  I told her that there really was no catch since, but that she could try Ubuntu off the Live CD and see if she liked it.  (Of course, you could say the "catch" is compatibility issues, but these are minor in most cases, and really, she wanted to know if there was a monetary catch).  

This really confused her.  She couldn't believe that there could actually be free software out there.  She asked, "Who own it?"  I told her that she owns it, and so does everyone else.

I showed her Open Office, Kile (she uses LaTeX) and a few other things.  She kept saying, "Oh, so it's the same!"  I showed her the Add/Remove feature, which she liked, but didn't seem too impressed with.  I then tried to explain wine, which didn't work at all.  Next time, I will not explain wine unless somebody is really interested in compatibility.  

The thing that really impressed her (other than the free aspect) was that she could use the language pack (she is Vietnamese).  

Overall, I found out that compatibility was not a big issue for her.  For her, "Will it work out of the box?" was a bigger question.  Of course, these issues are intertwined, but I think she really just didn't want to go through the trouble of installing a new OS.  Anyway, she seemed mildly skeptical but interested.  I gave her a Live CD and told her to take it home and see what she thought.  For an unplanned tour, I think it went fairly well.  At the very least, there is one more person in the world who has heard of Ubuntu Linux.
