### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2011-01-27
> **espressobeanies said:**
> Read b4 replying plz:

My dad is very security conscious. Even with all the improvements made with security, he doesn't trust computers. Yet he runs XP with tons of spyware and useless crap. I keep telling him that Linux is more secure, but his fundamental question is: how can I trust the developers and the vetting process for updates in which other people write that are sent to Canonical? He knows about virtualization and vpns. Any thoughts Bohdi?

Security in this sense isn't a question of trust, it's a question of "managed risk".  How can you trust Canonical?  You can't!  You can't trust Microsoft, Apple, the Debian project, Red Hat, Linus Torvaalds, or anyone else.  

But you CAN compare the risk when you consider things like accountability and transparency.  Look at it this way:  if you read the ingredients on your snack food, you may not know what all those funny names towards the end of the list are.  But you can be sure there are people out there who do, and some of them are researching to see if those things are harmful. Even if a tiny percent of the population has the technical acumen to recognize those things as harmful, they can certainly raise the alarm.

Or put another way, if one cook is making your dinner, he might poison you.  If three cooks are working on your dinner, it's less likely that they would all want to poison you, and much harder to pull of if one of them did.

It's the same, IMHO, with open source.  With most established projects, you have at least one or two other people looking at the code.  It's a lot harder to hide things in code that anyone can look at.  

I think people assume that just because THEY would never look through (or understand) source code, that nobody ever would.  They'd be surprised...

### Post by JOHNNYG713 on 2011-01-29
....When I am asked to explain the difference between Windows and Linux, I often use a toy analogy. [IMG]http://www.ultimateeditionoz.com/forum/images/smilies/grin.png[/IMG] 

Windows  is like a Game Boy. You go to the store and buy one all shiny new in  the box. You take it home, turn it on and play with it. Pretty graphics,  cute sounds. After a while though, you get tired of the game that came  with it so you go back to the store and buy another one. This cycle  repeats over and over. Finally, you go back to the store and say to the  person behind the counter, "I want a game that does this!" only to be  told that no such a game exists because there is no "market demand" for  it. Then you say, "But I only need to change this one thing!" The person  behind the counter says you can't change it. The games are all sealed  up in their cartridges. You discover that your toy is limited to the  games that others have decided that you need and no more.

Linux,  on the other hand, is like the world's largest Erector Set. You open it  up and it's just a huge collection of parts. A lot of steel struts,  screws, nuts, gears, pulleys, motors, and a few suggestions on what to  build. So you start to play with it. You build one of the suggestions  and then another. After a while you discover that you have your own  ideas of what to make. You don't ever have to go back to the store, as  you already have everything you need. The Erector Set takes on the shape  of your imagination. It does what you want.

Your choice of toys is, of course, a personal thing, so which toy would you find more satisfying?
