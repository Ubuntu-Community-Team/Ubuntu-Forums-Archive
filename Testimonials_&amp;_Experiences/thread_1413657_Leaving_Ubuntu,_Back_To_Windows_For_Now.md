### Post by sophicsage on 2010-02-22
I've been working with Ubuntu 9.10 since November.  I've posted a previous testimonial, but I had a few more things I wanted to say as I depart the community for now.

I am currently restoring my computer with a Sony Vaio recovery DVD set.  Vista is going back on it, then I will use my Windows 7 upgrade disc to get me back where I was before.

The positive aspects of my review are at the bottom.

There a few reasons I had to go back to Windows:

1) _Graphics Card Problems_

I have an ATI graphics card and I've had some 3D rendering problems, such as a purported lack of OpenGL support.

2) _General program compatibility_

There are various programs I use for work that need Windows compatibility.  They are data recovery programs that are necessary for my professional life.

3) _Program loading issues_

It's a real pain installing programs.  Instead of the typical GUI in a Windows environment, you're left messing around with terminal commands and such.  I just don't have time for that.

4) _Audio problems_

I still have serious unresolved Jack Audio problems, despite my best efforts and despite relentless searching for solutions on this board and elsewhere, I have not been able to use music recording software because of audio driver issues.  I've downloaded a couple of programs from the program manager, all to no avail.  The Jack Audio just doesn't work.  Music is part of my life and it's lacking right now.

5) _Poor support for WMV, AVI_

It's really irritating when you're far from home and you're trying to send your family a video message, and it's unsuccessful because you are having trouble converting it from an OGV file into something your family can view.  Worse yet, the conversion program I was trying to use never did work right.

6) _Poor webcam support_

The image on my webcam was very poor and grainy.  I was using the Cheese cam and it just didn't work like a computer of this quality should.  (It's a Sony Vaio NS series)

NOW HERE ARE THE POSITIVES

1) Ubuntu is basically _very, very stable and secure_.  I had no problems with viruses, obviously.  Once I had my non-free package installed, everything worked except for streaming Netflix movies.

2) The user interface is FAR superior than in a Windows environment.

SUMMING IT UP

I am putting Windows 7 back on, but I do plan on installing Ubuntu 10.4 when it comes out in April.  I will make my laptop a dual boot system, in the hope that Ubuntu has improved significantly when it comes the end-user experience.  I am impressed with the community support, but I would suggest that Ubuntu (and Linux in general) open up to the idea of creating a more user-friendly system, even if that means people PAYING for the software...if only a small price.  I commend the Ubuntu Community for being willing to help out with certain issues.  However, what I want is for things to "just work", instead of spending a day (or a week) researching and experimenting with things.

### Post by GodLikeCreature on 2010-02-23
My two cents:

1) _Graphics Card Problems_

Yup, if you have a piece of hardware that is not supported, that can be a pain.  

Having said so, have you seen this?

[http://www.ubuntugeek.com/new-ati-video-driver-10-1-for-linux-supports-ubuntu-9-10-karmic.html](http://www.ubuntugeek.com/new-ati-video-driver-10-1-for-linux-supports-ubuntu-9-10-karmic.html)

Hope it helps!  (Note that you will likely have to apply that driver everytime you receive a kernel update, unfortunately)

2) _General program compatibility_

Well, this is also true, but hardly something that we can blame in Ubuntu.  Ubuntu (in particular and Linux in general) is a different OS, should not provide compatibility with applications that are developed for another OS.  The technical implications of trying that are hugely complex, and to be honest, I think we should be thankful Wine can do as much as it does.

3) _Program loading issues_

Hmmm...  Not sure I follow?  Ubuntu 9.10 incorporates the Software Center application, which is simpler and clearer than installing many applications in Windows (no need to search for the vendor´s site, no need to download an application you are not sure is to be trusted...  You can even get screenshots!).  I know that does not work for all apps, but it does for a good majority of them.

Now, it is true that Karmic sported what could be considered as a preliminary version of Software Center, but it will be the one place for installations in Lucid, and should be great.

4) _Audio problems_

I record my own music with Ubuntu, Ardour and Hydrogen.  I used to do it with Windows, but it just works better in Linux.  Having said so, a piece of advice:

- Use a dedicated partition for Audio production and install Ubuntu Studio, or a similar distro that is optimised for these tasks.  You will save yourself most of the setup tasks and get a working Real Time kernel.  

- Go with Ardour for audio production.  It is really one of the best applications in the Linux universe.

5) _Poor support for WMV, AVI_

Hmmm...  Really?  Strange, Ubuntu always made this very simple on me, always finding the missing codec and downloading it on its own.  Have you installed the extra repos?

6) _Poor webcam support_

Yes, I suffered this as well, but there are workarounds you may try:

- If you use Skype, don´t use the version from the repos.  Download the latest Skype 2.1 beta from the Skype site, it does support lots more webcams.  There was also a fix I was using that worked for me, I can share it if you want.

One thing to keep in mind with Linux, though, is that you are no longer a Windows user.  I know this sounds obvious, but most people forget it or have a hard time accepting it.  

As a result, you need to realise that the assumption that all and every piece of Windows designed hardware will work under Linux is unreasonable and unrealistic.  I must admit that Linux distro manufacturers and the community itself have done a poor job at coming forward with this.  On top of that, unfortunately, we don´t have clear guidance about which hardware is supported, as Mac users do.

However, we need to approach hardware like Mac users do, understand that certain devices are not supported, and stick to those which are.  I recently bought a logitech webcam after I checked that it works (google is your friend), and it was easier than in Windows.  I plugged it in and there it was, working out of the box.

I believe it is wise to accept Linux limitations and think a little bit before we purchase hardware, be it a laptop or a small external device.  Eventually, the investment is about the same as in Windows, but we ensure a satisfactory Linux experience!
