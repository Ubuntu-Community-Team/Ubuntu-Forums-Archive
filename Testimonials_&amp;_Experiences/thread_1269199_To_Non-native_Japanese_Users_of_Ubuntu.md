### Post by Motorhead Kaze on 2009-09-17
Hey &#12371;&#12435;&#12395;&#12385;&#12399;&#12290;

I have been using an English install of Ubuntu with SCIM/Anthy since Feisty came out, and I have logged in in Japanese or switched to Japanese a few times, only to find that the switch was really incomplete. I even took the time to figure out how to get a Japanese version of Firefox. As a guy who reinstalls fresh a few times a year, and even though I am pretty fast at installs now, setting things up does eat up time.

Yesterday I decided to re-install Jaunty in &#26085;&#26412;&#35486; to see what I thought. Well let me tell ya ... if you can read Japanese, this is the way to go. Or, if you read only a bit, but you have used Ubuntu and Firefox so much that you don't really read the menu titles anymore anyway...just kinda know where they are... then just install in &#26085;&#26412;&#35486;. For those of you who cannot read that much, installation itself is pretty simple. You have installed in English before so will be familiar with what choices Ubuntu is offering you (whether to dual-boot next to your existing OS or just erase it and start fresh), and really there isn't that much to read anyway. You can pretty much see what is going on even when you don't recognize the kanji. 

With a 100% Japanese environment, naturally all menus, Synaptic and of course Firefox are in Japanese, you don't need to get the Japanese keyring or install SCIM...all that time you spend setting up your bilingual &#29872;&#22659; can be spent sipping coffee and reading the Sumo reports...whatever. My only issue today is, why ctrl+alt+bksp doesn't restart Gnome, but I will find out.

And, though I have not tried it yet, I am assuming that you really wanted to, after installation you could log-in as or switch to English, and when it asks if you want to change all your file names to English, you can do that so you don't have to be utterly confused as to where your Documents folder is, etc. System files like USR, SHARE, FONTS are still in English. In fact, most of the menus are just in katakana anyway, so that is a matter of getting used to reading katakana faster. The other route, for those who read Japanese but not enough to want to switch every time, was suggested to me by a guy on the Japanese Ubuntu Forum: Dual boot Ubuntu English and Ubuntu Japanese.

Anyway, just wanted to share this. Maybe it is what you wanted to do, but weren't sure.
