### Post by WBC on 2005-11-30
I too am a newbie and have installed many Linux distro's.  So far I have installed Ubuntu in several of my computers and got them to work.  I just installed KUBUNTU and at first is gave me a message in the install that said there was an error installing the ethernet card and so I checked to install it later.

Ethernet card Problem:
After the install the OS booted properly without ethernet card support.  I then went to system configuration and enabled the ethernet "0" card and set the workgroup name and rebooted.  Now the internet was working and I could see other computers on my Lan.  (Problem Solved)

Email setup (Kontact):
I tried to set up the email and it would not let me save.  I then rebooted and tried again and it let me enter the information.  "remember to click the button to check what your email server supports".  Reboot!  Now the email was working and the greyed out icons for "get email" were green and working. (problem solved)

My main point of writing this is to encourage others to be persistant in trying to get everything to work using the GUI before writing to the forum and not giving the program a chance to work.  Remember that if you have a slow computer that you have to be patient and resist clicking all over the place to help it hurry up.  All those mouse clicks make it even slower as the OS has to deal with that too which just adds to the delay!  Use the GUI first to correct problems and after exhausting everything that has to offer then it can be questioned in the forum's.  The one thing I learned is that if you use the command line too soon you may ruin any chance of success as those commands may cancel anything you might have done in the GUI.  Remember that I am talking to nubies here.  :)  
I am now enjoying Kubuntu and it all works as it should. :smile: 
WBC

### Post by solarcontrol on 2005-12-01
I remind new linux users how lost they were their 1st time using Windows.

This will be easier since they are (usually/presumably) familiar with windows and the gui operates in a similar way, and because they wont suffer lockups and the blue screen of death **while** trying to figure it out lol.

I also remind them of the incentives:

The security - no need to run a plethora of background apps to keep bugs out.
For the avg home user, it's practically bulletproof right out of the box and you can heighten security with ease.

The customizability - no need to buy expensive, resource-consuming skinning apps to make it look how you want (especially with KDE).

The privacy - vs the intrusiveness of Gates' MonopolyWare.

The community support - it speaks for itself (pun intended).

The price of not only the OS, but the software - yea.

On top of all that, it's fun!

### Post by BLTicklemonster on 2005-12-04
Hey, don't call me a troll, okay? My thing is, ubuntu is great, I love it. If I weren't into so many things, I'd not have a worry in the world, but I am, and I do.

The command line/ terminal is not a problem. For some reason, everyone stereotypes people with frustrations as having a problem with 1. terminal 2. it's different, not windows 3. get off your duff and learn.

Would that it was that easy.


My biggest problem is that things are needlessly difficult. There's nothing at all to installing something if you know ahead of time what all you must have to make a file work, and if you know (keep notes on) the multitude of ways that things seem to install. 

After butting my head into this over and over again, I gave up. Then ubudoobie hunted me down and gave me encouragement, so I decided to come back and try again. And I hope to eventually start making "how to"s of my own to share with people, and a primer on what you need to do to get set up right in the first place.

Yeah, ubuntu is killer, but there's so many quirks that needn't be there at all.
