### Post by Paulix on 2008-11-23
My first attempt to kick Windows out of the household was back in 2003.  Back then Linux was just too demanding and in the end I downheartedly wiped the linux partition off my PC and went back to Windows.  Then, 5 years later the kids got to an age where they use the internet, and after a couple of months Windows just got so clogged with viruses and spywares that it took 5 minutes just to open IE.  So I decided to give Linux a go again, and after researching the available distributions decided to try Ubuntu (Intrepid Ibex).

Wow, what a difference!  Everything worked straight away, and it even recognized my camera as soon as I connected it.  The kids have put it through its paces too, and I have had no spyware or virus problems whatsoever.  I've found every application that I've wanted for free, and frankly the quality is often better than some of the applications I paid for in Windows.  I'm over the moon with it and am currenlty setting up an Edubuntu pilot system for the local school, and I've no doubt we'll be rolling out Edubuntu there soon.

I work for a huge IT company and see how they work in their never-ending greed for more and more money, and I'm just so pleased to be able to use my IT skills to save money for the local school in the way of not having to pay for licenses to another huge and greedy company (to brainwash our kids into only knowing how to use their software) as well as kicking Windows and all that comes with it out of our house for good!

So really well done Ubuntu and the open-source community, you've done a fantastic job, and I will soon be signing up to get involved in packaging.

Paulix

### Post by tslawinski on 2008-11-23
Here! Here!

I used to work in the public school system as an educator and IT administrator. In the last school I worked in, I was looking after 160 workstations and 3 servers, and about 800 user accounts (all windows environment). It was a daunting task to keep everything working. High schools can be a very hostile computing environment. I ended up in the hospital during my last year due to exhaustion. Working in the public school system is hard enough without the stresses of maintaining a large network. Windows can be very fragile. A single application can severely hamper or break the stability of an installation. Windows is also very quirky. Good luck trying to nail down problems. Licensing costs are another issue. Schools are already severely hampered by very restrictive operating budgets. I think recent implementations like Ubuntu are starting to change people's perceptions of OpenSource solutions and in particular linux. I'm beginning to feel that if I go back into public education, I would only consider a solution like Ubuntu for both desktop and server. Who knows, maybe I will end up there again someday.
