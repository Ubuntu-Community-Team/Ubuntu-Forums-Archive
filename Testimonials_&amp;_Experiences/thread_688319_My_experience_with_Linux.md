### Post by killerclick on 2008-02-05
I've put together a fairly decent office computer out of spare parts and before installing Vista, I decided to give Linux a go.

First  I tried Debian Etch. It wouldn't recognize my integrated LAN adapter and wouldn't install without it. Wow, downloaded a DVD for nothing.

Ok, then I tried PCLinuxOS 2007. It wouldn't boot the LiveCD untill I searched the forums and added something like 'linux all-generic-ide' to the boot options. Then it booted and installed, however at the end of installation it wouldn't install the bootloader, error reported:. (nothing) I tried searching for this on the forums but this was apparently a rare error and I couldn't find a solution.

At last, I tried Ubuntu. It wouldn't boot the LiveCD except in safe-mode, however it recognized all my other hardware and installed without problems after that.  I shouldn't have installed the AMD64 version (despite having an AMD64 X2 processor) because a lot of applications won't work on it. Back to the good old i386. After reinstalling I had a little trouble getting 1680x1050, and apparently Opera 9.25 wouldn't work with Flash Player 9 so I had to install Opera 9.50 which locks up every few minutes. I got Apache, PHP, MySQL installed and running okay but this OS still doesn't work for me since there is no Adobe Flash or 3DS Max for it. There's Gimp to substitute for Photoshop but it's nothing to get excited about. Also I couldn't do such simple things as create files in the Apache's web directory without using sudo or gksudo which was a pain.

Well it was a dissapointing experience. I could ony install Ubuntu and I still had trouble. Even for a purely office machine it's behind Windows with the new Office 2007 (OpenOffice 2 sadly can't compete). I first used Redhat 6 in 1999 and I don't remember having so much trouble with it. Anyway, Linux has a long way to go to even begin to compete with Windows. Linux might be free but my time is not.

### Post by killerclick on 2008-02-06
In retrospect, I feel I've been too harsh.

None of the issues were the faults of Ubuntu or Linux.
The latest Opera won't work with the latest Flash player (fault of Opera or Adobe).
The latest Flash player won't install automatically because of MD5 checksum failure (fault of Adobe?)
No Adobe Flash, Adobe Photoshop or 3DSMax for Linux (fault of vendors).
Couldn't find a nice graphical utility to monitor bandwidth usage on the desktop (like NetMeter).
No games I want to play on Linux.

It's free.
No need for an antivirus, antispyware and stuff like that.
As most applications will be web-based in the future, it won't make much difference whether you use Linux or Windows as long as you have a good browser.

### Post by wally the duck on 2008-02-09
I put a new machine together last weekend (for my girlfriend) and decided to take a chance and go with Linux, even though she has no particular computer expertise. I went through reviews of many, many distros and finally settled on Ubuntu because it has a good desktop focus and seems to be the most aggressive development community.
I first loaded the 64 bit version, then took that off and went with 32 bit.
A couple of minor issues: I did go with some non-free apps (the usual suspects) and had one problem that took a couple of hours to figure out (no midi files playing in Firefox).

Overall, I could not be more satisfied.

I cannot list the countless hours I have spent in front of blue screensof death, of 'windows must restart' screens, of machines that would do nothing at all until reboot in safe mode and some obscure video of network card irq mismatch was fixed.... probably a couple of thousand hours since the mid 1980's. I've done CAD drafting and I had my own architectural practice and built the computers we used, so there were lots of machines over the years. But I don't remember a smoother, faster install - ever - than putting  on thisUbuntu 7.10.

It is easy to use, things work as they should, the apps seem just fine, the interface is nicely configurable, the app loading and updating is absolutely painless. (No CAD... but I don't need it on this machine).
