### Post by TBABill on 2012-03-06
The answer to most of the "Why" questions is simply because you lack the knowledge and experience to effectively manage the system, which is how we all were when we were new to it. It's a natural progression with something so complex as an operating system and the tendency is to compare something new to something we know and understand, which in this case is Windows. 
 
Once you dig through the problems that you tend to have, which should be minimal unless you are making expert-level changes such as moving an entire OS from one partition to another as you described above. It may be best to start smaller, deal with minor issues, then progressively get into more difficult challenges before driving yourself mad wondering why fixes posted online aren't helping or understandable or easy to find. Higher level fixes may make assumptions that your skill level is high enough to be dealing with those high level issues, making the minor points "assumed" by the poster and leaving you without a clear understanding of what the fix actually is and why/how it works.
 
It's not that Linux is overly difficult, but rather that it is inherently different from what most people are used to so it appears to be difficult. If you persevere and learn the ins and outs you'll likely find it to be an easily manageable OS that is also enjoyable to use, in many cases much easier to manage than Windows.

### Post by snowpine on 2012-03-07
In my opinion, there are two kinds of "easy."

Some things are "easy" the 1st time you do them. An example of this are the little cartoons that pop up in Windows. "Hi! I'm a paper clip! Let me tell you about some of the exciting features of Windows! Did you know that you can..." But this quickly becomes irritating to the intermediate user.

Some things are "easy" the 100th or 1000th time you do them. An example of this is the vi text editor, which makes heavy use of the keyboard's "home row" so that common tasks can be accomplished quickly with the minimum of hand movements. But if you've never used vi before, it's pretty confusing.

It's possible for something to be "easy" in both ways, but this is rare... usually it is a compromise/trade-off between shallow learning curve for new users vs. efficiency and productivity for experienced users.

Linux has traditionally been developed by and for the 2nd category: people who use computers all day, every day and have memorized the commands and shortcuts needed to do their job. These users are like any other sub-culture: they know the jargon and practices, and casually toss around words and concepts that may be baffling to those outside the circle.

You can argue that "Linux should be easy to use by someone who has never used Linux before without the need for any tutorial or instructions," but **nothing** in life is that easy. Someone raised on a desert island will never be able to drive a car, bake a loaf of bread, play a game of baseball, balance a checkbook, or operate a computer until someone teaches them.

Now all that being said, Ubuntu comes closer to satisfying the 1st definition of "easy" than any other Linux distro in history. You see the glass half empty, that Unity is very new and there are still some problems, but I see the glass half full, that Ubuntu is trying harder than anyone and closing in on their goal to make a "distro for the average user." :)
