### Post by its_jon on 2009-02-13
My fist operating system was BBC basic.
My first computer a 1983 Acorn electron.
My first introduction to a windows based operating system was 'TOS' 

Through university I was introduced to the then state of the art Macintosh computer, in high demand for its type setting capability's. Making the optical type setting room and its bulky equipment defunct almost overnight.  

So.... Graphics (or more specifically product development) is my field.

Windows and Linux were no surprise to me early 90's. I saw Windows as basically a repackaged Amiga that ran on standardized off the shelf circuitry. Linux appeared to me as a more technically based option that essentially did the same thing. 

I started working on games graphics with a programmer using dos based 486 machines. Later running win95, 98se, M.E, then the feather in Microsoft's hat - the Mighty XP.

win95 was as everyone knows a absolute nightmare, however having already bought win95 I was a bit miffed that I had to pay for 98 especially as it was not a vast improvement. ME...Yes ! getting better, however it was only the arrival of XP that appeared to sort out almost all problems and was actually worth paying money for. however by this point I had already payed for three prior upgrades !!!!

For anyone who had never bought a Microsoft product in the past XP was certainly worth the Money ! (at first)

At the launch of XP word was circulating about the momentum the Linux community was achieving. I decided to try Mandrake to see what it was all about. I had to agree that the look and feel of Mandrake was very similar to XP and that roomers that XP was a panic response to the direction Linux had taken may have been true !
I played with mandrake for a while however it didn't last too long. I managed to break it somehow, having no knowledge of Linux and no way to fix it as my Internet connection/hardware was now working under Linux support was difficult.
The handful of questions I had asked the Mandrake community were answered aggressively... I needed to get work done at the time so happily went back to XP. I would like to stress that to this day XP has never ever let me down as a operating system.

When I first found out about the launch of Ubuntu I applied for some free CD's
I was very impressed with the first Ubuntu and after many many hours finally got my modem to work. However I found myself back on XP (dual boot) most of the time, then I forgot my login for Ubuntu....so wiped it after about 3 months.

Vista Arrived..... It was Rubbish. Its still Rubbish.

To go forward something XP like which was going to continually improve was the way ahead for me. Having kept an sharp eye on the open source world ever since the promise shown by Mandrake I thought I would give Ubuntu another go. I certainly was NOT going to pay for another Microsoft upgrade 'Vista' especially as I really didn't like it at all. 
Other nag's with microsoft ... Most software you install on it has obnoxious 'upgrade now' pop-ups.... expirations.... spamware, spy-ware, adverts, virus threats etc etc.. The garbage that builds up over time slowing down your boot.... well.... you know what I mean. Vista appeared to me to have all that garbage actually built into the system 'out of the box !' 

Firefox
Thunderbird
Open Office
Gimp

The 3 application above I happily used under XP and I have to say were a major aid in my migration. I used Gimp as I really did not want to rely on the one VERY EXPENSIVE application for the rest of my life. Firefox I used because I was **actually scared** that Microsoft would one day force a Internet Explorer upgrade on me that would screw up my whole computer or install nag's that I may have to pay to remove at a later date.
Thunderbird as I preferred it, Open Office as I wanted to upgrade my Office applications but did not want to pay a huge amount of money to do so.
All these app's have excelled.

After installing Ubuntu recently I have had some Sound issues (which I have sorted). I have enabled Compiz features, Learned how to enable additional synaptic libraries ... all the basic day to day Ubuntu ways of doing things.

I find Ubuntu :-

As stable as XP
Much more Funky !
Very Productive
A Joy to Use
Garbage free !!!!!!
Very Secure
Free !

I mainly use my XP partition for storage now.

The last APP I used on windows was the music notation program Sibelius which I got to work under Ubuntu using wine..... However I recently discovered Musescore which also runs under XP so it should help others to migrate as Firefox and OOo have helped me. Musescore is Excellent.

Other app's that helped me were Gnome Commander as I used Total Commander on XP. That was another thing that peeved me off about Microsoft... They sent a heavy worded letter to the creators of Windows Commander requesting they change the name (hence Total Commander).... that sucks. Windows commander was/is a file tool that helped people to navigate file systems better. Handling your 'windows' better... Other monopolistic actions over the years that made the headlines also made me sick.
Linux by comparison appears extremely democratic by comparison to the monster that has evolved as Microsoft.

Ubuntu has eliminated so many of my Linux dislikes. The seeming reliance on typed commands which plagued my early Linux encounters. The ability to easily find and install software NAG FREE. 

Having said all this I am aware that I may be lucky that Ubuntu happens to fit my hardware much better than other Linux. and other new users may be experiencing the problems I have had in the past.
**OR** 
Maybe Ubuntu and Linux have really come of age and simply thrash Microsoft at the moment. That what I believe anyway :)
**Windows 7 ?** why ?... Roll over Rover, let Linux take over ! :P

I read the FAQ before I posted this. One point I noted was that most people do not install Operating Systems themselves... In this light Linux/Ubuntu has to be given the credit it rightly deserves in the recent ability for ease of installation.

Please Please Please... Manufacturers, please offer pre installed Linux PC's !
A working Linux install (especially Ubuntu) is less likely to require the level of after sales telephone support a Windows product will require. 

All we require now is a Ubuntu Magazine on the shelves of WHSmiths.
I would buy it !
I would even help to produce it.

regards
John 
Scotland

### Post by its_jon on 2009-02-14
Thanks for the link to 'full Circle'

I have read most of them now... although anything that looked like code I have to admit I quickly brushed over for content of interest.

The magazine has certainly improved as it went on... from a basic pamphlet style layout to a nice crisp 3 column production with a layout quite well suited to PDF viewing... Of course this was going to happen/evolve as the content level went up anyway.
As the magazine has went on it is certainly pandering to a traditional Linux market which I think is best left to the online community and dedicated Linux tec mags that are already on the shelves.

Cut out the code !.... 
In one article I noted the line (rough quote from memory) "the terminal today is not really needed even for advanced users to work with Linux and can be seen as a spare tyre for times when Linux needs to be fixed"
Yes !..... I know of the power of terminal commands and that it will be used often by developers ... but the whole point of Ubuntu is to provide a alternative OS for the average desktop user.
The average desktop user is ABSOLUTELY EVERYONE except software developers and Linux hobbyists. By concentrating on educating the masses about the usefulness of typed commands you are only preaching to the converted and repelling those who need to learn.
The Logo does not work either... Too fussy. Just use the Ubuntu logo ?!

Full Circle is a great name and I would suggest you print out the PDF's, get the support of Mr Shuttleworth and approach a publishing group such as Future Publishing in Bath with a proposal for a new magazine.
In another lifetime I created high end audio systems and was one of the first people to see the audio potential of the MP3 algorithm and derivatives. At the time of the SBLive I managed to obtain the tech speck's (white paper) for the new sound card prior to its release. I created a 4.1 high end speaker system which I toured around the interested publishing groups of the day... Computer Shopper, T3, Stuff, and the new MP3 user magazine who all featured a review, and pics. I took a PC with me linked to my hugely powerful P333 machine with state of the art SBlive to educate the press of advances on the horizon. I had a letter published in the British Audio Journal or the BAJ which although printed in its entirety was also rubbished by the Editor. apparently my warnings that the HiFi industry should follow the I.T. worlds direction or it would die was nonsense. "People would always play music on music systems predominantly". History has prove me right though. 
Anyway... my point is that all you have to do is make an appointment to see the correct people at these publishing groups with your magazine as it is.... Show its evolution and the download figures. Educate them with clear facts about the uprise and stability and Stay-ability of Ubuntu.... 

Windows
Mac
Ubuntu
Linux

There is a place for these 4 main IT publications..... Make your case.  

Another thing to consider.....

Linux IS for Geeks .... (oh yes it is)
Windows IS for the Novice
Mac IS for the Fashionable
Ubuntu IS for the AFFLUENT and INTELLIGENT 

That is also part of your case.
People are not mainly installing Ubuntu because it is free or because it is Linux. People are installing Ubuntu because they find it a better and a superior solution.
I know of a doctor who is head of radiology who has found Ubuntu and installed it himself (for home use)... He has the money to buy any Operating System he wanted... he Chose Ubuntu.
People who do choose Ubuntu because it is free also have the option to install Windows.... lets be honest ... its not difficult to obtain a dodgy copy of XP ! ... but these people are choosing Ubuntu.

So.... the target market as far as the publisher is concerned is not the Linux user.
Advertising in any Ubuntu magazine should be targeted at the Affluent, Aspirational and mainly more Intelligent people out there who know Ubuntu is a better way. 

Ubuntu Users are probably

a) More intellectual than an average Windows user
b) More financially affluent
c) Not a typical Linux Hobbyist 

The Ubuntu magazine could then draw advertising revenue from the luxury car market etc etc. It is a untapped market which looks set to expand rapidly as more and more clever sorts out there discover Ubuntu.

For ducks sake though .... leave the code to Linux Format ! :)
