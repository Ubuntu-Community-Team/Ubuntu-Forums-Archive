### Post by doublemeat on 2012-02-29
I know this has been posted countless times. But I'm not doing it as a complaint, rather as an observation and also my story on how I haven't GIVEN UP on Linux, but rather just rewritten my own personal use-case for it. I'd love to hear other stories.

When I first really jumped into Linux, I was totally hooked (I had also recently worked for Microsoft at the time). I immediately installed in on all my machines (five or six at any given time); notebooks, desktops, servers. Usually in dual-boot scenarios.

But then I spent months getting them to work "right". E.g. mostly driver and configuration issues. Getting fully functional on Mackbooks was (and still is) a nightmare.

Now that I can get Linux "well enough" fairly quickly, I run it all the time. Usually in a virtual machine though. I just love it's superior font-rendering (esp. Ubuntu). The look, feel, and featureset of Gnome 2.x is just absolutely perfect for me. Not to mention the hordes of incredible and incredibly specific niche software, all FLOSS.

But I'm a semipro musician, photographer, and videographer. In spite of herculean effort and sacrifice, I've never been able to use Linux as my primary OS for these things. My photography workflow is WAY too entrenched in Windows to switch (not for lack of trying very hard and very long and modifying/compromising my workflow). Video editing tools just aren't robust, advanced, and refined enough (yet).  And for music production, the hardware/software issues are just too fiddly and messy (have yet to get low-latency audio working consistently and reliably enough for production use).

More recently, I've also given up on Linux as a general entertainment platform (no Netflix client being a not-insignificant reason).

But, I am still absolutely enamored with the ease of use of the GNOME interface (with or without Compiz), look and feel, and customizability. The productivity tools fortunately are generally top-notch (e.g. Libre Office), and anyway I've moved mostly to the cloud for productivity software anyway. 

So, Linux (Ubuntu desktop x64 with Gnome 2.x specifically) has an enduring place on all my computers, running in VMs (except servers...Solaris with ZFS there). Virtualized Ubuntu is where I do most of my day-to-day work. And configuration is much less of a headache, what with "install and forget" purpose-built paravirtualized drivers, that rarely break on upgrades of either the OS or the virtualization drivers themselves. 

And whenever I get a new machine, I never have to install and configure Ubuntu, drivers, and apps from scratch! I just clone an existing VM image, change a few relevant unique identifiers along the software stack, and boom...good to go.

I have some gripes with Windows. I don't like the licensing/activation issues - especially one Vista installation in particular which periodically keeps insisting it is illegal when it is fully legit, non-hacked, non-shared, unique, only ever installed once [not even re-installed], no viruses, no spyware, very few third-party services/tray/background apps, no hardware has changed, and it is fine once reactivated [until it complains again]). I don't like Aero or it's technical and aesthetic limitations. I don't like that Windows 7 still looks and feels like Windows 95. I don't like it's exponentially ballooning "do-all" complexity. I don't like it's pricy-ness. But I have no qualms with it being very much for-profit and closed-source. (And that has nothing to do with the fact that I worked for them and had a good experience doing so.) 

In fact, I feel good making Bill Gates stock holding more valuable; the man may have been no match for, say, Steve Jobs in the innovation/genius department - but the man has unquestionably saved more human lives than any single human being in history of homo sapiens. (I'll leave that up for debate on whether that is a good or bad thing. I obviously believe that's a good thing.)

I've given Mac OS X a good run - from the very first version of Macintosh, and more so since the first Intel version - up until the current version even. I used to use it often with Linux AND Windows running in VMs, through two Macbooks (I really like their hardware). Mac OS solves many of the things I don't like about Windows - except also having an absolutely ancient GUI that among the four major OS GUIs is [arguably] by far the most self-inconsistent. I like how Apple found a much more customer-friendly licensing and pricing balance, that unlike Windows, does not severely punish legal users who just want to get some work done. ...But, I just don't like OS X. (Even with Macports and WINE.) I never will, unless they radically overhaul the UI and leave everything legacy behind. (And they probably will.)

*(BTW for the grammar police, I'm well aware that "GNU/Linux" is the proper name for a kernel, not a complete GUI'ed operating system. I'm aware that I'm using "Linux" incorrectly as such, in the context I use it. But I'm also aware that such bastardization is so common and prevalent, that even hard-core kernel programmer geeks understand what I mean. More importantly, average Joe Windows/Mac user knows what I'm talking about. It would be really tedious for readers to wade through "A GNU/Linux distribution with X-Windows and GNOME or KDE" every time I could otherwise just say "Linux".)*

[B]tl;dr: After six years of great struggle trying to replace Windows with Linux, I've given up on Linux my sole OS. I've also given up on using it for photo/video/audio and/or entertainment uses. I've resigned to using Windows for those (forever I guess or until PCs become irrelevant), and instead I use Linux (Ubuntu Desktop x64 with Gnome 2.x) running in a VM as my day-to-day productivity OS.

Have YOU struggled trying to replace Windows with Linux, given up, but found a comfortable and lower-maintenance place for Linux in your life albeit in [a] less ambitious role[s]?[/B]

> **ph4nt0m117 said:**

This is objectively not true. Independent testing shows that Windows, Mac, and Linux regularly jockey for the title of "most secure". It depends on when the tests are made, what suite of vulnerabilities exactly are being tested, and so on. I don't find any glee in saying, that at least to my subjective memory, Windows has "won" more of these controlled tests more often than the others. (I do enjoy pointint it out to Linux fanboys though, just to see discomfort and dogmatic rationalizing. But I do root for Linux more than Windows [in spite of being a perfectly happy former MS employee; I'm a nerd, nerds root for the underdog].)

Windows is obviously the target of hugely more viruses, worms, spyware, adware, and malware than the others, just due to market share. But obscurity (e.g. of Linux) is not "real" security. (Though for the average user not dealing with state secrets, it certainly helps!)

Well configured hardware firewalls (e.g. no UnPNP, ping response, or open inbound ports), and AV software level the playing field with Windows. (Firewalls are essential for any user and OS on the internet.) The only thing left - and granted it's a doozy - is user savvyness or lack thereof. User behavior is the single biggest vehicle for computer-related crime and malware (e.g. social networking scams, phishing scams, domain hijacking, etc.) - not OS vulerability exploitation. The human is the weak link, not the OS. 

I'd wager that the average Linux user is more "savvy" in that respect than Mac or Windows, but that has no bearing on which is a more secure OS. Furthermore, I know of not a single Windows user that does not run a self-updating AV suite. I don't know a single Linux user that DOES. Such AV software can protect against phishing attacks and even domain hijacks that might have nothing to do with the OS or risky user behavior. It could be argued that this fact alone balances out any potential "user savviness gap" between OS users, and might even tip the odds in favor of Windows. (Such a complex tangle of factors such a thing might never be able to be known objectively one way or another.)

I'm an advanced computer user (to be modest) and have been since 1987 - most of those computer hours on Windows. The only malware I've ever been impacted by, was from a 5.25" floppy disk from my university, which infected my 4.77 Mhz PC-XT running MS-DOS with the "Stoned" virus.

> **ph4nt0m117 said:**

I understand it's just friendly banter, but I still have to point out that this is just not true. (Well of course it is untrue...all I have to do is find ONE instance of a documented kernel-related bug causing a known system instability, to disprove your assertion of absolute; so...just look at the descriptions of kernel updates to see thousands of proofs ;-). 

Semantics aside, it's still not true. I've run various Linux distros on countless machines. I've had ALL KINDS of stability issues. Not necessarily more so than Windows, and not common enough occurrences to turn me away. ([This post]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showpost.php?p=11728351&postcount=33") goes into that more.)

I rely on my computers for a living. I'm a former programmer and DBA. A semipro photographer, video producer, and musician. I push my computers really hard. I've built my own desktops and servers since about 1989. (Not notebooks though...) I typically have two VMs running per machine (Solaris and Ubuntu - long story why). I do HD video processing and editing with CPU/GPU-bound processing queues days long. I do multitrack recording with professional low-latency hardware. Gigapixel panoramic photo processing. As a rule I use mirrored and striped arrays for storage (ext4, NTFS, and ZFS), and regularly swamp IO bandwidth. My home LAN (run of the mill brand-name SOHO gear) is carefully tweaked and I consistently get close to the theoretical "goodput" limit of gigabit ethernet. Maybe this makes me a good judge of instability, maybe it makes me an outlier. (I believe the former but am free to believe whatever I want!)

The real point here is that at any given time, there are THOUSANDS of bugs in the Linux kernel, userland tools, GUI stack, daemons, and applications. Those constantly shift around with frequent updates and occasionally regress. There are also myriad *known* sub-optimal and even sketchy algorithms and sections, and known (and of course unknown) fringe-cases not checked. It is simply not possible to have a perfectly stable modern OS. (However, billions of dollars does tend to do better at accommodating fringe cases in commercial OSes, than with FOSS.) 

It can never be categorically, objectively said that "Linux is more stable than OS Brand Z" - because such an assertion depends entirely on snapshots in time and the patch states the comparisons are made. (Not to mention what stressors and conditions are set for testing.) 

It's just not a meaningful statement. (Such a notion might be meaningful in a very narrow application or use-case -specific context, but never as a generalized statement.)

Finally, we can't ignore the problem of driver stability being largely out of the hands of the kernel developers. As long as driver performance is important and design tradeoffs are made to allow them to run as privileged processes, and with the underlying architecture concepts for dealing with hardware that all modern OSes follow, device drivers will probably always be the main source of system variability and instability. This is where a required and enforced driver signing policy can significantly improve overall system stability. Whether it is worth the onerousness or not is up for debate, but Windows >= Vista 64 has it and Linux doesn't.


> **ph4nt0m117 said:**
> Depending on what you use windows for, it is somewhat a better system, game wise.

Linux is a great platform for games specifically written for or ported to Linux. Most game developers say that Windows is the easiest and most rational PC platform to program for (with Mac OS X being on the "nightmare" end of the spectrum), but we're talking about end-user experience.

If one just wants the broadest base for acellerated 3D gaming in general, then Windows objectively trumps any other PC OS, just by objective quantity and subjective quality of the available library.

WINE helps broaden the appeal of Linux for gaming, but the quality (or even possibility) of experience depends on the age of the game. The older the better, the newer the crappier. WINE is by project definition always in a game of API catch-up.

Running windows games in a VM on Linux is a non-starter for "late-model" games, as they are always too far ahead of the state of virtualized video acceleration. Paravirtualized 3D video driver development is forever trapped in the game of catch-up that WINE is. There is some point on the curve thoug (as with WINE), where at some point in game age, it will run better on a VM with modern hardware, than it did on the native hardware of it's day. (E.g. Quake II is amazing in a VM on modern gear.)

Of course WINE is a thin translation layer, "not an emulator"; and virtualization is completely different concept (emulating the entire machine and peripherals hopefully with some hardware-based assistance). The similarities are that both WINE and paravirtualized video drivers have to somehow convert the graphics API calls of a non-native game, via host-native software, into something consuming native host APIs. While the routes getting there are vastly different, both approaches are forever locked in the same game of graphics API catch-up. WINE is usually faster for visually advanced games [with fewer layers of abstraction], and paravirtualized video drivers (and the virtual machine itself) is much cleaner in generalized concept (if not implementation), and a more hassle-free way to run a non-native game. Neither of these approaches will work for games using the latest APIs at any given time, until the APIs themselves mature and become reasonably static, which could be decades or never.

There is a promising exception to this though: VT-d (or IOMMU for virtualization in general). With at least a GPU video card (if not also storage adapter, USB controller, etc.) bypassing the host OS altogether and wired "straight" (relatively speaking) to the virtual OS, you can get near-native performance with any game that the guest OS and IOMMU'd hardware supports. But the virtualization software solutions available for that are either very expensive, or prohibitively fiddly for all but the most hardcore geek to get working (I'm an uber-hardcore geek and it's too fiddly for me.) At least as of today.

> **ph4nt0m117 said:**
> [With Linux, ] If your comp dies, your info doesn't!

False and misleading! (I know you aren't trying to be all scientific...so you get a pass.)

If your data drive fails completely, your screwed regardless of OS. (Although Windows does have an edge  there, as there are vastly more Windows-only local and/or cloud-based backup solutions than for Linux.) 

If your boot drive fails completely, you're screwed regardless of OS. (And with either OS your data drive[s] and data on it is probably still fine and safe.)

If other hardware dies (say power supply, mobo, RAM, CPU), then your equally inconvenienced regardless of OS. (In a few narrow circumstances, Linux would have an edge over Windows. I.e. if the mobo dies, and you can't get an exact replacement, odss are that Linux will complain less about the new one than Windows will. But if it's CPU, RAM, or NIC, then Windows will at worst just prompt to revalidate. If it's almost anything else [e.g. failure-prone power supply], no OS will care.)

The only advantage Linux has over Windows in data recovery, is if a drive only NEARLY dead, or just corrupted. With native Linux on-disk formats (e.g. ext4), it is trivially easy to download, burn, and boot into a live CD - then copy whatever data you can onto safe media. Then you can attempt to repair the corruption, and possibly then recover more data - and possibly restore everything to almost new. The same can be done with the default Windows on-disk format (via Linux live CD), including data recovery. BUT - your repair options with NTFS (while booted to a Linux live CD) are significantly more limited. If you have serious problems with an NTFS-formatted drive, depending on how much you value the data on it your best option would be to boot into the Windows recovery CD. ...Which as it turns out, is a royal freaking PITA. Heck, you may not even have access to one at all. (Anyone can download and burn a Linux live CD. ...And fortunately there are some freely downloadable BART CDs to natively fix NTFS but they aren't necessarily user-friendly.)

As for the odds of your data being corrupted in the first place, due to a crash, reboot, unprotected power outage, etc. - well no Linux-native disk format I know of is inherently superior to NTFS in protecting against data corruption. Btrfs is a possible exception, but as of yet it has no repair/recover function as expected of all production-ready disk formats. (So you could be really, REALLY screwed with Btrfs! And in fact I myself did lose an entire Linux installation because of this very fact, which is why I know about it. Fortunately it was a new, experimental install and so I didn't cry over completely losing it.)

ZFS is an amazing option for protecting your data against system/drive failure and corruption. Not just against overt system crashes/reboots/etc., but also against "silent" corruption (as agonizingly happened to much of my digital photos many years ago due to a faulty Promise RAID controller), bit-rot...all kinds of data disasters. Linux has ZFS-FUSE, Windows has nothing, not even ZFS-like. ZFS-FUSE is an inferior port of native Solaris ZFS, but it's actually still really good. A little slow especially on writes, but really, really good, and reliable. I use it. Alot - mostly the Solaris version. Even in Windows (hence the Solaris VMs running that I mentioned earlier).

On the whole, Linux offers absolutely no additional protection over data disaster than Windows. The only thing you could say is that with Linux, it's slightly more convenient to *attempt* to repair on-disk corruption. (And since we've already established that Linux is not more objectively crash-resistant than Windows, it is no less likely to result in non-hardware related on-disk corruption than Windows.) 

And the OS has no bearing at all on purely hardware-related data loss.


Finally argument: Linux is such an incredibly fragmented platform, that I don't think it is even possible for strong minds to come together cohesively enough to convincingly beat Microsoft or Apple on the Desktop. 

Time is quickly running out on the PC, and I don't think any Linux distro can close the gap in time (particularly when it comes to gaming, multimedia production, entertainment, and/or "install and go and it just works on the my hardware" ease of use). The PC will not be replaced or superceded, it will just be rendered irrelevant by whole new classes of technology (that tablets and smartphones give us glimpses of). The average home user (the bulk of the market) will be the first to say goodbye to the PC...without even realizing it. Then corporate users. And someday, eventually - specialized creative users that typically need lots of local horsepower and capacity. 

"Computers" as we think of them now, will soon only exist in data centers - and by then will probably "look" very different (e.g. not blades and blades of self-contained computers; maybe more like a loose network of hot-pluggable redundant serialized bus-based components on a building-sized redundant backbone, that any number of ever-morphing, ever-adapting "operating systems" [a distinction that will become increasingly blurry] inhabit.) Maybe then, Linux will rule the world. 

The Windows vs. Linux debate will be moot. Both will lose. (The desktop at least.) Or maybe, the distinction of living vs. dying will become irrelevant. (E.g. is Windows 8 a tablet or desktop OS?)

I kind of believe that Windows 8 will be the beginning of the end for the reign of Windows. Sure Microsoft can adapt it to all kinds of crazy new paradigms (and/or things we might now think of as "massively connected embedded systems"), but it will still just become irrelevant. Maybe even Microsoft (that I'm less sure of and I hope not). Just a hunch.


Phew, that was long.


TL;DR: Linux is not an inherently superior desktop OS than Windows in any broad objective category discussed here - stability, data-protection, virus resistance, or as programming and/or gaming platform. (It does have price and openness going for it!) Linux may trounce Windows (and vice-versa) in very narrow, application or use -case contexts - especially when we move the competition to the data center. But overall, user's particular use-cases and preferences should (and it seems generally does) play far more into OS choice than "X is inherently and broadly better than Y". I very much root for the success of Linux, and love and use it everyday for general productivity (usually in a generously provisioned VM). The PC will become moot and we will all become cyborgs running a version of Linux that resembles magic.

### Post by doublemeat on 2012-03-07
> **mastablasta said:**
> gimp in it's vanilla is indeed not on par with Photoshop. however. when one adds vsarious extensions available it (supposedly) can do anything Photoshop can and more.

GIMP is a powerful tool and I'd love to use it. However there is one core feature, that cannot be fixed with addons, that is missing. Without it, it cannot be used for serious photography use. And that missing feature is 16bpc support. (I said before "16bpp", which is incorrect. More on the difference between "bpp" and "bpc" later.)

I understand that v 2.8 is supposed to fix that. In fact, the new underlying GEGL engine (I think that's what it's called), as I understand it, is capable of 16bpc and 32bpc operation, but GIMP doesn't leverage it yet. Either way, v2.8 is supposed to have widespread support for 16bpp. But I've been waiting for something like 5 years for it.

But let's back up a little for some terminology and math: 16bpc means "16 bits per color" of color information. In three-channel color, this translates to 48bpp or "48 bits per pixel". GIMP's 8bpc = 24bpp. You may have heard of 32bpc, which is typically the same thing as 24bpc but with an additional alpha channel (8bpc * [3 colors + 1 alpha]) = 32bpp; each color is still 8-bit. 32bpc is the highest final color depth most consumer video cards run at (essentially color depth as JPEG but with an additional alpha channel). 

Keep in mind that 32 bpp[ixel] is not remotely equivelent to 32 bpc[olor]. The latter is *96* bpp[ixel] and typically used in HDR (high dynamic range) processing - and is often floating-point rather than integer. 

Consumer video cards can't (or don't) provide color that this level of subtlety - at least not as an end result. Not yet (it's inevitable though.) One exception is Windows 7 support for 48bpp, a significant improvement over 32bpp, but with no consumer-level video card support that I know of. Also, GPUs do typically calculate color internally at higher precision before final downsampling to 32bpp, otherwise gradient banding (after compositing many layers) becomes too overwhelming. Even then, many DirectX and OpenGL games still show significant gradient banding when many affect layers are calculated, e.g. smoke and lighting.

To understand why 16bpc yields so much better results than 8bpc, consider that the difference between 8 bits and 16 bits is not "two times better". **16bpc is *256 times better* than 8bpc**. (Whether or not the human eye registers an end result as that much better is a different question - addressed further down.)

Even though video cards and monitors cannot display 16bpc, that is irrelevant. The bit depth of the end result is usually of little consequence (as long as it's 8bpc or better, i.e. "True Color", which is usually "good enough" for the human eye as an end result.) The interesting stuff happens during intermediate transforms. That's where you need higher precision; because each processing step involves loss of precision. (For that very reason, pro audio tools often process tracks at 24-bit 192khz, even though the human ear can't discern the difference between that and much lower quality. It is the intermediate steps that can totally mangle the sound - mixing and processing of a dozen tracks, if not done at a high enough bit depth and sample frequency.)

The binary math is simple. For any given color component of any given pixel, the number of shades available for 8bpc = 2^8 = 256. 256 shades each for red, green, and blue. 16bpc = 2^16 = 65,536. 65,536 shades each for red, green, and blue. That's 256 times more shades subtleties, not 2 times more. Or to look at it another way: with 8bpc, there are only SIX shade subleties between the extremes of black and white (those in-between shades are much more useful to photographers than all black or all white). With 16bpc, there is 65,534 - or 10,922 times more shades of in-between. (By the same useful logic, 32bpc has 71,582,788 more in-between shades [for each of the three colors of each pixel] than 8bpc.)

GIMP in all of it's existence including the current version, is mostly limited to 8bpc (or 24bpp). This makes it completely unsuitable for all but the simplest artwork and/or photography, and just not at all suitable for professional use. No matter how advanced it may be in every other way. (And it is quite impressive in nearly every other way! Which makes this all the more tragic.)

There is a really dumb but naggingly persistent argument (really it's a meme - or even an urban legend) that goes, "You can't tell the difference between images processed in an 8bpc workflow, vs 16bpc." Which, of course, is complete bulldung. (The truth is that most eyes cannot tell the difference between 8bpc and 16bpc *final images*. Quite a different thing than *workflow*.)

The proof is easy to do yourself. Just find a photograph that has alot of smooth gradient in it, such as a wide-angle shot with alot of clear sky. Now, simply add another gradient on top of it (with any one of several layer blending methods that yield useful results). This gradient might be an artificial "black to transparent" layer - or it might be the same image darkened but with a mask applied, itself with a gradient. (E.g. to make a "darkened sky gradient" effect.)

Flatten the layer. In 8bpc, you should already notice significant banding in the gradient areas. Now do a similar step one more time, and the difference will punch you in the face. (Now consider that in professional photography, similar transforms can easily be done a dozen times.)

Do this two times in Photoshop - once with a 8bpc workflow, another with a 16bpc workflow. As a final step, flatten the images, convert the 16bpc one to 8bpc, and save both as a standard 8bpc (24bpp) JPEG file at highest quality. Compare the two side-by-side (or better yet the flip-back-and-forth test). You'll see significant and obvious banding in the gradient areas, of the one made in an 8bpc workflow. And not in the one with a 16bpc workflow. Even though the end results are both 8bpc (and displayed through an 8bpc adapter and usually even lower quality display.)

"Dithering", you might say. Yes, dithering is awesome - whether downsampling high-bit depth photos, video, or audio. But dithering is only useful as the last step when going, say, from 16bpc to 8bpc. It is not useful for intermediate workflow, unless you like your products looking (or sounding) like crap.

The results between 16bpc workflow and 8bpc are usually more dramatic in simple computer-generated (e.g. raytraced) scenes, which are usually jam-packed with impossibly smooth and continuous gradients.

My own standard Photoshop Actions [written myself over many years], that I use on every photo, includes about 100 sub- and sub-sub- routines, each delivering a flattened result into a main working space (or more often into a higher-level interim step) of less than ten layers. If interim steps were not flattened, I'd have a completely unmanageable mess of at least a thousand layers, and quickly run out of 16GB of RAM - I push it as it is. (And the CPU would be backed up for days.) The results in an all-8bpc workflow are simply grotesque and unusable. I even have problems sometimes in 16bpc depending on the image. I wish Photoshop had full support for 32bpc (and not just for HDR). So do many professionals.

**TL;DR: GIMP is awesome and amazing; but cannot be used for professional or serious work (at least any such work involving natural or artificial gradients), due to one simple reason: it's lack of suport for 16bpc color or higher. No plugin or addon can fix this, it is a core engine feature. v2.8 is supposed to fix that, and the underlying engine (changed in v2.6 I believe) is supposedly already capable. But until v2.8 arrives (and has been taking FOREVER it seems), and 16bpc fully exposed in all areas of the program, Photoshop is the only realistic solution for serious and/or professional image production.**
