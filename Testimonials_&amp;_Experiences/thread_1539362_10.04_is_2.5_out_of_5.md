### Post by Tamlynmac on 2010-07-27
> orky7
 
 Just a thought.
 
 For a number of years now I've used a spare HDD for testing Ubuntu upgrades.  I've even used it occasionally to test updates, where my confidence level  was shall we say less than 100%. ;)
 
 I use an inexpensive (used) 20 gig usb HDD and developed a testing  procedure  that tests all system components and required apps. Should the  new version prove to be undesirable, I waste no time reinstalling the  previous version or concerning myself with potential backup flaws. I  simply delete the upgrade from the HDD and alter my boot sequence.
 
 We also had issues with 10.04 on three of our four systems. After  testing,  I chose to keep all four systems in the 9.04 environment. Notice that  I'm not frustrated, nor am I complaining. Since stability and  functionality are my families  primary concerns, upgrading is only considered after testing confirms  improved performance in those areas. Of course speed is a factor, but  functionality & stability are mandatory.
 
 Might I suggest, that in the future you consider using a separate HDD to  test upgrades prior to committing. An additional benefit of a spare HDD  is it can be used for beta testing new versions of Ubuntu or FOSS  applications without impacting your existing system.

Perhaps, a simple solution to the anxiety associated with upgrading.
 
 Good Luck

### Post by robc02 on 2010-07-28
I do something similar by having two Ubuntu partitions on the same drive. By doing this I found that Intrepid was not going to work for me due to driver incompatibility at the time, so was able to go back to using Hardy - still intact on its on partition. I just needed to update grub.
Now, I have installed Lucid on the second partition (having got rid of Intrepid) and was able to spend time testing and tweaking it, while the other users carried on with Hardy. I have now switched to Lucid for all users, but Hardy is still there, just in case!
You do, of course, need a decent sized hard drive for this approach.
