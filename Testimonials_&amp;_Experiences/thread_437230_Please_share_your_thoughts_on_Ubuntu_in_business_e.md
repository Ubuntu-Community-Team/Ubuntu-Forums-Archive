i tend to disagree. although there are major advantages for companies to switch their servers to unix/linux, as for desktops, there are no other advantage other than cost. but the cost is seen as being worthwhile since you dont get the same amount of usefulness as you would from a windows box.

one example is vendor lock-in. open source zealots always say that microsoft keeps you locked-in, however, should you choose linux you too are locked-in, and by a far greater perspective. suddenly, you cant buy any printer, it has to be HP. you cant buy any video card, it must be NVidia. etc. etc.

so for most companies it would seem that paying that bit extra ends up saving on a lot of headaches. with windows, you have the choice in printer. if you wanna buy a lexmark, then go for it. but if you run linux, dont dare think anything other than HP. now yes, i know, its the hardware vendors to blaim etc etc, but im not looking to point fingers. im looking for the best solution now. i wanna be able to walk into a shop, buy any piece of hardware or software, and i want it to work. only windows gives this to us.

dont get my wrong, i am no fan of microsoft. in fact, i wish i could use ubuntu instead of  windows. but as far as fully equipt desktops go, ALL linux distros are lacking in various areas, for example, voice/video conferencing. so in conclusion, i figure that major companies wont make the switch, not because they are scared, but because it does NOT give what windows can give. windows IS more capable on the desktop.

An ordinary business desktop does not need 3D acceleration. The open-source nv, ati or Intel drivers will do fine; even Vesa (which works on all cards) will be okay. As for printers, okay it's probably accurate to say that in a mission-critical situation you'll want to use the printer that supports Linux. But if another printer manufacturer comes out and writes Linux drivers, then you're no longer locked in to one vendor. With Microsoft applications, you're continuously locked into Microsoft with no hope of relief.
