### Post by stil on 2007-08-21
It has been about 2 months since I started using Ubuntu on a regular basis. The initial month was fraught with problems and complications. After a time I moved from dapper to feisty and started connecting directly to the internet - and I believe this should be stressed as an important aspect of the OS. Without the internet any subseqeunt problems can be magnified by a matter of degree dependant on the availability of a connection.

I'm only a beginner in the sense that I'm not used to the way a Linux OS is organised. I've owned several Amigas which successfully introduced me to the CLI, and since their demise I've made do with the rather dull Windows platform.

In terms of hardware support, Ubuntu is doing what I need it to do. I haven't been gaming on my machine (A200 notebook) but that's not a big issue for me at the moment. What is good is that my lack of sound hardware support was fixed by someone working(coding) in their own time and with feedback from other users in the same situation as I was. And that - right there - is what attracts me the most to this OS. 

Initially I had driver support problems with the Vista OEM install. After a call to Toshiba led me to believe they employ monkeys on tech-support I searched forums for weeks on end to find a solution to what seemed to be an obvious and inexcusable flaw in the graphics drivers. After being ignored by Nvidia and Toshiba I moved to Ubuntu.

With that out of the way, I'm going to talk about how I've been using Ubuntu.

- OpenOffice provides a good platform for writing. Couldn't ask for more, really.

- GIMP has tablet support and beats out my old copy of Photoshop. And it's constantly udated. Win win.

- After following some tutorials and help pages I can now watch full-motion DVD (and it looks pretty damn good)

- RhythmBox does everything I ask of it, and the OS is stable enough to let me rip music while listening to RB as it updates the new tunes added to the library. *Cool*

- I'm slowly getting used the the terminal enviroment and have it on-call with a shortcut key. Using apt-get install to gain .lha support within a matter of seconds was a real eye opener.


In summary...

- Inexperienced users really need immediate net access or they're just making things hard on themselves.

- 'Just works'

- Comes with a lot of useful software straight from the get-go.

- Can consume much time when something isn't working right, but costs practically nothing in the way of monetary investment.

As a bonus, learning Linux opens the door to many other releases - and if you're sick of computers being treated as faux proprietary hardware for Windows then there's little reason not to make the change.
