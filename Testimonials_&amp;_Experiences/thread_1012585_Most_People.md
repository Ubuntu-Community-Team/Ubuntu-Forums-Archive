### Post by xarte on 2008-12-16
For a while there I tried to couch most things I said in personal terms: "I find such-and-such" or "in my experience" or "yes, that happens to me too" in an attempt to avoid generalizing and to show that I identified with what people were saying. But instead, that made it sound as though 'it was all about me' - not my intention at all.

So then I replace it with  "it is possible" and "some people" and "yeah, it can be like that, can't it" and so language becomes even more vague and meaningless.....

Having said that: I agree that "most people" is often used entirely inappropriately. Usually by someone who wants to claim membership of 'most people' in order to give weight to their argument, eg, "Most people prefer vi to emacs" or "most people prefer coffee" as if the emacs users and tea drinkers really care what most people do.

As others have said or implied, the key thing here is to asses why they are using the 'most people' or similar generality, whether it's identifying with, setting themselves apart from, etc.
