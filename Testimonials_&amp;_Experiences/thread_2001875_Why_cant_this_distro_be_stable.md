### Post by chocklet on 2012-06-11
I can't say anything about 10.04, as I am new to Ubuntu.  However...

After using Debian for a few months in the past, I can report that Debian worked pretty well but had a series of issues with accelerated graphics, sound, and printing, and that resolving those issues was not easy for an inexpert user.  We finally left it behind.

After tinkering with both Scientific and Cent I suspect that, with only main repositories enabled, Scientific and Cent can be stable.  However, I would advise inexpert desktop users that once you add other repositories (there is discussion about which are best) and multimedia and full internet capabilities, you can easily end up with the same kinds of problems that arise with other fully-functional systems.

With Debian stable, Scientific, and Cent, one need not stick with 2.6.32.  But a reoccuring phrase in threads dealing with optional kernels is, "YMMV".

Some might say that a user can have a "stable" system only if he/she has some expertise, a less-than-fully-functional system, and hardware that is old enough to work without issues with 2.6.32.  While there is some truth to the notion, it leaves out something important... it leaves out the reality that most desktop people have spent all of their lives using systems that are less stable than 12.04 Unity.

During the past few weeks we've used 12.04 Unity, pretty much as it is designed to be used, on a range of hardware from oldish to modern.  It has been solid.

### Post by QIII on 2012-06-12
This is not meant to be argumentative.  Simply a discussion.  

Snowpine: You have it correct:  "I find..." is valid observation.

"12.04 is unstable" is not.

The problems with a universal assessment of instability are four:

1.  Absolute statements are invalidated by a single anecdotal case to the contrary.

2.  Any person's individual experience is a sample size of one.  Statistically insignificant.  Even finding a hundred cases the same as yours is too small a sample size.

3.  A repeated failure with systems having a common piece of hardware evidences an issue with either the hardware itself or the OS's interaction with that particular hardware item.  That is bad enough, of course, but not indicative of overall instability. 

4.  Help forums, by their nature, suffer from a phenomenon known as "referral bias".  People come to help forums when things go wrong, not when things go right.  The experiences described here cannot be assumed to apply to the population as a whole and cannot be used to determine a trend.

"I am having problems with x" is valid.  "x is a problem" likely is not.

None of this diminishes anyone's problems, which are certainly important to them and should be addressed.  However, as a scientist whose work is centered around large scale modeling of complex systems, I must point these things out.
