### Post by OldHarry on 2010-08-30
Dear All,

We are pretty good Windows users. When we buy a new system we delete and reformat everything and recreate from scratch. Once we completely rebuilt a system within 1.5 hours, including applications and files, for a relative in desperate need. We have been using computers for 15-20 years. We also have qualifications in programming. The laptop we are using is old but still acceptable and in working order. It was working every day on Windows XP quite happily. We also started with a lot of enthusiasm, expecting some problems but nothing that we couldn't overcome. We would have been very pleased if Ubuntu had lived-up to even half the hype that we have been told so much on the internet. I am saying all this in order to head-off the inevitable responses to this thread.

We have spent the entire Bank Holiday weekend trying to get Ubuntu 10.04 to work on a laptop. In fact we've done nothing else but this. In addition to the laptop, we have also used another PC in support. This second PC was used to download files and look for information, something that we commonly do to make things quicker and easier. We have only taken a few tea-breaks, eaten less than normal, not gone shopping, forgone the cleaning and tidying, and not even switched the telly on.

The results of this hive of activity has concluded with the realisation that Ubuntu is frankly dreadful.

We do not recall a session that resulted in us switching it off, it just crashed in an entirely random manner. There was absolutely no apparent pattern. It might have been during the bootup, or perhaps after an hour of use, but sooner or later it would inexplicably crash. Maybe this is because 10.04 has seemingly affected a great many other people this way without any cause being firmly identified. According to the posts and comments we have been reading, it might be hardware drivers, video cards, mozilla firefox, and probably a host of other things as well. About the only thing that doesn't seem to have been identified as a cause is the Loch Ness Monster. And Ubuntu itself. The only certain solution we have found reported is that the problem apparently ceases of its own accord after a few months. This is a very odd way of solving a problem.

In the end we just became tired and disappointed. It looks good, it sounds good, but it just doesn't work. I am genuinely sorry, but it wasn't good enough or stable enough. Sometimes it would take three attempts to get Ubuntu to bootup - it would just go black with a cursor, then the next attempt it would just hang, and the third attempt it would succeed. Then there was the barcode of death - the bar of vertical striping that would appear in the screen after a crash, provided the screen just didn't turn black. We did consider trying to downgrade to 9.10, and even downloaded the file, but quickly found ourselves prevented from the attempt. 

Ok, there is going to be a culture-shock but this got silly. Anything that takes us and the computer longer than an afternoon to do is probably a non-starter. Big criticisms on our part were that we didn't realise we would have to write the operating system ourselves. There were big holes in the system requiring further work after we had finished. We didn't know why these couldn't have been sorted before the system was released, rather than expect people to know what to do without instruction. Normally, if there's a problem, slap in the disk or download a patch and the system builds itself according to what we want. Not so with Ubuntu. Its like the car from the Flintstones. Not only do you have to drive the car but you have to get out and walk it home as well. 

Everyone has something that seemed to work for their PC, but doesn't seem to quite work for the next one. But there doesn't seem to be anything solid that solves the problem for everyone. Its just feels like trial and error to me half the time.

I am seriously not trying to be clever but I never heard Scotty say, "The coding cannae take it Cap'n." I'll admit that maybe we might have missed something. I can't think what that might be because we tried everything. After all, there must be millions of people who are using this system quite happily without a problem. Unfortunately, we are reasonably intelligent and experienced computer users. If we can't do it, then something is not quite right.

Personally, I'm kind of hoping that someone will point out the very obvious thing that we missed. At least we can go away only feeling a bit embarrassed but still have the system we worked so hard to create. Frankly, what we will do next is a head-scratcher. Its not nice giving up, but there is such a thing as throwing good money after bad. And, yes, I realise that Ubuntu is free.

Apologies.

---
