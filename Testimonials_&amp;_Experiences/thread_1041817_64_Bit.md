### Post by lavinog on 2009-01-17
One benefit is that some numerical approximations will converge in less iterations...this can benefit many applications that require complicated calculations such as compression, video and audio encoding, and I think encryption.
Most other applications could still get a speed benefit in this area, but may only offer a nano-second of improvement. There is also a point that 64bit pointers (memory addresses) will slow the system down, but you may not see the difference.

The 4 gig limit is still an issue with some 64 bit systems...some motherboards were preventing users from using over 3.5g even with a 64bit kernel. I think this was resolved though in a recent kernel.

I have two issues with my 64bit os:
There are still a couple of issues with drivers: High speed usb, and some of my usb storage devices get corrupted data more than my 32 bit systems. I am also having issues with snd_hda-intel for audio where my audio device locks up randomly during ut2004 and I have to do a alsa force-restart to restore it. (I cannot recreate this on demand though)
The other issue is that there are still packages/programs that don't have a 64bit counterpart.
Freeorion has a bunch of dependancies that are not available in the repositories for intrepid. There are static linked packages, but only for 32 bit.

The recent 64bit flash and java is hugh thing. The previous hack was terrible...I had to reload webpages all the time because flash kept crashing.
I am almost at the point that I will be installing a 32bit partition for comparing the above driver issues...I just verified that it isn't a hardware issue by using windows.

To sum it up...64bit support is much better than it was in previous years, but there is still room for improvement...so if you want a system that takes advantage of everything your system has, go 64. If you want a system that is as sturdy and reliable, stay with 32.
