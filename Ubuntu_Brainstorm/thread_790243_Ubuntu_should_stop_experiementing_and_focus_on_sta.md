### Post by quickshade on 2008-05-12
Stable isn't always the best. What you have to think about is the 3-4 years that some of these distros will be running without doing a distro upgrade. If you include something thats out of date or will be in 1-2 years (firefox 2) then your talking forcing people to update in the middle of the distros lifetime. Which goes against Ubuntu's policy. Not to mention that one of the biggest bonuses to Firefox 3 is that it's not only more stable, but runs 100% better than Firefox 2.

As for other applications, You have to understand that many parts of linux are being cleaned up to support new and more efficient applications. Pulse audio being one of them. It was a mess to fix sound in Linux. So someone created Pulse audio, which while still buggy has way better support for sound. Or things like KDE4, people say it's unusable. But they don't take the time to realize that KDE3 is a nightmere to try and code for, has many flaws and has some areas that have only 1 developer who knows what he's doing. This isn't a good idea at all. In KDE4 they made sure that the code was clean, easy to understand and not bloated or unmaintained. One of the biggest features, Phonon, was considered a bit unstable when released, but it provided a huge advantage to developers and the KDE team, it can use any audio engine programmed to use it, this means that if an engine is shutdown or not maintained anymore, they aren't stuck with it (which happened in KDE3) 

I don't want to make this a long rant on KDE4 or why new releases are better, but Linux is growing so fast that new, better apps are created often, and to use a program because it works (but is bloated, hard to understand or unmaintained) instead of updating to one thats less stable but works better and has developers would be stupid. I do agree that having a stable OS is important, but having one that will work in a year is also important.
