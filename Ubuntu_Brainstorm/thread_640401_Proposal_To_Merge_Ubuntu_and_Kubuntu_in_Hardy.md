### Post by coolen on 2007-12-29
First, I'd like to say that this will not happen, and this conversation is probably completely pointless.

Ubuntu is what it is: it's an easy-to-use system, devoid of unnecessary choice, professional system.

We don't ask the user which desktop environment they want to use because, while it may not be particularly difficult, it's an unnecessary choice: if they have no opinion, why make them choose, and if they do have an opinion, chances are they've grabbed the CD for whichever one they prefer.

We don't use KDE by default because (and I don't mean to sound biased here) it's daunting for the new user. Sure, Gnome may take the lack of decision to a bit of an extreme at times, but KDE menus are cluttered. They're consistent, which is great, but for the new user, it's a bit much. KDE's strength is ultimate power to the user, but most new users don't expect this, and don't really want it.

Gnome was chosen because it is the best fit for the goals of the Ubuntu project. This may change: I personally think that if you uncluttered the menus, KDE would be fantastic for the new user, and am myself thinking of switching come Hardy.

However, I feel that GNOME has less of a learning curve to it as opposed to KDE, and for a desktop environment that *anyone* can use, it's much less distracting (see: bouncy wait icons vs. plain hourglasses).  If the goal was to have just a home environment, I'd be more inclined to say "sure, go for it!"  But for dual-use of home and work desktops, I'd say a basic GNOME install is the way to go, and the way to stay.
