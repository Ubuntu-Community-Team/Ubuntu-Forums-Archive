### Post by gnomeuser on 2009-01-04
> **smartboyathome said:**
> Well, I was thinking that it wasn't, guess I was wrong. Still, I know it doesn't use Xorg. :(

For the targets of Android: Phones, Set top boxes, possibly MID devices going without a X implementation is very likely a good idea. X is somewhat overloaded and hard to debug for these use cases. It also allows them to innovate and maybe bring something new to the table the rest of us might be able to learn from.

I actually think Android would make a good fit for a a portable player. You get good APIs for most common features like wifi, bluetooth, usb, media playback along with an easy framework to write your frontend in. Much of your code would be very well tested as well (it's hard to beat the amount of testing done by being deployed on a couple of million cellphones). I think it would be an interesting project to adapt Android for this purpose.

Imagine the fun stuff we could concentrate on making, mesh networking between players (ala OLPC Sugar). Imagine getting on the bus and other owners of this type of player will be auto discovered. You could now send them music, play little games such as playing 10 secs of a song on the other persons player and guessing correctly between 3 choices. You could do proximity syncing, once the player gets within range of your network it automatically resyncs with your computers music library. This latter feature would also do well with a car entertainment system, it could suck down all your music, movies the kids can watch during long drives all from the comfort of the garage. I don't know of any commercially available system that will do this and I think the key to making it work would be defining open standards for hooking into this wider ecosystem.
