### Post by teasum on 2007-11-17
I guess I see the basic point of your post, but I also see a logical flaw--Ubuntu isn't designed or intended to be so lightweight, or minimal, or whatever.  It's designed with a particular set of goals in mind.  Other options, many of which have been mentioned in this thread, are good alternatives, such as a custom installation, or Fluxbuntu, Xubuntu, etc.  (On an old laptop of mine, for example, I opted for a CLI installation with IceWM, Firefox, gaim, and a text editor.... works for me.)  In other words, if your goal is to bring linux to a wider audience, compete with Microsoft (it's bug #1, after all), then including Games makes sense, and does not appear as simply bloat.

Another point, however, has to do with the nature of linux apps, though I'm sure many here on the forums can address this better than I.  As I understand it, the preference in linux is for small apps to do a few tasks well, and to be interoperable with other apps, rather than large "I'll do everything you want and more" apps.  So, with your soundjuicer vs. rythmbox example, I think you bring up a problem of integration rather than bloat--doesn't rythmbox simply use soundjuicer to rip audio files?  Please correct me if I'm wrong.  So, this is not a case of two apps doing the same thing, but one app accessible in two ways to the user--as a stand alone, or via another program acting as a front-end.  The same could be said, for example, of streamripper and several other music apps that use streamripper as a backend via a plugin.

My approach to this would be to work on integration among apps to remove actual or perceived duplication of function, when possible, and to improve configurability.  I like the idea of an 'advanced mode' to the installer that would allow some greater control over the programs that are or are not installed.  

That's just my opinion!
