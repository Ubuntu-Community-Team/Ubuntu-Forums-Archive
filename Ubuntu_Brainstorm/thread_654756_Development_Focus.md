### Post by arvevans on 2007-12-31
Over the years much of Linux development has resulted from individual interests and has been driven by a need to fix specific problems encountered by these individuals.  Maybe I am just uninformed, but there does not seem to be a serious effort to categorize, track, and publish statistical information regarding help requests and use this to provide a reliable view of what needs to be fixed, improved, etc.

Case in point is the apparent recent increase in forum requests for help with WiFi cards and WiFi USB dongles.  Agreed that this will be a tough area to work on, but it is also one that may impede mainstream acceptance of Linux on the non-technical person's desktop.  

Multiple core CPU's and 64 bit hardware are now readily available at reasonable prices, yet many of us with 64 bit MP capable hardware are still running 32-bit versions of Linux because not all the mainstream applications have been ported to the 64 bit repositories.

These problem areas are only two of many that seem to get a significant amount of attention in the forum.  Are they the top issues, or are there other areas that are more important to more Linux users?  How can we tell if there is no statistical data that documents the request rate for each category. 

As popular technology evolves it seems prudent to use help request statistics to focus development in new directions to support that technology.  With this in mind, would it be possible, advisable, desirable, etc. to develop and publish near real time statistical information showing level of help requests for each hardware and software category?  If this can be done it should highlight areas of weakness and strength of Linux OS and could be used to better prioritize and focus development efforts in areas which need it most.

Admittedly, categorizing help requests is a real problem.  Many requests are not stated in terms that allow easy evaluation and categorization to a specific problem area.  Possibly some sort of user pre-categorization (check boxes, etc.) could be employed as part of the help request posting process?  It would not be perfect, but it would be an initial data point that could be used to start the problem sorting process.

### Post by arvevans on 2008-01-02
Maybe I am missing something here.  What I suggested was that that Linux development could benefit if we knew what problems are most vexing for it's users.  I did not suggest that we track who has what problem, who is using what application, who is complaining, or any such thing.

Over the past 45 years I have run a number of help desk operations.  As part of that function we did keep counts of the number of problems in each category (network access, LAN problems, hardware failures by manufacturer, etc.)  This information was used for statistical analyses to determine where the developers needed to put more time & effort.

Every time you make a store purchase a record of what you purchased is logged in the selling company statistical database, but your name and other personal information is not included.  The data on what was purchased (size, color, date, etc.) is corporate property and is tracked.  They use this to determine what products to stock in which store, and what time of year to adjust stock of particular items.  So, like it or not, anonymous statistical information is collected for almost everything you do.  This information belongs to the provider (several legal cases have proven that in the US) and you have no say in it.

Every time you make a phone call, an anonymous statistical record is made of that call(call'ed city, calling city, time of call, duration of call, etc.).  This helps phone providers determine how much bandwidth to assign for specific traffic routes and what time of day it needs to be provided. 

Every time you initiate a network session that passes through network routers, those routers add counts to the record of bandwidth used versys bandwidth available.  Network providers use this information to determine how much bandwidth is needed for various routes (how much static bandwidth, how much dynamic bandwidth, and quality-of-service predictions from this data).

I was only suggesting that it would be prudent to collect similar anonymous statistical information regarding which category of Linux software receives the most requests for help.  If that is violation of privacy then we all might as well throw our computers out the door and be done with both Microsoft and Linux.  Almost everything you do today ends up being a statistic that is used by someone to improve service, profitability, public relations, and so on.  Why should a Linux support group be the only one to not consider help requests as being indicative of problem areas that might need developer focus...unless this is FUD (FUD = the spreading of Fear, Uncertainty, & Doubt by one's competitors) from the "other OS" camp to keep us from improving Linux.
