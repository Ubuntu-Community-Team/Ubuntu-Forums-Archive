### Post by aysiu on 2008-08-01
Similar to the Apple store, the Canonical store would have hardware picked to work well with the software, software designed to work well with the hardware, and a more or less unified look and feel among products. It would be both an online store and a physical store one could walk into and try things in, just like the Apple store.

The big difference between the Canonical store and the Apple store? The only difference that matters: Freedom.

With the Apple store, Apple tells you "You can't use our software on unapproved hardware" and "You can't play these iTunes-purchased songs on Linux or on non-iPod players."

With the Canonical store, Canonical would tell you "If you're not sure what to buy, these products will work well together and provide you with a complete and integrated user experience. If, however, you want to use our free software with other hardware, that's up to you. You can modify the software, too, to suit your needs. We believe in freedom."

What do you think? Would you shop at the Canonical store? If not, would you recommend others do so?

Here's the Brainstorm idea:
[Idea #8518: Physical Canonical Store](http://brainstorm.ubuntu.com/idea/6847/)

Here's a blog post I wrote explaining the concept in more detail:
[Ubuntu: The Open Source Apple Challenger?](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntucat/ubuntu-the-open-source-apple-challenger/)

### Post by aysiu on 2008-08-04
Well, it would certainly make more money than pressing and mailing free CDs worldwide.

The profit margins for OEMs are slim, but they can still make a profit. The focus would be on hardware and support sales and not software sales (though the $20 boxed Ubuntu could also be available in-store on shelves). It's a viable, if not lucrative, business model.
