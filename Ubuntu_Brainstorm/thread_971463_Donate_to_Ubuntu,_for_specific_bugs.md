### Post by gnomeuser on 2008-11-06
> **snake444 said:**
> if this will be implemented , almost all the devs will be focusing on earning money by fixing bugs and getting $$ 
and the really important bugs will be set to will be fixed later

Depends.. 

Larger bugs might tend to carry with it a larger reward as more users might pledge money. You might also have a big problem that leaves out a large amount of users, by simple assumption fewer users -> fewer bugs. Fewer bugs would mean less money meaning there is incentive to fix that bug merely to get more bugs.

Here is the problem, the hourly rate for a qualified programmer is not going to be covered by the 10$ donation a user might to offer. If a programmer was thus going to make a living he would have to maximize profit by seeking quick bug resolution. This would tend to lead to bugfixes being suboptimal or even wrong long term, it would also lead to bugfixes not getting accepted upstream.

Additionally if you as a user add an offer of money you are going to expect your bug to get fixed (at least to a higher degree than a baseline of offering nothing). Disappointing users will thus happen often as they will offer a bit of cash and nothing happens.

Here one WebKit developer runs though how one 16 character fix ended up costing effectively 1200$ in work time and involving 3 engineers:

[http://blog.pierlux.com/2008/06/12/the-cost-of-a-bug-fix/en/](http://blog.pierlux.com/2008/06/12/the-cost-of-a-bug-fix/en/)

Do you honestly think we can end up offering 1200$ regularly for little bugfixes? There is a reason why Ubuntu does not turn a profit, profit is not found in fixing bugs for end users. Profit is found in specialised development as well as offering support contracts. This does mean that bugs filed by companies with a support contract will tend to get fixed first but they will hit the very same bugs as you do. Let businesses pay for your bugfixes, you can be helpful in finding them, adding information, gathering opinions from upstream (and related downstreams).

There is one place where user payment might make sense, if a large amount of users all want one feature, they might pool together and contract a developer to implement it. gathering 5000$ to get webcam support in Empathy for MSN e.g. would be a fine "bounty" target. This however has not historically been a big success, especially unfortunately Ubuntu users, seem to think they should get everything for free meaning they are hard to get to pay for such work (trust me on this one, I have some real world experience herding Ubuntu users to pay for driver development).
