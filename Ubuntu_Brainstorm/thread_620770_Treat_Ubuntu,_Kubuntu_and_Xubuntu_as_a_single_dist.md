### Post by aysiu on 2007-11-23
The real problem is that Ubuntu's philosophy is one CD, one application per task, few questions during installation--i.e., simplicity.

The other distros rely on asking you a lot of questions during the installation and making choices about what desktop environments you want installed when you may not even know what a desktop environment is.

Ubuntu doesn't require you to know what Gnome is in order to use Gnome. Kubuntu doesn't require you to know what KDE is in order to use KDE.

### Post by coolen on 2007-12-06
I'm voting this one down.

While I think offering a selection of environments if not a flaw (in fact, I'd like the choice), the fact is that it's not how Ubuntu works.

Aysiu said it'd simply be replacing the headache of Ubuntu, Kubuntu, and Xubuntu with that of Gnome, KDE and XFCE.

Well, consider each of these headaches from the perspective of the new user.

Worst case scenario, they'll have to choose one of the three desktop environments. Whether it's during the install or before the download, it's going to come up.

Best case scenario, they'll have a friend who uses an Ubuntu derivative who can just hand them a CD. They take it, install it, love it, then they learn about the other environments they can try out, give them a go, and make their own decision about which they prefer at a later date.

Putting them all on one DVD, it's a hard choice either way.

Having the option is great, and I like that other distros do offer this, but honestly, Ubuntu can't be everything. Ubuntu is an OS that requires as little input from the user during the setup process as possible, with almost no choices to be made. This is the niche Ubuntu has chosen to fill.
