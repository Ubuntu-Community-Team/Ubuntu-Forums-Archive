### Post by Objekt on 2010-03-10
I have tried both of the subject OS's on my Acer Aspire One netbooks, models D150 and D250.  Impressions:

Moblin: Interesting, but ultimately too weird.  I only ran it in a Live USB stick session, so I don't know all hardware worked.  I didn't have a router with wireless at the time, but the wired networking was fine.  The Moblin interface and lack of the usual controls (where the heck is "shutdown?") were just too different for me.  I suppose I'm set in my ways.  Moblin might work well for someone who has never used a computer before, or doesn't care to learn any details about how they work.

Ubuntu Netbook Remix 9.10: Installed alongside Windows XP (which came with the netbooks) on both of my remaining Aspire Ones.  I like the somewhat odd GUI compared to desktop Ubuntu or XFCE.  It makes good use of the tiny widescreen display.  I never have to deal with windows that are partly offscreen or larger than the desktop.  Wireless switch doesn't work (more on that below).

Here's another one you may not have known about: Kuki Linux 2.8.  It is an adaptation of Ubuntu 9.04, using the XFCE desktop, tailored specifically for the Acer Aspire One netbooks.  All hardware worked in Kuki, except the stupid wireless on/off switch (more on this below).  It was a joy to use.  I only stopped using Kuki because the netbook it was on died.  I haven't got around to installing Kuki on the remaining 2 netbooks, but might do so eventually.

The common thing that has NOT worked is the physical "wireless on/off" slider on the front of the Aspire Ones.  For some reason, it does absolutely nothing when running Ubuntu.  That's just dumb, irritating, and probably Acer's fault.  I can still enable/disable wireless through the OS, but that is nowhere near as nice as having the physical switch.  I don't know how Acer managed to make this switch dependent on software, and Windows-only software at that.  Dumb, dumb idea.  I don't see the point of a "switch" that isn't actually a switch.

### Post by JimTaverna on 2010-04-28
Haven't tried Moblin, and after having one friend tell me it was the second coming of Microsoft Bob, I think I'll pass. 

Netbook: HP Mini 110-1100 CTO, 1gb RAM, 160gb SATA HDD

As for UNR,

[SIZE="2"]***What I like:***[/SIZE]

**Clean interface with a nice structure to the menu layout.** 

I'd prefer at times that the App pane go away, a la FF's All-In-One Sidebar's hide feature, and some of the icons are pretty dumbed down and childish (friggin' Tomboy Notes?), but for the most part it does its job without insulting my intelligence. Isn't as configurable as I would have hoped, but maybe that will change in the future.

**Good to great hardware support.**

The only issue I've had is with my Broadcom Crystal HD accelerator, and that has more to do with lack of software support than UNR. I actually have the drivers loaded, though they're all dressed up with nowhere to go. With one exception. Flash 10.1. 

Adobe did a fine job here. Flash worked well in conjunction with the Crystal HD under Windows Chrome, since it supports the card, and hopefully the Linux BC Linux drivers will be comparable. Other than that though, it's almost useless since none of the supplied apps take advantage of it, Hulu being the exception. Still, most of my time is spent on the net and it's a big advantage having it around. Very big.

Wan and lan. Both worked out of the box, the Atheros AR9285 and AR3182M respectively, though the signal is a little weak on the AR9285 compared to my Win system. Better drivers and a little tweaking should fix that. A genuine pleasure not having to worry about setting up a wireless connection.

Webcam. Personally, I almost never use it or Skype, so if it didn't work, not much would be lost. Surprisingly though, UNR picked it up without a hitch. Someone did their homework. Kudos.

Media card. A Realtek RTS5159 that UNR sees as a removable drive. Haven't pushed it any further, so there may be an issue here, but for now, I'm a happy camper.

USB ports. Hell, if they got this wrong I'd be writing a post on an Etch-A-Sketch.

Intel 945GM chipset/video. Dumbed down version of the Win driver, or so it appears. Didn't tweak it for graphics acceleration. Interestingly enough though, Movie Player works extremely well, with no dropped frames. Far better than WMP. No idea why, but I'm not complaining.

Sound. IDT HD Audio Codec. Generic driver that functions well. IIRC, Xubuntu correctly recognized it as an IDT chip. Strange. Good sound, though I do miss my SRS Audio Sandbox.

ACPI. Perfect! Power management is spot on. The only hitch, and frankly I consider it a minor one, is resuming from suspend. Does so rather quickly, but video isn't restored unless I adjust the brightness. A quick tap and the screen springs to life. I've had a lot of systems over the years and I can honestly say that UNR's PM is among the best. Not surprisingly, battery life is about 15% longer than in 7 or XP. Given the fact that I have a crappy 3 cell battery, that's almost an eternity.

Heat. Technically not a hardware issue, though I'll throw it in for good measure. Minor decrease, based solely on anecdotal evidence as I've yet to install Conky. Ran in the mid to high 50's on 7, with the occasional low 60's when stressed. Feels cooler and fan startups are less frequent.

**OS**

I make no claim to being a Linux expert, though I've tinkered with it, on and off, since the mid 90's. There was something about recompiling a kernel for something as insignificant now as adding printer support that made you feel that you actually had a hand in the creation of the system you ran. And a damn good feeling it was. I never forgot that and as sappy as that sounds it's true.

Frankly though, if it wasn't a damn good OS, ahead of the curve and rock solid, with a fervent, if not occasionally crazed, fan base to help you through the learning curve, I wouldn't run it.

The bottom line is it works, it's far less complicated to use than in previous years, it's free and comes with a boatload of better than functional apps. Welcome to nirvana...     

**Intangibles**

It's not a freakin' Microsoft or Apple product.

*[SIZE="2"]**What I don't like:**[/SIZE]*

**Long posts.** 

I'll keep this brief. The ability, or rather the lack thereof, to select or deselect features, apps, etc on install. A BartPE for Ubuntu would be slick.

Better uninstall process. Maybe I'm not fluent enough on the ins and outs of Ubuntu, but after having installed and subsequently removing Chrome and a few other apps via Synaptic, there still seems to be a lot of junk left over. Needless and sloppy.

Memory management. I'm not familiar with how Ubuntu handles it, but I swear that when I was running Xubuntu, my total memory expenditure was rarely above 40%. Under UNR, it's more like 90% and for the first time I'm using swap. Can't be that much of a difference between the two distros. Pre-allocated? In any case, the system doesn't seem any slower, so no concern...for now. 

I could probably think of a few more things, but I'd be nitpicking, so thus endeth the list.

I wonder if you'll be able to dual-boot UNR and Chrome OS?...
