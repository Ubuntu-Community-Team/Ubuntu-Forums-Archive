### Post by robenroute on 2007-02-24
Let me first of all state that what I'm writing here is based on the assumption that you're looking for a motherboard. With that out of the way, I reckon reading reviews and manufacturer's documentation is inescapable. The way I would tackle things is based on choices: socket, north-/southbridge. Gathering information is essential in making those choices, but common sense would be worth even more.

Newegg is a reasonable source regarding users' reviews of all kinds of hardware, including motherboards. Just make sure you read all (!) the write-ups from the users as some of them issue 5 stars/eggs because the board has such pretty colours. Or just a single star/egg is issued because the board isn't compatible with the memory they happen to have. I find it very helpful to sort the users' reviews starting with no stars/eggs and ending with the full 5 eggs. Also make sure you pay attention to the date the review was written; often issues are resolved by newer BIOS releases.

Good luck.

### Post by robenroute on 2007-02-26
No thanks needed. choosing the right components can be a frustrating quest. Differences between motherboards are sometimes subtle indeed. Looking at the two motherboards you mentioned, the differences would amount to the VM-model offering Firewire and a HD-TV output add-on. Now, that HD-TV hardware thingy might not work with Linux, or it just might. You see? With more exotic hardware, chances for problems (support under Linux) will increase. Also, very new chipsets (e.g. nVidia's 680) might not be supported by Linux at all. It more or less depends on what you want to do with your computer. Do you need Firewire? If not, leave it out; that also makes the motherboard cheaper. Do you need TV-out? Integrated (on the MB) hardware is, generally speaking, more problematic than dedicated hardware.

The people at [www.phoronix.com](www.phoronix.com) relularly test hardware for Linux compatibility. Have a look there.

Good luck!
