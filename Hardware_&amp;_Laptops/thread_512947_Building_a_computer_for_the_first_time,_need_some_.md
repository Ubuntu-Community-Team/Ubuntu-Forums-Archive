### Post by arsenic23 on 2007-07-30
Hmmm..

I'd like a link to the CPU you were actually looking at.  But here's my thoughts on building a PC right now:

Personally I'd go with a [Dual Core E6600]("http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16819115003").  Quadcore isn't quite mature yet, and if you've never build a PC before it's not the greatest idea to go out and get the newest untested hardware.  So I'd go with an E6600 because it's one of Intell strongest chips right now, and I'm fairly sure you'll get more power for your dollar with this chip then pretty much anything else you could purchase.

Next for a motherboard I'd get something running the new P35 chipset from Intell.  Right now Gigabyte and MSI seem to be putting out the nicest boards, so I'd more then likely go with something from one of those companies.  My personal pick would have to be [this one]("http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16813128050") from Gigabyte.  Also note that if your set one quad core, this should handle socket LGA 775 quad core CPUs as well.

Is this this CPU your looking at ??
[http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16819115017](http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16819115017)
Because that is also the quad core I would choose if I had my heart set on it.  It should work with the board I sugested.

Now when it comes to purchasing a video card I have an odd bit of advise for you:  Buy from the same brand as your motherboard manufacturer if you can help it.  If you have a problem with your product having both motherboard and graphics card coming from the same company will give you a minor advantage on the tech support line.  Also I've noticed ( in some cases, not in all ) mild performance increases from running similarly branded board/graphics card.  Of course, if you plan on running Ubuntu or another flavor of GNU linux, you'll more then likely have an easy time of it if you buy Nvidia over ATI - at least as long as ATI is putting out for shot linux drivers.

For RAM, just buy whatever the motherboard sugests.  I personally almost always buy Kingston value RAM.  Don't let someone talk you into buying 'performance RAM'.

As for sound - I don't konw if I could give you any great sugestions.  If you don't feel the onboard sound is good enough ( and I don't think I'd use onboard sound for a media center PC - I might though, prolly triy it out before I bought a card... anyway I'm rambling )  If you buy a card:
Make sure it's linux compatible.
Make sure it's Vista compatible even if you don't plan on ever using Vista ( I've heard that some cards just flop in Vista but have never seen this myself. )
I used to push Turtle Beach cards, but they don't look like they have anything available that I would recomend - also I'm not sure how well they work in linux.

Well I hope some of this helped.  Oh, and, in the future you might not want to put the same post up in multiple sections, it kind of spreads the advice you might receive out and also makes it a little confusing when browsing for fresh posts.

### Post by arsenic23 on 2007-07-30
I wouldn't worry about the hardware not lasting 5 years.  If your buying upper-mid to high end parts you'll find that the only peice of modern PC that really feels like it goes out of date before the end of the machine is the gaming GPU.  For me I have a main PC that lasts about 5 years from the date I purchase it, after that I build a new one and my preivious main PC gets reconfigured and set up as a server or sold.  Often I'll get another 3-5 years out of that one.  But since this is a media center PC, what your going to have to worry about is video compression and standards.  So as long as your machine is set to run with the next gen high-def content now, you should not have to worry about any upgrade or replacements untill something else comes along.  Considering the fact that we haven't even gotten down to one perfered high def media, I'd say the next big format change is good a while off.

Gaming GPUs tend to have a little steeper of an upgrade cycle; I'd say the average gamer replaces their video card every 3 years.  Of course I know some people who love their PC gaming and purchase a new video card every product generation.

ANWAY...  When it comes to getting a graphics card for your media center the only thing you have to worry about is wether or not it has the outputs that you'll need to connect it to your devices.  If you want to use HDMI then go find reviews for the card and make sure there are people saying that it works in Vista.  Alot of the first HDMI compatiable video cards where not actually standards compliant and will not work with Vista's DRM lockin. All HD-DVDs and Blueray discs have DRM tech in them.  Even if you don't plan on using Vista, it doesn't hurt to have a card that has 'true' HDMI.  If your TV/monitor suports DVI, then that's more then likely the easiest way to do it.  In that case almost any modern video card will work, as DVI is pretty much the standard output now-a-days.

As for cases, just look around, read the reviews, and get whatever ATX case strikes your fancey.  But do NOT use any powersupply that comes with a case.  It's just bad policy.  Picking the correct powersupply is actually very important and alot of information about them can be misleading.  If you read Newegg reviews you'll find that a lot of iffy PSUs get good reviews because they 'work'.  What the people who are writing this reviews don't realize is that even though the PSUs function and put out power stable enough to keep the computer running well, flimsy PSUs that put out mildly uneven power can shorten the life of your other components.  Now I don't normally like to sugest power supplies, because I really like to have one running under a meter before I say anything, but I can tell you that [this]("http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16817171001") PSU is definately usable.  It's not the best powersupply you could have, but I know it gives clean power, and I've had extensive experience with a fair amount of them.

Now I'm asuming your not going to be overclocking your mediacenter PC, so let me say this.  Don't get tricked into wasting money on a aftermarket cooling kit.  With modern CPUs you can almost always use the stock heatsink that comes with the unit.  This is, of course, asuming you buy a boxed (retail) unit, and not an OEM.  Now it may have been true for some of the previous generation of CPUs, that cooling was something to put a fair bit of thought into, but modern CPUs don't generate a dangerous amount of heat.  On top of that, the heatsinks that come with retail CPUs tend to have a putty like thermal compound already on them so that all you have to do it apply the heatsink; very easy for a first time build.  Of course if you wanted a custom heatsink for fun, or for an overclocking project there are two things to consider.  Is the heatsink solid copper?  Copper is an excelent heat conductor.  How much surface area does it have?  The more fins on a heatsink, the more surface area, which is good - very good.  Anyway, if buy a case with a small exhaust vent on the side for your processor, you'll find that it almost always lines up with your Intell CPU.  I'm asuming Intel still standarizes the location of the CPU socket on their boards, if I remember correctly they always did.  

Anyway, I think I've started rambling, hope that helped.

### Post by myballisround on 2007-07-30
I'm no expert in custom building PC and it's also my first time so I would also like some helps. However, from what I have learned so far, the first step in building a PC is decide what you're going to do with it and pick the right CPU.

Once you have the CPU of your choice (through researching and asking for helps) you will then need to pick the right Video Card to go with it. Once you have both CPU and Video Card, the next logical step is choosing the right Motherboard and then RAM. That's it!

Everything else will fall in place afterward.

step 1: Pick CPU
you sure don't want to pick motherboard first and then find out later that it doesn't support the CPU of your choice.

step 2: Pick the right Video Card

step 3: Pick the right motherboard
the right motherboard is ones that support the CPU and Video card of your choice.

step 4: Pick RAMS
step 5: Pick the right case for step 3.
...
last step: Power Supply
Power Supply is last because you need to know how much electricity your motherboard, cpu, video is taking up so you can pick the right PS. 

Those steps to me are very logical.
