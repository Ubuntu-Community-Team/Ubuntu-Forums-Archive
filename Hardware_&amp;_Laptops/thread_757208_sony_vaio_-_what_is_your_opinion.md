I've owned a few Vaios over the years (I've currently got a TX650P sitting around somehwere). They're pretty well built and have some nice features, but I find them overpriced. Sony seems to think that sticking their name on a product justifies multiplying the price by 1.5.

Also, be aware that, under Linux, many of those fancy features won't work very well (if at all), since Sony loves to use little proprietary software programs to run everything. So, for example, all the "Instant On" media buttons that enable my Vaio's MP3 player, DVD drive, and photo album without fully booting the machine, just don't work under Linux. Neither does the built-in memory stick port. Or the WWAN cellular modem. You get the idea.

My advice is to look at other name-brand machines in the same price bracket. You'll probably get a better CPU, hard drive, and display for the same money, since you won't be paying the Sony-tax.

- Intel CPU
- Intel graphics chip
- Intel WiFi chip

Those laptops should work tip top with Linux. Same with the HP Pavilion consumer models.

Most of the HP/Compaq laptops with AMD CPU's also tend to have those pesky hard-to-get-working Broadcom WiFi chipsets (due to Intel and AMD being competitors it should be obvious that one will not encounter Intel WiFi chipsets in systems with an AMD CPU ...). So if possible make sure it says "Intel" everywhere and you should be fine and not have any problems.

I personally also once owned a Sony VAIO ... It broke down one day after the warranty ended and Sony's support was a bad joke. I ended up getting charged money for nothing. So I can't really recommend Sony laptops ...
