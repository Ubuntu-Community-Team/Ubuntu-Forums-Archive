### Post by Bartender on 2007-03-24
First, let's make sure.  Turn the PC off.  Stick something fairly soft and non-conductive, like a small piece of wood dowel, in thru the vent opening and between the blades so that it stops the fan.  Wait a minute, the fan may be mounted on the bottom of the PSU, inside the case.  If that's where it is, open the case and do the same thing to immobilize the fan.  Start it up. 
Still making the noise?  If not, pull out the dowel, and see if the noise comes back.  It won't hurt to run the PC for a few minutes without the fan, especially if you have a room fan you can set up on the table to blow into the open PC.

I'll tell you what I would do, then you can go from there.  If I was sure it was the fan, I'd unplug the PC, get it out on the workbench, follow basic static electricity protocols (wood floor is better than carpet, hold onto the case with one had while working with the other, etc.) and remove the PSU from the case.  You should be able to set it up on a book or block of wood outside the PC without having to remove all the wires.  Inspect it and make sure that the problem isn't simply a bent grille touching the blades.  If you want you can start it up again with the PSU setting just outside the case.  

If it is the fan, I'd turn it all off again and carefully remove the power cables from the devices inside the PC (take notes or digital pictures or label each cable so you can put it back as it was) and completely remove the PSU.

Then, I'd let the PSU sit for a day to discharge the capacitors inside.  Then I'd open it up, unscrew the 4 screws attaching the fan, disconnect the fan, and find a good quality, quiet, replacement fan.  That part is hard to describe exactly - your PSU fan might only have two wires, black and red, or it might have three wires.  
If it has 3 wires, the yellow is a signal wire for fan RPM.  If your PSU fan only has two wires it can be replaced with a 3-wire fan.  Just connect black-to-black and red-to-red and tape off the yellow wire.  
The fan wires may be connected with plug or soldered in.  You may have to do some soldering, or pop the little tiny connectors out of the plastic plug, or simply cut and splice the wires.
Also, measuring the fan can be confusing because there are so many sizes.  Generally, it ought to be a 120 mm, 90, or 80, measured along one side, not across.  The thickness is pretty standard too but you should also measure that and compare to possible replacements  

SPCR is a good website for oodles of information about fans, power supplies, do-it-yourself projects, etc.

Or you can just buy a replacement PSU.  Depending on the age of the PC, whether you plan on keeping it for a while, your level of comfort or discomfort with the above described procedure, etc. that may be the best way to go.  Don't scrimp on a replacement!  Most of the PSU's for sale are junk.  Again, SPCR is a great resource.
