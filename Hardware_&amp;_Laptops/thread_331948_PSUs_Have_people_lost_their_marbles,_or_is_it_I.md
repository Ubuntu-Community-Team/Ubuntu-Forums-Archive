### Post by Jussi Kukkonen on 2007-01-05
There are some explanations to this lunacy. First, PSUs tended to offer lower performance than they advertized, especially the small form factor ones. Second, many PSUs still seem to not function correctly when they are working closer to "full steam". This can be a problem with delicate hardware like TV tuner cards -- strange, unreproducable problems suddenly disappear when the PSU is changed (or the load on the current PSU is lowered)...

That said, I have a 350W PSU powering my media box and it's still probably totally overkill... I suggest going for quality instead of quantity on this one.

### Post by Cope57 on 2007-01-05
The Power Supply
Although many PC cases are sold with a pre-installed power supply, make sure it has enough to run your system. 
Here's a quick guide listing the power requirements of various system components:

Case and Power Supply

Component .......................................Approximate Wattage Required

Motherboard..................................... 15 - 30
Low-end CPU.................................... 20 - 50
Mid to High-End CPU....................... 40 - 100
RAM .................................................... 7 per 128 MB
PCI Add-in Card................................. 5
Low to Mid-Range Graphics............ 20 - 60
High-End Graphics ........................... 60 - 100
SATA Hard Drive................................ 10 - 30
Optical Drives.................................... 10 - 25

It is always best to have about 50 -100 watts more than your hardware requirements just to be safe. When you need to add more ram, you will not need to upgrade the power supply right away.
So do the math for yourself. Depending on your needs, you may/may not need to purchase a high-output power supply for your PC.

### Post by RandomJoe on 2007-01-05
> **jmpmjmpm said:**
> yawn yawn yawn, anything over 400w is made for the airhead gamers out there that *think* they are experts,
Or people who cram a Whole Lot of Goodies in their systems.  In my case, I upped my PSUs for the RAID arrays.  One system has six HDDs, and two CD/DVD drives.

That said, 2KW is far far more than even that setup needs.

> To add between 50 and 100 watts, so average that at 75w "to be safe" is just plain wasteful (in the uk, for a pc running 24/7 that would add around 5 pounds a month to your electricity. 
Actually, it won't add to your bill at all and may even reduce it.  The wattage of the PSU only tells you what its _peak_ power capability is.  Now, going way oversize like the 2KW monster may introduce certain inefficiencies that burn a little more power, and you are certainly paying up-front costs for the beefier hardware, but even doubling the PSU size isn't going to raise the utility bill - the unit will just be running at a lower percentage of total capacity.

Most important for energy conservation, though, is to get a unit that has a good [power factor]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_Factor").  I have replaced my older Dell PSUs because they seriously stank at this.  Most were doing good to hit a 0.75 power factor and some were considerably lower.  Unfortunately the worst are my laptop PSUs - don't have a replacement for those, and they're down around 0.45!  I shudder to think how much juice an office full of computers is wasting because of this...  Good PFC-corrected supplies will get the power factor up around 0.98-0.99 when under load.

### Post by PigCorpse on 2007-01-06
It's much more important to have a PSU from a reputable company than to have a PSU with a high wattage rating. Many times, the 12v line (the most important one) has very few amps, and the majority of the amperage is on the 3.3v and 5v lines, which aren't as important.

Good PSU makers:

FSP (or Sparkle, if the model number begins with FSP)
Enermax
PC Power & Cooling
Seasonic

I think those are the big ones, but there are others:

Antec
Thermaltake
Ultra
Hiper

There are definitely more, but I'm not much of an expert so I'll let my forum buddies (from [H]ard|Forum) give you more info:
