### Post by wallijonn on 2004-12-07
If you are going to build your own PC you first should look at all the mobos reviews. You should then do a google search for the name of the motherboard and 'forum'. At these forums you will most likely read about all the problems being encountered, what memory part works best with that mobo, what problems people are having with the disk subsystem, etc. You can get caught up with the overclockers group but you should only focus on which motherboard is 100% reliable.

In my case I built my machine with only one consideration in mind - 100% reliability and 100% stablility. As a computer technician my mind leans towards workstation and server reliability. My days of lapping cpus and heatsinks are over, my days of overclocking are over, my days of constantly installing the latest drivers and the latest software are over. I do not believe in using low quality parts - like cheap power supplies and inexpensive memory modules. 

One therefore builds their own PC not to save money but to ensure a level of quality excellence, one that will present the least amount of future problems. Or one builds a PC with function in mind, whether it be CAD / CAM, multimedia, programming, training, server, gaming, etc. 

In my case I use W98SE, W2KP, WXPP and Linux. If a motherboard is known to have Linux issues (by virtue of the chipsets incorporated therein) then I will not give it any consideration. If a motherboard is not listed at [www.crucial.com](www.crucial.com) or [www.kingston.com](www.kingston.com) I am not likey to consider purchasing it.

### Post by RayG on 2004-12-08
I would like to reinforce astoltz's experience. I run Ubuntu on a Biostar nforce2-based motherboard, using an AMD XP2000 CPU. The onboard sound was detected and configured without problems. USB hotplug works well too. In all, it was a relatively painless installation. I am not connected to a network, so I cannot report on how well the integrated network controller works. But running 'dmesg' shows that the system found the hardware. I **did** run into a rather intractable case of the "windows won't boot after installing GRUB" problem and I had to reinstall Win2k from a saved image. I do not know if that can be attributed to the motherboard's IDE controller or my hard disk. 

As far as all of the opinions go on this processor vs. that one is concerned, unless you are doing some high-end gaming, any modern processor, even the budget models, will be good enough for 99% of common tasks. On the low end of the spectrum AMD processors seem to be a few dollars cheaper than comparable Celerons. In day-to-day use the overall performance difference between Celerons/Semprons/XP's will probably be small. However, I have read several times that newer Celeron D processors are quite a bit faster in some applications than the older, but comparably priced Celeron-C's. If I were buying a Celeron, I would go with a Celeron-D.

I also run a Duron 1.8 GHz on a really cheap (Syntax-brand @ $9.99) VIA KT266-based motherboard, booting Win2K and Slackware. It's a decent performer too, but a little slower than the XP2000/nforce2 in most respects. I might as well add that I run a Pentium-III 500 on a generic motherboard based on an Intel 440BX chipset as well. I have tried numerous Linux versions on that one too without any issues. Though it is feels a bit slow sometimes, I don't complain about that one either. That may put some of my comments into perspective, I guess. :-)

Other than sticking with well established brand names in motherboards and cpu's, just buy what's on sale and be happy. 

Having said that, I would add that building a computer is a bit anti-climactic. It's little more than screwing parts into place and plugging cards and cables in. One last thing, shop carefully for a ***quiet*** processor heatsink/fan. You can save $15 by buying a really cheap one (I did.), but your computer will sound like a turbojet-powered vacuum cleaner taxiing for takeoff. Budget $25 for it with a clear conscience.

Have fun whatever you do.
RayG

Overall it just feels like something completely thrown together - and it is, because no one outside Nvidia's ivory towers can support it and legacy support in linux is the least of their concerns.

My next system will be intel. Until AMD convinces a chipset supplier (even if they have to do it themselves) to provide an acceptable level of hardware AND document the COMPLETE chipset (that means high quality accelerated GPU and audio and networking - the whole ball of wax) I'm going to be sending my money to the ONE (and only) CPU and chipset supplier that does. 

I have heard that line "if we don't send a message to the people like nvidia and ati that people use linux they'll never support us" for way too long. It doesn't require "support" for a hardware vendor to embrace linux - the whole point is we are able to do it ourselves. What linux (and OSS as a community) requires is that they properly document their damn chips and provide that information to us, the people who would be giving them our money. 

Period. 

I don't care if AMD comes out with an eight core 16GHz CPU for twenty bucks - if the only motherboard that will accept said CPU is a closed box, locked away in "good ol boy" licensing agreements and NDAs, it is useless to me and to anyone else in the linux community who might otherwise provide software support. 

My next box will have something like a 965 chipset and the fastest intel CPU I can get for $200. And if things keep going like they are at IBM, my next system after that may well be completely wintel-tech free. The rest of the world can send all the "economic messages" to Nvidia and S3 and ATi y'all like, but I won't be playing that sucker's bet again. You might want to consider whether you want to be standing in that betting line as well.

The SiS also has a dedicated DSP for audio, but it's documented well enough that OSS drivers can be had.

If you go with an AMD the best SUPPORTABLE option you have at present is the relatively mundane SiS chipset.  and even that is a grey market option because the fellow who wrote those drivers did so by violating the NDA after obtaining technical specs from SiS (iow you're not likely to find them - ever - in a commercial distribution... or supported by one).

The other manufacturers barely even provide lip service to the Free community and are likely to never provide adequate (and reasonable) documentation on their products. At least with an integrated intel chipset you have the option of using their solution or not, meaning you can try an ATi or Nvidia or whatever but then yank it out and send it back to them in pieces when you finally sicken of their lack of support. With an AMD, all the available options - from both a supportable perspective and the perspective of Freedom - utterly suck. 

Without a properly documented and well performing comprehensive chipset, AMD remains an evil bargain: slightly lower dollar cost, but you sacrifice your Freedom in the exchange. Unless you're willing to live with ten year old Matrox cards and equally old motherboard chipsets, all AMD roads lead back to Microsoft.
