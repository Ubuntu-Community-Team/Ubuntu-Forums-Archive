I'd like to comment that the interface is not what determines how fast your hard drive is. Your hard drive is limited by seek time and RPMs. I don't remember the exact numbers any more, but I've written a paper on hard drives. You won't find any significant increase with similar drives on SATA and IDE. I do recommend the SATA for other reasons, however.

### Post by StandardBlueCaboose on 2008-03-02
If you've got a quad-core running at 3.2 GHz, that means that all four cores run at 3.2 GHz. If you're going to be doing some really intense gaming, quad core might be worth it. But it's not just the price of the processor. It's the price of the processor, the processor-compatible motherboard, and the motherboard compatible RAM. The only advice that I can give you is get the best that you can within your spending limits.

From what I know about Ubuntu utilizing the cores (not much) Ubuntu will use both cores to their potential, but not four. I'm getting this from what I remember reading in a forum a few months ago, there's a good possibility that's changed by now. I hope somebody else can supplement or revise that statement for you.

I am currently writing to you on a 64-bit Kubuntu. It does everything I would want to do in a 32-bit environment. I have Firefox with flash, emulators for several consoles, compiz fusion, and all the programs I need. I didn't have any trouble setting it up, myself. I'd say that there have been definite improvements.

7,200 RPM is pretty much the standard for decent hard drives these days. If you want a fast SATA drive, your best bet is a WD Raptor 10,000 RPM drive. They're a bit more pricey, but I don't know of any faster drives meant for PCs. I'd recommend getting one of those for your system files, and another more sizable drive if you'll be needing a lot of storage. 

What hard drives are the best? I've never had a problem with a Seagate drive, but the Raptor is faster. A good seek time is generally < 8ms. A Raptor has about a 4.5 ms seek time, which is impressive.
