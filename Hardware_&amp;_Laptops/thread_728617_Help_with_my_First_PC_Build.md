### Post by xyphur on 2008-03-19
The power supply is not overkill... an uncle of mine has an 850w unit, mainly because of his graphics card being a power hungry hog when he's gaming, and while it does gobble up power, his machine is the definition of stable.

...at 610w you're well within reason while still being assured of a rock-solid PC from a stability standpoint, plus a PC Power & Cooling unit is one that you can depend on for a long time to come. If there's one component in a computer that should be considered the absolute most important above all else, it's the power supply. A shitty PSU will cause you nothing but headaches.

Zalman is crap. Thermaltake is crap. Generally speaking, If it's funky-looking, it's probably crap. In my experience, the fan bearings quit in no time at all on most of the gimmicky cooling solutions. I recommend Alpha heatsinks and either Sunon, Delta or Nidec fans. Not sure of the availability of an Alpha unit for that platform though... I'm still running a socket-A myself ;)

Like Red Shift said though, generally speaking that machine shouldn't give you any issues. Have fun with your 1st, it's an experience you never forget. :)

### Post by xyphur on 2008-03-19
Alpha make the actual heatsink... no fan is included when you buy an Alpha product. This is why I suggested those three fan manufacturers. Thermaltake are crap, like I said above. The bearings will wear in in no time flat and they'll start making that horrible grinding noise.

If you're looking for a flashy fan, then buy the crap brands because that's what they're all about... flashing lights, UV reactivity, etc.

If you want a dependable product that looks exactly how it should based on what it's purpose is (a cooling fan, not a UFO), then get yourself a Nidec Beta-V family fan. Their bearings are high-quality precision parts that are made to last. Both Sunon and Delta also make very good cooling fans. As far as I am aware, IBM uses Delta exclusively in at least their eServer xSeries machines. I have two old Compaq servers, one a Prosignia and the other a Proliant, both extremely old (pentium II days), and both of them had Delta fans that I salvaged from them for other machines. They still work and sound like new.

Heh, after reading thru the above, you might think I'd be associated with these companies... lol    I assure you I am not, I am just an avid overclocker and system builder and I like to think that I know my cooling products quite well.

### Post by xyphur on 2008-03-22
Red Shift: I commend your pointing out of the rough power comsumption values for the various components in said machine, but there is one fundamental flaw in your understanding of the wattage a power supply is rated at. Let me attempt to explain it a bit: A 610w PSU doesn't draw all 610w regardless of connected components... i.e: in this case, as your list clearly states, the nominal power consumption of the machine in question is on the order of about 225w, and given that said machine is being powered by a supply rated at 610w continuous power output at 40°C, it is operating at a duty cycle of approx 45 percent, give or take. This is a good thing, as the power supply will last longer not having to provide all the output that it is capable of. For example, given that this machine draws around 225w, if you replaced the 610w supply with a 250w supply, you'd be running that supply at about 95 percent duty cycle, putting alot more stress on it, which will shorten it's lifespan significantly. Additionally, having the extra wattage allows for future upgrades without worry of maxing out one's power supply.

Another point to mention which builds off the above explanation somewhat, is that even if you had a 1500w power supply (hypothetical), the machine's power consumption would still only be 225w, but the power supply itself would be running nowhere near it's rated output, increasing it's expected lifespan considerably. This provides some sense of reliability. Also, since you're not running it anywhere near it's maxium design output, the heat being generated isn't as high, and assuming the supply in question has temperature-dependant cooling, the fans on the more powerful supply won't be running as fast, thus making it much quieter.

Case in point: I have a Celeron 400 machine running m0n0wall as my router/firewall, and it's currently being powered by a 180w FlexATX power supply. At idle, it only draws 15w of power (no hard drive, no optical, no fans, nothing but a compactflash card and a few NICs. Even at bootup where CPU usage goes briefly to 100%, the machine still only draws 22w. This from a power supply rated at 180w. Also as I mentioned above, I chose a supply with a higher wattage because it was intended for a device that I would prefer being absolutely whisper quiet. Since the supplies I was choosing from all had thermal-dependant cooling, I chose the one that would never see it's rated maximum output, thereby making certain that the fan inside only ever has to dissipate, at most, 22w worth of heat (assuming 100% efficiency of the supply)

Hope that helps clear up power supply rated wattages a bit... and sorry for the novel :P

### Post by Red Shift on 2008-03-22
I complement you on you novel, but power supplies are designed to run most efficiently within a specific load range.  Case temperature and operating costs increase when operating outside that range because less power is delivered to the components per unit drawn at the wall outlet.

Here are links to a few PSU efficiency curves:
[http://www.codinghorror.com/blog/images/power-supply-efficiency-comparison.png](http://www.codinghorror.com/blog/images/power-supply-efficiency-comparison.png)

[http://www.corsair.com/_images/charts/tx750w_efficiency.jpg](http://www.corsair.com/_images/charts/tx750w_efficiency.jpg)

[http://img96.imageshack.us/img96/7291/clipboard08zi7.gif](http://img96.imageshack.us/img96/7291/clipboard08zi7.gif)

Using the 610 W supply in the proposed computer is equivalent to driving a Corvette at 20 mph.  The car will not last longer because it's designed to drive 50 mph and fuel economy will decrease at 20 mph compared to at 50 mph because the engine is not operating optimally.

To the OP, find a ~400 W PSU with 80%+ efficiency below 250 W and with the connectors, voltages, and amps you need.
