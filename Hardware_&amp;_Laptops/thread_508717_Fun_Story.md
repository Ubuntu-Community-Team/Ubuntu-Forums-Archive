### Post by ryangoff on 2007-07-24
I bought a Sony Vaio desktop a while back. A while back meaning it was at the height of "legal" and free mp3 sharing. I installed my first ATI video card and Soundblaster sound card. I had Napster. I had Kazaa. I let my virus / spy-ware protection lapse. I surfed my fair share of porn sites. 

Needless to say, "good practice" was not part of my computing vocabulary, and I got as a result, the slowest, most virus riddled box known to man.

Flash forward to the present day, and it sits in my semi-finished basement. I would start the computer up, wait a half hour, and then open up Windown Media Player, and turned it into my personal basement jutebox (the video card came with a remote control for volume, pause, and track skip, so it was actually pretty fabulous).

After hearing all about this mysterious thing called Ubuntu, I read up on it a little and decided to wipe the virus-riddled Sony clean, and throw on Ubuntu. This is where I really started to see where my hardware issues were.

I had a four slot USB card in the back that never worked with XP. For whatever reason it only worked for Windows pre-XP. Ubuntu recognized it immediately.

My bass and center channel on my speakers slowly kicked the bed with XP. It never worked at all with Ubuntu until I took the entire sound card out, and reinstalled Ubuntu. The internal sound card works just fine anyway for my purposes.

There was a whole card for a phone line in the back of the computer. I decided I didn't need it, and all was good.

Lastly, I took a stick of RAM out, and reinstalled. I think I fried it in the process, but I'm not quite sure.

After all this, I noticed that every time I started up Ubuntu, it would go to the Ubuntu splash screen, and load up. After the bar got to the max, the screen would go black and the whole system would hang. A multiple power off/power on sequences netted the same result.

Lost at what to do, I gave it a swift kick to the case just below the power button. Not hard, but just enough to show it who the boss was. I powered it up, and it went straight to the login screen.

I found that process to work *every* time.
1. Kick to front of case (or motherboard side of case, I later found).
2. Push power button.
3. Ubuntu starts.

*Awesome!*
