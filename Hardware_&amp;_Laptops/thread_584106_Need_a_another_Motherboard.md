I have used Intel and they are the most reliable for now.

a) The motherboard actually depends on the processor you intend on using, for example, if the processor has a LGA775 pin layout, then you need an LGA775 compatible mainboard to use it.

b) From the Intel side, you should not purchase a Prescott processor, it is rather powerful, but it also is a resource hog and it also heats up a lot.

c) AMD is cheaper, but I also have heard that AMD is better than Intel on some counts, so it would be worth it to have a look at AMD. But I like Intel better, had good times and is faithful to me.

d) There are certain processors(Both Intel and AMD have them) that do, Intel has the Prescott, don't know what AMD had/has.

2) Be careful about the HDD, CD-ROM and RAM you buy.

For example, my 200Gb Samsung SATA already has some bad sectors whereas my 80Gb SATA WD doesn't. The brands I recommend for HDD's are Western Digital(WD) and Seagate.

For RAM, I have always used Kingston and they have always lasted, except in a few cases. So I recommend Kingston for RAM though even Corsair does a good job:).

For a CD-ROM, I recommend a Plextor, I have used two Sony DVD writers for two years and each loses CD read/write support after one year.

b) Could you elaborate on "unusual things"?

### Post by afeasfaerw23231233 on 2007-11-01
for unusual thing:

you should have a look on your power supply unit to see if there are any burst or bulged cap. if there are, either replace the bad caps or buy a new psu. psu caused problem on my two old boxes and after i replaced it , it worked perfected.
you should be aware that your PIII psu may not be suitable for a P4 or amd equavelent cpu board since they draw more power. there is also a additional 12v power socket on a P4 era motherboard. check if your psu have this. P4 cpu draws much more power than PIII cpu. your psu should be at least 300W with 75% effeciency (i think) a 150 W surely won't work!

you can have a look of this graph [http://images.tomshardware.com/2004/12/21/the_mother_of_all_cpu_charts/cpu_history_big.gif](http://images.tomshardware.com/2004/12/21/the_mother_of_all_cpu_charts/cpu_history_big.gif) notice the power dis. column.

if your are going to buy a used board, check if there's any bad caps on the board. usually the seller would replace those are obviously bad before he put it for sale. there's a page describes bad caps of pc on wikipedia: [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor_plague](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor_plague)
mind if there is bad brand caps on the motherboard
i bought a PIII used motherboard and it still works fine now (all bad caps are recapped to sanyo)
other things were mentioned well by PmDematagoda

most of the randomly freeze up is due to bad cap. i have six experience of them, two on psu and four on motherboard (iwill, gigabyte and asus)
