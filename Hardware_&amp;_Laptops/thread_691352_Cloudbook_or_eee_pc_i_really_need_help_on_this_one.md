My review of my 2 Asus Eees:

4G-701:
[INDENT]Pros:[/INDENT]
[LIST=1]
[*]Looks great, draws lots of "wow cool", and girls think its "cute"
[*]The stock OS boots VERY fast, 15 seconds, and is very stable.
[*]Nearly no moving parts, only the cpu fan (not counting hinges and buttons).
[*]Very Durable, reasonable expectation of surviving a fall from things like coffee tables with little or no damage.
[/LIST]
[INDENT]Cons:[/INDENT]
[LIST=1]
[*]Small HDD
[*]MMC-SDHC slot has suspend/resume issues making SD cards a less than attractive system partition supplement
[*]Ubuntu and the Asus ACPI are only partially functional, biggest issue listed above in #2
[*]Hard to single hand
[*]Underclocked to 630mhz, must hack to get the full speed from the 900mhz cpu.
[*]Overpriced for what you get.
[/LIST]

2G-Surf
[INDENT]Don't pay more than $100 for this thing, RAM can NOT be upgraded, HDD barely holds the stock OS so forget about Ubuntu unless your going to boot from a USB or MMC device. I regret the purchase of the 2G-Surf and would not do it again, nor recommend it to anyone at its current price. If however it came down to $75~100, then it would be great as a email browser/couch surfer/internet radio/mp3 player. Final word, its an overpriced internet device, wait for them to get dumped on ebay for cheap.[/INDENT]

Don't get me completely wrong, I do like the 4G-701, I just think it should ship with binary software support, and that some work should be done to allow use of the MMC slot as a system drive if desired (and it is desired). For $399 the cloudbook looks like the best bargain. ### Post by teh_chris on 2008-03-12
as a moderate eee fanboy, here is my 2 cents:

i have had a 4G surf for 3 months now and i love it, but it's not perfect.  it's super handy, but i can't imagine using it for my main computer.

i am using the default easymode desktop and it's pretty cool.  it boots up super fast and the tools i use most on it (firefox) are easy to get to.  i might load eeeXubuntu on it after this semester is over so i can load network tools and stuff on it, but the ease of the wifi app has me pretty spoiled.

software problems aside, there are some physical design issues that take some getting used to.

if you have big hands, the keyboard takes about an hour to get used to, and even then you will need to type at about half your normal typing speed to maintain accuracy.  once you have mastered typing on a EEE keyboard, your typing on a regular keyboard will improve dramatically.  if you have hand problems like CTS, forget typing on a EEE.

my wife hates the EEE keyboard, so i held off on getting one for her, but if the cloudbook keyboard is bigger than the EEE's,  i might get one for her since she will need to run windows and the internal harddrive will be a plus.  it looks like the cloudbook has a bigger keyboard, but i haven't seen the two side by side in a photo to know for sure.

you need to see the eee in real life to appreciate it's size.  calling it small is the understatement of a lifetime.  with the lid closed,  it's roughly the size of a nintendo wii, but significantly thinner.  using it will warp your perception of laptops.  after a week of using my EEE, my wife's dell seems to have swelled up to a ridiculous size.  when i use her laptop, i feel like i am typing on a large pizza or some sort of giant oversized clown prop.

because they are so new and such a small size, finding a laptop sleeve or bag for either machine will be difficult.  i ended up getting a fabrix case made for my EEE which set me back about 50USD.  i carry the EEE in the fabrix case, and the fabrix case in the accessory pocket of my backpack so i don't have to worry about putting my text books on top of it.

i came very close to getting a wii carrying case for it, but decided against it.  the wii is more recognizable than the eee, and here in cincinnati wii's are still kind of hard to come by.  i figured someone might steal my bag thinking there was a wii inside it.  plus the pockets on wii carrying cases are useless for pretty much anything but wii accessories.

regardless of what machine you like, before you buy *anything* go see it in a store and type a few hundred lines in a text editor.  you seriously need to see one in real life to appreciate just how small it is.  these micro-laptops are fine for emailing and surfing, taking notes in class, and the EEE is awesome for watching xvids on, but i wouldn't dream of writing code or gaming on something so small.  you need a full-sized laptop or desktop for that.

so, if this is going to be your primary computer, don't get a EEE or a cloudbook, save your money and buy a cheap desktop.  think of the UMPC as a landing craft, it's great for traveling, but the serious stuff gets done on the mothership.

if you have a desktop or a laptop already, then go to a store and visit the EEE and the cloudbook and test drive both of them, but keep in mind that your impression will improve with about an hour's use.
