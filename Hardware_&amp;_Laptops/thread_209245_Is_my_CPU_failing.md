### Post by rmsoldato on 2006-07-04
I'll try to make this as short as possible.  

I like to encode home movies to dvds on my laptop computer.  For the past 2 years I have been able to do this without any problems under windows.  However, just recently (past 2 months) my laptop has frozen with a blank, dark screen halfway through the encoding process.  The only thing I can do is hold the power button until the PC shuts down and then boot up again.  I thought that perhaps it was a windows problem, and I've always wanted to make a change to Linux (liked Ubuntu out of all the flavors best); so I installed Ubuntu and looked for encoding apps.  Last night I started an encode and it happened again.  Part way through the process the laptop froze with a blank screen and I had to do the same routine to reboot.  So now I'm sure it's an OS independent problem.  My concern is that this is a sign that my CPU is starting to die, now that it seems that CPU intensive apps cause it to lock up after only an hour or so.  Should I be concerned?  Is there some way to remedy this?  I've tried to run the apps under different priority settings, and have experimented with different encoding apps, but all with the same result: the PC locks up.  My laptop is a Clevo running a Celeron 2Ghz processor with 1Gig of RAM.  All opinions are very much appreciated.  

ps. This is my first posting, so please forgive if I'm posting to the wrong forum.

-The Soldato

### Post by flarkit on 2006-07-05
Cleaning the heatsink is a very very good thing to do. Replacing the thermal paste between the CPU and heatsink could also help, but if you're nervous, perhaps take it to a qualified technician.

Also, if you have a LiveCD, I'd suggest running the memtest application, for at least 8-12 hours (overnight, perhaps). Those symptoms could indicate that your RAM could be failing (perhaps also as a result of overheating).

Good luck!

### Post by rmsoldato on 2006-07-05
Wow.  I have to be honest, I'm in awe at how quickly people have responded with helps and suggestions, especially considering that for most of you it is probably the middle of the night.  Anyway, I looked up how to clean a heat sink, and one quick fix that someone suggested was to simply put the end of a home, hose type vacuum against the fan vent to suck the dust out.  I did that for a few minutes (with the PC off of course) and then tried another test encode.  NO PROBLEMS this time.  Woohoo.  I was getting myself ready to have to buy a new laptop if I needed to, but ironically, vacuuming the dust out just saved me almost $1000 USD on a new system.  Thank you guys so much.  And the ultimate outcome -- I'm now happily running Ubuntu instead of Windows.  I don't feel any need at all to go back to windows.  I was tentative at first, but now it's 'buntu all the way.

### Post by flarkit on 2006-07-05
Awesome! It's great to hear that your problem seems fixed.

Just be very very careful with the vacuum-cleaner idea. There were reports that people fried their CPU or motherboard due to static discharge, after trying that idea. It's best to completely remove the heatsink for cleaning. Never ever try to clean the fins by wiping manually either, since this could crack the CPU.

### Post by cracker on 2006-07-05
If you use a vacuum, be very careful not to touch any of the chips or anything metal. If you need to clean better than that, use compressed air instead. It has no risk of electrostatic discharge.

### Post by ekarni on 2006-07-05
I suggest you grab one of those "canned air" bottles and use that to dislodge the dust.  If you're not familiar, this is an aerosol can filled with compressed gas and a fine nozzle.  Gives a nice focused squirt of reasonably high pressure gas to blow the dust away.  Most computer / office supply stores carry them (for that matter, anybody else that carries computer supplies has them too).
