### Post by Krydahl on 2008-01-01
1)  You can buy OEM CPU's without a heatsink, but most come with a heatsink. This heatsink should be fine if you're not planning to overclock. If you are, you might want to upgrade to a larger custom job. If you use the standard supplied heatsink, it comes with a thermal pad. If you buy your own you will need to get some paste. I use Arctic Silver.

2) Depends very much on what you're planning to put in the new machine and what the old power supply is. Truth is, most systems don't use nearly as much power as a modern PSU puts out - but if you're planning to put lots of drives and a high powered graphics card in there, you may well need to. Do you know the wattage of your existing PSU?

3) Sorry - can't help with this one. Start reading reviews.

### Post by angryfirelord on 2008-01-01
-If you buy the CPU retail (in a box), then you can use the provided heatsink and fan. There's a pad on the bottom of the heatsink, so don't need paste.

-If you buy OEM or use a different heatsink, then you may need to use paste. Just remember to use a very small amount. Too much paste will make it worse.

-As for the power supply, yes you generally need to upgrade that, but it's much harder to do so when you buy your PC from a manufacturer. With Dell, it's impossible because they use custom parts. So make sure that you can upgrade it first. Personally,  I would just buy a new case. If you don't want to pay for a case & a PSU (shipping tends to add up), then I would go for an Antec case as they make reliable pre-installed PSUs.

-For the motherboard, just read the reviews on newegg and you should see a trend. Intel makes extremely reliable motherboards and they should work on Linux with no issues, but they cost a little more. Just look and see, it can be a hit or miss kind of thing.

### Post by kyphi on 2008-01-01
That sounds like fun - building a new machine.

You do not state what your present power supply is.  The minimum I would consider is 350W.  The more peripherals you want to run the more power you will need.  If you do have to buy a new power supply please be aware that they are not all created equal - meaning that a PS 350W for $50 is not the same as a PS 350W for $80.

Heatsinks including a cooling fan come matched to CPUs and thermal paste is included.

You do not say if you are going to use PATA or SATA drives for both hard drives and optical drives.  PATA and SATA drives work through separate controllers.

If you are going to use a mix of PATA and SATA drives avoid the Intel 965 motherboards - the 965 is OK if using SATA only.

Intel Core 2 Duo - good choice.

A couple general nits about modern motherboards and PSUs:

1.  4 SATA connectors and 1 IDE connector is really not enough.  It would be fine if you could daisy chain multiple SATA drives on one SATA connector, but that's not common practice (I'm not even sure if it's in spec).  Some motherboards don't even have the 1 IDE connector.  Older boards had 2 IDE connectors and since you could chain 2 drives off each connector you commonly had 4 drives connected to the motherboard.  Just as much as you have now with 4 SATA connectors.

2.  The gaggle of wires originating from the PSU is ridiculous.  Unless you are using every single one, it just takes up place in the computer and impedes air flow.
  A much better way to do things is have the PSU have standardized connectors directly off it's back and come with an assortment of wires that fit the connectors on the PSU.  It has two benefits: First is that air flow will improve, making the system more "green".  The second is that it opens up a new market for the manufacturers to sell additional cabling for those that mod the system.

1.  The number of SATA connectors varies according to the mother board.  Mine is an Intel DG965WH which has 6.  Currently used for 2 SATA II HDDs and 2 SATA optical drives, 2 spares.  PATA is not in use.

2.  Reorganising the power cables sounds like a good idea - worth communicating to PSU manufacturers.  In the meantime, you can use plastic cable ties to keep the cables neat and hook them to the top of the computer casing.  That will keep them out of the way so as not to impede airflow.

I am not aware that on board graphics can be disabled in the BIOS.
