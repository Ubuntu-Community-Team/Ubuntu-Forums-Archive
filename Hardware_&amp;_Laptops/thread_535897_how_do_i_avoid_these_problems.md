### Post by Dave Crowhurst on 2007-08-27
I have been looking around to find a suitable machine to run Ubuntu on. The obvious choice is to buy a computer pre-installed with Ubuntu, such as [System 76]("http://www.system76.com/") or [Dell]("http://www1.euro.dell.com/content/topics/topic.aspx/emea/segments/gen/client/en/ubuntu_landing?c=uk&cs=ukdhs1&l=en&s=dhs"). 

Laptop vs Desktop. It really depends on your trade off between upgradeability vs mobility. With a laptop what you buy is what you are stuck with (okay I know some folks will open them up and fit new HD's etc but i'm talking about the average user here) whereas with a desktop it is much easier to add a second HD or replace the graphics card, network card etc. 

The advantage of a Macbook Pro is you can set up (in theory) a triple boot system OSX/Ubuntu/Windows. I know someone who has one just running OSX and he loves it. Apple hardware design is great (i'm typing this on an iBook) but you certainly pay for it. 

Sit down and be realistic about exactly what you need your new computer to do. It is easy to buy a more powerful machine than you really need, or to skimp a bit too much and regret it later on.

### Post by Dave Crowhurst on 2007-08-27
> **dnbvibe said:**
> well the reason i was gonna go with macbookpro is im gonna start going to school for graphic design and my school recomended a mac and the macprobook seems it has good specs and is cheaper and more mobile than the macpro

What software are you going to need to run for school? 
If you are not going to need a laptop then the new iMac may be a better option for you (though I know some graphic design professionals are unhappy with the glossy screens). 

If you need this computer for school it is worth taking into consideration how much time you will have free to get Ubuntu working. If you just want something that works reliably out of the box, and runs all the software that you need for school, then the mac (Macbook Pro or iMac) could be the best option for you at the moment. 

Compiz-Fusion is an add-on for Ubuntu GUI that gives you some nifty effects such as wobbly windows etc. You can see a [demo here]("http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5zkvu2daUnU&mode=related&search=").

[The Gimp ]("http://www.gimp.org/") is the GNU Image Manipulation Program. It is a freely distributed piece of software for such tasks as photo retouching, image composition and image authoring.

If you do choose to go with Ubuntu then you might find [Ubuntu Studio]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/UbuntuStudio") useful.

That depends :)

A new mac comes bundled with [iLife 08]("http://www.apple.com/uk/ilife/") which is a suite of apps designed for home users to allow them to create there own multimedia projects (photos, movies, music and websites). iMovie was emasculated in its latest incarnation, but as an iLife 08 user you can download iMove 06 for free. 

There are Ubuntu open source equivalents to all these apps. The [Multimedia & Video]("http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=138") forum, and [Multimedia Production]("http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=254") forum are probably a good starting point for choosing the best alternatives. 

Apple do not seem to want to support the open source community. They, to my knowledge, have done nothing to develop a version of iTunes for Linux.
