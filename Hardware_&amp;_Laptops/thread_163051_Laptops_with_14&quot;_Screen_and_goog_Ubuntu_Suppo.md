### Post by Miguel on 2006-04-20
*Disclaimer:*I'm in love with the asus W3

_A few technical comments:_[list]
[*] The Sony has an integrated video card. I know that the others have ATi, and that the usefullness of a good CPU is limited in linux.
[*] The ThinkPad is the ligtest and probably the strudiest but...
[*] The thinkpad isn't widescreen
[*] The best GPu is the asus'... but it is an ATI
[*] The Sony and the ASUS weigh the same (the ASUS does weigh 2.5Kg with CD-ROM and the standard battery, not 2)... but the Battery is larger in the ASUS and it has a dedicated video card. OTOH, the Sony is cheaper.[/list]

A few reviews:
[Sony Vaio FJ](http://laptopmag.com/Review/Sony-VAIO-VGN-FJ180-PR.htm)
[ASUS W3V](http://www.notebookreview.com/default.asp?newsID=2552)
[ThinkPad T60](http://www.notebookreview.com/default.asp?newsID=2864&Review=Lenovo+ThinkPad+T60p)

Please note that all FJ Vaios are practically identical, with only parts inside changing. Also note that the T60 review is the heavier 15inch-FireCL CPU - 9cell battery model, while yours is 14'', X-300 and 6 cell. This should be approx 1 pound ligher than the 6 pounds that user reports.

Last technical point: The ASUS model coexists with the W3F, which is the same model with a Core Duo and a X1600 graphics card (that now works in linux with latest ati drivers)

_Ubuntu comments_:

First, the stereotypical *"you will get better hardware support with the next release (Dapper)"*. This is especially true in the case of X1000 GPUs and intel core duos, which weren't available at the time Hoary was released.

Second, as Jeremy said, Intel HDA is a painful hit and miss. In some laptops it works perfectly out of the box. In some others, you simply change a few settings in System->Preferences->Sound (or volume) and it works like a charm. But, there exist a few renegade  HDA implementations that will make you suffer. There was a RealTek implementation on a particularly successful acer that created a very long thread.

Third, the ThinkPad is probably the most linux-friendly. At least that's been the case with ThinkPads for a long time. Maybe some arcane features don't work (like, let's say fingerprint reader, I don't know), but in general IBM hardware has been pretty well supported. 

You also have Jeremy's report about his Asus not giving him too much trouble. You can probably PM him so you know exactly what works, what doesn't and what is half-baked. About the Sony, I'd grab the latest Dapper live CD (flight 6 or beta) and go to a store and try to boot the live CD. You know, you are spendind some 1000 pounds on a laptop and want to know if it works ;)

Anyway, you can look [here](http://www.gagme.com/greg/linux/vaiofj-fc4.php) what works and what doesn't of a Vaio FJ-170 using Fedora Core 4.
