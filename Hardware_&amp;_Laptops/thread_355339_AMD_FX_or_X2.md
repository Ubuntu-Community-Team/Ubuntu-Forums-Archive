### Post by steefjeqv on 2007-02-12
Hi omghax,

Any decent motherboard will manage a mild overclock. Which brand & type do you use ?

Most important is the ability to raise your HTT (Hypertransport). Standard is 200 mhz.

Processor speed is determined by its  (locked) multiplier and HTT.

1800 mhz =  multiplier  9 x 200 mhz
2000 mhz = multiplier 10 x 200 mhz
2200 mhz = multiplier 11 x 200 mhz

Raising this HTT will speed up everything on your PC. Memory, PCI-slots, AGP,.... .

So before you start with this you need to :
- lock your PCI/AGP frequency to 33/66 Mhz (PCIe is 100 Mhz)
- run your memory with a divider (166 Mhz in stead of 200 Mhz)
- lower the HTT multiplier from 5 to 4 (800 Mhz in stead of 1000 Mhz)
All this can be done with your BIOS settings.

Now you can start raising your HTT from 200 to 210. Leave it like that for a few days.
After a few days raise it with another 10 Mhz. And so on.

There's also the possibility to raise the voltage (vcore) of your processor, but generally there's no need to do this. When you do raise the voltage dual cores tend to generate more heat.

It's not so difficult to get all of this done.
If you really don't want any hassle you can still consider buying the Athlon X2.

One other thing, when you overclock, the Cool 'n Quiet function will not work anymore.
The processor will always run at the overclocked speed. There are workarounds (solutions) for this but I've never tried these.

Greetings,
Steven

### Post by leech on 2007-02-13
I've always had issues with Cool N' Quiet anyhow.  Not so much in Linux (it works fine there) but certain games in Windows will have serious issues.  Though if you don't dual-boot, no worries there.

Really the only thing Cool N' Quiet is for is if you have a loud fan, and it generates less heat.  What he means by Cool N' Quiet not working if you overclock, is that your CPU will stay at the clock speeds that you have set, rather than clocking down when you don't need the CPU power.

A good example is for Laptops, you don't want your processor running at full speed 100% of the time because it drains the battery and causes more heat.  Desktop systems don't matter as much if you take this off, unless of course you have extremely loud fans in your case, in which case it's easier to just get quieter fans.

I should try overclocking my CPU again, I only have a AMD 3200+ Single core.  

And yes, L2 cache is basically where it will cache the instructions for the CPU itself.  Think of it as the same as your web browser cache, where it caches the files of the websites that you visit more often so that you're not always downloading the same information over and over.  The L2 Cache works the same way, but with as many instructions a modern CPU uses, it's best to have more L2 Cache, so it doesn't have to swap out as much.

### Post by steefjeqv on 2007-02-13
Hi omghax,

Changes are instant.

You can easily check your processor speed by using Gdesklets.
Gdesklets are applets which come with all kinds of functions.
Some of them give you a weather report, others are an RSS feed and so on.
There's also one who reads your processor speed. That's the one you need.

You can easily install them :
Click on "applications" - Click on "install/remove software"-Click on "Accessories"-Install Gdesklets.

After installation you can find them in the same menu as your calculator, terminal, text editor.....

There are different categories, choose system/cpu.
Just doubleclick one of them (I use the sidecandy version) and you should see your processor speed.
