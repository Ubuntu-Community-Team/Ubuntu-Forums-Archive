### Post by frith on 2007-09-13
There are so many factors to consider when looking at a laptop, even if you narrow it down to a tablet...

I have a toshiba m200, and I think its pretty great. I wish it were smaller and lighter sometimes, but it has a nice screen, a nice processor and its pretty cheap these days (being last generation). My mum has an Acer travelmate from a couple years ago that is less than 1kg and I think is totally awesome - 900MHz tho and 800x600, so its not playing any games...

The term you should be googling is 'hybrid laptop' - the ones with the 'twist' screen are called hybrids (cf slate). Personally I prefer the slate form - most everyone will have a usb keyboard and mouse for using in the office, so why bother carrying around that extra weight and space?

But as for your second point. handwriting recognition of differential equations. Yeah, good luck with that. Windows is pretty well known as being the leader of handwriting recognition at the moment, and linux might catch up, but it will be a while. And if you are expecting *any* OS to make sense of partial differentials or calculus of any kind, then you have seen some amazing software I have never heard of. I find its doing well to read words - and in that case it can easily match my scratch against not only characters but a known dictionary.

Just writing the equations won't be a problem, but if you want to render them into any kind of machine-readable form, you are in for some serious pain. I would suggest the following alternatives:

Notetaking: Just leave it in a graphical form - you can read it, you don't need the computer to know what it says...
Wordprocessing/Typesetting: Get intimate with LaTeX. This is one of those rare pieces of software which started on unixen and is now better on windows (eg MikTeX and TeXnicCenter are very well featured and easy to use)
Numerical modelling/processing: Here you will be using some kind of scripting language anyway, so you will need to just key it in. Octave is similar to MatLAB and free, there are many others...

In my case (toshiba m200) it was a pain to get the install started, but once I got it going its been pretty sweet. Its a well known laptop with an nVidia graphics card, so there is lots of info and other people have solved most of the problems. So I guess my main advice would be to get a popular laptop. Oh and check it agains the following:
[https://wiki.ubuntu.com/LaptopTestingTeam](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/LaptopTestingTeam)

Frith.
