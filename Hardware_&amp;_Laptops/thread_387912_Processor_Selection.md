### Post by chewearn on 2007-03-19
> **le_vainqueur said:**
> I'm planning on upgrading my computer (actually probably buying a barebones system), and I've heard all over the place that I should get a dual core processor.  I don't understand, however, why this is better than any other dual core processor such as a Pentium D.  
Numerous benchmarks have shown that the new intel Core2Duo is much faster than existing dual core processors, including the best AMD X2.  At the same time, the C2D consumes significantly less power.  You should check out the regular tech sites for comparisons.

> I've also heard that when comparing a dual core processor to a regular processor, one should multiply the speed of the dual core by 1.5 to get a realistic comparison between the speeds of the two.  Does this seem accurate?I don't think you can accurately compare; it depends on the application you are running, or the test you are doing.  Ideally, 2 cores = 1 cores x 2, but real world situation depends on processors load and OS.  I guess, the 1.5 is simply a rule-of-thumb.

I recently helped a friend purchased a barebone PC.  The general impression from the shopping trip:
Core2Duo systems are generally more expensive than AMD X2.  Note that this apply only to many of the barebone systems we saw.  Mainly, the reason is Intel systems has integrated Intel chipsets, which we were worried might be trouble to setup for Linux.  The alternatve is to buy additional PCI-E video card, which of course means additional $.  I think I saw a post recently about workarounds to setup nvidia card on system with integrated Intel video onboard; don't know the success rate on that.

When we were comparing older Intel (Pentium 4) to AMD X2, we felt the AMD wins hands down; for the same price you get much more in performance.

In the end, we got an AMD X2 system, with nvidia nForce4 GeForce6150 integrated.  And spend slightly more on 2GB RAM (!)

One thing to note, my friend is not a regular gamer, so 3D performance is not crucial, though he wanted to be able to install the 3D beryl goodness.  In the end, Ubuntu works 100% out-of box without any tweaking.
