### Post by tgalati4 on 2007-07-13
That's a myth.  Although I do know that Thinkpad batteries do self-destruct in 2 years whether they are used or not.

The procedure that I use with a powerbook is to use 2 batteries and I rotate them every couple of months.  I also use a program (xbattery or coconut) to log the date and battery capacity.

The thing that kills the batteries is heat.  Leaving them in AC runs the CPU in high-performance mode and allows the computer to run hotter all the time.  This cooks the battery.  By putting one in storage for a couple of months, you stretch out its life and you have a semi-charged backup that you can take with you when you travel.  That will get you through a 6-hour flight.

Most Thinkpads will run on AC power without a battery.  You can pull the battery after it's partially charged (say 50%) and put it in a drawer.  Make sure you have an UPS to plug your power supply into because any power glitch will lock your system.

With the battery-swapping procedure, I have managed to get 200 to 300 cycles with 85% capacity with over 2 years of use.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2007-07-13
The charging process heats the battery.  So charging to 100% and using the computer on A/C does maximize the heat load.  On the powerbook, the charge circuit turns off and the battery slowly drains to ~95% before the charger kicks in again.  This reduces the "constant-charging" problem.  I don't know how the Thinkpad charging circuit works, but most lithium-ion batteries have a sophisticated circuit to control the charge rate.

The problem with leaving the charge state at 40% for a long period of time is that the battery looses its maximum capacity.  So there are several factors to consider.  Having a backup battery is handy and it lowers the cycle count on each battery and allows an overall longer cycle life in my experience.
