### Post by TheVeech on 2007-02-03
Why spend a fortune to add gaming functionality to a PC?  I share the view of many that people should leave gaming to gaming machines, like the PlayStation or the Wii ;)

If you can do that, instead of looking for cutting edge hardware, get over to ebay and look for a bargain.  My laptop died last weekend.  I started looking for something new and got a bad case of upgrade fever.  I was on the verge of spending a grand.  But why?  I wouldn't use and don't need the power, so I suppose it was just the thought of getting something new that led to me getting carried away (i.e. you might as well get 'x' component, then 'y' component, because it's only 'z' pounds more, etc., etc. and you're getting a whole new machine, so it's 'worth it'.  Before you know it, the only computer game you're really playing is the Microsoft upgrade game).

So in the end I spent a lot less in buying something cheap and cheerful - a Dell - and swapped over the better components from my old machine (RAM, HDD, WiFi card).  Linux installed like a dream (first on the included HDD, and even easier on my old one).  There's a couple of very minor things I need to configure, but there's no rush.

Just take a break from the new machine sites, or they'll give you a headache, and have a look at ebay (cross-referencing the model with these forums and other sites to check for problems).  Doing it this way, you still get a buzz from getting something else, as opposed to something new, and you don't waste cash on power you might not need.  What's more, you're not scared of using a knife on your new machine to get rid of those stickers.

Whatever you decide, let us know what you do and if everything works okay.

### Post by TheVeech on 2007-02-04
The good news is that you can do pretty much all of what you want to do with a fairly basic machine (though, again, I'm not knowledgeable about Beryl, so you'll have to ask a specific question on the forums about this and have a look around for other people's experiences).  This means that, like me, you were going spend a lot to get a new machine with power that you wouldn't have needed for everyday things for a couple of years anyway.

It might be an idea to get a laptop with even less that you're specifying here, because I found it cheaper to just upgrade some parts of a basic machine with my old bits, whereas if I'd bought a machine that already has high specs in the areas I wanted, I'd have paid a lot.  Some machines are easier to upgrade than others - check also the amount of spare parts going for a particular model - this will make things easier.  The laptop I got hold of came with a 30gb ATA HDD which was big enough, but too slow, so I just stuck my 100gb ATA in it, which is a lot faster.  You can pick up a disk off ebay, too.

Similarly, make sure the laptop has wireless (but not through a PCMCIA card) because it'll have an aerial.  Mine came with a pretty poor Dell mini-PCI card, so I just replaced it with my Gigabyte one (using the madwifi driver and wpa supplicant).  Setting up a connection and the recommended option of WPA was pretty easy with the right information.  IIRC, the Intel 3945 is mini-PCIe, which is a different size - an older laptop will almost certainly have mini-PCI.  Intel cards are supposed to be well-supported, and Linux is often very forgiving of the older hardware you might get.

Then I just used my RAM to double it to a gig (even though System Monitor shows that I'm not using more than 400MB when I'm pushing the machine).  The C640 has got two easily-accessible slots for RAM and, luckily, the seller had installed the 512 in one slot, instead of using 2x256 chips.

If I went for a laptop with a 100gb HDD, 1 gig of ram, and a decent wifi card, I'd have to spend over the odds for the other bits that go with a machine like that which I wouldn't need.  One thing to pay particular attention to is that the machine uses USB2, not USB 1 if you're going to use USB for a digital camera, mp3 player, and so on.

As for setting up machines with Ubuntu, I've only ever had big problems setting up 64-bit machines, or when I've used a cheap screwdriver.  When installing, just make sure you're near another machine with a net connection to look up solutions.

### Post by TheVeech on 2007-02-13
This worked for me: [Enable WPA in Ubuntu]("http://www.debianadmin.com/enable-wpa-wireless-access-point-in-ubuntu-linux.html")

I don't know what your wifi card is like, but this should work.

As for Beryl, I can't think why it's broken.  I used it for a bit, but then went back to basics...eye candy's nice for a while, but I thought it got in the way of DOING STUFF!

There's also no need to disable security in the router.  When you're setting it up, do it wired, note all the settings, and you should be okay.  Basics you should cover are (off the top of my head):

1) Restrict wireless access to only known machines
2) Turn off broadcasting your SSID
3) Use a good WPA key
4) Change your router's password - again, to a good one

Any additions to this list are more than welcome.
