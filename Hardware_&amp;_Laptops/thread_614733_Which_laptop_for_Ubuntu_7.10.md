### Post by Mondoman on 2007-11-16
I'm using an Asus F3JP dual-boot Vista and Ubuntu and love it so far.  One key is to get decent graphics hardware, not just the builtin Intel stuff.  nVidia is better Ubuntu-wise, but I've been able to get my ATI mobility x1700 to work well with a bit of searching.  Gutsy's Compiz Fusion sure beats the pants off Vista and XP, especially for a laptop where really usable extra workspaces are a major boon.

This is my first Asus laptop, and I'm impressed.  No dead/stuck pixels, 2-year warranty, and it doesn't feel like shortcuts were taken.  As of September, Asus even includes a breakage/spills/etc warranty for free.  Watch out for current el cheapo laptops, though.  I wanted to buy a low-end Compaq, and all 3 in stock at my local store had one or more pixels stuck *on*, not to mention dead pixels.  I think with the recent shortage of laptop components, LCD panels with slight defects are being allowed through even on major brands.

### Post by CPNowell on 2007-11-16
I have an Acer Aspire 5602WLMi (UK model) Laptop and I must admit that since v6.06 of Ubuntu (& Kubuntu actually), I was beginning to give up on this flavour of Linux but then along came the Gutsy Gibbon!!

Using a spare hard drive so that I could do a complete virgin install, it went on so smoothly and I haven't looked back to be honest. With v7.10 of Ubuntu, hardware wise, anything I sling at it just works!

All the built-in hardware of this laptop just worked straight away without having to "mess about" with anything really. This includes:-

1. The built-in T.I. 5-in-1 Multimedia Card Reader (SD/MMC/MS/MS PRO/xD)
2. All devices hanging off of the Intel Mobile 945 chipset. (Wireless & Built-in network interfaces, all USB ports and hubs, Acer Broadcom BlueTooth Module)
3. The WXGA screen worked perfectly at 1280 x 800 straight after install. Even the login screen is the correct aspect ratio.
4. All the built-in sound functionality just works.
5. The built-in Acer branded Logitech Orbicam comes up great.

Everything I have plugged into my laptop so far has simply worked (in some cases with the help of 3rd party applications). For example, after I had installed the Vodafone Mobile Connect Card Driver for Linux (v1.0), I have been able to plug in both my T-Mobile GTFusion+ PC Card and my USB Huawei E220 HSPDA Modem and they both just connect and work (with my T-Mobile Web'n'Walk SIM).

In fact, the only thing I haven't tried yet is the built-in dialup modem and that is because I will probably never need it! I stopped "doing" dialup years ago anyway!

This is the best hardware support from a Linux package I've experienced yet.

Colin :)
