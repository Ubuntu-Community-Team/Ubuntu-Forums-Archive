### Post by SimonJW on 2006-08-24
I'm sorry to say that, whilst I'm sure the community **can** make a driver for pretty much any printer if they put their minds to it, they can't make a driver for every printer in existance. 

Until manufacturers start to recognise Linux as a valid platform on which it makes financial sense to support their printers, we are relying on the generosity of many volunteers, and the money of one or two company, to create drivers for hundreds and thousands of pieces of hardware without any information as to how they work inside.

Unfortunately, all the available information seems to suggest no-one has worked on this particular printer yet.

The only advice I can offer you, and I appreciate it is coming too late, is that before you make such as drastic move as changing your computer's operating system, you should check out whether your hardware will be supported.

Hopefully in the future, when you may be considering purchasing a new printer, check out the resources at [http://wiki.ubuntu.com/](http://wiki.ubuntu.com/) and other easily google-able sites, and you might be surprised to find that the printer you want will work well, and you can give Ubuntu another try :)

### Post by az on 2006-08-26
It's a chicken and egg thing.

Linux needs marketshare for hardware vendors/manufacturers to release specs or code for free-libre open-source drivers.  

Half-way is not good.  Lexmark released a bunch of drivers a few years back.  They were binary-only.  If you are running Red-Hat from five years ago, your printer may work.  Since it is binary-only, the driver can not stay current.

If the company released something the community can work with, they would not have to maintain it - someone would be able to maintain the driver and it would work with a modern linux system.

Anyway, linux and other free-libre OSes are gaining marketshare and the problem will eventually go away.
