### Post by w4ett on 2007-08-23
Well.....The long and short of it is that most QUALITY PSUs do have a fuse, however it is soldered in.  Unless you are comfortable with a soldering iron, I usually don't recommend PSU repair to the uninitiated.  (IOW, beware the label, no user serviceable parts inside :)  )  This is primarily due to the fact that the Filtering Capacitors retain a HUGE DANGEROUS Electrical charge in them, and can do so for a period of months or even years.

As far as replacement of the unit, purchase one of the highest quality, highest wattage units you can afford.  The higher rated units will run cooler and more efficiently than a cheaper, lower quality ones and will have a longer life cycle.

As far as the motherboard and ancilliary parts goes, only testing them with a new PSU will reveal any faults.

Hope this answers your questions.

### Post by ramjet_1953 on 2007-09-04
I'm afraid that turning the power selector switch from 230V to 110V will do more than blow a fuse.

If the bang that you described was very loud, I would suggest that one or more of the electrolytic filter capacitors has blown. I have seen them literally blow a hole in a circuit board.

As far as damage to your system is concerned, you will have to wait and see, until you install the new power supply.

Be prepared for a no-go, I'm afraid to say.

### Post by svtfmook on 2007-09-04
i'm sure the damage is onyl done to the psu.  i've shorted out many psu's.  most of which made loud popping noises, puffs of smoke, etc.

just replace it.  they can be had for less than $20.

i would suggest the coolmax 400w, it's cheap and reliable.  if you want cheap and proven stable, get a hyper 530w psu.  one of the highest rated tier 2 psu's available for under $60.

but first, find out if you have a micro-tower or something like that and make sure you are not using something other than a standard atx psu.  if you are using something other than a standard atx psu, then you need to find what will fit your case.  also double check your motherboard and find out if it's 20 or 20+4 pin power, and whether or not your cpu is 4,6 or 8 pin power.  then buy your psu accordingly.
