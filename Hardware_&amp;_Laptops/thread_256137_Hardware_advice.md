### Post by em3raldxiii on 2006-09-28
I'll throw my 2¢ in cuz I go through a LOT of hardware.

First off, I am going to recommend you use the AM2 socket if you are going with an AMD processor.  They are more future proof and support a few features that the socket 939 does not.  I am a big fan of ASUS parts (because I've never had one fail, but have had other brands fail).  However, I can vouch for the general reliability of Gigabyte and Microstar (MSI) as well.  For performace + features you might consider DFI, but I'm not 100% sold on them 'cuz they may have a few compatability issues. 

As far as video goes, I would definitely stick with the nVidia based video chipset because ATI + Linux = headaches.  As far as brand goes, again I am a sucker for ASUS, but Leadtek makes some of the best, Gigabyte & MSI are old standards, BFG is decent.  I can't say anything about XFX because I have never used one.

Okay, so here's a breakdown of what *I* would recommend:

Kingwin SK-523BKW ATX 19IN Case Black
Enermax Liberty ELT400AWT Modular Cable Power Supply
ASUS M2N-SLI Deluxe ATX AM2 Nforce 570
AMD Athlon 64 X2 3800+ Dual Core Processor Socket AM2 Windsor 2.0GHZ
Corsair Value Select PC2-5300 2X1GB DDR2-667 (x2 for your 4GB of RAM)
Seagate Barracuda 7200.10 320GB SATA2 3GB/S 7200RPM 16MB Cache
ASUS GeForce 7600 GT 560MHZ 256MB 128BIT 1.4GHZ GDDR3 PCI-E Dual DVI-I HDTV Out Video Card
Liteon SHM-165H6S DVD+RW 16X8X16 DVD-RW 16X6X16 Lightscribe (black to match)

Now, points to consider:  The motherboard I picked is fairly high-end, but not over-the-top.  Good solid popular workhorse.  The processor is the least-expensive X2 processor that I would recommend.  For an additional $40 CAD, you could go for the EE (energy efficient) model on the CPU and cut your heat production and energy draw.  The video card is pretty decent, but again not over-the-top.  You would likely be happy with a 7300 which is quite a bit less expensive though.  The hard drive I chose is good value for your money, and it's got a low latency (less than 5 ms) and a large cache for smooth HD access.  Corsair RAM is the only RAM I buy nowadays.  I have NEVER had a dead/bad stick of Corsair, but have wasted a lot of time mucking with other brands (most notably Samsung and Kingston).  Strangely, Samsung makes the memory chips that go on SO many other brands ... I think they just make a crappy PCB.  The power supply I recommend is Enermax (super highly respected), and my personal favorite.  You could go with a higher wattage model if you are going to plug in a lot of USB-driven devices (such as scanners that don't have their own power).

The bottom line for the machine above (not including keyboard 'n stuff) is $1,600 Canadian.  That'd probably be around 1,300 USD.  The biggest extraneous cost is the huge amount of RAM.  If you knocked it down to 2GB you'd be down to $1300 Canadian or 1000 USD.
