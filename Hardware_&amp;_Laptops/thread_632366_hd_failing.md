### Post by Herman on 2007-12-05
Generally, a new hard disk is worth having. Chances are, the other parts of the computer will still be okay for a while, but that will depend on your luck, how well you have taken care of the computer and the quality that was built into it.

Even if some other part of the computer does fail, a hard drive is about the most interchangeable of all computer parts, and can still be used in another computer, or placed in an external USB enclosure where it will be very useful.

In most laptops, the hard disk drive only takes two minutes to change, you just open a hatch underneath and slide the old hard drive out, slide the new one in and your done, as easy as changing a light bulb. At most you might need a tiny screwdriver to remove one or two screws.   :)

Some computers are very simple and quick to work on by design, and others very time consuming to do even the simplest maintenance on.
I haven't worked on a  4yo 12" Powerbook G4, but I hope it is a little easier to work on than the 1998 PowerBook G3 Series (WallStreet) I looked at. 

It helps a lot if you have the right tools, know-how and skills to do jobs like that yourself. 
I would bet that 99% of old computers that are scrapped and sent to the garbage dump could easily have been fixed and last for years if the owners could fix them themselves. The charges for time of a professional technician is the biggest reason why it might not be not viable to repair the machine.

I fix old computers for friends as a hobby that aren't economically practical to bother paid technicians with. The PowerBook G3 Series (WallStreet) I looked at required quite a bit more skill to do anything with than any other machine I have ever looked inside. I hope your  Powerbook G4 will be better designed than its predecessor.  If we had been able to purchase the parts we would still have been able to fix the old PowerBook G3 Series though. 

Regards, Herman :)

### Post by Herman on 2007-12-05
Well, it looks like there has been some design improvements between the PowerBook G3 Series (WallStreet) and your Powerbook G4.
All I wanted to do to the G3 was replace the BIOS battery so the clock would keep working and it could boot and remember it's settings. It turned out that the BIOS battery in an older Mac is called an 'PRAM' battery and it's the very last part you get to after you completely dismantle every bit of the whole machine. In most computers the BIOS battery is one of the easiest thing to reach. Unfortunatley, in the G3, both it and the main laptop battery are special parts which we didn't know where to get for a reasonable price as we would have had to make our own replacement parts, so it wasn't worth fixing that one.

The P4, on the other hand, looks like it wouldn't be very much more difficult to work on than most other laptops. You do need to remove the whole bottom cover first, but then changing the hard disk is easy. Here is a link, [Powerbook G4 Hard Drive Upgrade Guide]("http://www.xlr8yourmac.com/powerbook_g4/PB_G4_HD_upgrade/pb_g4_hd_upgrade.html").

As long as you are aware of what you are paying your $100 or $129 labour for, that's fine.
Some people would be wise to pay the money and let someone else deal with any technical work, while others would consider it a 2 minute job and would save their money by doing it themselves. It's a matter of knowing your own capabilities.

Regards, Herman :)
