Here is a short summary by the author Isidor Buchmann is the founder and CEO of Cadex Electronics Inc., in Vancouver BC:
> Simple Guidelines

Avoid frequent full discharges because this puts additional strain on the battery. Several partial discharges with frequent recharges are better for lithium-ion than one deep one. Recharging a partially charged lithium-ion does not cause harm because there is no memory. (In this respect, lithium-ion differs from nickel-based batteries.) Short battery life in a laptop is mainly cause by heat rather than charge / discharge patterns.


Batteries with fuel gauge (laptops) should be calibrated by applying a deliberate full discharge once every 30 charges. Running the pack down in the equipment does this. If ignored, the fuel gauge will become increasingly less accurate and in some cases cut off the device prematurely. 


Keep the lithium-ion battery cool. Avoid a hot car. For prolonged storage, keep the battery at a 40% charge level. 


Consider removing the battery from a laptop when running on fixed power. (Some laptop manufacturers are concerned about dust and moisture accumulating inside the battery casing.)


Avoid purchasing spare lithium-ion batteries for later use. Observe manufacturing dates. Do not buy old stock, even if sold at clearance prices. 


If you have a spare lithium-ion battery, use one to the fullest and keep the other cool by placing it in the refrigerator. Do not freeze the battery. For best results, store the battery at 40% state-of-charge.

### Post by noigeaR on 2005-12-20
[QUOTE=xyz]Avoid purchasing spare lithium-ion batteries for later use. Observe manufacturing dates. Do not buy old stock, even if sold at clearance prices.[/QUOTE]

this is actually something most people don't know about lithium ion batteries. the batteries will rapidly lose capacity after about 3 to 4 years due to chemical processes, regardless if they were actually used during that time or not.
so you can't buy yourself a stock of spare batteries to use for your laptop in 3 years when the manufacturer doesn't sell that type of battery anymore. the spare batteries will be dead by about the same time as the ones you actively use in your laptop!
the only thing that can actually help against the aging process is to put the (half empty) batteries in the fridge, but that's rather problematic too because you have to make sure the batteries are never exposed to condensation water while in there, so you would have to put them in a sealed plastic bag or something like that. personaly i think it's just not worth the trouble, but go ahead and try it if you want. just don't come crying if your fridge explodes or something :p 

see the wikipedia entry about lithium ion technology for some more interesting information
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_ion](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_ion)
