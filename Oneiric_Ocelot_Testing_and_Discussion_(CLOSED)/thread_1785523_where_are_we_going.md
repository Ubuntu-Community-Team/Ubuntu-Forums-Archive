### Post by Hedgehog1 on 2011-06-18
I have to agree that there are (at least) 2 groups of Ubuntu users.

There are those who want a secure, simple and (in many cases) free OS.  They may spend 90% of there time surfing the web, and the rest emailing, document processing and such.  This is (in my mind) the 'market' Ubuntu needs to peruse with it default layouts and options.

The second group are users who have very specific needs and mold  *whatever* OS they are using to fit that.  While this second group may also spend a fair amount of time surfing the net; each member of the group sets up their computer in a completely different manner.  As a member of this second group myself, the use of Gnome Shell 2.x, Gnome Shell 3.x, Unity 2D or Unity 3D has little effect on my daily operations.

Now I happen to find that Gnome Shell 3 is a little more intuitive to use, but Unity is ***much faster*** for the ***way I work***.  So while I have both loaded in my 11.10 testing partition, I use Unity almost entirely for my 'production work'.

At my day job, most of my Linux time is spent in 3-4 telnet sessions running side by side on a Windows PC.

At home, I run Win7 in Vbox to remote desktop to my office PC, and the other three work-spaces are for local processing.


It would be foolish/impossible for Ubuntu to try to develop a GUI based on my one-off needs.  Instead they should spend their time pursuing the 'mass market' (as much as there is) of desktop users who are willing to give a Linux Distro a try.

As an example, last night my wife and a young friend of ours were looking at some very silly YouTube videos on a cell phone, realized the screen was too small and invaded my man cave to use the big screen.  I fired up Chromium and handed the wireless keyboard to this youngster (who had never even seen a Linux distro before).  But things were familiar enough that we were watching 'The Gummy Bear Song' in a few seconds and there were dancing to it (and the darn song is still running through my head!).

This young lady and her fellows are where large number of users will come from, not the nerds.  They are using iPhones and Android phones, so windows is taking a back seat as an OS for more and more of them.

Honestly, if Apple released iTunes for Linux we would see a large number of users move from Windows very quickly afterward.

Anyway, my 2 cents...

### Post by seeker5528 on 2011-06-18
> **Jacobonbuntu said:**
>  No OS is good in itself; it is always the combination of user and OS that makes a good combination, or not. The discussion of what should be improved in Unity, no matter if it is about the user interface, the (commercially looking?) software centre or whatever, is a non-discussion without the answer to the question of what kind of user Ubuntu is aiming at for future releases. Exactly this is why all the posts of this forum seem to have their own universe, and there is no good answer, nor a bad one.

IMHO asking what type of user Ubuntu is aiming at is asking the wrong question.

For the default Ubuntu experience the goal is to provide something that has some appeal to the mass market.

It should be easy to get started with, not easy to outgrow, not displaying a bunch of confusing messages and not requiring a bunch of configuration, but providing configuration for the things that matter.

It should not assume people are ignorant or that the people that are ignorant will remain that way so should provide some *basic* range of stuff that is useful for more advanced users.

It should not use fancy utilities that try to coat everything with sugar and crap out rainbows if those utilities don't cooperate with traditional ways of configuring/controlling things without providing information on how to disable the fancy utilities (preferably in a user specific way when that makes sense) so you don't have to spend hours searching to find out what you have to kill, disable, remove, etc... if you need to configure things in a way the fancy utilities don't allow.

I think partly what screws people up when talking about Unity is that Unity is intentionally limited in scope, it doesn't need to be advanced, it just needs to be usable and consistent, whether you are dealing with one or two or three apps/windows on a single desktop or you have several apps with several windows spread across multiple desktops, it shouldn't matter. Limited shouldn't be confused with limiting and usable shouldn't be confused with '*I* need unnecessary feature/option X or *I* won't use it'.

More advance needs can be met with existing/future stuff that can be installed and personally I would expect people that consider themselves to be advanced/power users should be able to find out about these things as they develop/evolve.

Later, Seeker

On the developing a windowing environment for the broad user base part of the equation. As long as communication is there between the developers of the different layers so that what the developers of the upper layers expect from the lower layers is somewhat in line with what the developers of the lower layers are willing and able to provide, things should continue to improve, and outside of that individual developers should be able to focus much of their their development time working on things within their individual areas of expertise, with some secondary effort on some related things that affect them. 

Of course being open source, developers could work on anything, and don't necessarily have to now about some larger set of issues to fix something specific that is an annoyance them in something else they are less familiar with. And similarly new developers wanting to wrap their head around a project often can tackle small specific issues without having to know some larger set of issues.

Life in general is full of unforeseen complications, often if you try to see things in more general ways instead of getting bogged down in details, break a situation down into individual issues or smaller sets of issues depending on complexity, and resist the temptation to over think things, it makes them easier to deal with.
