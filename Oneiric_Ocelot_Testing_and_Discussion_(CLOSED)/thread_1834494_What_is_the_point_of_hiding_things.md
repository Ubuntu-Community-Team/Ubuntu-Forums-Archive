### Post by ErkDemon on 2011-08-27
Usability studies were also used to justify and defend the supposed  superiority of products like Windows Vista and the revised interface of  MS Office 2007. 

What sometimes happens with organisations that are tasked with making  changes to a design but are "creatively blocked", is that they get  usability studies to identify potential weak points in the previous  design, cut or change the supposedly offending parts, have another  usability study, cut out more parts, and keep hacking the thing about in  a sort of design death-spiral, with the thing getting worse and worse  each time. 

It's like that folktale about the guy whose young mistress keeps  plucking out his grey hairs and whose older wife keeps plucking out his  dark hairs, until he ends up bald. 
 
If a design change is genuinely a good idea, there's usually a positive  argument that can be made for making it. If no positive argument is  forthcoming, and the best argument available is that a study indicated a  potential problem in the older design, then your default assumption  should be that the new design is likely to be worse. 

It's tempting to assume that if you have round after round of usability  studies, that once the users finally stop picking out possible things  that could be improved, you've achieved some sort of state of  perfection.  
Actually, it's often more like the end result of a great movie script  that repeatedly gets hacked about by studio focus groups and director  after director until its a piece of ___, or someone with a plastic  surgery addiction, who only stops having things "fixed" when they've  completely ruined their face.  

That's how the interface for Office 2007 got so bad ... it's now an  appalling piece of work, but it's difficult to pick out and report  individual features that could be improved and write them on a  questionaire slip, because the whole thing is now so bewilderingly  inconsistent and alien that no individual feature catches your eye as  being potentially more wrong than the others, any more than any piece of  junk on a city junkyard stands out as needing special attention over  the rest. Ask a test subject what could be improved about a design  that's in Iteration Hell , and they're not going to tell you, "You know  what? This is a disaster. Just tear it up and start again", even if  that's the correct response. A usability study tends not to regard that  as constructive feedback, it'll instead going to ask the subject to find  a little more-specific corner of the design to focus on, and it'll keep  doing that until there are no more identifiable nameable distinctive  features left in the design that are straightforward enough for the test  subject to be able to point out. And at that point, the design is  probably destroyed.  

This is why we used to hire actual user-interface designers to design user interfaces.
