### Post by effenberg0x0 on 2011-09-21
Ubuntu documentation is great and Google is even better, but users are and will always be users. Anyone who's ever worked on IT within large companies knows what I'm talking about. IMHO this is a large portion of why the Forum exist (despite creating the sense of community, spreading knowledge, Open Source awareness, etc). 

The problem I see is that the Forum is in no way integrated with the UI right now. Only Launchpad is.

Imagine this: Something crashes at the user desktop. The "XYZW0123 has crashed" dialog pops-up. What if it had some clear and easy text like "Someone probably already reported this problem at UbuntuForums. There may be a solution a few clicks away! Would you like to see a selection of related threads at UbuntuForums?". [YES]/[NO] buttons right below. Clicking [YES] will open the default browser, search the keywords here, rank with SQL relevance, prefer [SOLVED] threads, etc. It's easily doable in technical terms, but means someone has to make "an executive decision" to promote the Forums to the first support level (over Launchpad).  

Being conservative, don't you guys you agree that, with a system like that, for at least 50% of the cases, general keyword searches would bring the users to real solutions / workarounds? Let's say 40%, even 30%. Wouldn't that be worth a try?

IMHO, that would mean less unnecessary, useless, poorly described bug reports on Launchpad and less unnecessary, useless, repeated threads at UbuntuForums. That could really improve the quality of the Forum and help reduce the overload on Launchpad. Moreoever, Ubuntu would be "teaching users how to fish", meaning it would be investing on educating its users on the ways of using Linux (being capable of searching for information, understanding issues, using forums, etc).

Just my 0,02. 

Regards,
Effenberg

### Post by effenberg0x0 on 2011-09-21
The average PC user (Windows user) don't have the culture of looking online for help because there's nothing to set on the OS. A very small percentage of them is aware that you can customize / fix the system to some small degree by using specific programs, patches, updates or changes to registry. When these user get to Ubuntu, many of them go away quickly as they can't understand the basics, deal with stupid problems, like a non-functioning wlan card, printer, etc.. 

In this sense, the welcome screen seems like a wonderful idea to me. 

I was thinking here that the help features of Ubuntu, such as better integration to online help and a help screen, to be developed somewhere in the future (lets hope), should be developed by help specialists: People that are aware and know more about typical user problems and better help approaches. In Ubuntu community, these people are the forum staff and members / helpers. Developers are more functionality/code-oriented and maybe don't have such a good knowledge on what exactly are the problems faced by users / what's users average capabilities.

Regards,
Effenberg
