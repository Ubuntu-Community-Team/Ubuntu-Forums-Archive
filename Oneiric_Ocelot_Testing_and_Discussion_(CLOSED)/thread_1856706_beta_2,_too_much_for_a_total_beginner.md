### Post by apollothethird on 2011-10-09
I strongly recommend 11.04.  It's highly supported.  The glitches he may encounter will most likely have already been resolved if it's a glitch with the OS or his configuration.  There won't be a need to wonder if it's a known glitch in the beta that will be removed in a couple of days.

Also, the glitch he encounters might just be something he has done that could easily be corrected.  Installing a beta put out for testing might be a sure way to put a bad taste in a new user's mouth concerning Linux.

After a couple of days you can use an easy apt-get commandline to upgrade to 11.10.  At that time he can join the masses of thousands that will be running the same thing that he's running.  He can share his experience with the masses and get better support for glitches or questions he encounters.

I wouldn't suggest waiting.  I think the time to install Ubuntu is now.  But install the latest release, not something that is being tested.

Also, I'd strongly suggest Untiy over Gnome.  This way he'll start out with the default direction of the OS and be less likely to experience the woes of the users complaining about change.  I also suggest installing docky to allow the user to select from a list of running applications rather than having to pull up everything running in a category when bringing background windows into foreground.

I wasn't very vocal in this flaw during the 11.04 phase.  I thought the developers would notice this flaw (because enough others pointed it out), and it would be resolved after Untiy matured.  But it got missed again.

With all the complaints I see about Unity, to me, that's just about the only valid one.  But again, it's resolved on my machine by installing Docky.  I'm sure most of the other complaints could be resolved just as easy by installing a tool to overcome the annoyance.

Unity is nice for a beginner, in that he can have access to just about anything on his computer (or the repository) just by typing one of two letters of what he wants.  Also, it has something resembling the classic Gnome category browse by clicking on the "All Applications" drop-down in the Dash search space.

11.10 (too much for an absolute beginner)
11.04 (ripe for an absolute beginner)

### Post by effenberg0x0 on 2011-10-09
I've really been avoiding saying this because it sometimes end in an unnecessary flame war. I ask next posters to not do this in reply to my opinion and keep the discussion mature. My biggest concern today on the issue of Ubuntu vs new user is this:

**Windows Common Post-Installation Problems*:**
- Wrong resolution (but usable desktop / default browser) - not critical for first use
- No Bluetooth - not critical for first use
- No Sound - not critical for first use
- Printer, scanner, other external devices - not critical for first use

**Linux / Ubuntu Common Post-Installation Problems*:**
- Won't boot (Grub & partitions, kernel parameters, etc)
- Won't go into GUI / stuck at console (no VGA driver, no proper fallback procedure)
- No LAN / WLAN

[I][SIZE=1](*) This is what I see in large implementations (companies with more than 500 PCs). We have statistics about it.
[/SIZE][/I]
The Windows user will generally have a working desktop and NIC. He will run Windows Update and, generally, that will fix things. 

The Linux / Ubuntu newbie will look at black console, have no GUI or no Internet and give up. I've seen logs where people type "help" at the console.... I don't see how someone who has never typed a command in a prompt will be capable of fixing this type of potential critical problems. Look at this forum. How many times do we see people saying they have no GUI, or no launcher, everyday? And sadly, when we see such problems reported in this forum, it means this particular user is already capable of searching for information and going to the right place (which is not true for most newbies). I estimate that these users, that manage to get to a forum, are at most 20% of the failed installs. 80% will give up.

Now don't get me wrong: I am *really* confident this is the best / more friendly and efficient version of Ubuntu so far. Yet, I don't think any IT services company will implement that in clients for, at the very least, more 3 or 4 months. Clients calling everyday because they have no launcher / panel or can't login at lightgdm cost money. Same goes for domestic users. IMHO, the things that were REALLY important, like a proper splash screen with basic explanation on how to use the system, more friendly and integrated offline help, working and rock solid Jockey/Auto-Install drivers properly, reliable GUI fallback, lan/wlan out of the box maximum compatibility, etc, haven't changed much since last release.

I'm talking about statistics for over 100.000 PC sales that resulted in support / return in the last 6 months.
