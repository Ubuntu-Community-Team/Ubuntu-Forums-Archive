### Post by ranch hand on 2011-06-07
Heck, I just thought it was an interesting article on design and the Linux desktop using Unity and Gnome shell as examples.

While I agree with a number of the authors points. I also have used computers that are now built into large agricultural equipment.  Usability in the beginning was terrible as they did not have any "real" users.   Folks majoring in Agricultural Engineering running the machines on Land Grant college fields is not at all the same as Farmer Joe in N. Dakota working on the family farm or ranch in some really weird landscape.

It is lucky that the AE majors are usually related to farmer Joe and wanted to hear what he had to say when the stuff became available.  Now there are less controls, things are more configrable and easier to configure.  Radar sensitivity can be easily too touchy in rough ground and gps needs to be very sensitive there.  The ability to communicate with a laptop plugged in to the onboard system must also be dead easy.  Unfortunately there is no Linux interface that I know of at this point.

I believe that would be a big step forward for that application of technology.  Fancy desktops are not, in the least, needed or wanted in that situation.  This may seem trivial to most folks on this (of any) forum.  It will be if the food supply slows down.

Linux would be a better platform for that, as it is in science, as it is a much better number crunching platform.  GPS control of such things as fertilizers and pesticides over a large field result in lower usage of both and more yield per acre.

There really are folks that are not geeks that actually use computers for other than entertainment and gossip.  I do not think this is limited to the science community.  I know agriculture and some of the computing applications.  The timber industry also uses very similar technology as do shipping companies (ocean and rivers) this makes for less pollution, more profit and things getting where they need to be.

This does require in the desktop environment great flexibility in configuration due to the various physical environments and conditions in which it is used.

Touch screens, for instance are great on the onboard systems on tractors.  They are set before you start moving.  Once you start moving your control interface becomes the keyboard and that is hard enough to handle while jolting along at 3 to maybe (real damned fast) 7 mph across tilled land (or untilled land if working on an environmental reclaimation project).

Designing interfaces for folks that sit on their butts doing next to nothing is, in the short term, a better move.  When those folks start being hungry they will loose interest.  Computer design really should consider the world situation as a whole and realize that the standard desktop needs to be flexible.  This also needs to be standard or the folks that need it can't afford it.  That may be not able to spend a lot of money for some rare interface or perhaps the time to bang something into usable shape.

Usability is in the hands of the user.  The "average" user does not exist except in the idle classes.  These are not the best bunch to, in the long run, be designing for.
