### Post by psykatog on 2010-09-13
A year ago, I decided that I'd had enough of Windows and just dove into Ubuntu (well, Kubuntu) head first with no programming experience.  It's been a learning process, and one that may have been impossible without some googling ability.  There are a wealth of introductions out there, and for the really programming-phobic there's even Linux Mint, but what I have in mind is something a little different.

I want to build a Ubuntu computer (or even just desktop) for a very specialized purpose, and produce a guide for anyone who wants the same.

In a week, I'm going to be ordering a new computer, and I have very specific plans for it.  I'm going to pick up a [convertible netbook]("http://www.engadget.com/2010/04/27/asus-eee-pc-t101mt-review/") and throw in [Pixel Qi's amazing screen]("http://www.pixelqi.com/products") to (essentially) make a superpowered e-reader.  I've lived in 5 cities in the last year and a half, and my book collection just can't keep up with me.  Also, I've recently started graduate school.  So what I want is a powerful e-reader that still allows me a full keyboard so that I can get some word processing done (in the way I'm still not convinced a virtual tablet keyboard could ever let me).  Of course, I'll also be browsing the web plenty, etc etc, but I don't need video editing, sound recording, or many other functions that a standard desktop setup (something akin to out-of-the-box LinuxMint) would.  I do very much need to solve problems like running the kindle for PC app through wine though.  (Btw - I didn't find much on that when I googled, and I made a post about that recently [here]("http://kubuntuforums.net/forums/index.php?topic=3113648.0").)

So perhaps I could name this project "(K)Ubuntu Scholar", write a brief abstract, and then precise step-by-step instructions that someone with no linux experience could step in and follow easily to achieve the same function.  Mine's just one example though - I could easily see a desktop optimized for audio recording/composing (both by installing the right software and doing things like turning off all animations).  

If there's sufficient interest, I think it'd be cool to put together a site/blog/wiki that would act as a Ubuntu recipe book.  I don't have the programming knowledge to really contribute to the Ubuntu project, but I suspect that there's a lot of us out there who enjoy the product and have plenty to say on the subject of "instructions I wish I'd had from the start".
