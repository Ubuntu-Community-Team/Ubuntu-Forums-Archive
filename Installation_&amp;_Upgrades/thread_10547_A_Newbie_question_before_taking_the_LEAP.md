### Post by wallijonn on 2005-01-09
Mic,

I suggest that for the moment you do not backup your hard disk to CDRs and go through the trouble of installing Linux. Just boot from a Live CD and play with that (without writting to your Windows hard disk). 

If you must try Linux, I suggest intalling it on its own disk drive. (I use caddies). This should mitigate Windows data loss and you will have to learn to choose which HD to boot from, either through the BIOS or through a GRUB floppy. 

Likewise SUSE is a little friendlier than Ubuntu to start learning Linux. Not that Ubuntu is hard to learn - it's actually easier in many respects. But the Ubuntu philosophy is that you start with a clean base and add functionality to suit your needs. SUSE starts from the philosophy that they give you all the tools and after trying them all out you get to know which tools you like the best. It's like trying different browsers, there's IE, Netscape, Mozilla, Konqueror, Galeon, Firefox, etc.

Since you are used to Word, notepad, writer, you can now install ABIword and OpenOffice in Windows. You can install them on your Windows system and play around with them before switching over. If you cannot afford to play with Photoshop you can try your hand on The GIMP. Both have a steep learning curve.  

With Linux you'll find that there are some things which are not as easy as Windows, like installing applications. With the Linux world things are laid out differently than in the Windows world, you will get to know Linux internally whether you want to or not. 

You basically have to take inventory of what you want to do with Linux. Do you mainly surf the web, mainly work with media files, like sound or video, do you play mainly games, are you writing a book, do you work with presentations, spread sheets, photography, CAD/CAM, programming, website, etc. ?

If your motherboard has mainly stadard devices then installing Linux shouldn't pose too much of a problem. If however you have WinTel or USB modems, wireless keyboards and mice, certain scanners or printers, USB sound systems, game controllers, etc., then you may have problems getting all of these devices to work in Linux. 

So, yes, Linux demands a little more than Windows. But as a start-up system with some basic tools, Linux comes with much more power out of the box, and it is not as prone to the viruses, trojans, BHOs, browser highjacks, dialers, email relay, worms, the ability to become a zombie or netbot which plague Windows. 

Is Ubuntu for you? Sure; just break into it slowly. Play around with it via the Live CD. Take stock of what your commitment is likely to be. Then make an informed decision gleaned through your experience of actually using it.

### Post by wallijonn on 2005-01-10
Mic,

If you can get your friend to install a caddy system just for the IDE "B" channel (where your disk drive resides), you will save yourself a lot of heartache.

You will need the assistance of your friend.

What needs to be done is:
1] disconnect his hard disk IDE cable.
2] set your Ubuntu Disk in the caddy to "Master"
3] Install Ubuntu. (Make sure everything works correctly).
4] Power down, change the Ubuntu disk to "Slave"
5] reconnect your friend's drive.
6] go into the BIOS and select "boot from drive 'B' first", escape, F10, yes
7] you should now boot into Ubuntu.

If it fails to boot, chances are that you'll need to change /etc/fstab and change 'hda' to hdb'. I doubt it, though.

Otherwise you'll end up having to backup your OS and data, then wiping it, then creating 3 partitions on it, restoring WinOS to the first partition, the 'data' to yopur data partition, and installing Ubuntu **to it's own / partition (boot partition)** so that it doesn't touch the first disk drive's MBR. 

The latter method is more dangerous than the former method. Your Linux savvy friend should have no problem, though.

Another option would be for you to buy an external USB HD drive enclosure, move your Windows OS and data to it, then install Ubuntu to your in-computer HD.

btw, I bought ViPowER VP-10KF-66 caddies for $10 each. You can see them at [www.vipower.com](www.vipower.com) . Do a [www.pricewatch.com](www.pricewatch.com) search for 'mobile rack'.
