### Post by jimbren on 2006-06-13
So, upgrades are painful but fun, right?  They are for me, or at least they were.  

You see all the new bells and whistles, you marvel at how much faster your system is, or the spiffy new look and feel of some aspect of the system.  You also reconfigure all kinds of crap you forgot you had customized, lose data, get a neck ache from slouching too long, and end up wondering in the end if it was worth it, probably while some no brainer piece of hardware gives you fits when it worked just fine pre-upgrade.

Dapper, and finally pulling my head out on few things, has changed all that for me. I downloaded the Kubuntu 6.06 .iso on June 1st like a good little boy, but decided to hold off on installing it for a few days.  Every new OS has some level of breakage that isn't found until right after release, and I wanted to avoid that.  I also wanted to avoid the flood of everyone else doing their upgrades\installations at the same time and bogging down the repositories.  (Installing Breezy on zero day left me with a half configured system as the repositories timed out while everyone else hit them).  

I've been running (k)Ubuntu for I think two years now, maybe a little more, and I love it.  I decided with my first installation that I was going to make the jump completely and not have a Windows partition at all. Previous experience taught me that I got lazy and used Windows by default, ignoring whatever linux installation I had almost completely.  Not going to learn if you're not going to do.  

I've been slow to pick up some best practices with linux, which is stupid and has been a real source of frustration for me.  When I installed Breezy back in October, I didn't have a back up routine, and rather than build one just to tear it down, I copied what I needed to DVD just before I ran the installation.  I lost stuff of course, but not really anything that mattered.  My writing was intact, as were my mp3s.  Various KDE customizations--gone, all gone.  Most of my pictures--gone but recoverable from another machine, albeit a pain in the ***.  My pr0n--gone (brief panic).  Then for some reason, I failed to make a /home partition, despite the fact that I'd gotten into the habit when installing linux on other machines.  

It took me a while to get my system back up to speed, but it was worth it.  I learned a lot along the way, and spent a lot of time creating a back up routing just the way I liked it.

This past Saturday morning, I decided to play around with my 6.06 CD.  I spent about 15 minutes using the live CD, and decided it was time.  After verifying my last back up, I let fly, resigning myself to spending the better part of the weekend (at least) with a not-quite-the-way-I-like-it system.

I spent about 5 minutes answering the installer's basic questions, keyboard, timezone, user, etc, then another 10 minutes or so with the partitioner.  It didn't really take me that long to plug in how the partitions should be divided, but Nightmares from MS Fdisk Past prompted me to double and triple check my settings.  Once satisfied, the installer went to work--really fast.

Less than 25 minutes--more like 23 minutes, I think--after the system starting copying files, I was booting into my shiny new Kubuntu 6.06 installation, complete with Long Term Support.

I ran the system updater right away--now included in K by default--which took maybe 10 minutes from start to finish.

Then I spent a few minutes with [BUMPS]("http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=143"), and I was able to play my mp3s view a DVD without any problem.  

So that's less than an hour from a formatted HDD to a fully functioning system.

After these two minor steps, I was ready to restore my data, which was being backed up weekly to an external USB drive.  This took about 5 hours (more than 7gigs of data), but I left once I kicked it off, lest I waste the day (again) staring at a progress bar.

I returned that evening with a fully functioning, fully configured, fully working just the way I like it system.  

No data lost.  

No time wasted fixing shit that broke.

No pains in my ***.

Everything just worked.

And they say linux isn't ready for the desktop.
