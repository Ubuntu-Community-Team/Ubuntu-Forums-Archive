### Post by aysiu on 2006-11-15
For just about everything except dial-up, you're in luck. There's nothing to order. All the Ubuntu applications are free. You can test out Ubuntu without installing it. Just pop the Desktop CD in your computer and reboot--you'll get a live session that *won't* affect your hard drive but *will* give you a fully functioning Ubuntu session.

The interface is fully point-and-click, but if you're going to be configuring Ubuntu, you may need to copy and paste a few commands to the terminal to get some stuff working. And even if you don't **need** certain commands, people will give them to you anyway, just because it's easier and faster than describing to you what to click.

You can use .doc and .ppt in OpenOffice. In fact, try OpenOffice for Windows and see how you like it. OpenOffice can also export to PDF, and Ubuntu comes with Evince (a PDF viewer), and you can also install a Linux version of Adobe Reader if you want.

I think theoretically Ubuntu does support wireless and dial-up, but not always and even when it does, it can require a bit of setting up. Broadband ethernet is almost always supported out of the box with no extra configuration.

### Post by christhemonkey on 2006-11-16
You can visit:
[http://packages.ubuntu.com/](http://packages.ubuntu.com/)

That has all the software for ubuntu in one place.

Although, when you go to download something, you need to make sure you get all the pacakges it depends on.
Take for example, the package Debian Jr. Card Games (called junior-games-card)

It depends on:
ace-of-penguins
pysol

Which in turn depend on:
libc6
libpng12-0 
libx11-6
zlib1g
python-support
python2.4
python2.4-tk

Which in turn depend on lots of others...

This is why it is much easier to sort out your internet connection, then all these dependencies will be managed for you and taken care of.
