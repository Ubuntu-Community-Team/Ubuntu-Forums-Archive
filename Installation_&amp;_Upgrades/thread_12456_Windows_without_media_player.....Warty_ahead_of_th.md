### Post by celloandy on 2005-01-25
I don't think this will ever really be a problem, because the anti-competative arguments don't really apply to Linux distributions, in general.  Microsoft was attacked because they shipped their own media player in an effort to supplant the media players of other companies, and thereby take control of the media player market.  Most Linux distros come with a variety of media players, almost all of which are made by other companies, groups, or individuals, and which, in general, represent a pretty large portion of the media players available for the Linux operating system.  They do not generally force any one player on users, and furthermore, they don't even care which players users choose, as they don't stand to gain financially or otherwise because users choose one particular player above any others.

Basically, I can't see how any of this could be construed to be anti-competative in the sense that it puts one distro over the other, as almost all of these players are freely available and can be shipped with any distro, nor do I see any argument that the behavior is anti-competative in that it encourages one player over the other, as so many players are easily available, and no one stands to profit from the use of any particular player.
