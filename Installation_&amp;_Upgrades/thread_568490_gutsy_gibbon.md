### Post by Herman on 2007-10-05
I have tried the method of upgrading and it worked fine for me. A few other people are not as lucky, I don't know why, probably due to the complications rsambuca pointed out.

My preferred method is to make a new installation in front of or behind the original installation. The new installation will most of the time automatically mount the older install anyway, so it is simple to move your data from the older installation into the newer one. 
Usually I don't bother to delete the older install for a long time, maybe until the next version comes out, when I do the same thing again, but in the opposite direction.
I can do things that way because I have the luxury of lots of hard drive space and I don't store very much data. I also have an unlimited broadband connection so there's no problem or cost for me to install all the added software again. I like the idea of beginning again with a fresh new installation, I think it's fun setting things up all over again.

Either way, you should learn how to back up all your important files in Ubuntu and restore, (or transfer) them, that's what's really important. That's one of the things I like the most about Ubuntu too, it's very simple and fast to back up and restore things like email, calendar, memos and tasks, as well as important data. And you don't have to pay for third party software for doing that either. In Ubuntu, backing up and restoring is really easy! Here's a link about that, [Back Up and Restore]("http://users.bigpond.net.au/hermanzone/p13.htm")

Regards, Herman :)
