### Post by darkod on 2010-05-24
You don't have to upgrade trough all releases. In fact, most users that were using 8.04 LTS waited for 10.04 LTS to upgrade directly to that.
On top of that, if you set up separate /home partition it allows for easy clean installs, so you avoid doing an upgrade.

As for deciding for the user, I wouldn't totally agree. In fact, in many things they left too much choice to the user. Look at all the thread about grub installed on a partition because in the window with the question they left not just the disks but also all the partitions. And the users who don't even care to learn how a partition is marked in linux tick all the boxes and voila, problems... Just one example with hundreds of threads about it here at the moment.

MS XP was not a "program" as you put it, and without many added programs could actually do almost nothing.

With ubuntu or almost any other linux distro, you can install and use as a complete system without adding anything else.  As it comes on a CD you have to accept that there is a limit to what that CD can include, and whilst you may not agree with it all, as I don't, it is the simplest of jobs to add/remove to get rid of what you don't want and add what you do want.  If you don't like that as a philosophy, you could always try another distro that comes on a dvd, 4.7GB as opposed to 0.69GB.  Personally, I prefer the Ubuntu way; download a CD and then add whatever I want to it.

PS:  You may also find that a clean install would give far fewer problems than an upgrade.  As darkod says, use a separate /home partition and life suddenly becomes so much simpler.
