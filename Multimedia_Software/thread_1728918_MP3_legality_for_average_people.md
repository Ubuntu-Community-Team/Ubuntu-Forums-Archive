### Post by Dngrsone on 2011-04-14
The .mp3 codec is a copyrighted piece of code.  The manufacturer of any hardware that uses the .mp3 decoder has to pay a fee to the copyright owners to use that technology.

Since Ubuntu ships as a purely open-source operating system, users have to add in any proprietary software they want to use, such as .mp3 encoder/decoders.

When you purchase a music CD, you are purchasing a license to listen to the music contained on that disc.  You are allowed to encode that music into a format that will allow you to listen to that music on a media player for personal use.  You are not allowed to distribute that music.  You can play the music for a friend, but you can't play it to a concert audience.

There are wikis concerning .mp3 and copyrighting; they would probably be a good place to look for further information.

Actually this isn't the case.  MP3 is encumbered by patents by a number of parties.  Copyright only protects form, not functionality.  It can't really be violated if you don't have access to the source code.

To clarify, every mp3 codec is copyrighted by some party but copyright is not the protection that causes some codecs to not be offered freely in software repositories.
