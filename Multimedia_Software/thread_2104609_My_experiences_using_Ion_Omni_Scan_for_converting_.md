### Post by silbar on 2013-01-13
Greetings,

During the recent dull Christmas break (no snow for skiing) my wife and I decided to look at some of our 35 mm color slides.  We have a lot of them, all taken before we switched to a digital camera.  "Gee", she said, "wouldn't it be nice to be able to turn those into digital form so we can get rid of all those trays taking up space in my closet."

So we bought a Slide and Film Scanner from Amazon, from Ion, called Omni Scan.  It has taken some time to learn how to use it -- details follow.

First, when I plugged it into my computer via the USB cable, powered it up, nothing happened.  I usually expect Ubuntu to take note of a new USB device, but nothing.  Oh,oh!  A Google search on "Omni Scan linux" gave no indication that the machine is compatible with linux.  So I called Ion's tech support and was eventually told that it was NOT going to work on linux systems.  Just Windows and Macs.  Bummer.

Before figuring out how to return it, however, I decided to see if it really did work on the Win Vista bootup option -- I hardly ever go there.  Well, it does, particularly if I read the instructions in the Quickstart Guide more carefully.  The operative hint was to go to a nearly dimmed-out menu item named "USBconnect" and hit the Enter key.  This makes a connection to Vista and allowed me to move scanned images made with the not-dim-at-all "Capture" menu item from the Omni Scan onto the Vista desktop.

So it does work on Windows.  I figured I could then copy the JPG's from there onto a flash drive and later read them in Ubuntu, where I could use GIMP to polish them as needed.  Sort of solved, but cumbersome.

The next morning, however, I decided to try again to see if it would work directly in Ubuntu.  As I said, "operative hint", and the USBconnect action here pops up a window saying a "camera" has been found and what did I want to do with it?  Among the choices it seemed to suggest the Shotwell app, with which I was unfamiliar.  

On eventually choosing Shotwell, I found it quite convenient for importing the JPG's from the scanner and then deleting the JPGs in the scanner for the next round of scans.  (Without an SD card, the Omni Scan has a 16 MB memmory, which will hold about 15 or so scanned slides.)  Shotwell also lets you put the slides in the right orientation and even allows you to rename them from titles like "Pict01.JPG" to "Maggie.JPG".  It also has a nice slideshow option, allowing you to cull out the less desirable slides.

From Shotwell's temporary library, you can then drag (copy) them off into a folder of your own choice for permanent storage and for being available to crop and work on with GIMP.  A small bummer there is that if you renamed them in Shotwell, they revert to the Pict01.JPG names.  I have learned to wait to rename them in my permanent folder.

The JPG images are all about 1 MB in size.  I have decided to copy the JPG's into the flash drive, for later burning onto a CD-ROM disc.  After GIMPing to resize them to something like a 5"x3" image (about 50 kB), I then "Save As" them as "Maggie.jpg" (or whatever) in the same folder.  The JPG versions there are then trashed to keep filling up the computer's RAM. (The originals are on the flash drive, remember?).

So, I am not returning the Ion Omni Scan to Amazon, after all.  I might even think about buying an SD card to scann more than 15 slides at a time. 

Hope this has been of interest or helpful to someone.

   Rico Silbar

### Post by silbar on 2013-02-21
An addendum: At the end of the above I wrote

[INDENT]I might even think about buying an SD card to scan more than 15 slides at a time.[/INDENT]

So I did buy one -- a 4 GB card, the smallest, is overkill for this application, as it could contain over 4000 scans.  

There were some confusions in getting it to work, however.  When I inserted the SD card the first time, I found I could not even turn the scanner on!  OMG, I've busted it (I thought).  Indeed, it was a bit hard to get the card properly seated, and that might have done it.  To my relief, I found that on REMOVING the card, the scanner would turn on again, normally, and use its internal 16 MB memory.  Whew! -- now I only have to return the card for a refund.

Before doing so, however, I did the unusual thing of looking at the QuickStart manual.  Here I discovered the line that said to insert the card WITH ITS LABEL FACING DOWN!  Doing that strange thing, I found that things work as they should.  It is even easier to put the card in and to get it out.  So much for RTFM.

Rico
