### Post by urbanus on 2010-05-15
Linux graphic drivers have come a long way, both the open source and closed source versions. 

Closed source Nvidia drivers have for a long time now been the best ones when it comes to actual performance. They have produced good 3D performance without video tearing. It's closed source nature does provide some challenges, usually most visible in the boot loader screen, which looks much better with an open source driver. 

ATI drivers does also give good 3D preformance, but have lacked important configuration possibilities, like over/under-scan. This has been fixed, but the video tearing problem still persists (in my experience). There are open source drivers with 3D acceleration, but they don't provide anywhere close to the acceleration that the closed source drivers does.

It should also be mentioned that many people experience better performance in Windows than in Linux when playing the same (native) games.

With the possible release of Steam onto the Linux OS, are the graphical drivers up to the challenge? Can they provide a good enough experience that will convince people to game in Linux instead of Windows? Because, when it all comes down to it, what does Linux provide that most people can't do in Windows, and why would they shift to Linux if Windows provides a better experience?

I'm aware of the ideological arguments, like only using open source software, something that Windows falls flat on. But, when it all comes down to it, Steam won't be open source, and most people just want things to work (hello Apple), and don't care about these things.
