### Post by Welly Wu on 2012-01-31
I discovered Clementine by reading some threads posted by users searching for a Linux equivalent to the Windows software Foobar2000. I use Banshee to import my 1,100+ CDs to convert them to .FLAC files, but I found that it is prone to crashes and it does not do a terribly good job of organizing my large digital music library. It is difficult to navigate through my music library using Banshee and it constantly reports that I have several hundreds of gigabytes worth of .FLAC audio files when Nautilus tells me that it is far less (but growing as I continue to import my CDs).

Clementine has a clean user interface and it is well connected to online music subscription services. I like the fact that my music is organized alphabetically which is to say it is organized by the name of the artist or band. Drilling down further, it shows me all of the albums that I currently own and the individual songs. This makes a lot of sense to me and it makes it easy to navigate through my music library or perform a fuzzy match search based on some parts of a key word or phrase. I also like the fact that Clementine does not try to add a slew of new features that result in features creep or bloat. It is a pretty focused software application dedicated to organizing and playing your music yet it includes the ability to listen to music from a variety of different sources such as Internet radio or paid music subscription services like Spotify. So far, Clementine works properly and it has not crashed on me yet.

There are a lot of other software applications that try to help Ubuntu users to organize their digital music libraries and connect to Internet music services, but Clementine gets the job done pretty well enough to warrant my recommendation. If you want an easy to understand and easy to use way to organize your music library, play music, connect to your paid music subscription services, and enjoy some tunes, Clementine is one of the better software applications that gets the job done.

Now, if the software developers of Foobar2000 were to port it to BSD, GNU/Linux, and OS X, then that would be a game changer.
