### Post by uc50_ic4more on 2009-04-03
I have the opinion that commercial, proprietary technologies will always be more popular than opposing open technologies. The former has a lot of money and a legal system behind it, whereas the latter depends on grass roots movements and word-of-mouth.

I think proprietary formats; especially those with a corporate backing, are much more palatable to hardware manufacturers (I suppose I'm thinking of .mp3 players as an example) because there is a corporate entity accountable for development and support.

Commercial software is also often a little more polished to the end user, as the manufacturers can employ focus groups, market research and teams of focused, managed designers to make the product as shiny as possible. Then they can patent it and DRM the bujeepers out of it and enjoy the forward momentum provided them by locking their users by way of technological exclusivity (.mp3 players that cannot play .ogg, DVD players that cannot play Theora) or pervasive mindshare (computer = Windows).

Up and above all of this, however, is the fact that OGG *is* becoming more popular and accepted, as are many open formats and software. Acceptance on a scale this large, though, takes years, if not generations, and patience is required. Use F/LOSS software, advocate for its use and enjoy the experience of being a part of the F/LOSS community. Let the popularity of these projects among the rest of the world's users take care of itself!
