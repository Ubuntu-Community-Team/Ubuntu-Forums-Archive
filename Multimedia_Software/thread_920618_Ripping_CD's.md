### Post by paulg on 2008-09-18
Vinyl never really went away. Audiophiles and the cool kids kept it alive. CD audio is technically a superior format. In theory CD audio will support a wider dynamic range. However, in the last 2 or so decades studios haven't been using that dynamic range and have been slowly making every CD LOUDER! Older mastering done for vinyl tended to have more dynamics (across the same album anyway) which is why many audiophiles are hanging onto there original vinyl albums. Remasters on CD are just made to be louder (by todays standards) by using dynamic compression so that the apparent volume of the CD is louder than the original master. New albums released to vinyl tend to be targeted to audiophiles or fans and mastered specifically for vinyl (because vinyl doesn't have the same dynamic range as  so they don't suffer dynamic compression to the same degree which is why you will find many audiophiles and such seeking the vinyl releases. This is really just a summary of lots of other editorials popping up. Here's one from the [Rolling Stone]("http://www.rollingstone.com/news/story/17777619/the_death_of_high_fidelity/print").

For myself I've done some blind a/b testing and couldn't tell the difference between lossless and OGG -q6 for most music on some decent headphones. I did a little better with classical (-q7) so I started encoding to -q8 for a little safety. Since I started this, I've ripped half my CDs (new ones and older ones I listen to a lot) and the price of storage has come down considerably so now I find myself re-ripping a lot of audio to lossless, not because I need to, but because I can, have the storage for it, and if I rip it lossless once I know I won't need to rip it again.

For recommended MP3 settings (and for other codec settings) Hydrogen Audio forums have some nice recommended encodings. Their explanations are quite thorough and they give different examples why you might use one encoding setting over another (e.g. in a car vs. hi-fi listening room). See their recommended LAME settings wiki [here]("http://wiki.hydrogenaudio.org/index.php?title=LAME#Best_quality:_.22archiving.22"). Depending on your storage space and whether or not you can transcode FLAC->MP3 on the fly a HQ MP3 encoding is good enough for most people so you'll probably be happy with this if you find the correct settings you like.

Still another option is to use [rockbox]("http://www.rockbox.org/") on your iPod which should give you native FLAC support on your player (depending on what version you have and not for the faint of heart).

You mentioned a program Gnormalize? I'm not familiar with it but I assume it does some normalization of the sound file? This is not a great idea as it's irreversible. Sometimes it's unavoidable (e.g. really really quiet home recording) but if you find you need something you should look into using the [replay gain]("http://wiki.hydrogenaudio.org/index.php?title=Replay_Gain") tag. I have no idea of the iPod supports this natively.

### Post by bonzini on 2008-09-18
If you have disk space, then by all means rip to FLAC.  That way you don't lose anything.  Ripping takes a long time and you don't want to do it twice.

I don't have an iPod myself (I have a Samsung that plays Oggs), but I suspect iPods can read AAC (?) and so that might be a better option if you have a big classic with 80gb or 160gb.

If you rip to (say) Ogg, then re-rip to some other lossy (apple-recognized) format, you are decimating twice and that cannot be good for your sound.

One thing that's cool with eg Rhythmbox is that it will convert automatically from your library format to your portable device format, providing that you have the right codecs installed and all is well-configured.

Again, my experience - I plug in my Samsung, which is not configured as an MTP device, and I drag the songs I want from my music library to the Samsung.  They are automatically converted from FLAC to quality 7 ogg.

I am not a metal kind of guy; more sort of ambient / downbeat.  My ears are 52 years old.  I think as people get older and their hearing starts to change that it is more important than ever not to mess with the fidelity of the recording too much.  So I try to ensure that there is as little deterioration of sound quality as possible added along the chain from files on my hard drive to my ears.

I bought a set of AKG 701 headphones awhile ago to replace my Grado 80s and that was a great investment.  I also bought a HeadRoom Total BitHead; another great investment.  I get spectacularly good sound from my laptop now.  If you have headphones that take some oomph to drive, the BitHead is great.  Also its sound quality generally is a LOT better than anything in a laptop or desktop.  HeadRoom has much finer gear but convincing my dear wife of my need for HeadRoom's $2500 top-of-the-line DAC / headphone amplifier is not on today's agenda.

I hope that some of the foregoing is useful!
