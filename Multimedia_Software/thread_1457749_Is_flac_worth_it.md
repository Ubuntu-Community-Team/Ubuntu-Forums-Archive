### Post by rossmoore on 2010-04-19
You'd have to rip from CD to Flac, no point converting MP3 to flac. (As already noted by others above).

Is it worth it? Depends on:
[LIST=1]
[*]The quality of your ears
[*]The quality of your amp and speakers
[*]The quality of your soundcard
[*]Your willingness to transcode your music when you want to put it on a portable player that doesn't read flac
[*]The amount of disk space you want your collection to take up
[/LIST]

Most people can hear the difference given a good setup, but it really depends on how much you care. As with most things in life, you have to weigh the pros and cons for yourself. Try the same song, one a high quality rip to MP3 and one a flac rip, both from the same CD source. Can you hear the difference? Try a few different songs.

For me, if I can get music in flac I get it in that and don't mind the transcoding necessary for some players. But most of my music is MP3, which is fine.

### Post by Native Dialect on 2010-04-19
Ripping tracks from a CD, to FLAC format, is not really worth it either. It would not be a genuine FLAC recording. While CD audio quality is better than most digital formats in use these days (e.g. mp3 or 256kbps AAC files), it is still not as good as FLAC. Unless the original format was FLAC, you won't have much to gain. 

As far as real FLAC audio is concerned, the difference is only truly noticeable, if you have the appropriate hardware. Laptop speakers? Desktop speakers? Earbuds? Some throw away headphones you probably spent $40 on? Such outputs won't cut it. At the least, you need something like Sennheiser HD 595 headphones or Beats by Dre etc. In other words, you need an audiophile setup (that also means having a good soundcard and not just the junky on-board card that comes with your mobo). 

I mean, you can have stellar headphones and an average soundcard, and still get a note worthy upgrade, but it would be like watching Blu Ray on a 720p television rather than a 1080p television. Sure it is an improvement, but the value isn't really there. FLAC files are huge. Don't waste the space if you can't truly put it to use. If you really want to hear what a genuine FLAC file sounds like, I suggest you go download Trent Reznor's latest NiN release. He gave it away for free on his NiN webpage. You can torrent the FLAC and then download the mp3 version of the album, for comparison purposes.

### Post by NightwishFan on 2010-04-19
Flac is designed to be lossless, which means it will have the exact quality of the source and then compress it. This compression is based on the raw audio data, so it will be about half the size of a pcm. (.wav)

Flac is great for ripping CD audio. CD Audio is in 16-bit 44100hz PCM, which I think is about the max range a human ear can detect, someone else can correct me if I am wrong. FLAC will take this exact pcm audio and make it about half the size.

My verdict? If you are a casual listener it will not make a difference, but if you like to have perfect copies of all your audio go with FLAC. As was said you can even check them for damage. Flac does to audio what .png does to images.

### Post by NightwishFan on 2010-04-19
Think of digital audio of any sort as 'perfect quality'. Anytime you convert it the quality is permanently lost. Lossless preserves the sound, but not the format. For example:

wav -> flac -> wav = no loss in audio quality, since both are technically lossless as wav is raw with a header.
mp3 -> vorbis = loss, since both use different models to encode
mp3 -> flac -> mp3 = loss since the format is lost even though the quality is preserved to flac.

Regardless of equipment, as long as you keep the correct bit and freq, flac will always sound the the source. It depends on what you need.
