### Post by Alan James on 2010-04-25
Most DVD players can't read a video by itself. The reason is a complex coding issue that several of my clients have not been able to understand, even after I explain it to them. I usually just tell them to trust that I know what I am doing and they usually oblige me. However, because you asked, here is what I tell them.
 

 When the DVD format was being created a group of companies got together and asked themselves what kind of features they wanted DVDs to have. Menus, subtitle tracks, secondary audio tracks and in movie pop-up information were among the most requested. They all decided that in order for these features to be supported on all of their machines they would all have to agree on a set of codecs, a compression rate and a file system for the disks. This allows a DVD player to know exactly how to read the disk when booting, displaying menus and playing videos.
 

 MPEG was picked as the standard codec and this MPEG videos are thus always supported on DVD players but very few other types of videos are. If you want a video to play on every DVD player you have to encode it in the standard DVD encoding. At my company we do all of our DVD creation in a program called Adobe Encore, which is unavailable in Linux. However, there are ways of creating properly encoded DVD in Linux but unfortunately I don't know how. If you want to know how it may be worth Googleing or starting another thread asking that question.
