### Post by Gallienus on 2009-07-10
Hi folks,

Is there an easy way to learn if the left and right channels of a stereo sound file (more specifically a wav file) really do contain different information or are in fact identical? I have some wav files, and when I listen to them, I honestly can't hear any difference between the left and right channels. If there really is no difference, I would obviously prefer to convert them to mono to save space, but before I do that, I want to be absolutely sure that I won't be losing any information. (I'm tone deaf, and just because I can't hear a difference doesn't mean that there isn't one ;) ).

Gallienus.

### Post by ajgreeny on 2009-07-10
It can often be difficult to hear the differences between channels on a stereo recording but headphones will usually help, as they tend to exaggerate the stereo effect.

You could also install audacity and use that to load the files and listen to them in stereo, and then with the channels combined to see if there is any difference you can hear.   If the differences are obvious you may even be able to see them in the channel traces that audacity shows.  There may also be other ways that I'm not aware of, using the command line, so wait to see if any other answers appear.

### Post by bobince on 2009-07-10
You can usually see whether you have mono simply by zooming up and looking to see if the left and right waveforms are consistently the same.

To make sure, you can subtract one channel from the other (to get the 'side' portion of a mid-side split - see [http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nFiIJAIKN0o](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nFiIJAIKN0o) for how to do that in Audacity). If the results come out completely flat, you have centered mono material.
