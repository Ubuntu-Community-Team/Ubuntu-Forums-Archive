### Post by Bucky Ball on 2016-02-25
Thanks for the Bitwig tip. I've never heard of it. Looks great and the educational price is reasonable, as is the regular price. If you think that is expensive, have a look at the price of the full version of Ableton or any other professional quality commercial recording software on the market. You'll find is fairly competitive, perhaps even a shade under-price for what it claims to be capable of.  

To your request: there are plenty of decent DAWs out there. Are you talking specifically about Linux DAWs?

Either way, it really depends what you want to do. You mention 'DAW' and you mention Ardour and Ableton Live as examples. Fair enough, but they are totally different beasts used for different scenarios, as you would know. Pretty much like comparing Cubase and Ableton Live. One is for recording midi and analogue in a studio and the other is best suited to ... live performance (and recording and manipulating tracks 'on the fly').

It would be best if you identified here exactly what you are wanting to do with the software and we might be able to get more specific with suitable software. 

Do you want something that is going to work like Ardour or Cubase, a dedicated studio DAW, or do you want something that is more suitable for live work like Ableton, or are you looking for something that will happily do both (Bitwig actually looks like something that is as close to handling both scenarios as anything I've seen, Win or Linux)?

I'm interested to hear if there is anything like Ableton as if that is what you're after, I'd stick with what you've got. I've seen nothing previously but haven't sniffed around in awhile. Music notation? I use Sibelius in Windows for music notation as nothing in Linux comes close so I have Win installed for that and Cubase, Protools, and occasionally Ableton, but not for awhile). I have a dedicated studio desktop which dual-boots Win and Ubuntu (am currently editing video on Blender with that box). 

It pains me to say this, and it is a regrettable situation, but having come from Win myself about eight years ago I have found nothing that equals Cubase, Protools, Ableton or Sibelius in the Linux world and don't see anything looming on the horizon. Sure some people will disagree with that statement, but hey, that's fine. I'm always ready for further enlightenment and look forward to people's suggestions. My feelings are if what's available can do the job in whatever OS it uses, use it! :)

I do use Audacity in Ubuntu - a beautiful, elegant and powerful little app - but I was using that in Win before I migrated to Linux quite some time ago now. Still using in Ubuntu now (and Win when required). Will have another dabble in the Linux audio world again when I have time later in the year to see if there's any new developments. Here's hoping.

Good luck.
