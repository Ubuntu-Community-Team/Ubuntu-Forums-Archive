### Post by Maheriano on 2009-06-25
My roommate somehow found out about this guy that owns a company making these SharpVu boxes where you can watch television over your internet without any sort of cable connection. Tonight he actually came out to my house, hooked everything up and 5 minutes later was giving me a live demo of how it works.

Before I get into the details, you can check out any additional information on their website at [www.sharpvu.com](www.sharpvu.com).

[IMG]http://www.sharpvu.com/mediac/400_0/media/verismo-vunow-pod.jpg[/IMG]



So this thing is actually a little mini computer but just looks like a regular cable box. It has HDMI output on the back and a remote to control it and the entire custom operating system and interface it run by Linux.
<off-topic>
When I was using my computer to change my wireless settings, I was flipping between desktops in Ubuntu with the Desktop Cube and when he saw me doing it he shouted, "Whoa, that was cool! What was that? How did you do that? Did you make that?" When he found out I know Linux he tried to recruit me to write the software for the next release.
</off-topic>
So I got everything set up and this guy started flipping through the menus, it was pretty cool. How it works is it connects to hundreds of different websites to watch movies/shows from that website. So if you want to watch a NBC broadcast, you would just click NBC and choose any show you want to watch. On the back end it pulls the video from [www.nbc.com](www.nbc.com) the same as if you had visited the website yourself but it integrates it into the frontend seamlessly. And it would be perfect for someone that didn't have a computer hooked to their television, like I said it's standalone.

Then after checking out a few shows like that (lots of Onion News videos), he flipped over to Live Internet Television which is over 200 channels of live streaming television. I could watch BBC or CNN broadcasts live as they were being streamed to television. I don't know which website he was getting those from but it was really cool.

It also had internet radio and some old school movies you could watch like Liar Liar or anything else. Then he stuck in a flash drive into the back of it and he could browse that the same way with any movies, videos or anything else he had on it. There was also a place where you could watch YouTube videos, log into your account or search for any video on any of those sites....Google Video, Metcafe, YouTube, it searched all of them at once.

After it was all over I was impressed but not enough to buy it, it's $190 CAD for the 480i version and (about) $260 CAD for the 720p version. None of the live streaming broadcasts are 720, only the web content like YouTube videos and stuff. So I made sure he was finished and showed him Moovida (ex-Elisa) as a media centre on my machine that did pretty much everything his box did and did it better. The YouTube interface is better, plus it handles music organized by meta tags and pictures, stored shows and movies on my machine. He was pretty impressed and probably disappointed at the same time I guess. I felt kind of bad but had to let him know there are free alternatives out there.

So in conclusion, here are the good and bad of the box:
**_GOOD_**
- standalone meaning you don't need a computer hooked to your machine
- WiFi so you don't have to have it hardwired to the internet. It could receive a wireless signal and then send that signal out over the network
- Can access other forms of media across the network
- Linux based
- Streams live television (still not sure how)
- Can watch it from a laptop anywhere on the network over WiFi.
**_BAD_**
- one interface only and can't change it
- doesn't manage photos nor songs
- No newer movies due to copyrights....stupid copyrights
- Indexing not as good as Moovida
- Need to input wireless code on screen with Zelda style letter selector. Anyone sitting there can see what you enter.
- More expensive than just hooking your computer to your television (if convenient)
- Not accessible outside the network though I imagine another machine running Apache would take care of that.

Anyone else see this? Thoughts?
