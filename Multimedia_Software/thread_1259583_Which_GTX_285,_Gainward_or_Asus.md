The only reason I'd buy a GTX 285 is if I had a huge monitor.

### Post by rmjohnson144 on 2009-09-06
It all depends on your plans with the rig.  A 750w will run a single GTX 285 maybe a single GTX 295 (has two GPUs in hardware SLI built in).  If you plan on overclocking, it will add to the power load.  I have an 850 watt Antec and I had two GTX 295 and it ran them overclocked slightly, but at 900 watts and was too much of a stress.

Just remember to get a little more power than you need so as to not stress the power supply.  The watt rating usually is a peak power usage and not meant to run full rated speeds at all times.

as far as need goes.  A single GTX 295 will play most games at maximum settings and high resolutions.  Only a handful of games take full advantage of the graphics as most games are ported from gaming consoles and usually require only a Vista Rating of 3 to 5 out of a possible 5.9.  

If you plan on upgrading to Windows 7 and DX11, then you may want to upgrade the video card last as it's best to have the latest fastest video card over fastest greatest motherboard/cpu.

It's really only up to you, and any route will suffice just fine.  I'm currently running my backup rig on a dual core pentium with 1200MHz DDR2 ram and it plays games great on my GTX 295 with Windows 7 installed. and most of my games play great on my ATI HD4670 at lower resolution and it is so low powered it doesn't even need a power cable pluged in.  

anyway, a smaller monitor requires only a smaller video card, while a larger monitor requires a larger card.  

-=Mark=-
ps, I wouldn't bother with SLI setups as the performance increase is negligible over just buying a higher end single card.

### Post by rmjohnson144 on 2009-09-07
again, the video card choice will be determined by your monitor choice.  if you go 1680 x 1050 or less the 285 will handle the job.  if 1920 x 1200 or higher the 295 will might be a better choice.  also, the higher the resolution the more memory you'll need.

My self, I'd go with a 285 and if down the road you feel you need extra buy a second one.  Two 285 are much faster then a single 295 and will run a lot cooler (my 295 runs so hot all the time I have to turn up the fan to 100% to keep temps below 90C), but the two 285 will cost more.  

To me my 295 was over kill and if I could do over I would have bought the 285 and loose a few FPS on a game or two.  My 295 is just overkill.  I mean 100+ FPS is great, but overkill.  Most monitor run 660FPS max anyway, and your eyes perceive smooth motion at 32fps, TV shows are 28FPS.  so as long as you get 45FPS on your game (usually a 45 FPS score will give about 30 FPS as a lowest score).

About your power supply, I've had good luck good with Corsair, PC Power and Cooling, and Antec.  Most of them are already rated at 24/7 use and would allow you a 650ish watt psu.  But 750 would be fine if the cost was cheaper.

be sure to look around and video card reviews and compare scores.  make sure to be aware of the screen resolutions being used.  only use results with same resolution as you will be using, otherwise the scores won't mean anything.

Good luck
-=Mark=-

### Post by rmjohnson144 on 2009-09-08
There will be a slight difference.  The DX11 is more efficient and will do multi-thread to the CPU better making a lot better especially in SLI mode.  DX11 programs should run on DX10 card with DX11 options disabled since they are the same architecture (API is shared).

As for Asus, I've had bad experiences lately.  Mostly with them making custom boards and having problems with hardware mods or adding custom heatsinks for better cooling.  I was able to get around it by buying seperate GPU heatsink and memory heatsinks. In a way it might be better to go that route as they will upgrade to future cards easier, but are a little more work, plus more parts equals more chances of something going wrong.  That aside, Asus video cards have been reliable.

Also, if you can wait on eVGA cards to get in stock, they have very good overclocking software with their products.  They have not only an OC utility (Called Precision), but an overvolting utility (called GPU Voltage Tuner) which will probably need a better heatsink if you want it to overclock much.

I know I push eVGA a little much, but I really think the support advantages will make it worth the wait.  Normally it takes only a few days for newegg to restock items.  There's a notify button to email when it becomes in stock and usually has an estimated restock date listed.

In the end though, any GTX 285 will work great and I'm sure you'll be happy with whatever you chose.

Good luck
-=Mark=-
