> **cpeachey said:**
> Space & costs are not an issue but I do want the best quality. I thought MP3 was not so good, are the other formats better?

Flac is much better than MP3 for quality. MP3 (and .ogg voribs, musepack, AAC, etc) are all 'lossy'. This means that during the encoding process, information is 'thrown away' in order to achive higher compression. Lossless, like flac (also wavpack, ape for other free/semi-free and wma 'lossless', ALAC for non-free) do _not_ throw away information. 

So you can rip your discs to .flac, and know that if you want, you can burn them back to CD-Audio (or wav) and get 100% of the original quality. 

By the way, MP3 is a patented, non-open format, so its worth avoiding just for that IMO.

### Post by cascade9 on 2009-11-26
No, MP3 is not 'unlimited'. 

> *** --freeformat    free      format bitstream**   Produces a free format bitstream. With this option, you can use -b with      any bitrate higher than 8 kbps.
    
    However, even if an mp3 decoder is required to support free bitrates at least      up to 320 kbps, many players are unable to deal with it.
    
    Tests have shown that the following decoders support free format:
    
    FreeAmp up to 440 kbps
    in_mpg123 up to 560 kbps
    l3dec up to 310 kbps
    LAME up to 560 kbps
    MAD up to 640 kbps

[http://lame.cvs.sourceforge.net/*checkout*/lame/lame/doc/html/switchs.html](http://lame.cvs.sourceforge.net/*checkout*/lame/lame/doc/html/switchs.html)

While it is possible to make MP3 bitrates go over 320K, its got issues. Mainly that the files will not play on the majority of MP3 decoders. 320K was as far as the MP3 developers went. 

Vorbis will go to 500K/sec (-q10) but IMO there is no point going past 320K, or even 256K, with lossy files. 

BTW, a 'good' MP3 player? Depends on what you call good. My current player (iRiver e100) supports MP3, ogg vorbis and flac, cost less than $100 US.
