### Post by TheFu on 2020-10-02
The answers depend on the hardware involved.  Sorry.
Everything is a trade-off.  If you have a Core i9 with 64G of RAM and NVMe scratch storage system, the answer is completely different than if it is a 5 yr old Celeron w/ 2GB of RAM and a USB powered 2.5 inch scratch disk.

Plus you didn't say what you were recording and how that might impact system performance. If you are playing a high-end, heavy GPU, game, then less of the CPU and GPU can be used for the screen capture.  But if you are just recording how-to guides, then 15 fps is fine. No need for 30 or 60 fps recordings.

The way I handle recordings is to allow huge files during the recording with nearly zero transcoding.  That way the files get stored without any dropped frames.  Then I use transcoding later to get the quality and size and resolution needed.

So ... nobody here can answer the question with do x, y, z.  The only answer is that you need to see how the different settings impact the recording on YOUR system, in YOUR environment, doing the things on the box you want while recording.  Trial and error.
