### Post by Rob Sayer on 2015-07-09
Well, the matroska container can use most any video format but most people use h.264 ... ie. mpeg-4 AVC ... or the free/open version of it, x264.  I think the other 2 are more limited.  And h.264 is a *lot* better supported.

I don't think ogv or vp8 spec is as powerful as AVC.  But video encoding is complicated, and I suspect that your ability to use all those nice encoding parameters may be more important than the specs per se.  I know there's only so much I want to be a video geek ...

### Post by Yellow Pasque on 2015-07-11
First of all, let's deal with actual codec names and not container filename extensions

Theora - Theora doesn't seem compelling now that VP8 is free and widespread
Dirac - I don't know too much about it or have experience with it.
VP8 - I find VP8 fairly close to H.264/AVC in terms of quality, though H.264 seems a little better with quality/size when encoded with higher settings. 
VP9 - it's meant to be a free alternative to H.265/HEVC. I haven't personally experimented with it too much, but there's a good comparison here: [http://www.streamingmedia.com/Articles/ReadArticle.aspx?ArticleID=103577&PageNum=1](http://www.streamingmedia.com/Articles/ReadArticle.aspx?ArticleID=103577&PageNum=1)

So yes, VP8 and VP9 are competitive with their equivalents, though their weakness is device support. Which one you pick depends on what you want to do with the video. I recently ripped a lot of my DVD collection (for personal backup/archival purposes) to HEVC (inside a Matroska conatiner) and am very happy with the results. If I was intending to upload it to the web, I may have used VP8/VP9 (i.e WebM).
