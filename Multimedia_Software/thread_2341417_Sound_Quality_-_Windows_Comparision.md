### Post by Bucky Ball on 2016-10-28
If you are using pro-level audio cans you would be aware that the sound 'as is' is without equalisation and the end product that is released to you is not intended to be listened to muddied by the user's idea of what it should sound like. This can be confusing. Once you start diddling with an EQ it is no longer 'as is', i.e. as it was intended to be listened to.

You will note that audiophile amplifiers worth thousands normally have two knobs: volume and balance (left or right). That's it. If you're having to tweak the EQ to get anywhere near the way it sounds 'as is', go back, wrong way. There is something else seriously amiss.

The other confusing bit is: it doesn't sound the same as in Windows. Fine, but in Windows, have you got an EQ set to emphasise certain frequency bands and you can't replicate that in Ubuntu? Have you an idea of what the 'as is' sound of the recording is supposed to be or are you going for an idea of what a well recorded, EQed and mastered track is supposed to sound like? On good equipment it should sound like it does out of the packet with no fiddling.

About how we perceive sound and what the brain does with sound (and how it expects it to sound). [Psychoacoustics]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychoacoustics"). A fascinating topic. :)

I suggest you boot to Windows, switch off all EQ and play the tune 'flat', as it was intended, or 'as is' as you put it. Boot back into Ubuntu and get rid of EQ. Is it still radically different? (I have the Sennheiser 555s and the HD 443s and studio monitors. Haven't used an EQ in years. In fact I sold my EQ hardware about seven years ago.)

Other suggestion is you get this music onto the best amplifier you can find with the best speakers or headphone and play the track flat (and you won't have much choice if you're doing this with an audiophile's equipment). It should sound great. Now you have a reference, an actual 'as is'. You may be surprised. Many are.

No EQ required there on a good system. You should be able to hear the bass with full tonal range and clarity whilst still clearly hearing the highest of cymbals, high-hats and triangles. There should be total 'frequency separation'. EQing will mess that up the further you tweak. :))

Get back to us with a comparison between Win and Ubuntu with NO EQ on either. (Once you know how close they are without EQ you have a reference for your situation, or at least a comparison to work from, a bottom line.)
