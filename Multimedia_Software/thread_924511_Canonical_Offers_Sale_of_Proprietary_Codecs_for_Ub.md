### Post by markbuntu on 2008-09-19
This is a pretty hefty misrepresentation of what Canonical has done. What they have done is offer a boxed version of Ubuntu for retailers. Among the things they include with that version is support from Canonical for three months and the restricted codecs, which they had to buy licenses for to include in this boxed for retail version.

The way I see it, retailers would not even have put this on the shelves unless the people who bought it could take it home and install it and watch Youtube or Hulu or listen to their local radio station right out of the box.

It is one thing to be a user, who is not restricted by the licenses of these codecs, it is another to be a Corporation and try to distribute them. Personally, I think they made a good move. It makes more people more aware of the issue and may perhaps lead to beneficial changes in the way these proprietary formats are used and distributed.

Look at it this way, if Canonical had distributed these restricted codecs without license or even helped people to get them, Microsoft and others could have ganged up and bled Canonical dry in court, sucking up Canonicals resources and diverting them from doing the good work. 

When fighting monsters, you need to choose your fights and the battle over restricted formats has not even reallly begun. We need bigger guns and more public awareness and support before we take these people on.

### Post by omegamormegil on 2008-11-18
In addition to the boxed Ubuntu you describe, Canonical IS selling paid for media codecs in the Ubuntu store.  Check it out:

[http://shop.canonical.com/index.php?cPath=19](http://shop.canonical.com/index.php?cPath=19)

It's not Canonical's fault that popular media formats are owned by people that put restrictions on the distribution of software that can play them.  I think it's good that Canonical is making software available for people that would prefer to purchase the codecs, as opposed to having software of debatable legality installed on their systems.  

My understanding is that it is illegal to distribute the DVD decryption software, and other codecs for playing certain (popular and commonly used) music or video formats, but it isn't illegal to use the software.
