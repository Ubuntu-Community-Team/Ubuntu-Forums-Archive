### Post by KingTermite on 2008-06-02
No.

MP3 being lossless rips at a "bit rate". What that really means is a "sampling rate". For example, if one rips at 128Kbps, then there are 128,000 samples taken per second. That seems like a lot, but it really isn't sufficient to reproduce the perfect sound wave.

Now imagine the graph of the sound wave. Basically, your sampling rate is an attempt to reproduce that sound wave. Think of when you did graphs in pre-algebra or something in junior/high school. Remember how you could do the graph with 4 points and it would look all choppy and funny, but if you did it with 12 points it looked much better? Same concept.

So by representing it by any "sampling rate", you have degraded the true and original graph (fewer points to represent on the graph). So when you are trying to convert back to FLAC, you are taking that lower quality graph as your "starting point".

Yes and no.  The other people in the thread correctly note that once the music is encoded as MP3, some quality is lost for ever.

However, there is nothing stopping you converting them to FLAC, WAV or CD if you wish to for some reason.  Nothing will be lost.  Converting to CD has the advantage of allowing you to put that disc into a normal CD-player.
