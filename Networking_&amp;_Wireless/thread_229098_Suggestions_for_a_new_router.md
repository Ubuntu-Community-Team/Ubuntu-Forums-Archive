### Post by em3raldxiii on 2006-08-04
I own a D-Link DI-604 which I have been using for well over 4 years now.  It's rock solid.  I have 4 computers total, but actually one of my computers has 2 network cards which are both plugged in; as well, I have a wireless router which is connected to the D-Link in this configuration:
 
Phone line -> 3-Com DSL Modem ->
 
D-Link -> 3 computers (one has 2 network cards, the other is plugged into the Linksys
D-LInk -> Linksys wireless -> 1 computer plus one card in one of the previously mentioned computers.
 
One of the computers on the D-Link has a static IP, the rest are autoconfigured (dynamic) with the D-Link.
 
When I was installing Ubuntu, I had it plugged into the D-Link which had been originally set up years ago when I was using Windows.  Ubuntu automatically recognized & configured the internet connection with NO troubles whatsoever.
 
NOTE:  REMEMBER that it's very case sensitive in some very hard to notice places.  Always ensure you are using the correct case for all of your interaction with the router.

### Post by em3raldxiii on 2006-08-04
Yup, that one will do fine.  Notice the WAN port on the back is a standard ethernet CAT5 ... no problemo.  *EDIT:  Assuming you already have a broadband router connected to your telephone/cable outlet*

Keep in mind that networking between computers spanning the router to the switch (that is, a computer plugged into the router trying to talk to a computer plugged into the switch) can be a headache, and may not be worth it.  They would all be abe to reach the internet just fine, and any computer plugged into the same host device would be able to talk to eachother.

Instead, you might consider going with a router that has 8 outputs OR have a router that has only 2 and going to a gateway switch with 8+.  That would make overall networking between computers less paiful.

### Post by em3raldxiii on 2006-08-04
Most switches DO have an autosensing uplink port (that I have come across anyway), so you probably won't have too much trouble.

As far as configuring your switch, well ... it kinda depends on the make & model.  I've only used 2 for my own network in the past, and both were kinda old, so I don't know if they really apply to our little thread here.  However, in my experience, most switches are a snap to set up.

Your router will most likely be administered through your browser.  If your computer is already configured to work on the internet via direct connection, then basically you will plug in your router, follow the instructions for logging in (probably open your browser and type in the appropriate IP such as 192.168.0.1 for my D-Link), and follow the wizard to "spoof" your computer).  Then you will connect your switch and follow whatever instructions came with it.

When it comes time to make a switch-connected computer talk to a router-connected computer, you'll probably have to come back and ask questions :D

Good luck!
