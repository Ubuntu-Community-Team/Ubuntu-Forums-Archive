### Post by jonobr on 2009-02-10
Hello

lisati , hope I dont come of as a jack a$$, but the conversation when talking about traffic trapping/ monitoring or control of access will go a lot deeper.

For example, should/must the hotel track customer traffic?
Is this for blocking specific internet access eg to adult content websites etc?

Taking the question asked at face value, it was a question about making sure that users agree to the accepted usage policy of the hotel. This policy could be defined by the hotel, municipality or government.

Imagine the hotel blocks access to adult content or certain discussion forums which could be considered tasteless, offensive or hateful etc.
Some countries/people would believe that to be a good thing and it could be mandated by law.
Other countries/people (and lawyers) could look at that as discriminatory, an infringement on the right to free speech,  and all sorts of things which may or may not be true, but would make excellent grounds for a lawsuit.


On monitoring the traffic, if your government/country doesn't require you to track the customers at your hotel, and you have the ability to monitor, again, you could open yourself to all kinds of problems despite what your terms and conditions say.



I travel a lot throughout the year , and stay in a lot of different hotels.

They mostly do things the same across the board.
Majority that I stay in, are signup landing pages of the hotel requiring you to pay, or in some way identify yourself, particularly on wireless. With wired, you could be identified as you are physically connected from your room.

This way, they can have your info as well as your connection ID if they needed to , most have the so called AUP (or usually called T&C's - terms and conditions of use) and require you to be verified and agreement before allowing you to commence.

Can hotels track your usage with this info? Of course.
Must they? Depends on their infrastructure and laws covering providing service.

IN the US the law is called CALEA, and requires ISPs to be able to track users and their data.
The ISP is handed a warrant to track the required user data and they provide either a tap or data stream to the Government or LEA= law enforcement agencies.

This was a requirement placed on the ISP themselves, so the question is, what about an entity such as a hotel , or college campus who provide access to others, how are they affected.

There are 3 conditions in the US  for this case, where sometimes, they are responsible, and sometimes not
They are covered here (This whitepaper talks mainly about academic institutions)

[http://www.arl.org/bm~doc/calea_academia.pdf](http://www.arl.org/bm~doc/calea_academia.pdf)

This Calea law changes as there are frequent challenges by groups to the validity of the law or certain aspects to it.
Most of this is around freedom of speech/ provacy rights, power of the government etc,


This is lifted straight from the cleaa law posted by the FCC

> We note that establishments acquiring broadband Internet access to permit their patrons to access the Internet do not appear to be covered by CALEA (assuming they were otherwise "telecommunications carriers" under CALEA). Examples of these entities include schools, libraries, hotels, coffee shops, etc. . . .


So, your requirements for monitoring / capturing or controlling your service to your customers really depends on whats required from you as a business, what the law of your region/country , law enforcement agency is etc.

Get it wrong and you could end up with a law suit.

Again, taking the question below at face value, I had assumed the scenario was something like this...., law enforcement come to or contact the hotel
and say, someone named joe bloggs  in this place has been downloading bad images. You could hold up a piece of paper saying, well Joe Bloggs agreed with us that he would only use this for legal internet browsing,

The question didn't really go into anything more then that, however, more specificity could probably yield information as to the requirements.

You could argue, that the question implies the be ability to track/montior and control, however, local law may just require that the hotel inform the user that the connection be used for only saintly non-offensive or legal purposes and customer acknowledgment is required.

Again, Im hoping Im not sounding like a jacka$$ or know it all.
And am open to correction on all the above as the law on tracking/interception is changing and subject to a great deal of public opinion/scrutiny (just ask George Bush :-) )
