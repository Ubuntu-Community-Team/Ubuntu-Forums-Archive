### Post by Cheesehead on 2011-04-08
Unless your current router has other problems, you don't need to replace it. Instead, simply add a switch.

A router connects two networks (for example, LAN and WAN). So it includes a DHCP server, and a firewall, and the ability to forward ports between the two networks, and all that stuff.

A switch is (oversimplified) like a splitter, creating more LAN ports on the *same* network. No firewall, no setup. Plug it in and -poof- more LAN ports.

Here's a confusing bit: Just about all routers *include* a switch...and you can use it easily. That's why most household routers have 1 WAN port and 4 LAN ports. The LAN ports are that switch.
So if you buy a router (instead of a switch), *DON'T* use the WAN port. Plug everything into the LAN ports - including the cable to your existing router. Your existing router will see everything on the same LAN. Which model of router to purchase doesn't really matter, because you're not using any of the router features. You're just using it as a switch.

### Post by Cheesehead on 2011-04-09
Routers are a commodity. Unless you plan on reflashing it and experimenting with it, any common wireless router with good reviews should meet your needs.

Don't fret the number of LAN ports on a new router, since your old one is still available as a switch.

Wireless reach can be affected by building materials, other rf interference, and by the placement and orientation of the antenna in the room. If your wifi signal is weak, try moving the router around and try different antenna directions.

DMZ is a common term. We know what you mean. I have never had a router that lacked this option.

If you do plan on reflashing and experimenting, then be sure to do your research, and remember to buy two of them.

I built my router. More fun that way.
