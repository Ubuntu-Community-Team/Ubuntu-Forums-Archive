### Post by swampdweller on 2007-07-23
I've been using Ubuntu for just under a year.  I'm no expert.
I do recall, however, that wifi was by *far* my greatest
frustration.  That lead me, like so many others, to ask
"which card" should I get in order to have the least difficulty.
Well, that question has been answered many times by others,
most of whom are far more knowledgable than me.

I recall wondering, at one time, if there weren't simply a device
which could plug into my existing RJ ether jack to adapt it to wifi.
The nice young man at the local tech shop assured me that no
such beast existed.  However, I am writing to you now through
just such a beast.

It is inexpensive, and requires only a moderate amount of
fussing.  What makes it so underemployed, I'm guessing, is the
fact that it masquerades as a wireless G router.  Well, in fairness,
it is.  It is one of the cheapest, smallest, and most commonly
available (as in WalMart) routers you can find.  It is less
expensive than most wifi cards.

It is the little Belkin wireless G router.  The part number, which
you'll have a hard time finding on the box, is F5D7230-4.
The mode which functions as Ether-Wifi adapter, is called
"wireless bridging".  It has been rumored that not all versions
support this, however, I have purchased four, all within the past
week (July 2007) and all mine have.  Furthermore, the manual
devotes an entire chapter to the process.  That said, I suppose
Belkin could drop the feature, I certainly don't presume to
represent them.

Now, the fussing.  Belkin does not guarantee that you can bridge
to Access Points made by other manufacturers, and, from my
experience, that does not seem to be mere legal tush covering.
However, because they're so inexpensive (WalMart actually
had them on special) I simply got an extra one to plug onto the
existing gateway my ISP supplied.  With its little conjoined twin
sitting beside it, it is now perfectly Belkin compatible.
Other than that, you just have to be mindful to follow the
instructions carefully when setting up the wireless bridging.
Other generic wifi issues of course apply, like watching out for
interference from other 2.4G devices (phones, security cams,
etc).  I set the "conjoined twin" (aka Belkin bridging server)
to its own channel, separate from the wifi built into the gateway,
because other users in my house still use the original AP, and
this way I'm not taking wifi bandwidth from it.

Now the good stuff.  No drivers are required.  Your OS sees only
the good old RJ ether port.  Furthermore, the setup for the little
external box is done from your web browser, again, no software
required.  There is no place for incompatibility in this strategy.
If you have an RJ port, and a web browser, you're singin'.

What do I know, maybe there are more sensible solutions,
but this works great for me, and these little Belkins are cheap!

Here's hoping I've saved someone some wifi grief.

regards,
Swampy.

This is a great solution for a desktop and not so good for a laptop.
