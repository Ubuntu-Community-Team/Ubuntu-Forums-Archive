### Post by gunghang on 2010-10-21
Linux Mint Isadora connected to the net through my Sierra Compass 885 without any special downloads or geeking (no offense to anybody intended).

I bought the AT&T version of the Sierra 885 Compass in Bangkok.

After installing the software on my old iMac and different Windows OS on different computers, and after struggling for months to get this damn thing working on Windows XP & 7 (any Windows users are advised to download Wireless Watcher from Sierra), I tried it on Linux Mint 9.  I went to System Tools - Preferences - Network Connections.  I added a wireless connection.  Pop up menus appeared, and I entered things like "Thailand" and "Truemove" and that was that.  I connected automatically.  My jaw is still in my lap.

Linux so totally f***ing rocks!  I mean, holy turd, dudes, it took about a minute to set up, not half an hour.  It connects in a few seconds, not a minute or two.

I'll try it on Xubuntu Karmic and Lubuntu Lynx and gOS Gadgets (Hardy) and gOS Space (Gutsy) and Kubuntu and SUSE.  (Between my desktop at home, my mobile netbook, and my notebook at work, all of which have mulitple boots, I've racked up a few systems.)

[Linux has shamed Microsoft before.  Connecting to the printer at the office took a little doing on XP & Vista.  On Xubuntu, I noticed the printer was on the print menu.  Xubuntu had taken the intiative and sniffed the printer out by itself and son-of-a-gun-George connected.]

For those going through the anguish I went through when I first heard of "Ubuntu" and installed Hardy Heron through Wubi and began my journey down the rabbit hole:  There are moments that make it all totally worth the pain.

Unrelated:  A co-worker once thought back to when he'd go to a music store and buy music vs. downloading it.  I wonder if the same thing will have happened to operating systems within a few years.  I bought my last couple of computers without any OS at all.  They all run Linux.  I have run Linux both as a sole OS and as part of a multi-boot machine.  I bought my first computer that came with Linux (Lindows) in Chiang Mai six years ago.  Microsoft is charging money and actively policing unauthorized distribution.  Linux is free and encourages distribution.

My guess is that within three to five years, the vast majority of users will be running open source software.  Any thoughts from anybody else?
