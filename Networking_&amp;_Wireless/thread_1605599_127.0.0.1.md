### Post by zzantozz on 2010-10-25
It's not actually only for internal use. 127.0.0.1, alias "localhost" in every major consumer OS, is a way of naming your own computer when entering some kind of network address. For instance, if you were running a web server, you could go to [http://localhost/index.html](http://localhost/index.html) or [http://127.0.0.1/index.html](http://127.0.0.1/index.html) instead of needing to know the real ip of your box. As denti mentioned, it's known as the "loopback".

lol. After all that...

All 127.*.*.* addresses are loopbacks. 127.0.0.1 is just the de facto standard for *the* loopback.

Per [http://www.iana.org/assignments/ipv4-address-space/ipv4-address-space.xhtml:](http://www.iana.org/assignments/ipv4-address-space/ipv4-address-space.xhtml:)
```
Prefix   Designation      Date     Status
127/8  	 IANA - Loopback  1981-09  RESERVED [5]
```

and:
[5] 127.0.0.0/8 is reserved for Loopback [RFC5735]

From [http://bugs.debian.org/cgi-bin/bugreport.cgi?bug=316099](http://bugs.debian.org/cgi-bin/bugreport.cgi?bug=316099), it looks like Debian--and probably Ubuntu, therefore--once upon a time used 127.0.1.1 to avoid adding the canonical hostname as an alias of "localhost".
