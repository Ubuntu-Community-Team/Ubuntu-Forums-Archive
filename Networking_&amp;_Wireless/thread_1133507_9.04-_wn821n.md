However, the signal is crap.  I have a dual boot, and I get high signal in Vista, no problems at all.  When I switch to Ubuntu, I barely get a bar, and my wireless router is only 10ft away.  For some odd reason, I actually get another router sitting right next to it at almost twice the strength, and a neighbor's router as about the same strength.

I popped in a Hawking USB wireless card (comes up as a Ralink Technology RT2501USB), and I easily get three bars to the exact same router.  I click over my wireless, and side by side, drastically different signal strengths.  I can't remember the command, but I even forced the throughput to 100Mb/s, and that did nothing.

The connection is pure sludge, too.  On the USB, smooth and fine.  On the built in, almost unusable.  If I take the laptop outside, it can easily die (which doesn't happen in Vista).  If I suspend, when I come out of suspend, it doesn't want to reconnect if I'm in any other room, even though it recognizes the router.

I've went wardriving, and I got plenty of hits, but not near what it should have.

I don't know what driver setup this uses, but I would put the out of the box in the "Unsupported" category.  It's just a nightmare to use, and you'd be better off walking around with a 50ft Cat-5 cable.  Luckily, I have plenty of these USB cards laying around.  And hey, if you like doing crazy stuff like me, having an external antenna that you can swap out for the old pringle's can setup is very fun (especially if you have mount it to a TV tower with motor rotation... I can easily pick up signals half a mile away or more.)

If you find an answer, let me know.  USB cards tend to hit doorways while walking around, breaking them.

### Post by GraniteGopher on 2009-04-28
Unfortunately, Linux has made great strides, which masks the fact that manufacturers still don't pay any respect to Linux drivers.  NVidia is a shining example of throwing linux a bone and supporting.  It wasn't too many releases ago that getting an old video card setup was also a nightmare.

The problem here, I would guess, is the chipset manufacturer.  Nobody can force them to release their drivers as open source, nor make them write binary drivers if they want to keep them closed source.  So, Linux users are forced to either use a wrapper, or developpers have to shoot in the dark and guess how it works.  Both ways end up with problems like this.

On the upside, Linux is pushing to support new chipsets that will make wifi less and less important.  This includes bluetooth for tethering to a smartphone, direct 3G (and 4G) mobile internet support, and support for upcoming WiMax chipsets.

I personally favor WiMax.  It blows away 3G for speed, and doesn't have the bandwidth limits, which is a problem for smartphone tethering as well.  I have a WiMax router, and have to plug in via cat-5 since I don't have a card or built in chipset. (Of course, for extended periods, I just plug in a wireless router to the WiMax router, but then I run into the problem with the RTL driver.)

The only real suggestion I can give you is for you to look for a good USB wifi card.  I remember about four years ago when I had a PCMCIA wireless 802.11b card that was completely unsupported by RedHat. (Wifi support back then was FAR worse than today.)  I had to spend $150 to buy a wireless bridge (an instant cat-5-to-wifi device... plugging a cat-5 into my laptop, and the other end into the bridge, which acted as a stand alone wireless device that connected my cat-5 to the hotel's hotspot) just so I could get a connection.

Now THAT was ridiculous.  Luckily, in the four years since then, I've found the wireless bridge to be an invaluable tool.  It's great for linking two wifi routers together over very large distances, and they are great for plugging in to DVR's that support cat-5, but not wifi.

Don't give up on Linux.  Do like I did, and go with a dual-boot setup.  There's really no good reason to delete Windows with how cheap drive space is now.  If nothing else, use a linux partition backup tool (usually part of bootable emergency recovery CDs) and archive your Windows installation to DVDs.  Just remember to deactivate the pagefile, uninstall any programled that can be downloaded and reinstal, defrag, and shrink the NTFS volume as small as possible.

The great thing about Linux is that it is always improving, and you can run it without it being perfect.  When you want to have some real fun, play with dual boot drive encryption.  Though that may get you into a bit of trouble in China, I don't know.  I don't want to be responsible for you being arrested.

Sorry I couldn't help more, and was is a bit off topic.  Just wanted to stress, don't give up!

Cheers.
