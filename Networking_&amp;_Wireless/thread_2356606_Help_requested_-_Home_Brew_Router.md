### Post by TheFu on 2017-03-25
I wouldn't run webmin on a router. That is just providing 20 more attack vectors.  There are different opinions on that.

Good idea to run a constantly patched OS for your router. That's a big issue with commercial routers - they aren't patched and usually vendors / after-market firmware updates don't happen often enough, if ever.  I'd NOT use wifi in a router. 

A separate WAP that can be used where is it needed might be worth considering.  Wifi RF interferes with computer components and the router isn't usually where the wifi antenna is needed anyway. Plus using a WAP removes all the funky wifi issues from your router.  Been using different Ubiquiti stuff here and at client locations for a long time. They are good AND cheap for the money. I'm addicted to PoE - lets me place the wifi antenna where it is needed without anything more than a CAT5e ethernet cable.

Ok - so when it comes to the router itself, most people would get an x86 Linux router distro that is maintained all the time - smoothwall or ipcop.  Another option is to follow the guide here: [https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2016/04/the-ars-guide-to-building-a-linux-router-from-scratch/](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2016/04/the-ars-guide-to-building-a-linux-router-from-scratch/)  and a follow up article: [https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2016/09/the-router-rumble-ars-diy-build-faces-better-tests-tougher-competition/](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2016/09/the-router-rumble-ars-diy-build-faces-better-tests-tougher-competition/)

I'm running pfSense on an [APU2c4]("https://www.pcengines.ch/apu2c4.htm") box that was under $150 as my router.  I expect it to last at least 10 yrs, so for $15 a year, I get a fast, silent, capable, x64, router with medium-sized business capabilities. After enabling all the HW offloading to the intel NICs, never been happier. Without that offload, performance was 1/3rd slower.
