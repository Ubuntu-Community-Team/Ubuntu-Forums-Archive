### Post by Mr. C. on 2007-03-11
That is a lot of wind! :-)

Your question - what modem will perform best?

Answer: get a non-winmodem (eg. true hardware-based) modem.

Today, there are two types of modems typically.  Those that do their stuff in hardware, and those that rely on a proprietary software-based solution called "winmodem".  By all means, get a hardware-based model.

It will be difficult for anyone to recommend a brand / model do you, as they vary country.

Any true hardware-based modem should suffice.  The poor connection you experienced was likely  either due to poor signal on your phone line.  If this is the case, there will not be much you can do to increase the modem's communication rate, short of rewiring your line, and asking your phone company to ensure the line from your house to their junction box is clean.  The line must be clean to obtain speeds higher than say 28 or 33k.

Having said that, some modems are much better than others in terms of their ability to deal with poor line quality.  Again, why don't you look around your online shopping sites to see what you can find, and then come back and ask for recommendations from such a list.

MrC

### Post by Mr. C. on 2007-03-20
Once you understand the basics of how serial ports and modems work, it won't feel terribly difficult.  My "simple" statement is in regards to communication with a serial modem.

Your first goal needs to be to open a connection to /dev/ttyS0 (or S1, ..., as the case may be), and see if you can manually issue commands.  I used to use simple software serial connections, and issued modem commands directly to see that the connection was responding (ATm ATDT, etc.).

Once that's done, then move onto getting the modem scripts to dial correctly.

After than, then focus on configuring the strings that are transferred and received.

More often than not, these GUI-front ends hide users from much of the complexity, which is great.  But they also create such a dependency on them, that users are terribly lost when problems occur, and this is more common than not.

MrC
