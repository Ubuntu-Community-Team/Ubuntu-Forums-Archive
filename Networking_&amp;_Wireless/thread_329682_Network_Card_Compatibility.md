Because many of us already have hardware, and we dont' really want to toss otherwise perfectly good equipment aside without giving it a fight. Furthermore, it is in the geek nature to make things work. While more vendors are making Linux drivers for their hardware, don't forget that Linux was born from a community of hobbyists, and much of that fire is still alive today. Many of us also realize that it's still not at the point where the proportion of market share using Linux is not large enough for a vendor boycott to make any difference. For every 1 person who doesn't buy something because it doesn't work in Linux, there are 1000 computer sold that include that device as an OEM option. Joe Sixpack doesn't know or care that it might not work with other OSes.

Having said that, I *don't* buy hardware that doesn't work under Linux. But when I first started using Linux, and even now, I sometimes have old hardware around that I want to get working.

Compatibility of devices in Linux isn't too dependent on the distribution. All a distribution is is a certain combination of course programs with different kinds of spit and polish, and different guarantees that the included components will work together. Of course, the level of support does vary from distribution to distribution. I've found Ubuntu to work impressively well with "magic black box" support... pretty much everything has just worked... 

As for a compatibility list, Ubuntu is based on Debian. As far as I know, there isn't an official compatibility list, just an amalgam of different experiences. Check out places like [http://www.linuxcompatible.org/compatibility.html](http://www.linuxcompatible.org/compatibility.html).
