### Post by effenberg0x0 on 2012-04-15
Most users that will join UbuntuForums in upcoming weeks are not regulars here. We must accept that they're non-technical users, unaware of forum etiquette or how to search for information and of everything that happened with the OS in latest versions. We won't change them and we can't blame them for being ordinary non-technical users, everyday people, the ones Ubuntu is targeted at. 

But, at any rate, we need an effective way to deal with them, a unified resource to easily direct them to. In this single thread, 10 days before the release, we're already seeing all kinds of uninformed opinions and criticism. Can you imagine what it would be like after Release Day?

Otherwise, release + 1 month will be difficult for us to manage here at UbuntuForums.

Having such resource would be much more useful to Ubuntu and its community than discussing pros and cons of innovation processes, the way they were handled, design choices, etc. We all had months to actively participate in the development discussion mailing lists and influence the OS current state. Some of us did, some did not. Some managed to influence it, some did not. It's useless to discuss what's good, what's bad, how it should have been done, if it's better or worse than previous versions or other OSs. It doesn't matter now. This is all in the past, it's done. We're looking into the future now.

We don't have much time (10 days) but, if we all join efforts, I'm sure we could do something very complete and helpful.

If anyone agrees and is willing to help with this task, get in touch and we'll make it happen.

### Post by Penguinnerd on 2012-04-15
As one of these LTS users who will be upgrading, I think I can suggest some things to have prepared. I'm a pretty technical user who always tries each release, despite having no intention of actually upgrading to them.

1) People with older hardware will complain about speed. Know when to suggest a switch to Xubuntu. Also remember that Xubuntu is only supported for 3 years instead of Ubuntu's 5 years.

Aside: Speaking of Xubuntu, people who are switching will want to know how to configure the panels. It's very different to configure from gnome 2.

2) Tell people that they can get an old-style desktop by installing gnome-panel from the software centre and changing the default session to Gnome Classic. This is good if the speed is a non-issue, and they just want it to look "normal". It won't have a significant speed improvement though.

3) People will want the window buttons moved to the right. Some people will be stubborn about this. I've been using clearlooks in 10.04 for 2 years now, and others will be upset that it is not available in 12.04. At this point, I've found several ways to do this, but I don't know what the best way is.

4) People will be confused by the way you find apps in Unity. They will need to know what applications are named so they can do a search for them. They might be used to just clicking the second icon under "graphics" without needing to know the name of the app.

5) Keep in mind that not everyone who ignores the forums for two years is non-technical. Some people don't use an OS as a hobby, they use it for work. It's not worth everyone's time to mess around in a web-forum every day. Some very technical users will be thrown off-balance by these changes. I'm one of them. Don't be condescending.

Most of all, remind people that 10.04 isn't dead yet!


@ xebian: We're talking about introducing them to 12.04 and Unity, not the process of installing 12.04. But I agree, a fresh install is better than an upgrade.

### Post by effenberg0x0 on 2012-04-15
Hi Guys, I think one thing that can help us is to look at latest threads in here and other forums you use, posted by people that already are updating. I did a quick thread digging. From what I could see so far:

- They don't understand what happened to the old UI;
- They don't understand why it changed;
- They don't understand that it is not mandatory to use the two new DEs (while they can use other tutorials to revert to the old DE at their own risk);
- They don't understand that there was a 12 months (at least) process of designing, redesigning, fixing, changing various aspects of Unity, in which everyone could have participated and that, in fact, a large number of community members posted to it and engaged into discussions about the changes everyday. They seem to think one individual solely designed it;
- They don't understand that Gnome3 is not designed by Ubuntu;
- They don't know what lightdm is and where is gdm;
- They don't know what the difference is between Unity, Unity-2D, Gnome3;
- They don't know how to use the new DEs (where are the programs, what are the names of the screen components - dash, launcher, indicators, global panel, lenses, etc);
- They don't know that they can change some launcher/dash settings using gnome-control-center ("Settings") or even MyUnity;
- They don't know what's software-center, where's synaptic, how to install programs;
- They don't know what happened to their beloved "cube" compiz plugin;
- They don't know what to do when a Unity session fails but 2D works (many, even having a decent VGA card, never installed proper drivers, relied on vesa). We need to provide some very basic explanation on hardware accelerated desktops, targeted at non-technical users (what they are), and point to tutorials on how to install adequate drivers for their VGAs;

These are just some examples I could find in a very quick search, but feel free to add whatever you can think off. Even if you can't write a complete and thorough explanation, that's OK, we can complete each others' work in upcoming days. Add questions even if you can't think of proper answers or don't have the time: others can write the answers and it already helps. 

We can take the next two or three days to research the forums, think of what topics you'll write about, there's no need to rush and send things immediately. With a small group of people helping, 10 days is more than enough.

As soon as I start receiving stuff, I'll create a draft Wiki page and add everything to it (plus what I'm writing), organize things the best I can and send you back the link to it. Even if it's not complete and perfect, we can improve it in the remaining 6 or 7 days. 

Thank you all for helping in this task. I think it can make a difference for some users.

Regards,
Effenberg

PS: Let's simply disregard comments about which DE is best or worse, what strategy would be ideal or not, criticisms, etc. This is not what this is about and it's a waste of time at this point. This is about helping non-technical users. Technical users are able to search information and fix most issues themselves. I'm assuming people are either willing to help users through this specific task or not willing to do it (which is fine). That's the only thing that matters.
