I don't really see why. I could create a patch that makes the menu bar hot pink. That doesn't mean the Unity developers are obligated to take it. They have a perfectly reasonable view of what left clicking on the launcher does: it makes things appear. That patch is completely functional, but it could muddle up the design.

Of course, I don't blame the person who made the patch: I don't think Canonical has done very well communicating what Unity is and where it is going. I like to point at [http://unity.ubuntu.com/](http://unity.ubuntu.com/) from time to time, because that's an example of Unity's outwards face. Yes, that screenshot is from 10.10 Netbook Edition. It isn't that I think Canonical doesn't care, but I think a lot of their people are so comfortable working internally that they forget the thing is (trying to be) a community project. I'm sure there's all kinds of activity inside Canonical, but from out here I see a woefully neglected website and a project that seems fine on its own.

### Post by prusswan on 2012-02-17
Because it was not the first time that a bug report resulted in a won't fix because of some subjective design decision (which is irrelevant to the troubled user), even as subjective as design decisions go. Unity and related issues (stemming from making that the one and only DE available on default install, and when the user is left vulnerable and dependent on that single launcher which may fail to start, the fault is deemed to lie squarely on the other components such as ccsm!) aside, another one I have seen is the 30 seconds delay on boot for network configuration [https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/upstart/+bug/886414](https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/upstart/+bug/886414). From the affected users' perspective, not only did they fail to fix some of the issues since 10.04, they managed to make something working well, worse (and even without that issue, 12.04 boot is much slower but that's another story).

All in all, given such treatment some users are just not going to take this bug reporting thing as seriously as before, or maybe some of us are no longer the target audience anymore.
