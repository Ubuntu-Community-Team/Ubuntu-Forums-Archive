### Post by smc170 on 2012-03-07
So a few days ago, I downloaded the first beta of the much anticipated Ubuntu 12.04 Long Term Support release. So, does it ail my quest to find the perfect distro? Read more to find out!


First off, if you want to follow along, you can upgrade easily by pressing Alt+F2, and then typing "update-manager -d", without the quotes, obviously. So, if you're up for the beta challenge, go ahead and update!



As you can see, the desktop doesn't look a whole lot different. In fact, you really cant tell the difference unless you start using it, or if you look in the settings. So it's kind of nice to have a nice, familiar desktop that won't exactly rock your world. The top bar is all the same, but features a preview-effect so new users will be able to notice that the global menus and buttons are pleasant.

Starting with the dash, it looks virtually identical to 11.10, but, that's before you look at the new settings, which brings some much needed features to the new LTS release.


Along with the new launcher adjustment slider, the dock now attempts to match the general color theme of its wallpaper. For example, if I have a wallpaper with a good amount of green present, the dock turns a greenish hue to match the background. This feature is great with the included wallpapers, as they are all pre-loaded, but 3rd party pictures off Google Images are hit or miss. Sometimes they will be very accurate, but at the same time, the hue the dock selects can be very different from the overall picture color.

Another major feature of the dock, and arguably the most drastic, is the fact that by default, the dock will not auto-hide, nor hide at all for that matter. It remains static, even when you maximize, lets say, a Firefox window. It acts like an extended border of the screen, not really allowing to go past the edge when expanded. While it's an arguable feature, I love it. I think the Ubuntu team really knows what they're doing with this feature. I find that I'm no longer waiting on the dock to reveal, and that it's now quick and easy to locate, use, and I find myself being much more efficient in my general desktop use.


No odd sizing!

It really is a beautiful setup, not scaling websites oddly, but keeping it nice and pleasing, but if you really hate the sticky dock, you can enable auto hide, but it seems to be very buggy.

12.04 includes new privacy settings, allowing you to choose which programs have "access" to your information, and how they can use it. Relatively old news in the tech world.

The dash replaces the obnoxious big buttons, for a recently used bar that shows you, well, all of your recently used applications, which is much more helpful than the old buttons. The dash also features a new Video lens which allows you to search for videos both on-board, and on the web from YouTube, Bing, and Vimeo. The video search function is nice, and works decently when searching for relatively general videos. When it comes to specifics, though, the lens has a harder time finding related videos, and appears to sometimes guess.



The dash is relatively the same, and works as before. But a new feature that could potentially set Ubuntu apart is the HUD. The HUD is what the Ubuntu team believes to be the innovation in the way we use computers. You basically press Alt, and then type the command you desire to do. I haven't used it a whole lot, and to be honest, I don't really plan to. It's nice for people who want to be able to do 90% of their actions using the keyboard only, which is good for hackers and terminal users who are generally shortcut-savvy.

I have to admit, when I did learn a few commands, it felt pretty darn awesome to be able to do a specific task within seconds, without even having to open the dedicated application. For example, I use the command above probably most of the time, so I just open the HUD and type; "New Message" which brings me up the option to instantly create a new Thunderbird message, taking out the middleman and just getting straight to the good stuff. While it may not "Change the way we use computers", It is definitely nice, and can be quite usable to someone who wants to learn the many command options.


Minor features, like a right click application menu just makes 12.04 that much more usable.


Ubuntu 12.04 brings a-lot of major innovations like the one mentioned above, but also features tons of little tweaks and features that would make this review just to boring :). The team definitely aims at stability and usability, which is good considering it's going to be a whopping 5 year LTS release, probably hinting businesses that use Ubuntu to upgrade. The distro is unusually stable, especially considering it's a beta release. There are a few little problems, but hey, it's a beta, and most of them are most likely to be fixed in the next few weeks.

12.04 brings a great supply of features, while still being stable and familiar to conventional 11.10 users, and at this moment, achieves the Electrons favorite distro award! Major props to the Ubuntu team for spitting out a fantastic update.
