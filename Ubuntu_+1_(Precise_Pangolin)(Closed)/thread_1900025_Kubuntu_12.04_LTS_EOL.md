> **Canonical ending support for Kubuntu, reassigning lead developer**
By Ryan Paul | Published a day ago

Jonathan Riddell, the lead developer of the Kubuntu project, announced today that his work on the KDE-based Ubuntu variant will no longer be funded by Canonical after the upcoming 12.04 release. Kubuntu will be developed entirely by volunteers, much like other community-maintained variants of Ubuntu.

Riddell will continue to be employed by Canonical, but working on Kubuntu will be confined to his free time. In order for the Kubuntu project to continue operating, Riddell says that community members will have to take a more active role in doing unpopular tasks such as ISO testing.

"The practical changes are I won't be able to work on KDE bits in my work time after 12.04 and there won't be paid support for versions after 12.04," he wrote. "This is a rational business decision, Kubuntu has not been a business success after 7 years of trying, and it is unrealistic to expect it to continue to have financial resources put into it."

Riddell and the Kubuntu team have done extraordinary work over the years to make Kubuntu a competitive Linux distribution. The quality and maturity of the distro have risen sharply over the past few years—it now rivals the best KDE distributions and has displaced openSUSE as our preferred environment for KDE testing. Although Kubuntu has managed to attract an audience, it has never been a commercially successful product for Canonical.

KDE's underlying Qt toolkit was recently added to the default Ubuntu installation. There are a lot of areas where Qt's integration with the rest of the Ubuntu environment could potentially be improved. It makes sense for Canonical to work on making Qt a first-class citizen in Ubuntu rather than funding a KDE variant of the distribution.

Kubuntu users who want to join the development effort and participate in testing and maintaining the distribution can learn more about how to get involved at the project's website.

> **keithpeter said:**
> What does 'iso testing' involve? I ought to do some of that for Ubuntu, at present I'm just doing normal tasks and checking anything odd as a possible bug.
I was going to give you the link that Cheesemill beat me too.

It does not involve reporting bugs anywhere but to launchpad.  You do need to file a bug with launchpad before giving a failing mark to an ISO and include the bug number on the ISO testing report.

This is good sense.  It allows the the QA folks to put an ISO testing team endorsement on the bug report.  Bug reports with that endorsement get a LOT  more attention than they would otherwise.

What you are doing is joining the ISO testing team.  This involves joining their mailing list.  This is not a list that is very active so you will not have a flooded in box.  Usually all you get is notices of builds to test.  There may be one there may be many for any given ISO.

There is an ISO for every family member (usually and always at the final release).  You can test one or as many as you want.

For Ubuntu you usually have the Alt install ISO and the LiveCD ISO to choose from.

There is a well defined bunch of tests that need done for each.  Each test has a page explaining what they want done.

I always tested the Live CD for the Live Session and Manual Installation.  I also did the OEM Installation on the RC and Final Release ISOs.

If you have a MS install you can test the the auto next to install.  There is the use the whole disk install.

Not sure that is the whole list of possibilities but it is most of them.

All these tests are important.  The more folks that test the more real hardware is tested.  As anyone that has actually installed a dev release knows any given upgrade can work fine for most folks and fail completely with one type of hardware.  So too can an ISO.  If only 5 people are testing an ISO there is a lot of hardware that is not being tested.

If an ISO for the Final does not work on your hardware you have two choices.  Bitch about the lack of love from Ubuntu/Canonical OR blame the real responsible person which is YOU for not doing ISO testing.

That same blame attaches to anyone that has not installed and tested the dev release if, when installed the new release does not work on your hardware.

That is the long and the short of it.
