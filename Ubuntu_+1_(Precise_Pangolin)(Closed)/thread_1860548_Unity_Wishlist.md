### Post by Bobboau on 2011-10-14
Unity is coming along nicely, but it still has a lot of ways in which  it needs improvement. what are some of the things that you think need to be done to make it the best interface the universe will ever know.

For me the biggest problem I have with unity by far comes in the form of task switching and workspace switching, both of these feel clumsy to me. for instance thanks to grouping I have to click three times to get to the important firefox window I want, once to select the group (which will open something useless like the downloads window) again to initiate the 3d window picker, and then from there I need to look and visually identify which one I want and click on it again. I would MUCH rather just have half a dozen firefox (or whatever) icons and be able to just click on the one I want.

Another thing I would like to see is official support for the desktop cube, it seems to mostly work already, so this should be an easy win, all you need is better integration of activating it (workspace switcher, actually middle clicking on the launcher  would be my preference). the consistent problem with all implementations of the cube have been how to activate it and the best solution I have found is 'go to the edge of the screen and middle click'. with the cube in this setup all you have to to is move your mouse to a screen edge, hold the middle mouse button down and move around to see which workspace you want, let go when you find it.

More compiz plugin support (the extras package, or some subset of it, would be nice). It would also be nice if you could grace us with different wall papers on different workspaces, I have waited SO MANY years for this, the gnome people always blew it off.

pie in the sky wish; widgets. real ones. KDE's plasma widgets are a great example of what I would like to see.

### Post by cuppsy on 2011-10-15
Still getting my feet wet with 11.10. I'll say Unity seems more solid, and I dig the changes from 11.04. Going forward most of my wishes aren't with Unity itself, but the pieces surrounding it. I want 12.04 to be mostly focused on stabilizing the current setup, which I'm guessing it will be. The second biggest change I want is GNOME3 is get customization back into the fold.

Just looking at the shift from 11.04 to 11.10 (GNOME 2 to 3), I've lost the ability to load the launcher from the top corner (instead of hammering the left side futilely). I also seem to have far fewer options within Appearance (why can't I fully tweak my theme like with 2.32, or adjust my wallpaper, etc.).

Beyond GNOME's tweaks, while I dig Unity (am likely in the minority), I wanna seem them focus 12.04 less on adding more features (the ones in 11.10 I think shore up a lot of my issues with 11.04) and more on allowing people to use Unity how they want (min/max buttons, the way the global menu is displayed, adding a traditional menu or other items to the top bar, things like that).

Beyond that, I wanna see them work with other developers in getting as much software as possible working perfectly with the global menu and launcher. Most notably is WINE, which I know is likely a nightmare, but I run SQLyog fairly regularly and can't launch it from my launcher and it's wonky at best with the global menu bar (double title bars or a white stripe on the bottom of the screen).

And lastly, keep going with the Messaging menu. I know people rip on Unity for slowing down productivity, and I can see that argument, but being able to check IMs, see an e-mail, change settings and do lots of other little tasks from my Messaging menu instead of moving away from my work is nice. Let's add in the GNOME3 ability to respond to an IM (or an e-mail even) from that menu without leaving my current workspace. That'd be schweet.

### Post by WorLord on 2011-10-19
> **rg4w said:**
> Is [the rationale behind the inability to move the Launcher] explained anywhere online? Ayatana, LaunchPad, etc.?

Actually, yes.  I was following Ayatana for a while, and saw mention of some of the usability studies and such.  

The reason the Launcher is on one of the sides of the screen is simple: vertical screen real estate.

More and more screens are 16:9 or 16:10 as opposed to 4:3.  Which means, even if the physical size (which is measured diagonally) gets bigger, vertical screen real-estate either remains the same or (usually) shrinks.  

A decade ago, I had a 1600x1200 17" CRT.  I now have a 22" flat panel at 1680x1050.  I have 150 *fewer* pixels than I had a *decade* ago.  In very practical terms, this translates into "fewer lines of text and code I can see at one time without scrolling," and since I'm a dev, that gets really annoying.  (My laptop is worse: 17" screen, 1600X900.  Previously, I had a 15" screen, 1600x1200.).

Taking away another 48px, on top of the 24px for the panel... well, that's REALLY no good.  On the other hand, I seem to have horizontal pixels to spare, so the left or right hand side of the screen would would be good. 

Now: why is it on the left side of the screen?  That answer is rather obvious and was borne out by testing: every window's scrollbar is on the right.  People complain about trying to hit a browser's back button and accidentally summoning the Launcher?  Just wait until they summon the launcher every time they try to scroll up or down.  

Compound this with English and pals being top to bottom, left to right languages - placing more importance on the top and left sides of any given reading surface - and the decision was pretty easy to arrive at.  I arrived at many of the same decisions myself - the first thing I did in any given Gnome 2-based setup: remove the bottom panel, install Docky, and attach it to the left side of the screen.   (This actually paid dividends, because Unity's Launcher is written by the same guy who wrote Docky, and updating to 11.10 imported all of my shortcuts from Docky into Unity's Launcher.  Truly, a nice touch.  :-)

It's like someone putting child-locks on your kitchen cupboards. If  you're not a child, it can be seen as offensively patronising. And while  it's one thing to be patronised by someone who's genuinely a design  genius, in this case, the people responsible for Unity clearly don't  fall into that category. Painfully so.

A third reason why Unity might be seen as "dumbing-down" is that its  interface redesign is supposedly optimised for 16:9 movie screens, and  for small-screen devices. There's been a big shift in emphasis in the  industry with the success of the iPad to considering small devices that  let you watch movies and be a more passive consumer of content, and the  Unity redesign seems to be consciously targetting that market. So if you  use your PC for actually creating content or doing serious work, and  you've invested in a large screen, it might slightly niggle you to find  that your workspace has now apparently been redesigned with big buttons  and small-screen optimisations for people who want to buy a shiny piece  of consumer bling to use as a leisure device. 
And the fact that there's no standard inbuilt customisation controls to  turn the desktop back to a more conventional layout suggests that the  design brief for Unity was maybe to target the consumer electronics  market at the expense of desktop users. Maybe Canonical reckoned that  there was more money to be had by turning the OS into something that  could be licensed to tablet and TV manufacturers. 

Anyhow, a lot of desktop users basically thought that Canonical was  giving them the finger, and was letting them know that they weren't  considered to be particularly important any more. Canonical gave the  impression that the new plan was to ignore boring old PC users and  people with old hardware, and chase after the Apple-user demographic and  the people who buy the very latest consumer electronics, because that's  where there was a higher concentration of disposable income, and more  opportunities for a company like Canonical to get a slice of the pie. If  you can tell a tablet manufacturer that your userbase is  demographically the same as people who buy iPads -- that it attracts the  Apple crowd -- then that tablet manufacturer is more likely to pay you  money for permission to put your OS on their machines.   

So that's the suspicion that people have about what's happening -- that  Ubuntu is being converted as far as possible into an Apple-clone  consumer electronics OS, 'cos that's where the obvious money seems to  be.

On the bright side, if that IS the plan, they seem to be making a right  mess of it, so if the Ubuntu userbase isn't completely fragmented in a  year's time, maybe they'll have to start making more conciliatory  gestures to more traditional PC users. Right now, their userbase seems  to be fleeing in all directions to alternative distros and alternative  desktops. A couple of years ago, most people who used Ubuntu seemed to  be fairly happy with the default desktop, now most of the people who  defend the Ubuntu project most strongly seem to be saying that it's  great ... if you use a third-party desktop, or one of the alternative  distros, or you download special tools to let you undo the changes made  by the last couple of releases. 
Unity appears to have fractured the Ubuntu userbase, as different groups  skeet off in different directions to find different workarounds to the  new interface's perceived failures. 

The irony is, that in trying to make Ubuntu more attractive to people  who don't like to tinker -- and doing it badly -- Unity seems to have  turned Ubuntu from a successful, stable, reliable, "inclusive" OS that  you could recommend to anyone and that "just worked", out of the box,  into an OS that you only consider mentioning to people who are prepared  to spend some time tinkering and checking out all the "scary" aspects of  Linux like distros and alternative desktops, that most "consumer" users  run a mile from.
