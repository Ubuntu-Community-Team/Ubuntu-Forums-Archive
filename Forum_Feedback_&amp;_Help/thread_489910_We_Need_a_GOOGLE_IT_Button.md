### Post by sw1995 on 2007-07-01
Why assume that new forum members are not searching in the first place? I guess if more users prefaced their comments with "Hey, I didn't do squat to answer this for myself, but here goes nothing!" then maybe I would see a need to be a bit frustrated. However, the ability of the Ubuntu community to suspend ego in favor of community is what makes these forums great: unconditional support.

I suspect that even though many new users understand how to search for answers, the instinct is to feel as though each problem comes with a different set of circumstances that are best dealt with from square one.

Let's say I am a new user and I am having trouble getting my WIFI set up. I search through the forums, but more often than not, every WIFI problem is A LITTLE different than mine and the solutions I read are directed towards the specific idiosyncrasies of said problem (let's say I am using an old Dell laptop, however the solution I found was directed towards a Gateway desktop...this little difference may be just enough to trigger uncertainty for the new Linux user). Why not assume that new members are doing this more out of courtesy than laziness, as in i.e. "I know I am going to need help at some point, so why not now before I make a complete mess of my system later and take up even MORE of your time?" If these little issues are fixed early on, then it makes the assimilation into the higher ideals and practices of Linux much more feasible. It seems to me that it is all about "jump starting" the new Linux user.

I think jumping to conclusions and regarding this behavior as lazy or inconsiderate runs completely counter to the ideals of the ubuntu forums. I would even say the tone of voice implied by a "GOOGLE IT" button versus, for example, a much more inviting and inclusive statement like "Google It!" is more up to speed with Ubuntu's positive mindset.

"GOOGLE IT" is intimidating and bossy.
