### Post by aysiu on 2007-04-26
> **hardyn said:**
> Interesting... please share?

Thanks.
Well, people feel the need to make known that Linux isn't perfect. It means, first of all, that they were under the impression Linux *was* perfect--probably from one of those "tired of spyware? try Linux!" articles. It also means they think posting a thread about it actually will *do* something to change Linux (make it perfect?).

The rants usually start out something like "I've been a ______ for 30 years and have worked on ____, _____, and _____." The first blank is *programmer*, *system administrator*, whatever lofty title you want. You can imagine the rest of the blanks. This is somehow supposed to make us think that if so-and-so is so brilliant and can't figure it out, **no one** can install and configure a Linux desktop distro. It's also supposed to mean "logically" (but erroneously) that anyone with less technology education would have an even more difficult time with the installation.

It never occurs to this person to research hardware compatibility, to ask what practical steps (not a rant) she can take to make Linux better, or to listen to what experienced users have to say. It also never occurs to the OP that she may not be the first person to have posted almost that exact same rant.

The other interesting part, from a sociological perspective, is that Linux users get defensive to the point of being personally emotionally agitated--as if attacking Linux is attacking one's family or one's home. Maybe some people think of their operating systems (or kernels) that way, but it's a little strange.

The funniest part for me is that I'm able to now take a step back from this and look it at it relatively objectively. But at one point or another, I've been on both sides (ranting and replying emotionally). If you want to see my rant and my own reply to it a year later (quite entertaining reading), check [this](http://www.psychocats.net/essays/linuxvwindows) out.

There are simple ways to stop these rants, but efficacy demands that everyone involved with desktop Linux adopt these strategies (i.e., not going to happen, so the rants will continue to appear):
1. Stop claiming desktop Linux is perfect. Stop making it sound like magic. Stop making it sound as if desktop Linux is a drop-in replacement for Windows or "Windows without all the problems."
2. When someone complains, try to redirect that energy to something positively productive: donating money, contributing code, making artwork, filing bug reports, creating documentation, working on marketing efforts, etc. Rants don't make desktop Linux better.

But the cycle will keep repeating itself, despite our efforts to make the above two strategies more popular.
