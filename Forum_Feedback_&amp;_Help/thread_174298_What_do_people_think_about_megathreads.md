### Post by 23meg on 2006-05-11
I voted against.

In most cases threads that are candidates for merging into megathreads *are* megathreads in themselves; they're usually too big to read from end to end. The signal to noise ratio is already low in them, and merging them would make it even lower, making it even more difficult to pick up some signal. People often post to big threads without reading what's already been said, and the discussion branches off too much to be of any possible value. 

Everyone claiming Linux "isn't ready for the desktop" or Epiphany is "better than Firefox" or "OMG GNOME SUXX0RZ KDE R0X0RZ!!!"  should make their case with a *first post* which should constitute a starting point and general center of gravity for the oncoming discussion. This will encourage people to make a case in a compelling, coherent manner. Forcing everyone into one big pool of posts that nobody properly reads will do the opposite.

### Post by 23meg on 2006-05-11
They don't *have to* read all the posts, in either case, but if they have the chance to make a starting post, to have their own thread, I feel they'd be in a position to make their case in a more original manner and there would be a slim chance that they'd bring a new perspective into a debate that has taken place hundreds of times. If they're poured without question into one big pool, it would be like treating that place like a junkyard where only the "meaningless debate" takes place. I'm not going to argue that "Gnome vs. KDE" can ever be a useful debate, but I'm against having rules that imply the opposite.

### Post by 23meg on 2006-06-26
I've made a 180 degree turn on this and I want megathreads. Reasons why alternatives aren't effective:
[B]
- Not having megathreads and keeping things as they are:[/B] This way all the forum indexes get littered with unnecessary thread titles which take up space that genuine, needed threads should occupy. Think especially the "New Posts", "Today's Posts" etc. queries, which many people who visit regularly perhaps use more often than individual forum sections; in these, titles from all forum sections come up, so a "Knome vs. GDE" thread in Ubuntu Cafe can push down someone's obscure video driver issue thread and make it go unnoticed. In a sense (an admittedly exaggerated one but you get the idea) not having megathreads will turn all the forums into a huge megathread. 

**- Dedicated "dead horse beating" subforums:** Ignorant users who open threads in wrong sections, don't do searches etc. don't care much about where they post something and in many cases won't even notice the existence of such a section; they'll just go ahead and start a new thread in Ubuntu Cafe or Absolute Beginner Talk. With megathreads they can be policed quickly and efficiently. 

Also I believe such forum sections won't look good in an official distro forum site. Giving a first time visitor the impression of "we discuss Gnome vs. KDE here, and root vs. sudo here, and here's where we bash Microsoft" etc. on the main page isn't good PR.

**- Deleting old threads: **Will cause moderation overhead and some unnecessary debate over "why was my thread deleted". Plus there are too many threads, and a few valuable posts in them that will be wasted.

I'd like to hear what the admins think of this.

> Megathreads are controversial, but there is no added benefit (in regards to KDE v. Gnome) to having fifty separate threads with hundreds of posts each over having one thread with thousands of posts.

I disagree. Megathreading stifles nuances and lumps things into the moderator's, rather than the user's, value-judgements. Seeing as this forum's software is already linear, rather than branching, many similar, nuanced threads is the only reasonable way to go.

The topic of KDE vs GNOME vs Xfce is one that's generally admitted to be one of personal preference. Yes, of course you're always going to get tedious discussions along the lines of:

[FONT=Courier New]GNOME fanboy: KDE sucks and is ugly bloatware
KDE fanboy: GNOME sucks and I've got memory usage stats to prove it! And BTW God says you're an idiot.
Xfce fanboy: What about meeeeeee?
GNOME and KDE fanboys in chorus: You suck too!
[/FONT]
Such yah-boo discussion will then carry on until everyone is so sick and tired of it that they stop posting to it (i.e. ad nauseam rather than ad infinitum). It will die a natural death. Moderation of such nonsense really requires a light touch: kill ad hominem attacks and potentially libellous statements, and otherwise laissez faire will work. It will be obvious to posterity that such a thread's ignominious death will have been well-deserved. And who knows? Someone might find a use for something in it some day.

I can see the temptation to consolidation of the numerous tripe threads into one graveyard megathread, but I feel that this is misguided for a variety of reasons:

1) The value-judgment of what constitutes tripe is not infallible.
2)[/FONT][FONT=Franklin Gothic Medium] The value-judgment of what constitutes tripe is not generalizable.
3) Disturbing a graveyard has a nasty tendency to wake up the zombies, especially if you put a live discussion in there.
4) It prevents/slows real tripe from sinking to the bottom, as it naturally would.
[/FONT][FONT=Franklin Gothic Medium]
> No beginner is going to read all fifty separate threads or one merged thread.

No-one with a life is going to read the entire site. They are going to use the search function, which returns a list of threads, as it should. Once some megathread turns up in the search results, one is expected to find a second search function that is not located in the search menu, and repeat your search for every such megathread. Welcome to UI hell.

More fundamentally, the logic:

1) There are fifty threads combined into this megathread
2) Your search has returned a megathread
3) Therefore your search would have returned all fifty threads

is fallacious in the extreme. If anything, in the unlikely event that someone's searched for something so generic that it brings up hundreds of results, it's quite an effective error message.

> But the separate threads give the illusion that the discussion is a new one.

As far as at least one person (i.e. the OP) is concerned, it is. Yes, FAQs do get annoying, but if you don't have anything constructive to say on them, it's best simply not to click on them. And, yes, he may be mistaken, but this presumption is dangerous.

Moderator-enforced necroposting serves only to antagonize the newbies in such a way that makes them bound to post flame-bait. (IMHO, *any* necroposting needs to be done with the most extreme caution.) And quite frankly, what is a newbie to make of Linux geeks if he is immediately redirected to a flame-fest of material that is largely tripe, and most of the remainder getting progressively stale and less relevant?

> The merged thread also allows people an *easy**avoid*
