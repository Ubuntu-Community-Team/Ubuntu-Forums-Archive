Without addressing every single thing you've mentioned so far in the thread (I have to get back to work soon), I do have some observations. 

There are indicators in your post and other threads that raise some flags. This concern is coming from someone with years in the IT and tech support and services realm (which, by the way, is more pertinent to any support conversation here than age or gender) ;), and having come from the position of training others on how to do support.

First off, you admittedly lack experience, which in itself is not necessarily a bad thing. Everyone starts somewhere when they help, and the best way is to jump into a problem that you, yourself is comfortable with and work into the more difficult problems as you gain experience. This is how it should work for the most part. As for unanswered threads, instead of responding to them solely to respond, it's better to respond if you are comfortable that you can actually help. Sometimes you do need to search along the way as what looks simple on the surface is actually more involved as you get into it, but that's not the correct starting approach. The goal is "point A to B", but if you just start dumping in other letters for the sake of having an answer, you will probably end up with a jumble of alphabet soup that makes it more difficult for others to backtrack on. 

As far as having "better" suggestions than anyone else responding to the thread at that point. That's actually a fairly tough judgement call. Some problems have a cut and dried solution, but there are *so* many ways to do things many times the best thing to do is let the thread run it's course unless you are *positive* your answer is the right one. Just because someone may be using a different path to the resolution, it doesn't mean it's the wrong path. 

One thing I don't think I've mentioned on this forum yet is that in my own experience, I've seen something very interesting when it comes to working with some people who are just learning or coming to a forum for assistance. There are people who copy, paste or enter commands, etc. sequentially, even if the answers are coming from different responders. Which is fine if the thread is on a linear track, but if even more people jump in with an alternate route, the OP can end up with a rather large mess and no one can be really sure how they got there. This is not meant to be alarming or discouraging (I tend to be encouraging and promoting), it is just something to keep in mind while responding to threads. 

Switching gears, to echo what others here are saying, yes you can be entirely friendly and helpful while at the same time not knowing personal information with the exception of where their current experience level may be. If this concept is troublesome to you, (again, I am not trying to be discouraging) it is something you will have to work out for yourself (and not try to have others work around you). If you feel that something to "lighten the mood" may be jocular humor or along those lines, this may not be the right place for you as it (1), can make others feel uncomfortable and (2), may not follow the code of conduct. 

There will never be a case here where you need to ask for someone's name or attempt to draw it out in the conversation. If I came across something like that, I would report it. Forum nicknames work just fine here. 

I am going to say something here that many may not agree with. I think it would be a good idea to take a step back for a while and do a lot of lurking before jumping back in. You will get a better idea of what generally works and what doesn't and also be able to answer with more confidence, less risk of offense or making the person coming here for help uncomfortable and as a side effect/bonus, learn how to become a more effective troubleshooter.

Cheers.

### Post by Tamlynmac on 2010-08-10
It's been my experience, this forum tends to be deliberately age and  gender blind. The goal (I suspect) is to not isolate individuals based  on gender, age or any other component.

 IMHO the goals of this site are not the same as those of a social  networking site. Here the primary objective is to engage in activities  specifically related to an OS. Interaction between members (outside the  Cafe) is primarily focused on Ubuntu/Linux. Member knowledge is not a function of age or gender, but rather  based on their experience(s), invested time, etc. The learning curve for  Ubuntu/Linux differs for each individual and no one member may have all  the answers.
 
 Since the forums are not a social networking site and have specific  guidelines (COC) as to how members interact with both the site itself  and other members - there is no need for excessive personal information.  Logic suggests that without the forums mandating a copy of one's ID,  some individuals may not truthfully respond to said questions. Some  might even find it entertaining to indulge in deceiving others, basically invalidating said information.
 
I doubt many who come to this site seeking  assistance are concerned as to the age or gender of those  assisting them. Is it really necessary to know this information when  receiving or providing assistance? Would it be a shock to find out that  one was helped by an 80 or 12* year old male or female and does it really  matter? I don't believe it does.

One of the only instances where this may be applicable IMHO, is when a  member needs in depth assistance and the level at which said assistance  should be conveyed.  However, this may also apply to experience and thus  age once again becomes irrelevant. The answer lies in how we  communicate when helping other users/members. Not in our gender or age.

* These ages were selected arbitrarily and should not reflect the age  limits of those who use Ubuntu. They where used solely to express my  point of view.
