### Post by frankleeee on 2008-12-15
Unfortunately the argument for or against a reinstall is based on correlation and unmeasurable variables. If you have the ability to manipulate the computer and understand how the programs run and can access the right area then this argument works for (you). But not everybody is a geek or has the patience or even wants to be one, nor wants anything but a working computer setup. I don't see people on here very often complaining about a reinstall and losing important information. 

We all know that a fresh install beats a upgrade generally in fixing problems and having a more likely good starting point to move on from, granted if you have a non-corrupted disc or download. This may be true for those of us who know how to get everything setup but don't know all the files that can be manipulated or want to, or want to learn all of the command line stuff. 

With the basic Ubuntu releases now you can get by without hardly ever using the terminal except for auto-remove (for ease) and auto-clean. Personally I know probably 10 or so commands beyond this, but usually have to look them up to get them correct. Yes I know there are third party things that the terminal is effective with but I think that the terminal may be part of what keeps people away. Yes I know that it is important for accessing apt sources.lists but if as time goes on the developers makes all this stuff more easily accessible more people will switch over or dual boot.
