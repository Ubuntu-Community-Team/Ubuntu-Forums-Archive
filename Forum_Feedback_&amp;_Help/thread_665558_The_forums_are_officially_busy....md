### Post by popch on 2008-01-12
> **aysiu said:**
> I've been on these forums for almost three years now, and I have to say what I just saw was pretty impressive... and scary.(...)  The questions used to be simpler and easier to solve..

I have not been a user or even a contributor for as much time as you have, but some of your observations have become obvious even to me.

Some of the trend in the 'answerability' of questions could be explained away by two causes: The more recent Ubuntu releases took care of many of the chores which generated so many questions, and users can find the easier answers more readily in the threads already there and answered. 

However, I am somewhat doubtful as to the number of non-asked questions accounted for by those two causes.

> **aysiu said:**
> I'm hoping, though, that with the proliferation of threads people's support threads will not go ignored.

It is my evaluation that exactly that might happen. There appears to have been over time a "drift" in peoples' needs and expectations which has not been quite met by the forum at large.

Please do not read this as a critical comment on the forum, the organisation or the community behind it. It's just that the capabilities of a forum are somehow coming closer to a limit.

If you look at it closely, you will realise that any forum - and indeed this very forum - can support a small repertoire of transactions very well: [LIST=1]
[*]Someone is looking for a particular piece of information or for a solution for a particular problem, and someone else (or many others) happen to know the answer, the solution or a suitable source.
[*]As a secondary transaction type (the first derivation of the first one) someone is looking for the same kind of information and can find answers posted to the same or similar questions already present in the forum.
[*]As a second derivation, someone is trying to find a piece of information but lacks the aptitude (or simply the vocabulary) to search for himself, and some members search the 'repository' of the forum in order to answer those questions (in the sense of #1 above).[/LIST]I do not mean that in the least bit maliciously, but we take much pride here in not telling people to "RTFM". In doing so, many of us even refrain from pointing out the proper reading matter to consult and go to the pains of paraphrasing or re-inventing material which has been prepared by people best qualified to document and explain.

We also try and help many people who plainly appear to be too lazy to do their own reading. That's very nice and friendly but in the long run not very useful, because those very people tend to be overwhelmed by the answers they are bound to receive, and to just give up and whither away, so to speak.

All of those three types of transactions somewhat rely  on the number of requests being small enough so as not to need any scheduling or allocation entity or process.  It is easy to see that #1 is most vulnerable to a growth in the  number of requests, followed by #3.

The risk that 'resources' (people willing and able to help) waste all their answering time by looking into threads they can not or need not answer grows with the number of queries. The total sum of time wasted in this particular way grows much faster than the number of threads posted (with the square of that number?)

There are other shortcomings of the forum as opposed to others repositories of knowledge. One is the half life of the information stored in the forum. A respectable part of the information in the forum becomes obsolete with each new release or even patch, as well as with each new or updated article in the documentation repositories such as the official doc site or the community wikis. Since those resources are not coupled, much effort is drained from willing and capable resources.

Also, quite a few threads arrive at a complete answer only after some bouncing to and fro of many opinions, questions and false starts. While that process can be entirely enjoyable to those involved, it can be very difficult for people who need the same or similar answers at a later time to find the wheat in all that chaff.

Lastly, some of the very valiant tries at creating contributions of longer lasting value are lost because the very form of the forum does not lend itself to conserving such material over any length of time. The concept of the Sticky Thread is a nice attempt in that direction but is not a notable success in that particular area.

Sorry, this has become a bit longer than I thought at first, and I have no answers. Hopefully, some of the question raised here will lead to solutions.

get a feel for what type of issues the mostly new users are having.

database for an answer to their question. It seems to me that the cycle starts
over with each release of Ubuntu as a new batch of  new users appear.

tracks the release dates of Ubuntu versions. 

The “How do I install it?” , “How do I get sound to work?” ,
“How do I  get  Flash to work?”  seem to be  followed by more complex 
question between release dates as the new users get the basic stuff
to work and move on to other issues.

of an effect,  and the frequency of more complex questions does seem to be rising.

person gets XYZ thing working on his computer they show it to all of their
friends, and show them how to do it.  It is not long before they are answering
question about XYZ on the internet to show the world what they know and
help spread the goodness that XYZ represents.  Some of those you help today
will be helping others in time.

The more new users today the more new helpers tomorrow.
