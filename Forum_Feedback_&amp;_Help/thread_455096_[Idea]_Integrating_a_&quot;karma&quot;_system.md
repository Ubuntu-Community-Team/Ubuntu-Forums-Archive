### Post by persofi on 2007-05-26
Hey folks,
I'm quite new to Ubuntu and this community but have to say I'm very impressed. The people here are very nice and helpful and considering the size of the forums, this must mean that the moderators and staff people must be doing an amazing job.

So maybe it is best to change nothing, but still proposing something cant hurt. So here it is: I am active in a small political forum (also running vBulletin), where there is a "karma" system. Meaning, every user can klick a positive-negative karma button on every post if he likes too. Like somebody giving you good advice --> you give good karma. If someone is ranting and raving and insulting constructive critics --> bad karma. I'm sure you are familiar with the concept as many forums use it. Also the discussion system at "digg.com" (sry if its inappropriate to give the link) is in a way similiar, but not the same.

Here are some of my thoughts what this system necessarily has to have:

1) There needs to be a history of the karma you got in your control center. It must include the user name, the post it related to and a short (1 sentence) reason. Karma may not be anonymous! Sometimes you get negative karma with an interesting reason, get the urge to write the user a private message, asking for clarification and an interesting private discussion emerges. It has happened to me countless times; 

2) There should be some kind of rank system, but the algorithm shouldnt be public. Karma-hunting is a bad thing, though not as bad as post-hunting. The rank should be included in every post, but it shouldnt be too specific, rather you should be able to see "Wow, this guy often writes good stuff" or "Well, he doesnt seem to be too popular around here":

3) There has to be some elaborate system to give the karma some "value". Meaning you cant give too much positive karma in a certain timeframe, karma in response to another karma is valued less etc... Maybe even karma from "good" posters should be worth more than those from "bad" posters.  This would be quite complex to work out I suppose. However there is no reason why it cant evolve gradually, like everything else in the Ubuntu world ;)

I realize in a forum this big, this is probably alot of work or even bandwith to implement. Unfortunately there are also sometimes problems, that people are insulted by you giving them negative karma and then sending you insulting private messages. It doesnt happen too often but I suppose the karma system in itself will slightly increase moderation efforts.

So it needs to have some really good effects to be cost-effective in implementation. I think the merits outweigh the costs:


a) People are even more inclined to help, and are more rewarded for it. People say "thank you" in some way more often. Humans are actually quite simple. Good "karma" makes them happy for a short time. It may sound strange but I have really experienced this myself. Also on a site as big as these forums, one could possibly effectively implement a "digg-like" system, that the posts with the most positive karma can go in some lists like "10 best how-to threads ever"   "best posts of today" "best design ideas of today" ... Reputation and "fame" like this is a very very good motivator for people to write good articles.
 
b) It is a better system to assign ranks than post count / registration date. In the FAQ it is explained that the current system is a compromise, because you dont want people juding posts not on their content but on their posters. Of course, this is a valid concern. However I fear this cannot be realized.
 
There are these mainy questions, which are pure "science": A certain problem needs to be solved with code or instructions. It either works, or not. If a 20-post-something which can code circles around the staff solves this, there isnt gonna be a problem, because everybody open-minded realizes he is right and nobody has a real reason to object him. In this instance there is no difference between a system based on karma or post count. 

If however believe there are some fundamental questions, like the future design of some Ubuntu concepts, which are more of a theoretical nature and where many standpoints are possible, I fear your noble idea of equality will not come into play. People see the post count, people see the registration date, people see if you are a moderator, People know you if you have been around. Your "reputation" of course has an effect. It's exactly the same thing in science: If you are a postdoc and go against the mainstream opinion, you better have some really brilliant article and can back it up very well. 

So it is my argument, that people try to draw some inferences of your knowledge by any means possible anyway. Since we cant stop them from doing that, we should give them a system which is more to the truth. In my mind a positive/negative karma system is much more meritocratic than anything based on post count.

c) General accepted rules of behavior and norms would evolve out of the community much simpler. Sometimes I read a discussion, where I feel one party is attacking the other ad hominem, discussing unfair and rude. But I myself have nothing really to add to the discussion or cant really take sides. Making a post "You should be more polite" seems a little inappropriate and kindergarden, but karming them negative is not. I have myself had posts, where I was arguing with some guys, and there were maybe 2 or 3 openly against me, but 20 or 30 negative karms. It is not pleasant but we are mostly adults here and should be able to accept constructive criticism.
Another thing are very very very simple questions, which could easily be answered by the search function. Typing "use the search blablabla" is boring and often in the same time you can help someone directly. Thats ok. But helping him + karming negative would be better;

I wouldnt go as far as removing the current system. A combination of karma + post count is better; on the one hand, you reward the people who have been around a long time and shown great loyality to the Ubuntu project. On the other hand, you reward those who are posting very good things, but also very effectively.
