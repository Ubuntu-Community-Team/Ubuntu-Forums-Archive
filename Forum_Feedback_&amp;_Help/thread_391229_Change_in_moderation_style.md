### Post by eXisor on 2007-03-23
After much deliberation (first - do no harm) I decided to take KiwiNZ at his word and hence, to post my thoughts on this matter.

You mods are doing a good, valuable and much appreciated job, but this thread indicates a certain disquiet with some of your decisions. Read this not as an attempt to stir the pot, or an attack, but as an attempt to pinpoint the underlying principles which may be at issue.

So...

Yes, I also perceive a change in moderator policy implementation. Unfortunately, my perception is that it is tending towards post/topic intolerance, particularly, and perhaps ironically, in the backyard.

A few personal perceptions (PP), possible points of contention (POC), and questions designed to probe for a possible solution (Q).... Please view these as posits, not statements. 


1) PP: Posts seem to be reviewed and jailed by a single moderator.
    POC: We all see things differently. Perhaps jailing a post should always require multi-moderator review before jailing? 
    Q: Is there a mechanism for this? Should it be a moderator rule?


2) PP: Team Moderator.
    POC: It seems likely there is inter-moderator communication over and above what we actually see in resolution threads. Supposing so, this hidden 'consensus' or 'decision-made' approach does little for the transparency of the process and (in my view) promotes an us/them perception.
    Q: Perhaps inter-moderator discussions should be part of the resolution thread, there for all to see?


3) PP: Premature moderator intervention.
    POC: Although the facility is there to report a 'bad' post, my perception is the majority of jailed posts are not reported by members, but are intercepted by the mods.
    Q: Are moderators allowing enough time for the thread posters to respond/deal with the matter themselves? Is ignoring a mildly insulting post not more effective than jailing it? Aren't we all advised to 'not feed the troll'?


4) PP: Moderator procedure and protocol
    POC: Moderators rename, move, join and close threads based on their own  perceptions. Sometimes this is at odds with the intent of the OP and her hopes for the thread.
    Q: Should an OP, out of respect, not be consulted regarding such acts before they are actioned? (Not asked for permission, but consulted).
    Q: Should these decisions not be multi-moderator? We all suffer from personal bias/prejudice, or sometimes just have a bad day.
    Q: Is there a temporary measure/state that can be implemented while the OP is consulted?


5) PP: Resolution centre pragmatics
    POC: As a poster in a particular topic I am as affected by a thread closure as the OP. According to the rules of the RC, I am unable to contribute to the resolution topic when I may have useful thoughts on the matter.
    Q: Without breaking the rules of the RC, or getting permission for an exception to be made, how can I reasonably contribute to the RC process? (I think opening a thread pertaining to the matter in the backyard needlessly exposes the particular matter to parties who may have no vested interest, and further, may seem an attempt at forum insurrection).
    Q; Could the RC rules not be relaxed to allow thread contributers to air their views on a thread closure? If this is abused, the same jailing process that applies elsewhere can be applied.


6) PP: A moderator moderates a thread after participating in that thread.
    POC: I think moderators should be disqualified from moderating any thread they contribute to. This, in my view, should be added to the moderator rules.
    Q: I know this is happening informally now, but is it an enforceable moderator rule?


7) PP: Jailed posts are not visible, so we cannot learn from them.
    POC: What does a particular rule actually mean? Rules can only be fully understood when we see how they are enforced. This is the nature of all laws: they are written, implemented, and then spawn refinements, addendums and exceptions the original writing does not cater for.
    Q: If I could see what is getting jailed, would that not make it easier for me to avoid the pitfalls myself?


Addressing some of these POC's may, in my view, alleviate some of the 'perceived moderator intolerance' that underlies this thread.

Thanx for this opportunity and your kind consideration.

### Post by aysiu on 2007-04-06
I think moderators who keep reminding people that we're volunteers aren't looking for extra appreciation, just recognition that people shouldn't be abusing moderators or feeling overly entitled to some service, as if they're customers. Personally, I don't use the "we're all volunteers" line from a moderator perspective, but I will use it as a user helping another user who is demanding and presents an unhealthy sense of entitlement in a support thread.

As for the closing of threads, there are a couple of situations where closure can be necessary. 

1. Discussions about implementing a forum feature that the staff has already decided is not going to happen. Allowing that discussion to continue is disingenuous. If there's no possibility if it being implemented, there's no point in discussing the pros and cons. 

2. Discussions that, by their very nature and topic, invite violations to the code of conduct.

But I will agree that in *most* cases, I wouldn't close some threads other moderators would. We are all human and sometimes err. In many situations, we consult each other, but sometimes we go on our best judgment. And we do often reverse our decisions after discussing among ourselves or seeing objections in the Resolution Center. 

My personal take--if the subject is controversial ("What do you think about capital punishment?") but not by its very nature offensive ("What do you hate about Chinese people?"), it should never be closed. If people are violating forum rules in individual posts in the midst of an otherwise constructive discussion, those individual posts should be sent to the jail, and those individuals should get infractions. Everyone else should be able to discuss the topic respectfully and intelligently.
