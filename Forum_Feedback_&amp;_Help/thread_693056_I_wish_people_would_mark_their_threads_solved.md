### Post by popch on 2008-02-13
> **hyper_ch said:**

(Must be some remote cousins of mine).

Pop ups are also nearly invisible once you have instructed your browser not to show them.

A possible solution for the problem could consist of yet another tag for individual posts. All but the OP of a thread can have that tag. It is set or cleared by the person posting it. 

Its meaning is 'I, the poster of this particular post, am of the opinion that this post could effectively solve the OP's problem'. Of course, that tag should not be set for inquiries about symptoms, expressions of sympathy or even for snide remarks.

Once that tag is set, the OP (the 'owner' of the thread) can be given several options. The options given can even be made dependent on several conditions, such as
[LIST]
[*]potential-solution-tag present in thread
[*]potential-solution-tag ist last post in thread
[*]potential-solution-tag's age more or less than some given period of time
[*]thread owner has/has not posted since tag was set[/LIST]The expected behavior of the OP of a thread with such a tag would be to acknowledge that the tag was seen, plus a statement whether the problem was, in fact, solved. I am somewhat undecided about what to do about follow-up problems.

There are several ways how the OP can be nudged, assisted or even forced to acknowledge and evaluate the tags, such as
[LIST]
[*]showing a prompt when logging in, like that for showing new PMs
[*]redirection to a page listing threads which are tagged and unacknowledged (with the option of visiting other threads, of course)
[*]cues and hints of differing subtleness when posting to a tagged thread[/LIST]There are, of course, some things which could be done that might be considered less friendly. An unacknowled tag counter in the user's profile comes to mind.

### Post by MJN on 2008-02-13
Whilst not exactly the circumstances you describe it's made me think of why I'm not all that keen on the [SOLVED] idea - it seems to potentially minimise interest in a thread. Sure, maybe a particular post does solve the issue but that's not to say it's the only/best solution... Indeed, as far as the OP is concerned the problem may well appear solved but is it really?

For example, say someone is getting permissions errors on their website. Someone, with good intent, comes along and suggests **chmod a+rwx /var/www**, or even **chmod -R a+rwx / **(extreme, yes, but you get my drift) - the OP no longer has his permissions problem. Great! But might this 'solution' miss out on peer review? I'd be less likely to join in with the thread (i.e. probably not read it at all) if the title already says [SOLVED] by the time I come to see it.

Furthermore, if someone comes along with a similar, yet perhaps not identical, problem and tags it onto your post who is going to read it? As far as others are concerned the thread is solved, done-and-dusted, so the newcomer will have little choice but to start yet another new thread despite their issue being on-topic for the existing one.

What do you think? (Feel free to wildly disagree - I won't get upset - I can see there are pros and cons here). Speaking of pros, what would you say are the benefits of adopting the [SOLVED] concept? I can think of some but there may well be more I'm unaware of.

Mathew

### Post by popch on 2008-02-13
> **MJN said:**
> What do you think? (Feel free to wildly disagree - I won't get upset - I can see there are pros and cons here). Speaking of pros, what would you say are the benefits of adopting the [SOLVED] concept? I can think of some but there may well be more I'm unaware of.

Mathew

Pros:

People wanting to help do not open endless series of threads only to see that the OP's problem is solved or the OP has lost interest.

People looking for help/advice/tips can focus on threads which promise a solution.

People posting things have the satisfaction of seeing that the things were in fact useful. That is partially solved with the thanking functions.

There ought to be some effects on discipline, too, if the forum clearly communicates that users are expected to say so when they were being effectively helped.

Should the forum or the community decide on some kind of 'solution harvesting', the threads generating solutions would be easier to find. There are signs that some people are starting such attempts; see the programming subforum, for example, where discussions about FAQs are going on.

The danger that suboptimal solutions remain unchallenged appears not all too great to me. As far as I can see, even threads which are clearly marked as solved  generate quite some traffic and dispute.

Just a few thoughts on the topic.
