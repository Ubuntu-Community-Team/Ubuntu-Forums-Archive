### Post by rookcifer on 2010-12-20
I agree with one of the above posters.  In general purpose operating systems like Linux, Windows, OS-X, *BSD (and even OpenVMS and XTS-400) there will never be a 100% guarantee of security.  The code base is too large and developed by too many people.  Yes, there are some EAL7 OS's out there, but most of them are highly specialized and usually embedded (like for airplane navigation, etc.).  If you go by the older Orange Book (TCSEC) criteria, there is one general purpose OS that has been evaluated A1 by the NSA (GEMSOS).  A1 is the equivalent to EAL7.

Beyond the OS, most of us have no control.  That is, we have to use standard off the shelf hardware, which could have bugs of it's own (either intentional or not) that could compromise security no matter the OS running on it.  This is why NSA has its own chip manufacturing plant (so they wont fall victim to Chinese backdoors in hardware).  So unless you create your own hardware from scratch in your basement and then code your own OS and all its applications, you can never be 100% assured of security.

All that said, Linux (and the BSD's) are the best choice for the security conscious user.  You should not worry yourself with viruses and malware -- they simply are not an issue on anything but Windows.  If your security is ever compromised on a Linux machine, it will be through a direct attack (say you have no firewall and are running an exploitable service that some hacker wants to directly target on your machine.  Servers have to worry more about this than desktop users).  Merely picking up a virus by visiting a webpage is unheard of on Linux.  It would be *much* more difficult to make happen even if hackers targeted Linux like they do windows.
