### Post by OpSecShellshock on 2010-03-21
Who owns the domain? If it's his, then it's his. The hosting provider may or may not choose to tell you anything about it in that case. If the domain belongs to the hospital (which I'd hope) then they might be willing to change out the email address used for official contact. However, it's probably going to have to be someone in a management position who is able to verify their identity in order for the hosting provider to be willing to make any changes. I mean would you stay with that provider if all it took to give over the registration details was a phone call from someone claiming to be the new guy?

So I guess this guy who was let go used his personal email address for official contact rather than an address at the domain itself? Usually when registering a domain for an organization you go with something on that domain with a generic name like "admin@" in order to avoid these things being tied to a specific person. Honestly, if the hospital still has the actual hardware of the web server, and the data, and all of the access to the data, and if the former employee's accounts on the hospital's systems are gone and he can't get at any of it, I would let the domain go before trying to get at the guy's email account.

Really though, there's a time for system administrators and there's a time for lawyers. This is potentially medical data. I'm not sure what the lawyers would do, exactly, but first of all the hospital might be liable for the risk created in giving the web presence over to an individual employee's personal control. So they should know about that anyway. Second, the lawyers might be able to get a feel for what could be pursued in terms of communication either with the former employee or the hosting provider in order to regain control of the site. Either way, though, this is no longer a technological problem, and the responsible thing to do is pass it up the chain of command.
