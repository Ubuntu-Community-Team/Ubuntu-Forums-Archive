But in the security world, bits beat $billions. I'm sure the NSA have little trouble cracking a 64-bit key these days, but even a 128-bit key is billions of billions of times harder... the only way to crack it if the key is securely generated is through a flaw in the encryption algorithm or very good luck in picking the right key to try. Adding bits to a key is trivial while every extra bit essentially doubles the cost of the hardware required to break it.

And everyone should have secure encryption on any computer that travels with them because there are plenty of people other than governments who'd like to get their hands on your banking credentials or business documents. Once you decide to encrypt it there's absolutely no benefit to using insecure encryption that a few billion dollars can break rather than a secure algorithm that no-one can break.

128 or 256 bit algorithms are the same for all practical purposes.  I believe it was noted cryptology expert Bruce Schneier who said: "Which is more secure, a 1000 ft. tall fence or a 10,000 ft. tall fence?"  That's about what this 128 vs. 256 argument amounts to.  Both are unbreakable by brute force.  I don't care if NSA has a vast underground lair of supercomputers -- 128 bits is far too large to crack before the sun burns out.  Of course, this is assuming that NSA hasn't mathematically broken the algorithm.  If they have, brute forcing isn't even necessary -- they would have the keys to the kingdom.  But I guess we will never know if they have, as they certainly wouldn't give up such a secret.

All evidence from criminal trials around the world suggests no government can break strong encryption like AES128.  If they can, they haven't felt these criminal trials (of which include child porn and terrorist groups) were important enough to reveal their secrets.
