### Post by perspectoff on 2011-01-01
Not a law. A proposed law by some very ill-informed lawmakers. It's a very good article by the New York Times.

Government wants backdoors to all communications over the Internet, because their alternative is to conduct "risky" (video or audio) surveillance of suspected criminals (such as drug cartels).

There is no law requiring this -- some members of the US government just want to enact one.

Unfortunately, as the article points out, backdoors are a security risk. The US government is proposing creating more backdoors, while simultaneously being worried about the ability of the Chinese to encroach into our critical (energy and banking) services via these very same types of backdoors (that famously have existed in the Microsoft OS, for example).

So on one hand the government bemoans the porous nature of security of the Internet yet then on the other proposes laws that will make it even less secure.

Of course, such a law would be impossible to enforce. Communications providers would locate offshore or completely out of the US (aren't Skype servers located in Germany?), and then the only real solution would be to restrict all Internet portals into and out of the US (a la North Korea and China).

"Tyranny in the name of national security endangers the basic freedoms of all of us."

When the RSA standards of encryption were first invented and published in the late 1970s, the US passed a law forbidding export of this technology in the name of "national security." It allowed encryption which the government was not able to decode (initially), so it was felt that forbidding export of the technology would be protective.

Of course, that was silly, as the technology was exported anyway and just re-incarnated abroad. The only ones who suffered were American companies who wanted to deploy the technology overseas in order to encrypt their secure business communications worldwide, but were prevented from doing so by the law.

So the law did nothing to prevent the spread of the RSA encryption technology worldwide, but instead was applied only to American companies to their competitive disadvantage.

Now those poised to propose the legislation (to place backdoors in all American communications) again appear willing to sacrifice the competitiveness and security of American business internationally in the interest of a misguided sense of "national security". This is just because they don't want to use "risky" video and audio surveillance techniques.

Frankly, I don't believe the argument.

Technology has existed for over 30 years by which a wiretapping device can be pointed at a window of a "secret" meeting room and the vibrations of the window analyzed and reconstructed and the conversations within thereby monitored. This is the technology Soviet spies used in order to spy on the military, aerospace, and satellite industry of the 70's and 80's in the US. (Top-secret facilities had no windows for this reason).

This type of surveillance is hardly "high risk" audio surveillance, and drug cartels generally don't conduct business in windowless top-secret facilities.

I very much understand and sympathize with law enforcement's desire to intercept communications between lawbreakers, but the number of channels and methods of encrypting information on the Internet are now legion. Does anyone remember the encrypted messages embedded in pictures transmitted by Usenet, IRC, Flickr, Google Pics, etc. several years ago? Now we even have tinyURLs and video-web chats and evanescent peer-to-peer connections that represent rapidly shifting sands for monitoring.

Law enforcement might have bought into the mentality that a law-enforcement "Neo" type of character (a la the movie Matrix) can navigate the corridors of the Internet out-thinking the global computer, but it was a stupid premise for the movie (and Keanu Reeves gives the impression of being the slowest thinker, not the fastest) and is likewise a stupid premise for law enforcement. (Methinks too many lawmakers have been unduly influenced by that movie in their wishful thinking.)

No, there are other methods that law enforcement will have to use. The old time tracking device is pretty-sophisticated these days. Not only are they able to be tracked by GPS, the ability to record and transmit ambient sound and conversations (in a vehicle, for example) via satellite is far superior to that of the 70's and 80's. 

Since many cell phones have GPS locators built in, these days, it would not surprise me to find that law enforcement were tracking criminals (with the aid of cell-phone companies) through their cell phones anyway.

So lawmakers would do well to study the history of RSA encryption and not repeat mistakes of the past. They ought to look at ways to facilitate law enforcement with appropriate and achievable methods instead.

They also ought to stop using all the funds that could be used to bolster law enforcement for their own extended vacations, billion dollar campaign seasons, and million dollar inaugural parties!
