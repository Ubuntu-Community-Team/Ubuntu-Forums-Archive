### Post by coljohnhannibalsmith on 2015-11-25
Hello,

One of my online security concerns is keeping my itinerary private. Frankly, I don't know how to accomplish this. I often use travel websites to plan trips and purchase passage on commercial mass transportation; and I don't want my itinerary or mode of transport to become available to any potentially hostile third party. This can become especially problematic, when I have established sites that I book through. It occurs to me that all any interested party, with the necessary technical means, needs to do is lurk at the destination site, wait for a login, then monitor traffic within the site itself. This also goes for any kind of social activity website.

The only firewall I can imagine for this, is to purchase passage or arrange social meet-ups through sites based within TOR hidden services. I have surfed these hidden services only twice; and so far there doesn't appear to be any offerings. I absolutely think there should be. Another possibility could be to create my own websites, open or hidden, for these purposes. That way I can vet membership and monitor traffic to ensure the complete anonymity and security of myself and any members. This appears to be the only way; and I think I'm going to have to be the unlucky fellow that gets stuck with the job.

Any insights appreciated.

### Post by ian-weisser on 2015-11-26
> **coljohnhannibalsmith said:**
> I don't want my itinerary or mode of transport to become available to any potentially hostile third party.

It depends upon who you are, and whom the third party is.

Example: Many senior members of the US Government, including hundreds of members of Congress, the Supreme Court, Cabinet members, and senior Generals and Admirals ride common carriers around the United States and overseas openly under their own names quite frequently without much problem from hostile third parties.

You can encrypt and hide the ticket purchase transaction all you like - your hostile can obtain your itinerary rather easily using social exploits anytime later. A myriad of exploitable people have access to your entry in the reservation system ("Hi, my wallet and phone were stolen, and I lost my flight information"). Example: Common carrier passenger manifests are *intended* to be accessable by emergency responders. 

A really capable hostile can log your keystrokes, or check your card statement to find out which travel companies you have dealt with. Perhaps you should consider pivate transport.
