### Post by DuckHook on 2022-01-17
Welcome to the forums, rwethereyet2

You are not asking about "security". You are asking about privacy/anonymity.

Do not expect 100% ironclad privacy/anonymity anywhere on the Internet. If you want such privacy/anonymity, you are in the wrong place. For guaranteed privacy/anonymity you are better off with the traditional measures of physical maildrops, fancy codewords and cloak & dagger.

The Internet can be made *more* private/anonymous&#8212;maybe even *highly* private/anonymous&#8212;but it cannot be made *completely* private/anonymous. Expectations must be set realistically at the outset to avoid false senses of security and impossible goals.

Just as significantly: it is foolish to think that anyone can evade a nation state if that nation state is sufficiently intent on finding/identifying them. Three&#8209;letter agencies have budgets in the hundreds of billions. They run farms of supercomputers that can easily model the quintillion interactive components of a nuclear explosion every millisecond. They use AI algorithms that the rest of us cannot even dream about. They have a vast army of techs, gurus, geniuses and plain&#8209;old spooks in black SUVs who can identify, surveil and track anyone, anywhere, anyhow, anywhen.

Against this, is anyone really naïve enough to think that a VPN and an anonymous email account can stop them? If they want you&#8230; They. Will. Find. You.

The role of privacy/anonymity is not to hide from three&#8209;letter agencies or nation states. That is a ridiculous objective. Its proper role is to obfuscate our virtual trail sufficiently such that we can maintain some semblance of identity integrity against the *real* scoundrels: the crooks, the scumbags, the data vampires and identity parasites. If protection against this second tier of threats is your goal, then something can be done about that.

But, more bad news: the vast majority of processes and procedures needed against these more realistic threats have far less to do with technology (like anonymous email) than with personal behaviour. There are no "magic bullet" apps, platforms or technologies. You will only be as private/anonymous as *your personal behaviour* allows. No technology on earth can protect one against oneself.

Further thoughts:

[list=1]
[*]It is important to separate privacy from anonymity. Though related, they are not the same. Though some measures address both, different measures are needed for each.
[*]People need to stop thinking of privacy/anonymity in binary terms. It is a continuum.
[*]In our modern society, it is also a trade&#8209;off. The cost of greater privacy/anonymity is measured in reduced convenience/utility.
[*]Most people are unwilling to put up with the severe life limitations that hard core privacy/anonymity demands. They are hooked on social media, online shopping, instant messaging, smart phones, online banking, credit cards, online bill payments, email, web&#8209;searching, and yes, even technical forums such as this one. I'm certainly unwilling to part with these modern facets of life. They are no longer merely "conveniences"; they have become "necessities". Therefore, there is an upper limit to how much privacy or anonymity we can even expect, never mind achieve.

And each of us must determine where we draw that line.
[/list]
If you are deadly serious about both your privacy and your anonymity, then you can start with the following (and these are the easy ones):

[list=1]
[*]Give up social media—or at least move *entirely* to obscure social media platforms that actually make good on their promises not to track you—good luck convincing your friends to do that.
[*]Never shop online.
[*]Switch *entirely* to privacy focused messaging services. Again, good luck convincing friends and family.
[*]Use only one&#8209;time prepaid "burner" credit cards.
[*]Use only burner phones on which you install minimal/no added apps.
[*]Use burner phone numbers.
[*]Use an email anonymizer.
[*]**Never** use Google anything. Switch to DuckDuckGo for your search engine. Switch to a non-Google browser like Firefox. Delete your gmail account. Switch to paid cloud storage. etc etc.
[*]Set up a string of VPNs including a self&#8209;hosted SOHO&#8209;based one.
[*]Harden your browser (this is not as easy as it may seem).

You are more of a "tech" person than you give yourself credit for. The fact that you use Linux and ask about security issues at all on a forum such as this places you out of the ordinary.

Good on you.

The anonymizer service that I use is one called AnonAddy. Website is: [https://anonaddy.com/](https://anonaddy.com/)

I use it because it's run by a small operator (I like to support little guys), is FOSS and is available on fDroid. This gives me reassurance that the app at least won't track me. Whether the platform does or not is a different matter and is addressed below.

It is not a fully-fledged anonymizer like the one you reference. What it does is act as a gatekeeper/clearing house/mail forwarder. The anonymization process occurs during routing. You must still provide your real email address to the AnonAddy platform. Mail sent using AnonAddy is then stripped of your real email address and routed through AnonAddy's servers. Return mail goes through AnonAddy before being routed to your real email address. They say that they don't keep logs or records of your e-mails, blah-blah-blah, but if one is truly security conscious, how much can one trust such reassurances? In the end, what have we to go on but their word? Or, if they are audited, the word of their auditors?

See what I mean about limits to anonymity? Trust has to start somewhere.

In the case of your reference anonymizer, on the face of it, they would seem to be more on the "highly" private end of the scale, but you also must trust their word that they don't log, that their VPN is secure and not broken, that they don't keep records of your actual email and so forth. Unless you physically tour their facilities, you don't know. And if a secret court within their jurisdiction forces them to start logging without telling you, they will have no choice but to comply. So the same caveats apply always and everywhere.

My use of AnonAddy is not to stop nation states and three-letter agencies; it is stop the second tier of threats: the crooks and the data slurpers. It is also very effective at tracing who is selling my data.

Example: I'm a golfer (thus my avatar). When I book a course, they require an e-mail address. They say they need this to communicate with me in case my booking is cancelled, but they also invariably sell the info on to the umbrella marketing agencies that own and run their booking platforms. If they know all the places I play, the days of the week I play, how often and what type of courses, from these facts alone, they can assemble a pretty accurate idea of my income level, my employment status and possibly even my political views (we all tend to fall into behavioural baskets). Such info is highly valuable not only to commercial interests in the golfing industry, but to actors as diverse as, say, car manufacturers to vacation agencies to political parties. And that's just from my golfing data.

I don't like this at all. I don't like how it opens me up to exploitation and manipulation. I detest having a little simulacrum of me walking around out there that I gave no permission to spring into existence, that I cannot influence in any way, that never expires and that I cannot destroy. One that technically/legally doesn't even belong to me.

So, I give each course a different email address. It becomes very revealing which of them sell my info. Some are outrageously shameless about it too. But the stuttering apologies of the worker bees are pointless—they don't make the rules, though one hopes that if enough such complaints make it up the food chain, that something will change.

I have no worries about my life being in danger over tracking where I live, but that isn't the case for many people and I've visited some places where being tracked was obviously happening by the govt.  The trade off was submit to tracking or don't come.  In many parts of the world, the govt is the owner of the telecom services. This happens in some places we usually think of as democracies.

BTW, I'm not doing anything of interest to anyone, but that doesn't mean I want it available to the world either. It isn't any of _their_ business - and "_their_" means govt, neighbors, and sometimes family and friends.  I certainly don't want online tracking to happen in any way by any of those companies - even the ones where I have a business relationship.  

I'd be happier if Netflix forgot everything I watched after 30 days. Same for Amazon for the stuff I watched or bought.
I'd love to order stuff/food online, but I'm unwilling to load an "app" on a personal-tracking-device just for that convenience. Sometimes it is a hassle. I'm willing to have that inconvenience rather than give up the privacy and be tracked for some free fries every 6 months.
