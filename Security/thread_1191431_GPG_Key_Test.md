### Post by kevdog on 2009-06-21
Just curious to your key selection.

Your master key is an RSA signing key (signing keys by convention are always known as the master key -- not the encryption key -- its a convention).

After choosing a master key, there can be multiple subkeys -- either signing or encryption.

By default -- usually in most circumstances there is only one master signing key, and an encryption subkey.  Encryption is usually either ElGamal or RSA.  

By convention now (as of 5/2009) -- the default signing key algorithm is RSA -- it used to be DSA however due to the problems with the SHA1 has vulnerabilities and the DSA working only with 160bit SHA1 algorithm, they had either a choice to move to DSA2 which could work with SHA256 (please note that SHA512 is rounded in all circumstances to SHA256 so it provides no advantage) or RSA.  Based on compatibility purposes with older GnuPG implementations, RSA was chosen as the format rather than DSA2.

So you have a DSA2 signing subkey and a ElGamal encryption key.  I guess my question is why the DSA2 signing subkey?

Here's my gpg.conf file:

```
personal-cipher-preferences AES256 TWOFISH CAMELLIA256 AES192 CAMELLIA192 AES CAMELLIA128 BLOWFISH CAST5 3DES
personal-digest-preferences SHA512 SHA384 SHA256 SHA224 RIPEMD160 SHA1 MD5
personal-compress-preferences BZIP2 ZIP ZLIB
default-preference-list SHA512 SHA384 SHA256 SHA224 RIPEMD160 SHA1 MD5 AES256 TWOFISH CAMELLIA256 AES192 CAMELLIA192 AES CAMELLIA128 BLOWFISH CAST5 3DES BZIP2 ZIP ZLIB
cert-digest-algo SHA512
s2k-cipher-algo AES256
s2k-digest-algo SHA512
```

As you can see, I put all 256 bit ciphers first and all SHA-2 hash functions in descending bit strength.  

However, it's probably better to use AES-128 first because the asymmetric algorithm (RSA 4096) is somewhere between the strength of a 128 bit and 192 bit symmetric cipher.  Thus, there's no reason to use a 256 bit symmetric cipher when the public-key cipher isn't nearly as strong.  Moreover, AES-256 has attacks faster than brute-force while AES-128 does not.

Yeah, since symmetric ciphers are so efficient and since most everyone's CPU is capable of crunching them without issue, it doesn't make any practical difference in speed to use a 128 bit cipher.  As you say, we may as well use AES-256 since we wont see any noticeable slowdown.

> Also, I have read about the weaknesses found in AES256 and 192, however it seems they are in very ideal conditions. This paper (March 2011) states that AES256 is the most secure. I don't know much about the authors but it seems legitimate. :D

There were a flurry of attacks on AES-256 in 2009.  One of them was on the full 14 rounds of AES and had a memory and time complexity of 2^99.5.  This is much better than brute-force and makes AES-256 significantly weaker than AES-128.  However, it is a related-key attack which is impractical in most situations.  Another attack building upon that one was able to reduce the complexity down to 2^70 for 11 rounds.  Since AES-256 has 14 rounds, it doesn't apply to real world implementations (and it's also a related-key attack which isn't practical in itself in most circumstances).  

But as Schneier says: "cryptographic attacks always get better, they never get worse."
