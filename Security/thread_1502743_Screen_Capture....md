### Post by BoneKracker on 2010-06-07
> **an0dos said:**
> Perhaps so. On the other hand, I fail to see how this presents a significantly increased risk over using my ISP's DNS. Those records can also  be subpoenaed. In general, companies will try to do what a judge says because you never want to **** off a judge.

 Am I misunderstanding something?

BTW, the parental controls for netgear routers that have "Live Parental Controls" are actually powered by openDNS.
I didn't say it was increased risk over using your own ISP.  You just pulled that out of thin air.

What I meant was that I think having an account with OpenDNS is an increased risk over NOT having an account.  I think using OpenDNS without an account is preferable to using OpenDNS with an account, and it's also preferable to using your ISP's DNS (unless you have a really slow mtt to OpenDNS).

Here's my point: I imagine that when you sign up for _an account_ with OpenDNS (as opposed to just _using_ OpenDNS), it is likely that the amount of information being collected and retained on you is greater.  You see, they can't really correlate behavior using an IP address an "identity", because IP addresses are, more often than not, allocated dynamically), but they can correlate behavior against an _account_.

Yes, the authorities could subpoena both the DNS provider and your ISP and achieve this correlation, but only if the information is being retained.  The minimum amount of information being retained will depend upon the laws, but much more information may be retained if it has value to the DNS provider (i.e., if they can sell it).  The DNS provider cannot correlate an IP address with an identity without the cooperation of the ISP, which they should not be able to obtain without a court order.  But, if you sign up for an account with the DNS provider, you are cooperating in being tracked by identifying yourself every time you do a name service lookup.

Now, you may only be using a pseudo-identity, but that behavioral information still have marketing resarch value, so it will be collected and retained (whereas it would not be collected an retained if you had not signed up for an account).  Once the information is collected and retained, it can later be associated with your _actual_ identity with a court order.

To try and restate that simply: if you sign up with OpenDNS (as opposed to simply using OpenDNS), you are probably causing information to be stored that could later be used against you.  If you never do anything that anybody anywhere could ever object to and you have complete faith that no governmental entity would ever, ever want to take any kind of action against you of any kind because of who you are or what you might someday do, think, or say, then don't worry about this (but you ought to think hard about that before you say that's true).

There are other means of blocking content without subjecting yourself to being tracked.
