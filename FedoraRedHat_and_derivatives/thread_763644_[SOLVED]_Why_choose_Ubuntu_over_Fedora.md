### Post by forrestcupp on 2008-04-23
This is not really a very unbiased place to post this question.  If you post this in an Ubuntu forum, of course you're going to get a bunch of people saying Ubuntu is better.  If you posted this in the Fedora forum, I'm sure they'd all be trashing Ubuntu and saying Fedora is better.

They are both great distro's.  And contrary to popular belief, the Fedora forums are at least almost as active and helpful as the Ubuntu forums.

As far as I can see, there are 2 main differences. 
1. The apt vs. rpm packaging system.  Usually apt and deb's are considered more evolved than rpm's.  But I don't think the "dependency hell" with rpm's and YUM is relevant anymore after advances that have been made.

2. Fedora is a lot more uptight about Free Software.  If that's what you're after, then Fedora is great.  But while Ubuntu is very Free Software minded, they make it much easier and more automated to get non-free stuff, like multimedia and driver support.

### Post by msrinath80 on 2008-05-01
This is quite an interesting discussion. Indeed many folks have pointed out why they favor either distribution while a few others have voiced equivocal opinions. I would like to drop my 0.02$ as well:

I began my GNU/Linux journey with a Red Hat distribution (RH 6.0) on an i486SX @ 33 MHz with 16 MB EDO RAM and a 200MB HDD. I liked it a lot. As time progressed I tried other derived distributions such as Mandrake (now Mandriva) and found them fun too. I read about Debian and Slackware and ran away from them almost instantly. Now I cannot remember why?

I've used most of the Red Hat versions up to RH 9.0 (with Ximian desktop etc.) after which I was not sure whether to switch to Fedora Core 1 or not. Interestingly, I put aside my inexplicable fears and tried Debian (woody) for the first time. I was literally shocked the first time I tried it. It took me quite some time to understand how the aptitude front end worked. I tried very hard to get used to it but failed. Also noteworthy was the ancient software factor in Debain (i.e a Kernel 2.4.x based distribution) which resulted in much of my laptop (IBM Thinkpad 390X) hardware not being detected, thus causing more frustration.

A few years later I heard about Scientific Linux (derived from RHEL sources) from my System Administrator. The Red Hat based label was more than enough incentive for me to switch to it. I used it for some time without problems. As time progressed however, I realized that I was using a very ancient GNOME and bundled software. Curious to try out the latest in FOSS, I started researching distributions again. This time I went in a different direction. I thought if I have tried Debian, I should probably try out Slackware too. I installed Vector Linux to do this. Unfortunately, I was not very impressed with the "Slack" way of managing packages. Then someone told me about LFS (Linux from Scratch) where you build your own GNU/Linux system from scratch. I gave that a shot. Finished my first successful LFS build after 4 days of compiling software. I was very impressed with the responsiveness of the bloat-free system that I had built. However, I soon realized that to get it functional for day-to-day use I would need to go through building X, GNOME, Mozilla etc. This was a little discouraging as I was quite lazy at that time.

Now this was the time I heard people start talking about this Ubuntu thing. I checked out some screen shots of Feisty and was quite impressed. I liked it immediately. I started using it on my Desktop and my new Laptop (Fujitsu S7110). It worked quite well. It detected most of my hardware and I was happy. Same was the case with Gutsy. I thought that finally, my distribution search had come to an end. Unfortunately, after Hardy was released, some of my hardware which worked fine on Gutsy (my HVR 950 tuner in particular) started to show signs of new problems. This was a little frustrating. I tried to find answers to the problems using the forums and Google but was not quite successful. So one day, I decided to check out Fedora 9 (the preview release) and see if it had problems with my tuner as well. To my amazement, it detected the tuner the moment it was plugged in and popped a friendly message in the dmesg log stating that it needed a firmware file to enable the features. I found the firmware by Googling and pretty soon my tuner was working again. Although my Laptop runs Hardy, I will probably switch to Fedora 9 (in 12 days I think it will be released) as it supports my hardware out of the box.

So ultimately in my case, I know for a fact that the search for an ideal distribution will result in me jumping back and forth until I end up with a distribution that completely supports my hardware. Bottom line, it all boils down to hardware support. I know of very few zealots who will sacrifice hardware support to stick with a distribution of their choice for whatever reason. If your NVIDIA/ATI card had zero support (for argument's sake) in Ubuntu or say your sound card does not work with Ubuntu, the fact is that you will almost certainly switch to a GNU/Linux distribution that supports it. Of course one can argue that I could fool around with Hardy a little bit more (like building custom kernels etc.) and my hardware would work just as well. But the real question is do I want to when I have the option of a distribution that supports it out of the box? I think not.

To conclude, my suggestion is similar to what many others have voiced in this thread. Try both distributions. Try a few others if you have the time and interest. To ease later frustration, pick the one which best supports your hardware. Make an informed decision.

### Post by igknighted on 2008-09-22
Why choose Ubuntu over Fedora?

1) You are a new linux user and want a system with a higher level of abstraction (not presented with as many complications)

2) You want a Debian based system (to learn it or because you are more comfortable with it)

3) You have tried both, and find the Ubuntu system preferable or more compatible with your hardware.

You could certainly come up with a list of why you would choose Fedora over Ubuntu (reverse 2 and 3, and add "want the latest versions of software, even mid release", as well as "want a FOSS only distro")

Personally, I am a Fedora user.  I find yum preferable to apt, I find Fedora's implementation of the b43 driver (broadcom) works FAR better than Ubuntu's, and I like Fedora's policy of always pushing out the latest and greatest.  But that is my choice based on my circumstances and preferences, hardly my belief that Fedora is the "better distro".
