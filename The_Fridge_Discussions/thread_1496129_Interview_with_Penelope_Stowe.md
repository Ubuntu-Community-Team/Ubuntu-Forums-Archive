 **Isabell Long: Firstly, please tell us a little about yourself.**

 Penelope Stowe: I&#8217;ve been an Ubuntu user on and off for almost 3 years now (my &#8220;other distro&#8221; is, admittedly, MacOS). Until recently, I was working in publishing, but I&#8217;ve quit my job and am now exploring possibilities for what I&#8217;ll do next. I figure it&#8217;s a good time to be adventurous and push past things I&#8217;d planned, and look at new possibilities.

 I tend to be interested in everything, and, while I don&#8217;t believe in knowledge for knowledge&#8217;s sake, I do believe that anything you learn can be useful and often is.

 **IL: What inspired you to get involved in the Ubuntu community?**

 PS: The short and immediate answer is that a friend badgered me about it enough over about 6 months before I got actively involved. The longer answer is that I finally got to the point where I stopped being too shy to get involved, which had to do a lot with having friends who were involved and were enthusiastic about getting me involved. I&#8217;d been using Ubuntu long enough beforehand that it wasn&#8217;t a huge jump to getting involved, just a personal hurdle.

 As for why I started using Ubuntu, I&#8217;ve always had friends who were Linux geeks, and I finally got around to giving it a try in 2007, and everyone told me Ubuntu was the way to go. I&#8217;ll admit I&#8217;m not a full-time user and I&#8217;m not sure I&#8217;ll ever be. However, I&#8217;ve always been interested in free culture, so using free software is a natural progression from that. I&#8217;m much more a philosophical user than I am a &#8220;this just works&#8221; person, especially as I increasingly need accessibility tools, many of which don&#8217;t &#8220;just work&#8221; yet in any Linux distribution.

 Also, working on Ubuntu is something I can do when I&#8217;m physically unable to do much else. I have a physical disability, and sometimes am limited to things I can do lying down, but as long as I have my laptop I can still be doing things for Ubuntu.

 Finally, I love the Ubuntu community. It&#8217;s one of the friendliest communities I know of any type, and I do think the community is the strongest part of the operating system.

 **IL: What are your roles within the Ubuntu community?**

 PS: My current big project is trying to revive the Ubuntu Accessibility Team. It&#8217;s been going on quietly as a support only team using the mailing list and forums for a few years, but I&#8217;m hoping to get it to a point where it&#8217;s updating documentation for what&#8217;s available, and where there&#8217;s some organisation addressing what the team would like to see in future Ubuntu releases as well as some coordination with upstream. There&#8217;s been a lot of enthusiasm from people for this, so I&#8217;m hoping it all comes together. Accessibility is such a difficult thing because it is so varied. What I need is completely different from what someone with a visual impairment needs. Even people with other mobility problems may have different needs than I have. There&#8217;s also a huge emphasis on discussions to focus on development, and I&#8217;d like to branch that out some - because we really need to update documentation and awareness. Ubuntu could open itself up to a large group of new users if accessibility could be improved, or even if people knew what already existed.

 I am also one of the team that runs the Ubuntu User Days for new users. We started Ubuntu User Days to provide a day of more basic &#8220;how to&#8221; for setting-up and using Ubuntu, and the first one was a great success. The next one is June 5th. I&#8217;m hoping it goes as well as the first. We&#8217;ve got so many ideas for what we want to have happen. It&#8217;s nice to see it all come together.

 Finally, I&#8217;m active in Ubuntu Women where I have been helping to get the mentoring program running again, and have been doing anything else they ask me to do. I was a little less active towards the end of the Lucid cycle. However, I&#8217;ll be getting more involved again as I have more time.

 **IL: You&#8217;ve done quite a lot in the short time you have been involved in Ubuntu. Is there anything you haven&#8217;t done that you&#8217;d like to try?**

 PS: A better question is if there&#8217;s anything I haven&#8217;t done that I don&#8217;t want to try. I definitely want to get involved with documentation. I think it&#8217;s really important, plus it&#8217;s somewhere that I can put skills I already have to use. I&#8217;d also like to learn how to bug triage and help out the bug squad. Also, one of the things I&#8217;m going to do - when not working - is try to learn to program. I took a couple of programming courses in college, and so will probably try to refresh my memory of those languages and pick up Python. I&#8217;m sure there are other things I either haven&#8217;t thought of, or am not remembering that I want to do, but generally it comes down to: if I hear about it, it&#8217;s probably on my long list of things to learn or do eventually when I have time/energy/resources.

 **IL: What other things are you interested in outside of OSS and Ubuntu?**

 PS: I&#8217;m a pretty solid book geek (mostly fantasy, but I do branch out). My main client at the job I just left was a science fiction and fantasy publisher, so it was a wonderful way to feed my book addiction. I&#8217;m also very interested in disability studies and disability rights, and I&#8217;m quite interested in how much of the disability rights and studies movements these days happens online as well as offline.

 My non-techblog is [wheeledtraveler.blogspot.com]("http://wheeledtraveler.blogspot.com"), although it&#8217;s seen less use recently as I&#8217;ve traveled less and not been spending time blogging. Since I theoretically have more time now, that should change.

 Originally posted by Isabell Long in [Full Circle Magazine Issue #37]("http://fullcirclemagazine.org/issue-37/") on May 28, 2010
