### Post by stwschool on 2009-07-12
I saw a post a couple of pages down about a mission to convert as many people as possible. Quite aside from the fact that if Windows users tried to 'convert' me I'd probably have to smack them over the head with a frying pan, I have to ask do we really want to dumb down the linux gene pool?

I admit, when I started on Linux, like most I suspect, I got a bit evangelical about it. The thing is really I was just banging my head against a brick wall and eventually realised that actually it doesn't matter to me if other people use linux. It's just an operating system.

Another thought occurs to me as well, however. Surely, we are now in a golden age of linux. What do I mean by that? Well, we have the hardware compatibility which has long been the bane of life on linux (yes there are issues with ATI and intel but you get the idea, it's possible to get a good PC that will run on linux and most peripherals work well). With Wine we can use a huge range of Windows software in addition to a fantastic library of linux apps, and almost anything else we can run in virtualbox. We have easy-to-use GUIs and powerful CLIs. More importantly, linux comes with an automatic IQ barrier.

Surely, if we end up with a whole bunch of people with no clue using linux, we too will have a lot of problems to contend with as our software gets dumbed down and trojans run rampant.

Most people just don't have the brainpower to use a computer properly (ie you tell them not to install any old random crap after de-virusing their computer for the 27th time and they go and do it again). One of the things that helps linux maintain its cleanliness is that most of its users are intelligent, and choose to educate themselves about computers, after all they chose linux, so they must be consciously making decisions about computing and not just using whatever's there.

So, I ask all of you, do you really want windows users on linux? I for one am unsure.

### Post by aysiu on 2009-07-12
I'm sure a lot of hardcore purist Linux users already feel the userbase *has* been dumbed down just because people like me are using Linux (I cannot do Slackware, Gentoo, LFS, or even Arch).

I'm not a system administrator, a programmer, a hacker, or a gamer. I just web browse, email, photo organize, listen to music, and do some light website editing. I prefer point-and-click to the terminal most of the time, and I don't build my own computers.

Am I dumbed down enough?

I don't know. I'm kind of glad that I'm able to use Linux, even though some people are probably horrified that someone as uneducated as I am could use it.

Yes, malware will come as Linux gets bigger, but it'll be almost entirely social-engineering-based malware, so smart users won't have to worry about it, especially as implementations of AppArmor become more robust. There are only benefits to Linux getting more popular. Very few downsides. If you want to be extra "elite," you can move to FreeBSD.

Bad coders make bad code, well sure. But as far as I can tell, poorly-made applications tend *not* to get used. They are either abandoned, improved, or incorporated into other projects. I'm going to go out on a limb and suggest that a lot of the time, Windows-users stick with buggy software that they've paid for because they want to get their money's worth, or because they don't know any better. The only thing that ties me to specific Linux applications, by comparison, is familiarity or perhaps some vague emotional attachment. I can't think of many applications in the repositories that don't have duplicates. If Transmission was buggy and lame, I'd switch to Deluge instead. And so it continues. All stuff in the repositories has to go through some level of quality control, and the idea that bug-ridden, crashy applications can make it on to the default desktop is almost unthinkable. 

Any how, I disagree with the OP's assertion that there is any sort of IQ barrier to using Ubuntu. All it takes is knowledge that it exists and having the wherewithal to burn an ISO image. That puts it beyond the reach of many, sure, but not because they are stupid. You can't blame anybody for not knowing what Linux is, or for never having burnt an ISO image before. My girlfriend uses Ubuntu exclusively, and she knows nary a thing about Linux or computers in general. She bought a laptop from Dell with Ubuntu pre-installed, which completely removed the installation barrier. For most purposes, using Ubuntu is far easier than using Windows. I'd say that most clueless computer users only use their computers for browsing the web, playing simple games, and editing documents. All they need to do on Ubuntu is figure out how the Gnome menu works, and any halfwit can do that. 

The only noobish questions that come up around here that irk me are the ones where people complain that Ubuntu doesn't work *exactly* like Windows. Or when people encounter one unique hardware-specific problem and then arrogantly declare that "Ubuntu will never succeed on the desktop so long as this remains a problem!"

Still, advanced users have a choice. Nobody is going to take the command line away from you.
