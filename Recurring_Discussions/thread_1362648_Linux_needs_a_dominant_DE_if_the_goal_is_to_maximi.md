### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2009-12-23
I disagree about needing a "dominant" DE in particular. The fact is, no matter how fancy it is from a UI standpoint, other situations will want / need their own environments. For example enhanced text-based interfaces, mobile devices and toaster ovens are never going to run desktop environments for desktops.

The current direction is working very well. KDE and GNOME, with FreeDesktop as a middle man, are making great steps towards consolidating around common protocols and (where applicable) back-end tools. As a trivial example, the notification frameworks used by KDE4 and GNOME are moving closer together and some nice work is going on to merge the two standards.

We have somewhat disparate desktop environments, but they rely on a common base. Those commonalities are reaching further and further as time goes on. I think this situation gives us a stronger, more flexible platform. For example, operating systems like Maemo, [litl]("http://blogs.gnome.org/lucasr/2009/11/04/litl-webbook-some-technical-comments/"), ChromiumOS and Moblin can pop into existence easily and share that base in common. (Although it is probably worth noting that the four I listed have many foundations specific to GNOME. Work in progress).

I think it is a delusion to think that one environment could fit everyone based solely on form factor. Instead of wasting time chasing that delusion, focus should go in to optimizing the space *between* desktop environments, where the significant delta we see today (eg: Phonon vs. GStreamer) is not really necessary. With work on those foundations, KDE and GNOME would live happily ever after and the free desktop ecosystem would come out stronger.

Maybe "similar" is too subjective in this case.  Personally I have zero difficulty identifying (among other things)

- buttons (rectangular/square(-ish) items bearing text and/or images, and provide some kind of visual feedback when moused over -- a glow, outline, colour change, or cursor change)

- a scroll bar (usually a vertical/horizontal rectangle located along the edges of a frame or window, with buttons on the extremities and some kind of sliding mechanism in the middle)

- Save-As dialogs (some kind of file/directory browser, with a text field for a file name, and a button that says "Save", and often a picture of a diskette)

The point is that these kinds of standard UI elements are *very* easy to identify, even if they are styled differently, or put in different places within a larger container.  You see a button and you know it's something you can click on.  If you can't tell then you're either computer-illiterate (in which case you no longer fall into the "average computer user" category), or the UI is just very badly-designed.

Look at the previous page where there were screenshots of Chrome, MSN Messenger, and WinAmp.  They all look completely different from a UI/toolkit perspective, and yet any average computer user could use any of them and figure out how to make them work.  They could figure out where the menus are, what the buttons are, etc...

Keyboard shortcuts on the other hand I will agree should be as uniform as possible.  Things like Ctrl-A, Ctrl-S, Ctrl-Z, Ctrl-C, Ctrl-X, and Ctrl-V should be select-all, save, undo, copy, cut, and paste across everything (well, except for oddball, niche things like Vi and Emacs maybe).  But for your average computer user those are standards that one should be able to expect.

Likewise, dialog boxes and windows should be laid out in an obvious way to guide workflow.  The specific toolkits and themes used shouldn't matter if the elements are placed in a logical, sensible order.  Human Interface Guidelines (used by most big software projects and companies) govern this sort of layout, and they should be followed for a reason.

> **ubuntuman001 said:**
> I am merely noting what needs to happen if Linux is ever going to be more mainstream in desktop computing (yup we already rock at server computing) which is the interest of many commercial and community groups and even individuals.

There is already a dominant desktop for commercially-supported desktop Linux distributions: Gnome.  It is the default desktop environment on the industry's three heavyweights: Red Hat Enterprise Linux Desktop, SUSE Linux Enterprise Desktop, and of course, Ubuntu.  This has been the case for several years, so your "note" is not new or significant at this time.  In fact, having a dominant desktop hasn't helped the popularity of desktop Linux at all!

Do not labour under the oft-stated, but false view that consumers want a default.  That is not the case.  Ask any experienced marketer and they will tell you that consumers actually want the opposite: customisation for their individual needs, and technologies that "just work".

Consumers want a replacement, whether or not it looks or feels familiar, and do not care for defaults.  That means if Linux is to become more mainstream in desktop computing, it needs to be able to do everything that Windows does, but do it easier, more efficiently, more effectively, and provide wider range of means to do them.  This means more high-quality applications, out-of-the-box hardware support, easy software installation/updating/removal, support for multimedia content (including DRM-protected media), and perfect compatibility with MS Office file formats.  So far, no Linux desktop distribution comes anywhere near meeting all those requirements.  Obviously, it will be nigh impossible to meet all those requirements without heavy financial investment (for licensing) and compromises in the FOSS philosophy (to implement proprietary technologies under non-disclosure agreements).

A dominant desktop environment for Linux should be the last of worries.
