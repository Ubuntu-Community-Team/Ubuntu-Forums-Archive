### Post by aysiu on 2008-12-17
I like the physical form of Mac hardware (yes, I know the internals are the same internals on non-Apple computers; I'm talking about the externals). The keys feel nice and not cheapy. I like the power button. I like the magnetic power cord. I like the ease with which the lid opens and closes but the hinge doesn't come loose easily. I like the look of the OS (Aqua, metal, etc.). The icons are extremely high resolution.

And if you buy into the whole Apple experience (Mac OS X, "Mac" external hard drive, Airport Extreme, iPhone, iTunes, Apple Care), then everything integrates very nicely together. That's what Apple provides you with--an experience.

Now, that said, there are a lot of reasons I don't use Mac OS X, and they mainly have to do with the interface. I prefer an actual maximize to zoom. I like the option to resize windows from any corner. I want an Alt-Tab to always restore a minimized window when I select it. I prefer to work in windows and not by application. I like keyboard shortcuts for everything, not just what Apple decides keyboard shortcuts are good for. I like to cut and paste files, not just copy and paste them. I like a package manager for installing applications.

Those are just some examples of Mac annoyances for me.

Ultimately, it's up to you. Find an Apple store or find a friend who owns a Mac and play around on one and see if you like it. My wife loves her Macbook Pro.

### Post by simonbanyard on 2008-12-18
> **hellion0 said:**
> Playing devil's advocate ...
Ubuntu's still better, though, and it's widening the gap.

How so? You have highlighted some of the misinformation out there such as right clicking, but the make a very sweeping statement. I'm not having a go, I'm interested in your opinion.

Before I go on, I'm typing this using Ubuntu 8.10, of which I am a huge fan. I run it on a Dell Vostro 1700 Laptop, I also have an iMac. My take on things is that OS X is an *extremely* advanced OS. It is elegantly simple - remember that most people aren't as advanced in their understanding of computer OS's as most of us here. It works really well too. I can honestly say that I have never had a problem with it, which is down in part to the closed nature of it. To be fair to Apple, It is only the UI layer and their apps that are proprietary, the kernel is open-source [(Darwin)]("http://developer.apple.com/opensource/index.html"). Apple have contributed quite a bit to the FLOSS community too. Certainly webkit should be applauded for standards compliance. It's important to remember that OS X is Unix too.

IMHO there are still *too* many broken bits of Linux. Wireless is still very fuzzy (the Atheros chipset is *extremely* common amongst the netbooks - support is shaky at best), HSDPA modem support is broken by the lastest hal-info package. Admittedly, Apple only have their products to support, making the support of OS X less onerous. Finder, which is what the desktop environment is called, is really simple to use. It is very intuitive as Apple are *obsessed* with [usability]("http://developer.apple.com/documentation/userexperience/Conceptual/AppleHIGuidelines/XHIGIntro/chapter_1_section_1.html#//apple_ref/doc/uid/TP30000894-TP6"). I use the Gnome desktop as I'm not a fan of KDE 3.5 (too ***K***lunky) and KDE 4 IMO isn't mature enough, although it looks promising. I personally like gnome, although I find it a bit cartoony. I know that there are other window manages out there, but most of them are still not mature enough for daily use. Interestingly enough, I like GnuStep as well!

Not so long ago Mark Shuttleworth pointed to Mac OS X as the benchmark that Ubuntu must compare itself to in terms of usability and finish. It is getting there but there is still along way to go. A massive step would be to adopt and adapt Apple's [interface design guidelines]("http://developer.apple.com/documentation/userexperience/Conceptual/AppleHIGuidelines/XHIGIntro/chapter_1_section_1.html#//apple_ref/doc/uid/TP30000894-TP6"), or at least develop a similar set of guidelines *and* enforce them. At the moment linux still isn't for beginners, unless you know what you are doing with computers, Linux is second best next to OS X, ***at the moment***. Windows isn't even third...
