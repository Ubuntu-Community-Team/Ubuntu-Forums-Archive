### Post by undecim on 2010-04-06
I have three main reasons.

My first reason is that Linux is free (as in "free speech")

Clickin "I Agree" to one of those EULA's is the same legally binding action as putting your signature on a contract. I don't want to sign a contract every time I change the 1s and 0s on my hard drive.

It's not that I'm going to run out of ink in my virtual pen or anything, but I don't want Microsoft telling me "You are not allowed to share this with your friends, no matter how much they need it" or, "You are not allowed to change this software at all, even if it's just for yourself."

If Windows doesn't work the way you want it to, then tough luck, because you aren't allowed to fix it. If your friend can't read a document you sent him because he didn't shell out another three-digit sum for the latest Office, too bad because you can't (legally) let give him your copy.

My second reason is the community. If you ever have a problem with your computer, just post in these forums and you will get several people looking into your problem. I spend at least an hour per day helping people on these forums. If I find a problem that I'm not quite sure about, I'll do research on it and subscribe to the thread so that I can stay updated and respond quickly to any new related questions or problems that arise.

And then, when I have a problem or question that I can't answer on my own, I come here and get that same kind of help. I've not once seen a forum user insult someone or tell them to RTFM, or anything like that. Everyone I've had the pleasure of interacting with with in the community has always been kind and helpful.

But even if freedom and the community were non-issues, I would still use Ubuntu because it's technologically superior.

Don't like the default theme? Change it. Can't find a theme you like? Make a new one.

Need something done automatically? Writing a bash script is surprisingly simple for even some of the most comlicated tasks. You can set up a cron job to run it every X minutes or hours or days.

Ever worry about getting a trojan when you download applications from the internet? Just use Ubuntu's software center to download trusted, signed applications.

And you don't have to worry about viruses spreading to your computer over the network even. Ubuntu has all ports closed by default.

And that means you don't need to worry about anti-virus software, which in turn means less code slowing your computer down.

Computer won't boot? No problem. The same disc that you use to install Ubuntu can also run a live environment so that you can get a word processor and finish that paper of your on time instead of having to fix the problem first. Then when you are done with your paper, that same live cd can usually be used to help fix the problem.
