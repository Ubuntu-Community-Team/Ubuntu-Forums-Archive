### Post by aysiu on 2012-02-29
Google won me over when in 2006 they refused to hand over user search data to the US government when the other major search engines handed it over without a fight.

That won't happen every time, and there clearly are times when they--like any other corporation--will hand over user data to law enforcement. But they at least care a little bit more than the other companies.

They provide great services, and, more importantly (despite all this "monopoly" talk) there is no vendor lock-in with Google. They use common file formats, they allow you to easily export your information if you decide to leave Google services, their browser supports web standards.

As far as I'm concerned, a monopoly is only dangerous if it leads to vendor lock-in--if you cannot use anything else because you'd lose functionality and not because the other product isn't better. Yes, they have a virtual monopoly on search, but if a better search engine came along, nothing would stop people from using that search engine instead. Likewise, a lot of people like Gmail. If Gmail became the *de facto* email, nothing would stop people from using other email services instead.

### Post by synfluent on 2012-02-29
A number of us began our careers in this industry during the 'sixties and 'seventies when openess and sharing of code was the only to spread the word about what we were doing.

Once the economic momentum kicked in, our world was changed dramatically. Anybody remember Bill Gates' scathing letter to Byte regarding software piracy?

Now, we are working in a world which has taken the economic proposition to new level- and a scary level to many of us- with the devil's bargain of 'use my software tool in exchange giving me your personal data' being almost a status quo.

Many of us 'worked' the character-based internet and moved things along quite nicely until the Mozilla browser came along and helped create the web. It was inevitable even back in 1993 that the economic momentum would shift to the web and find it's economic model.

Google's search engine does provide a service and I give up a little autonomy every time I type in some arcane item that interests me. So far this has not been such an issue but I feel that a tipping point is quickly coming.

I no longer stay logged in to google for mail and I find myself turning off my Airport card unless I have a need to access cloud files and I am skeptical of any free software tool that requires me to connect to their servers. I just leave it alone.

I bristle at the close coupling required with iTunes to use my iPhone and iPad have begun to look for ways to communicate outside of that paradigm, too.

I certainly do not suggest that I would return to loading up punched tape from a TTY through an acoustic coupler to a time-shared 360 at some university.

No, I would just like to have a little bit of that Splendid Isolation that Zevon sang about.

And, a little less google watching over me.
