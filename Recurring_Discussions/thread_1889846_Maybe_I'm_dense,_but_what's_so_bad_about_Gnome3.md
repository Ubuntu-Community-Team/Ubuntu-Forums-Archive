### Post by 3Miro on 2011-12-02
Unity and Gnome-shell tend to be less efficient, especially if you like a mouse-only mode of operation. Here is my long explanation that I wrote as a response to a Unity-fanboy:

[http://ubuntuforums.org/showpost.php?p=11498991&postcount=37](http://ubuntuforums.org/showpost.php?p=11498991&postcount=37)

Here is the entire thread:
[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1888412](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1888412)

When it comes to comparison between Unity and Gnome-shell vs the classic Gnome 2, KDE, XFCE and LXDE, the best summary is: while classical interface aims to put as much as possible on the screen so you can gain easy access to all features, Unity and GS tend to give you the bare minimum which results in a cleaner (less cluttered) but also less efficient interface.

Ultimately people are different and no interface would work good for everyone. People should look at alternatives and pick what works best for them.

Having said that, most of the GS vs Unity stuff right now is just people trolling.

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-12-04
I want to preface this, lest I'm misread: I know you're not talking about the Shell, and neither was the previous post, but I want to make clear that I'm not, either. With the extension base, the Shell is the most customizable thing ever, and the issue is with core Gnome settings. 

For core Gnome settings, I don't like seeing "advanced" settings pushed off to another settings manager (particularly when all other settings have been grouped into a single settings dashboard.) Gnome Tweak Tool is officially supported, and yet it seems to want to remind you that it's a hack, which it isn't, even. It's a tiny app included in the repos for every Gnome 3 distro, but not installed by default. And all its settings correspond to settings for the other settings manager screens, from power management to appearance and so on. Why a separate tool? Why not an "Advanced" button on the corresponding individual settings screens? There's just nothing that the "Advanced" settings have in common that they might be grouped by. The only trait they seem to share is "settings that would have otherwise been left out." 

It's really no different from having Mouse and Touchpad separate from Pointing Devices. It's not new to Gnome 3 - it's just getting used to Gnome's weird philosophy for settings management all over again.

For that matter, I never found Ubuntu Tweak necessary or useful - it's similarly a random collection of settings that are either difficult to find or absent in other GUI settings managers. Rather than remember where things are in this or that settings manager, eventually, you just give up and use gconf (and now dconf, though not all of gconf's settings have been migrated yet, meaning both are in play.)

So it's nothing new. Having things moved around is a nice reminder of how terrible Gnome is at organizing settings and a new shot of the old worry that something you depend on is going to disappear in an update.



An example from my experience, if it'll help illustrate what I mean, on this go around went like this: by default, closing my netbook lid suspended the machine and locked the screen, and the power button brought up the Shut Down interactive. At home, I use my netbook to listen to the radio, I might have a download running in the background, etc.; at work, it's my gradebook, and I can't well type my password and wait ten seconds for the machine to wake up when someone steps into class late. 

I needed the power button, and only the power button, to suspend, and through a happy accident, in 11.04, I had not only this, but the option of suspending from the session menu instead, which locked the screen.

Disabling suspend was easy - "Power" settings - but ran into a bug that caused the backlight to shut off instead. Fixed that in Screen. The only control for the power button itself was in an unexpected place in dconf (I.e., *not* under power management) and it turned out that screen lock was hardcoded into the screensaver by the Ubuntu folks, meaning I had to disable it completely, in another unlikely place in dconf (Desktop? Really?) Everything works now, and I can still suspend from the Switch User screen if I need to lock the PC. But in the process of Google searching for these keys, I find that Gnome briefly considered not allowing the lid-close behavior to be configured *at all*, and that Ubuntu's screensaver hack is something they're "considering" rethinking for Precise.

This doesn't seem reasonable to me. I don't like to have to resort to hacks like this for basic functionality and be reminded that the powers that be are indifferent to the presence of configuration features that are, if I can say it, easy to get to even in Windows. For every one person like me who goes to the trouble of finding things, there are ten people who ask to find them in the support forums, and ten people for each of them who don't bother and decide to just live with it, or in extreme cases, choose a different desktop environment or OS because the one they had didn't work.

Slow, yes, antiquated, no. It's slow because you only have so much screen-edge space to put quick-access items in, so you have to prioritize. You have lots of programs, and you don't use most of them very often, so you stick them on the menu, which is slow, but has room for a lot of stuff. Meanwhile, you put quick-launch icons for your most commonly used programs across the top panel (or even the bottom one if you want), and you have a window list on the bottom panel for quick access to open windows (which is very important, as switching between open windows is a more common action than launching a new window, and probably the single most common interaction a user has with the desktop environment).

In other words, the menu system is slow because it was designed for a task for which speed is not the number-one priority, and because other tasks needed speed more. It is not a step forward to come up with a faster replacement for the menu at the expense of these tasks.

The problem with Gnome 3 shell and Unity is that they combine the concepts of window list and quick-launch, and in doing so, make the window list much slower to interact with. Gnome 3 fallback mode doesn't have this problem, but also has some significant feature regressions from Gnome 2.

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2012-06-07
SilentThunderStorm, the one thing I don't think you're accounting for here is that Compiz was never a part of Gnome. Ubuntu used it as the default window manager under Gnome 2, but Gnome's own window manager at the time was Metacity. Metacity is now Mutter, and Compiz is still Compiz and still Ubuntu's default (now with the Unity plugin added to replace Gnome Shell.) Gnome didn't remove Compiz features, because Compiz features are not Gnome's features, and there was no "move to Mutter" because Mutter *is* Metacity. If you're using Gnome Shell, then you're opting not to use Compiz, the same as if you were using Metacity under Gnome 2.

The cube and trailfocus are still in Compiz, and you can still run a Gnome Classic session with Compiz. There's no conflict with Gnome 3 there (although trailfocus started being glitchy for me back in 11.04, and I haven't run Compiz in 11.10 or 12.04, so I don't know what condition the plugin is in.) In terms of workflow, there's no difference between the cube and any other method of managing workspaces, and GTK3 now has a built-in feature (new to 3.4) to dim inactive windows if the theme supports it, but there's nothing keeping you from using Compiz and the same old plugins, either.

Customization is great if there are actually developers to support for all of the options that are available. Frankly, in my experience, Compiz plugins have tended to break without anyone taking much notice, and I'm rather glad to be done with it, myself, but it's exactly as available and functional as it ever was.
