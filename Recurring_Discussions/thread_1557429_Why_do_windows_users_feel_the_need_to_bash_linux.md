### Post by rjbl on 2010-08-21
At the moment it certainly looks like there is a lot of whispered FUD about the 'Net, particularly aimed at Ubuntu and OpenOffice. There have been a couple of recent 'think pieces' in the UK's self-appointed 'opinion formers' - like the Telegraph and Guardian.

Their narrative runs as follows:-

1. Linux is strictly for Geeks, real people can't use it.

2. Ubuntu is a bitch to install and can't recognise common hardware

3. Installing / De-installing applications is difficult and error-prone.

4. There aren't enough GNU/Linux applications available to run a functional computing system.

5. Open Office is crap - because it does not interoperate with Microsoft Office.

Of course, the narrative above is complete nonsense.

What might be its hidden message? Well, the Microsoft Corporation may well feel a bit threatened at this time. Its core products; the Windows Operating Systems and its Office suite are not having a Good Time at the moment, are they? Vista bombed with the OEMs and consumers; Windows 7 hasn't really recovered much of the lost ground. The big US and English-speaking corporates have shown enormous reluctance to rebuild their systems around new hardware and Windows 7. By and large they ain't shifting from XP. 

Similarly, there has been no great enthusiasm to 'upgrade' business OA beyond Office 2000 and some movement towards Open Office /Star Office in the English-speaking world. Star / Open Office took most of the European business market years ago. 

The Server OS market? Windows lost the global Internet eight years since, very few public servers out in cyberspace are Windows. Most of the ISPs base their service on GNU/Linux - it is cheap to run and maintain and bullet-proof. 

The games market? Seems to have gone dedicated console. Microsoft have shut down their core game product - the redoubtable Flight Simulator. FSX bombed in the market place totally and they don't do any other games.

Maybe, just maybe, Microsoft feel that their world is beginning to slip slide away? 
 

rjbl

Also, there's a very simple reason that big corporations haven't switched to 7 yet: rebuilding a system entirely is a *massive* job. There are many, many factors you have to consider, and it takes a long time to plan and execute a switch.
