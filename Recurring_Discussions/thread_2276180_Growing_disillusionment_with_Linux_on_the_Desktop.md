### Post by freakalad on 2015-04-30
I've been an avid (GNU/)Linux user for close to (more than?) a decade & have heartily been drinking the CoolAid.
I'm still running it on my own machines, but bitter experience has shown me that it's simply not suitable to laymen users day-to-day (with very few exceptions - I've tried MANY times). I work in ICT & do a lot of support work, and this is a battle I've lost many times over.

On servers, "cloud" & embedded it's pretty (mostly) rock-solid, even with systemd, but on the desktop it is *still* a situation where I have to live with caveats & exceptions, and increasing I'm simply sick & tired of having to put up with all these idiosyncrasies - it's like a slow death by thousands of little paper-cuts.

A very simple but illustrative example would be touch-screens.
I now have 2 laptops that has a multi-touch screen, but OS support is *incredibly* poor, despite this becoming the norm. 
Hardware is capable, but there has been very little advances made in this area *at all* - it's as if the de-facto paradigm is *still* stuck in a terminal, whereas the rest of the world is simply moving on. People took Mark, Canonical & Co to task & have been vocally critical of the move in this direction, but at least they're trying to pull us into a moving future - kicking & screaming if they must.
There are many other examples I could give.

I've not done distro-hopping in several years, but of late I was compelled to, in order to find something that works for me - rather than me constantly *on it* - and I'm very reluctantly & sadly coming to the conclusion that "Linux or the Desktop" simply does not cut it!

At this rate we're going to see the Mattel Hoverboards & flying Deloreans long before we see the arrival of the "Year of the Linux Desktop".

And maybe that's OK.

Maybe the desktop is not the best place for Linux, and maybe what we really need is a glorified browser host, and in that regard, maybe Chromebooks are better than traditional laptops & desktops (like my beefy X1 Carbon I'm trying to find a great OS for)?
Maybe Windows or OS/X with Linux VM's are a better way to go?

MS have made some significantly meaningful strides in the last few years (and I'm in no way a MS-fan), and with W10 poised to take on RasPi's, Arduino's & 3D printing (with Docker coming in the mix), I'm really hard-pressed to find a reason to stick with the Linux desktop beyond habit if I can have a pleasant UX with a (mostly fully) FLOSS stack on top of it.

People may want to point to the "camaraderie of the community", but this is a hard pill for me to swallow.
It is true that at some point the IRC channels were useful, but at present #ubuntu does absolutely nothing to contribute to a productive discussion - I've lurked & helped, but by own queries is frequently met with.... less than polite or helpful feedback, and I can see how newcomers are quickly turned off by flippant comments (eg. "RTFM" & "LMGTFY").
Other niche-related channels might be more useful, but it's pretty hit & miss.

Sadly, I do think Ubuntu is drifting away from being "Linux for human beings", and increasingly back to being the domain of DA's, DevOps & systems Engineers.

This post is not meant to be belligerent or cynical, but illustrative of a point that's dawning on me: I've exhausted my patience working *ON* the tool (my computer, my desktop), and I need to be able to rely on & work *WITH* this tool.

I've committed a decade (maybe more?) of my professional career to working extensively with/in/on Linux, but maybe it's time for me to stand back, take stock & ask myself if this isn't mostly wishful thinking?

### Post by d-cosner on 2015-05-01
In my opinion there are a few things keeping Linux from being main stream on the desktop.

Most people start out on Window, it comes on their PC and they are taught on Windows in school. People acquire a Windows mindset, they end up expecting Linux to work the same. The development of most desktop environments is also a huge problem. Microsoft stays with pretty much the same environment for years before any real change happens. Everything more or less just works and stays that way. Well, other than Windows 8 but it serves to illustrate how people react to drastic changes.

Gnome, KDE, Cinnamon, Unity, etc all started out missing features, they evolved into something good in the end but how many users want to run something that's not finished? We see this cycle over and over and some things just never get fixed... It's kind of hard for manufacturers to support something that can drastically change at any given time. Red Hat enterprise does stay with a mature DE but it looks old and unappealing to new users.

Application updates are a problem too. Users want current versions of the applications they use everyday. They do not care so much about the underlying base of the system but they care a lot about their favorite applications. A Windows user can update their applications by going to a site, downloading the new version and installing it. Granted this is not a very safe practice but that is what they know. In Linux they have to wait until the next release in most cases to have their application updates. Sure, there are ppa repos but installing them scares off new users from using them.

Hardware support is another big problem. I see new users struggling everyday with their graphics card, wifi and printers. These devices are generally supported in Windows and the Mac OS, other than where instances of planned obsolesce by the vendor happen. How is a hardware vendor supposed to provide Linux support when it drastically changes all the time? Take Ubuntu for example, each new release every single package needs to be re-compiled for the new release!  

Experienced users can find solutions to every one of these problems but new users struggle and often end up giving up. There are solutions in the works, Snappy will greatly improve package management. As it stands right now Ubuntu is the only distribution that really seems to want the desktop. Think about this too, say Ubuntu succeeds and Ubuntu desktops start appearing in stores for the average consumer. The competitors will most likely try to claim patent and intellectual property infringements. Look at Android, vendors are forced to pay a Microsoft tax because it is cheaper than fighting them in endless court battles.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-05-01
Without a specific distribution that's preinstalled on a readily available big-box product, the notion of Linux displacing its desktop competitors will always be a myth and a pipedream. Not enough people care enough about the issues that motivate the typical Linus enthusiast to ever constitute any kind of influential market demographic. Almost everyone considers a "computer" to be the hardware combined with the software that runs on it. Consumers consider computers and computing devices to be unitary things, and that's how they want to buy and use them.

Things like touchpads, etc. are often inextricably intertwined with proprietary code that makes them usable, or even at all functional.  If vendors do not write Linux code, then support in Linux will *always* be non-existent or marginally adequate (see Nouveau, for example.)

The people who make and use Linux should focus on making Linux as good as they can make it, and let the chips fall where they may. That is *not* the same as seeking to displace Microsoft and Apple.  That's totally out of the control of Linux.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-05-02
> **jimmy-frydkaer said:**
> 


Blaming Ubuntu, or GNU/Linux in general, for driver problems...


Drivers are a market issue. Vendors create drivers for the hardware they want to sell to the Windows market.  That's the incentive.  These same vendors perceive no Linux market for their products, so they have no incentive to write Linux drivers. 

Add to that the simple reality that FOSS driver developers are dependent on trying to reverse-engineer a hardware peripheral if the vendor does not release sufficient data about the device, and few do.

So, yes, it is naive to come to Linux and expect the same level of driver support found in Windows, especially for new products.

That said, driver support in Linux is remarkably good in general, and the kernel's ablity to identify hardware and load the correct drivers is strong. 

It's not like everything is cookies and cream in Windows.  E.g., the Win7 install image has no network drivers, which means it cannot access Microsoft servers to complete the installation.  Nor does it warn the user.  Users must have already secured the correct driver for their specfic hardware, burned it to a USB stick, and have it attached and be ready to install that driver early in the install process.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-05-05
> **Stinger said:**
> 
 
Ubuntu base system, with improved UEFI install routine. 

UEFI, as a BIOS replacement, will almost certainly be universal.  As long as vendors don't see a financial incentive to cater to the Linux market at the expense of sales in the Windows market, they will implement the Windows-specific implementation of secure boot that Microsoft requires for certification. (Confusingly called "Secure Boot", although secure boot capability is a useful  thing that UEFI enables and could just as easily be offered via a non-vendor-lock-in approach, assuming some organization was willing to take on the role.)

> Functionality out of the box like Mint, with improved graphics card support...

If proprietary video drivers are to be shipped and enabled with distributions, then that effort must come from Nvidia and AMD. FOSS drivers will always be as good as FOSS developers can make them given constraints of time, personnel and available information.

Whether distributions package and ship non-FOSS and patent-encumbered codecs, etc., depends on each distribution's ideological stance and, importantly, its, and its customers', exposure to potential legal action from patent holders, which is primarily determined by its legal location.  (Red Hat, for example, is a U.S. registered corporation so if it shipped patent-encumbered code, both it and its customers could be liable to legal action and, because Red Hat indemnifies its customers, it would pick up the bill.)

> A sexy, fast, no nonsense, innovative Desktop Environment like elementary OS, with improved stability, locales support and better support for network shares like Ubuntu and Mint. 

Whether Elementary's Freya should be the standard others follow is debatable. But, yes, things should work.  Chasing networking compatibility with Windows and OS X is very likely a never-ending process --- a perpetual game of catch-up -- given their ability to make life hard for the competition anytime they wish.

> Basically, everything needed for building a better, close to perfect, Linux desktop is already available, but no distro I know of has had the skills to combine all this into **a close to perfect Linux desktop**

In the end, we can't do anything with a 'desktop'.  It's applications that motivate platform choice.  Because applications are almost always available for any desktop on any Linux distribution, we sometimes forget that. But, it's the need to use Office, or Adobe, or Whatever, that keeps people on Windows or OS X, and compels them to look for direct equvialents before they dare migrate to Linux, no matter how disgruntled they might otherwise be with Windows as a desktop.

So, I'm all for better desktops. But, I think we're deceiving ourselves if we think the desktop alone is enough to attract and keep users.  Linux needs applications that deliver capabilities Windows and OS X users didn't know they wanted and can't get on those platforms.

### Post by SeijiSensei on 2015-05-05
Linux Mint plays fast-and-loose with patents and copyrights.  You cannot legally distribute software like proprietary video and network drivers, support for DVD and MP3 playback, etc., in jurisdictions like the US and Japan.  Making a copy of Ubuntu that includes all these proprietary items would require paying licensing fees to the patent and copyright holders. > 

The NGO writes that the switch to LibreOffice is a consequence of a June 2012 law, which says that free and open source should be the default option for the country’s public administrations.

The Ministry of Defence is the first central government organisation to switch to an open source office productivity suite. However, there are many regions, provinces and city administrations in Italy that use LibreOffice, including the Regione Emilia Romagna, the provinces of Perugia, Cremona, Macerata, Bolzano and Trento, and the cities of Bologna, Piacenza and Reggio Emilia.
Not alone

The project is also one of Europe’s largest. The largest European public administration using free software office suites is the French Interior Ministry, with some 240,000 desktops. In France, many ministries use open source office suites such as LibreOffice, including the Tax Agency, the Minstry of Finance, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and the Ministry of Agriculture. LibreOffice is used on some 72,000 PC workstation at the French Gendarmerie.

In June 2014, the government of Extremadura (Spain) confirmed that 10,000 PCs in its healthcare organisation are running open source office applications and that it plans to do the same for its 22,000 PCs in the administration itself. The city of Munich (Germany) runs LibreOffice on 17,018 workstations.
