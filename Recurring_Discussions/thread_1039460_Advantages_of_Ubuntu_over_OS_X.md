### Post by wnagourney on 2009-01-14
I don't mean for this to be provocative, but I have been an Apple PPC user for many years and have installed various Linux distros over this period and have yet to find a compelling advantage of any distro over OS X (except for cost, of course).  I suppose if I had an Intel Mac, things would work better in Linux, but I have no intention of buying a new computer (particularly an Intel Mac).

I recently installed intrepid on an iBook G4 800 Mhz. I was pleased to see that there was some rudimentary 2D graphics acceleration (which didn't exist in YDL 6.1, my former installation on this machine).  However, everything is slower than on OS X - apps take longer to launch and  scrolling is embarrassingly sluggish (reminds me of my old 68040 machine).  Even simple things in OS X were difficult to get working in Ubuntu - with some effort I got wireless working but I have yet to be able to get a DVD to play correctly. Apparently there is some library which has not been built for PPC and which the codecs depend on. Also, Flash is not available for PPC linux computers and the alternative is not very satisfactory.

I would very much like to see a compelling advantage of Linux in addition to  the somewhat abstract one of using open software (which I agree with). I should mention that the hardware is very closed (particularly in the GPU area and with the WIFI hardware) and this conflicts somewhat with the spirit of open source.  Of course, much of this is moot with x86  (where at least Flash and GPU drivers are available) but I am not interested in that architecture.

Thanks for listening.

### Post by innovati on 2009-01-15
Well, I use both daily, and often simultaneously.

For me, OS X is 'unix done right' and I feel that the reason it is so good is that is built on open-source technology, but one group of people have built a proprietary desktop on top of it, using custom libraries.

I see this are being necessary for the calibre of product that we have as OS X, and so I see it having a certain advantage over linux in that way.

Why, when I have OS X, do I still choose to install and virtualize Linux?

For me, Linux offers thousands of programs that work beautifully and easily.  Sure, I can download and install most of them on my mac, but it's much harder to get them running and keeping them updated.

Did you know on Ubuntu, if you've installed fonts through synaptic, they stay current?  OS X doesn't do that for me.  I know when I use Ubuntu that I'm using a stable, efficient environment and it takes the hassle out of using open-source software.

Now, what I think we need to see: instead of virtualizing Linux on a mac, why not recompile all linux software for mac, and have it run in an X-server window in your mack desktop, but running natively on your mac.  And then maybe find a way to execute and run the mac desktop on your linux operating system as you use it instead, so it's native.

The only way Ubuntu will beat OS X in the next few years is if Canonical takes development, closes it more (and still published source at the end) and really starts working with developers, offering security and protection for their product and helping to get big names like Adobe to port their product to Ubuntu.  I'd love to see some Apple/Ubuntu collaboration going on, but that's probably like suggesting incest to most of the people on this board.

Bottom line: both are great, and they both serve slightly different purposes.  I see the advantages of both, and use each where it is most advantageous.  And 2 for the price of 1 can't be beat, so why not throw Ubuntu into your toolbox for free?

### Post by skullmunky on 2009-01-15
everyone has their own situation and needs, so my reasons probably won't apply :)  

1. I like variety!  I like the distrowatch.com motto - Making Computing Fun Again!  I use linux, osx, and windows, though mostly linux, and that way I can always enjoy the things that make each one special and fun.

2. you covered Free in both senses, which are both compelling to me.

3. a corollary to Freedom: reliability. 

I don't mean that a linux system will never crash and die.  What I mean is, if I want to install Linux on something, I just do it.  I don't have to deal with special magic install discs, licenses, validation keys, serial numbers, hardware locks, any of that.  Ok, some of that is a poke at Windows, but for example I picked up a lovely old beige G3 tower with the AV card; the drive was all corrupted, and it took me weeks and weeks of digging around on the web, closets at work, back alleys, everywhere to try and find the precise install media that it needed.  Same issue goes for applications, of course.  There's something comforting in the knowledge that when the worst, or even when you just need to set up a new machine, all your applications are just an apt-get away.

I used to compulsively horde installer discs.  I have these stacks and stacks of CD's with exotic OS9 apps that nobody will ever use again, and they're all probably rotted by now anyway.  Ubuntu's package management has made my life so much easier.

4. I just like the KDE desktop better than the OSX desktop.  File management is a little more powerful than in Finder.  

5. I like Amarok better than iTunes.

6. I do a lot of coding and I use the terminal a lot.  

7. I do a lot of work with interactive 3D, visualization and simulation, and there are more options in Linux than in OSX.

8. That smug kid in the Apple ads makes me feel bad about the fact that I even own a Mac.  

So I installed linux on it and now I feel better!

### Post by MikeTheC on 2009-01-19
The issue here is really one of developer priorities.

Fundamentally, there's no reason we can't have a first class video production app (or suite, if that's the better approach), image organizer, image editor, web site creation app, media app, and so on and so forth.

Let's face it: there's nothing like iLife for Linux.

There's also nothing like Premiere, Pro Tools, Soundbooth, Dreamweaver or iTunes for Linux. That doesn't make Linux a "horrible platform". It just makes it one that's less appropriate for the "non-tinkerer" home user who isn't otherwise fascinated with Linux.

But why are we looking on such things as "fightin' words"? These are simply opportunities for Linux to advance. And what makes Apple such a bad role model for Linux when it comes to the direction for software development? Clearly it'll be the better option, fundamentally, because of both the GPL and the peer-review nature of F/OSS source.
