### Post by djchandler on 2010-02-28
Dapper (6.06) was my first experience with Ubuntu. Before that I had used several incarnations of Fedora going back to before Red Hat split off the "free" version from the enterprise release and it was still Red Hat. I had some experience with Debian as well, but not a whole lot.

Gutsy (7.10) and Karmic have a lot in common, being the last release before the next LTS. In my opinion, these versions were or are betas if not alphas for the coming LTS release. There's a tendency to try new things by default. In Gutsy it was installing Compiz by default and activating it just because a proprietary driver with 3D support was installed. With Karmic it's the use of the new journaling file system ext4, Grub 2 in a beta version and a different means of tracking the hardware config at startup to make boot times faster.

New features are fine, but both of those releases, Gutsy and Karmic, made me feel a bit used. I mistakenly assumed the release was stable when in actual usage the experience was quite different.

With Karmic, at the same time that a lot of people are adopting Windows 7 that features a different bootloader (Vista had it too but who used Vista?) as opposed to the old NT bootloader, Canonical changed to a beta of Grub2 in the same month as the public commercial release of Windows 7. This makes things confusing for some of us old-timers when something goes wrong on a dual boot system. After having the RC of Win 7 and then the RTM already for two months and coexisting with Jaunty, it came as a bit of a shock to find a beta bootloader that does things much differently with Karmic.

It made the learning curve  seem steeper because I had to learn two very different approaches to booting on both systems at the same time. IT pros and a few people obsessed with such geeky things didn't have a *lot* of trouble, but the novice user time and time again has had problems. I know, because I was trying to help some of them asking for help here only to find thta some of my knowledge had become quite obsolete with both OSes. There's been a couple of threads that I participated in where the question was, "How do I get rid of Grub 2 after dumping Ubuntu and get my Windows 7 system bootable again?"

Problems with ATI graphics drivers (on older cards) and drivers for wireless networking persist. On my laptop, the Atheros wireless driver is in the kernel since Jaunty, but Jockey (one of those new hardware "helpers") informed me a proprietary driver for the Wireless was available. Jockey blacklisted the kernel driver for the hardware when I tried to remove the proprietary driver, (which was worse) and left me with no wireless networking at all until we tracked down a bug in Jockey. The kernel driver, which should have been working, was blacklisted and the config file had to be edited by hand.

But Ubuntu is ahead of nearly every other Linux distribution. I just gave OpenSUSE 11.2 a try and the feel was less polished. But the worst thing was trying to install the ATI FGLRX driver from their repository. Despite a couple of months of people complaining that installing from the repository repeatedly failed due to a checksum mismatch, nobody has done a thing to fix the problem. OpenSUSE is a hassle in other ways too once you have gotten used to doing things the Debian way.

When Lucid appears in April, I'm going to stick with it for two years or maybe just use the upstream Debian stable release. The one thing I am sure of is I don't want to fool around with another last intermediate release before the next Ubuntu LTS two years from now.
