### Post by toupeiro on 2010-09-04
From my vantagepoint, Ayisu and KiwiNZ are both right in specific respects.

300 rubbish OSes will ultimately not deter a company to pre-load a good, and stable OS with corporate backing for support, or invest in an enterprise linux distribution for their business.  grassroots OSes without tiered enterprise support offerings will be out of scope for most companies.

However, the diversity of even 6-12 different good OSes with enterprise level support can leave companies wondering which is the right direction to invest in?  Which Distro's methodologies are the easiest to deploy and scale, which will have most vendor support with the vendors we need to work with, etc etc.

The Biggest without question are RHEL and SLES.

most companies have at minimum some small presence of Linux in their datacenter and perhaps desktop as well.  The question is, what keeps the number from becoming a majority presence?

In the desktop thats clear.  It's overall software compatibility with commonly exchangeable proprietary formats in predominantly Microsoft environments.

In the server area, it becomes an issue of vendor support.  Vendors who deploy on linux server are guilty of not recertifying as new versions come out, and not cross-certifying.  A vender might say, we only support RHEL5.  Another vendor might say, we only support SLES, and then perhaps you have one that says, we can only support Gentoo or Ubuntu.  The problem then comes in supporting many different flavors of Linux with different underlying structures and methodologies, and more overhead to meet software compliance with your business partners.  Vendors will also not broaden their scope to cover what I call "grassroots OSes" or forked OSes from larger distributions by the community without a tiered support offering or support certification procedure.

Windows on the other hand, is relatively easy to certify applications on (although costly). That is, IMO. the biggest reason linux has not stormed it out of the datacenter.   I have several RHEL servers that I can maintain for periods of over a year with 60-100+ user sessions and sometimes several hundred workloads per host without ever rebooting.  To me, it never can be a question of overall stability.  At least, not for anyone from a technical perspective who has been "deep in the weeds" with both platforms for many years.  For many companies, I think it's a trade-off.  What does downtime mean to you, and if you have to support multiple linux distributions to increase your linux footprint, does that cost outweigh your business impact and TCO of windows server/desktop environments?  

That can actually be fairly complex data to acquire at times.
