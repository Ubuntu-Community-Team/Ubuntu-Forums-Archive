### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2010-09-29
I can appreciate your frustration, but I think you're assuming much when it comes to the "mindset of the developers".  A developer can fix 999 bugs but if someone is frustrated by the 1000th they'll say the devs aren't doing enough to fix bugs.

I don't develop Ubuntu, but I've written enough software to know that "fixing bugs" is not always trivial and straightforward.  Sometimes the only way to fix a problem is to redesign something so fundamentally that you stand to introduce a dozen more in the process.  Sometimes you can't reproduce a problem someone reports, and you can't get them to give you any information on their problem.  And sometimes, you run into a situation where the bug is not in the software itself, but in some dependent library, whose developers have fixed it upstream.  Do you upgrade the library, potentially breaking or introducing bugs into other programs, or try to backport the fixes (if you can figure out how), or let it sit until the library gets updated in the next release?  Not always easy questions to answer.


As far as extending the development cycle:  I would point out that Debian isn't more stable simply because it has a longer release cycle; it's more stable because they don't release it until all the bugs marked "RC" (release-critical) are fixed.  Of course, this naturally adds time to the development cycle, but the point is that simply adding more time doesn't mean more stability.  And of course, the two things people tend to criticize about Debian are the length of the release cycle and the unpredictability of it (when will Squeeze be released? Who knows??).

One thing I would recommend to anyone who is lobbying for some change in the Ubuntu development model is to find a distro that is doing what you are asking for (because no matter what it is, chances are some distro is doing it), and run it for a release or two.  See the good and bad aspects of what you are recommending, and then you can talk with some real insight about it.

"The problem" is both that the overall quality level of bug reports isn't very high, *and* that there isn't enough bug triage and development workforce to deal with the existing pace of reports. It's both that there's too much noise in bug discussions, *and* that the existing triagers / developers have too little time. These are simply two different non-exclusive views of the same problem space.

I can't really sympathize with any statement beginning with "The problem is.." or "The problem isn't..", since such statements offer a one-dimensional look at a complicated problem. If there was less noise in the bug discussions, and bug reports overall were higher quality, more bugs would be dealt with in a timely manner, despite the workforce remaining constant; and if we had a 25% greater amount of bug triagers, still more bugs would be dealt with in a timely manner, despite the noise. My point is that these two ways of looking at the problem, as well as others, are not mutually exclusive. The situation can be improved on different fronts simultaneously. There's definitely a point in advocating an increase in bug report quality without having to assert that it's *the problem*. There's definitely a point in trying to attract more triagers and developers, even though the shortage of workforce is not *the entire* problem. 

I suggest viewing the QA situation as a *problem space*, rather than a singular problem. It's an entanglement of multiple problems of constantly differing severity, which is hard to gauge accurately at any given point. Improving any single aspect of it will contribute to improving the overall situation, and improving one aspect doesn't necessarily detract from the others.
