### Post by samjh on 2009-11-13
I voted "No".

If the cycle was lengthened to 12 months with a hard freeze at 6 months, the obsolescence of packaged software would make Ubuntu less appetizing.  Fedora has a 6-month release cycle, OpenSUSE has an 8-month cycle, Mandriva has a 6-month cycle.  All are desktop-oriented distros.  Ubuntu shouldn't lag behind.

Right now, I think the current release cycle is producing reasonably reliable production releases.  In proportion to the increase in number of users, problems have not increased by any significant amount.  There is really no sign to indicate that the quality of Ubuntu releases are getting poorer, and no indication that Ubuntu's release quality is any less than its main rivals, Fedora and OpenSUSE.

### Post by skotos on 2009-11-13
I think that users (of different levels: from end users, to sys admins and developers) need to trust *Ubuntu.
Kernel regression bugs such as those now concerning USB devices and too heavily forced changes such as those introduced by Pulseaudio and Amarok 2 in the past do not do well to *Ubuntu.

GNU/Linux is far better than Windows, but this scheduled releases seduction is dangerous: experienced users feel frustrated by subtle regressions that come out silently but dangerously, while unexperienced users look at regressions in multimedia environments as simply unacceptable matters and just go away, discouraged.

I simply feel astonished by the fact that my EEEPC runs the latest UNR and that it does not work with the second display because the GUI gets splitted and broken, or by the fact that USB devices that have been successfully working out of the box for years simply stop working with no logs...
This is the behaviour I would expect at an alpha/beta level...

I think that this is unacceptable from a strategic point of view and dangerous...

Probably, **instead of releasing big changes packed in two stages per year, many updates or upgrades might be progressively released by the standard repositories in the regular flow of an LTS.**
