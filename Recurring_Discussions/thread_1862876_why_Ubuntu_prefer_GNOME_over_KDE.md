### Post by sanderd17 on 2011-10-17
There was also a bit of marketing in it.

Gnome has a fixed 6 months release cycle, and Canonical based the Ubuntu release cycle on Gnome (Ubuntu is released each time about one month after a new version of Gnome).

When you have a short, fixed release cycle, you get a lot more media attention. You get articles at every stadium of the release. If you compare that to rolling releases (like Arch), they don't get that much media attention. Or "release when ready" schedules (like Debian) also don't get a lot of attention.

So Gnome was a good DE, with a good release schedule, and Canonical thought it would be ideal to base their OS on it.

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-10-17
In the past, KDE had some issues with being semi-proprietary. Gnome on the other hand has always held the freedom front. Bigger distros tend to use Gnome, while KDE is the chose for most of the smaller distros.

If you look at distrowatch, KDE is overall more popular than Gnome, however, the largest distros like Red Hat/Fedora/CentoOS and Debian/Ubuntu families all use Gnome.
