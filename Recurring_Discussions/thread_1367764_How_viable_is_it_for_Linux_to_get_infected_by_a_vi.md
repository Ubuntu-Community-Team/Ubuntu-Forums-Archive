### Post by kennethadammiller on 2009-12-29
It's possible, but you have to understand the motive behind why a computer virus is created.

First and most important, there are several critical criteria that must be met. 
1) the virus creation must not be inordinately expensive in any area. 
2) it must have mass effect. so trying to affect the linux community is like purposely limiting your zone.
3) there must be something gained from the proliferation of this malware, whether it be intrinsic or materialistic.

with most of the source code available for the entire operating system and all it's applications, it's really not much of a virus. it's more like a... intended mischief. I understand that the operating system itself is very secure, but that's linux in general. This idea also depends on how close you get to unix. I heard that openBSD or netBSD, one of the two, had only two known flaws in a decade or something. but i'm sure that's wrong; you get the point. the OS is very secure.

this doesn't imply that all of the software that runs on the linux machine is secure however. There are poor programmers on all sides of the programming world mac, *nix, and MS. so nothing is impossible. 

take these few indicators as a sign of how frequent or what type of malicious software attacks exist for linux.
   there is no anti-virus for a linux system. if there is, it's not very popular. at all.
   most types of attacks against linux aren't automated viruses meant for an unknowing clicker to stumble upon. they are specifically crafted devices meant for specific machines. they are created knowing the people are on the other know how to use a computer. so when an attack occurs on a linux machine, the stakes are higher. 
   
none of this excludes the possibility of virus attacks against linux machines. they are just unlikely at the time. remember, nothing is absolutely and completely secure.

### Post by lloyd_b on 2009-12-30
> **lostinxlation said:**
> Linux itself being secured doesn't mean there is no risk of malwares. What if somebody makes a few lines that cause some damage in the program and the program is posted in repository ? I'm not sure every programs in the repository are inspected because only way to do it is by someone reading hundreds of thousands of lines in the programs.
 
I can bet Linux will gain more popularity if they improve the usability and make it more user friendly, and that will happen next few years. Then more applications are written for Linux and people will use it as they do with Win/MacOS today, which attract those with malintent to write programs that damage you.

Programs aren't just "posted" in a repository, like posting in a forum - they have to be checked in by somebody with repository access.  Those people with such access are generally rather careful about what they check in - they aren't going to just grab a random program off the internet and check it in without seriously reviewing it.  For the most part, they rely on the reputation of a project's maintainer(s) to determine if a package should be added.

Trying to sneak something malicious into an existing package is even tougher - you have to submit a patch to the maintainer of the package, who will, unless he/she knows you very well, scrutinize the patch.  Since patches only contain the *changes* to files, rather than the entire files, it's generally pretty easy to spot anything weird.

And for most projects, patches are *publicly* submitted, so it's not just the maintainer(s) of the project who will have a chance to review the patch.

Reputation is the "currency" of the Open Source economy.  Those who have it will tend to guard it carefully.  While it's possible that a reputable developer will "turn to the dark side", it's not very likely, since that would destroy the reputation he/she had spent years developing...

In short - worry about viruses getting into Linux via Flash or some such, rather than worry about the repositories.

Lloyd B.
