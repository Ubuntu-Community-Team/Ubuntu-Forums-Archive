### Post by gato2707 on 2011-05-14
I'm an end user just like many others, for many years I enjoyed the excellent GNU / Linux distribution that you and your company Canonical have created.*Today I speak respectfully to you to ask publicly that listens the requests from Ubuntu end users.

I, like all Ubuntu users appreciate your efforts to make the user interface is increasingly better and easier to use.*We also welcome the work you have done to offer an alternative to Gnome-Shell has been proposed from Gnome.

But I, like others found that the interface has been proposed, the Unity-Shell lacks two features that I consider most important: A Menu Tree and an Independent Taskbar.*Features that are present in virtually all other desks, both the environment of free software, and proprietary software.

Include this pair of options within the Unity-Shell, will surely be that I and many others continue to be users of both Ubuntu and Gnome itself.

The Menu Tree could be located anywhere, it does not matter if is in the current Unity Task Bar (Dock) as it could work very similar to what is now Cairo-Dock offers, or could be located in the*same place it has in the traditional Gnome desktop, or anywhere else you and your team see it fit.

What it should allow the Menu Tree, is to directly access applications without going through the Dash of Unity and using only the mouse pointer.*

It is necessary that the new Taskbar will be independent of the current Unity Task Bar, where they accumulate indiscriminately, both available programs, such as those running, and it should be possible to switch from one program to another, simply by clicking mouse pointer.

I think that both requests are completely reasonable, I'm not asking you to go away none of the current characteristics of Unity. So it will be time who decide what type of access is the one that most appeals to users. Personally I think at the end will be the occasion which determines the access way.

I do not think this question of choosing between the red pill and blue pill. There on the table hundreds, if not thousands of pills, and all lead to what many consider the truth called GNU / Linux.

Nor do I think that is a valid debate to talk about resistance to change, because I, like most Ubuntu users made the big change from the closed solutions to get here, and we like the changes, especially when changes also mean progress.

We are ready for change, in your hands is charting a course that will change.

Is Ubuntu Ready for change?
