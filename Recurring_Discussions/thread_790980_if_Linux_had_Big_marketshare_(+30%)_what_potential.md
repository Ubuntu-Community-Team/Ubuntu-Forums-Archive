### Post by Mr A Mouse on 2008-05-11
Couple of problems:
1: **Viruses:** With a 30% market share, virus writers would start setting their sights on writing Linux viruses. Note to the scoffers--yes, it can happen. Just because we're theoretically more secure (because users lack root access) doesn't mean we're invulnerable.
2: **Incompatable distributions:** So you go to write the next killer IM client. Do you base it on libpurple, or do you go after some other library? And what happens if (for instance) Ubuntu supports libpurple, but Debian does not? (Note: last I checked, libpurple was still in Debian's "testing" category, not "stable".)
3: **It's not 1337 enough anymore:** It's unfortunate, but there are Linux users out there who are emotionally invested in the fact that Linux is a "Geeks Only" operating system. If it becomes mainstream, some of them will leave the Linux community simply because it's not as obscure as it once was.

I know--I probably sound like pessimist, and I honestly don't mean to be. But when you're dealing with market share, you deal with *people*--and unfortunately, people are not always as nice as we might hope they could be.

### Post by Mr A Mouse on 2008-05-12
> **LaRoza said:**
> Linux has over 30% market share in the server market, the target of viruses.

I'm forced to respectfully disagree--not about server market share, but about the target of viruses.

Virus writers are getting smart. The days of the virus that wipes your hard drive has lessened: nowadays, virus writers try to recruit bots, both for the famous large-scale botnets, and for smaller "Steal your credit card" style scams.

Fundamentally, you have three target markets for virii:
1: Business servers
2: Business workstations
3: Home computers

Business servers are (theoretically, at least) the most watched and monitored piece of equipment in the computer market. I know where I work (a rural hospital in the American southeast), we scrutinize the behavior of the servers religiously--if one of the servers even burps, we know about it, and quickly. I would assume that companies with greater IT needs would be even more zealous in server monitoring. Business workstations are also watched--maybe not as well as the servers, but still watched fairly closely. 

This leaves home computers as the most accessible market, precisely because they do not have "professionals" monitoring them. Couple that with the fact that today's home computer is barely less powerful than a server, and the home computer market is the "low-hanging fruit."

And yes, I know--I'm taking a very complex scenario and reifying it almost to the point of ridiculousness. But this is the kind of thinking I've seen in what little of the "black hat" community I'm aware of.

> Real viruses would be hard to make and even harder to deploy because of the rapid development and variety of targets.

Rapid development and variety of targets in the Linux market are probably two strong pro-Linux factors that would be sacrificed on the altar of "Big Marketshare," were such a thing to ever come to pass. I simply cannot envision Linux gaining a significant desktop marketshare with the deeply fragmented (read "forked, re-forked, poly-forked, and generally forked-over") development stream. I may be wrong (it won't be the first time ;)), but to me the various competing Linux distributions looks more like a crab bucket than a viable contender in the desktop market.

Rapid development--now that may not go away if Linux gains market share ... provided that we also gain a significant percentage of the available OS developers. 

Don't get me wrong, LaRoza ... I'm honestly not trying to be a pessimist. But in many ways, I think the pursuit of marketshare may just be the very worst thing the Linux community could possibly do.
