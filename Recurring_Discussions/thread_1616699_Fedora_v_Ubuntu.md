### Post by 3Miro on 2010-11-08
Fedora 13 came with a few bugs in the graphical updater that made the first 20 minutes of it somewhat unpleasant. It also doesn't come with RPM Fusion repositories enabled by default and even when you have enabled them, then you are still supposed to know what 64 vs x86 means. Fedora is great, but you need to have at least a basic understanding of what you are doing. On the other hand, Ubuntu is the only large distro that I know of that assumes its users don't know anything.

The above can be viewed as an argument for either one. I don't think you can say that Fedora is "clearly" better than Ubuntu.

### Post by scottuss on 2010-11-08
I've tried Fedora on and off, I've found yum to be extremely slow, especially when compared to apt.

I also have to have a nice smoothly rendered font, because of the LCD patches that Ubuntu has out of the box, I find the fonts to be much nicer than on Fedora.

Apart from that, I can't think of any massive differences. Some of the System Management tools that come with Fedora are really nice, but a lot of these are available on Ubuntu.

### Post by ajgreeny on 2010-11-08
I am another user who has tried, and keeps trying fedora, but who comes back again and again to plain gnome flavoured Ubuntu.

Some people may prefer the yum system of package management, but I agree with scottuss that it is phenominally slow compared with the various apt tools in Ubuntu.  I can also tell you that not so long ago, the magazine gave Fedora 10, I think, 10 out of 10 as a score in a review, but only 9/10 to ubuntu.  There were many letters asking how on earth that could be so, when even in the review they had remarked on a few difficulties in fedora with things like restricted audio and video formats.  The response to that was that they were looking only at the OS at installation, and not at such additions.

Seems a very short sighted view, in my opinion;  what's the use of an OS if it will not play such things without a lot of palaver, as needed in fedora.  I know it can all be done, and codecs etc etc, added in both OSs, but for my money, (actually, no money at all), Ubuntu wins, hands down.
