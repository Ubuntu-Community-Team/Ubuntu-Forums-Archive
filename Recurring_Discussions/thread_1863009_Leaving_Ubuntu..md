### Post by sanderd17 on 2011-10-17
I moved to Arch after problems with Natty.

The advantage of Arch is that you put a lot of work in it to configure it for the first time. But after that, it's rolling and you can keep using it. I'm now on Arch since June, and I didn't need to fix anything bad. 

The advantage of rolling releases is that, if something breaks, the number of new packages is only small, so you know where to look quite fast.

I don't advise it to everybody though. You need to feel comfortable with the CLI before you can start using it.

I think that the problem is that Gnome2 has been keeping a constant look & feel over the versions, while fixing minor bugs and adding enhancements. People were always excited about what the next version of Gnome2 would bring.

But now since Gnome2 is becoming obsolete, its users might feel betrayed. Now they have to look to other DE's and hopefully find one that mimics Gnome2. I see that Cinnamon seems to look nice.

I personally feel that Gnome3 (even fallback or classic) it's just a nerfed-down version of the old and powerful Gnome2. 

For example, before you could just do right-click on any item in the Applications Browser and add it to the top Launcher. Now, Right-Click and Left-Click do the same thing.

I think there is a user-base that got very well used to the Gnome2 Interface, and all it's subtleties. Those users will probably never get that in Gnome3. Ubuntu, being among the first disros to use Gnome3 by default, is more likely to be abandoned by  these users.

I myself love the old Gnome2, but I am open to new options (e.g. unity), I just find that Unity is both slow and missing some functionality (System Monitor Graphs, Log Out / Shutdown options suddenly gone). I will stick to 10.10 for now, but next year it will not be supported anymore. 

I guess I need to find another DE until then.

Usually, if people are happy enough with something and they got used to it, they want to keep it that way. Gnome3 represents a new "learning curve" for old users.

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2012-09-28
No, it really doesn't. Ubuntu isn't yet amazing, but it's as easy to get used to as Windows is. A person who's used Unix and all sorts of prior Linuxes but finds Unity uncomfortable isn't talking about user-friendliness in the way that that phrase is normally used. It says something about the user and it says something about Unity, neither of which should be taken as judgemental, but it doesn't say anything about the base Ubuntu system, which is just basically a Linux that mostly works. 

Is this the catch-all maybe-not-using-Ubuntu-so-much-anymore thread, now, since the OP's gone? If so:

**tl;dr:** I might be losing a spot for Linux in my life, but it's nothing personal. I like Gnome, I like Metro*, I really like my Nexus 7. Etc.

I'm really considering it. I bought a Nexus 7 specifically as a gateway drug into the Android world, and it was a good hit, enough that I do want to go back for some more. It's powerful, rock-solid, forgiving, and extraordinarily light on cognitive load; I never really have to think about how to make it do things, so I can focus on thinking about what I'm doing instead. Android feels bulletproof.

I'm thinking of replacing my netbook with an Asus Transformer, one of the Android models (though I haven't decided which,) and getting a dual-display desktop for all the sorts of tasks I really just need a desktop OS for. But I'm extremely indecisive. 

**Of Ubuntu:**

Ubuntu is causing some irritation for me after getting used to Android. The relatively fewer available apps and some of the hoops I have to jump through are getting taxing; part of that might be the netbook hardware, but part of it is the software available, too. For instance, I love the experience of the Google Play store, and the Software Center is exactly right in concept, but it's so slow and buggy (click to install something, click to install another thing, have the system "forget about" the first thing) that I don't think I'd actually find even the process of downloading and installing executables altogether that much a step down. 

As a wholly non-representative example, I got a second-hand scanner, and of the three free scanning apps available, one simply wouldn't work, one worked perfectly but for a penchant for freezing up, and one worked perfectly except for the actual scanning, which was fuzzy and washed out no matter what settings I pumped in. There's one scanning app for $10 USD, and that's totally reasonable, but it also might end up being no better than the others, and I've not really seen a substantial advantage in the paid apps over the free ones. I'm really getting tired of having to spend so much time trying and vetting apps. 

In general, nothing ever seems quite as smooth and automatic as I'd like, and there are always little features in flux. Under Shell, which I really love, there are just little hangups like the lag in the Overview search, or bits in the handling of secondary displays, like the way some apps will launch on the secondary display instead of the primary or the fact that windows from external monitors can't be dropped in between workspaces to create new workspaces in the filmstrip the way windows on the primary monitor can. It's just little bits of polish, but they're the things that make the system seem complete and fluid. And, for that matter, *forgiving,* which is something Android has in spades over Ubuntu. (Even if a lot of the features, like, say, external monitors, just don't exist there.)

**Of portables:**

After a month with the Nexus 7, I also really want a notebook or convertible with always-on, phone-style power management that doesn't drop my WiFi connection just because I closed the lid, and that's biasing me heavily toward Android or even Windows 8 RT over anything I can install Linux on. 

A part of me is really considering the possibility of waiting for the Windows 8 RT Transformer, and that would reduce the necessity of the desktop, too - but I can't quite bear the thought of doing everything within such a locked-down system and having to respond to survey questions that *Internet Explorer* (***ooragh***) is my primary browser. It would also mean deciding between Office's weird schismatic presence within its own weird little traditional desktop environment or using Google Drive for all of my word processing, neither of which options is at all appealing. 

I also do a lot of unofficial tech support tasks, and a friend of mine suggested going with the Windows convertible to get used to it, since it's the OS I'm generally being supportive about. But Windows *8* isn't going to hit any consequential level of penetration for the people I'm around in a *year* or so, and I'm probably going to be thinking about my next round of computer purchases in *two* years, by which time I really imagine Google will have a fully armed and operational desktop OS, whether in future Android versions or something else, and I won't even have to think about Windows. 

So if the Transformer is the Android version, I'm still doing the heavy word-processing tasks (and a fair bit of the rest of my work life besides) on the desktop, but I'm cool with that.

**Of desktops:**

I'm trying to figure out what I'd need in a desktop, and I don't need much for processing power, but I need dual displays and I want to stay sub-$500 USD for the tower (plus the cost of an SSD to replace whatever HDD it comes with and possibly the cost of the OS.) 

Between Ubuntu / Gnome, Windows 8, and OSX on a Mac Mini, I'm really undecided. Windows 8 is new, and OSX is new to me, so either of those options is just a little exciting in that sense. It'd be nice to try some new things. Gnome 3 seemed new and shiny for a little while, but it seems almost silly now to be excited about something as minor as a new look and a slightly simpler way of keeping track of my windows when they're the same damned applications they were when I switched to Ubuntu in 2008.

And I'm considering VMs, but I don't like the hassle of dual-booting, so there's really only one primary OS, whatever I go with. Gnome's biggest strength over the competition, to my taste at least, is in the way it manages application windows, and the applications themselves port to the other environments, so I don't see myself using Ubuntu as a VM - it'd be a base system I might optionally also run VMs within. 

So in toto, I'm extremely indecisive, but I might end up with OSX or Windows as a desktop OS and Android on the go, which means Ubuntu gets lost in the shuffle, which is sort of like "leaving Ubuntu." But any observations or suggestions would be awesome.

My random brain-vomit on the subject. 

*[size=1]Yes, I'm still calling it *Metro*, dammit. I'm not the one getting sued, and I'm not going to be the one inconvenienced, either.[/size]
