### Post by saulgoode on 2008-08-23
The distinction between the first two options is of no significance. "User friendly" is just a marketing term which has been abused to such a point as to have become meaningless. At best its definition presumes the opinions of the user, but more typically discounts those opinions altogether -- unless they happen to agree with the feature set being marketed.

I responded "Completely free distros", though I am not particularly comfortable with the word "completely". One of the main tenets of Free Software is that a recipient has the right to share copies with others. Whether a distro sells or gives you a copy is not really the issue; it is whether they permit you to make and share copies. 

Of course, if they permit you to share copies, they might as well give you the "first" copy and benefit from the goodwill that engenders. So, in my opinion, the best direction for GNU/Linux is represented by the likes of Debian, Slackware, and Ubuntu -- wherein users are not restricted from sharing the software and payment to the developers is on an entirely voluntary basis.
