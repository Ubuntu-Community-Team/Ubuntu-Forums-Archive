### Post by AtlantaBob on 2009-05-10
Hi guys,

Not to start a war, but... I just bought a Mac Mini. And impressed doesn't begin to cut it.

Now before you tune me out, know that I have ubuntu 9.04 running on an old Celeron at work (I'm a grad student) and it's on my parents' new Dell Inspiron (even they think it's MUCH better than Vista). I also have it running on a Dell Vostro 1500. 

I have to say, I like the idea of Linux -- the ability to change what you want if/should you decide to do so is fantastic (although admittedly difficult for most in practice.) Also, the idea to be free from DRM restraints such as those found in the latest version of MS Windows is great. In use, I've found a number of great ideas that have been implemented by developers on Ubuntu (and XUbuntu). And the tools (crontab, awk, grep, etc. are fantastic)!

However, after getting a Mac (and a low-end 1.83 GHz Mac Mini at that) I have to say that Linux on the desktop (in my opinion) simply isn't ready yet. It's made a number of great strides -- including in user interface (I for one, think that the 9.04 user login screen is brilliant). However, it's clear (again, to me) that the user interface elements, the documentations and the refinements aren't up to the competition (in this case OS X, as in my experience, Vista, and Windows 7 are regressions). 

Don't misunderstand me, Linux is wonderful and the UNIX-type tools that it implements are fantastic. And even though I use it at work and my parents use it at home, it's still an OS built by hackers, for hackers. There are too many instances where the user-interfaces, logic, etc. are built for a particular developer rather than a hypothetical (and trans-application) user. Don't get me wrong -- I code programs that I write for my own use in the same way. There are shortcuts that I make that are meant for me and for me alone. But I don't distribute the work I do for everyone else (as most of my programs are specific for my research).

And while Linux runs pretty smoothly (again, in my experience) on desktop computers, there are a number of problems with running it on laptops (e.g. Ubuntu tends to run hot on laptops, the infamous Ubuntu laptop-hdd-killer bug.) And with people buying more and more laptops every year, Linux MUST come to terms with proprietary laptop hardware (difficult, I agree, particularly when vendors don't contribute.)

Instead, OS X has paid someone to do all of the small user-interface stuff -- made sure that the fonts are correctly set at the beginning, that the default UI isn't an ugly brown (I know, many people disagree with me on this), and that the interface is consistent across applications.

When you factor in the combination of closed hardware, and developers who aren't user-interface pros, I'm not sure how much Linux on the desktop/laptop can grow. Certainly for users who don't mind the techiness, it will always have it's place. But for mom, dad and grandma, it seems unlikely that they will ever accept it on their own.

Instead, Apple seems to have produced a product that offers all of the advantages of linux, without the hassle of looking up problems and editing arcane config files. All for a couple of hundred dollars in extra cost. 

For me (and again, I realize this doesn't apply to everyone) it's well worth it. At the moment.

I still intend to use Linux on my work computer (and, at the moment, on my laptop, although the proprietary issues are making me think of switching to Windows 7 RC). However, on my "home" PC, I'll be using OS X for the foreseeable future. That's not to say I wish any ill-will towards Linux. I hope it gets better and better and continues to challenge Apple and Microsoft. But at some point, it seems to me, if Linux wants to gain wide use (and it may not want to), that more developers will need to work on the finer points of UI, appearance, program naming, etc.
