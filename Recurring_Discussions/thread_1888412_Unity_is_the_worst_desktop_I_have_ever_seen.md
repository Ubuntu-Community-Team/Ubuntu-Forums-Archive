### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-11-29
[QUOTE=Paqman]So I think it's a bit harsh to say Unity is bad because of it. It's just that it doesn't suit your preferred style of interacting with the machine. It's hardly a new idea either, keyboard-based launchers like Gnome Do and Synapse have been popular for a while. Horses for courses really. But don't confuse your personal interface preferences with actual quality.[/QUOTE]
Just to be clear, I'm not defending the assertion that Unity can't be used with one hand, and I don't have any problem with search-based launching, as I'm one of the people with whom Synapse was popular and now use Gnome Shell. = ) 

[quote=3Miro]Try the following: grab a beer, sit in front of Unity and then get the mouse in one hand and the beer in the other. Now try to open and close programs and windows, move windows between different workspaces and switch workspaces as fast as you can.[/quote]

But now that I'm looking at this, it's less about "one hand" than "mouse-only," and it doesn't really apply to the casual sort of use cases I was referring to. 

"Opening and closing windows" doesn't make sense to me. They're very different things. Closing windows is the same no matter what you're using (aside from the "Fitts' Law" bit, but Unity's okay there.) Opening windows presumably means launching an application or opening a document. If it's a pinned app, launcher, or desktop icon, depending on your DE and age, that's one click or one double-click regardless of the system. If it isn't, it either takes at least two clicks and more precise mouse movement or some use of the keyboard, depending on the DE.

Workspaces don't in Unity, and I don't know why one would bother. Whether under Unity or Shell, they take one more click than XFCE, but allow you to see what's in them (moreso Shell than Unity, but the principle is the same.) The navigability is theoretically slower but less prone to error and irritation. But the workspaces themselves are handled in a fixed row (good for assigning them to specific tasks) under XFCE, spatially in Unity, and dynamically in Shell, so there's just no direct comparison at all. They're used in different ways for different things.

Judging the methods based on the number of mouse clicks is the real problem, not the beer.

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-11-29
> **vasa1 said:**
> Amazing.
Canonical should hang its collective head in shame for not accommodating this most obvious usage pattern :D

What isn't obvious about this? People do both hands on the keyboard, one hand on the keyboard and one hand on the mouse, and one hand on the mouse one hand free for whatever. I am not picking on some obscured functionality, I am picking on basic features that EVERY other DE has had for years. Canonical is now removing some of that functionality. It also not the case that Unity has more than other DE in terms of keyboard shortcuts. Maybe Unity have better default keyboard shortcuts (actually I think it does), but those are still things that you can do with other DE.

Here are some simple examples:
---------------------------
I am working on workspace 1 and I want to move to workspace 3 (mouse only). Gnome 2 has an applet on the panel that can do this with just move-scroll (or move-click). KDE, in addition to the applet, you can also scroll over the desktop background. XFCE and LXDE, in addition to the above, also allow reserving several pixels to the left/right of the screen to just drag the mouse to the edge and scroll the wheel even if you are using maximized windows (the screen edge is often times closer to the mouse than the worspace applet).

Unity maximized many windows by default, but doesn't allow for either a panel applet or reserving edge-pixels to scroll-move (there is a switch workspace indicator, but it is two-clicks also). You can scroll on the desktop from a non-maximized windows, but then it changes only one workspace at a time (I have to scroll twice), that means involves two actions.

Gnome-shell can only change workspaces if you go through the zoom animation. Move the mouse to the top-left corner, then move the mouse all the way to the middle-right of the screen to select the workspace. Mint's version of Gnome-shell has a Gnome 2-ish bottom panel which makes things better and I do know that most of those things can be Modded in Gnome-shell, but this requires coding your own GS extensions.
---------------------------
Another example is to move a window to a different workspace. KDE, XFCE, LXDE and Unity allow to drag the window to the left/right edge of the screen. Gnome 2 can do that if you have Compiz (Metacity cannot do that). Gnome-shell can only do this with the zoom animation moving the mouse from one end of the screen to the other.

If you have to move the window through several workspaces (i.e. from 1 to 3 or 4), then in KDE, XFCE, LXDE and Gnome 2 (Metacity), you can drag the opened window using the panel applet. Unity cannot do that unless you zoom-out in a Gnome-shell style, and Gnome-shell can only do that if you move the mouse form one end of the screen to the other.
---------------------------
In KDE, XFCE, LXDE and Gnome 2, I can have a panel with small launchers (24 - 30 pixels). On that I can put pretty much every application that I have installed (I can easily fit over 20 launchers), thus I can have one-click access to anything that I may need. Unity and Gnome-shell don't allow for small icons (32 points for Unity is close to 50 pixels) so I can only fit a small selection of applications, the rest has to be picked from a global menu with keyboard search options (Mint has a Gnome-shell menu, but even that requires extra mouse-clicks to access individual categories like Internet or Media).
---------------------------
Suppose I have one workspace with one instance of Chromium opened with work related mail for Gmail and Claws-mail (I keep my mail separate to sort out the spam more easily). On another workspace I have two instances of Chromium with many tabs related to my work. Then I take a lunch break in my office and I decide to go to another workspace and open Chromium with YouTube to watch a Starcraft II cast while eating and then I try to open yet another instance Chromium to check Facebook (one hand is greasy so I can only the mouse).

In KDE, XFCE, LXDE and Gnome 2, I can have a panel with a list of opened windows so I can see what is opened on the current workspace and current workspace only. Then I can switch between those with one move-click, so I can easily switch between YouTube and Facebook (even if the windows cover each other).

Under Unity, I cannot keep those 5 instances separate. If I click on Chromium, it will ask me to select between all 5 windows, there is no way to fully separate my work among the workspaces.

Gnome-shell uses the same program oriented approach, even though you can use the same program to do completely different things, you cannot logically separate the instances of the program.
---------------------------
Last, but not least is the point of vertical vs horizontal space. On my 16:10 laptop, I had Fedora installed with Gnome-shell. Even when I maximize the window, I still have about 25% of the screen taken by panel with a clock, FF tile and couple of tool-bars. Good themes can fix that, but those were not available at the time. Unity does a much better job with vertical space.

At work, I have a 4:3 monitor (better for reading papers), then Unity takes valuable space on the side (if I auto-hide it is more work to access anything on the panel). The option to move the panel to the bottom and have it not-auto-hide would be great, but alas it is non-existent.

In KDE, XFCE, LXDE and Gnome 2, I can have the panels and applets in any configuration that I want, depending on what I am doing as well as the size and shape of my monitor.
---------------------------
If the above scenarios don't apply to you or if you don't care about the extra mouse-work, then Unity and/or Gnome-shell would be fine for you. Otherwise, they are not fine. Unity needs small things to become better, Gnome-shell needs more tweaking, but the it is also very flexible (if you have the coding skills). In time, both interfaces will improve with extensions and customizations, but at the present they provide nothing extra compared to KDE, XFCE, LXDE and Gnome 2 and they also lose quite a bit of functionality.

I don't hate on Unity or Gnome-shell (although GS does make me want to scream). I don't start threads complaining, I only occasionally comment on the Unity/GS related threads. I simply don't use Unity or GS. If I can help people on the Help or Desktop Environments forums, I even try to help. However, the statement that those two are somehow "great" or "the best" is just flat out **not true.**

### Post by Larkspur on 2011-11-29
3Miro, here are some possible solutions to your usage problems in Unity:

Moving from one workspace to another:

Bind the window selector to one of the corners freed by the lack of a bottom bar, then switching workspaces in Unity is a "move-click" too.

Move a window to another workspace:

Right-click the titlebar and select the workspace you want to move the window to.  Admittedly, you can't do this in maximised apps, but since you'll be dragging windows anyway in your example of best practice, you can drag the window away from the top panel.

Size of launcher bar:

At 32 pixels, you can fix 17 launchers on the bar without it starting to scroll.  If you are habitually using over 17 programs and hate scrolling the bar or typing the first few letters of the program you want, then, unfortunately, Unity is not for you (unless you install the folderview screenlet). 

Being able to see only the windows on the current workspace.

Bind another corner to activate Scale for only the current workspace.  This is arguably better than some other DEs because with a panel list, the window names can get compressed to oblivion if you have too many.  Also, activate the Scale Add-ons to have window names in Scale, so as to tell the difference between similar windows.

Launcher takes up too much space on a 4:3 montior:

The 32-pixel bar takes up too much space?  I don't have this problem with my 4:3 monitor (I do a lot of reading and writing on it too), and I used the 48-pixel bar.  Anyway, the option to move the launcher bar to the bottom of the screen is no longer non-existent; just follow the instructions [here]("http://www.webupd8.org/2011/11/install-ubuntu-unity-bottom-launcher.html").  To keep the launcher bar on screen at all times, set the Hide Launcher value to "Never" in ccsm (though I don't know if this works with the linked-to plugin).

In GS you can use:
a) Application switching, by default bound to alt+tab, across workspaces
b) Window switching within the currently focused application, by default bound to alt+[key over tab],  across workspaces

c) "Immediate" window switching, across applications, within the current workspace; not sure of its default keybinding but I have it bound at alt+esc (look for this action in the keybinding settings, under navigation)

Your example of two terminal windows in workspace 2 would be solved by either the (b) or (c) shortcut.
