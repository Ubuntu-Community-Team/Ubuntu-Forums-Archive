### Post by FrozenFox on 2008-03-16
The easy solution is to pick which one you like yourself from the livecd's.

Personally, I don't use either "full-time"  (right now, anyway. I'm triple booted between XP, Debian Sid, and Ubuntu Hardy 8.04, but i'm removing ubuntu) but I've used just about every distro you can think of for a month or more. More, in the case of pclinuxos/mandriva and ubuntu.  Imho, pclinuxos is a little better for new users than mandriva. They are compatible with each other because pclinuxos was based on mandriva, just as ubuntu is based on debian. PClinuxos is the only distro out of any of them i've ever tried (like 20+) that picked up my mouse side buttons correctly without manually fixing it myself.

Particularly, I dislike Mandriva's package management, though I'm unsure if it's changed as I used it a long time ago. I had nothing but problems installing RPM's because the dependencies frequently failed to be chosen and installed correctly. Ubuntu ftw.

Also, debian based distros like ubuntu have more users, and more people are making packages for debian based systems than things like Mandriva, which is an important note for someone who hardly knows what they are doing. Experienced users wouldn't mind such things because they know how to install stuff themselves, but compatibility is key to new users. Ubuntu ftw.

In terms of forum support and newbie friendliness, both are stellar.

In terms of software, ease of use in general, stability, and professionalism, both are stellar.

In terms of hardware support, they're both decent.

In short: load the live cd's and find out yourself. The only real differences are that the package management for ubuntu is generally easier to deal with and you can more easily find new software outside of the repositories for a debian-based system than mandriva or pclinuxos or the like, which is only a minor issue and my opinion on such is slightly out of date.

Make sure you pick the right desktop environment, gnome or kde, before you start, from the livecd's. I prefer kde. Some find gnome easier, some find kde easier, blah blah. Use kubuntu if you like mandriva's setup more in that regard, save yourself the trouble and hd space.

But seriously, how silly is it asking this question on the ubuntu forums, particularly in a poll?

### Post by angry_johnnie on 2008-03-16
Mandriva may be easier at first, if you're a newcomer.  A lot of things work right out of the box.  That means you won't be searching for codecs and whatnot, at least the common ones --you know, mp3 and the like.   

You'll have the option to upgrade to power pack (I'm really not sure of what that's supposed to do, but I suppose it'll give you some of the more obscure stuff, like m4a, mp4 or whatever), but I think you have to pay for it.

If you don't want to pay for a power pack, you'll still have synaptic.  However, I think Mandriva uses rpm packages and, while you shouldn't --really-- take my word for it, I believe the debian package system is much better.  

In the end it all boils down to one thing:  What do you want to do?  Try both of them and see which one feels better.  Which one makes you feel comfortable?  Mandriva will be easier in the sense that you'll have to do much less.  Ubuntu may take a little more time and patience to get used to, but it is worth it.  I go looking for a new distro every other day, but it seems like I always end up coming back to Ubuntu :-).  But that's just because I find it easier and, overall, better.

It's your choice.

### Post by mocoloco on 2008-03-16
Having used Mandriva myself for some time, here are the reasons I came to like Ubuntu better:

[LIST]
[*]More software in the repositories.  I like to download and try out all kinds of apps (why not? they're free!) and Ubuntu has TONS more software in it's base repos.
[*]Ubuntu is more popular.  I know that logic alone sounds stupid (a la MSWin) but in this case more popular means more devs are looking at it, which means faster improvements and bug fixes, as wells as contributes to the above point of more stuff being added to the repos. Plus a bigger community means quicker help in forums and such, even after accounting for the newbie surge effect.
[*]Mandriva wants your money.  That's not a bad thing at all, but I didn't want to commit any cash while I was testing a bunch of distros, and I got the feeling that to really get the most out of Mandriva you have to join the Mandriva club.
[*]Apt-get rocks!  I've decided I love Debian-based package management a lot more than the RPM world.  I ran in to some issues on more than one RPM-based distro, but I've never had problems with package management on Ubuntu.
[/LIST]

Hope those are helpful.  It may turn out they both meet the needs you have, in which case just decide which you think is more fun to say ;)

### Post by jayson.rowe on 2008-03-16
Just curious, why are you looking at two different Desktop Environments on two different Distros?

I think you need to get the GNOME vs. KDE thing sorted out before deciding on a Distro to stick w/ but that's just my opinion.

Ubuntu is known more for GNOME, but has a great KDE distro called Kubuntu.
Mandriva is known more for KDE, but has a super GNOME implementation.

As you answer your question...I don't think anybody can, but yourself. You have to try both (even if from a LiveCD before installing) to see what works best for you. The biggest difference between any 2 distro's are going to be default installed package-set and the package management system.

I prefer Debian based package management, and that's one reason why I'm on Ubuntu, and although Mandriva uses RPM, I think they do better @ RPM package management than any other RPM based distro.

Both distro's have great communities. Ubuntu's is much larger, but Mandriva's, although smaller is equally as helpful (If you hang out on the Mandriva forms for any amount of time you'll run into Adam Williamson who will always go out of his way to help you).

I seriously tried to give Mandriva a go, and I think I could have stuck it out, as I enjoyed the distro, but one thing that keeps me coming back to Ubuntu is the great package management, and the ability to do [mostly] safe in-place upgrades when a new release comes out.

I do, above all reccomend spending at least an afternoon in each LiveCD before comitting either one to HDD!

YMMV!
