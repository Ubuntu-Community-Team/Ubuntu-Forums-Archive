### Post by Mark Phelps on 2011-12-15
Let's "get real" for a moment, OK?  Developers, like ALL the rest of us, have limited time -- they can't possibly fix EVERYTHING that folks report, so they have to be selective.

Furthermore, like nearly all the rest of us, professional Developers have a Management structure -- and it is THOSE folks, not us, not the folks reporting bugs, that decide WHICH bugs are going to (first) be investigated, (second) fixed, and (third) incorporated into future updates or releases.

From simple experience, the "bugs" that get fixed first tend to be the ones that have the largest impact (most severe), affect the largest number of folks (most widespread), are the easiest to fix (least costly in time and effort), and can be combined with other repair efforts.

Which means, if you discover some obscure bug, which only complicates using Ubuntu (or worse, an app), that has workaround (albeit a clumsy one), and that will likely take a lot of work to investigate and fix -- that bug is going to get pushed way down the stack of items to be addressed.

Case in point -- on one project I worked on some years ago, we had a "rating" system that assigned severity codes to reported problems.  Those problems that were very obscure, only complicated the program use, had workarounds, and would be very costly to investigate and fix -- were relegated to the category of "WhoGAS" -- Who Gives A S..t, which meant, they never got addressed.

Humour aside (the previous was meant to be funny), the more serious the bug, the more likely it will get addressed.

So, yeah, obscure items will tend to linger -- from release to release to release.

That's not a criticism of Ubuntu or Canonical, that's how things work.

The comment regarding Mark Phelps signature is totally out of place.  Let's see - since your signature DOESN'T say not to contact YOU, then I guess the thousands upon thousands of requests that come in should be PM'd to YOU.

You are missing a very key piece of the equation - this is all VOLUNTEERS.  People taking time from what they could otherwise be doing with their lives to try improve something FOR FREE.   If you haven't checked lately, technical support is VERY expensive.

I've been a programmer, programmer/analyst, systems programmer and systems administrator professionally, as well as writing my own software for sale many years ago.  In any of the capacities, and in particular in the systems programming and administration areas, people come up with things that are extremely important to them.  Look in this forum alone for requests for help because wireless isn't working or the video is strange.  In my case I was paid some big dollars to be available 24/7/365 and my knowledge base.  People knew I would either fix the problem myself or involve the vendor's national support structure and get the problem fixed.  It would be fixed as quickly as possible and fixed right.

Now, imagine someone coming up to me and saying that an application was misbehaving but rather than accepting it was an application programmer who needed to work on it, this person keeps hounding me about how it's my system and I need to fix it (I've been through this countless times).  Let's also say the problem involves printing to a printer this user bought without going through the normal channels, so that the printer has never been tested/debugged/etc. on the system.  This is a highly critical problem to them - they can't get their reports out.  But I have a list of things that need to be done that effect a few hundred users in the company - that impacts them from doing their job the best for the company.  Who gets the attention?  If you guessed the guy with the printer because his problem is highly critical to him, you'd be wrong.  If he follows normal channels he can have a printer that we know works up and running in a couple of hours.  Total impact - 2 lost man hours of work.  Now we have the few hundred people that are impacted 8 minutes for each hour of work.  In that same 2 hour time period that's 16 minutes per each of these workers, so with 200 workers impacted that 3200 minutes.  Divide by 60 and you get somewhere in the neighborhood of 53 man hours wasted.  Who gets the attention now?

This same thing holds true for volunteer written and supported OS's like Linux.  Everyone's problem is a BIG problem to them.  But if you are 1 of 20,000 people using the product, and it only affects people doing the same work in the same way as you - lets say 200, but there are other bugs which effect all 20,000 people, guess which comes first.

Now, suppose you are a Linux developer.  You have a set of programs to support, yet someone keeps hounding you because their wireless doesn't work.  Yes, no working wireless is a bid deal to the user, but as a developer I didn't have anything to do with your choice in hardware, and the vendor of that hardware has seen fit not to provide Linux support - like a driver - for it.  Big problem to a user, but as crass as it sounds - not my problem.  But, I volunteer to TRY to help you anyway.  How does it suddenly become my full-time job, no matter the day of week or time of day, to dedicate my life to finding you a driver?

So, response times to problems can vary depending on the product and how the problem may impact others, and then on the time and priority list of the VOLUNTEER working on it.

Believe me, there are few, if any, users on the forum that haven't had some problem, and that problem is hugely important to them.  But in the overall scheme of things it's an isolated thing.

So, make educated judgements about the levels of support - if it's really THAT important to you, there is PAY support available.

Also, don't twist something like Mark Phelps signature around to fit your cause - read it for what it is and really think about being in his shoes.

### Post by cariboo on 2011-12-15
I can only comment about [https://bugs.launchpad.net](https://bugs.launchpad.net), as that is where I report all the bugs I run into. Over that last year or so, Launchpad has improved greatly with things like automatic duplication notifications, invalid bugs being closed and in general timely actions on bugs.

The major problem is that many users don't know how to create a proper bug report, and when they are asked for more details, they seem to ignore the request. When I ask someone to create a bug report, especially if the are a newer user, I link to the [bug reporting wiki page]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/ReportingBugs"), so they should know what is expected of them when they report a bug.

Another thing that many users aren't doing, is using the affects me too button, the developers have stated openly that the more people a bug affects, the sooner it will get looked at.

In general getting bugs fixed in Ubuntu has improved greatly since I first started using it.
