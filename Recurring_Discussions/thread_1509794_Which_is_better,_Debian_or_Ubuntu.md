### Post by NMFTM on 2010-06-14
I don't think Debian is really suitable for standard desktop usage for the following reasons:

**Debian Stable:** Updates about every two years. So, if your a desktop user your going to get very irritated at the lack of software updates. Stable is what Debian is all about. Testing and Unstable exist only for the purposes of improving Stable releases. Which, generally is not what your desktop user is going to want to use.

**Debian Testing:** Packages update about every two weeks. So, if your a desktop user your going to get very irritated if some package updates that breaks things and you have to wait a minimum of two weeks for it to be fixed. It won't happen terribly often, but when it does it'll be very annoying. Because it'll take so long to get fixed unless you want to do it manually.

**Debian Unstable:** Packages update about every day. So, if your a desktop user your going to get very irritated when you update and stuff frequently breaks. Although, the bugs will be fixed faster than in Testing.

Basically it's a sliding scale between stability and updatability. Stable rarely breaks but it rarely updates. Testing breaks every so often, but when it does your going to have to wait awhile for it to get fixed. Unstable breaks fairly often, but you won't have to wait long for it to get fixed.

[B]
Ubuntu:[/B] Updates about every 6 months. Or sooner if you enable  backports, which is completely optional. Based on a recent Debian build  and worked on by a team of people for about 6 months until it's thought  to be ready for prime time. If something breaks, chances are it'll have  been broken from the start. So, there won't be many surprises.

### Post by snowpine on 2010-06-15
> **jaceleon said:**
> In terms of security, which is better? In speed (though I think crunchbang + debian definitely beat xubuntu 10.04 by 4 times)? In updates?

CrunchBang's speed and security will likely not be affected very much by the switch from Ubuntu to Debian. Ubuntu and Debian use the same packages and applications, and CrunchBang of course is a custom build.

The biggest change will be the support cycle. Rather than a semi-stable base distro that is supported 18 months, CrunchBang will use an ultra-stable base that is supported ~3 years. Users who want a "rolling release" can easily achieve it by switching to the "sid" repositories. A win-win situation in my opinion.
