I agree, the repositories are mostly complete now for both 32 and 64 bit. However, I do a great deal of development work, and occasionally rely on software not available in the repositories. Here is where you run into trouble. You cannot deny that x86 is more widely supported.

So granted, it's rare that I can't find everything I need for x86_64, but it happens. But then I still fail to see any benefit of x86_64. So, yes, my system boots 2 seconds faster with the 64 bit edition, and I can finish decompressing a huge file 4 minutes earlier... but that means nothing to me when 2 seconds faster means 30 seconds instead of 32, or 4 minutes earlier than around 6 hours.

If I might get on my soapbox and complain about people who blindly promote x86_64 without any reason why: "It's newer--it's gotta be better! OMG 32 more bits WTF!"

Try it and see for yourself and don't believe the FUD! If you want truly optimized performance, run Gentoo/Arch Linux, in my opinion. Even then, you probably won't notice much difference.

Chances are most people won't ever have problems with software unavailability... but it's definitely more likely. That's why I say it's a bit of a toss up.

I believe the only compelling reason to use x86_64 is for memory support. If you have 3+ GB of RAM, you'll likely be wasting it on x86, and then 64 bit makes a lot of sense.
