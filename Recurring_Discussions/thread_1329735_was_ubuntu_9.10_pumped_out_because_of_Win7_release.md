### Post by ajgreeny on 2009-11-17
Even if it were so, does it really matter?  The schedule for releases is April and October every year, (OK 6.06 was a bit different!) and who cares what else is released.  The best way to avoid these sort of problems is not to move to the new version straight away, but to wait for a month or so for some of the difficulties to be sorted out and solved.

I always have a small partition on my setup with the new version, more or less at release, and just play with it for a while, but I link all my personal files (docs, music, videos etc etc) but not any configuration folders from my old home, and it is then possible to mess around with the new version and test it out on my system, without compromising my ability to work properly with the computer.  Sometimes I miss a version completely, as I did with 8.10, but generally I sort out the problems and then install the newest version as my working system, and await the next version upgrade.

At the moment, I'm not certain about karmic, it may be another that I miss completely.  If the OS ati driver is updated so I can run compiz at a proper resolution and colour depth, perhaps I will move to it, for that's my only problem at the moment.

Anyway, why complain about something that we all got for nothing, anf that has the odd problem that does not suit us personally.  It's a bit like jumping in the deep end of a swimming pool when you don't know if you can swim or not.  Test it properly before you try to use it as your main operating system; you know it makes sense!

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2009-11-17
There was already a rockin', rock solid release out before Windows 7 ever came along: 8.04 LTS. 9.10 was released on schedule (once every 6 months) with an Enterprise LTS release every 3 or 5 years I think (someone feel free to correct me on that). If you do a quick search you should easily be able to find the Ubuntu release schedule. It has been there for all to see for a long time.

9.10 was no big secret nor was it any early release to compete with Windows. There is no competition in this respect and that idea is a conspiratorial fantasy. It works like this:

8.04 = 2008, April
8.10 = 2008, October
9.04 = 2009, April 
etc ...

Elementary. Your question is a Furphy. ;-)

ps: I don't install until 6 months after release (but only use LTS releases now anyhow). I run three machines on a network and get very short windows for tweaking during the year. The rest of the time they MUST be stable and reliable. Might stick 9.10 or something even newer on a partition somewhere if I get the chance over the holidays. 

My theory? If it ain't broke, don't fix it. The idea that the latest is the greatest (most stable) in the Ubuntu world is silly. It is a throwback from MS thinking and look what happened there: Think Vista and I don't think that was released undercooked to compete with anything, just to be the new glossy flagship of contemporary computing ... unfortunately that flagship had some fairly major leaks. That cake was well undercooked but look how many people gleefully forked out their cash to have the 'latest and greatest'. 

In my opinion Windows 7 was released partly because MS only had XP, the OS they were attempting to kill off, to compete with Linux in the netbook market (forget Vista which was their 'latest and greatest' ... wouldn't run on a netbook; MS didn't see the future surge in netbook popularity but MS are not great at foresight - we'll never need more than 64kb, I think that's what Bill said all those years ago) and partly to save face after the Vista disaster by bringing out a real operating system. They took the hint and Windows 7 looks closer in concept and design to Ubuntu and Mac than the older Windows anyhow (IMHO).

If you have the time to tweak, freak and in the process learn something and help developers and other users by sharing that new knowledge, go for it! 9.10 - The more the merrier (and helpful). If you want stable then as you were; re-install 9.04 (if that was stable). :)
