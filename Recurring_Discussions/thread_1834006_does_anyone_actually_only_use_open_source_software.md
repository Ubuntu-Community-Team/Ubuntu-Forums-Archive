### Post by ninjaaron on 2011-08-26
> **b2zeldafreak said:**
> I'm interested in why people choose to use only open source software, or more specifically, why people choose to use only "free" software. Can someone who chooses this tell me?

This is mostly an ideological decision for the very few that make it.  There are real benefits to using open source software as much as possible, because it is usually safer and more secure than closed-source.  The code is also usually cleaner and at a higher standard because it is subject to constant peer-review.  This doesn't necessarily mean it is more feature-rich than proprietary code or creates more "usable" programs, only that the technical foundation is more secure, almost by default.

If you choose to use *only* free software even when there are better or more convenient proprietary alternatives (I have difficulty describing flash as "better" than anything), it is because using closed and proprietary software empowers the companies that use it and the systems that reinforce it.  It perpetuates a system that restricts the passage and usage of knowledge.  As someone who is looking at a future in education, I must admit that I find the idea of "intellectual property" deeply troubling, and I would prefer not to empower that system.  I still haven't come to the point where I am ready to sacrifice the convenience afforded me by using many of the technologies and ideas protected (or rather imprisoned) by intellectual property law.

Almost nobody actually does it.  There are other ideals that are more important to me, and I only have the energy to maintain a certain number of them at a consistently high level.  While I believe the ideology is good, FOSS isn't high enough up there for me.

### Post by Chimes on 2011-09-09
Although the clarifications by means of misunderstanding in this thread have been valuable, it is visible in the original post that ninjaaron meant "free" in the stricter definition espoused by organizations like debian. That is, a definiton of "software freedom" in which no mechanisms are employed that might fetter the distribution, modification, or use of said software or hardware (such as patents or trademark assertion, restrictive licenses) and components of the software or hardware are made publicly available (such as publishing schematics for hardware rather than just software or hardware equivalents of binary blobs). 

(An aside: It is an interesting, almost ouroboros-esque question of whether restricting the restriction of code's use constitutes a sort of restriction in itself, or (because, in effect, it is more likely to guarantee the code's continued availability) the de facto least restrictive license possible. Obviously, the publication of software under the LGPL and the public domain are responses in that debate which lean toward the former answer.)

Nonetheless, I feel like one of the earlier responders in this thread may have pretended to not understand what Ninjaaron was referring to.
