### Post by murderslastcrow on 2011-06-08
If you absolutely need advanced non-destructive editing and color modes like LAB and CMYK, use Inkscape, Krita, or Photoshop. Krita works with CMYK and a lot of non-destructive elements, but it's not really for graphic design, more for illustration.

Inkscape is pretty incredible, but not exactly the most flexible since it doesn't prioritize pixels (for obvious reasons).

So if you really need to print something with color accuracy for a client or a project (or just to be correct and have a nice outcome), you can either play with the CMYK separate plugin and move a file between programs, or use Photoshop. It works great in WINE, so you shouldn't have much trouble, so long as you own it, or a trial, or a student copy.

Aside from those two issues, they provide the same essential functionality, plugins, and filters for the most part (you can use a lot of Photoshop stuff in GIMP, like brushes and certain extensions and plugins). Where there are differences, it's usually just in the way a dialog is set up, not in actual functionality. Although GIMP does have a few options and plugins not available in Photoshop, as photoshop has a few options not available in GIMP. But they're not the most important ones, just cool little tricks and things.

Ie. GIMP 2.7 (soon to be 2.8) is way better than Photoshop 7 except for the issues I mentioned earlier, and that's good enough for most people, honestly, even designers. I use GIMP in my work all the time.

Also, they have single-window mode in 2.8, and they have GIMPshop to make it more like the Windows version of Photoshop in verbage and layout. But if you've ever used the OS X version of Photoshop, you may notice a lot more similarities with GIMP's layout on Linux. The differences in layout aren't so huge that anyone should complain about them- if you're used to one program's layouts and have your muscle-memory built for it, it's not GIMP's fault for being different. If you start with GIMP and head to Photoshop you'll have the same issue. They're both good pieces of software, just deal with the UI. It's not horrible.

Anyway, that's an opinion from a designer who uses GIMP for work. Sometimes you just need Photoshop's color management, but that will most likely change with GIMP 3, which may take a few years to come out. At that point, I honestly don't think you'd be smart to ignore GIMP.
