I don't much care about what the average person thinks of Linux (or either of the Steves for that matter).  They're unlikely ever to use it (knowingly) in their lifetimes.  I care more about what other professionals in the IT field think of Linux, and that's who'll be reading his comments.  To suggest that Windows is becoming irrelevant in business IT is simply bunk.  Even someone managing a mixed environment of Linux and Windows servers and Linux and Windows desktops knows full well that Microsoft isn't going anywhere when it comes to medium and large enterprises.  It's certainly not going away in small businesses either. 

Linux has no leverage in most vertical markets that I have contact with.  Medical?  Nope, and it's getting even less competitive if anything.  Expanding health IT hasn't led to an influx of new players offering open-source solutions.  It's lead to an increased dominance by behemoths like GE with proprietary systems and Windows desktop applications. I once consulted to a manufacturing company that had adopted a Linux-based system to run their operations because it was one of the few alternatives available at the time.  When Windows competitors arrived on the scene, they switched platforms, and I lost a client.  The open-source model is good for some things, but one thing it's not good for is building closed proprietary systems.  Vertical market applications are generally all proprietary and closed.

How about a new entrepreneur setting up a small business?  She needs a simple accounting system.  GNUCash or QuickBooks?  You know the answer as well as I do to that one.

I use Linux on the desktop and on the server. My clients use the Linux servers I build and maintain for specific purposes.  They're also using AD and Exchange. Desktop users?  They're all running Windows.  I don't see that changing much in this decade.

I agree that, for consumers, more and more of their interactions with computers will come in the form of appliances.  But most of those people are going to be looking at desktop monitors when they come to the office each day, and more of them are likely to be looking at Windows than anything else.
