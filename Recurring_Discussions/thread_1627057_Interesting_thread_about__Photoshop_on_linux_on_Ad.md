Convince Adobe of what?

I wouldn't say there is no market, but that is not the point. The point is whether the return justifies the cost in development from Adobe's point of view.

PS is a big project and I supppose it is not so easy to port it to Linux and there is quite a bit of overhead cost involved. Will the potential increase in sales be profitable enough to be attractive after offseting the developmental cost?

On the other hand, Adobe can already access the Linux market without doing anything. As long as people who want PS bad enough are dual booting, running WINE and virtualizing Adobe is still selling ps license(well uslesss you use a pirated copy but that is another story) So if you are Adobe, from a purely $ perspective why would you spend the time and money on developing a Linux version while you are already making money off those who would actually pay for it? 

Slightly off tangent, I have made this point on the other thread about Ubuntu's app store. Those who think that the app store will somehow show big vendors such as Adobe that Linux users are willing to pay for software and as a result convince them to port their products to Linux are indulging in wishful thinking, for exactly this reason. It is never a question that some people are willing to pay.

BTW, the guy is wrong that Linux has to change before commercial vendors would enter the market. In some areas  support for Linux by commercial vendors are very strong. Scientiic computing is an example.  Softwares like Mathematica, Matlab and Maple all have Linux versions. It is not just because there is a demand, but because Linux is the natural platform to do that sort of things and a large portion of users of these softwares, if not actually the majority, do use Linux. Same cannot be said about Photoshop, not by a long shot.

That would minimize the initial investment.
