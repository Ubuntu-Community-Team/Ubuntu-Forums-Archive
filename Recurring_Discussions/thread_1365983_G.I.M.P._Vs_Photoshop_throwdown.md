Think of it like a document within a layer. The layer can be edited as an instance, rendering almost every change to it as non-destructive. They're just very neat and great to have.

It's worth noting that CYMK support is irrelevant for computer graphics that doesn't not involve printing (ie, web design/software design). Obviously GIMP can "convert" RGB to CYMK, but the conversion is imperfect by nature (dimensionality increase). Meaning The GIMP is unsuitable for professional printing work where "color accuracy" is important, but not unsuitable for regular printing work.

Adding CYMK support required a complete redesign of the The GIMP's internals.

### Post by Shibblet on 2009-12-29
> **Frak said:**
> CMYK is very important to photographers. Even more than RGB (by a landslide).

CMYK is NECESSARY for a commercial printer.

Offset printers can only print in solid colors.  So, in order to "Create" a full color appearance, you have to print in a 4 Color Separated process.  Which means you have to break your color plates apart into 4 separate colors.  Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black.
[ATTACH]141691[/ATTACH]

After these colors are broken apart.  A screen needs to be applied, also known as a halftone.  What that does is turn shaded areas of an image into a dot pattern.  Just like you see in newspapers.
[ATTACH]141692[/ATTACH]

If you notice the dot patterns are at different angles.  That is so when one dot lays overtop of another dot, the shade of color comes through.  If you blend a Cyan Dot with a Yellow dot, you get a green dot.  And depending on how far overlapped the Cyan and Yellow dot are, will determine the shade of green.

What you end up with, is an image like this.
[ATTACH]141693[/ATTACH]

Now, this is called a color-halftone pattern.  Which is a color image comprised of Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black.

This had been greatly enlarged for demonstration purposes.  But this is how color printing is done.  When you see a color image in a magazine or newspaper, grab a magnifying glass and look closely at the print pattern.  It's all done CMYK.

Here end-eth the lesson.  :)

Exactly.

Your RGB Colors are part of the "Additive" or "Light" color spectrum.  R+G+B = White.  Your colors are transparent, and when light is shown through them, they make White.  This is how televisions and monitors display their color.
[ATTACH]141783[/ATTACH]

CMYK is the "Subtractive" or "Opaque" color spectrum.  C+M+Y = Black, B = Highlights.  Your colors are transparent, but your substrate is opaque.  Paper doesn't light up the way a television does.
[ATTACH]141784[/ATTACH]

These aren't the same images anymore, are they?  The bottom one has been converted to CMYK.  In which case you lose your color gamut that was available with RGB.

RGB is for anything "On-Screen", CMYK is for anything printed.
