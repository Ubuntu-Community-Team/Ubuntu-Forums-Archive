### Post by Mike_IronFist on 2009-08-08
**Why I convert people to Ubuntu**
Let's be frank. Lots of people don't like change. But when you show them just how green the grass can be on the other side of the fence, it makes them want to take a trip over and see what that healthy new lawn is all about.

I'm speaking, of course, about Windows users and persuading them to switch to Ubuntu. Windows might have a lawn that ain't too shabby, but boy, the grass is greener than emeralds on the Ubuntu side.

You're probably wondering why I convert people to Ubuntu. Well, it's not that I want to legitimize my own usage of the OS, or that I want to feel less lonely about my OS. The simple point is, I've found something wonderful, and I feel that human urge to show others how wonderful it is. It's like finding beautiful scenery and taking a picture so others can see. You just gotta do it.

**How I convert people to Ubuntu**
And so, I always start with, usually, avoiding the word "Linux". Why? Because Linux evokes images of either "What the heck?" or black screens with white text that say programming nonsense. In other words, Linux is associated with confusion, nerdiness, high technology, etc. People assume that since it's not the most common operating system type that it must be hard to make sense of because it's foreign.

Instead I open my laptop to the Ubuntu boot screen, and I introduce Ubuntu. I say "See how fast it boots? If I ever need to get on the internet fast, or put something on a flash drive, I use Ubuntu because I can get the job done right away." It sounds like an infomercial, but the fantastic speed at which Ubuntu boots doesn't disappoint.

I immediately demonstrate the compiz effects. I ask questions like "Do you think Vista looks nice? or OSX? Look at this." A few wobbly windows, animations, and desktop cubes later, the potential converts are already impressed. I also break out Cairo-Dock to show them that I can have a dock just like the folks on Mac OSX get. Nice eye candy. At this point they say, "Well it looks nice, but I don't know if I'd want learn a whole new operating system." That's when I reply, "Ubuntu is probably the easiest thing I've ever used."

I demonstrate with Add/Remove. "Do you download programs on the internet for Windows? How do you find them?" Of course they do, and it's usually via a Google search.
*(By the way, for those unaware, Google uses Ubuntu as their default operating system, and they probably will until they can safely replace it with the Linux-based Chrome OS.)*
I respond that "Ubuntu doesn't require searching the internet for an app that does what you need. Suppose you need a photo editor." I type into the Add/Remove search bar "image editor". I get tons of results. I pick a random one and select it, then click apply. "You just pick it, hit apply, and Ubuntu takes care of all the installation for you."

For other ease-of-use demonstrations, I usually show how easy it is to get connected to wireless internet, how easy modifying the look of Ubuntu is, (You can even make it look and feel like Windows if you like!) and how the compiz setup actually adds efficiency to the desktop, not just eye candy.

But then they are bound to wonder, "Can this do the things I need it to do?" This comes out in the form of phrases like "it's nice, but I wouldn't want to give up  -Insert_AppName_Here- just for that stuff." That's when I bring in the grand finale - the default software.

I show off GIMP, talking about how it nearly rivals Adobe Photoshop, I show off Rhythmbox and its podcast functionality, an alternative to iTunes. OpenOffice.org is, of course, the Microsoft Office killer, Pidgin demonstrates a superb IM (and if all goes well, eventually Empathy will) and last, I open Mozilla Firefox.

"It comes with Firefox too." I add. This seems like an afterthought, but Mozilla Firefox is the most popular piece of Open Source software ever released. People get pretty excited seeing it in Ubuntu, in addition to an Instant Messenger that they can log into with their own AIM, MSN, Yahoo, or other IM account, and they are usually impressed to see that OpenOffice provides all their school or office jobs with the necessary outlet to get it all done. Demonstrating simple little things (like the fact that, yes, you can watch Youtube on it) add a little more incentive, as does showing that some Windows programs can be run in Wine.

I explain also that hardware is rarely an issue like it is in Windows. "It runs like a charm, and if you need to use something with it, there's no waiting for hardware to be 'installed'. Is usually just WORKS." Of course, "It just WORKS" has been a slogan for the Linux movement for years regarding hardware drivers. I find that it holds true more than any other Linux claim. What also holds true is that there's even less a threat of viruses than on Mac OSX, which already had a sterling reputation for security. "It's nearly impossible to get a virus on Ubuntu." I add. "Ubuntu needs no anti-virus of any kind."

But then it all comes down to the last thing. "On top of all that, you get all this, and it's all absolutely free. The entire purpose of the operating system? Free. It's totally legal and is made with the intent of being installed, for free, and copied, for free." Free is a powerful word. Knowing that you can use this as long as you like, install it on however many PC's you like, being able to run it off the CD, and the fact that you never have to deal with painfully draconic "Genuine Activation" schemes makes the OS look even better.

I have converted several people to Ubuntu users and intend to convert several more, because I have yet to see someone get converted to Ubuntu and regret it.

**The results of converting people to Ubuntu**
I feel very happy when I've made someone else happy with a new Ubuntu setup. After teaching how to get restricted media and graphics drivers working in top shape (something that usually takes only a few minutes) anyone I've helped with the installation of Ubuntu is very pleased with their results. The new operating system they have is fun, easier to use, more efficient, and just plain better all around than most products that they'd have to PAY for under most circumstances. You can't put a price on that warm feeling you get making another person happy.

So far, I've converted 6 of my friends over but I've gotten dozens of others interested in at least trying out Ubuntu. Once I move into my University campus, I will surely begin entertaining and converting many more. I enjoy making the world smile, and with Ubuntu, I can, cheesy as it sounds, get a smile at least one computer at a time.
