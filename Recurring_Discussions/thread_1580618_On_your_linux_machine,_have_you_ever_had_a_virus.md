### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-09-23
If you study the reasons why Linux, by itself with perhaps a GUI like Gnome, is secure, then you consider the amount of work put into individual applications to make them more secure on top of that, it would be an extremely rare occurence to get a Linux virus infection, and would require your permission to some extent, or the use of arbitrary code which could work in user space.

Noting how anal we are about security, in general, and the fact that Linux is used extremely often on servers, and is quite popular in that space, I think it's safe to say that getting a Linux virus on Linux should be an utter shock to anyone.

The main threat I think might become more prominent soon is the use of a cross-platform virus. Still, unless your OS provides a way for it to live and nourish itself with its own permission, which Linux doesn't, there's no way it can do any harm. You need to execute it, yourself, and give it root privileges to do any long-term damage and keep itself alive.

Anyway, we all know that security in Linux will never be like Windows, and even if it were largely imperfect, it's the best we've got so far.

The main use for antiviruses in Linux is to catch spam or attachments that have infections and notifying the sender or refraining from sending it to anyone else. Basically trying to keep the spam and malware down for the Windows users of the world.

At least, that's my take on it.
