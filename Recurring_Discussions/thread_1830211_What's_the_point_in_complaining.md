### Post by Pirate Zoro on 2011-08-21
Even now, months after the release of Natty, I constantly see people bitching and moaning about how Canonical "shoved the [Unity] interface down our throats" or threatening to leave if they can't have their GNOME, their way. And I don't see the point in complaining or throwing such fits. This is Linux. If you don't like an environment, just change it. Don't like the look of Unity? Try KDE. Unity too slow? XFCE and LXDE are great speedy alternatives. There really is no point to all the whining.

Okay, so I've only been a Linux user full-on for about 8 months. But in those short months I have tried and experimented with the 4 major DEs, plus Unity, various window managers, docks, launcher programs, and just trying out everything I could get my hands on (breaking my install on more than one occasion ;D) . Now, if I, being a newbie at the time I started playing around, could do it, why not the people who have been using Ubuntu, or Linux in general, for YEARS? They are supposed to be the veterans, the expert users to teach the new generation of users the ins and outs of the system. And what have they taught? That if something changes, whine and complain and throw a tantrum.

I realize this sort of turned into a rant at the end, but that was my 2 cents on the matter. There really is no point when you can change what you want.

> **FreeTheBee said:**
> If you don't agree with an idea, because you think it is fundamentally flawed, it is fairly difficult to give criticism that will be considered constructive.

If you think this, then you have a very shallow understanding of constructive criticism. Constructive criticism is very specific as to what the problems are and offers specific suggestions for improvement. Saying Unity = bad is neither constructive nor helpful.

Constructive criticism is: ____ in Unity needs work because ____, and one possible way of resolving this problem could be ____.

Your "constructive criticism" is not helpful because it doesn't advocate repairing what exists but simply advocates no change. You're ignoring the fact that the UI must change to be more compatible with newer hardware, in this case: touch interfaces. You're also ignoring the fact that the old Gnome is no longer being developed, thus forcing change. You, and many others have already found ways around this problem, so the rest of us (including the OP) are confused as to why you stick around to complain. Several ways of resolving this could be:
[LIST]
[*]Ignoring posts discussing Unity (since you've moved on, the Unity discussion isn't important to you anyway).
[*]Refraining from posting negative criticism on a UI that you have no/little experience with.
[*]Refraining from posting negative criticism on a UI that you do not even use.
[*]Deciding to participate in a more constructive way.
[/LIST]

Constructive criticism is also being aware that the criticized should not be faulted for not listening to criticism. So you can feel free to take my advice or leave it.

Finally, it seems to me that most people use the words "fundamentally flawed" when they really mean "I don't like it."

### Post by angryfirelord on 2011-08-21
Well, when things change people tend to complain about it. The thing is the decision for Canonical to choose a custom UI was made based on the direction that Gnome was going. It wasn't Ubuntu that abandoned Gnome 2, it was the Gnome team that abandoned Gnome 2. Therefore, Canonical had several choices to make:

[LIST]
[*]Drop Gnome as the official desktop and go with KDE 4
[*]Go along with Gnome 3
[*]Use another desktop such as XFCE
[*]Try and create something different
[/LIST]

Obviously the first two choices would have yielded the same howls of disappointment as they do today. KDE 4 in my opinion still has speed and usability problems and it wouldn't have flowed from the same idea of using Gnome 2. Same for Gnome 3.

XFCE seems to be the option that some have taken already and the current release (4.8) is very good. But it still lacks some customizing features that would be suitable for a new user. Therefore, the choice Canonical made was one where they personally could exert some control over.

Choices like this certainly aren't made on a dime. But I think that Unity will eventually pay out over time as the general usability issues and bugs are worked out. One person in particular noticed some similarities between Unity and Mac OS X Lion:

[http://cassidyjames.com/b/61]("http://cassidyjames.com/b/61")

I'll admit, I was a denier at first, but after using it I've started to like it.
