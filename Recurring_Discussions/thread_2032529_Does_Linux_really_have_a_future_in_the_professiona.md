### Post by DoctorVarney on 2012-07-24
Before I begin, please note, I am not a trolling cynic who supports large multi-national corporations.  I do not have a lot of funding behind me.  I want to believe in Open Source  and was originally drawn to the ideology behind the movement simply because of my disillusionment with Microsoft and Apple.

So can open source really cut it in the professional world? Well, let's look at what we get for our money.  One buys into the (so called) 'cult' of Apple Mac and one gets a product that just works, straight out of the box (it really does!)   One buys into multi-national giant of Microsoft/ Intel and spends many long, tedious hours dumping useless software, installing upgrades and security measures and tweaking the damn thing... but eventually it does work.  And Windows, when stripped to the waist, has proven itself ready to work.

But Ubuntu.... Uh!  I mean, seriously...!  Half an hour into the course and it seems apparent one needs a degree in computer science to just install the thing.  You get the impression, from the way people talk, that the software is all out there, ready for the taking.  Too good to be true?  Well, if I learned one lesson in life, it's that if something; anything appears too good to be true - it invariably is.  Period.

The big question - how does Linux pay for itself - is answered duly with aplomb in the Ubuntu FAQ.  Of course it is - because everyone that's used Microsoft or Apple is going to wonder why they just paid all that money for their software if this is so great!  We know the answer the Open Source Community gives us - but is this really the way to carry on?  Proprietary software works because it quite simply has the money behind it, generated from sales, to put in place a proper support network to ensure efficiency.  Look at how dependant the human race has become upon computers in the last 20 years... could that have happened if we couldn't depend on it in the first place?  No.  It's not so much a question of whether we can depend on Apple or Microsoft - but more - how can we depend on something that does not generate revenue to pay it's programmers.   For God's sake - the two main proprietary operating systems on this planet are based on Unix!  Doesn't that deserve something more than a pat on the back for being free?

I could be here for hours relaying to you some of the technical difficulties I've encountered in setting up Linux on my computer... but the message I'm trying to get across here - is that Open Source is going to have to get competitive if it's going to take off and become anywhere near a challenge to the multi-national software giants and more than just a geeky figure of fun among paid professionals who need to be able to depend on the computer to get work done in the real world.

Again - please don't get me wrong.  I am on your side!  Everything Linux preaches as the prophet of practicality and fairness - I want to believe in.  Like a messiah from the heavens to save us from man-made inequality!  But, like all big words and great intentions - they need to be backed with big actions in order to mean anything in the real world.  They need money behind them.

Listen to me  - if Linux can prove itself - I'd buy it!  I don't expect something for nothing... but if things carry on the way they are, I don't see how we can expect much more than big ideas coming to nothing... as they invariably do when funding is virtually non-existent.

Linux, instead of being free, why not do some great deals - like lifetime free updates for instance?  There must be hundreds of ideas that fit with a fair business ethic, other than 'giving it away for nothing'.  You don't have to do that.   You have to ask yourselves if it's actually fair to expect programmers to work for nothing.  The more people that migrate to Linux because they're getting a fair deal, the more the product will earn for it's programmers.  It's simple business, yet you don't have to exploit customers if you can get the right balance of fairness and practicality.  Take a leaf out of DAW creators, Image Line's books, for instance.  They don't give you anything for nothing - but offering value for money just brings more faithful customers into their fold year upon year.  You should see how FL Studio has developed in the space of a few years!  Everyone is happy with the product, on both sides of the counter.  Truly, a product I can believe in!  Yeah... and guess what?  It only runs on Windows!  Grrrrr!!!

Anyway, I need to cut this short, as it's in danger of becoming a wordy rant.  The bottom line is that Open Source could become a fantastic business and a worthy competitor to Microsoft and Apple, if only it would start getting a business head and push towards quality, user-friendliness, cross compatibility and efficiency.  Otherwise, it will just carry on being a hobby and an interesting experiment.  Also think of the employment it could generate while people are losing jobs left right and centre due to the current economic downturn!

Well, I am ready for the onslaught of attacks my thoughts will probably invite.  I don't profess to great knowledge of the Open Source movement, economics  or computers in general - but from a purely objective standpoint, as someone who relies on tools to get the job done -  all I'm saying is - getting commercial and generating some serious cash will  be the way to get this thing called open source knocked into shape and ready for work in the professional world.

If anyone thinks I'm wrong and wants to correct me, I'm all ears.  I will happily take on board due criticism if I'm shown otherwise.  Maybe I am wrong... but from the point of view of a complete novice, I can only relay to you my frustrating experience with Linux so far in the practical sense.  I can say one thing for sure though - it's precisely the feedback from the novice user's desktop experience that tells you if a product is working properly or not and tells us how well something is going to succeed in the real world, where not everyone can (or wants to be) a computer expert.

Thank you for reading - and, as said, all fair minded criticism and discussion is welcomed.

I think you're operating under a misconception about Linux. Almost all of the serious development work on the core components of Linux is done by professional programmers working for large corporations. Intel, Google, Red Hat and so on all contribute extensively to development (heck even Microsoft have contributed to the kernel) and many of the core components (eg: filesystems) are also sponsored by corporate money.

This is because Linux is a critical tool, it powers the servers that run the internet, and it powers the high-performance computers used by science and industry. This stuff generates real money, so is funded.

The community tends to contribute mostly applications to run on that existing platform, although something like 15% of kernel commits do come from non-affiliated sources.

*Desktop* Linux is different, nobody has so far been able to successfully monetise that. Mark Shuttleworth is trying, and Canonical has managed to both stay afloat and actually grow, but we don't know how much of Canonical's income comes from the desktop.

### Post by PhilGil on 2012-07-24
[@DoctorVarney]("http://ubuntuforums.org/member.php?u=1697196")

I was going to accuse you of venting without trying to fix the problems you're having, but after checking your post history i can see you've been working on troubleshooting.

Just some random observations on being new to Linux that you might not have thought about.

1. Hardware support in Linux tends to lag behind Windows as the Linux drivers have to be incorporated into the kernel. This sometimes requires reverse-engineering and is not a trivial task. If you buy state of the art hardware Linux support will be lacking for a time.

2. Most people have never installed Windows from scratch. A bare metal install of Windows is likely to require significantly more time and effort to get your hardware working properly than a Linux install. It's remarkable (to me) that Linux supports so much hardware out of the box.

3. I noticed a lengthy thread on installing themes in Gnome Shell. If you prefer Gnome Shell to Unity Ubuntu may not be the best distro for you. Ubuntu tends to be the most stable when you stay as close to the installed defaults as possible. Troubleshooting a non standard desktop environment may not be an easy task for someone who's new to Linux. And, as others mentioned, Gnome Shell is still very new so installing themes and the like isn't as intuitive as it should be.

4. Slow down. Ubuntu works very well out of the box, so take the time to understand Linux better before you start mucking around. Customization is one of the greatest strengths of Linux (not to mention really fun to do), but it takes a bit of understanding before jumping in.

5. Take notes. When something clicks, write it down. If your troubleshooting was successful, write it down so you'll know how to fix it the next time. Take notes on applications you'd like to try; copy the URL's of useful Linux sites. Use gedit, zim, tomboy, a paper notebook - whatever you're most comfortable with.

6. It took you years to learn Windows. You can't expect to have that level of proficiency with Linux after only a few weeks.
