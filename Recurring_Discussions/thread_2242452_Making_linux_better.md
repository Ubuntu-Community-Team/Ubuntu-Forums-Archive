> After some research I'm wondering why linux is such "mucky" water.


Commercial operating systems are produced by a single entity with a small guiding force with regards to what software is written and how it should work.

Open Source is thousands of individual projects, each of which can produce one or more software packages.  The smaller ones may be one guy with an idea.  The larger ones are a collaboration of many people with a unifying project for that software package.  For example, apache.org started with a web server and have branched out to many things supported by a large community.

Open Source can also come from commercial entities.  A company might make a product (crossover-office for example) and have an open source component which makes a base for that product (wine, in this case).  Hardware vendors often open-source the drivers for their hardware, or at least cooperate with volunteers who write the Linux drivers.

Governments also contribute a lot to Open Source.  NASA and various labs across the USA contribute a large amount of code specific to research.

The key fact is that each of these entities creates a package because THEY wanted it, they build it the way they want it to be, and they do it for their own reasons.

The short description is this:  Open Source is a democracy, while closed source is a kingdom or an empire.

> 
A lot of debate about the best distributions and such, but also the fact that linux is the "butt" of os jokes.


As are Mac OS and Windows debated as to what is best, and are the butt of jokes.  Not only do fans of one OS joke about others, but fans of an OS often joke about the quirks of their favorites.

>

Because the developers all do what they want for their own reasons.  Some of the developers are paid but mostly they're not.  Each software project has its own motivations and its own design philosophy and frequently their own country, although most large projects have people from across the globe.  Each has their own definition of success and their own goal in mind.

That's why they can't "get together."

Put Mac and Windows in the coffin?  I can't think of a single reason to try.  It takes all kinds to make the world go around, and some people like Macs and some people like Windows, and some people like other things.

There is no one-size-fits-all, for anything, on any topic that the human mind can conceive.

Well, I look at it a little more economically...in the supply/demand sense.
Commercial software seems to meet a demand that Open software doesn't. There are two possible reasons for this: Open software really doesn't meet the demand, or demand is artificially distorted in favor of commercial software. Turns out both are true.

Example of open software not meeting demand: Twitter. RSS feeds are open, and have been around for a really long time. Twitter figured out a way to popularize feeds, and apply them to both mobile devices and social networking. Open software didn't do that. Did Twitter's advertising budget help? Of course it did...but Twitter did not destroy a budding popular open RSS explosion. There wasn't one.

Example of a distorted market: iTunes and vendor-lock-in. There is nothing special about iTunes, except that you can't export your music library to another player...or a non-apple device. Generally, market distortions like this are best addressed by regulation instead of more software.

The big companies sell a promise to seem simple and functional, the promise that they *won't need to know* how it works. One goal of Ubuntu is to compete in that popular space.
You will find it very difficult to compete with *simple* and *functional* using logic, because companies roll them into an emotional argument: *safe*. Their product seems safe (even if it isn't).

Instead, use emotional terms that emphasize the 'safe' message: "Man, it's so *easy* to use, and it's *fun*, and it was so *simple* to try."
