### Post by aysiu on 2010-08-04
I don't see what the big deal is. Yes, there isn't a reliable way to confirm it's 1%, but we know it's relatively low compared to Windows.

If you consider there are probably 3 billion computers out there, 1% is 30 million. That's a lot of Linux computers if it's true.

It is much lower than Windows market share, but why is that a surprise? It doesn't mean Linux is crap and Windows is quality. Popular use doesn't necessarily mean quality. It makes perfect sense for a desktop/laptop/netbook OS that has [list][*]No unified or corporately-funded marketing campaign[*]Virtually zero brick-and-mortar demo presence[*]No brand-name recognition[*]A "for geeks and programmers only" reputation[/list] to have such a low marketshare.

The real problem here, for those concerns about [Bug #1](https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+bug/1) is the lie that the computer consumer market is a meritocracy. It isn't "make the best product, and everyone will use it." There are a lot of other forces at play. Sure, make the best product. Sure, quality matters to a certain degree. But you can't discount how much branding, advertising, vendor lock-in, and ready availability matter. Most consumers do care about the impression that "everyone" is using a certain product, even if that produce it worse than a lesser known product. Consumers do care about file format compatibility, even if the programs they use to create those files are worse quality than programs that support more open formats.

From a strictly technological (not sociological) standpoint, there is nothing inherent in Linux that prevents it from going mainstream. If Ubuntu had released a smartphone, it would probably have been dead in the water against the iPhone. One of the reasons Android has been as successful as it has been is the Google brand name.

### Post by Frak on 2010-08-05
I know everybody is going on about "you can't prove it's 1%". Of course not, statistics can't be proven, but they can be taken as a reliable source. Let me add a dash of common sense to the mix to show you why they're accurate:

1. Are the amount of desktop Linux computers on the internet high enough to constitute an outlook of the entire Linux market. I argue yes. You'd be very hard-pressed to find a bunch, I mean a ton, of Linux desktop computers that weren't networked.

2. How many people use a browser that doesn't report their OS within their User-Agent? I argue very, very little. If you're browsing the web, you're most likely using Gecko or WebKit, in which all of the popular implementations submit their OS.

3. How many people use a browser that hides their user agent? Once again, I'll say very little. Personally, that would get annoying after a while of sites offering me Windows binaries when I'm there for Linux software, but other than that, I have personal stats from my site that show high Linux visits. If most people used User Agents, that statistic would be utterly random.

4. A jump even a few percent would indicate such a wild bug that needed to be fixed. Going from 1% to 5% is an extreme jump, and would indicate that the majority of users were either not online or were using the combination of the above. All of which I see as very doubtful.

5. This result is echoed consistently across many data providers. If it was just one, I could see it as OK to question the validity, but many statistics show this result as Linux only hovers around 1%.



The statistics are accurate. The best thing I can say is, well, live with it.
