### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-08-14
It isn't legal to download them. However, to own them after you download them, since you purchased them, is perfectly legal. Depending on the record label, there are several ways to regain your music if something happens. However, the RIAA, which governs the majority of labels, strictly prohibits copying music in such a way. This is why iTunes has its own method of authorizing music files bought through their store.

Of course, as there are plenty of DRM-free vendors, downloading the music again and keeping it would only make it appear as though you had used a non-DRM music service, rather than stealing the music. So, you can very easily get away with it, and you should be able to ethically. However, ethical correctness and legality are two entirely different cases, although they should match more closely in an ideal world.

You own the music, you should be able to get it back some way. People have been doing this for ages before the internet. Unfortunately, many people simply steal, which is disrespectful to the artists who created the music, regardless of how much the labels take out of that money.

We should encourage musicians to get started with something like Magnatune and expand from there, or switch to them if possible, to create a more beneficial relationship between the musician and the consumer. This way, these foolish regulations may change for the better of both parties.

### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-08-15
Let me put this clearly.

In the United States, it is illegal to download or upload music through any peer to peer format, whether it be a program or torrent. If the distributor you are obtaining the file from is not p2p, and is authorized by the music's copyright owner, it is legal.

In Canada, it is legal to download music through p2p programs, but not legal to upload it.

Also, when you buy a CD, you do have the rights to copy the CD to a different format of your choosing, for the sake of convenience, and you are also authorized to lend it to friends for listening (not copying). This is in the United States and Canada.

Outside of these countries I don't really know. When it all comes down to it, it depends entirely on the license the music is originally distributed under, and that relies on what label you get it from.

If you're buying it from an RIAA governed label, the restrictions above apply, regardless of if you own the music.

So the best way to prevent this from happening in the future is to buy the CD and rip it in FLAC. Of course, this isn't very practical, and not all CDs are up to FLAC quality. It's very inconvenient if you want something better.

However, since most people are perfectly satisfied with the quality they rip, and billions more are satisfied with stealing even lower quality music, they pretty much assume you will find the FLACs if necessary. And I've never heard of a label prosecuting someone for downloading a higher quality version of music they legitimately own, nor have I heard of them ever discovering this.

But rest assured, it's illegal.
