### Post by DapperMe17 on 2011-11-05
You're frustration is surely understood about Unity. Canonical switched to Unity from the "classic" Gnome to compete more visually with Mac & Win8, in order to reach more contemporary users. You can't blame them!

Great thing about Ubuntu, is that you have a choice of 4-5 alternative window environments! (XFCE, LXDE, etc...)

I have a couple of very simple suggestions for you. 

1. If you like classic Gnome, but don't like the direction of Ubuntu Unity, then make the switch to Xubuntu (XFCE). It's very much lke classic Gnome, and looks & configures great. Easy switch through Synaptic by installing Xubuntu-desktop, then log out, and log into a Xubuntu/XFCE session.

2. Seriously consider partitioning your hard drive for 'buntu with a seperate root (/) and home (/home) partition. You can do this with GParted, but would have to reinstall using the custom partition method during the installation process. 

Links....

[http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/xfce](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/xfce)
[http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/purexfce](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/purexfce)

or

[http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/separatehome](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/separatehome) ...and reinstall your system.

### Post by Hooker Jay on 2011-11-05
I feel the pain, and my first brush with Unix/Linux was back in the mid 1990s on dial up where I would telnet to a UnixWare 2.1.2 shell account which turned into a Slackware shell account while I was in mid-compile and forced to re-dial. Now that's a sysadmin with a sense of humor! :)

But yeah, I'm tired too ... 

First off, the gory details us computer sages love ...

Gigabyte GA-K8N Ultra 9 (F8 Bios)
AMD Athlon64 3200 Winchester
ATI All-In-Wonder 2006 (i.e x1300 256MB)
Creative X-FI PCI Xtreme-Music

Primary Master: NEC DVD-RW 2500A

Secondary Master: Western Digital 200GB IDE 
(Non-bootable archival/music drive - 3 partitions, 1 pri, 2 logical)

Third Master: Western Digital 80GB SATA-II 
(Non-RAID PATA Mode - reserved for Linux)

Fourth Master: Western Digital 80GB SATA-II 
(Non-RAID PATA Mode - 1 pri partition w/ WinXP SP3, 1 logical partition for games)

After many months and headaches fighting with Ubuntu 11.04 to 11.10 due to sound card, video drivers, losing Gnome to Unity, seeing how Gnome3 was worse than Unity, and GRUB trying to impose itself on that IDE drive, I had to give up for a while and take a break. Recharge the batteries. Get WinXP re-installed. Calm down. Resist urge to smash PC into a billion bits ...

Anyway, what I'm trying to achieve is a stable WinXP/Linux dual boot installed on the two 80GB SATA-II drives non-raid, and leaving the MBR on that 200GB IDE alone if it can be avoided as its a non-active non-bootable archival/music drive. The three partitions on it should always appear in WinXP as F, G, and H respectively, or my OCD kicks in and the kernel betwixt my ears panics. I found the best way to ensure that happens (after ALOT of trial and error) is to unhook the 200GB IDE drive completely before installing WinXP. That way the two partitions on its own dedicated 80GB drive will be labed C and D respectively, the DVD-ROM will be E drive, and the other 80GB SATA drive won't get a drive letter since its unpartitioned. Then I shut down, hook that IDE drive back up, boot back into WinXP. VIOLA -- all partitions on that IDE drive are the way I want them ... 

... and then I install a rolling distro of Linux! :P

This is where my GRUB issues are coming from. No matter how many times I tell any Linux installer put GRUB on /SDC (i.e 3rd master SATA II drive devoted solely to Linux), it'll eventually impose itself on /SBA -- that IDE drive I want left alone -- whenever I apply updates, upgrades, or make major configuration changes like drivers and kernels. And there's no way I'm gonna keep unhooking that drive every time I boot to Linux because GRUB is too stupid to remember or detect where it was installed to in the first place. It detects WinXP on the other drive fine, has never mucked with its MBR, and 99% of time includes an entry to chain-load to its boot loader without failure. 

Of course, when GRUB breaks, I can't boot into WINXP -- its entry is gone, and doing the /fixboot and /fixmbr in the WinXP recovery console is a lost cause since its no longer the active/boot drive all thanks to GRUB having a creepy stalker fetish with that damned IDE drive. By the time I remember FDISK from a Win98/ME floppy may cure the problem, I'm well into my 3rd, 4th, or 9th re-install of Windows. IDE drive unhooked. SATA drives wiped clean. Cursing like a sailor ... 

Is it a lost cause?!? 

Does my IDE drive have a permanent stalker named GRUB?!? 

Or should I tell my OCD to go take a flying leap and let GRUB have its way with that IDE drive?!? I'm willing to tolerate it if it means no loss of data and in cases of an emergency where GRUB breaks and all efforts to repair it from a LiveCD/USB prove fruitless, I can still boot back into WINXP after unhooking the IDE drive, running FDISK, and marking the WINXP drive active again ... 

Figured I'd ask before attempting a Debian Squeeze or Linux Mint 11 install ...
