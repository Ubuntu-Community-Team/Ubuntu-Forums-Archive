### Post by kaldor on 2010-03-19
It's not that Linux isn't ready in my opinion. It's that people expect it to be what they already have. When I started Linux, I didn't compare; I started from scratch and appreciated it.

People want MS Office on Linux. They shouldn't want that. They should use OpenOffice or another free suite, not try to get their own stuff working.

People want MSN, not pidgin. Note: It's Linux, not Windows.

"Linux sucks cause it can't play games". No, it just doesn't run the games you want it to. Just don't use Linux.


My point being is that it isn't really a problem with Linux. The problem is that people don't want to change away from the software and such that they know. If more people stopped trying to compare, and learn to do things the "Linux way", I think there'd be much less frustration with new users. 

It isn't Windows. If you want to do everything that you do the same way as on Windows, don't switch to another OS. 


Linux is fully capable of taking off in the market. It's just that people "need" their current apps/programs to work, when they shouldn't. In some cases, yes, commercial software that's Windows-only is a must. But when it comes to stuff as simple as "I need to make Word documents, so I can't switch to Linux" it's just silly.

:)

Edit: This goes for *BSD and Solaris as well.

Edit2: This is why Mac OS X is popular these days. It's more compatible than it has been for a long time now. People can do the exact same thing as they can on Windows, meaning they'll adopt it. If MS released Office 2010 on Linux, or Adobe released Photoshop, etc, the same would happen.

### Post by toupeiro on 2010-03-19
> **swoll1980 said:**
> So is that what we are? Are Linux users a bunch of people that lowered their needs, and expectations? Here I thought we were people that opened are minds.

My reasons for changing were quite the opposite.  Microsoft stopped raising, and in many circumstances lowered its own bar on OS productivity, innovation, and security.  My needs aren't going to taper at their loss of focus.  Linux offers opportunities to continue to leverage new, innovative technologies to assist your daily compute tasks without the cost (not only in a monetary sense) of using a windows environment.  

Throughout the last 10-12 years, In Microsofts attempts to tighten their grip around technologies on their platform using patents and money, more and more opportunities to truly maintain their position using innovative advancements have slipped through their fingers.  In their attempts to attain the level of simplicity they felt their users wanted/needed, they've turned their OS into a cesspool of vulnerabilities.  In their attempts to address all the vulnerabilities they've created, they've implemented an elevation layer that put their OS into a borderline unusable state (which is why most people disable UAC).  Now here's windows 7, which commercially Microsoft says is "everyones idea"  It's a true shame, that they couldn't take credit for it themselves.  Hey, I don't MS bash this bad too often, but I've been working with Microsoft products since the hayday of MS-DOS and I've seen their successes and failures every step of the way.  The majority of the first decade of my career was spent fixing windows more than doing anything innovative with it.  It wasn't until I because a UNIX/Linux administrator that I felt I was really doing more innovative things rather than fixing the same broken things over and over again.  I'll credit MS where its due, and in the server market, they still do good things, in the home and enterprise desktop market, they have failed miserably in my opinion, and haven't truly shown any signs that they know it, let alone that they know how to recover from it.

Mac .. what can I say, Mac is a niche computing environment.  Apple has full knowledge of it, and full intention to remain a niche computing environment.  People who are in that niche will use Mac.  Some people love them, I personally think it's an overpriced Intel machine with an OS more proprietary by license than windows.  They do make some very cosmetically appealing machines and software, but I can make linux look just as pretty if thats what I really cared about with regard to using my PC.  Heck, I could skin the heck out of windows with Object Desktop.

I think most people don't use linux, because, most people  can barely manage to maintain their windows systems.  Windows, the flagship OS of desktop computing, which is supposed to be for everybody, is one of the most security riddled, bloated, and painful OSes out there, but most of these people do know a "techie" that can help them out.  Why would they switch from a system where its almost guaranteed that somebody on your street will be able to help you, to one that probably nobody in their immediate circle has even heard of?  I think for a person to truly appreciate what linux can do for them, first has to come to terms with what windows is not doing for them.  Before that happens, I'm not convinced they will willingly switch to linux.

If you're one of these techies that is always helping out a neighbor with a windows problem, and you see a point where linux could provide real help, the first thing you need to do is make sure they are with you, understanding their problem, how it happened, what they need to continually do to stop it from happening again, and watch their reaction.  Do not shield them from the work its going to take you by saying "ok, its done, have a nice day."  They can go to geek squad and pay $99/hr if thats what they want.  If you really want to help someone, teach them how to fix their issues, and if they feel like its something they can't do, give them reasonable alternatives to their problems (enter linux.)
