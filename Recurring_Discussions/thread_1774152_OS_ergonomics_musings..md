### Post by jhonan on 2011-06-03
The increasing popularity of smartphones and slates have driven this radical re-think of OSes. It's down to one thing; touch. When you're not using a mouse and a keyboard for primary input, then this requires a redesign of the OS.

This week Steve Jobs commented that while OSX Lion represented OSX and iOS moving closer, it wasn't practical to give desktop machines touch-screen interfaces - it's just not ergonomic.

What you've got are OS manufacturers attempting to address two very different segments; the casual/consumer user (slate/smartphone/netbook) and the creator/techie user (desktop machine, keyboard+mouse)

Both have very different requirements. As you rightly pointed out, Unity would be very suitable as a touch interface. I'm convinced Canonical want to make inroads into this segment (casual user, media consumer), so are using the Unity release as a testbed. The trouble is, that their traditional techie userbase are impacted, as they prefer a classic Gnome2/KDE style interface (YMMV)

If I pick up slate and want to check my email, I want to get there as quickly as possible. Look at iOS - one touch on one obvious icon a matter of seconds after switching on the device. Compare to any traditional desktop OS; which involves waiting for boot-up, clicking a menu, finding the entry for 'browser' and clicking it, and then probably having to maximise the screen. Much more hassle for what is a simple task.

Ah, but if I pick up a laptop and want to update a piece of code on a remote server using ssh/vi. Would I prefer to use an iOS/Android interface (that probably can't do the job to start with) or would I go straight for my Gnome2/KDE interface, knowing I can get to a stable terminal app in a few seconds? *And* I can use a proper keyboard?

And (not forgetting my washing machine analogy) - If I want to put my washing machine to a normal 60 degree cycle and start, do I want to start messing about with swiping my finger across a touchscreen, or just turn a knob?

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-06-04
On washing machines, I've been very tempted recently to start a topic on the one UI that really has become more and more complicated in recent years for no usability reason at all: the microwave oven control panel. Nothing has ever been faster or more efficient than a power dial and a time dial, yet there are twenty-five buttons on my microwave oven, none of which get my food cooked as quickly as turning a dial a tick would.

I've had plenty of thoughts on Unity and Gnome Shell and defended the changes in topics enough of late, and bwgia, I highly recommend a quick search of these forums for either, which would lead you to many individuals expressing your concerns and a fair number of people offering explanations and rebuttals. I will point out, at least, that putting everything on the left is a consideration for mice, not touch, because it's based on the idea that most folks in the Western world *read* left to right, and while a slate with controls on the left requires you to use your left hand or stretch, neither of those things is applicable with a mouse or trackpad. We tend to have navigation controls on the left and menus that populate starting with the left and so on.

On another point that's coming up a bit here, I do see mice as somewhat obsolete, but not because of touch screens. Trackpads are faster in operation and more ergonomic in a number of ways, they make possible all the fun gestures and features that come with a touch interface, and they lack things that mice can do only in tasks you should be using a drawing tablet for anyway. 

A touch interface, by comparison, lacks quite a bit of functionality and efficiency. It's only appropriate for a small device, because in a large display, it requires moving your arms around the entire interface, and it would certainly be more than awkward in any dual-monitor configuration I can imagine. It requires a fundamental compromise between a display's size and position and such with those of an input device. There's a reason that keyboards and mice aren't hung from the wall and that displays aren't laid flat on the desk.

In a small device, at the same time, it requires large click targets. It trades the "hover state" for some added efficiency in clicking a number of things quickly, such as in a soft keyboard, but soft keyboards themselves are a complete waste of time, about as ergonomic as T9 texting. 

I think of touch being ideal for small amounts of quick input without sitting down and plugging yourself in - nothing is faster for browsing, or pausing a movie, or skipping a track on a music player. For anything more involved, an Apple magic trackpad and a good chicklet keyboard are still the best tools for the job.
