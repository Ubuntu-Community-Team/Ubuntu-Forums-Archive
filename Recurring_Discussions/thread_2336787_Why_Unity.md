### Post by GXdmWQH on 2016-09-11
This isn't a question of support, just one of choice. I'm thinking about the future of Ubuntu and wondering why Unity was chosen as the default desktop environment. I expect there is a very simple answer, something along the lines of "it was created by the Ubuntu foundation to meet specific criteria which make the OS complete" (or similar), but however simple the answer is I'd still like to hear it.

The reason I ask, for those inclined to ponder, is that I just don't understand why anyone would opt to use it over the alternatives. It doesn't seem to fit any of the criteria any better than the alternatives:


[LIST]
[*]It is less light-weight than Xfce, it's probably a middle-weight frontend on the whole
[*]It is very difficult and limited in terms of customisation, by comparison to everything else (I'm a KDE user, this strikes a note with me)
[*]It is entirely unlike the other major competitors in functionality (OSx, Windows XP/7/8/10) so the "learning curve" is great for newbuntuers (I like that word).
[/LIST]

These are the core things I think the default DE needs to be... It meets none of those criteria. Why was it chosen? If you want Windows users, you offer Xfce. If you want Mac users, you offer Elementary (although given their track record I probably wouldn't suggest that one, maybe Gnome?) If you want customisation, you offer KDE.

I won't deny there are further extremes on all fronts (Xfce is by no means the leanest DE, but it's at that end, and there are probably things closer to Windows OOB than Xfce), but the point stands in spite of my awful ability to make it. So, can anyone tell me why, against all odds, Unity won?

### Post by ian-weisser on 2016-09-12
The simple answer you seek is "Unity was created by Canonical after discussions with several desktop environment projects in 2011, and none wanted to commit to creating a converged Desktop/Tablet/Phone user interface and codebase." However, that simple answer leaves out A LOT.

Start with a primer on Unity's history. Several good ones are out there. For one, see [http://ubucon.org/events/ubucon-summit-us/talks#ubuntu-redux](http://ubucon.org/events/ubucon-summit-us/talks#ubuntu-redux) , Jono Bacon's version of Ubuntu history, including the 2011 discussions with Gnome that led to Unity. It's not perfect, but it's one good starting point.

> **GXdmWQH said:**
> I just don't understand why anyone would opt to use it over the alternatives.
Your criteria are relevant to you. My criteria are different, and revelant to me.
Perhaps a better question to ask is 'Why do you like Unity?'...which is already a thread in this forum.

Happily, in the Debian/Ubuntu universe, you have a wide choice of free Desktop Environments to choose from. This community embraces that choice.

When you start talking about what a user interface 'should' be, or trying to impose your own criteria on others, you will find here that the mods here are likely to close the thread quickly.

You are welcome to discuss the projects and features you like, but trashing projects or others' choices is considered rude.

> **GXdmWQH said:**
> [COLOR=#000000]If you want Windows users, you offer Xfce. If you want Mac users, you offer Elementary (although given their track record I probably wouldn't suggest that one, maybe Gnome?) If you want customisation, you offer KDE.[/COLOR]

This assumes that Ubuntu should cater to whatever User Experience people first imprinted upon. I strongly disagree with that. That's the classic argument of trying to trying to increase market share by positioning as a (slightly inferior) free clone...something we are not. That argument was debated and rejected a decade ago.

It also assumes that Windows and Mac will always have a superior User Experience. One purpose of Unity was to create a new, clean, independent User Experience that is platform-independent and *easier* for unskilled users to learn than OSX or Win. You may not realize just how much more quickly you mastered Unity than the last time you had to learn a DE.

Finally, it assumes that new, unskilled users have a preference in the first place. We *know* from a decade of experience that most don't have a clearly understood preference...which is why precisely Unity is so devastatingly understated and bland for them. For those few with a real preference, that DE is just an apt command away.
