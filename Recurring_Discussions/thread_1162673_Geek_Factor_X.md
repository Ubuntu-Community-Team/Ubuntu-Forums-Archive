### Post by GOfree on 2009-05-17
Two sides to the issue (or more)...

On one side, people say that the geek factor hurts free software in the markets.


> At issue: Linux must overcome the Geek factor 
Lets face it. Those who know about Linux are labeled Geeks. Right or wrong, it is a simple fact. Also, Joe Schmoe wants a PC, he goes to the store and purchases a PC. The PC has Windows (name the flavor here) on it. Schmoe buys it, learns how to use it (somewhat) and then when Joe buys another computer, guess what... He wants to buy something with a familiar name and something he has used before. 

But the real reason is that Linux got off to a rocky start because Geeks (like me) decided to use the word grep instead of search and tar instead of copy, and vi instead of edit, and.... are you following me here? So, while DOS/Windows used english we used geekisms (case in point - Biff for email). We loved the ability to look smart and leave others in the dark - well... now our own ways have painted us into a box (shrink wrapped, shiny, and labeled Windows) Lets say Joe decided to be daring and load Linux on his system.... Where does he get help? Remember, Schmoe is not like us. He just figured out that the mouse (that little thing he once thought of as a foot pedal that powers things like an old sewing machine) is really used by the hand and it clicks on stuff!!! Who is he going to get the information from? I know... our pat Geek answer! The MAN pages! (Help is so UnGeeklike...)

[dwain.erhart@...; The de-marketing of Linux, Tech Republic; [http://techrepublic.com.com/5208-12853-0.html?forumID=102&threadID=214893&messageID=2195565]](http://techrepublic.com.com/5208-12853-0.html?forumID=102&threadID=214893&messageID=2195565])

LOL! :)

Having just spent the last three days straight installing and configuring FreeBSD for the first time, I feel that the poster really hit the nail on the head. The average LoseDoze user, Schmoe, is not like us, the average free software users. It takes a special kind of person to wade through the manuals and webpages to find a way to setup a computer system -- and enjoying doing it.

On the other hand, for people who like this sort of thing, maybe the geek factor is something to savour.


> Why install Linux on an IBM P70? Besides the geek factor, it&#8217;s to make it useful again. ### Post by GOfree on 2009-05-21
Yes, it is good that there is a wide spectrum of GNU/Linux/BSD distributions--those for the casual user and those for the user who wants to get into the guts of the system. If someone comes to these operating systems without previous experience, it is good to have a place to start that is very user-friendly, like Ubuntu.

I guess what I am pointing out is rather obvious, but the tone of the first opinion (in the first post)(which seems to be very common) is that "user-friendliness" is the only alternative--not merely a starting point but the end-goal itself--as if to say that "Linux" can *only* be successful if it becomes like Windows ("LoserDoze", sorry, bad joke).

I applaud Ubuntu for filling the niche of transition distro for Windows users, but am personally apprehensive that it will go too far toward replicating the Windows experience and thereby lose the beauty of what makes "Linux" unique. For instance, all the GUIs are fine for someone starting out, but will it make learning command line operation unnecessary. Also, user-friendliness seems to require making the system more complicated so that the intention of making the system easier to use, paradoxically makes it harder to understand (e. g. start-up scripts in Ubuntu compared to rc.conf in Arch, or Gnome Network Manager compared to netcfg).

I just wonder if distros that are so intensely going after the "average user" are somehow betraying themselves. It seems a little like an insecure person trying to act cool in order to make friends. Yes there are some fantastic desktop environments and applications that can and are winning over converts. But are they coming over for the right reasons? Will they have any appreciation for the true character of the system? Will they respect the system for what it is or will they quickly dump it again as soon as they discover that there may be some learning required?

I guess as a new convert myself, this is something I needed to say. I am glad that GNU/Linux is more than a mere Windows replacement. It is an amazing system that allows a person to really get into computing for its own sake.
