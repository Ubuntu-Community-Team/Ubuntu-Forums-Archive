**Dell,  Ubuntu,  and the college student**

MCFARLAND (WKOW) – Abbie Schubert paid more than $1,100 for a Dell laptop from their online store hoping to enroll in online classes offered by MATC.  MATC offers these classes online in a variety of subjects and before enrolling specifies their requirements for compatibility for online course work.  They recommend a Windows 95+ or Mac OS* system with minimal computer hardware requirements.  Windows 95 and Mac OS 8 are over 10 years old and antiquated by todays standards.

When she attempted to start her coursework, something stopped her.  Her new computer did not meet the minimal requirements.

Schubert says she ordered her laptop online at Dell.com expecting to buy your classic bread-and-butter computer.*

She didn't realize until the next morning her laptop defaulted to the Ubuntu operating system.

Schubert had never heard of the Ubuntu operating system, an alternative open source system based off Linux.  While most of the world knows about Windows and Mac OS, Ubuntu is a relative newcomer in the operating system market.  Established in 2004 by billionaire Mark Shuttlesworth, it  relies heavily on community support and distribution.  It's free price tag and ability to be customized has made it very popular among computer users seeking an alternative to the dominating paid operating systems of Windows and Mac OS.

Dell computers started carrying computers with the Ubuntu operating system pre-installed on their customers at their online store a few years ago.  The decision was well received with computer fans who make up a large majority of Ubuntu users.  All laptops and desktops continue to be defaulted to the Windows option at the online store and the purchaser needs to select the option of having Ubuntu replace Windows.

She called Dell the very next day and says the representative told her there was still time to change back to Windows. But she says Dell discouraged her. "The*person*I was talking to said Ubuntu was great, college students loved it, it was compatible with everything I needed," said Schubert.

So she stuck with it.

Later, she discovered Ubuntu might look like Windows, but it doesn't always act like it.
Her Verizon High-Speed Internet CD won't load, so she can't access the internet.* She also can't install Microsoft Word, which she says is a requirement for MATC's online classes.
Ubuntu would indeed not run the Verizon disk supplied to configure her internet, a disc that was designed to run on Windows only.  Ubuntu does connect to the internet using Verizon, it does not require the disc supplied by the internet provider.  Verizon who provides internet and cable services for the local area does not have a Ubuntu specialist available to answer questions or help Ubuntu users.  Ubuntu will automatically hook up to the internet once an active internet cable is plugged in or wifi service has been detected.

Ubuntu users usually get the help and support they need for technical issues online at many different community forums and website.  The official forums are [www.ubuntuforums.org](www.ubuntuforums.org).  Ubuntu forums is sponsored by Cannoical Ltd. (Shuttleworth's company that produces Ubuntu) and currently has over 63,000 registered users and over 5,000 people can be found online at most times.

MATC does require that students use Microsoft Word (a part of their flagship productivity suite Microsoft Office) as a program to turn in their essays and assignments.  Microsoft Word is the dominate document processor the world over and has been the standard choice of students and business alike.  Students can actually receive a steep discount at most campus stores with their student id's.  What MATC should have been a bit more specific in that they require the students to turn in their assignments using the “.doc” standard.  This would have allowed Schubert to use Open Office, an alternative office suite to Office.  Open Office (a open source project sponsored by Sun Microsystems) come pre installed on every Ubuntu computer and is free of charge.  Open Office can open, edit, create, and save documents in Microsoft Word's “.doc” format and the new version of Open Office can also use the newest Microsoft Standard of “.docx”.

This was not readily apparent to Schubert  and as a result, with no internet and no Microsoft Word, she dropped out of MATC's fall and spring semesters.

She also says Dell claimed it was now too late to get Windows and any changes she made herself would void her warranty.

"I'm extremely frustrated," said Schubert. "I wanted to get back to school, but I needed a computer to be able to do that."

27 News contacted Dell, but the company has not responded to us yet.

In recent developments however, Verizon says it will dispatch a technician to try to assist her accessing the internet without using the Windows-only installation disk.
MATC also says it promises to accept any of Schubert's papers or class documents using whatever software she has installed.
