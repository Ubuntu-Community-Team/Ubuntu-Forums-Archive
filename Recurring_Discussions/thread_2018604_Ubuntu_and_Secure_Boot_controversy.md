### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2012-07-06
Seems to me that Microsoft's secure boot license created a no-win situation for the FOSS distros.  It's a choice between either sticking to your ideological guns and being locked out of a bunch of upcoming hardware, or finding some compromise that's going to enable your software to run but irritate a lot of people.

I've seen a lot of criticism of Ubuntu and Fedora over their respective approaches, but nobody saying "Here's how we can stay free and still make Linux installable on these locked-down devices".

### Post by Dr. C on 2012-07-07
My comment on this is that I strongly support what Canonical and Fedora are doing here and I also strongly support the FSF for criticizing Canonical and Fedora over this. To understand what is really going one here I would suggest taking a good look at the work of the [Software Freedom Law Center]("http://www.softwarefreedom.org/news/") on the subject of secure and restricted boot. One will soon find out that the real issue here is DRM not "security" as is clear by the submission of the BSA, MPAA, RIAA etc [http://www.copyright.gov/1201/2012/comments/Steven_J._Metalitz.pdf]("http://www.copyright.gov/1201/2012/comments/Steven_J._Metalitz.pdf") and the response by the SFLC that identifes the very serious anti-competition and anti-trust issues here. [https://softwarefreedom.org/resources/2012/SFLC-reply.pdf]("https://softwarefreedom.org/resources/2012/SFLC-reply.pdf")

This is a very high stakes game where billions of dollars are in play. For example if Microsoft dares to interfere with Canonical's approach there is a good chance that the European Commision which has allready tasted Microsoft's blood to the tune of over $1 Billion may wish to go for seconds.
