### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2015-10-11
Well Tadaen,  the default Gnome and Ubuntu desktops come with the capability to use all of gtk3 tools for screen effects, navigation, window customizations, etc.   Via compiz and other setup/tweak tools, you can enable many things including 3d acceleration and transparancy effects etc.

I personally love vanilla "Unity" - - to me, it offers the right "blend" of looks and effects without overwhelming the user.    There are enough customization methods to do what I want.   My desktops generally focus on a great background pic, and if I didn't like the transparent Launcher, I can hide it, make it smaller, etc.   I especially like the way the Launcher search works, and the way open AND focused apps are marked . . . in comparison to KDE, Xfce, Openbox, Windows 7,8,10.   No obtrusive rectangles representing open apps that crowd the panels, just left and right caret markers indicating open and/or active apps (best solution for that I've ever seen on a desktop).

I also like KDE in general and Plasma 5 is looking great.    But I've gotten to the point where I really don't want to mess around with the desktop, doing customizations, etc.   I just prefer the simple but "elegant" way Unity works for me.

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-10-11
It's more the requirements of the window manager and compositor on each, especially reliance on hardware accelerated 3D capabilities, than it is a bit of splash and wobble here and there.

Here, I find Unity and Gnome are fast and smooth on Intel Haswell video, and likewise on Nvidia with a proprietary driver. I don't find that with KDE, where I need to disable desktop effects to get equivalent performance on Intel.  With Nvidia on KDE, I see screen tearing I do not see on Intel. (KDE's display and compositor settings tool, is, IMHO, obtuse. Options are offered without explanations of why one might be chosen rather than the others. Choice of at least one setting can create a broken, black, screen that persists after a reboot.)

Appearance, colors, etc., is an aesthetic issue.  Adding different themes and icons can make a big difference, depending on what we like.

### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-10-11
You said it is not going to be a flame thread but then you proceed to do exactly that.

For a number of years the developers of certain other operating systems have been taking advantage of developments in computer graphic hardware. And from time to time some people people complain: Why is Linux not like that?

I see nothing wrong with Linux developers taking advantage of the graphic capabilities of the hardware. Some desktop environments are designed to give the user the ability for endless modification of the user interface. I do not see that as the design aim of the Ubuntu developers. I like the default simplicity of Unity. I prefer it that way.

I also like the fact that space is left for community developers to add utilities to modify the desktop and to produce alternative desktops that use a different compositor. Which brings me to another reason why Ubuntu does not seem to offer much that needs "high graphics capability." There are plans to replace the present compositor/window manager. And that fact is changing everything related to Ubuntu.
