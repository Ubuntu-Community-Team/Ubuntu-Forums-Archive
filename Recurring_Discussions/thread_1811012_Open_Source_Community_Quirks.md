A lot of people last used Windows from Vista or earlier, where nothing worked out-of-the-box. Even today Windows' out-of-the-box support is a bit sucky; after waiting 10 minutes for Windows to "look on Windows Update" for a driver for my new printer, it ended off being quicker to insert the CD and manually install the driver. Whereas Ubuntu did already have the driver and the detection process took less than 10 seconds. It's not lying, my friend - it's experience. Maybe outdated experience in some cases, but definitely not all.

Suspend and hibernate are problems on Windows, too. My girlfriend lost an essay because Windows automatically hibernated and wouldn't wake up - not entirely Windows' fault because she hadn't "saved" her work, naive girl. Unfortunately, it's the whole BIOS system that's broken. Different motherboards and different BIOSes do power management differently and practically every motherboard needs a different driver to be able to do power management in such a way that Windows won't puke at it. Once the manufacturer has it working in Windows they consider it "good enough". Often they don't share specifications with the open-source community because there's NO specification, and their code is a mess so for pride they don't want to share it with us :-)

The Linux kernel has a thousand different hacks to get power management working across different motherboards that do it badly. It's a wonder we can get it to work at all.

> 3.) Why do open source developers let their philosophies get in the way  of their products? Isn't computing a pragmatic science, where we're more  interested in results than in ideologies and philosophies? Take Ubuntu,  for example. You can't play mp3's, avi's, ect. out of the box. I  thought this was because of some legal issues (and it is in some  countries), but then I find Linux Mint, which is essentially just a more  complete version of Ubuntu.

For many Linux users, not shipping binary blobs is a feature. They don't want "black boxes" on their system.

There's also legal problems due to software patents (and software that might possibly be illegal). Linux Mint is not a company that can be sued. Canonical is, and could be sued for patent violations for H.264's library for instance, if they shipped it.

Ubuntu needs to do something that satisfies open-source advocates AND people who want it to "just work". So the restricted software is easily installable just by clicking a checkbox in the installer. The really "hot" stuff that could get Canonical in serious trouble is split up into a different, tiny entity called Medibuntu that's also easy to add to your system.

It's a pretty good solution for an imperfect world.

If you're wondering why the "it should be all open-source" philosophy exists, it's because computer software is made by human beings. Humans have politics and philosophies and that's just something you have to deal with!

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-07-24
Lets see if I can add anything that hasn't been mentioned already.

In terms of Hardware support, windows is better, but not because of MS. MS is the only OS company that makes no drivers (other than very generic ones). MS relies entirely on manufacturers and that is why you get issues like crashing Vista or old hardware not working on newer versions of windows. Manufacturers have incentive to sell hardware, once it is sold, there is little in it for them to keep supporting newer versions of windows. FOSS people support hardware for their own needs and have the incentive to keep newer version backwards compatible.

Try installing a generic version of Windows on your laptop with very specific hardware. All pre-installed windows versions come tweaked for the hardware. Then you will know actually how good windows hardware support is. When you hear FOSS people complaining, remember that many of us build our own machines.

An OS is much more than drivers. To get a fair comparison of performance, compare a System76 computer with pre-installed and tweaked Ubuntu vs any pre-installed Windows. There is a reason why the largest supercomputers in the world are almost exclusively Linux (Windows doesn't even make a dent in that market). In terms of Stability, Security and Speed, Windows cannot compete.

Only old ATI video cards have issues. Newer video cards work great.

Out of the box: once you install windows, you have to spend hours installing drivers and AV and whatever else. If you buy the proper hardware for Linux, everything really just works. I always give the example of my sister's laptop that gets everything from wireless to visual effects straight from the LiveCD.

FOSS philosophy does lead to better software, even commercial software (i.e. software that you sell and make money). The only time you get issues is when FOSS collides with the greed of the companies that are trying to make unreasonable profit. There is no excuse for a hardware company to not release the specifications needed to make proper drivers, they already got their money by selling the hardware, if they don't provide specifications they are just abusing the users.
