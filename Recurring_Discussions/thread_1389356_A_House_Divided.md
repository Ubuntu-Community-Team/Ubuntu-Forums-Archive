### Post by Mornedhel on 2010-01-25
The distro fragmentation is *inevitable*. The whole point of the FOSS movement, before other zealots misunderstood it as "let's take Micro$h*t down", was and still is "you can fork it if you need to". More than being about choice, it's about not being locked in someone else's.

Yes, I realize this is much easier for a developer who can actually fork something, and non-programmer users will have to wait, or learn to code, or be really really vocal in dev mailing lists. Such it has always been, such it will always be. Users are not entitled to *anything*. If they were, they'd be paying for it.

When applied to distributions, the fork option results in people starting a distribution that meets their needs better than other distributions. If that distribution is good enough for other people, they'll use it. If not, once the original cast loses interest, it'll disappear. If it's all-around pretty good, it might go on as a mainstream distribution, else it'll remain a niche distribution.

Different people have different needs. There are many different people. Ergo there are many distributions.

Unify all the distributions into one, satisfy 80% of Linux users, and watch as the remaining 20% fork their own distributions, attract more Linux users, and the process starts anew.

This also works for software, libraries, etc.

About the Linux community not being welcoming to newcomers: that is simply not true. The Linux community, having its roots in the hacker community, is welcoming to any newcomer *provided he/she/it shows a willingness to learn and think by himself*. I refer you to an essay by Eric Steven Raymond ("esr"), [How To Ask Questions the Smart Way](http://linuxmafia.com/faq/Essays/smart-questions.html), which explains how to get a polite reaction from hackers.
