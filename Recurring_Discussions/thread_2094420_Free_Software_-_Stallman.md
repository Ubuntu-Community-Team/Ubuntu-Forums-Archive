### Post by Mikeb85 on 2012-12-13
Free software is a good thing (free as in freedom).  I do believe programmers should be able to earn money from their craft, but having the source code available, and 'owning' the right to use and modify the software as you see fit is a great ideal.  

Richard Stallman's main argument, and this goes into ideas of progress and the evolution of computing in general, is that, in a free software world, knowledge is accumulated and not lost, innovation/progression happens much quicker, but he was always a little too anti-capitalistic to figure out a practical way of making free software practical.   

Thanks to the more pragmatic Linus Torvalds, and folks at companies like Red Hat, SUSE/Novell, IBM, etc..., Richard Stallman's ideals have been worked over into a slightly more practical form, people can profit off free software, and there's never been greater incentive to use and add to free software.  

And for the record, I'm all for free as in freedom, I'm more than willing to pay for my beer (and software).

### Post by MyTinFoilHat on 2012-12-16
At its core, Stallman's philosophy has always been about putting the user first - above all else. I can't/won't address all the issues stated here, although I can appreciate how strange/different/outlandish his philosophies might be to some - especially those who live a digital life to the tune of Facebook, Google, Twitter, Pintrest, etc. 

Stallman's ideas might seem downright alien to some who don't remember a time when you weren't threatened with legal action just for letting a friend borrow a book or making for them a mixed tape. These ideas of not sharing one's entire life online might easily be perceived as fire-fearing, neanderthal notions supported by ludites, but those of us who might be branded as such can easily see a fundamental wrongdoing to the Rights of the human being beyond the keyboard - those same individuals who aren't asked permission before any number of companies, businesses, developers, etc make a grab for data from the user's cookie jar.

Stallman's philosophy has always been centered around the user's ability to investigate and control his environment as well as the ability to govern the accessibility of his data. Stallman might sound like just another long-haired tin foil wearing wingnut to some, but his ideas are sound. No one said that all intelligent people have awesome social skills. Regardless, I believe he sees the problems with the digital age - those ideas, schemata that are conjured in marketing and advertising offices - by people who haven't a clue, whose sole purpose is to grab data and sell it in order to turn a profit. 

Stallman's philosophy is starkly contrasted with a world in which the status quo has become "Share", "Like", "Re-tweet", or "Opt-out if you don't want to be involved". In the case of the latter, the point is not that the user has the control to do so, but rather on the problem of assumed of user consent, that the user must take action.

To better illustrate this, let's look at a hypothetical statement together.

Everyone reading and responding to this thread likes asparagus, tuna fish and peanut butter sandwiches (yes, mixed together). Your continued use of this particular thread means that you agree to these statements and are hereby legally bound to make and eat the aforementioned sandwiches when accessing this thread. You surrender all rights to claim the contrary if you do not "Opt-Out" of these statements by doing the following: submitting a video recording of yourself standing on your left foot and jump up and down 4 times shouting "Hum-a-lum-a-ding-dong, ding-dong-merry-hummer-humming-mum" and sending to blahblahblah [at] blah.com

Of course, this example is extreme and by (probably? - hopefully!) everyone's standards, ridiculous - yet here we have an entire industry centered around such ridiculous thinking: one that assumes user consent to participation and forces the user to choose whether or not to continue use of the service, software, etc and dance like a little puppet, or "go somewhere else".

What's really problematic about balancing these seeming extremes (ROI versus User Right) is this: money. How does one respect the user rights, gain their willing consent without losing buckets of money? Offer support services, at a cost.

This has been Canonical's model for monetizing their team's efforts. Well, that and selling spiffy looking Ubuntu gear. The problem with all that is... YOU. You know what you're doing, you know what to look for that seems out of the ordinary (malicious code, spyware, etc). You have the tools and talents to tweak Ubuntu into oblivion. So, how on earth is Canonical going to sell services to a community that can (for lack of a better term) provide for itself? It can't. So, what's a company headed by a smart, millionaire playboy astronaut to do? Well, they develop a feature in concert with the top online retailer and sell your "anonymous" data.

But you can always opt-out.

Yes, this is true. However, the principle here is this: While it may have been known to most in the community, it was more or less forced down the community's throat. "This is part of the distribution. Get over it. BUT you can opt-out..." and "Oh, btw, get used to this because there's more to come."

Therein lies the root of Stallman's recent comments.

> **llanitedave said:**
> That said, I simply can't agree with his premise that software freedom is a moral issue.  There are plenty of good reasons to make and sell proprietary software that have nothing to do with immoral motivations or behaviors.  
^ This.

When software terms are fully disclosed, the user can make an informed choice for their own benefit however suits them.  If they find that a proprietary apps adds value to their work, and they choose to pay for the object code and have no interest in the source, that is also their freedom.

Stallman's Four Freedoms are important and have changed the world.

But when a developer gives us the fruits of his labor completely free in both senses, that's a gift, not a requirement. We have a word for requiring people to give the fruits of their labor to someone else, and it isn't "freedom".

Indeed, the Four Freedoms are made possible by a higher principle, the one which governs the Berne Convention, in which a majority of nations in the world acknowledge that a work is solely owned by its creator at the moment of its creation, and that the creator has the freedom to determine the terms under which it's shared.

IMHO, the freedoms of creators are no less important than those of users.  

After all,  a user can always ignore any creation whose terms of distribution they don't agree with.  In this sense it's not possible for any disclosed software terms to cause harm. Everyone always has the right to choose something else, or to create their own solution.

I see a Fifth Freedom:  the freedom of every individual to choose any software available under any terms they find valuable to themselves, whether free or proprietary.
