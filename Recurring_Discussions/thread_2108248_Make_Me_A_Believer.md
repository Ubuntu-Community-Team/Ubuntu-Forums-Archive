### Post by audiomick on 2013-01-24
Firstly, you say you keep going back to Windows. If found that I only really made the jump by deciding to always boot Linux first, and only resort to Windows when I had something that had to be done in a hurry and I couldn't manage in Linux. It turned out that there was practically nothing for which I *really had to* boot windows for (regarding day to day stuff), and that after very few weeks of that, maybe two, windows started to feel extraordinarily clumsy.

I might point out that I am forced to maintain a Windows install. I use a number of programs relating to Digital Audio devices, and two Acoustic Analyser programs, that only run on Windows (no Mac either...). These days, I only boot windows at all for them, and practically never go into the internet with Windows (even to the extent of rebooting into Ubuntu when I am out on a job and need to download firmware or what have you). I recently had cause to boot the Win7 on my netbook, and discovered that the last updates I had done there where in about July 2010, quite possibly the last time it had been in Win7.

So, on to the reasons for Linux.

One main one is the contrast between, on the one hand, a Company selling a product, taking money for it, and claiming it is practically infallible  (which it certainly isn't) and definitely the best choice (which is entirely debatable), and, on the other hand, a group of people offering a product and saying "Here, we think this is pretty good. It works for most people most of the time, but there may be a problem. If there is, tell us, and you can get help here...". I'll leave it up to you to figure out who is who in that comparison...

The other thing is the "help you to help yourself" basis of Linux documentation in general. My feeling is that Microsoft is interested in taking the maintainance of my computer out of my hands an into the hands of one of their trained professionals. Finding out information about how the thing really works (and not just "do this and do this and it just works") is, in my experience, extremely difficult. 

As an example: My applications at work involve a fair bit of "out of the box" network stuff on digital audio networks. Windows assumes you are wanting to set up a small home network or an office network, none of which is applicable. I have got it figured out, for the large part, but I always have the feeling that I am trying to do something with my computer that the producer of my operating system thinks I shouldn't be able to do.

The contrast with Linux is that the information is *all* freely available. A lot of it is extremely complicated, and I might not understand it. I might even choose to take the machine to someone and pay him to solve the problem, but no-one is pushing me in that direction. If I have the time and inclination, I *can definitely* research it myself and look after it myself.

I know that Linux is not perfect, and likely never will be, but I have got to the point where I almost feel sorry for my laptop when I have to boot it into Windows. ;)

### Post by NilPointer on 2013-01-25
What's good about Linux is that it's free (as in freedom). It doesn't force you to do anything. If you don't want to use it - it's your choice. But if you want to use it - you're always welcome. What I like about Linux:

1. When I used Windows, I reinstalled it at least once a month (up to three times a month, actually). Either I got virus, or it just became too slow. It was really painful experience, since I had to reinstall all software, and full reinstall took almost entire day. But I use Ubuntu 10.04 for over 2.5 years now and I never had a need to reinstall it. So, Linux saved at least 1 month of my time.

2. I used to catch BSODs on Windows once a month. When doing something important - BAM! Sorry, but your system decided to reboot because it was done by third-rate programmers. But I run Linux on desktop (for over 2.5 years) and on laptop (about half of year) and it never crashed. Also, on Windows I had BSODs when inserting flash drives with bent connector, and deadlocks when connecting a corrupted hard drive. And Linux handled these hardware problems properly, I even was able to recover data.

3. On Windows, antivirus software lately became more like "requirement". Windows has lots of security vulnerabilities. Some of them get "hotfixes" after few months since discovery. Others are never fixed. And Windows tends to run unnecessary vulnerable network services by default. Which means, that even connecting machine to Internet is risk itself (unless it's behind NAT). Browsing web pages without antivirus is like asking "hack me, please!". On Linux, malware doesn't exists at all (well, it exists only in source form, which you must compile and run as root). Antivirus may be needed only if you run a mail server, to prevent infection to Windows machines. And when vulnerability discovered in open-source Linux software, it usually fixed in hours after discovery, due to open-source nature (compared to months in Windows). Everyone can find vulnerabilities and send a patch to fix it. And desktop Linux distros don't run any unnecessary network daemons by default.

4. Linux has package managers, which make updating and downloading software very easy.

5. Most drivers (excluding proprietary ones) are already included into Linux kernel, which simplifies installation of OS.

6. I had an interesting situation recently. I have accidently improperly removed flash drive with important data. When I plugged it into Windows machines, they were unable to read it. Both Windows XP and 7 gave me "Disk is not formatted. Format now?". My last hope was Linux. And when I came home, I first tried opening that flash drive from my Linux desktop. Surprisingly, it opened it without any problems and I've copied all files. I learned one thing from that: using Windows can lead to loss of data, because it can't read slightly corrupted FS and will advise to format it, while it's in fact quite recoverable. But most ironic thing, is that this flash drive was formatted into FAT FS, which was designed by Microsoft. And Microsoft's driver, written by programmers, who supposed to know FAT specification better than anyone else failed to read FS, but Linux implementation of FAT had no problem reading it at all. It's clearly visible, which driver is work of amateurs and which is work of professionals.

7. It's great OS for programming. Many compilers/interpreters are already bundled into distros, and others can be easily downloaded via package manager. It's much more flexible than Windows. I was able to do lots of things, that I couldn't on Windows.

8. Finally, Linux is free (from cost side). I would never pay any money to Microsoft for such inconvenient, insecure system, which can easily lose your data, crash at it's own will, and needs frequent reinstalling. However, I don't mind donating few dollars to some free projects at all.

### Post by Paddy Landau on 2013-01-28
> **xArcticGx said:**
> My school pre-installed a lot of useful programs like emacs, GIMP, a few accessibility tools, Maple 16, WINE, etc.
Regarding WINE, it is important to understand that it is an imperfect solution (although better than trying to run Linux programs under Windows!). A few programs work perfectly; some work OK; and most don't work. Refer to the [WINE application database]("http://appdb.winehq.org/") to see what has been tested.

I strongly recommend that you use [PlayOnLinux]("http://www.playonlinux.com/"). PlayOnLinux is a front-end to WINE, making it very much easier to install, configure, run, and uninstall Windows programs. To install, I recommend that you go to the [download page]("http://www.playonlinux.com/en/download.html") and follow the instructions; but you can, if you prefer, install from the standard Ubuntu repositories (if you decide to use Ubuntu).
