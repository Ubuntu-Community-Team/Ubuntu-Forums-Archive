### Post by doas777 on 2011-05-28
> **aysiu said:**
> How would someone who has "antivirus" know? "Antivirus" applications try to identify malware based on outdated lists and imperfect heuristics (basically, guesses).
true, but you have to admit, the assertion that any one could seriously use w9X (even in the most common sense fashion) for years with out ever contracting a single piece of untrustworthy code, is pretty ludicrous. especially in the days prior to the California anti-spyware act, when thousands of legitimate companies were legally developing spyware and making backend packaging deals to get it widely distributed. statistically, the idea is absurd. talk about a long tail....

I firmly believe that common sense is the best security you can have, but I also firmly believe that no one can ever have perfect security. "secure" is an ideal endpoint on an infinite spectrum.

I understand what you are saying (that AV is imperfect), but there is a converse to your assertion: even though you can never use tools to know that you are safe, does not mean that by not using them, you are any safer. it means that you have blindly ignored one set of partial information on the state of your system. just because the data is incomplete does not mean that it has no value. the consequent extension to your assertion is what is important: that you must accept that everything is potentially unsafe and to take cautious action accordingly. the point is not to malign AV systems, but to remind users that "no infections found" does not mean you are safe.

be careful with terminology. a virus by definition spreads without user action, as does a worm. Only Trojans require user action. 

viruses use vulnerabilities in network aware applications to spread, whereas worms use system vulnerabilities in network services  (or make use of portable media) to spread.
over the last 10 years the major vendors have done a much better job of coding secure IP stacks and services, and are now patching security flaws automatically in very short periods of time, so IP based attacks and wormable/viral vulnerabilities no longer stay vulnerable to make these kinds of malware worthwhile. since 2005 or so, almost everything has been trojan based. hack a server, plant malware, and trick clients into going to the server (drive-by-download). bad P2P downloads, application cracks, etc (traditional trojan tactics). 
this is true of all platforms, including windows.
