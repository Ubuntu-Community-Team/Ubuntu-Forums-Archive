### Post by cluepon on 2007-12-13
It is amazing. Just simply amazing how many **ignorant** statements that have been made of both sides. 

BSD has it's uses. Linux has its uses. Disclaimer: I use a Linux Desktop, but I use FreeBSD for server work. I tend to use the right tool for the job, without spiraling into silly religious debates over which one kills the other. 

A good primer for this whole (stupid) debate can be found here: 

[http://www.over-yonder.net/~fullermd/rants/bsd4linux/bsd4linux1.php](http://www.over-yonder.net/~fullermd/rants/bsd4linux/bsd4linux1.php)

To wit, from the article: 
> 
BSD is what you get when a bunch of Unix hackers sit down to try to port a Unix system to the PC. Linux is what you get when a bunch of PC hackers sit down and try to write a Unix system for the PC.

But, let's address a few of these really ignorant statements: 

1 ) Better hardware support. - Anyone who has ever used BSD, knows that it's capacity for hardware support is second to none. In fact, it's been my experience that in cases of really strange hardware configuration, FreeBSD boots it. Nearly each and every time. I've seen older than god SCSI cards recognized by FreeBSD..working out of the box. 

2 ) The name sounds better? The fact that this was even put down as a reason should tell anyone with any technical acument that this entire thread is silly. 

3 ) Better support - About evenly matched, to be honest. BSD users, and folks on the mailing lists...generally and broadly speaking, tend to be a more "techie" bunch. They tend not to screw around when it comes to issues, and are quite helpful, and rarely put down or put off questions. 

4 ) Community support - there are no numbers, but the bottom line is, there is PLENTY of community around BSD. In fact, I would suggest, being a bit below the radar, the community *quality* around BSD is superior. For instance, you dont see alot of "BSD vs. Linux...DEFEND YOUR OS!!!" threads or ML posts. 

5 ) Linux is popular and growing every day: so is BSD. The development of BSD is a bit more cathedral like. In FreeBSD for instance, when you install a STABLE or RELEASE branch of the OS, it has met real and defined QA targets. There is a good standard in place as to what STABLE is. You have alot of options when it comes to FreeBSD, according to how bleeding edge you want to be. For those who love adventure...there is CURRENT. =)

6 ) Competition: if you knew anything, you would know there are PLENTY of different flavors of BSD. FreeBSD. NetBSD. Dragonfly...each with their own emphasis. 

7 ) Kernel stability. This is another dangerous religious argument. Stability means different things to different people. But, I would like to ask...what you base YOUR opinion on. When it comes to LAMP operations, a FreeBSD system built from the ground up, with a tuned kernel is infinitely more stable, and uses resources far more efficiently than any linux system. But, again, this is based on MY experience. I am sure there are Linux servers out there that could give a FreeBSD system a run for its money. When it comes to stability, in my opinion, there is NOTHING better than a system built from source, and not from binaries. 

8 ) BSD just copies Linux: another ignorant statement. BSD and its predecessors have been around far longer than linux. Anyone who had done a modicum of research would know this. Note: I am not even saying it's the other way around either. Linux and BSD are very different entities in their own right. They each have a different methodology to doing things. There are ideas exchanged, but I think its a bit disingenuous either one copies the other.  

9) Other arguments based on logical fallacies: the OP, and a few of the "fanboy" types of responders need to do some actual research, both hands on and with google. And saying the name is "cool" isint a reason. 

10 ) Documentation. Linux documentation. There is tons of it. But it's so spread out sometimes as to be useless. As far as actual OS Level documentation, I have found that the FreeBSD handbook ( [http://www.freebsd.org/doc/en_US.ISO8859-1/books/handbook/](http://www.freebsd.org/doc/en_US.ISO8859-1/books/handbook/) ) is a good standard to judge by, when it comes to OS level documentation. Take a look. =)

As Harlan Ellison said, you don't have a right to an opinion, you have a right to an *informed* opinion. FreeBSD has its uses. Linux has its uses. Linux blows the doors off BSD when it comes to embedded applications. BSD blows the doors off of Linux when it comes to security (see open and net BSD). 

But alot of these opinions are subjective. But in order to have an informed opinion on all of this, you need to actually have experience and know what your talking about. From some of the "Linux RULES MAN!" replies here, it's evident there is a lack of informed opinion. 

Just my $0.02. Read BSD for Linux users. You may learn something, and might even gain some (grudging) respect for BSD.

(Side note: I don't lump darwin/os x into any argument when it comes to BSD/Linux. While Darwin is BSD based, it's a very strange flavor, and in my experience...not very useful without the Apple stuff. It's contributions to BSD in general, though, shouldnt be dismissed.)

> **Dimitriid said:**
> So at the kernell level how is BSD more secure?  Burden of proof on your side gentlemen, leave name-calling aside.

First, let's get something clarified: Linux, by itself, at the core, is a kernel. That's not the way it works in the BSD camp. In the BSD camp, the base system is designed and developed in concert. It's all about the integration. It's all about the "base system" with BSD. In Linux, you have the kernel, which Linus works on, and you have the distro, which is (typically) a set of binary packages. These two are combined to create the OS. Now, Debian has been working to have more concert between the kernel and the distro. That's a good thing. But the bottom line is, you have to take into account how the two are designed and built, because they have a very real impact on security. Linux is about the kernel, BSD is about the base-system. 

So, let's talk security and BSD:

OpenBSD has a security record that Linux (or, for that matter, ANY OS, *NIX, Windows or otherwise) wishes it had. Secure by default is the motto with those folks, and they have strong crypto integrated into the core system. 

With stack-smashing protection, privilege separation, heavy use of randomization when it comes to how passwords are used, and their method of password checking...a default install of openBSD is about as secure as you can get. And then of course, their use of sandboxing means you can get a system that is highly structured and locked down. They are also doing interesting things in terms of how X is used and secured. 

Their current stat: "Only two remote holes in the default install, in more than 10 years!"

And that simply speaks for itself. But, does that mean Linux is an insecure OS? Hardly. It all comes down to the practices and standards of the distro. It also comes down to the purpose of the OS. Linux does do security, and while I don't know of any linux distro on the same level as OpenBSD,  I would never consider Linux a "fat" target, per se. Its attack surface is pretty low. Certainly lower than the Windows camp.

OpenBSD can, and does, put a lot of thought into the stability and security of their kernel. Not just bug fixing, but good, clean design built with consistent coding practices. Your timeline comparison is misleading. Berkeley Software Distribution was indeed started in the '70s; however, it was not a complete OS implementation. It was "merely" addons and substitutions to commercially offered Unices which still depended upon the customer having purchased Unix. It was not until 1992 that a complete BSD OS was written, and (unfortunately) owing to legal entanglements this was not really made available until 1996  (OpenBSD was founded in 1995).

Examining GNU/Linux using your logic for when open source development begins, I would claim that GNU Emacs started development in the '70s as well. No, it wasn't GNU at the time (GNU did not exist); but it was authored by the same person who later created GNU, and offered under a very GPL-like license. The GNU project did not get announced until 1983, and it also was dependent upon commercially-licensed software in order to provide a complete OS. 

Linux (the kernel) entered the picture about 1991 at about the time BSD was technically "completed", but unavailable (owing to legal disputes). It might be argued that a complete GNU+Linux operating system was available in 1992 with the creation of the Softlanding Linux Systems distribution; or one might wish to wait for the first FSF-endorsed GNU/Linux distribution, Debian, in 1994 (or even wait for 1996 when version 1.0 was released).

All other arguments aside, it would be misguided to consider BSD *as a complete OS* to have preceded GNU/Linux as a complete OS (and certainly not by 'a decade or two'). It is also inappropriate to assert that BSD *as a set of add-on components* to a complete OS preceded GNU as a set of add-on components. Their developmental timelines were fairly parallel.

GNU did adopt many of the BSD tools early on, but I would also mention a quote by Keith Bostic (an early BSD developer),

***"I think it's highly unlikely that we ever would have gone as strongly as we did without the GNU influence. It was clearly something where they were pushing hard and we liked the idea."***

I do not wish to disqualify them, merely to question your reasoning in disqualifying earlier releases of GNU software because they likewise weren't a "complete operating system"; or that the software wasn't labeled "GNU" when originally released. 

> **Harpalus said:**
> My main intention was to assert that it was BSD, those patch sets, not GNU or Linux, that was a major force behind open source, but I should have been phrasing it better. Not all the BSD developers particularly cared about GNU, but it did have influence. Besides, the original assertion was that Linux is "the only reason BSD exists", which is nonsense, and even if we factor in GNU, they're not Linux. ;) At any rate, this is a separate debate entirely.

When did BSD adopt the goal of producing a stand-alone operating system? I find no record of such interest until Keith Bostic started a project (NET/2) to replace the Unix utilities in 1989 (first released in 1991). Before that time, BSD contented itself with providing their add-ons to licensed Unixes -- mind, those add-ons **were extremely significant** -- nonetheless, this goal of producing a *complete* BSD OS was ideed inspired by Richard Stallman's GNU Project. And while I would not say BSD (as a complete OS) wouldn't exist were it not for GNU, the influence and motivation provided by GNU were indeed substantial.
