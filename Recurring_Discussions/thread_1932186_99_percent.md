### Post by JDShu on 2012-02-27
I've come to this conclusion on this issue over the past several months.

*Does it really matter what percentage of all desktop users use Linux?*

I don't mean this in the usual sense that UF people mean it. That is "oh let people use what works for them". Because frankly, as anybody who has studied UI at any elementary level know, people don't know what they want. But I digress.

I used to think that it mattered, because the higher the market share, the more companies would develop for Linux, and therefore it would be better for Linux users in general. What I didn't realize at the time, was that the open source community is actually pretty effective. In the time that I *really* got into Linux, which is around 3 years, I have seen much improved hardware support, more games, and generally easier to use and prettier distros. In particular, I've noticed that open source projects don't need more users. They need more developers. Or at least, more users who are willing to spend time writing good bug reports and communicating with the developers. These things related to getting more Linux users, but not so much to "Linux Market Share". That is, every new Linux user may eventually become a contributing member of open source development, and improve the ecosystem, but we don't need to have Microsoft or even Apple level market share for this to happen.

### Post by MisterGaribaldi on 2012-02-27
I agree with a lot of what has been said here. However, there's a few things to be added, historically, as well as what's going on now.

The whole "99% vs. 1%" or "95% vs. 5%" argument had nothing to do with Linux. In that worldview, all that was being talked about was Microsoft and Apple. Not Linux, and certainly not Google because they weren't even *in* the OS business at that point.

Apple has made a LOT of in-roads in terms of expanding the non-Microsoft marketshare, thanks initially to such things as the iMac and, to a much larger extent, the iPod. The iPod really put Apple back on the map in terms of relevance in the public mind, and actually catapulted them to a position of stature and relevance the likes of which Apple had *never* known before.

Because of mobile-geared OSs, in particular Blackberry, iOS, and now Android, the whole "99%" thing isn't even a relevant discussion any longer, simply because the next generation platform (i.e. non-desktops) is one that Microsoft has not held the lion's share of. In fact, arguably, they're not even a minority player, given that at this point most people prefer iOS and Android to everything else, and even Blackberry still beats out Microsoft. What's that say for Microsoft's OS platform?

At this point, I think the only way Linux will expand its dominance is through mobile devices, such as smart phones and tablets. This may possibly have a backward-effecting halo effect on the desktop space, but unless the Linux community really reaches out to commercial software developers, "Linux on the desktop" will never really gain much traction. Of course, and I think it's pretty much common knowledge, Linux on the server is huge, with Microsoft only owning a "significant" amount of share, but certainly not like what they do on the desktop.
