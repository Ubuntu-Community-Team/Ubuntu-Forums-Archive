### Post by irrdev on 2011-03-19
Technically speaking, Linux is a kernel. There's a big divide between what we've come to think of Linux, however, and the kernel Linux. Ubuntu, Fedora, openSUSe; nobody will argue that any distro is Linux, with or without a DE. However, when arguing the popularity of Linux, the rise of mobile devices has truly changed the entire market. For example, webOS runs a pure Linux kernel. By 2012, EVERY single HP computer will be running webOS alongside Windows. Technically, this is Linux. Technically, Ubuntu will have to take a backseat to a "distro" that doesn't incorporate package management or any other common "Linux" feature most of us have come to expect. Technically, Linux will be more popular than Mac OS X. 

And then there's Android. Google uses a customized Linux kernel. Originally, Google tried to submit the changes upstream, but then opted out. Is this Linux? Because, if it is, then we're a minority community among millions of Linux users who don't even know that they're using Linux.

My point here is that Linux has evolved. Linux HAS gained mainstream acceptance, just not in the "purified" FOSS form that many original Linux users had hoped for. That's doesn't change the fact that it is still Linux.

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-03-19
Linux is just the kernel. The only use of the kernel is to let you run programs on top of it. Even if you use just a terminal and no graphics, you get bash-shell which is part of the Gnu project and has nothing to do with Linux itself.

The word Linux is used for many distribution that have the Linux kernel at their core. People argue that the proper word should be GNU/Linux, since a lot of the stuff that sits on top of the kernel is software from the GNU project. You can also have GNU/FreeBSD, which bash Gnome and so on with just the BSD kernel as opposed to Linux.

However, most distributions ship software that is no associated with the GNU project itself. If you want to get technical, GNU/Linux/Mozilla and GNU/Linux/Google are even more appropriate names since most of us spend most of our computer time on-line.

Due to the popularity of Linux, the name is also used as synonymous to Free Open Source Software.

### Post by youbuntu on 2011-03-19
Linux = kernel; the software which interacts and translates between hardware and OS

GNU = "GNU's Not Unix" - everything else (applications, OS etc) which sits _around_ the kernel, and forms the Operating System from its' many, many pieces. There are also BSD portions, using the BSD licence, and others, but probably mainly GNU components.

This is why we like to refer to the Operating System as "GNU/Linux" or "GNU & Linux", or the kernel alone as "Linux". "Linux" has become the de-facto (incorrect) term which people use to describe a distribution of the GNU/Linux OS, which has the "Linux" kernel at the core.

People will say I am being fussy by calling it "GNU/Linux", but it actually makes perfect sense. For example, when do you hear of a distro being called, or mentioning "GNU" in their title? But they almost *always* call it Linux. So, why is the "GNU" portion (a massive portion of what comprises the distros, btw) always missed out of the title?

That is not accurate, or fair. Linux is NOT an OS, it is the kernel alone.

### Post by abyrne on 2011-03-20
Most people just leave out the GNU part of GNU/Linux simply because it's easier to say. Ubuntu has a kernel. Fedora has kernel. Mac OSX has a kernel. Hell, even Windows has a kernel. But we don't say Aqua/Apple Kernel or iOS/Apple Kernel (yes, they both run on pretty similar kernels).

At this point, we understand that the only 'Linux' part of any distro is the kernel, and the rest is GNU/Gnome/Sun/Canonical/Whoever else knows how to use C++. We just choose not to say that part because we're, in lack of a better word, lazy. 

So yes, by common usage, Linux could mean the kernel or a distro using that kernel. 

Linus Torvalds didn't originally think his software would be running the innards of a rapidly grossing phone OS. There's a lot of things he didn't expect Linux to become. He wasn't even going to originally call it Linux. The only reason it's called that is because his buddy who owned the FTP server that originally hosted it named the parent directory "linux".

And just because my HTC Incredible is running closed-source software from Google and Verizon, doesn't mean it isn't still running Linux at heart.
