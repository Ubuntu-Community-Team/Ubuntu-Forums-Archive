### Post by TBABill on 2010-10-19
I think a lot of the doomsayers are looking at what they perceive as missed opportunities, such as the time during Vista when any Linux distro was probably a better alternative but there was no mass marketing aimed at capturing disgruntled Windows users. Or when netbooks were new and had low power capability where a Linux distro would better shine, but failed to capture the market.
 
The problem with those "perceptions" are people thinking like businesses. Distros are not in business to take over the computing market to gain more users, which for a business would lead to profits. Linux distros are open source and free to use. They survive on marketing on websites, user contributions and other methods of generating income (for the most part...not talking paid versions of desktop distros or RHEL/SUSE paid enterprise versions), not selling the distro to every user for a fee. 
 
Because distros are free they do not gain financially just from new users. Yes, they may gain contributions and click through income generation from ads, but just gaining customers does not gain income. That's where the arguments fall short. To "compete" with Windows is silly because, yes, everyone using Linux would be wonderful, but if the majority stays on Windows forever it in no way lessens the strength and capability of the Linux desktop distro. 
 
The argument is flawed. If Linux devs of all distros truly cared about taking over the desktop you can bet things would not be free and advertising would be heavy wherever affordable. Even if it stays a niche product, the desktop does continue to evolve and improve so for those users who can benefit from a Linux distro and choose to do so, more power to them. There is no competition for the end user....we use what works best.
