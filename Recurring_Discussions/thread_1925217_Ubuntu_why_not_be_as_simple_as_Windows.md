### Post by rg4w on 2012-02-14
In my own experience with the computers I've installed Ubuntu on, pretty much everything has worked out of the box.  In the one case where I needed a proprietary driver, Ubuntu notified me of that and prompted me to install it with just one click.  For software I've been able to find everything I need in the Ubuntu Software Center, again just one click away.   And printing to both printers at home and in my office worked first time out with just the stock system.

That said, I understand that not all computers made will enjoy such compatibility.  I gave a talk on this at a Mac dev conference a while ago, encouraging the app makers there to consider porting to Linux, but with this caveat:  Linux has to work harder than either Mac or Windows, and 100% compatibility with all machines simply won't be possible.

With Macs, one company make both the hardware and the OS, so they have total control over everything in the system.

With Windows, while the OS and the computer come from different companies, most computer vendors design their systems specifically according to Microsoft's specs.  With some 82% of the market, who wouldn't? 

Ubuntu's job is much harder than either Mac or Windows, because in most cases it's going to be installed on a computer designed from the ground up specifically for an OS that isn't Ubuntu.

Given this, it's really quite remarkable that Ubuntu runs at all, and indeed it runs very well on a majority of modern computers made by nearly every major manufacturer (and more than a few minors ones).

The key to this is OEMs, bringing computer vendors on board with the certification and preinstallation programs Canconical offers:
[http://www.canonical.com/partners/oem](http://www.canonical.com/partners/oem)

This has been paying off, slowly but well.  Every few months we see another computer or two from companies like Asus, Dell, and others that has Ubuntu preinstalled, and the list of certified machines is growing at a much faster rate.

Most Ubuntu-preinstalled machines are available only outside the US, such as the model introduced to the Italy market last summer and the three Asus models released for Asian markets in Q3.

In the States we have vendors like System 76 and ZaReason who offer Ubuntu preinstalled, and resellers like Linucity here in Los Angeles who offer only Linux-compatible hardware with your choice of Ubuntu or Mint preinstalled.

I suspect we'll see ever more of these, but in the meantime most Ubuntu installations are onto machines designed for an entirely different OS, and still run remarkably well.

If you've run into snags with yours, the good folks in this forum can probably help get your system running well in no time.

### Post by 1clue on 2012-02-14
There are a lot of topics being discussed here.

First, the Apple/Mac OS comments most recently posted:  Apple has a proprietary chip in their motherboards.  If that chip is not present, then MacOS will not run.  It's a deliberate incompatibility to prevent people from using their software when you didn't buy their hardware.  IMO this is not even sort of related to the other topics.

 Software on any computer comes from various places.  You have your operating system vendor, your hardware manufacturers and your third party app vendors.  When you're talking about commercial vendors with large market share, then chances are you get widespread support and a relatively consistent look and feel.

When you talk about a company like Apple, they control a whole lot more about the hardware people plug in.  They have strict standards controlling how hardware works on their systems, how drivers work and how software works.  They've beat the subject home repeatedly by offering a simple upgrade path and lots of support to companies that play their way, and a trip through the seventh hell for the ones who ignore them.  You can rewrite almost anything including window managers, but you gotta do it their way.

Both Apple and Microsoft have a similar approach in that there are people who do nothing but specify standards to give everything a common feel and a consistent interface.  IMO Apple does a much better job at that sort of thing, but that's just an opinion.

Now look at Linux.  There is no single company controlling anything at all.  There is no standard place to get software.  (Before anyone points at Software Center, what does Debian do?  Gentoo?  Arch? All these organizations reinvent the wheel or steal the wheel from somewhere else)

On top of that, a hardware manufacturer makes a driver based on likely sales.  They see Microsoft as the largest market share, so they make a couple drivers to cover the most common configurations.  Then they might look at Apple, and make a half-assed attempt at a driver for that.  Some very few will make a driver for Linux.  Some of the rest will cooperate with somebody else who wants to make a driver for Linux, but chances are no.  So you get a bunch of companies or individuals who try to figure out a black-box driver that may or may not work, and almost certainly will not be fully functional on features the driver writer was not interested in.

Long story short, hardware on Linux has a HUGE variance in terms of support.  My box has a card in it that was bought new a couple years ago, and Linux STILL reports it as the wrong type of hardware, not just the wrong model number.

Linux drivers are written by entities which want to see that hardware supported.  If nobody with the ability and time -- or the money to hire somebody -- to write it steps up, then it doesn't get done.

Case in point, my Canon all-in-one laser printer would not hook up on Linux for over a year.  I finally got driver support by jumping through a lot of hoops, but it was in no way easy.  My printer is not common for homes, and not big enough for most offices.  As such, almost nobody has an interest in writing drivers for it.

The good news is that there has been a lot of effort in the Linux communities to fix these problems, but there are still problems.  Software Center and its equivalent in other distributions is a fantastic thing, but there is a large set of software in every one of these systems which does not exist in the main repository or any of the other ones.
