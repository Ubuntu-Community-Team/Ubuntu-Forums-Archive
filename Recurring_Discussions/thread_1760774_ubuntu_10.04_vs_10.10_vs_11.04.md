### Post by fabricator4 on 2011-05-23
> **beew said:**
> Forget about this superstition that LTR is better, LTR means older software and no bug fixes.

That's incorrect, LTS (not LTR) is Long Term Support.  That means bug fixes as well as critical updates.

The reason for running LTS is to get a reliable machine with Debian-like stability.  Those that do not like the instability of 6 monthly releases should stay with the LTS release.  This is as valid a reason as any for choosing a particular release or distro an any other.

The [Canonical Definition]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/LTS") definition for the LTS release is that it is enterprise focused, more thoroughly tested, and is NOT based on new features or cutting edge developments for these reasons.

In short, it takes the previous two years of development and makes the most stable system possible.  Not everyone wants to upgrade every six months, or have to work through problems that are inevitable if you follow this release cycle.  In particular, the LTS release is a good one to recommend for newcomers: If it works on their machine there's a good chance it will never get broken by a kernel update or bug fix. New Linux users who just want to use their computer have a enough to get their head around without changing a new set of problems every six months.

Bug fixes made in later releases DO filter back to the LTS.  For example, the 3G card in my Eee PC had a minor but annoying problem under 10.04.  I noticed that when I tried 10.10 a few months after it came out that the problem with the driver module had been fixed (some time around December I think).  By January this bug fix had filtered back to LTS and the 3G now works flawlessly under 10.04.

> 
10.10 has one more year of support and by then the next LTS will come out. I wouldn't want to run 10.04 until 2013.


You don't have to, you can upgrade to the next LTS as soon as it comes out if you wish, 12.04.  I normally recommend people wait for 4 or six weeks for any unexpected problems to get ironed out.  If it were to turn out to not be a successful LTS release (hasn't happened yet and probably never will) then you've always got the option of leaving it another month or two to see if it hardens up.

The concept of the LTS is a brilliant one - it starts off as the most stable release the previous two years of development can give it, and gets better and more stable over time.  The stability rivals the slower release cycle of Debian.  It's definitely the release that I recommend to newcomers with one very big exception: if there's new hardware that is supported better that makes Ubuntu work better on their machine (such as solving wireless network problems) out of the box then that is obviously the release to use in that case.  In most cases though, newcomers are bringing older hardware that works perfectly fine with the last LTS release. 

My feeling is that if newcomers were steered towards the LTS release and discouraged from accepting the online upgrade except for LTS releases, the support problems seen here would be very much less.  I for one would like to see more posts about people wanting to learn to use their systems more, and less posts about people who have broken systems because they upgraded and now can't boot, or that they've installed the latest release and can't get it work well, or at all.

You enjoy the cutting edge, as do I - I keep a few spare partitions around for the purpose, but that's not to say that everyone wants things that way, or that it's the best thing to be recommending to new users.

So, respectfully, I ask you to rethink your attitude to LTS releases, and what is best to recommend to new users who just want a trouble free first experience. ;-)


Chris.
