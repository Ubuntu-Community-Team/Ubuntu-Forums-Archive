### Post by ninjaaron on 2011-07-04
Well, some computers do come with Linux.  Several Asus computers, several Dells, all System 76 machines and others.  Macs also come with something that's pretty much Linux (sure, the kernel is a branch of freeBSD, but most of core components can be found on any GNU/Linux system).  The new Chrome Books are also linux machines.  In addition, most smartphones, eReaders and tablets come with Linux at the core.  In addition, a lot of 'stupid' phones, satellite TV boxes, ATM's, MP3 players and other devices with simple operating systems are based on Linux.

But, as we all know, Linux doesn't come on many PC's and Laptops (if we don't count OSX).  It does seem that companies such Google and Canonical and projects like Meego are changing that slowly.

Now, I do think the Linux desktop, particularly the Ubuntu Desktop is now viable for the masses, but I think that is very recent.  Basically, I think 10.04 LTS is the first commercially viable Linux desktop.  I don't think 11.04 is commercially viable, but I do think the next LTS will be again.  I have a few friends who are totally not tech-savy who use 10.10 and 10.04 with no problems.

So, I think Linux is ready for the average user.  On the other hand, there are problems for specific groups.  Professional artists who work with images, film and audio have no tools to compete with professional grade applications on other systems.  Adobe has talked about releasing Photoshop for Linux, so that is something.

Games are also a problem, though more games are also beginning to be developed, though not nearly as many as most would like to see.

There is also the issue of peripherals. A lot of external hardware works great with Linux.  Some does not, and there is nothing that can be done about it.  Some work partially, but the system cannot load the proprietary software so certain features remain unusable (I speak from experience).  Obviously, that would change if more OEMs pre-installed Linux.

Many users don't worry to much about this, as they need the computer mostly to create documents, organize music and photos, send emails, surf the web, perhaps manage finances, watch movies, print, and play solitare.  These users, who I believe make up the majority, would be perfectly satisfied with Ubuntu or it's branches.

So they don't want it because they just want something with a recognisable brand behind it.  It gives them a feeling of security.  Best leave well enough alone.  Most people value comfort more than freedom.  I don't know that that's so wrong, though I do think it's a fallacy that Windows provides a more comfortable user experience than Ubuntu.

Anyway, that is my opinion.  I am long winded.
