Basically the company behind those "free trials" is making that PC cheaper by paying the PC manufacturer to include the software.

And because Linux doesn't have many software companies driving their sales, the cost for those PC's are higher.

### Post by aysiu on 2010-09-16
Three reasons: [list=1][*]**Microsoft subsidies**. Ever notice how Dell always "recommends" the latest version of Windows on every page of its website... even the pages you're buying Ubuntu systems from? You may not realize this, but they don't *really* recommend Windows, just as some star on TV who says she always uses _______ product to feel fresh in the morning doesn't necessarily use said product... or didn't before she got paid to. Microsoft either pays OEMs to put that recommendation up there or coerces them into doing so through offering some kind of discounted Windows license (or threatening not to).[*]**Third-party software subsidies**. Whatever trial software comes on a computer is definitely paid to be put on there. Norton pays to put its antivirus trial on there. RealPlayer pays to put its software on there. Dell or HP didn't decide to put those there just for fun. And you'll generally find trialware on Windows preinstalled machines, not on Linux ones.[*]**Economies of scale**. The Windows license isn't the only thing involved in selling Windows preinstalled. You need to install a test image and test it thoroughly, create a restore partition or restore disks, train your customer support staff on how to support the operating system, create user manuals that reference the operating system. That all costs money, but if you use that one operating system on almost all of your computers, and you sell a lot of those computers, you get higher returns for every computer you sell. If you put in all that effort to sell only a few thousand of a particular operating system, you're selling at a loss. In other words, the time and energy and money you put into supporting an operating system is almost constant or just goes up by a steady percentage based on how much you sell. The return you get back on that investment is exponentially tied to how many systems you sell of that type.[/list]
