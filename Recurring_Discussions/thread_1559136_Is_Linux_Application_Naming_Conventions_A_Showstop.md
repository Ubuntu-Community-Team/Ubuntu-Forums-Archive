### Post by ticopelp on 2010-08-24
While I think that app naming certainly could be an issue for some, I think that if a user clicks on their applications menu, sees "F-Spot Photo Manager" or "Gedit Text Editor" and are hopelessly confused about what said applications actually do, I don't think throwing more energy in that direction is really going to be productive in the long run.

Usability and clarity of purpose are great, but ultimately users can't be babied into never having to learn anything new when they switch operating systems, and I think it's a mistake to try. 

This isn't intended as an insult to new users or people who don't know a lot about Linux; after all, I was one of those people, and in some ways I still am. But looking at the default apps as they're installed on Lucid, I honestly think they're as clear and straightforward as they can be, and it seems doubtful to me that changing the names of the apps themselves would have any significant further benefit.

The user has to be willing to meet the operating system halfway and be willing to learn and try new things. If they're not, they might as well stick with the operating system they're using, because if they won't feel happy and safe using any user interface but the one they're used to, no amount of tweaking and accomodation on the developer end is going to solve that problem.

Also, Indicator Applet is a bit of a strange example because volume control isn't the only thing it does. I agree that perhaps the applet could be split into more discrete sections, but honestly that sort of non-intuitiveness is hardly isolated to Linux.

### Post by bunburya on 2010-08-24
I think having inconsistent naming conventions is part of the feeling of diversity and customisability that you get with Ubuntu. It reflects that it's not really the one company churning out all these programs and delivering them to you all bundled together. Even though many applications are officially bundled with GNOME and probably have a lot of the same developers working on them, I certainly get the feeling that, even within the GNOME core applications, each project is unique and is accompanied by its own community.

I think GNOME strikes a good balance by focusing on the function, rather than the name, of an application (I only found out a few days ago that "Movie Player" was actually Totem). In the menu that comes as standard on GNOME, applications are also grouped together according to their functions so it really shouldn't be that hared to figure out.

Of course, there are other ways of doing it, and KDE in particular has a very strong naming convention for its "house brand" applications, like KOffice, KMail etc. So if your friends are having a hard time adjusting to the variety/ambiguity of application names on GNOME, I would suggest referring them to KDE.
