### Post by ninjaaron on 2011-07-22
> **KiwiNZ said:**
> To me an OS should be the "Grey man" in other words , it's there does it's job and is not in your face.

That is why I like the OSX interface it's plain, it's functional and it is not in your face.

I hate GUI's designed in Crayola's

I think that's sort of what the new interfaces are tending towards; taking away a desktop cluttered with icons, moving towards widgets packed with content. and the application interfaces are moving more towards fullscreen content with a minimum of space dedicated to controls.  To me, that's the beauty of Windows 8: it lets you run multiple applications with almost every inch of the screen being dedicated to content.  I only want to see interface when I'm using it.  Just give me the content!

On the other hand, that's actually what I don't like about OSX, one of my only complaints about it.  You have the top panel, and then you always have the window title bar below it.  You have two lines of wasted space and then however much the program's controls take.  You get all the content squished to the bottom of the screen while the top is filled with controls that don't help me when I'm not using them. Controls that don't give you any information should be invisible when not in use.  At least in windows 7 you can hide the start bar (though this is also not good because then you can't see the time or the systray).

On the upside, OSX is allowing you to fullscreen almost every application now (which we've already been doing as long as I've been using Linux).  This still leaves the problem of the disappearing systray and clock.

My ideal desktop is always filled with information and content, and all controls are invisible until I need them. Content goes to window borders, and the window and application controls only appear on mouse-over at the edges (As you might imagine, I'm a huge fan of the Ayatana scrollbars and the Unity "love handles," not to mention lenses).  The systray is always visible but it only contains icons that give information, and it only occupies a small panel that doesn't fill the entire edge of the screen.  Default empty desktop contains no icons, but is filled with news and social feeds, weather forecasts, and system information, or whatever other content the user wants... 

... a completely invisible interface that delivers pure content unless it is being manipulated.  Anyway, I think Windows 8 and Android are the farthest along this path, and Unity is getting there too... but more slowly.  I'm sure OSX will move in that direction too.  iOS is basically there, and I'm sure OSX and iOS are only going to become more similar to each other with time.
