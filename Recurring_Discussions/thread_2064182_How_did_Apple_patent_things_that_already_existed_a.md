### Post by Paqman on 2012-09-28
> **kio_http said:**
> I've always wondered how things like rounded corners could be patented

They can't. Patents are different from [trade dress]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade_dress"), which is what rounded corners would come under. Essentially if you've got a distinctive looking product and someone tries to copy the appearance of it, then that's an issue of trade dress.

To be patentable something has to be:
[LIST=1]
[*]Novel
[*]Non-obvious (to an expert in that field)
[*]Capable of being manufactured
[/LIST]

### Post by Paqman on 2012-09-28
> **v1ad said:**
> 
Do you see the idiocy of patents now?

Nonsense, patents are an important way of protecting and stimulating innovation. Without the ability to patent an innovation nobody would invest the time and money it takes to develop one. In a world without patents an inventor could spend years and all their savings to develop a novel new product, only to have it reverse-engineered and copied within weeks of bringing it to market. These days there are manufacturers in China who can produce a cheap copy of just about anything very rapidly. Since they don't have to do the R&D they can undercut those who developed the products. Without patents nobody would be able to raise the cash to develop new ideas, because the investors know they'd never get their money back.

Patents themselves aren't the problem, the way they're applied in some countries such as the US are.
