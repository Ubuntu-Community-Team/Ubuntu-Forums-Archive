### Post by Mikeb85 on 2011-11-07
To Ubuntu and Canonical's success.  There, I said it.  

**Ubuntu is NOT Linux for Linux people.**  If you use or have ever used Debian, Slackware, Gentoo, or if you work out of a terminal, you are NOT Ubuntu's target audience.  Ubuntu might work for you, but you don't matter.  Kind of how Mac OS-X is not geared towards FreeBSD or PC-BSD users, it's geared towards normal people (but especially hipsters).  

Look at the mobile space - it's not BSD vs Linux, it's iOS vs Android.  Android is successful not because it's Linux, but because it works (and while it works in part because of Linux, other Linux mobile OSes have come and gone).  

This is why CCSM and Synaptic are not included by default.  The average user doesn't need them.  This is why there's paid apps in the software centre, because the average user associates quality with a cost, and is willing to pay.  This is why Unity is the main DE, and not a Windows 95-esque DE like Gnome 2 or XFCE.  

And for 'normal' people, ones whose computer using habits are not already shaped by Windows or Gnome 2, or who likely don't even have any computer using habits, who just expect their system to work without fuss, Ubuntu does work.  

(for the record, I'm an experienced computer user, Ubuntu also works for those who like keyboard shortcuts, it can do anything any other Linux distro can, it's very efficient for pros as well as newbs, but that's just not the point)

Edit - another way to put it - 100% of existing Linux users =\ 200 million users (Ubuntu goal)

### Post by thatguruguy on 2011-11-07
I posted this in a different thread, but it fits here as well:

> **thatguruguy said:**
> There's some really simple math involved in the creation of Unity and Gnome Shell.

Linux usage on the desktop has hovered around 1% for the past several  years.  You can claim that it's Microsoft's fault, or Apple's fault, or  some basic failing in human nature, but the fact is that Linux  (GNU/Linux, for those of you who insist upon using that) hasn't achieved  significant market penetration.  Nothing that Canonical, the Gnome  devs, Richard Stallman or Linus Torvalds does is going to change Apple,  Microsoft or human nature.  What ***can*** be changed is the product that is being offered the public.

Usability testing has shown that people not familiar with Linux like the  Unity interface.  They are the target audience.  And maybe they should  be, since it's the only way to move beyond the 1% ceiling.

If you're a dev and you think that your personal preferences are more  important than the preferences of the public, you need to reconsider  your approach to what you're doing.

I get that it's possible that Ubuntu (as well as any distribution which  uses either Unity or Gnome Shell) will lose some people who prefer the  "old" way of doing things.  If, in exchange, they gain new people who  never would have tried Linux otherwise, then that still scores as a win.

There's an old saying, frequently attributed to Albert Einstein, that  "Insanity is doing the same thing, the same way, over and over and  expecting different results." The way that things have been done hasn't  achieved the desired results (namely, broader usage of Linux on the  desktop).  And there's no reason to believe, after all this time, that  continuing to do things the same way will suddenly achieve different  results.

Unity might not be the ultimate answer. Gnome Shell may not be the  ultimate answer. But there's no question that Gnome 2 and the desktops  based on it weren't the answer, and it's time to try something  new.
