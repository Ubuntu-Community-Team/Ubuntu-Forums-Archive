### Post by Justblair on 2009-05-04
A friend and I have been discussing Linux recently, we both bought the 20gb Linux version of the EeePC and both found xandros a little disapointing.  As experianced windows users, Xandros just seemed clunky, awkward and limited to us.  Well I had moved over to Windows after not too long with Linux and my colleague was in the middle of doing the same.

We both felt that Xandros was not the Linux distro for those used to windows, but maybe there was another distro out there for us.  From this a plan was hatched, one that we are working on right now.  Call it a project, or call it an experiment, but we are aiming to convert our systems to Linux and dedicate 31 days to evaluating Linux from a Windows users perspective.

We will blog throughout our trials and tribulations.  

In the 31 days we aim to duplicate all the funtions that we enjoy from our Windows applications but with open source software.  As we are Microsoft Trainers with lots of experiance of Microsoft OS's but little to none in Linux, we expect this to be an  interesting if perilous journey.

The question we aim to find an answer to is basicly 

"Is the Linux operating system capable of delivering the function and style of delivery that an established Windows user can adapt to easily."

We have pretty much decided on Ubuntu or its varients as being the linux flavour we will use, I have registered here because I am sure that we will be asking for support during this time.

There are more details here in the project outline...

[http://www.ayetea.com/announcing-a-room … ndows.html]("http://www.ayetea.com/announcing-a-room-without-windows.html")

Comments are most welcome

It has Ubuntu's vast repository of free Linux applications to try out, from OpenOffice (compares to Microsoft Office) and Abiword (compares to Microsoft Word), Brasero for burning CDs, Gimp compares to Photoshop, Audacity for music editing, etc. Just about the only thing that Windows does better than Linux (thus far) is to support big-time gaming. But really. Gaming? That's what your XBOX and Playstation is for.

On my older computer with it's limited resources, I use [Crunchbang Linux]("http://crunchbanglinux.org")( which is based on Ubuntu and shares it's repositories) for a lean, mean, fast OS. But it has none of the "Windowsy" familiarity, so I installed [LXDE]("http://lxde.org") on it (in keeping with the Crunchbang philosophy of making it usable on older hardware) for a nice, familiar looking desktop environment. It's not nearly as resource-hungry as XFC (Xubuntu) or KDE (Kubuntu) but it still offers the sweet, easy-to-use "familiarity" that I was used to on WinXP (which is all I have known 'til I switched a little more than a month ago to Linux). So on my old computer I have my own little custom-made "Ubuntu Lite" (Crunchbang + LXDE)."

Check out the wicked-cool screenshots on the LCDE and Kubuntu web sites to see what I mean about the familiar desktop. 

Drive it around, kick the tires, read Ubuntu user's blogs about their own journey from Windows to Linux. I blogged about it [here]("http://robinzrantz.xanga.com/699518102/lock-up-obnoxious-new-converts/") and [here]("http://robinzrantz.xanga.com/700714546/resurrection-week/"). They can help your test subjects know what to expect.

Good luck with the project, and have fun at it! 

As a newbie myself who has only been on this journey for a short time, I have already learned a whole lot and had a blast playing with it!

### Post by tsali on 2009-05-04
I sold Ubuntu equipped machines to customers (most elderly) that had no previous experience with Windows. In most cases it was their first computer.

The very first day was usually pretty good, but I ultimately ended up replacing Ubuntu with WinXP on about 30% of those machines because of several issues:

1- Unreliable DSL Support - I could not configure AT&T DSL from the GUI and when I did, it performed poorly.
2- Poor dial-up support; some users did not have broadband available to them and couldn't understand the added expense of a hard modem. After that, they couldn't understand why they couldn't use wmconnect or some other low cost provider.
3- Inability to buy hardware bargains at Walmart, bring them home and make them work.
4- With Windows, they could always depend on a son or daughter who was familiar with Windows for help; Ubuntu left them on their own...usually calling me. These folks don't get IRC channels and forums.
5- Enough quirky software to make the user experience scary. On at least one occasion, a large picture got hung up in the print cue, attempting to print repeatedly (why didn't Evolution resize it?)

I lost a good bit of money with your experiment. As a result, I ONLY sell Windows systems these days unless the customer asks for something different.
