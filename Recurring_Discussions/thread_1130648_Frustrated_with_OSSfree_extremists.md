### Post by Alterax on 2009-04-20
I'd promptly rid myself of the efforts of the free software "extremists" if I were you.  That would include the GNU utilities, the standard C libraries, 90+ % of the applications that you run.

Sounds ridiculous?  Yeah, I thought so too.  Rather than shame you for complaining about the "extremism" of Free software advocates (while simultaneously enjoying the fruits of their extremism as your operating system), I'll ignore the blatant cognitive dissonance and explain why you shouldn't use proprietary technologies:

*clearing throat* Free software isn't just about price.  It's about something that goes well beyond the limited sphere that you call your life.  A proprietary codec is great--until some company decides they have the self-appointed right to sue you for using it--and if you make any content using it, you are at their mercy as to whether or not you can actually enjoy or share this content.  Since they own the codec and decide who has a right to use it, you are giving them ownership of your data--and if they decide you can't use it, well, that's just tough.

A proprietary driver is nice--until you realize that the companies that make it are interested in doing the absolute bare minimum of getting your hardware to run--not to its full potential but to the bare minimum they can get away with.  I mean, how many of us cheer and swoon like fangirls at a Jonas Brothers concert if ATI comes out with a new feature in its proprietary driver?  We could have had this functionality YEARS ago had we had access to the code.

Do you think it's any miracle that Broadcomm hasn't released any 43xx wireless drivers for use on GNU/Linux systems? Technically, those of us that are using their chipsets are doing so IN SPITE OF the vendors' efforts (or more appropriately the lack thereof).  ATI does the same; if I'm using their proprietary driver on an x86-based machine, well that's nice (despite compositing and several other functions they haven't bothered to port)--but on my PowerPC-based laptop, they're simply not interested. Not that there's anything magical about the x86 architecture, the binaries can be compiled for PPC, ARM, any number of architectures if the source code is available.  But not only are they not willing to do waste thirty minutes to make a build for other architectures, they are also dead-set against letting me use the source code so that I could do it myself.  Even if only for my private use.

That's the freedom that you give up when you use proprietary drivers.  You lose the most important right--which is the right to modify the software so that it works optimally with your particular hardware.  And if you hack it and use it anyhow, you violate their licenses and open yourself up to legal liabilities--which were set in place by these vendors to begin with.

I'll be the first to admit that the progress of the free alternatives are sometimes behind the proprietary counterparts.  But with the proprietary software, you are stuck with what functionality they decide is good enough--if anything at all.  The free software equivalent on the other hand may not be as good initially, but as it is open to experimentation, improvement, and portability of code, I find my hands aren't nearly as tied when it comes to using it--and that my bug reports are actually read and considered instead of being filtered into /dev/null.

With that in mind, I don't expect that this has changed your mind about proprietary software.  But those are my reasons for choosing it, and I stand by them.  It's why I chose a free operating system to begin with, not because I can save a couple of bucks.  That's why I'm proudly a Free Software advocate, and despite your frustration at my extremism, I will continue to work and hack away so that you and I and everyone else can reap the benefits.
