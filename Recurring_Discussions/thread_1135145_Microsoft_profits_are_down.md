However, I do believe that Microsoft has passed it's peak and it's downhill from here, for several reasons. 

Firstly, XP. Microsoft developed an operating system that was good enough for everyone and then left such a gap between it and the next version (Vista) that it gained a massive foothold AND became more stable. Vista's downfall wasn't just it's bloatedness (even 95 had hardware requirements beyond what people had at the time), it was because it provided nothing that users would migrate for, but gave them plenty of reasons to stay put (UAC popups, expense, incompatibilities, driver issues). Windows 7 doesn't seem to provide much reason either. 

Secondly, Netbooks. Boy, they didn't see that coming did they? Microsoft has spent so long using Moore's law to sell their OS upgrades, they completely missed the boat initially and then rushed XP out there. Now their whole foothold in the Netbook market sinks or swims with Windows 7. They can't continue supporting XP forever and still focus on Windows Vista and 7. It's just not economically a great idea, especially for the low cost they are now selling XP. So Windows 7 has to run great on Netbooks, well at least better than the competition which brings us on to the next point...

Three. Ubuntu. Firstly notice I say "Ubuntu" and not "Linux". I think I need to make this clear, Ubuntu is what is going to dent Microsoft. Users need a brand that they can rely on, that can be marketed and supported... not a kernel. Ubuntu itself was pretty late onto the Netbook scene, at least officially with the Dell Mini 9. But it's the ARM spin that I think will have MS on the ropes because AFAIK, they didn't design 7 for ARM and if they are planning on releasing an ARM version they've kept it pretty quiet. Ubuntu is also making great headway on the desktop, I believe 9.04 is the best release yet, not just from my experience, but from the sheer lack of complaints on these forums and slashdot compared to every other release. That said, I don't think Jaunty or Karmic will be the game changer, perhaps not even Karmic+1. But probably 10.10. At that point I believe the Gallium3D graphics stack will be pretty much implemented, Kernel modesetting will be stable, the OSS graphics drivers will have high enough performance and hopefully a GL 3.1 implementation. Pulseaudio will finally be working for everyone. Wine will have matured, it will likely have a mostly working D3D10 implementation and hopefully the USB support required for stuff like iPod syncing in iTunes. 
*That* IMO is when things are gonna start snowballing, when most users can just run their Windows applications on Ubuntu with no issues, when graphics Just Work out of the box switching will not be a big deal any more, users won't need to search for replacement apps, they can just carry on with their current ones.

Ubuntu only has to hit the 10% mark. 1 in 10 people running Ubuntu on *some* device and then MS has a problem, because, if there's nothing to lose, but loads to gain what's keeping them on Windows?
