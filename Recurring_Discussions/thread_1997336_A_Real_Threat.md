### Post by ExSuSEusr on 2012-06-05
Throughout the years, we've seem many *threats* come along that seemed destined to undermine the growing success and future of Linux.  

Linux has show the true power of Open Source, of the power of *world wide* collaboration.  It has shown blossomed from, quite literally, an Operating System that was nothing more than a terminal only usable by those with the expertise to author their own drivers (figuratively and literally) to a truly versatile OS that can be mastered by practically anyone.

We have recently had a few heated debates in this forum that led to violations of the CoC.  With that said, I still think it is very important to fully understand the implications surrounding this threat.  I am of course referring to the UEFI.

I am going to spend a little time discussing a few key points and in the end I would like to know what you think.  If you agree, if you don't and most importantly why.  I am not telling you *how* reply, just that I am looking for genuinely thoughtful replies - more substantive than "well, it is just aint gonna happen... just cause."

Some people here think the UEFI is a good thing.  And, that's fine - frankly when you look at UEFI, on paper it does sound good... no it sounds great.  But, there is a lot more to the story here, a lot more.

For those of you who aren't exactly sure what UEFI is, please refer to the following links, so you'll better understand my, as well as many, many other's concern:

**_What is UEFI?_**

The following link provides you with a clear and understandable definition and explanation of what UEFI is.

[http://www.extremetech.com/computing/96985-demystifying-uefi-the-long-overdue-bios-replacement](http://www.extremetech.com/computing/96985-demystifying-uefi-the-long-overdue-bios-replacement)

So, why do many of us thing this going to be very bad?

[http://www.freesoftwaremagazine.com/articles/uefi_and_windows_8_bad_news_gnu_linux](http://www.freesoftwaremagazine.com/articles/uefi_and_windows_8_bad_news_gnu_linux)

> There are times when I think that there is a special, darkened room at Microsoft peopled by a bunch of guys who seem to have nothing better to do than sit and think up some new wheeze to nobble the opposition. The rap sheet is an inditment in itself: trusted computing, internet driving licenses, DRM, bullying hardware vendors and attempting to strong arm sovereign nation states. You wouldn't think the list could get any bigger. It just has; but then, recidivism in incurable. It may not, as has often proved in the past, come to anything but if it does it would be a problematical for GNU/Linux. The irony is that it may not actually be intentional, but then, the universe is littered with the victims of the law of unintended consequences. So, what's the Hydra's latest head? UEFI. That's what.

Essentially, the point of the debate is this:  Should the hardware vendors decide not to allow the UEFI to be disabled - you CANNOT install your favorite distro of Linux on your new computer. So, a lot of people are under the assumption that the hardware vendors will simply include the option and that everything will be fine.  But, can we really trust a company as shady as Microsoft to not interfere for their benefit?

Let's take a few moments to review a bit of history:

**_The Dark History of Microsoft_**

Let's start with the most recent escapade.  

[http://www.networkworld.com/news/2011/101311-microsoft-social-responsibility-251962.html](http://www.networkworld.com/news/2011/101311-microsoft-social-responsibility-251962.html)

> Microsoft **will now insist its hardware suppliers comply** with the company's social responsibility requirements.

Did you note the play on words there?  I sure did.  "Social Responsibility..."  Reminds me of the language being thrown around to describe UEFI... "Secure Booting" and "Users disable at their own risk..."

Thus, the stage is set to require the hardware vendors not to enable the option of disabling the UEFI.  Under the guise of "social responsibility" insomuch that they could be putting their end users at risk by allowing them *monkey* with the new built-in security. 

But, therein lies the point - it isn't *truly* about security.  It's about Microsoft ensuring their future (with substandard poorly designed products) by **ensuring** alternative operating systems cannot be installed on new computers.

This wouldn't be the first time Microsoft has attempted, through unethical practice, to maintain their illegal hold. 

[http://www.computerweekly.com/news/2240149216/Cabinet-Office-resets-conflict-of-interest-consultation](http://www.computerweekly.com/news/2240149216/Cabinet-Office-resets-conflict-of-interest-consultation)


> Andrew Hopkirk &#8211; a former director of technology at the National Computing Centre, who presented himself as an independent consultant &#8211; had facilitated a public consultation meeting that recorded an outcome in accord with Microsoft's position and subsequently attracted accusations of bias.

Basically, he more or less worked for Microsoft and under the guise of being an independent consultant attempted to sway the UK away from Open Source (Standard).

With all the things that UEFI can do... the most focus has been placed on security.  Why?  This is by design, you can count on that.  It is so because it strikes at the paranoia of the average user.  "If we disable this we might have our identity stolen." It sets the ground for Microsoft to *require* vendors not enable the option.

The list goes on... and on.... and on.... and on.

**_Why Would Microsoft Care?_**

Simply put, they have to.  By law (US) a company is required to put their shareholders above all else - with the exception of compliance (EPA, OSHA, IRS, so forth).  That's not necessarily a bad thing, but the practices that Microsoft engages in to achieve this *is*.

So, let's look at the big picture.  

Apple and Android own the cell phone market.
Apple and Android (with Amazon's Kindle making headway) own the tablet market.
Apple essentially owns the portable media market (MP3 players, etc) with several other companies not falling too far behind.

So where does that leave Microsoft?  They failed to make an impact in the cell phone market, their tablet was more or less dead on arrival... it leaves them with their OS.

Here's a little snippet of the market overview:

[http://www.netmarketshare.com/](http://www.netmarketshare.com/)

The only category that Microsoft stands out is with the Operating System (desktop/laptop) and web browser.  

Now consider this... consider the rate at which Mint / Ubuntu / Fedora / so on are growing.

For a quick experiment I Googled the phrase "Operating System"

Fourth down on the list was "Ubuntu" - directly below that was "Howitworks Operating Systems"  -- Windows was only mentioned at the bottom of the page.  The implication here is that people are reading about different operating systems and possibly considering them as an alternative.  Something Microsoft most surly does not like.

[http://www.pcworld.com/businesscenter/article/169869/social_media_takes_a_hit_microsoft_fears_linux.html](http://www.pcworld.com/businesscenter/article/169869/social_media_takes_a_hit_microsoft_fears_linux.html)

> Microsoft acknowledges Linux threat to Windows: Another SEC filing confirmed that Microsoft sees Linux as a threat to its Windows OS, something Microsoft executives had made pretty clear already through various public proclamations. Microsoft obliquely referenced the use of Linux in netbooks as a cause for concern.

The claim is that they were only concerned because of netbooks.  Now, can you honestly believe anything that comes out of the mouth of a Microsoft representative?  Do you really they were only concerned about "netbooks?"

Ok, let's say that they weren't lying (that'd be a first) - if there were worried about NETBOOKS why in the world would they not be worried about desktops and laptops????

**_But the Vendors can Ship Linux Friendly..._**

Right.  Do you really believe a company like Microsoft - that has a history of threats and strong arming hardware manufacturers is going to sit idly by and *allow* these vendors to ship products that allow for a competing OS to function?  When they have this opportunity to lock the competition out?

You can argue that there's no way they could do this - that there are too many different hardware makers out there - but they don't need but one major company.

Finally - the very fact that Fedora and Red Hat have agreed to PAY them only makes things worse.  It legitimizes the actions of a corrupt monopolistic corporation hell bent on total domination and control.  Now, if it goes to court - they can simply cite two of the biggest players in the Linux world as having AGREED to the terms..

I am telling you as sure as I am typing this, this isn't going to be good for our OS.  And, it doesn't look like anyone of any serious power has taken notice.

We're told to sit back and everything will be fine.  That we can more or less trust them.  That if they do carry out what they have planned all along that the community will just find a work around.  The problem is, as I have said before, the work around will render Linux back several years into a terminal based OS that only the most experienced will be able to use.

So, if you don't agree please enlighten me with your reasons why. I am genuinely curious.

I leave you with this:

[http://www.linuxforu.com/2012/02/uefi-should-linux-users-be-worried/](http://www.linuxforu.com/2012/02/uefi-should-linux-users-be-worried/)

> In short, machines with UEFI on board may simply be incapable of booting GNU/Linux, BSD or any other open source OS.

But we can always override the default settings, right?

Actually, no. Any changes to the UEFI firmware will require a digitally signed image or &#8220;key&#8221; that can only be had via the OEMs or, obviously, Microsoft. Naturally, the latter will not only deny licensing such keys, but compel OEMs to withhold them as well.

And of course, Microsoft will not admit it that easily, will they?

They are justifying this act by claiming it to be an enhanced level of protection against rootkits, boot-time viruses and other malware. Picture this: we know GNU/Linux is not malware. But what if general users of the Windows desktop wish to give Ubuntu or Fedora a spin? They can&#8217;t boot it, as the UEFI mechanism will reject the boot disk as unauthorised (or, in other words, &#8220;malware&#8221;)! How is GNU/Linux going to win converts in such a case? Now, that is a tricky question!

Let&#8217;s assume for a moment that Microsoft gives in and allows dual-booting GNU/Linux with Windows 8. Yet again, there is a technical catch. In order to boot under the digitally signed keys, open source boot loaders such as GRUB will need to incorporate proprietary signatures within their code. This goes against the very ideology of FOSS, and, more precisely, will not be possible under the terms of the GPL.

This isn't just another "oh no this will be our undoing... haha..."  This is for real.
