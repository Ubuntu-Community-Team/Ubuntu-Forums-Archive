### Post by ve4cib on 2010-02-13
Based on my limited experience I'd put IE8 lightyears ahead of Safari.  Granted I've only ever used Safari on Windows, but I found it cumbersome, more than slightly unstable, and just generally annoying to use.  Rather much like iTunes in fact.  But that's a whole other story.

IE8, unlike its predecessors, is actually a pretty-decent browser.  Yes, it has its quirks and annoyances, but so does everything else.  Firefox can be arduously slow to load, Chrome's UI is less-than-stellar (IMO), Safari just crashed on me all the time, and Opera... well, I haven't used Opera enough to say anything about it one way or the other.  It seems to be at least on-par with IE8.

> why do they continue to push it even though they know it can't compete with other browsers?

They do it because they *can* compete with other browsers.  IE has such an entrenched market share -- especially in the business world -- that it's all but impossible to eliminate it.  Just look at the market share IE6 still has, and virtually anyone who's touched a computer since XP came out could tell you that IE6 *sucked*.

With IE6 and IE7 Microsoft was essentially playing it very conservatively, and riding on the enormous success of the 90s.  They had a huge market share, and essentially no competition in the desktop browser market.  They were slow to add new features, and blatantly ignored standards knowing that everyone would have to conform to their way simply because of the market share.

Fortunately other browsers, notably Firefox and Safari, started seeing a pretty drastic surge in popularity, and suddenly MS was forced to re-evaluate their complacent attitude towards their browser.  IE8 is their first major forray into trying to regain that huge domination they used to had.  Is it perfect?  No.  But it's a far cry better than its precessors.  And I fully expect IE9 to be even better.

IE may have lost face over the last few years, but don't kid yourself into thinking it's not a major player in the browser market, or that it's not a competitive product.

The more MS pushes IE, and keeps improving it, the more Mozilla, Apple, Google, Opera, and everyone else have to push back and improve their products.  If nothing else this is resulting in more browser competition and innovation (and better adherance to web design standards) than we have had in years.
