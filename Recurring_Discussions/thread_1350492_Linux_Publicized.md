### Post by aysiu on 2009-12-11
It's the same reason you don't see advertisements for alternative power supplies for a TV or advertisements for potato-supplied electricity.

Apart from power users (which I loosely define as those who family members and friends go to for computer help), no one wants to download, install, and configure an operating system. Very few people care about partitions, boot loaders, and drivers.

People want to buy computers, not operating systems. If you have well-configured Linux preinstalled laptop options that are competitively priced, then it makes sense to advertise (Dell is a great example of how **not** to market Linux... in fact, their Ubuntu implementation is almost marketing Windows!).

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2009-12-11
You don't sell an engine, you sell a car. Linux isn't a product; in the strictest sense, it's a kernel.  Even in the loosey-goosey-you-know-what-I-mean sense, it's still just a nebulous mass of free software stacked on a Linux kernel.

If someone incorporates it into a product -- such as a Linux-powered phone, netbook, commercially supported distribution, appliance -- then they tend to advertise the product.

And that's how it should be.
