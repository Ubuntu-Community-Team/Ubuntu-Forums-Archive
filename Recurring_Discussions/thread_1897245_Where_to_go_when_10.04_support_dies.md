### Post by pneumatic on 2011-12-18
I've spent hundreds of hours trying to find something good about the new Ubuntu interfaces when it occurred to me that I just want Gnome 2: a taskbar that I can see all the time.

I've reinstalled Ubuntu 10.04 and I am happier than I have been in a long time.  I can run Windows on one side of my Compiz cube and use edge-detection to rotate.  It is amazing for productivity (I am an IT director that manages mostly linux servers in a heavy Windows environment).

I've tried Mint 12 and that is just as much garbage as the Unity stuff.  I've come to the conclusion that it would be worth a lot of money to keep a 10.04 supported for as long as possible.  Is there someone that I can pay to stay on 10.04?

Why did the developers take it upon themselves to remove choices?  Why can't I simply have a static taskbar on the screen?  Should I just run Linux in a Windows VM in order to get real choices?

Who thought that it would be a good idea to remove choices?

Is Xubuntu any better?  I've tried KDE and that really seems like what Gnome is chasing (not what I want, albeit more choices than Unity).

Is someone at Ubuntu considering a separate tablet edition so that we can have our real desktop edition back?

### Post by VanillaMozilla on 2011-12-20
Agreed.  Gnome 2 has its warts, but even so, after Gnome 2, everything else is a shock.  It's like an early Mac user having to move back to Windows 3.1.

The problem is that Gnome has just up and pulled the plug on Gnome 2.  They have some substitutes, but they're primitive by comparison.

A lot of people, including Linus Torvalds, have moved to XFCE.  It is at least compatible with Gnome, but it's visually primitive.  I didn't think looks mattered much until I looked at some of the simple Linux desktops.

I wouldn't install the whole xfce package that comes complete with apps.  You will have a mess of duplication, with a lot of programs that you don't want.  You can try just the desktop, I think.  I have no idea how you get it to look and act like Gnome.

Other than that, I think the best strategy may be to hold out a little longer and hope they fix up the Gnome alternative (gnome-classic or gnome-fallback?) so it works like Gnome 2.  I can't remember, though.  One of those is not intended to ever support the fancy features like the rotating cube that you use.

By the way, how do you do that?  Do you really have actual Windows and Linux running simultaneously?
