### Post by ubiquitousblake on 2010-04-04
I have always been one to enjoy the perks affiliated with software that is professionally maintained, however, has it also occurred to any other that since Shuttleworth left Canonical, Ubuntu has become more of a business product than an example of open-source ingenuity? Now, humor me for a brief moment. Think to when Bill left Microsoft. Even he seemed concerned about the actual product. Ballmer runs Microsoft purely from a buisness standpoint, in my opinion, and the company has suffered considerably. Giving room to companies and communities like Apple, Novel, Canonical, and Mozilla (to name a few) room to grow immensely from their original market values. 

My fear, is that Canonical is also becoming more business oriented. Now, I'm not so naive as to allude that a company would not focus on profit and productivity, but could one not argue that lately the main perceived intent behind such Canonical products like the Ubuntu-one Music store, and Ubuntu-one itself are products not for purely for the betterment of the system. Although they may assist in the seduction of other OS users, they could be viewed as a cash crop for Canonical. 

As Ubuntu continues to grow and evolve, we must always remember the guiding focus of the intent behind the OS (mind you I'm paraphrasing): Simplicity, and open-source. Never a business product, but a human product. 

Although profit would surely assist in the development of the software and its public awareness, the strive to break from profit orientated systems to one where people as a community truly care is a highlighted motif within both the Ubuntu community and developers.

In retrospect, I just hope against hope that Canonical does not, in the long run, lose focus. I love Ubuntu. I love Linux in general. But most of all, I love open source products. Canonical has done well so far, I just hope that they can prove to us that they can continue to do well. 

What are your thoughts? Disagree; agree?
