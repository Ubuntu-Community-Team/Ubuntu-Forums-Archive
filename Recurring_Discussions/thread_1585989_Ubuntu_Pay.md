### Post by frup on 2010-10-02
The OS is still free and will always be libre.

The entire time I have been using linux people have been complaining:

a) Linux doesn't have enough professional apps.
b) Canonical needs to become profitable to make sure ubuntu doesn't die

a) always has the old catch22 users vs apps. Canonical has figured out a reasonably unobtrusive way to try and encourage Linux development / ports. Even if Linux magically became the dominant OS not all software would be free as in cost. For those of us that only want to use FLOSS or free apps, we don't have to use this service, but there is a significant amount of people who do want this and it isn't freedom if you stop them having what they want.

Judging from what I have seen, in the area where proprietary apps become free after some initial period, surely these kinds of apps would be more likely to do so. Compared to the status quo, while not "proper" FLOSS, it's still a less evil form of proprietary.

b) Canonical is close to profitable according to the various non-evidential blogs that post about these sorts of things from time to time. Little things can just bump them over the limit. That means a more secure future for Ubuntu and more room to actually innovate the things YOU use.

As much as one could argue this damages the Ubuntu brand (which to mainstream society it will have no effect on) You can also argue that moves like this provide what a proportion of users want... This means potentially more users, which is what developers want.

The great thing about FLOSS is that every potential user is also somewhat of a potential developer. There are so many cool projects in the Ubuntu community that aren't made by extreme linux kernel hackers but regular users who have fallen in love with the OS. Because they're regular users they add something which is unique.

Since Ubuntu's existence it has made tough decisions. You see for every decision Canonical makes they get attacked from two sides. When Rhythmbox and Totem started asking you if you wanted codecs, the zealots proclaim, "This is not free software". You then have the gutless "Mint" crowd proclaim "This is not usable software". With attitudes like this on both sides, I'm sometimes surprised how far things have come.  

Ultimately I'm a freedom lover, my VRMS outputs:
  1 non-free packages, 0.1% of 1899 installed packages.
  1 contrib packages, 0.1% of 1899 installed packages.

I could get rid of both those too, I don't need them, they are just a little convenient (rar and ms core fonts)

But who am I to tell another user what they can and can't do. That's not freedom. 

I also fall in to the crowd who doesn't necessarily think all games should be completely open source. Ideology aside, while community contribution and collaborative work is really good and helpful on things like engines, the artwork and stories don't really work the same way. They are too subjective and need someone needs tight control over their creation and often it is best if they are hidden until release because quite simply, if you see a story unfold piece by piece and change here and there, no one is going to be happy and by the time it is complete it is going to be boring. Sure it could become Free once it is created, but for the large part I think most developers prefer the collaboration advantages of Free software not just the ideology, so what would be the point?

That was longer than I intended when I initially replied.
