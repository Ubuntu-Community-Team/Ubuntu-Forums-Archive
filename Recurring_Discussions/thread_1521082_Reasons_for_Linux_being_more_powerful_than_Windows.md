### Post by 3Miro on 2010-06-30
1. Go to YouTube and search for Richard Stallman, those videos should be easy enough to follow and he very well explains what "free" means. Free as in freedom refers to what you are allowed to do with Linux, there nothing that says that you shouldn't be paying for it. In fact, there are payed versions of Linux (Red Hat and Mandriva for example). Ubuntu, however, is both free as in freedom and free as in cheap.

In short, if I make a small program for myself then it doesn't cost me anything to share it with everyone else. Then someone else will spend some time to make it better and then share the improved version so that we both will have a better program. Then a third person will come and so on. Thus you have an open system where everyone if free to modify, share and improve. Thus a program becomes larger and better all the time, people that make "free" software usually make it for themselves and not just to sell it to someone else. Decisions on functionality are made upon "what will work best for me" as opposed to "what can we best market and sell".

2. MS knows what the viruses are, where they hit and how they work. They either don't want to fix the problems in Windows or don't have the resources to do so. As large as MS corporation is, the Linux community is much larger. Remember that Windows users, as numerous as they are, are not allowed to modify or improve Windows even if they know how. Linux is open for contributions from anyone.

3. Linux has been "famous" for a very long time. It is the dominant platform on supercomputers and Internet servers and has been so for may years. Ubuntu is the version that aims at making Linux popular for regular desktop computers.

4. I am not sure what you mean. Linux is very close to BSD Unix and I believe over 90% compatible. For the most part, a program for Linux will fork on Unix and the other way around, with only a recompiling. Only some system tools (i.e. drivers related) might be significantly different.

That's not true. Linux is not based on the Unix kernel. As I understand it Linux is designed to be Unix-like, it looks and acts like Unix, but it is not based on any of the original Unix code.
