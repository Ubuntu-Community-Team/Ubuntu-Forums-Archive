The config file is:
```
~/.wmii-3.5/wmiirc
```

It doesn't exist by default, so you can use the:

```

/etc/X11/wmii3.5/wmiirc
```

```
~$cp /etc/X11/wmii-3.5/wmiirc ~/.wmii-3.5/
```

### Post by wdaniels on 2008-05-21
I think Linus makes his point well enough that I don't think I could explain it in any more useful terms. And I tend to agree with him on the point about over-simplifying things for users - it quickly reaches a point where you're only making something easier to use by reducing the number of obviously wrong things to click on, which doesn't really help anybody.

However, I may not entirely agree with the strategy, but I do like the result, because it does almost exactly what I want anyway - I just don't need any more options than Gnome gives me.

It has nothing to do with "power users" or anything like that and I really don't like that way of grouping and thinking about users - I don't believe the probability of there being a particular option to let somebody do whatever it is they happen to want, has much to do with their level of understanding of the system and it's architecture. In fact the reverse may be truer - "power users" can probably accomplish their goals happily enough using just whatever is available (likely a terminal) whereas less-experienced users need more GUIs with more options to do the same.

Ultimately, I don't see any reason why Linus should really want to encourage people to use one desktop or another, except that given his status in the Linux community, probably a lot of people ask him, in which case it's their own fault if they don't like the answer. The only thing I don't understand in all of this is why anybody would think that what Linus thinks his desktop should do has any relevance to what they want for their own!
