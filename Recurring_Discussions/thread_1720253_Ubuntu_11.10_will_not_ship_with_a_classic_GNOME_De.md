Incidentally, Gnome 2's taskbar items really were, like, 100 px wide. The application menu is fairly broad as well, and system tray icons are still the same size that they were in Gnome 2. Desktop icons are going out of style, in favor of dock icons, and the dock icons are the same size that the desktop icons were.

The click target for task switching has *always* been much larger than a 20 px icon, and a lot of people prefer desktop icons for launching applications because they're accessible for exactly those kinds of big, sloppy clicks. The dock in Unity or Gnome 3 is combining all the features of desktop icons, task switching, *and* quick launch. It has little to nothing to do with touch interfaces. Unity's panel and dock are a *little* too big for my netbook screen, but I don't think I'd mind them at a normal display resolution.

If you fork Gnome it ceases to be Gnome. In which case it does not have the position or branding of Gnome. Nor the support of Gnome which is a major organization backed by companies and communities. Gnome already has policies in place that are practical and functional. Gnome has funding.

You start your fork of Gnome you essentially have a snapshot of the latest Gnome 2 code. You have to drum up interest to gain developers and testers. Skilled focused developers are essential. Considering most major distributions have already shipped GTK/Gnome3 the developer tools commonly available are already geared toward Gnome 3. In fact Ubuntu, which is arguable the most influential desktop distro has already built their environment on Gnome 3.

Even if you get working code and a decent team of developers you have to have it included in a major distribution to have any real use. To include it means the distributor has to have it be packaged in a useful way. This means alongside GTK2 and GTK3 applications. This is certainly possible though you will have to rename or specially package anything that is similar but already based on GTK3 (such as Nautilus). Considering your team has the time, effort, or will to do so of course. Such a move is a multifaceted and complex situation involving management, community, funding, politics, reputation, and redundancy. All of those are factors that ignoring any could result in a considerable amount of wasted effort.

Any real adoption of a 'gnome 2 fork' I optimistically estimate will live in a 3rd party repository and installed to /opt. You might have a small core of more technically inclined users (that know/care enough to use Gnome 2 specifically). I estimate this based on the fact the users will want to seek Gnome 2 and will need the skill to add this third party repository. This might make up a large part of active forum communities but not the Desktop user base as a whole.

I wish such a project good luck and good success. It is open source after all and even small or one man efforts can accomplish a lot.
