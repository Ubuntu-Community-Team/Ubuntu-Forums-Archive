For the less than computer savvy, Windows 7 is a very good OS. Someone with a high profile in the Ubuntu Community, can't think of his name (one of the founders) essentially described Win7 as being the first MS OS worthy to be called an OS. I think he's right. Since most users are less than savvy or have to have MS for work-related reasons, I doubt that Win7's market share will change much. They just want their OS to work, and they're willing to pay. That's OK. I'm **not** willing to pay. I like self-sufficiency.

However, I expect distros like Ubuntu & Mint will begin to make real inroads with more savvy users. It has with **me**.  I started out trying to use Linux in the late 90's - RedHat, but gave up. After endless hours of hacking configuration files (and never getting my sound card to work), I gave up. It was always an issue of time versus productivity. Ubuntu has changed all that.

For users like me, who have older computers and don't want to spend hundreds of dollars on windows upgrades, anti-virus software and the like, the new Ubuntu (and its variants) are a godsend. Linux has really grown up. Expect it to make more inroads with pseudo-techies like myself - folks willing to spend a little extra on the learning curve if it saves them a few bucks.

The big breakthrough will be when Ubuntu (or any distro) is as beginner friendly as Windows. It's getting there.

Three things have to happen (in my view): 1.) Maintaining and updating software needs to be centralized and just about dummy proof. 2.) Canonical needs to kiss Adobe butt or hope HTML5 conquers the world. 3.) The Linux world has to be willing to share the bus with non-GNU software makers. I think Canonical is headed in that direction and it's a good idea. I'm all for GNU, but let users decide. Folks make money for a reason - so they can spend it (most of them). If Joe Blow is willing to shell out $800 dollars to run Adobe Photoshop on his Linux Box. Let him, I say. In the long run, I think it will benefit all of us.
