### Post by the8thstar on 2010-09-06
From the article:
> 
Here are some things that we think could be done to make Linux more popular on the desktop. This would demand the support of a more or less unified Linux community, so it might be unfeasible, but one can dream, right?

    * Center around one Linux desktop distribution.
    * Center around one GUI.
    * Polish the product (yes, you have to think of it as a product) until it shines, and make sure there are plenty of polished apps to go with it.
    * Be fanatical about consistency and usability.
    * And this may be the hardest one of all: Don’t build for Linux’s current hardcore audience of tech geeks. If the goal is to bring in new users, that approach is a big no-no since it will alienate the vast majority of the population.

I believe most of these 'dreams' are incompatible with the way Linux is today.


**Center around one Linux desktop distribution**

You can create a new distro in a snap, for your own sake. And why not fter all?


**Center around one GUI**

Gnome, KDE, E17, XFCE, etc... different GUI for different users with different machines. Gnome would kill a PIII with 128Mb of RAM, so there has to be several gui's around to make sure that a wide variety of computers can still run Linux.


**Polish the product**

Well, the default Ubuntu Gnome and KDE look pretty sleek from the get-go. And you can tweak at will on top of that.


**make sure there are plenty of polished apps to go with it**

That's a pretty tall order... a lot of apps don't make it to version 1.0.


**Be fanatical about consistency and usability**

Controlling and checking apps is already happening. 


**Don’t build for Linux’s current hardcore audience of tech geeks**

I am not a tech geek and I make it a point to stay away from the command-line. I'm sure a lot of users are non-geeks. What we really need is public advertisement.

### Post by utnubuuser on 2010-09-06
That is a good article.  I'm going to put forward an idea here.  The idea is this.  Nobody wants a "free" "open source" OS.

To see real success for Linux on the desktop, only one thing needs to be done.  The source must be closed. It must be made Proprietary.

You can have the Libre version - just as it is today - better than the current proprietary OSs. That's my opinion -Ubuntu is a great OS. - I actually enjoy using my computer with Ubuntu installed. - But for commercial success, and lets face it, when we're talking about Ubuntu/Linux on as many desktop/laptops etc as possible, we're talking commercial, and we're talking about a closed source OS with a EULA like the big commercial vendors have.

I've heard tons of suggestions about better marketing, polished UI, etc, etc. 
These are the points the article suggests would make a difference to Ubuntu/Linux:>     * Center around one Linux desktop distribution.
    * Center around one GUI.
    * Polish the product (yes, you have to think of it as a product) until it shines, and make sure there are plenty of polished apps to go with it.
    * Be fanatical about consistency and usability.
    * And this may be the hardest one of all: Don&#8217;t build for Linux&#8217;s current hardcore audience of tech geeks. If the goal is to bring in new users, that approach is a big no-no since it will alienate the vast majority of the population.

The truth of the matter is that A.) You can't sell something that's already free, and B.)Free software = free mind.

I'm recommending that the GPL be changed/expanded, to allow for proprietary vending.
Ubuntu/Linux would immediately see a huge jump in the number of installations if there were a way to have the OS be proprietary, and a price-tag attached to it. - There'd be an army of sales-people lining up to install Ubuntu on every existing computer.

I know that sounds like the very anti-thesis of Libre software, but there exists one simple little fact: The way we wish things were, and the way things actually are.

Closed-Source, proprietary licensing means private, marketable, and that's what the world wants.  Private/Marketable -  To give away at their discretion, and a price at which to buy/sell/steal the "product". 

Apple succeeded with their product because they had a "product". - 1 OS installed on only 1 set of hardware - theirs. Apple is exactly the same as Lynux - if Torvalds had known that Jobs already did what he did, according to Torvalds, there would be no Linux kernel.

Ubuntu/Linux can easily take the same approach.  Frankly, I'm really surprised that no mover and shaker has taken up the call to produce a computer specifically for Linux/Ubuntu. With the accompanying "pretend" privacy where the end user has to agree not to do what he/she inevitably will.

Can you imagine computer hardware optimized for Linux? Not code optimized for hardware, but hardware optimized for code. Well, I don't see that happening without a EULA.

Humans are competitive by nature, and no-one likes a competitor who takes the stance that non-competition is better sport - it's like playing, or worse, watching, a soccer match against a team that either has no goalie, or no goal at all - and that's how free software comes across in a "commercial" environment - the desktop market - no goal, no goalie.

I don't know the how-tos and wherefores, but I feel very sure that somehow a proprietary version of Ubuntu or any other Linux distro is possible, and that, in my opinion, is how the desktop market can be won. - Make it buy-able, sell-able, and steal-able.
