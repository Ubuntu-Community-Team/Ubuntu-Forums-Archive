### Post by sgtv on 2011-04-29
In this thread I&#8217;m going to honestly describe you my experience of using  this brand new Unity&#8482; desktop environment. I&#8217;ve just finished updating  my machine and while it&#8217;s busy downloading Kubuntu now, I decided to  utilise time properly and write this post. Hopefully it will save many  people a lot of headaches and they&#8217;ll just do the same (download Kubuntu  or other distribution like me).

This post is generally a message to Ubuntu UI team.

[SIZE=3]Background[/SIZE]

I  would like to start with telling you what I thought about previous  releases of Ubuntu (which came with good old Gnome). 10.04 was the  distribution that finally convinced me to make Ubuntu my main OS. It was  just great: extremely stylish and beautiful dark UI mixed with sheer  brilliance of Compiz and rock-solid stability of Linux kernel won over  aging Windows architecture and ugliness easily. Then came 10.10. It was a  pinnacle of Ubuntu brilliance. Just about everything became even better  despite already being perfect. It was a lot of joy for me to learn UNIX  architecture and commands using it. I also finally realised the power  of Vim and Emacs during my days with it. Not the last thing was the  power of Gnome environment which allowed me to do advanced things easily  and without headaches. What I&#8217;m trying to say is everything was so  great you could just leave it and it wouldn't suck or look bad in next  10 or so years.

[SIZE=3]First Experience[/SIZE]

Now ***What The F$*%*** is this...

[removed for security reason]

Yes.  This is how warm Unity greeting looks like. Whenever you try to  configure a simple thing, it will crash just like that. I tried to  disable grid placement in Compiz and this is what it gave to me. It also  crashes like that if you try to modify window shadows. In fact, I  couldn&#8217;t change window shadows at all &#8212; turned out the setting was for  popup menus. The setting for window shadows is non-existent (or buried  somewhere deeply).

Now, after I got this, I obviously tried to go  back to launcher and fire up some screenshot app to capture this moment  of Unity suckage &#8212; only to find out that it crashed so interestingly  that application launcher is now absent from this bar completely:

You  can notice at the bottom of that bar that application and recent  document launchers are gone (I hope I spelled them properly). Thank God  there&#8217;s still a terminal and Alt+F2 shortcut works allowing me to fire  up gnome-screenshot. But apart from this I&#8217;m simply lost &#8212; the ability  to comfortably launch applications is gone. The top global menu panel is  gone also being trashed by some visual garbage.

Eventually I  figured out that despite top panel looks crashed, the menu buttons are  actually still there and work: you can click on some part of that panel  and various submenus will pop out. The Alt key somehow didn&#8217;t work.  Nevertheless, I launched Gimp through terminal to capture some more.

This is how my cube looks now:

[IMG]http://img836.imageshack.us/img836/1892/suckage3.jpg[/IMG]

Notice  there&#8217;s also some ugly blue arrow appears at the top left corner for no  reason. It probably signifies a place where one should put cursor in  order for this launcher to appear. But what&#8217;s the reason for it when I  rotate cube? I can&#8217;t click it anyway... And what sense does that top  panel make now while rotating cube? The way Unity improves UI? What a  garbage. I cannot use cube normally now...

Being lost and leaved with broken UI I started to  investigate the last part of Unity which was still more or less working &#8212;  a launcher. The first thing I thought of is how do I add my own  launcher item? In Gnome it was perfectly easy: right click on panel  brings context menu with &#8220;Add...&#8221; command, you enter command, choose an  icon and you are all set &#8212; works perfectly. Even a cat would&#8217;ve  understand if only it needed to use computers. Now... how do I do it  here? After few minutes of clicking around I&#8217;ve finally realised that  it&#8217;s simply impossible now &#8212; you cannot [easily] make your own launcher  item with your custom command anymore. Nor you can customize launcher&#8217;s  command:

[IMG]http://img405.imageshack.us/img405/672/suckage4.jpg[/IMG]

I  had a useful Vim launcher in my panel which allowed me to easily drag  some file on it and it would fire up Vim in terminal with that file  ready for editing. This launcher you see on the screenshot &#8212; it came  from my old panel configuration. And it&#8217;s impossible now... Not only it  doesn&#8217;t allow you to make a launcher as such, It actually doesn&#8217;t allow  you to drag & drop documents on launcher items anymore... Nothing  happens.
[SIZE=3]
Further Thoughts[/SIZE]

Apart from  this, the cascaded menus are now gone. I.e. if I have more than 100  applications installed, the only way to find the one I need is to use  search field. In Gnome I had a convenient cascaded menus set which  allowed me to fire up my stuff with only mouse in my hand. Now I need to  go to that launcher thing, go back to keyboard, write a name of  application I need and hope it will appear. Also, in Gnome when you  installed something you could easily guess where the new application  icon would appear. Of course even this is broken now as the only way to  have all your needed apps at hand is to put everything you need in this  damn launcher bar.

The next major flaw is that Unity is somehow  much slower. Not only my laptop boots twice as longer now, the graphic  response is awful: I can see how menus flicker when they are being  painted. No such thing was in Gnome.

And by the way, I don&#8217;t have  any proprietary drivers installed. I got Intel 4500 MHD graphic chip  which works (worked perfectly in Gnome) flawlessly in Linux. So all  these problems are not driver-related...

I think I&#8217;ve had enough of this Unity torture so in the end of my post I would like to say two simple things.

**First**.  If it works &#8212; don&#8217;t touch it. Really, why would you touch something  that works perfectly? It&#8217;s simply illogical. Let&#8217;s look at a door. Door  is a human invention which allows you to go from one room to another.  Door is simple and it works &#8212; you don&#8217;t need a degree to know how to use  it. Door is so simple and great it doesn&#8217;t need fancy 3D effects on it  nor it does need some additional devices which will carry you through  the doorway &#8212; you just open it and walk through yourself.

Gnome  was such a door to navigate through my system. It was customizeable so  you could do anything with it. It was simple and was getting out of your  way when you needed it. It was stable and predictable. It was fast and  easy on eye. In short, it worked. And worked great.

And I hope  from my previous words and screenshots you now realise that Unity  violates just about everything which was great and worked in Gnome.

**Second**.  You shouldn&#8217;t pursue modern trends in UI design to make it better. The  fact that Windows has that fancy launcher doesn&#8217;t mean it&#8217;s good.  Honestly Windows launcher panel actually sucks seriously as it doesn&#8217;t  allow you to drop documents on items and it doesn't allow you to  customize it well either. As you can see, Unity gladly sacrifices  simplicity and power of Gnome for some trashy Windows junk just in order  to be &#8220;modern&#8221;. There was simply no reason to replace a working Gnome  environment with this Unity abomination except to please some Windows or  Mac OS fanboys. Look! What a sexy 3D panel we got now!

[*sighs...*]

[SIZE=3]Conclusion[/SIZE]

In  conclusion, I would like to say that Unity is a total abomination for  beautiful Ubuntu UI. It tries to solve problem which is already solved  and fails to do even this. In fact, it just breaks everything, puts you  in a cardboard box of it&#8217;s dumbness and punch you in face whenever you  try to fix something.

***

Meanwhile my Kubuntu download is  ready so thank you all for reading and I hope I convinced you to turn  away from Unity and save your nerves and health.

Ubuntu UI team, please, retire.

### Post by kkinder on 2011-04-29
Unity is a worthy foe indeed. When I approached it yesterday, I was but a young lad unaware of the power it holds.

Launch Firefox I did. Start a terminal too. Then, as my mouse reached for the new "global menu", trepidation gave me a shiver. Is it really going to save space? Is this the new way?

I forged forward, but as my mouse pointer reached the Bookmarks menu for Firefox, the menu ... changed. In morphed into the menu of the terminal emulator I travel with. But why? Was I being deceived?

Then I realized that I was carrying the amulet of Focus-Follows-Mouse. While Unity had let me pass carrying the amulet, I realized now it was a dirty trick: For as one reaches for the global menu with a mouse, the mouse passes over other apps, thus changing the global menu.

Determined to go to my Firefox Bookmarks menu, I tried moving my loyal browser toward the menu, over the Terminal. But what happened now? Firefox ... maximized. Entirely on its own! Who had cast this ghastly spell on my beloved browser?

I realized then that I was in over my head. I looked for the dockbar, but it had vanished in a mysterious puff of black magic. In a cowardly retreat, I pressed ctrl+alt+backspace, only to be confronted by ... it being disabled in X's configuration files. Switching to a terminal, I cast a spell: apt-get remove --purge unity.

It worked, for now. The dark arts of Unity were confined to a repository of the underworld, but only until it finds the strength to rise again. Legend foretells of a time, perhaps in exactly six months, when the Unity Dragon shall rise again, more powerful and with fewer features.

Will the world be ready? Only time will tell.

As for people that have problems that prevent them to be fully operative on a keyboard, the same or less number of clicks can take them to the application:

- I method: put an application in launcher and click on it. If you have many applications in the launcher, the applications on the bottom will "fold". However you can still see the icon of the application (even more so if you have visual memory of its relative position). If you move the pointer to the launcher around the folded icon of the application you want to open, the launcher will expand around that icon. Only one click to make it start.

- II method: going through the application icon. As before, on the launcher go onto the application icon, right click on it and the old categorization will appear. click on one category and the list of application in that category will appear in the "lens", finally you click on the application you want. Same number of clicks as in the old gnome.
