### Post by dburgan on 2011-10-20
Sorry if this is a tiresome topic, but I wanted to give my perspective on the new Unity desktop.

At first I was very resistant to it. I use Ubuntu all day long as my primary operating system and productivity is very important to me. Unity, as implemented in 11.04, was a big threat to my productivity, and it had me very concerned. As a result, I reverted back to Gnome 2 as the UI and ignored Unity.

With 11.10, I decided I was going to give Unity one more serious chance. This time I focused on configuring it as much as I could to match my needs and to make me as productive as I could be.

The result: I believe Unity just might be the right path forward.

However, I have several strong suggestions for Canonical if they really want Unity to win the battle for the desktop:

1. Stability. Unity has come a long way in 11.10 but the number of weird behaviors is still a distraction. Unity is aiming for a world-class polished user experience, and these distractions do not fit in to that world. If Canonical wants to beat Apple and Microsoft, they are going to have to get this cleaned up.

2. Configurability. Unity in 11.10 seems to be better configurable than in previous releases, but it is a far cry from the configurability of Gnome 2. Also, the configurability options right now are spread out across several programs and are not super user friendly. I recognize that this will take time and there are areas of configurability that Unity will never enter into, but there needs to be a lot more ability to customize than there is now. The desktop is an intimate user experience; users need to be able to easily tweak it to make it their own.

3. Performance. Gnome 2 ran fine on my old Pentium 4 machine, but Unity noticeably chugs behind it, even in Unity 2D mode. As Canonical tightens up the code, they need to do some profiling to tighten up the performance as well. Unity in 11.10 seems to perform much better than in 11.04; I am hoping this trend continues.

All in all, I am going to try to stick with Unity. I think it was a seriously gutsy move by Canonical to try to make Linux truly approachable by novice users, and I applaud Canonical for having the courage to make a risky bet. I think the battle for the desktop is far from over, but it seems to me that Unity is now a serious contender.

My $0.02 worth ...
