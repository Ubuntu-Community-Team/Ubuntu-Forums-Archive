"Hi. You're going to call off your rigorous investigation. You're going to publicly state that there is no underground group. Or these guys are going to take your balls. We cook your meals, we haul your trash, we connect your calls, we drive your ambulances, we guard you while you sleep.** Do NOT $%^# with us."

The important bit is in bold.  Gamers are the ones who install the software, run the cable, configure the routers, setup the servers and maintain the applications.

Do gamers represent a large portion of computer users?  Larger than most people think.

A majority?  Probably not if you count all the corporate drones who couldn't figure out how to turn on a computer if they were not told how 20 times.  Which is, incidentally, why I think corporate PCs these days are never shut off.  I digress.

My point is that the people who setup, maintain and operate the servers and networks the rest of the Windows crowd depend on are also the same people who would adopt Ubuntu (and by extension, Linux) if only it ran their games.  

The question isn't if that segment is the majority.  It doesn't take a majority to effect social change.  It only takes a sufficiently large minority.  So what you have to argue isn't if there's a majority of gamers but rather if there is the critical mass of gamers needed to effect that social change.

Given that the majority of IT professionals I have met in my career in the past ~15 years were also gamers I think that critical mass is more than achieved.  All that is needed is a trigger.  And since the applications have already had either counterparts made (OOo vs. Office, GIMP vs. Photoshop, etc.) or been made to run (Wine, Crossover Office) it certainly isn't the applications holding these people back.

What's left?

Games.

Can it be done without gaming?  Sure.  That in and of itself negates the notion that gaming is critical to success.  But if we qualify it with a timetable then gaming is absolutely critical.  

Is gaming critical to Ubuntu's (or Linux) adoption on an infinite timeline?  Nope.  It'll probably get there in maybe 10 years.

Is gaming critical to Ubuntu's (or Linux) adoption inside the next 5-10 years?  Absolutely.

Throw up a poll asking if people would dump Windows completely for Ubuntu (or another distro) right now if they had 100% assurance their games would run.  I'd bet cold hard currency that greater than 40% would say yes.  Critical Mass for social change is somewhere in the 10-15%.  ;)

### Post by Greyed on 2008-06-27
> **Vitamin-Carrot said:**
> Good business dictates that you target the largest market to make the most dosh, especially when it comes to games.

No, good business dictates you go to where you might get the most return.  Which is why in my message above where I describe the angle I push I point out that Linux is an **untapped market**.  In simple terms 10% of 100 and 100% of 10 are both 10.  If they expect to get 10% of 100 but can also get 100% of 10 then for their purposes *the markets are the same.*  They get 10 either way.  So why not develop for both and net 20?  

I know that is simplistic math but the problem is we don't have a reasonable gauge of the true size of the Linux gaming market.  Every Linux game sold to date has also been a Windows game.  All Linux sales counted as Windows sales because there was no way at the check-out stand to know if the gamer was buying for a Windows box or a Linux box.

Ironically the best way to gather this information is in the hands of a company that, while extremely popular with Linux users has said they will not develop for Linux; Valve.

People who use Steam as a way to get games are asked, once or twice a year, if they want to submit their machine's statistics anonymously.  This lets Valve know where their target audience is.  Furthermore this information is readily available for anyone to see:
[http://www.steampowered.com/status/survey.html](http://www.steampowered.com/status/survey.html)

For example we can see how many Steam users have down...er..."up"graded to Vista:
```

Windows XP		1,435,368	80.77 %	
Windows Vista		267,911		15.08 %	
Windows Vista 64 bit	47,687		2.68 %	
Windows 2003 64 bit	12,516		0.70 %	
Windows 2000		10,839		0.61 %	
Other			2,684		0.15 %

```

About 18% of people who have volunteered their stats.  

Now, I picked this metric because we could surmise that "Other" might be Wine users.  I am not sure how Wine reports itself to Steam.  But if that is the case this look exceedingly low until one realizes something.  Steam only asks once or twice a year.  You answer yes or no right there.  If the gamers are mostly in Windows when they play games and Steam is a system for delivering games then it stands to reason that many otherwise Linux users are going to report Windows in this metric.

I'd love to see Steam ask a second question and poke at the partition table and detect which partitions their users had installed.  These partition types are know and can be associated with different OSes:

[http://www.win.tue.nl/~aeb/partitions/partition_types-1.html](http://www.win.tue.nl/~aeb/partitions/partition_types-1.html)

Have the first metric show how many partitions the user has installed and then a second showing how many of each partition type, counting duplicates once per machine.  So if a person breaks out his /, /boot and /home partitions he doesn't show Linux 3 times, only one.  If you see 1 NTFS and 3 Linux partitions then you know that the person dual-boots into at least one flavor of Linux.  So the first metric would count them as 2 partition types and the 2nd counts them twice.  Once for Windows, once for Linux.

Obviously this metric would be slanted for Windows since, again, most gamer dual-booters are going to be in Windows when gaming.  But it will begin to give the one thing that is sorely lacking in any discussion about gamers and Linux.  Real numbers on Linux installs that could be used as gaming platforms.
