### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-10-08
Every distribution is different, and has its minor flaws. Of course, if you would really like more control over your Linux without a lot of breakage or too much learning (like Gentoo), Arch is a great option to get exactly what you want out of Gnome and nothing more.

Of course, it requires you to be willing to read a small wiki page and try to understand the basic structure of the elements a Linux distro is made up of. The power of Arch is that it's whatever you want it to be. In my situation, it's far more stable than an Ubuntu release. I've heard that rolling releases had more bugs, but it seems to be the opposite case for me, at least for now.

However, I think the leaps forward Ubuntu has made in the past couple years alone have been MASSIVE, and that we grow accustomed to these changes and improvements far too easily. Of course, Linux, Gnome, KDE, and associated applications have all grown and improved tremendously over that time period, as well.

I can recognize a distinction between Ubuntu from two years ago being a bit too rough to get along with for a Granny, to a point where everyone I knew felt comfortable learning it. I think it was somewhere in between 9.10 and 10.04 where this transformation occurred locally, where the number of Ubuntu users I saw and knew more than doubled.

Hopefully this trend continues, but as Linux continues to shock and awe new users, it will continue to diversify things. Outside of gaming and Adobe, NORMAL PEOPLE (the majority of computer users) seem to love and adore Ubuntu when they can get their hands on it, and if they dual-boot they always reserve Windows for second place.

It's like having a rotten old piece of clothing on with a vital organ attached, and only carrying it around because you need it, while wearing an amazing outfit outside of it. Windows has become the dirty, smelly sock of so many people I know that it's almost sad to watch how we've grown dependent on it.

However, I think that it would do everyone well to be a bit more open about the software they use- the more people use Linux with Windows or Mac, the more compatibility people will want, and the more apt newbies will be to try it and demand their software run in Linux as well.

The more open source you get a Windows or Mac user familiar with, the more they'll realize they want it.
