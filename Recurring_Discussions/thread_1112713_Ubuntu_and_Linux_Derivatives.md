### Post by IanShuttleworth on 2009-03-31
I've seen a lot of threads recently about people wanting to start a new linux or Ubuntu derivative. Although I'd typically encourage freedom and choice, I can't help but disagree with the people wanting to create these derivatives. Often the changes that people are proposing in their spin-offs can easily be accomplished by quick package changes. 

A couple years ago I saw this video and just now realized how it connects with this situation. It's called "The Paradox of Choice: Why More Is Less"
[http://video.google.com/videoplay?docid=6127548813950043200]("http://video.google.com/videoplay?docid=6127548813950043200")
If that video is too long you can watch the 20 minute Ted talk by the same guy: [http://www.ted.com/index.php/talks/barry_schwartz_on_the_paradox_of_choice.html]("http://www.ted.com/index.php/talks/barry_schwartz_on_the_paradox_of_choice.html")

Both of them are very interesting but I think the first is more in depth as its 64 minutes long. Basically he says that more choice is actually bad because it creates consumer paralysis. 

I think we should treat startup derivatives with more scrutiny and encourage other ways for people to accomplish their goals. Any thoughts on either the videos or linux/Ubuntu derivatives?

### Post by markbuntu on 2009-06-04
I watched that Ted talk that the OP is referring to and it makes some good points about the generalities of human behavior when confronting choice. The biggest problem is time. People have a limited amount of time to make a decision and never enough time to investigate all options and make the absolute best choice. There is always too much information and not enough time. There are two ways that people deal with this, either they are dissapointed or they are satisfied. The key here depends on how well their decision making procees meshes with their expectations. 

What it really boils down to is how people are presented with choices and how they react emotionally once a decision is made. To many choices can lead to a paralysis of deciding not to choose or semi-random chooosing. This can lead to dissapointment or satisfaction depending on the person. Many people will almost never be satisfied as they seek perfection and can never be sure they made the right choice. A minority will be almost always satisfied because they choose something that "is good enough for me" and don't look back.

The trick is to present choices with a reasonably limited list of pertinent information so that people can feel they made an informed decision in the short amount of time available to them to weigh the options. What needs to be done is to aim the perfectionist to their perfect choice while not overwhelming the good enough person

We already have the start of this. There are distribution remixes aimed at specific tasking like media production and netbooks which have widely differing expectations from their users but can be based on the same distribution.

I think the future is in a handful of popular general distributions, each with a slew of task/hardware specific remixes. People would be able to rapidly narrow the decsion making tree without compromising choice. 

I see this as especially critical in two areas, netbooks and media production. Netbook and laptop remixes would be better off focusing on one model or manufacturer so the hardware is guaranteed to work. This is not the case with most current netbook remixes because their resources are too scattered trying to be all things to all people.

The same with media production remixes. The needs of a graphics artist are far different than those of an internet DJ or someone running a video production studio.

So, the decision tree should be bottom up. Hardware/task/distro.

I just went through this with my Acer Aspire One. It took forever. But I eventually stumbled upon Kuki linux which is a Jaunty remix specifically for the Aspire One which I use when I travel for email, web surfing, music playing, etc. Jaunty would not support all the hardware OOB but Kuki linux was remixed specifically for the Aspire One with a kernel tweaked for the hardware but is also Ubuntu Jaunty with all the repos and security updates. It is wickedly fast and great with the hardware. I am currently wondering if installing Amarok will slow it down too much or screw things up. The good thing is that I can try it if I want to.

The point I am trying to make here is that having a zillion distros is not a bad thing as long as people can find one that works for them. What is needed is a field guide to the forest of trees whose trunks are based on hardware and tasking that reach into the sky of distributions.
