### Post by koenn on 2010-02-01
> **yester64 said:**
> Doesn't that constitute to 'i use linux but..'?
That was my whole point. sadly, linux lags :(

Depends from where you look at it. By the same reasoning, Windows lags. Or, it's OK, *but* ...

Simple example : I love the way Unix/Linux lets you mount anything anywhere, while on windows, you're always dealing with drive letters for partitions and drive letter mappings for network shares ... it's a headache to keep consistent paths across servers ... OK, you can work around some of it, *but* ... (etc)

It almost all of these cases, it comes down to picking the right tool for the job. I *could* use  gedit on my ubuntu desktop to write VB scripts [for the windows systems I manage], but I can't test them (there's that 'but' again) - they simply won't run. 
Is that a flaw in Linux ? I don't think so.

I also think a lot of this "buts and ifs" come from people who think Linux is "Windows without the viruses" or "Windows at no cost", and don't understand that it is actually
a/ a different operating system  -- so no, not all software is compatible with it ...
b/ a different licensing model (which makes redistribution of certain programs / addons /  codecs/ ...  problematic)
c/ a different development model -- and some of the quirks, e.g. in software delivery and installation or package management, ... , are directly related to that)
d/ ...
e/ ...

Technically, Linux can run on desktops as well as anywhere else : from the largest server cluster to the tiniest hand-held, and everything in between. 
There's probably some work left in the non-technical areas : maybe the concept of a 'distro' was OK for the 90's, but not for today. 
Or maybe the main thing that's lacking is support from hardware vendors (eg re. drivers, or pre-installed systems, or ...) and software vendors ( to get that small busisness bookkeping software ported to linux).
 
Or maybe the IT industry should realize that open standards benefit everyone in the industry in the long run. 

Or maybe I just don't know.

### Post by aysiu on 2010-02-01
The problem is that people don't know how to market Linux properly.

And by *marketing* I don't mean buying advertisements for non-existent products or obnoxiously evangelizing to your friends and family that Windows is a piece of crap and that Linux will solve all their computer problems. That isn't marketing. That's foolishness.

No computer platform is best for everyone's needs and preferences. Period. So for your product to be effectively marketed, you have to figure out what its strengths and weaknesses are, find the demographic that would benefit the most from the strengths and most likely overlook the weaknesses. Likewise, you have to really play up the strengths as strengths and downplay the weaknesses.

The real problem with Linux is that too many fangirls and fanboys make it sound as if Linux is great for everything. I've read too many times people saying "Linux can do **everything** Windows can." Uh, it can't. Sorry. But that's okay. It doesn't have to. It shouldn't have to. Linux isn't a drop-in replacement for Windows, and it shouldn't be marketed that way.

Take a look at all the "articles" and blog posts about the iPad since Steve Jobs announced it last week. There are a lot of people defending this product (that hasn't yet been released) even though it is lacking a lot of "basic" computing features. Why? Well, I'm one of the iPad defenders, and it's simply because the iPad is not marketed as a computer replacement. It is a niche device that is really good at a very few things.

If Linux is to succeed in the consumer market, it needs two things most of all (it could use a lot of other things too, of course, but these are the two most critical): [list=1][*]**Proper Marketing.** As previously stated, that means playing up the strengths and identifying good use cases for it. Email, Web browsing, Security, Photo and Music Management, the Basics. Simple. Free. Uncluttered. Linux. That's honest marketing targeting a very particular demographic. No false expectations about Windows-only software running on Linux. No claims of doing everything other platforms can do. That would be just one kind of campaign. You could have another campaign playing up the Ubuntu Software Center as the "app store" that pre-dated Apple's iTunes App Store and Google's Android Market.[*]**Properly Configured Preinstalled Options**. I want you to imagine you are an exec at Microsoft (Steve Ballmer, for example). All the major OEMs approach you (HP, Dell, Acer, etc.) with a proposition, "Hey, Steve. How about we stop selling Windows preinstalled? We're just going to sell Linux preinstalled computers and also provide our customers with a Windows 7 CD and let them repartition things, install the OS themselves, and add in all the drivers and stuff." Would you answer back, "Hey. No problem. Windows is so easy to use, it's a non-issue"? Maybe if you wanted Windows to tank! You can't simply, as some people who seem to think, buy a Superbowl ad (or World Cup ad or whatever ad at a sporting event) saying "Linux is cool!" or "Ubuntu is cool!" and expect that to make everything better. Yes, there are a few power users out there who might try Ubuntu themselves. The vast majority of computer users will be really confused. "Where do I get this Ubuntu? I download it? Then what?" If you want Linux to uptake, you need a product for people to buy. Android is taking off because it comes preinstalled on HTC and Motorola phones. It wouldn't take off if you had to buy a Windows Mobile phone and install Android yourself on it.[/list] Even then, with marketing and preinstallation, that's not enough. Linux cannot just come preinstalled. Right now, you can get Linux preinstalled, but... [list][*]It's not available in every country or even most developed countries.[*]The options that are available are few and far between.[*]The options from major OEMs (not mom-and-pop operations) are littered with "[We] recommend[] Windows 7."[*]The customer service for the major OEMs often do not even know they sell Linux preinstalled, and they don't know how to support Linux, even with a script.[/list] To make in-roads, preinstalled Linux would need a company behind it that is willing to match hardware to software, and market the hell out of Linux. It would be something like Apple, but not proprietary.

Everything works together. There's an eco-system. You buy the Ubuntu media player that automatically syncs to Rhythmbox. You buy the Ubuntu computer that automatically works with the Ubuntu external monitor. You wouldn't be locked into these Ubuntu hardware options, but they would be guaranteed to work.

Right now there is not enough focus in hardware support. One thing that works in one version of Ubuntu has a regression in the next Ubuntu that makes it stop working. Linux tries to support all hardware, and so it doesn't have guaranteed support on any given piece of hardware.

That's my rant. There has to be a well-thought-out implementation and campaign.
