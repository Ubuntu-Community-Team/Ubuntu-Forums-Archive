### Post by cprofitt on 2009-10-25
Windows and OS X have the ability to state market share based on the number of computers that are sold... how does Linux have its market share measured?

Linux does get sold on machines... but we also know that Linux more often is an OS that is put on a machine that was originally sold with one of the other two OSes.

-----
> When we look at desktop market share, we can see the following (as of statistics reported in October of 2008): Windows boasts the lead with 91% market share, down 5% from March of 2003. Mac holds 6% market share, an increase of 4% from March, 2003. Finally, Linux has doubled its market share since 2003 with a current 4%. While the Windows operating system does seem to be losing ground, it is Apple that must be credited with the loss. While the Linux market share has improved, it is still obviously not a major contender.
[source]("http://www.neowin.net/news/main/09/01/01/2009-linux-and-the-desktop")

No source was given, but this article states 4%. The issue is that is states Mac as 6% and I am not sure Mac has that share worldwide; these numbers may be for the US only.
-----
> So, depending on the figures you choose, the GNU/Linux install base on the desktop could easily be 2-5% by this line of reasoning. 
[source]("http://itmanagement.earthweb.com/osrc/article.php/12068_3818696_2/Linux-Desktop-Market-Share-Greater-Than-One-Percent.htm")

This article has some sources, but is still conjecture.
-----
> Market researcher IDC expects to announce within weeks that [Linux PC market share in 2003 hit 3.2%]("http://story.news.yahoo.com/news?tmpl=story&u=/bw/20040213/bs_bw/b3871118mz063"), overtaking Apple Computer Inc.’s MacOS. And the researcher expects Linux to capture 6% of this market by 2007. That’s still tiny compared with Microsoft’s 94% share.
[source]("http://blogs.zdnet.com/ITFacts/?p=5334")
-----
> I believe that the all the ZDNet figures were spot on. If anything, the Linux desktop market share has continued to increase and is probably currently at 8-10% and rising. Dell and the other PC OEMs wouldn't have invested in selling Linux pre-installed if it appealed only to less than 1% of the desktop market.
[source]("http://blog.linuxtoday.com/blog/2009/05/1-linux-market.html")
-----
> Numbers are likely to be much higher in places like Russia, India, China and Brazil -- "where government actively promotes the use of GNU/Linux," Pogson added -- but "NetApplications samples very little outside USA/Europe."

> In short, "I believe 2009 is the year of GNU/Linux and the share is around 10 percent," Pogson opined.
[source]("http://www.linuxinsider.com/story/67031.html")
-----

In the end I think it is impossible to get an accurate picture. Many Linux users run Windows in a VM so that they can test websites or study malware. Many linux machines do not hit the 'web' counter sites that are primarily based in the US. One of the features is freedom inhibits anyway of measuring deployment through big brother like features.
