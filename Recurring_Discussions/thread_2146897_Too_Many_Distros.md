### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2013-05-23
In my city, there is a Mexican restaurant on just about every other block.  Most of them serve pretty much the same food, which they probably buy from the same supplier.  One could argue there are too many Mexican restaurants in my city.

That would be a sound argument if all those restaurants were owned by the same chain or even franchises of the same company.  But they aren't; they're (mostly) each independently owned and operated.  Their goal isn't to promote the eating of Mexican cuisine, it's to make money selling a type of food that's popular in this town.

Distros are much the same.  Linux distros aren't all made by some big corporation that wants to promote the use of Linux.  They are made by individual projects, each of which has its own goals and ideas about how a distro should be made and supported.  

People are going to make distros; some for fun, some for profit, some because they perceived a need that wasn't being met and tried to meet it.  The ones worth keeping will stay, the ones with little real value will fizzle.  Good ideas will make their way into other distros.

Anyone who doesn't use Linux because "there are too many distros" didn't want to use it that badly in the first place.

### Post by vexorian on 2013-05-28
All of this comes from mistaking Linux (the kernel) with an operating system. When we compare Ubuntu and Fedora, we do not have different Linux distributions, we have different operating systems, period. There is not one "Linux" operating system we all want to see succeed, there are tons of different operating systems, each with their user base. And it is great this way.

Imagine what would have happened if some years ago, the "Distribution quantity police" would tell Canonical: No , we don't need your new distribution. There were already more than 6 distributions back then. Red-Hat, Debian and Slackware  were doing just fine. A new one was created though, and it had a great effect. 

Reducing the amount of Linux+GNU-Based Operating Systems is the wrong fix to problem that is being solved in more effective ways through initiatives like the Free Desktop project. What we need is not to have less distributions, but to have better interoperability and standards that they can follow. So that when I make my own new distribution or Desktop Envrionment, I can follow those standards and things can work together.  Proprietary software may love hegemony. But free software works better as various solutions and choices working, interacting and being constantly recombined together.

Some people want others to make the choices for them. Those people should stick to windows, their lives will be a lot more comfortable.
