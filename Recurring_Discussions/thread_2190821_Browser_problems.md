Sorry, but I've spend quite a few hours on this one and read a very large number of posts, which are plethoric because these difficulties are long-standing.  Some posts describe my exact case (Firefox and Chromium just disappear after a few seconds). You can't expect every user to provide detailed diagnostics in that sort of situation, where hundreds of people report similar but slightly different failure modes, and no reliable solution has yet emerged.

Sometimes, you can blame the computer and not the OS, but that probably doesn't apply to the majority of reports of browser trouble. One unsolved hardware case I had (and has been reported) was that Toshiba Satellite portables seriously overheat with Fedora 16. They didn't work too well with W7 either, until someone suggested deleting Toshiba's own crapware (thermal regulation seems to depend on software with these machines). There was no solution for Fedora & I wasn't tempted to try an Ubuntu flavour on the Satellite.

That shows how hard it will to find a broad and general solution to the numerous major & minor difficulties we encounter with Linux distros. M$ has more resources for fixes because of their established market position. Apple just charges a lot of money for hardware/software bundles.

Unfortunately, browser unreliability is a really big issue that needs a higher priority, because it makes an OS unusable for most people, even those motivated to make the move to more interesting solutions. It can't be treated as just another bug of the kind one has to get used to.

I don't think we disagree that the majority of users havn't had too much trouble with browsers. Unfortunately, for several years now, an unknown number of people have been unable to use ubuntu at all (on their particular computer) because none of the available browsers works or is sufficiently reliable.

A secondary difficulty is that (as far as I'm aware) ubuntu doesn't have automatic means of getting feedback, nor the resources to fix minority problems like this. With M$, you would just wait and do nothing until a bug (like the one I mentioned that affected 1% of Vista users) gets quietly fixed in an update. Several contributors said that Firefox has similar arrangements, which didn't work with the browser bug because the application crashes before the invitation to send feedback has time to appear.

As a result, ubuntu is perhaps over-dependent on more or less formal bug reporting, which can be intimidating for ordinary users. With this incident, I just established that the bug has been widely discussed over the years, and decided it would be a waste of effort to investigate my particular instance of the problem. Perhaps the forum moderators could be a bit more flexible when deciding priorities, and keep a count of the number of users who say they have been affected by a bug that has already been reported, without expecting everybody to play the proverbial geek.

Finally, apologies for the colloquial English in my last sentence. By 'this one' I meant the bug in question. I wanted to say that it should be taken more seriously than it has been up to now, because it obliges those who are affected to abandon ubuntu Linux entirely. Most of us lack the time to try different distributions, versions and kernels  This can be damaging for the reputation of Linux, even for people for whom it meets all their software requirements.
