### Post by MrE on 2008-11-27
I've been using KDE4 (4.1.x, that is) for a while now; on my desktop since the release of Intrepid, and on my Eee PC 901 since the release of Mandriva 2009.0

I think that KDE4 is a quite a step back, for all the exciting new features it brings. Far too many things are either broken or have simply removed because the equivalent KDE3 feature hasn't been ported to KDE4 yet. Surely such a major version release should give the user more, not less?

This is a bad choice (dare I say, attitude?) on the KDE4 team's part. It is clear that KDE4 is an unfinished product that should not have reached gold status yet. In its current status, it is a Release Candidate at best...

Don't get me wrong, I'm not dissing KDE4, I'm sticking with it despite it's shortcomings. I just think that the developers were too eager and too much in a rush to release KDE4. As a result it has lost a lot of its wow factor. It should have been the 'Leopard' or 'Vista' release of the Linux world - instead it's more of a 'nice try'


For the same reasons, Kubuntu and Mandriva (the only distros I'm aware of that ship with KDE4 as the default desktop environment) should not have made KDE4 the default. Here too, it seems that the developers of the aforementioned distros are too eager to ship 'the latest and greatest' and have lost track of what users really want: stable, working software.

Kubuntu, i.m.h.o. is still playing catch-up to Ubuntu; it's getting better, but it's still lacking the strong identity that Ubuntu has got, and the early move to KDE4 doesn't really help with that.
Ubuntu looks like Ubuntu, there's no mistake there, but Kubuntu looks like, well, many other KDE based distros. Shouldn't the Kubuntu developers focus more on bringing it more in line with it's big brother instead of trying to be the flash kid on the block? 

What do other people think?

### Post by awkward42 on 2008-11-30
KDE4.1 just isn't ready yet for day to day productive use, so much so for the way I work that I've spent an entire day reinstalling 8.04 and getting KDE3.5 set up again how I like it.

My biggest issue is with file management. Konqueror has been deliberately crippled to force people to use dolphin as their file manager and konq solely as a web browser.
However, Dolphin is missing key features that Konq used to have such as the integrated terminal. The one it has doesn't follow the directories clicked on in the graphical tree, and when you move or resize the window it doesn't follow. 

Also, I do a lot of inputting of IPA characters. In 3.5 I set up uim so I could press ctrl+shift+i and type X-SAMAP to input IPA, then press ctrl+shift+i to return to standard input. This was removed by the upgrade process, and I couldn't find a way to set it up again before I ditched KDE4

Then there is the issue of the lack of a simple drag and drop from menu to the desktop / panel (right click, chose to add to panel, close the menu, click on the kidney shaped waste of space, then move the icon to where you want it, then click the kidney again so you can actually use the panel. 

The "screen as a desktop metaphor" might be "decades old", but it works. The KDE 3.5 panel and menu may be "a relic of the 1990s" but it works well. I'm not really bothered about snazzy desktop effects or how streamlined my menus appear - they may look pretty but they don't help me get my job done. Sure form is important, but function must come first. KDE, like Windows Vista and much of modern society is suffering because the designers decided that form was more important.

The fact that in almost every discussion any negative comment about KDE4 is treated with a dismissive "wait for 4.2" should be a clue that it is not ready for the mainstream. It's a preview, proof-of-concept release that is probably ideally suited to those who like to use cutting edge software for the sake of it and those who are diligently finding and fixing bugs. 
The vast majority of computers users though want to (shock horror) *use* their computers, not just admire how pretty the software looks.

When 4.2 is released I will read all the reviews of it I can find before thinking about "up"grading.

### Post by zealot12 on 2008-12-10
Hello all. I am a relative newbie to Linux coming from Mac OS X Tiger. Now, let's not get it twisted: I didn't migrate by choice. My Ibook conked out on me after several years of grueling use and I simply can't afford a new right now on a student salary lol. Initially, I needed something that came as close to a Mac OS as possible on my old Intel Celeron PC to use full-time. 

The KDE4 environment, at least from general screenshots, seemed to grab my attention superficially more-so than the GNOME desktop. It struck me as being graphically like a Mac. The first thing I automatically liked about Linux right off the back were options. I am *all* about the options! Well, I ended up trying the Kubuntu 8.10 distro first, because it seemed to be closest to my system requirements as far as KDE is concerned. I am rolling with 256mb ram, 2.8ghz proz, and 80gb hard drive with RealTek on-board drivers. It ran Windows XP pretty nicely, but I realize this setup is not on par with average system specs sold today. Mandriva, also using KDE4,  had these astronomical system requirements that made me feel like it would drastically slow down my system. In any case, I downloaded the LiveCD version of Kubuntu and this is where it got really interesting. Now, as a relative newbie to the Linux side of things, my thinking was that it was kinda like Windows, kinda like a Mac, somewhere in between. I just figured the install would be as seamless as the download/burning process. Not so. First off, Kubuntu just would not run on LiveCD on my comp at all. After the 10th time, I went in on SafeGraphics mode and it finally booted...albeit *very* slowly. However, despite this slowness, I understood that running from a CD wouldn't be an accurate portrayal of speed as is would be running from the hard disk. 

At this point, I was sick and d*mn tired of XP, so I said "what the hell" and just installed Kubuntu on a whim basically. I had my important docs backed-up on a disk (from my Ibook), so nothing on XP warranted any concern from me. I tell you, it must have taken me an entire weekend to install Kubuntu, set it up the way I wanted, AND tweak it to where it would run somewhat smoothly on my system. I mean, I was really getting angry with this thing too. But, it was just so pleasing to my eyes that I couldn't help but stick it out, keep playing with it, and let it marinate for awhile. By Monday, I was *ok* with it. I mean, it looked great! But it was still sluggish, Plasma would crash the system periodically, some functions appeared broken--incomplete if you will. Several times when I tried installing packages with Adept, the screen would lock up and I would have to reboot. I probably rebooted my system 40 times over the course of two days. So yesterday, I decided that maybe my system just *ISN'T* ready for primetime. KDE4 is seriously for computers who can handle it, or for serious patient people who happen to have a really cool computer. 

I finally buckled down and installed Ubuntu 8.10 to try it out, despite my dislike of how corny GNOME looked, at least on screenshots. BOY was I shocked at how much better it was for my life! If I had installed that desktop in the beginning, I would be up and running already. It is easier on my system, easier to get up and running, and is fairly stable. It's quite snappy as well, especially if you use Seamonkey for a browser instead of Firefox. I don't quite like how it looks honestly, but sometimes you really can't judge a book by it's cover. 

So wrapping it back around (I know I'm long-winded), what do *I* think of KDE4? I think...and I have learned...sometimes things that look good on the surface aren't always so good underneath. I can't really blame the lack of functionality and stability I came across on Kubuntu, since it would seem like a distro is as good as the particular desktop environment that's used, right? And again, those who have better more powerful specs than I may have a different experience with it. But as of now, GNOME to me is the greatest thing since sliced bread.
