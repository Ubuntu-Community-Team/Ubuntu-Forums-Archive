### Post by QIII on 2010-09-10
What can one do that the other can't?  Frankly, on the desktop there are some small things each can do that the other can't.  The casual user may never encounter them because they simply would not use them anyway.

On one extreme end, you have to ask yourself why more than 90% of the world's supercomputers run Linux. You might ask why 60% of web servers run Linux. Not the sorts of things a casual user needs to worry about, but certainly in the category of "What can Linux do that Windows can't?"

They are both computer OSs that do a wide variety of things.  They simply do them differently with different structures, file systems, apps, etc.  They get stuff done for you.  They are tools.

The comparison you have asked for is probably without value.

What can an orange do that an apple can't?  Make orange juice.  But if you happen to like apple juice, the orange is not for you.  A matter of taste.  Functionally, they are much the same - their purpose to you is to provide nourishment.

I have four primary reasons for using Linux. 

1.  Open source software.  I can take part in development, bug reporting and improving.  If I wish, I can download the source and make it do what I want it to do, provided I abide by the licensing agreement.

2.  Free as in freedom.  Tied to #1, but with at least this addition -- I am free to shape my "user experience" to suit my tastes rather than taking my Ford in any color I want, as long as it's black (or, in this case, blue).

3.  Free as in beer.  You can't beat something that works and doesn't cost you a dime.  You don't have to buy the upgrade every time Windows X comes out. 

4.  Windows, in and of itself, is a capable OS.  I don't slam it.  It works for millions (billions?) of people and suits their needs.  There is nothing wrong with that.  People should certainly use what works.  What I do find terribly wrong are the business practices, hegemony, monopoly and arm-twisting of its purveyor.  I am disgusted by the fact that one corporation wields so much power over an entire industry that it can demand that software developers and hardware OEMs kneel, grovel and prostrate themselves before the altar in Redmond in order that they may soil themselves in fear while they present their mewling supplications, begging to be granted the "Made for Windows" stamp of approval so that they can sell the fruit of their labor and survive as vassals owing allegiance to a monarch as the cost of continued good favor.

Note that mine is an indictment of a corporation, not of any person in particular.  Bill Gates is a genuine visionary.  He's a legendary philanthropist.  And, much after my own heart, he's a geek.
