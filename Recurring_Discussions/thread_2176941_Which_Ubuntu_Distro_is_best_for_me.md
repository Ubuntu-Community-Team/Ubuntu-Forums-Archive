### Post by johnlittler on 2013-09-26
What is best really varies from user to user.

A distro wich one person regards as 'fast and stable' may be seen as 'slow and unstable' by another person.

Do you still have any Linux system installed?

If you have no Linux system installed, I suggest you install Ubuntu 12.04LTS, which has support until 2017.

Once, you have done that you can install alternative desktop environments simultaneously. By doing that, you can decide what feels right for you. Try the desktops of Gnome, Gnome Classic, Xubuntu and Lubuntu etc.

Incidentally, I installed Unbuntu 12.04LTS and then installed Gnome Classic and then removed all the Unity system and 'zeitgeist'. Compared to Unity on 12.04LTS it is fast  and stable.

I do not know how technically minded you are, so please forgive me if I say, "By the way, on the stability point, many problems are caused by too much 'tweaking' without real knowledge." If a new user leaves the system alone, it usually proves stable. Before you make any changes, please double check on the forum. I should not preach really. I have tweaked until I broke in the past.:D

### Post by DuckHook on 2013-09-26
My bad.

You did already mention that you are no longer using Ubuntu, so all of the commands I mentioned are academic at this point (although it will still yield helpful info if the problem recurs on a new install).

On the question of which distro, there are many options that might suit you and I agree with johnlittler and his suggestion to just try them all. Your specs may just be on the shy side to run full-fledged Ubuntu (Unity is a graphics pig), in which case, you may wish to give Xubuntu or Lubuntu a try. If Mint is running well for you, there may be no pressing need to switch back either.

On the subject of tweaking, I am of two minds. It does break systems, so not a good idea for production systems. But part of the fun in Linux is poking and prodding it, taking it apart, figuring out what makes it tick, so if not a production machine, there's a lot to be said for playing with it till it breaks. No better way to learn it, actually, than to trying to fix what one breaks.

Last but not least, I note that you mention a built-in Intel graphics chip and the desire to run Steam. Your graphics subsystem may be too underpowered to do modern gaming and you might do better investing in a stand-alone video card. This could end up speeding your system up too. Generally, games are heavily dependent on GPU and RAM. Beef those up and you should be good to go.
