### Post by aysiu on 2010-02-17
> **arnab_das said:**
> 
Are Linux users partly to blame to Linux's lack of popularity? Erm, do we secretly want linux to remain as an OS for the geeks? There are *some* Linux users who want Linux to remain out of the hands of "the masses."

It is not a universally shared sentiment, though, especially not in the Ubuntu-using communities.

That said, the most important factor in desktop/laptop/netbook Linux's lack of popularity is the barrier to entry... i.e., there is one.

Let's say I want to buy a Windows computer. I can go to the computer store and get one. I can go to an office store and get one. I can buy one at pretty much any online computer store (or even some non-computer online stores). There are a variety of models, and customer service folks will answer my questions so that I can purchase the Windows computer that's most appropriate (or most expensive, depending on the person "helping" me). If I buy a printer, multimedia device, scanner, monitor, or any other peripheral, it will either work with Windows straightaway or include a CD with Windows drivers I can install to make it work. Pretty much any commercial software available will have a Windows version.

Let's say I want to buy a Mac computer. It's a pretty similar situation to what I described above. There won't be as wide a variety available and not as in as many outlets, but I can pretty much find Mac OS X preinstalled if I want it, and it'll ship to pretty much any country I'm in. Not every peripheral will work with Mac, but there is a considerable percentage of peripherals that do *and label themselves accordingly*. A lot of commercial software is available for Mac *and is labeled as Mac-compatible*.

Now, let's say I want to buy a Linux computer. Hm. Not so easy. In certain countries, there are a handful of vendors who will sell you Linux preinstalled. The smaller vendors will offer you no opportunity to try the models out in person, and the pictures online tend not to be very detailed. More importantly, the Linux computers are often priced higher than the equivalently-spec'ed Windows computers. If you happen to stumble upon a Ubuntu-preinstalled Dell computer available in your country, the website will warn you away quickly from Ubuntu back to Windows, and if you talk to a Dell customer service representative, it's likely that rep will have no idea Dell even sells Ubuntu-preinstalled computers. Most hardware peripherals do not indicate on their boxes whether they are Linux compatible or not. Most commercial software available in stores are not Linux versions (even if Linux versions exist).

"Install it yourself," you say? Well, there you go. That's my point. The barrier to entry for Windows and Mac? Virtually none. Find a Windows or Mac computer. Buy it. Barrier to entry for Linux? High. Find an obscure vendor... maybe... maybe not... find a mainstream vendor who actually supports it... again, maybe, maybe not. Or install it yourself.

It's no big mystery why the marketshare is so low.

Meanwhile, Linux mobile phones are flying off the shelf left and right, because the barrier to entry is low, and HTC is not saying "Oh, we have these Linux models, but we highly recommend Windows Mobile... the most important thing you should know when buying an Android phone is that it is not a Windows Mobile phone." The companies that sell Android and WebOS phones actually (*gasp*) want those phones to sell!

### Post by ve4cib on 2010-02-17
> **aysiu said:**
> [[...]](http://ubuntuforums.org/showpost.php?p=8842082&postcount=36)

I would agree completely with this statement.  If we extrapolate a little bit to the point where we can say "higher barrier to entry equates to higher nerd-factor" then we can see that some Linux distros certainly have a much higher "nerd-factor" than others.

Ubuntu (and its official derivatives) are probably among the lowest "nerd-factor" distros; they do a lot of auto-configuring for you, install standard, commonly-used applications out of the box, and generally try to make it that all you have to do is install the OS, reboot, log in, and get to work.  Minimal barrier to entry with distros like that.

On the other extreme you get things like Arch, and other build-it-from-the-ground-up distros.  Yes, Arch provides an easy-to-read beginner's guide.  But it still has a vastly higher barrier to entry than Ubuntu does simply because of the extra work required to install everything you might want.  And if you've never, ever used Linux before then:

1- The command-line is intimidating.  Yes, once you get used to it it's not bad, but it *looks* complicated to the beginner.  Those hard-core computer geeks and hackers in movies use command-lines, and they obviously have lots of experience with computers.  Mr John Q Public therefore assumes that high levels of experience are required to use the command-line, since only highly-experienced people ever use them in movies.  Regular people use GUIs.

2- The command-line is less intuitive.  I want to open a file with a GUI, so I double-click on it.  I want to see what's in a folder with a GUI, I just look at my file browser.  With the command-line you're throwing away decades of research into human-computer interaction, and forcing the user to remember arbitrary words and symbols instead of actions and visual cues.  Yes, the command-like gives someone with experience more control over what happens, but for someone who's never touched it before they quickly feel overwhelmed and confused.

3- Installing from scratch assumes you know what you want to install.  Beginner needs "graphics" for their computer, so that they can see windows and use their mouse.  We know that that means Xorg and a window manager, probably a login manager, and a handful of applications to do stuff with.  Mr Computer-Illiterate has never heard of Xorg, has a vague idea of what "apt-get install" does, but has no clue what Gnome/KDE/XFCE/Fluxbox/LXDE/Matchbox/etc/etc... are.  And then if he wants Windows 7-style effects he needs video drivers.  His Windows or OSX machine came with these pre-installed and pre-configured.  Ubuntu does a good job of suggesting things, or installing open-source drivers and configuring them.  But how is a beginner sitting at a command-line supposed to know he needs FGLRX?

Obviously some do-it-from-scratch distros do a better job than others at auto-configuring things, and giving you tips on what to install.  But it's still a huge barrier to entry compared to other systems.  And that, to the general public, means it's intended for geeks and nerds.

Finally, one very important thing to remember about these kinds of debates, especially here, is that we do not represent the general population very well.  We're all Linux-users (or almost all of us are anyway), and as such there are probably very large segments of the population who would write us off as "computer geeks" immediately, without even stopping to consider anything else.
