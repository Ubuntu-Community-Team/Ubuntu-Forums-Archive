I'm going to say... It depends.  

Ask yourself if you're ACTUALLY willing to give up Windows and everything that goes with it.  

I say this because if what you want is the Windows experience without Windows ("...will it open Windows docs?  Can I play Windows games?  What about running Outlook? Can I keep Internet Explorer?") then you are going to be sorely disappointed.  That's a big "if" and a question only you can answer.

### Post by Giant Speck on 2009-03-31
I'd say if you are having frequent computer problems, problems that are so bad you have to ship your computer somewhere for it to be fixed, then your problem probably doesn't have anything to do with Windows Vista at all.  It's probably a problem with your hardware configuration.

Regardless, if you are interested in trying out Ubuntu, or any other distribution of Linux, you should start with a LiveCD to get a feel of the operating system.  See how files are stored.  See what programs are installed by default.  Get a feel for the differences in the graphical user interfaces.  When you are confident enough using the LiveCD, try dual-booting Ubuntu with Windows.  That way you can learn more about Ubuntu, while keeping Windows for programs you cannot use in Ubuntu.  If you truly feel you can use Ubuntu without relying on Windows for any programs, you can wipe out the Windows partition and install Ubuntu over it.  On the other hand, if you end up experiencing major problems with Ubuntu, Windows will still be there on the other partition and you can wipe out the Ubuntu partition instead.

When migrating to Ubuntu or Linux in general, it's best to do it in a slow transition.  Just jumping into Ubuntu without knowing what you are doing can lead to serious problems.  Ubuntu isn't perfect, and it certainly isn't like Windows.  Some people switch to Ubuntu completely, thinking it will do everything that Windows can and will feel just like Windows.  Those people end up facing the truth that Windows and Ubuntu are nothing alike.  Some don't like the change and switch back.  Others embrace the change and continue using Ubuntu.

It's your choice.  Make it wisely.

But also, I still recommend getting your hardware problems fixed.

### Post by ursus262 on 2009-04-10
Well, there is the old saying: "jfdi".  I got fed up with Vista on my new Dell laptop when I bought it new two years ago.  I now use Kubuntu and, to be perfectly honest, I don't regret it for one moment.  Here is how I approached the change - an approach that you might feel comfortable with:

Firstly, I downloaded the ISO image and burned it to a CD from the Ubuntu website.  There are some instructions on how to do this, and it is relatively straightforward.

I then took out the hard drive with the Vista installation on it and put it in a safe place.  I went out and bought a new drive from the local PC store and put it in my laptop.  In most cases, it's just a case of removing a screw, and the drive simply slides out.  Be careful though, not to drop or damage the drive and put it in a safe place.  On no account touch the edge connectors, or you could cause static damage.

Restart the laptop and insert the CD and shut it down again.  Then, connect the machine directly to your router/modem with an ethernet cable, restart the machine and let it boot from the CD.

Simply follow the instructions, let it automatically update from the internet through the ethernet cable and you're done!

And, if it all goes wrong, don't worry, just put the vista drive back and you're back to where you were at the beginning.  However, I am confident you won't need to do that!
