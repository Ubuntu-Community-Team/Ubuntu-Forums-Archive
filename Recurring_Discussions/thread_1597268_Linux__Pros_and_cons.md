### Post by kaldor on 2010-10-15
Cons:

- ATI support is frustrating

- Where's my creative software? GIMP and Inkscape have *nothing* on Adobe CS and Adobe Illustrator.

- Printing is a major downfall on Linux. My HP and Brother are both hit/miss. Neither will work on Ubuntu.. but the HP works fine on LMDE. The Brother will only work with OpenSUSE because even after installing drivers, it just prints off gibberish errors.

- Oh crap! A kernel update just bricked my system!

- Mostly lacking modern, up to date games. Sure it has loads of games.. but what good is that when 25% of them are Quake III clones with graphics from the early 2000's/late 90's? 

- Skype support for Linux is frustrating.

- Choice is both good and bad. Some programmers will think, "Do I package an RPM? A DEB? Both? Will I be able to make it work on Both Ubuntu and Debian? Or both OpenSUSE and Fedora? What about CentOS?"

- On Ubuntu I tend to get frustrated due to being unable to install/remove software the way I want. Why must I drag out a big chunk of my OS if I remove firefox? 

- It's annoying trying to find Linux computers. System76 and ZaReason are not cheap. No mainstream brandnames market *good* Linux-based systems. You can save money by buying a Windows-based PC and then removing Windows and using Linux. Even then, you just gave money to MS when you won't even use their product.

- Server =/= Desktop. Linux excels in both at varying areas. There's a reason why Linux is dominating the server market; it really is "tried and true". On the Desktop, however, probably due to lack of mainstream support, it can be very frustrating depending on your needs.... such as printing.

I'd also like to mention that the terminal isn't really needed anymore on Ubuntu/Mint. Your average user should be able to get by just fine without typing commands (I don't know why people whine about copy and pasting, though). But that hardly matters if they can't get their current printer/camera/whatever working on Linux when it works perfectly fine on Windows and OS X.

Point being, not everyone wants to fight with their machine.

On that note, I use Linux on all but the Mac. Why? Because it works for me.. unless I want to print a document. Printing works on and off depending on distro and distro's version.

Linux is not flawless.

-- Yeah, I don't get the printing hangups with Linux. Although I can install all my printers and make them work, rendering is very slow compared to Windows drivers. This is true for all my printers (Brother, which has the best support, HP, and others). While everything prints fine, it is so slow that for large jobs I do indeed use Windows printing. (The gibberish problem happens in both windows and linux for me from time to time, and is a function of the communication handshake having not been established correctly, usually from an inpatient user turning things on and off, plugging and unplugging, etc.)

-- Yeah, kernel updates are a bear. Computer developers (including kernel developers) tend to have the latest equipment and make changes that aren't compatible with a lot of older equipment. They then release the changes into the wild, bricking a lot of older equipment. I have had a kernel update freeze my system 4 times in the past 3 years, requiring me to revert to an older kernel. 

I no longer allow automatic updates on my production systems. I have a parallel test system and a production system. All updates are done on the test system before they are allowed on the production system. But I think that is good practice for any computer system anywhere, if you are a scientific, corporate, or critical systems user.

Having said that, Ubuntu does retain older kernels to be available in the bootup sequence so that if a kernel incompatibility occurs, it is not that hard to revert to the previous working kernel.

BTW, this problem is not at all unique to Linux. Windows and the Mac have the same problem -- their major updates are just far less frequent.

-- As to games, it is true that game developers develop for the widest audience. Since Windows is on the majority of computers, they develop for Windows. But games are having a sea change, currently, being developed in cross-platform methods. Because the Mac OSX and Linux share similar structure (they are both basically *nix OS's), there is a reason (since OS X) to develop for a combined platform.

There are a number of pretty modern games, like Urban Terror and Sauerbraten, that are not from the 90's. Further, there are modern games being released for Linux which are not free. IMO this is a good trend. Not everything in Linux has to be free. Good games sometimes require a good revenue stream. These games are the same quality as their Window counterparts.

--Skype works fine for me in (K)Ubuntu for everything except desktop sharing. I use it routinely from my desktops and from my laptops (with built-in camera/mic). 

--Packaging is not a huge barrier. I worked with a developer who used CentOS (a RedHat derivative) and used .rpm packages. I sent him a Debian/Ubuntu developer and in 2 hours they had a .deb package. Big deal. This is a non-issue. It has to be said that the biggest Linux base in the US currently is RedHat (/Fedora/CentOS), not Ubuntu, at least in the corporate, scientific, and military/government communities. I think it is critical that attention be paid to Linux apps being available in both .rpm and .deb packaging, but, again, this is really a trivial endeavour. 

In the long run, the co-evolution of RedHat/Fedora/CentOS and Debian/Ubuntu/Kubuntu in the Linux world will determine the userbase of Linux in comparison to Windows, so having compatibility between these two distro ecosystems and an easy transition between packaging systems for developers is definitely crucial.

--Yeah, software removal is finicky. It has to be said that the Ubuntu developers work on this continually and recent versions are far superior at this than older versions. The poster appears to be a long-time Linux user and is including his opinions that probably have accumulated over a large number of years. Things do evolve rapidly in the Linux world, though, and this is an area where I think developers have improved significantly.

Also, I have to give a tremendous amount of kudos to the Ubuntu MOTUs in making apps from Gnome (Ubuntu) seamlessly available to KDE (Kubuntu) and vice versa. That effort is huge and very, very worthwhile. I am primarily a KDE user (Kubuntu), but love certain Gnome applications. 

Oddly, they so far seem to have less success in making RedHat applications work well (like the Plymouth splashscreen). But, as with all software anywhere, it is only a matter of time.

-- Linux computers? WTF? All computers can be Linux computers. Linux users do on average tend to be a bit more adventurous and intelligent about computing, and delight in managing their computers in an intelligent way.

Linux users don't tend to be the 5 year old kid painting with crayons or playing Rugrats games (as shown on one Windows commercial) or grannies or college kids wanting to send their pics from one place to the other. Linux users like to install their OS everywhere. I have not found a single computer I can't get Linux to run on well (although sometimes I have to use different distros for the oldest computers). Heck, I have a self-contained Linux OS on my USB drive. In contrast, there are many, many computers I can't get Windows to run on. Mac? Hah. 

-- Server does not equal desktop? While in the past this was true, the evolution of Ubuntu has seen all server functions easily installed in a desktop version. This is especially true from Lucid onwards, where the tasksel selection is pretty impressive (one step server installations).

This is one reason the command-line interface terminal will not and should not die. Scripting and the command line are important to efficient server function. It is difficult to run them from GUIs, although many functions can be accomplished with a GUI.

Yes, you can do most things from the GUI interface in Ubuntu (and the pretty derivative Mint), but power users don't. The GUI is to get casual users able to do most everything that is routine.

My biggest gripe with both Windows and Mac, in fact, is how hidden (by layers of GUI) they have made the things I really want to customize and automate in them. It takes me 100 times longer to find out where I need to customize things in Windows and Mac than it does in Linux. 

Whenever someone says they only want a GUI interface, they are usually an amateur user. A GUI interface can always be written for a command-line process, but doing the opposite can be troublesome (for anyone except the original developer).

BTW, the first GUI interface was X Windows. It preceded both the Mac interface and the Windows interface, and, in fact, it is argued that both OSs copied the X Windows interface from the early 80s (although my memory puts the Apple GUI first). X Windows always provided a GUI overlay to command-line commands (originally for Unix systems).

-- Driver support is indeed more widespread for Windows systems. That's because there are more Windows computers sold off the shelf to consumers. (There is no question that Gates/Allen were brilliant in copying Japanese marketing techniques from the 80s).

But with the arrival of huge hard drives, it is now trivial for computer retailers to put extra partitions on the hard drives so that both Windows and Linux can be installed on the same system. HP already does this.

They may not want to advertise this very loudly because they would anger Microsoft, which provides significant incentives to computer manufacturers to put Windows on the systems. But I think you will see this increasingly, so that the easy installation of Linux (such as Ubuntu) is facilitated by just popping in the disk. (Currently there are a few sometimes difficult hard drive preparations required before installing multiple OSs on the same computer). 

As computer manufacturers become more savvy and receptive to enabling multiple OSs on the same computer, then Linux will be much more widely adopted. When it is widely adopted, accessory manufacturers (cameras/iPods/MP3 Players/whatever) will be quick to be certain they have good drivers for Linux.

It should be mentioned that Google uses Linux, and all Google products are Linux compatible. Sony is bringing out a Google TV, which is Linux based.

Google is a major driving force (think Android and stuff) in making Linux mainstream in regards to devices. My commercial DVD player and HDTV both use Linux internally. Because of this, they are able to play everything I put in on them (in regards to DVD, audio, and video formats, that is).

The number of devices with embedded Linux is growing exponentially. If you want another example, look at the Indian planned mass-production of Linux-based smartphones. (Nokia has a Linux-based smartphone, too, for example.)

San Jose-based Echelon is a company that is engineering a Linux-based smart power grid for the energy sector, to be more secure and efficient. So Linux devices are not just small, they are large scale as well. It is already well known that Weta Digital used Ubuntu servers creating the movie Avatar, and their production team uses Kubuntu computers.

Linux is used by NASA, the US military, the French and German governments, universities, banking systems, many supercomputers, and on and on. It is not merely a hobbyist's OS (lol).
