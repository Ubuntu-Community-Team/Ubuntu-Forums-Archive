### Post by MikeTheC on 2008-12-09
I'd used RedHat 7 and RedHat 8 for a time. By the time I "went back" to playing around with Linux (I'd also used some other distros, too), RedHat had made the transition and now Fedora Core 1 was out.

I really liked the visual styling of RH8/FC1 and FC2, but it started to loose some of it's luster for me as it went forward. But, that being said, I still liked Fedora.

I'd agree with the earlier poster that, at least "back in the day", it seemed like a lot of software had issues and wouldn't run without help. The problem is that, in approaching Linux as a "normal computer user", I was in no way prepared to even figure out why the app was breaking, let alone how (specifically), and forget about figuring out how to fix it.

Of all the distributions I'd used up to the point I first tried Ubuntu, it was always the case that at least some number of apps didn't work at all (they would abort on launch). Now, don't get me wrong, the "community approach" is fine and all, but I felt that if someone was going to go to the trouble of putting out a "professional" distribution, then at a minimum all the associated apps should at least *open*.

I've never had the degree of problems with Ubuntu that I've had with other distros, period. Obviously, newer releases work better and more flawlessly than older ones, but Ubuntu at it's worst has still given me a user experience better than most others at their best.

This all being said, I do have the Nadoka engine installed, because there still are a few stylistic differences on Fedora that I really prefer.

And isn't it the great thing about Linux that you don't have to "throw the baby out with the bath water" when there's some detail you don't quite like?

### Post by gnomeuser on 2009-05-09
Fedora has a lot of good stuff in it, except their Mono stack really it is very well maintained and they do important work to further Linux. You get rapid evolving software but surprisingly it doesn't seem to affect stabililty much, in fact I often find that things work nicer in Fedora simply because they keep even the stable releases updated so you get all the latest fixes.

I love yum and RPM is a much nicer format to work with as a package maintainer (but [still](http://davidnielsen.wordpress.com/2009/01/09/doing-the-right-thing-by-default-or-why-rpm-and-fedora-are-doomed/) [not](http://davidnielsen.wordpress.com/2009/03/07/a-follow-up-on-why-rpm-deb-fedora-debian-and-all-classic-packaging-systems-are-doomed/) right). 

There is much to love about Fedora, as a user you get lovely doses of dew fresh software. Every package is very actively maintained and often by people who are or works closely with upstream. The maintainers care which is important, no issue really gets to linger unlike in so many other distros. Fedora very often aims for solving a problem the right way, even if that solution is hard, will take time and require things to be broken for a while. This earns them more hate than they deserve for it, Linux is a better platform for their boldness and if they didn't do it either we would be at a standstill or someone else would have to. 

The downsides definitely include a some what poor and incomplete Mono stack, also you don't get much support outside of ix86/x86_64 (PPC is officially supported but it is largely a lie these days). There also isn't much direction outside here and now, no grand scheme for what we need to fix - where we want to go and what the vision is for the future of computing. The target audience and what products to offer are also ill defined, leading to some confusion in the way Fedora evolves. 

The governance model is firmly a meritocracy, but there is a sense of elitism and entitlement that can scare new and old contributors alike. It is very political, unless you enjoy that sort of thing stay a user. You will often see lenghty flamewars on seemingly odd topics (like if a cursor should blink), this is just the nature of Fedora.

I think Fedora is a vital part of the Linux ecosystem, many of the maintainers are great people who care deeply about their work. That shines through in the distribution. As a user you find a home that is well cared for, it comes with everything you can, legally, ask for (and then some care of rpmfusion.org). You get to be part of shaping Linux and you get early access to some exciting technologies.
