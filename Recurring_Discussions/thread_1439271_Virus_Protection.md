### Post by k3lt01 on 2010-03-26
To further add to what Lisati has said.

There are, from what I can tell, 3 trains of though regarding Ubuntu (Linux) and virus'.
1. There is No such thing as  Linux virus in the wild. The wild being out of control so to speak.
2. Virus are Windows problems and Windows users should protect themselves.
3. On the OFF chance a Linux virus does come about Linux dosn't allow them to do anything without your permission.

Now there are things called cross platform virus' and they come via applications that are made to be used on multiple platforms, e.g Windows, Mac, and Linux. The thing to remember is, 99.99999999% of anything that can possibly affect Linux must have your permission to do so. In other words, if you don't allow it it can't do it.

Before I leave you with that to consider I will also leave this with you. Anything in your /home directory is theoretically fair game to the outside world if you open it to the outside world. Be careful what access you allow to your machine either in person or by sharing over a network.

### Post by HermanAB on 2010-03-26
OK, some anecdotal evidence:
I have been using Linux since 1995 and have never had a virus or malware on my personal machines or on any of the others that I manage - and at one point I managed about 3000.

I have repaired about 5 web servers that got infected with spam blasting programs.  Those machines did not belong to me and the desperate owners were referred to me to see whether I could help.

These web servers were all infected due to poor password management.  In other words, some user thought that it was a good idea to use a 4 character password on his account which allowed a FTP or SSH brute forcer to get in.

Finally, there are many complaints on these forums from users that got their machines hacked after they installed a VNC server with either a weak or no password.  So don't use VNC for remote management.  Use SSH with a proper password and you should be fine.

### Post by SonicSteve on 2010-03-26
I would like to add a small point of clarification about what the "wild" means.

While yes it can mean "out of control" in the sense of computer security it really doesn't mean that. In our sense it means that a virus is actually out infecting computers and a real threat that you could actually run into. The "wild" then more refering to the internet. There are viruses that are infecting only small numbers of computers that would still be considered to be in the wild even though they are far from "out of control".  

My advice,
1. Stay up to date with your software. 
-Windows is terrible at doing this, it only updates microsoft programs well. The rest are left to fend for themselves.
-Linux updates every program on your system via the update manager.
2. Have AV installed, but don't depend only on it to protect you. Like others have said, you are the best defense, for linux and windows.
3. If possible only install programs from Ubuntu's software centre. It's constantly scanned and maintained. Once you leave the sofware centre for programs you be giving permission for someone else's programs to install on your computer. Then ask yourself this question, "Do you really know who they are and what they are offering?" Most times you'll be OK in Linux, in the windows world this is far from true.

The other nine were good advice, but this one is less true.

If your computer is not listening to incoming ports, then there's simply no need for a firewall. (I think this is the fifth time I've explained it to someone today... I wonder why there's such interest in firewalls today?)

Better advice would be "Don't run any software that opens incoming ports unless you really have to - and if you do, then make sure it is secured tightly".
