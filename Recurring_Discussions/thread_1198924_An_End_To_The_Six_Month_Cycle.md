### Post by gnomeuser on 2009-06-28
> **days_of_ruin said:**
> Updates break stuff. If you want stability rolling release sucks. Also most users don't want to have to worry about updating everything.

Typically though users do want:

1) whatever hardware they buy to be supported which means either updates to the kernel or costly backports to the supported kernel.

2) leaf applications to be up to date, you need at least some level of application upgrades during the lifespan of a release. Something like Banshee e.g. makes frequent releases and each bring new functionality the users are generally excited about and it is the only way to get a supported version to them. The same goes for things like the browser.

There is though a large mass that can easily stay pretty dormant for great lenghts of time, there is very little reason to upgrade gcc or glibc for major bumps in an existing stable cycle e.g. 

I think either road is wrong, it's a fallacy to think that just keeping version numbers stable will mean stable software. Bugs tend to get fixed as development goes on and very few projects have the manpower to keep a stable branch that gets backported fixes but no new features (and this completely ignores cases where bugfixes are dependent on new features). The best thing a stable version gives you in knowledge of your current bugs and no easy, inexpensive (developer time wise) way of fixing them. 

It is also a fallacy currently to think that bumping even tested updates constantly will result in a stable OS. While bugs do get fixed and software does tend to improve stability over time there will be new bugs and experience tells us that we cannot hit every single one in testing. Additionally there are problems with resources pushing what can be hundreds of megs of updates each week, and users nearly never have a consistent system since something is bound to change with every day as updates are pushed. (See Fedora who manages to do this and surprisingly the result is quite good aside for making peoples pipes sweat)

I think we need a hybrid model of some sort, but what that model will look like I am currently not sure. I have though been giving it a disturbing amount of thought.

### Post by MaxIBoy on 2009-06-28
The main issue I see is that by keeping all kinds of legacy software lying around, lot of users are forced to use old software. For example, when the bugs in Intel's graphics drivers are fixed, anyone who installed Jaunty will need to either revert their drivers, or perform a risky dist-upgrade operation. Dist-upgrades invariably result in breakage of some sort (at least, that's my experience.) I'm prepared to deal with that, but not everyone would be. 

Also, a lot of software developers are being forced to use old versions of libraries, or they're being prevented from using recently-developed libraries. This creates a vicious cycle: software developers know that most people don't have the latest version of SDL, for example, so they write for an older version. Because of that, there may be issues with introducing the library later on. And on it goes. There will be an even-bigger problem when drivers start getting OpenVG acceleration.

### Post by kaitwospirit on 2009-06-29
I don't think that the problems with our release cycle are with the 6-month releases and a separate LTS version per se, it's how these things are marketed.

EVERYONE is encouraged to get the latest version of Ubuntu. For many of the people who gather here on the forums, that's probably what we want. We want to be on the bleeding edge and enjoy the fun, and don't mind a few bugs or issues. We like the new shiny. But for most people, it's too much. I'm in college and the Windows leg of our public computers still all run Internet Explorer 6! That's too extreme and IT should update them to something now that we're two version numbers behind, but as long as there aren't any security holes, most people really don't need or want the latest version of software. What they want is something that just works, and won't expose them to too much risk. 

There's a sweet spot there: if your software is just too outdated, especially now that the importance of interoperability is so high, it won't "just work," and an outdated browser will expose you to too much risk. On the other hand, though, most of our six-month releases won't "just work" for everybody and breaking your system certainly qualifies as a risk. For a lot of us on the forums, the balance is for six month releases. Others need longer, but don't really get that Ubuntu offers that option.

Canonical does not make it clear that most ordinary computer users who are not concerned with new technology and shiny should be on LTS releases. Perhaps to remedy this we should call the two kinds of releases something different and play up our six-month releases less and our LTS releases way, way more, but I'm not sure of a good way to label this: altering the way we do version numbering could help.
