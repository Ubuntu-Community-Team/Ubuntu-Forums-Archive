### Post by Tuxoid on 2008-04-07
After browsing through the forums and looking at a variety of opinions of, Linux programs, the core Ubuntu family of distros, Desktop Environment, debates on Terminal vs. GUI support options, attitudes on what makes Linux desktop ready, and ideas of why Linux hasn't been widely adopted, I have decided to take a stab at a thread basically acting like a real-time status of where the Linux desktop is at.

I am not adding a poll to this thread as people can have very different thoughts that may not necessarily fall into a single answer (then everyone keeps answering 'Other').

We are definitely in a different world than we were even in 1998 (when the last major desktop environments today made their first major release). Today, Linux is considered an intelligent choice for businesses from what I have seen. Gnome and KDE are now at a point of near seamless desktop environment integration. I guarantee many average-users would be shocked to know that Gnome and KDE are basically built on a system that by default is just a terminal window with nothing else.

Drivers are getting major attention despite the difficulty in making drivers without specs from the hardware manufacturer. You basically couldn't run Linux on a laptop in 1998, and although it's not perfect, it's more than 'just possible'. Device manufacturers are now actually making drivers for Linux when apparently there was 'no market'.

We are seeing a shift in support, although, Linux is needs to push its competitive power by not just being 'equivalent', but better. It's a good initiative to make Linux free software that is free of charge as well, but with the unfortunate evolution of the proprietary software market in the 1990's, buying software came to be considered a better idea than getting software made by a set of volunteers for free. Free and Open Source Software is a strange concept for many. Many users believe that can trust a company more than a set of volunteer programmers. This is the unfortunate way the software industry has skewed the truth.

In the end, any piece of software developed by a global community has had more thought put into it than a very market-calculated piece of software. The community consists of humans, not the corporate wall.

Why I feel Linux is having difficulty is because it isn't as focused as it could be at competing with proprietary vendors with free software. What if we could stop calling certain Linux software 'alternatives', or 'equivalents'. What if, regardless of price differences, OpenOffice was easier to use, and had more features than Microsoft Word right out-of-the-box, without the need to install anything extra.

It seems to me sometimes that Linux is merely trying to keep up with proprietary systems, opposed to being better in means of a desktop platform. Once I have learned XLib programming, I intend to do something major that I hope will simply blow people away. Until then, I only have a concept in need of completion.

I see a moral advantage to Linux, but average-users have been brainwashed into believing proprietary is better. Linux has the power to disprove this and free the users that have been dominated by proprietary vendors and give them back their free speech on what their software should be.

### Post by Tuxoid on 2008-04-10
I think that the most difficult thing about Linux is getting people onto the system.

1) Not everyone knows of an alternative to Windows and may not even know about Macintosh (let alone Linux).

2) People are comfortable with Microsoft Windows (most people that is) and any issues it may have. To them, it is a predictable experience.

3) Even though this no longer the case realistically, Linux is still seen as a complex, technical OS. People may know what Linux is, but may not have a clue on how it's operated.

4) Linux defies the conventional design of an Operating System. Linux alone is just a kernel with some GNU utilities. Whereas Microsoft Windows and Mac OS X is everything up to and including the Desktop. Linux can be packaged in such a way that it is as cohesive, but this has caused difficulty for people who are looking in at Linux as this has spawned countless incarnations. This allows for a more personalized experience and I believe the distro-based design of Linux is better for the user in the long-run, it's not what their used to. That doesn't mean that Linux should consolidate as that would ruin it's future. A consistent core and open (i.e. the Linux kernel) that would support multiple forms of hardware on the Desktop, but still having the ability to change and model the user experience based on the distro's maintainer. Therefore, the level of compatibility sky-rockets, as the user experience can be different across many distros, but all of those incarnations of the user experience can have the same low-level software support.
