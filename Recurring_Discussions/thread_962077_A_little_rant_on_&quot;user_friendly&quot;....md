### Post by SomeGuyDude on 2008-10-28
I was about to post this in the Other OS Talk thread on IMagicOS, but I realize it's a lot more general.

I noticed that the IMagicOS page keeps talking about how "hard" Ubuntu is ([http://www.imagicos.com/why4.html](http://www.imagicos.com/why4.html)) specifically referencing Synaptic and the shell. This got me thinking about this crap with being "user friendly" and why it's messing things up.

In this case, it's clearly training users to fear the command line, as though messing with the terminal is some mystical force that a "good" system wouldn't require the use of. It's saying computers are really, really, really hard to use and you shouldn't have to even have the faintest grasp of what's happening. Just click on some colorful buttons.

This is how people break their computers and then get freaked out when things don't work. This is why so many people on Windows and OSX do such mindblowingly stupid things on their systems and then try and get help with stuff like "I thought setting folders as hidden would make them more secure so I hid my C drive now my computer won't start up help me" (yes I've seen that). If they had a basic understanding of their computer, that wouldn't have happened.

Just because something isn't so piss-easy that you could do it even if you were a 6 year old with head trauma doesn't mean it's bad. It's like driving a stick versus driving an automatic. Takes some more work but in the end you have a lot more control over your vehicle.

Now, I'm not saying that things should be as hard as possible and that being intuitive or simple is necessarily bad, but I really do believe that on some level people should "learn" their computers. Instructions should be clear and the methods should make sense, that much is obvious. I just cannot wrap my head around this idea that any steps breaching beyond "click button for thing I want done" is too hard for people.

There's this idea that in order to use Linux, you have to be a computer nerd, and that irks me. When I first tried Ubuntu I didn't know a root directory from a reese's cup. But I took a little time and worked through it, found out the system was fantastic. Yeah it was different and there was a little learning curve, but in the end it was worth it.

I'm not an advanced user, I still describe myself as casual end user. Any nerdiness came about because I spent a little time learning how to use my system, not because I'm some massively competent power user that had the skills needed to sink into anything.

I guess what I'm saying is that by trying to make things more "idiot proof", it leaves people flailing around blindly, clueless when something doesn't go right or doesn't work the way they expected it to and absolutely flailing if they have a need that isn't explicitly covered by the system.

It's not that I'm being, like, elitist or anything. Really, I think people are a lot smarter than they get credit for and all these "even a fetus can use it!" systems are CAUSING the situation just as much as perpetuating it. Maybe I"m wrong. I dunno.

### Post by etnlIcarus on 2008-10-29
I've sort of half-read every post in this thread so I figure it's time to add my own opinions:


I don't expect that people should 'know' computers before using them and I don't think that should be an expectation other people hold, either. There's no reason a, "user friendly", interface has to be completely useless in educating the user, nor does that UI have to be counter-productive by teaching the user bad habits.

Sadly, the above expressed opinion is an *ideal* and never in my life have I known an *ideal* to be a *reality*.

Ignoring the command line for a moment, I direct you to the utter abortion of the filesystem:

Most people pronounced the filesystem, "unusable", a good 15-20 years ago. However, rather than fixing the filesystem and making it human-readable, internally consistent and reinforcing the logical conventions of system design, they instead focussed their efforts on encouraging the user to circumvent the filesystem:
- recently used file lists
- 'media managers'
- shortcuts to default folders at every corner.

As a consequence of the above, even people who aren't completely hopeless with computers often don't know how to copy a word .doc without opening up MS Word and re-saving the document because they have NFI (no f***ing idea) where their files are and think the only way to get to them is through MS Word.

----------------

The objective of anyone designing an application, suite or environment for end-users is to simplify the tasks they have to perform. This is great; you shouldn't need a degree in computing to do something. However, often, this noble philosophy becomes mutated into the objective of obfuscating the *actual* system into a fantasy system, where magic is possible and the natural, physical laws of *userland* (intentional pun) are inconsistent.

To continue with this analogy; when the user can cast spells and be magically transported to what they want to do, they never learn how to walk to their objectives. When they find themselves in a situation where those spells are unavailable, they don't know what direction to walk in.



P.S. I'm guilty of using the listed, "spells", on occasion. -_-

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2008-10-31
The tool vs. appliance comparison hits the nail on the head for me.  If you could nail down exactly what someone wants to do on a computer to a finite and very specific list, it would be trivial to implement a rock-solid device + OS that would to it all.

But what people want to do keeps changing.  Every time a new product or version of a product comes out, people act like every feature is a must-have.  

And this is where trouble comes in for Linux.  Because people start confusing what you *can* do on Linux with what you *must* do.  

For instance, vendor XYZ puts out a new whatzit, and decides only to support Windows and OSX.  Some clever linux hacker figures out how to get it working with his linux box, and either releases code or a howto.  Then Joe Newbie who bought a whatzit and uses it with Windows and thinks its the bee's knees decides to try Ubuntu.  He expects whatzit to work perfectly, because that's a big part of why he owns a computer; so surely every OS supports whatzit.  But when whatzit doesn't work out of the box, someone points him to the hacker's howto, and he goes on a rampage about how hard Linux is and how it should have GUIs or automatically detect the whatzit or what have you.  

So never mind the fact that Linux, and the Linux community, have enabled you to use things never designed for it; Linux (not XYZ) gets blamed for being hard to use.

Case in point: earlier someone was complaining about compiling software in Linux.  I hear this all the time.  Since when do you *have* to compile software in Linux?  Last time I checked there were several huge repositories of precompiled binaries a click away.  

"But I needed to run foonutz 2.3 to support my XYZ whatzit, and it's not in the repo!"

So the problem isn't that Linux is hard, the problem is that the author of foonutz 2.3 hasn't packaged his software for you.  If there's a program on Windows that comes as sourcecode, or as a pile of .dll and .exe files that you have to manually copy to the right folders, does that mean Windows is hard to use?  Or does it mean the author of that software needs to get off his lazy rump and write an installer?

Back to the appliance vs. tool comparison:  my fridge doesn't have an ice dispenser.  If I try to retrofit one myself, and it breaks, is it a bad fridge?  Or is it my fault for doing something to the fridge it wasn't designed for?

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2008-11-04
> **Spark* said:**
>  you are saying "computers should be harder to use, so people are forced to get smart about them", which is ridiculous. You can get tired about this all you want, this is simply not going to happen.

You're saying this as though the desire to make interfaces "harder", as you put it, is purely arbitrary, and that there is no cost to abstraction.  And I don't mean monetary cost, here.

The other day my seven year old son and I were waiting in the car, and I was showing him how the controls of a car work.  I showed him the gas and brake pedals, and he wondered why they weren't labeled as such.  It got me thinking about this thing again.  Would there be value in labeling the pedals in a car?  Would it make a car easier to use?

And what if we abstracted the gas and brake pedals more, called them 'go' and 'stop' pedals instead.  How about combining them into one control that "goes" when you push it and "stops" when you let up?  Would this be an "easier" interface?  Why bother the driver with having to know that one pedal puts more gasoline into the engine, and one pedal applies a braking mechanism to the wheels?  Does the driver need to know this?

Whenever you abstract a mechanism or process, you have to provide the abstraction with information that the user would otherwise have had to provide.  You either do this through making assumptions, or by trying to automatically determine information.  Now the question is, what does the user do if your assumptions are wrong?  Or what if your automatic process fails in some unforeseen way?  This is the cost of abstraction, and every time we want to simplify an interface, we have to take it into account. 

Do we need to leave grandma at the terminal prompt?  Perhaps not, but we need to count the cost of abstracting everything down to a handful of icons.  What happens when Grandma installs a bad firefox add-on, and needs to launch in safe-mode?  Do we add another button?  How do you anticipate every possible situation Grandma might encounter, and distill that down to a simple interface?  

I'm not proposing an answer, but my point is you can't simply make this a black&white "easy interface" vs. "arbitrarily hard interface" issue.  "Easy" in the sense you describe is only easy if you don't need to do anything it doesn't offer.

### Post by sicofante on 2008-11-06
> **lykwydchykyn said:**
> Sure, but the real question is -- how is it currently not easy enough for grandma, and what do you propose changing to make it so?
I can think of at least four areas where Ubuntu's UI design is lacking: integration, consistency, feedback and naming (or "geek language enforcement").

**Integration**
How many address books needs an average Linux user? Let's see: Evolution (or Thunderbird, etc.), Pidgin, eFax... Mac users need just one because the system, by design, enforces the use of a central address book. That doesn't mean "use MY address book", that means "use ONE address book". In other words: design address books to talk to other apps and design apps to listen to address books. Then choose whichever address book you like better, and rest assured the other apps will understand it. (LDAP is a standard for doing such a thing.)

Inserting a spreadsheet or an image into a text document can be painful. Is it because application designers don't follow any rules or is it because there are no enforceable rules? I'm afraid it's probably the latter.

Another example is password management: again, you have a Gnome Keyring which is useless in most non-system (non-Gnome) applications. I don't know if it's because it's hard to use for developers but the fact is every application needing passwords uses its own password manager. The lack of integration is prominent in Linux and Windows.


**Consistency**
We keep double-clicking on icons because that's a tradition created by the Mac's single-button mouse. Right mouse button made select-before-action useless but we still use it. That's OK (although the web has shown single clicking is the way to go...). Now Nautilus offers the chance of single clicking on icons to launch/open apps/docs. However its behavior is very inconsistent. Because it's not built into the API (it's just a Nautilus patch), most apps will still require double-clicks. The user is left with no clue about what needs to be double-clicked and what needs to be single-clicked. The same happens with most system tray icons, only there it's much worse since the three mouse buttons can behave differently for each icon.

Other examples of inconsistency are shown by the use of different libraries to build software: Qt based apps behave quite differently from GTK++ apps from Java Apps. They even look differently. A fundamental piece of software in Ubuntu (OpenOffice.org) doesn't follow desktop-wide standards and even renders text differently from the rest of the system. 

Having more than one system application to do the same thing hurts consistency too. Should the user install via Synaptic or via Add/Remove? You will probably recommend Add/Remove to newcomers but they will find it too lacking in short time. It's been suggested to make Synaptic appear after clicking an "advanced" button in Add/Remove, but that hasn't happened and so far they are two different apps doing essentially the same, which only adds to confusion.

Consistency is probably the number 1 issue in Linux desktops. It's poor in Windows too, it's much better in Mac OS X.


[B]Feedback
[/B]Far too many actions do not have a proper feedback in Linux desktops, including Ubuntu. Double-clicking an icon on the desktop has no guaranteed feedback and too many times the user is left without any indication that the click was succesful. No busy cursor, no sound, no nothing.

You don't get any sound or other signs of having successfully plugged in a new device like a printer, a scanner, a pendrive or other peripherals. (I know, sometimes you get some feedback, but too often you don't, which takes us back to the consistency problem.)


[B]Naming (or "geek language enforcement")
[/B]Linux developers have failed throughout the years to "translate" their jargon into ordinary people's language. To "mount a volume" means nothing to no one but a computer geek, while "enabling a disk" or "activating it" would have meant a lot. Showing the disk disabled (by means of a darkened or greyed-out icon) and creating a simple action to enable it (right click->enable) would have been enough. But we're still saying "mounting" and calling a disk "volume" (I know the difference, but is it meaningful to the end-user?) and must explain to new users that this is geek-slang for "activate" or "enable". To make things worse, latest Intrepid Ibex's Nautilus adds an eject icon to every disk in the system. It's pretty obvious you can't eject an internal hard disk drive, so why did the designers choose that poor analogy for "unmount" which in turn means "disable"? What a mess. This kind of mis-naming things appears everywhere ("daemons", "root", "super-user", "proxy" are just a few examples). Making a system intuitive is much easier if the system uses the language of the user instead of forcing the user into learning a new language which usually is totally unnecessary.


All these issues make it not easy for Grandma or for my lawyer. While I believe Ubuntu is heading in the right direction, saying it's as easy to use as can be (or as Mac OS X, for instance) and there's little to improve is being really blind. I hope these few examples show that there's a large room for improvement and also that this improvement doesn't mean that the user must read the f... manual or learn the innards of computers, but that the developers must still study a little on user interaction design.

Making a system intuitive is much easier if the system uses the language of the user instead of forcing the user into learning a new language which usually is totally unnecessary.


One must be careful to determine what is slang or needless jargon and what is actually a meaningful technical term.  Putting everything in "layman's terms" only creates another layer of uncertainty in a system.  Sure, there are plenty of examples of poor choice of words, but fear of showing the user a word they don't understand can only contribute to confusion.  Have you ever gotten one of those awesome errors on Windows "The system encountered and error."?  Or, "There was a problem, and the system was shut down to prevent damage to your computer"?  How useless. Sure, normal folks hate getting a screen full of "gobblty-gook", but at least you can paste that gobblty-gook into a forum or email and get someone to tell you what it means.  I like a system that tells me what it's doing precisely and without mincing words.

Another good example: the root user.  There is a user, UID 0, and its name is "root".  So what's wrong with saying "root user" or just "root"?  Is it jargon, or just accurate?  A lot of people want it to say "administrator" or "administrative user".  Well that's fine, until mr. user goes looking to log in as 'administrator', or sees a mysterious user account called "root" mentioned in a log file.  Then it becomes confusing. Where is "administrator", and who is "root"?  

Now, root is a silly, unintuitive name for the user account UID 0.  It would probably have been better to call it administrator or system or something similar.  But it's there, it's done, and at this point it would cause meaningless confusion to change it.  Kind of like "the c: drive" on Windows.  Silly, pointless convention, seeing as most people haven't even got an "a: drive" any more, much less a "b:" drive.  But there it is.  And what kind of havoc would be wrought in the windows universe if they changed the primary hard drive partition to something else?  

I will agree, though, that no one should ever use "super user" or anything else other than "root", with descriptive text when needed (e.g. "root (the administrative user account)").  Super user is slang and has no place in error messages or documentation.

### Post by tsali on 2008-11-12
In general, most people can observe a haft or hammer and genralize their experience with other objects in the real world to application to those objects.

This is what I think is really meant by "user friendly"

Is the tool, in this case, the DE, designed to allow the most number of users to generalize common knowledge to its use? For example, a user might expect an icon of a DOOR or GATE to represent an object that will "lead somewhere". Even the general language of computers applies this principle...a "pipe" directs output from one command into another.

It's interesting that the computer itself has introduced metaphors for which there are no counterparts IRL but have become part of our common experience nonetheless. How many "windows" IRL have widgets in the "frame" that manipulate them (few). Yet, today, most have no issue with the min, max, close widgets.

So, "user friendly" does NOT necessarily refer to that tool or system that is most efficient or even most effective; it refers to that tool or system that is most ACCESSIBLE. It does NOT refer to tools beings "tailored to preference" as the four handled hammer suggests.

As with many tools, highly skilled artisans can become very efficient and even effective with them, but that does not make that tool "user friendly".

While CLIs are very efficient for trained individuals, they are the equivalent of a "dark room" for new users. They can eventually find their way around by stumbling over things or following the voice of a helper, but GUIs "turn the lights on".

Of course sometimes the "generalization" goes the other direction as well. My five year old niece placed a CD into a toaster because she saw an icon representing that on her mommy's computer...
