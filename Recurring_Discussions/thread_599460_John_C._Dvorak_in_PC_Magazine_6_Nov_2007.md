### Post by justinmiller87 on 2007-11-01
John C. Dvorak mentions Linux over two articles in PC Magazine, in a negative, but constructively criticized manner.

The first is in his top ten bright ideas. Number three (right after a MS split) is the Unification of Linux where he writes:
[quote="My Top Ten Bright Ideas"]More than anything else, the industry needs a unification of the Linux operating system, with one purveyor that can control the details of the operating system in such a way that you do not have all these confusing distros. The current flavor of the day is Ubuntu. In a year or two, it'll be another distro. Years ago there were good reasons for this internecine battling - a form of tribalism - but they are no longer valid. Linus Torvalds could make this happen overnight.[/quote]

The second is in his Inside track, where he writes:
[quote="Inside Track"]The market share for operating systems is still led by **Windows XP, at 83.5 percent**, followed by Windows 2000, with 3.9 percent. The Mac OS comes in third, with 3.7 percent, followed by Vista, with 3.5 percent. Linux as a desktop OS is still moribund, ad 1.3%, the same as **laggards** such as Windows 98.
This does **not bode well for Linux on the desktop.** There needs to be a rigid Linux **standard or reference platform.** Too many people have too much gear that simply doesn't work with Linux; either no drivers exist or the gear is just incompatible. People do not want to put together an elaborate system only to find that their printer doesn't work or that their system doesn't recognize the hard drive. Some sort of **seal of approval list** that covers the top 10 Linux distros and what works with them should reside in one place. Right now this information is scattered. It needs to be **sensibly consolidated** in one site with someone  handing out "Approved Stickers."
Of course, the likelihood of this happening is zilch, since there are **too many anarchists** in the Linux community. Not that that's a bad thing. (Emphasis the author's)[/quote]


What are your opinions? Personally, I don't think we need three-hundred-fifty different Linux distros, but maybe it does need to be brought down quite a bit. I'm new to Linux, and as Ubuntu is the only Linux based OS I've ever successfully installed after attempting RedHat and Suse, I'm obviously pulling for Ubuntu. A top ten list of Distros does sound like a good idea, but maybe that's still too many. Maybe a top five - one for paid commercial solutions such as RedHat and Suse, and another for distros like Ubuntu, and other completely free counterparts.


References:
Dvorak, J (2007, Nov 6). My top ten bright ideas. PC Magazine, 26(21/22), 58.
Dvorak, J (2007, Nov 6). Dvorak's inside track. PC Magazine, 26(21/22), 59.

To see John Dvoraks dream happen, we would have to kill Open Source as a development model.

The very *essence* of Open Source is a kind of anarchic freedom.

Don't like the way someone has put *x* piece of software together? don't like it's interface? Here you go, go and modify the source code and release it as your own if you want.

The reason there are so many distros is because of the simple fact that the linux community is full of tech geeks each with their own little dream of what an OS should look, feel, and behave like.

Yeah, Linux is anarchy of a sort. But it's a good anarchy, and I like not having to conform to someone elses rules, and being to run the world around me the way **I** like it.

### Post by az on 2007-11-01
Sure, Dvorak is an old crank.  So what?  That's his caricature and it serves him well.

I usually agree with what he says.  But not on this.  Ubuntu will topple windows without being binary-compatible with anything (including itself) because **binary-compatibility is not the answer.**  Of course, if that's all you've ever know, of course you would think that it's important.

Linus Torvalds could change that overnight?  Sure he could.  But he has adamantly refused to do so for years.  A stable kernel ABI is a detriment to development.

Unification is nonsense.  There is really not much difference between Gnome running on Ubuntu and Gnome running on Novell.  There is no difference between KDE running on Kubuntu and any other distro.  The end-user doesn't need to reach under the hood any more.

The only exception is his point about hardware drivers.  Sometimes, you need to tinker to get your hardware to work.

But that has nothing to do about the choices made in designing the desktop.  That has to do with the vendors themselves choosing to write free and open drivers because they will sell more product that way.  

It's a chicken and egg situation.  If no one uses linux, nobody will want to spend their time writing drivers for it.  If hardware doesn't work, no one will use Linux.   Fortunately, the hard work of chipping away at the trouble spots is already underway and doing quite well, thank you.  

Just think of how poor driver support was just two years ago.  Not to mention five years ago.

Bottom-line:  When a new hardware manufacturer looks at Gnu/Linux, they look at Ubuntu.  There has never been a distro which mobilized a community without taking it over (perhaps that's the secret?) as much as Ubuntu.  

And all of the work that has been done here is completely transplantable.  If a new printer starts to work in Ubuntu, it doesn't take long for other distros to get it too.  If Ubuntu ceased to exist tomorrow, all that work would still be there.  And that's thanks to the development model that Dvorak disagrees with?

In fact, the very thing that makes the distros separate, allows them to reuse code and therefore provides a great deal of unity.  That's why Gnu/Linux supports more hardware than any BSD.

### Post by samjh on 2007-11-02
Linux should have some standards.  Standards like interoperable look and feel between major desktop environments, distro-neutral packaging systems, and either a standardised ABI or a set of specialised standard protocols and frameworks for application and driver development (eg. CUPS, FHS, etc.).

The goal is NOT desktop dominance.  That is only a side-effect of making a great operating system.  We should be looking to make development and improvement of Linux easier. 

Having standards makes life easy both for developers by facilitating focused development through collaboration and intelligent debate, rather than a bunch of half-informed people making half-arsed decisions and developing half-arsed solutions that don't even go half-way towards contributing to a better Linux kernel or Linux distros.

Standards also make Linux more user-friendly by providing consistency, and by accelerating the development of improvements by developers.

The best FOSS projects are all organised with set processes for code contribution, examination, and merger; set processes for new features and deprecations; project management schedules, etc.  Even the Linux kernel is a tightly organised project.  Look at the GNU projects: their organisation is pretty regimental.  KDE and Gnome are also very organised and focused.  Projects like Eclipse, OpenOffice, and Mono, are organised around corporate models but they are extremely successful.
