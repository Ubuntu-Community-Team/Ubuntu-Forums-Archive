### Post by jw5801 on 2008-05-06
If you care about sound quality at all DO NOT transcode from mp3 to ogg vorbis! Both are lossy formats, that means that when you encode something that is lossless (ie. your CD) to mp3 or ogg, some data is thrown away, reducing the sound quality. Thus going from something that is already missing data to another lossy codec, throws away even more data, hence reducing sound quality. An mp3 (or ogg) that is encoded at a nominal bitrate from an already lossy source will sound _significantly_ worse than if it had been encoded from a lossless source, whilst taking the up same amount of space.

That being said, why not flac?! Open, lossless, and lots better support than ogg!

### Post by xtifr on 2008-06-05
I use Flac for archiving, Ogg for my portable player and my laptop, and mp3 only when there's no other reasonable alternative available.  With lossy formats, the bit rate is actually far more important than the format, and with a sufficiently high bit rate, nobody can tell the difference, but Ogg offers much higher quality at lower bit rates, resulting in much smaller files and therefore I can carry more music around with me.  Plus it's free/libre/whatever.  What's not to like?

My portable is a Samsung.  It seems like almost all of their recent models include Ogg support.  Actually, from what I hear, most portables coming out of Asia include Ogg support these days—something about the generic chipsets which have MP3 and WMA also including Ogg because, well, because they can, I guess—but Samsung actually advertises their Ogg support, while a lot of the other players don't even mention it.  In any case, don't believe the myth that few players support Ogg; nothing could be farther from the truth.

In addition to other sources for Ogg files mentioned earlier in the thread, I should point to the [Internet Archive's Live Music Archive]("http://www.archive.org/details/etree"), which offers over 50,000 concert recordings (that's complete concerts, not songs) from over 2.500 different bands, all lossless, most with Ogg available as well (if it has mp3, it has Ogg), and all under a Creative Commons license or equivalent (mostly [by-nc-nd]("http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc-nd/3.0/"), but that's still nice).  It's dominated by jam bands, but there are hundreds with other styles ranging from punk to alt-rock to bluegrass to jazz to funk, and dozens of big names including some real surprises, IMO.
