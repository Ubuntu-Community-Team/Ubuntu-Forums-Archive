### Post by silkstone on 2008-07-04
I started with Ubuntu in February 2007 - Edgy Eft - and then upgraded to Feisty. In May of this year I did a complete reinstall to Hardy, and so far I'm happy with that apart from a couple of minor bugs.

But why bring out a new version every six months? The main reason for me upgrading is that older versions don't update the included software to the latest versions - so if I want, for example, Firefox 3 from the repos I need Hardy.

Isn't this counterproductive? Wouldn't it be better to concentrate on one version and get everything working as near to 100% as possible, rather than reinventing Ubuntu every six months?

I'm really pleased with Hardy on both my laptop and my desktop PC, and I'd much prefer it if the focus was on improving Hardy and providing the latest versions of FF, the GIMP, OOo, etc for a couple of years, rather than bringing out yet another version of Ubuntu.

It seems that as soon as one version is close to being properly debugged, it is superseded by the next version and the debugging has to start all over again.

Sorry - this isn't meant to be a rant, but I really can't understand the benefits of a six-month cycle of redundancy. :)

### Post by YaroMan86 on 2008-07-04
I find the six month cycle to be a good thing. Think of other operating systems release "cycles."

Get ready for Windows bashing here, but nothing too bad.

Remember how long it took us to get Vista? From announcement to release, it was around 5 years. And it turned out that despite such a long time for development, Vista lacked the quality of most software that's pedal to the metal 5 year development.

Rushed software is horrible for quality, but its also usually a Very Bad Sign if a program takes more than a regular amount of time to be developed.

I haven't been disappointed by Ubuntu. 6 months may not cause each release to be some revolutionarily new and feature-filled, but such a thing shouldn't be important to why a new release is made.

Six months is enough time to handle new features from X and GNOME. Bug fixes from the previous release. And, yes, new features for usability, performance, and a more robust system.

The problem we had with a lot of commercial operating systems is that there are program managers who aren't thinking like technical people, but marketing people. Again, Vista is an example of this. No real change over XP as far as most people could tell. So what's different? DirectX and a glitzy new interface. (Both of which, btw, are easily beaten out by Ubuntu. Seriously.)

My point being, a six month cycle keeps a development team on focus, and keeps the "wow-em-and-zow-em" crowd from making the development fall on its face.

NT's original development took 2 years longer because upper management kept on shoving wanted features in, and adding engineers. Remember that law: Adding people to a late project just makes it later.

LTS releases (Both of them) are good in that you can use them for a very very long time before even a remote need to upgrade actually show up.

Myself? I upgrade every six months. Started in Feisty, upgraded to Gutsy, upgraded to Hardy. And I'm watching Intrepid with much interest.
