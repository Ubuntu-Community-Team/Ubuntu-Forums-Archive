### Post by arvevans on 2010-12-19
Bugs for one person are not necessarily bugs for another person, or for the Linux community.  Many of the reported "bugs" are related to not understanding or not learning how Linux works, or why it does things the way it does.  Remember that "Linux Is Not Windows".  The architectural concepts are quite different...and for good reasons.

One reason for having a common bug reporting system is so the developers can look at how many persons are experiencing a particular situation.  Then they can evaluate the amount of time to make changes versus bug severity and potential impact on the total user community.  It is impossible to write a bug fix for someone not reading the user manual.

There are a great many individual programs incorporated within Linux, and many individual developers who are supporting each of these applications.  Most of these developers do not work for Ubuntu.  Almost none of them get paid for the work they do in writing Linux Apps and for tracking & repairing any bugs that are reported.  If you have a problem with a specific part of your Linux experience, it might be a good idea to look at the help section to determine who wrote and who maintains that particular software.  Then, if no response seems to be coming from your bug report, try sending an email to the particular developer of that code to see if he/she/it may or may not be aware of problems you are having with their code.  If you do this, you will need to first do your homework in order to develop a very clear and concise description of the problem...including step-by-step methods to use in replicating the problem on the developer's hardware.

As a user of community written (i.e. FREE) software you do have a responsibility for learning how to use it, and for making clear and technically concise bug reports.

Please be aware that UNIX & Linux has been under development since the early 1970's.  Some of the programs you may now be using were written by persons who are long dead.  In many cases someone else has stepped up to maintain those person's code, but not in all cases.  If you suspect that your programmer has died, you may need to make yourself knowledgeable enough to write code and then volunteer to maintain the software originally written by the apparently deceased developer.  This is what they mean by "community development"...join the community and help develop the code.
