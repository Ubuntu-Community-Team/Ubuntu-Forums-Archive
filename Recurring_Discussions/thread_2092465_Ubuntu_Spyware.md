### Post by PhilGil on 2012-12-07
In before thread is locked.

Technically, the shopping lens is, by definition, spyware. It sends your search queries via the Internet to a third party.

Whether it's web cookies tracking our behavior or a discount card at the local grocery store, we make choices about how much value we gain by giving up a bit of our privacy. It's extremely unlikely that Canonical is using the info it receives from searches for nefarious purposes (it's likely that it doesn't retain any data at all).

I'm not a fan of product results in my searches; I see little value in it. I understand Canonical's desire to monetize Ubuntu (and a financially health Canonical benefits every user), but I'd still like to see the shopping lens be opt-in rather than opt-out.

### Post by MyTinFoilHat on 2012-12-08
As a relatively new GNU/Linux and Ubuntu user - and as someone who strongly believes in the importance of user choice and user control, I'm throwing my opinions into the ring, too. 

It doesn't come as a surprise to me that Richard Stallman wrote such biting remarks about Ubuntu, especially given what I already know of his advocacy for User Rights and the importance of transparency in development.  Stallman's words were biting, if not slightly belabored, but beneath all the ruckus is a very valid point which seems to have fallen on blind eyes and deaf ears. Some here have chosen to focus on attacking the timeliness (or lack thereof) of Stallman's comments and I find that simply childish because it does not address his point.

For what it's worth, it also seems to me that forums staff have taken all this without the requisite grain of salt. Rather than addressing the core comments, the points behind Stallman's remarks, it seems they're playing the thread shuffle game in an effort to suppress this topic -as if nonchalance and oppression were a means to a positive end. Yes, staff members, there are threads about these changes in Ubuntu, but the topic of this thread is about Stallman's comments. Stop with the damage control. As Ubuntu is part of a larger community, the entire community has the right to its voice. And, should you consider silencing my voice here, please know that that action speaks volumes about your stance on liberty in the modern age and others will see this, understand it, and you will have made my decision to take action that much easier. Perhaps, by extension, you will have made that decision for other users, too. Time will tell.

Back on point:

I believe that this community and every fork that has come before or after (at the very least) owes Stallman not only the benefit of a doubt, but the simple respectful acknowledgement for what he is presenting to the entire community, not just a singular fork of an OS. After all, were it not for his efforts and the efforts of other, like-minded, forward-thinking people, none of us would be here having this conversation because this community, this platform, may not exist in this form at all.

I don't necessarily believe that Stallman is catastrophizing this seeming stand-off between the principles of open software and the changes Canonical has set in motion. Don your ruckus filter and read between the lines and you will find that his words are cautionary ones. He is effectively citing that recent changes in Ubuntu as a “slippery slope” because these changes undermine the founding principles of the GNU/Linux, open software schemata. In this, he is absolutely correct. Granted, his methodology for addressing this comes across as over-the-top to some (many?), but does that change a fact? No.

At its core, the GNU/Linux – and, by extension, Ubuntu, is about openness and liberty. It's about providing users choices, respecting freedoms, and insuring never-ending user control. It is for these reasons that I exorcised the likes of Windows and iOS from my life. I don't want to live, work, or play in a walled garden; nor do I want, need, or desire to have my freedoms, my choices, my control torn from me. That is Stallman's point and I support his warning to this community. Yes, even as a newbie.

I chose GNU/Linux and specifically Ubuntu because I want control. I also wanted and needed a system that was friendly to my experience as a non-technical user. I've long since tired of modern efforts to “enhance a user's experience” by way of throwing ads in our faces, apps surreptitiously handing-off MY data to third parties, opting-in-by default. 

Every step this community takes should be aligned with the founding principles that enabled its existence, not what one multimillionaire or board of trustees, or social media club thinks that community should look like.

Technical or not, I am part of this community, too, and like everyone else I have an opinion as well as a say in its direction. When I have an opportunity to contribute in my own way, I shall – and that contribution will not stiffle the spirit of liberty and openness.

This community has an unwritten agreement with itself to honor the founding principles of open software. Without it, this community, specifically Ubuntu, potentially risks becoming the next Windows, iOS, Facebook, Twitter, or other equally invasive embodiment of a corporate machine that would sell-out its users – the very same people that made it successful - for a profit.
