### Post by marl30 on 2010-12-06
Since I have been on these forums, I have heard many opinions as to why more people are not using Linux. I've heard that it's because of reasons such as lack of better compatibility, and not many ported priority softwares such as Photoshop, Dreamweaver, etc. I have also heard that it's because most of the popular games are mainly available for just Windows, hence, this turns off a lot of gamers. I've heard other reasons stated as well, but these are just a few that came to mind.  However, though I do believe that these issues may prevent people from desiring to try Linux, I don't necessarily believe they are the main reasons why more people are not using Linux. For me, the two primary reasons why more people are not using Linux are misconception and ignorance.
 
Misconception &#8211; a large percentage of people never think about trying Linux because of the misconception that it is mostly a command line OS that only the technically savvy can operate. I was a part of this group. I heard about Linux years ago but never decided to try it out because I thought that performing most task would require learning and using command line. I found that intimidating. I didn't actually see that the opposite was true until I came across a Youtube video that was showing off what Linux and Ubuntu could do. I bet if a lot of people who are using pirated versions of Windows knew they had a free alternative that was just as easy to use as Windows, they would go legit without second guessing.  
 
Ignorance -  The other major group of people who haven't thought about trying Linux just don't know about it. These are often the average person who doesn't really care about which OS they are running. They only care about using something that is pleasing on the eyes and will do a good job of browsing the internet... Facebook, Youtube, instant messengers, etc. They just want to be able to play their music, watch videos and do simple everyday computer stuff.  In this group you usually find those that often get frustrated by viruses and would listen to any recommendation that will prevent them from having another horror experience with viruses. I do offer a bit of computer support to people I know. Whenever I come across the reoccurring virus cases, I always use the opportunity to recommend Ubuntu as the perfect solution.  
 
I firmly believe the two reasons mentioned above are the primary reasons more people are not using Linux and not so much the common notions that were mentioned earlier. Another thing... being that Linux is mainly about free open source software, it will always be difficult to compete with the commercial power of Microsoft (Microsoft spends millions on promotions &#8211; Linux rely mainly on the word getting out via its community). For this reason alone, I can't see Linux ever gaining ground on Microsoft in terms of the desktop market. Not even Apple will ever come close to them; well,  at least not with their current model.  I am confident that the popularity of Linux will continue to grow, but I don't expect it to ever surpass Windows.  It would take a major slip up from Microsoft in order for that to happen. That is very unlikely though. If Linux is to gain significant ground on any desktop OS, it's more likely to be Apple's OS than  Microsoft's.

### Post by aysiu on 2010-12-07
I've given this topic a lot of thought over the past five years, and I think it boils down to three major obstacles: [list][*]**Ease of acquisition**. It is far easier to get a Windows- or Mac-preinstalled computer than to get a desirable and appropriately priced readily available Linux-preinstalled computer. So the vast majority of Linux adopters have to be tech-savvy enough to know how to download and burn an .iso, boot from CD, possibly repartition a drive, etc. That is definitely a minority of users. You could argue "Well, the less tech-savvy can always have their tech-savvy friends install and configure Linux for them." My experience has been that less tech-savvy computer users tend to be extremely afraid of change (and I don't even mean extreme changes like the whole operating system--even little ones like small settings changes within a program or moving the taskbar somewhere else). Many of them are fearful that their computers were working "just fine" before and that whatever their tech-savvy friends propose as a change will screw things up.[*]**Suspicion and fear of the unknown**. Because many less tech-savvy computer users are afraid of change, they also react to problems in change differently. If they have a problem with Windows, they just think everybody has problems and are far more likely to just say "Computers are some finnicky" than to say Windows is the problem, but if they're new to Linux and Linux has _any_ problem, however small, they're far more likely to attribute that to a Linux deficiency than to computer use in general. Windows can't print = "I'm having trouble with my printer." Linux can't print = "Why can't Linux just use my printer? I want Windows back!" [*]**Vendor lock-in**.You've heard it time and again that some users need 100% (not 99.999%) compatibility with Microsoft Office. I know a lot of people with iPhones or iPod Touches. I even know some iTunes users who have DRM'ed iTunes songs from back int he day (they are no longer DRM'ed, but old purchases still are). This is where the Linux evangelist will say "Oh, but Microsoft Office 2007 works just fine in Wine" or "Ubuntu now has support for iPhones built in because of libimobiledevice." Uh, not good enough. Hacks and workarounds that almost sort of kind of get things working... or get things working until the next release or firmware update aren't good enough. These users are used to a seamless experience where playlists update, everything syncs, and every new version of Office will install, not just what's approved as Platinum in the Wine App Db right now.[/list] When I take these three things into account and think about all the Windows users I know who are miserable with Windows, it's sad, but I can't really think of anybody who would be an excellent candidate to switch over to Linux. It's either vendor lock-in, fear of the unknown, or both.

I think you make some good points above. To them I would add another angle:

Questions as to why Linux has failed to generate significant market- or mindshare are usually asked by Linux users. As noted above, Linux users tend to be tech savvy: we are the kind of people who like to play with computers for the sheer joy of messing around with something. I don't think this activity is limited to computers. It's the reason I work on my own bike, bake my own bread and the like. I like to know how things work, and I like making them work. 

In this specific instance, though, there is a big disconnect between the Linux community and the general computer purchasing populace. For most people, computers are commodity items no different from microwaves or TVs or vacuum cleaners. They are tools you purchase to perform specific tasks which are used for a while--until they break down or are made obsolescent--and replaced. The are not something to play around with for the fun of it, just as your average person wouldn't start poking around a microwave to see how it worked. Because they are commodity items, computers are expected to be sold with the same kinds of amenities other commodities are sold with: warranties, 1-800 numbers for repairs, etc. They are not meant to be bought, then modified after purchase.

In my experience, most of the questions from the Linux community as to why people use other operating systems are couched in either technical or philosophical terms. While both of these may have valid points, they are exactly the wrong arguments to make if you're trying to get people to switch to Linux. The argument which would need to be made would be the same argument for any other product: how will this item make your life easier than it is now?  

The answer to this isn't, "you can run Linux on an older, slower machine than you've got now' because most people simply replace a computer when it's time for a new one, just like they replace the washing machine. Telling them they can keep their old machine isn't a convincing argument: computer tech advances too rapidly, and the lure of the new is too strong. Remember, computers are commodities. They're used, and replaced.

The philosophical argument doesn't work either. Very few things in this world are free, and the thought of paying for an operating system isn't an alien one any more than the idea of paying for a car. And telling someone Linux will allow them to escape from the restrictions Microsoft and Apple put on their computer use doesn't work when most people aren't aware of these restrictions and, more to the point, when these restrictions don't impact their lives. If you own a Mac and an iPod, you can use both to the full extent most people want to and never run into a restriction.

In order to convince people to use Linux you would have to show them that using Linux would make their computing lives easier in the kinds of every day tasks they do. And the issue is that Linux doesn't do that. It's a perfectly good operating system, but there it has  no killer feature from the point of view of most of the computing population.

There is only one computing company I know of which has figured out how to tell people their products will make people's lives easier: Apple. Jobs was the first guy to realize that computers are commodity items, and he was the first to try and make them like commodity items. The original Mac was supposed to be an 'information appliance', but it was years ahead of the technology it needed. The current Macs are what he had in mind.

Even more to the point, look at the iPhone and the iPad. Their entire design is meant to abstract away the operating system and present a UI which makes mobile computing easy and transparent. The iPhone is a very carefully targeted device, aimed right in the middle of your average computer user, designed to reveal only enough to that person to get the job done. It's also the kind of device which could never come out of the open source camp*, because open source people are categorically against restricting access to hardware and software. But, in order to make a complicated device usable for everyone, you need to abstract away large parts of it.

*I know that Android is officially open source, but in practice the various Android phones are about as closed as the iPhone. Sure, you can roll your own kernel, but using that kernel will require you to expend a lot of energy doing end runs around your cellular provider.

### Post by dpny on 2010-12-08
I'm gonna grab this quote because it highlights something I think is huge.

> When the iPad was announced, critics focused on the features it didn&#8217;t have (no webcam, no Flash, no USB ports),**but Apple with its clever marketing department convinced the hoards that the device was magic**

I see a lot of this in the OSS community: Apple's success is explained away by arguing that Apple essentially tricks people to use inferior hardware and software. Inherent in this argument is that if only people weren't so gullible, they'd all stop using Apple products and switch to what is obviously superior: Linux. This belief manages to simultaneously flatter OSS people and denigrate Apple, but it doesn't explain anything which is actually happening. It's just a way of extending OSS preconceptions to avoid the issues at hand.

One of the reasons Apple is so successful is that it designs its computers holistically: hardware and software together, reinforcing each other. This demands an enormous amount of industrial design, and at Apple the designers are as important as the programmers. Design is a word which gets shirt shrift in the OSS community, usually relegated to the status of making things pretty. Good industrial design is a holistic process which, when done well, helps to ensure that the device is used in the most efficient way possible. 

Thing is, the tight integration of hardware and software enforced by Apple's industrial design does result in devices which a vast number of people find magical to use. As much as some OSS people would like to, you can't explain away Apple's success with the iPod/iPhone/iPad with the lazy suggestion that Apple is just better at tricking people. Apple creates products which are popular, inspire enormous loyalty, and which people are willing to pay more money to have. This isn't luck or bad behavior: it's successful business: there are many times the number of people using iOS devices in the US than there are running Linux on their home computer.

I'm not saying this to get people angry, but to make a point. I think there is something to be learned in the OSS community from Apple's success. Unfortunately, it seems to be that most people in that community would rather cast aspersions at Apple rather than learn from their success.

Windows can't print = "I'm having trouble with my printer." Linux can't print = "Why can't Linux just use my printer? I want Windows back!"
[*]**Vendor lock-in**.You've heard it time and again that some users need 100% (not 99.999%) compatibility with Microsoft Office. I know a lot of people with iPhones or iPod Touches. I even know some iTunes users who have DRM'ed iTunes songs from back int he day (they are no longer DRM'ed, but old purchases still are). This is where the Linux evangelist will say "Oh, but Microsoft Office 2007 works just fine in Wine" or "Ubuntu now has support for iPhones built in because of libimobiledevice." Uh, not good enough. Hacks and workarounds that almost sort of kind of get things working... or get things working until the next release or firmware update aren't good enough. These users are used to a seamless experience where playlists update, everything syncs, and every new version of Office will install, not just what's approved as Platinum in the Wine App Db right now.
[/LIST]
When I take these three things into account and think about all the Windows users I know who are miserable with Windows, it's sad, but I can't really think of anybody who would be an excellent candidate to switch over to Linux. It's either vendor lock-in, fear of the unknown, or both.
Vender lock-in is probably one of the biggest drawbacks, IMO. I know a few people that gave Ubuntu a bit of thought and when they found that they couldn't install their games, MS Office, or iTunes, they decided against even bothering with the OS.
