You have something going there, but I found myself disagreeing a lot with the meat of it&#8230;

> I hear about this a lot, "But, Canonical HAS contributed other open source projects, why aren't people latching onto them! It's not their fault that Gnome/X/etc. won't pick it up." To put it simply, they don't pick it up because Canonical is just spinning the wheel, duplicating effort if you will&#8230; *Snip*

*&#8220;For notifications, I fail to see what they've done to it. It just looks like a skin and then they put notification hooks in some applications.&#8221;*
So, instead of analysing this thing, you've decided it must be a bad idea? NotifyOSD is based on the existing Notification Daemon specification, which is meant to happily support any notification daemon under the sun (not just the default one). Unfortunately, lots of applications abuse notifications as cheap dialog windows (and worse). This project is about using them properly. It benefits a lot of other projects that may want to try new things with notifications, including upstream Gnome. (See: gnome shell's message tray for an example of why this is a good thing). Lots of applications have been patched to check the notification daemon's capabilities and to use notifications *specifically for notifications*, in a way that doesn't make assumptions about presentation.
[https://wiki.ubuntu.com/NotifyOSD](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/NotifyOSD)
[https://wiki.ubuntu.com/NotificationDevelopmentGuidelines](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/NotificationDevelopmentGuidelines)

On the music store&#8230;
I, too, am not a huge fan of the implementation and much prefer what's happening for Banshee's Amazon store. I didn't know that profits went to the Gnome foundation; that's cool!
However, two things:
Rhythmbox is not "dying," and even if it was it'd be a good thing Ubuntu was supporting it, since Rhythmbox is an official part of Gnome and will be for the foreseeable future.
There is an Ubuntu Music Store for Banshee, and it can basically be implemented in any player that supports multiple music sources. All it needs is to embed a little web browser that shows the U1 store page at 7digital, and upon buying stuff the music is transfered to your Ubuntu One space online.

The Ubuntu One client is free software. The server code (as in the bits and pieces that glue together various layers on Canonical's end) is proprietary. OwnCloud is more interesting anyway, but if anyone wants to play with the client, it's there ;)
It isn't "just a Dropbox clone," since Dropbox aims at being fully cross platform. U1 lacks some features that Dropbox has (restoring old versions of files), but it has lots of stuff it doesn't, too, like syncing Tomboy notes and your DesktopCouch database.

*&#8220;Software Center is a glorified WebKit browser for which they should be going with PackageKit anyway.&#8221;*
Aptdaemon is a step in that direction, and PackageKit is in the roadmap (somewhere). Not sure where you get "glorified Webkit browser" from, since it is certainly not a web browser, although it does use WebkitGtk for rendering (since it's quite good at it). Saying it's just a glorified WebKit browser, though, is like saying Yelp (System &#8250; Help and Support) is a glorified GtkHTML (and soon Webkit) browser.
At any rate, Software Center is about presenting applications in a shiny, engaging way, and the steps to achieve that go a lot deeper than one may immediately think. One movement that's afoot, for example, is to make package descriptions that don't suck. Another thing is adding repositories in a friendly way, organizing packages nicely (debtags, eventually), and presenting add-ons for packages.
It's about grasping that giant heap of information provided by the Debian package system, using every last morsel of it to present meaningful data, and then some.

The Linux Standard Base says we should be using RPM and, as far as I'm aware, has nothing about PackageKit. The important part of PackageKit isn't (or shouldn't be&#8230;) about user interfaces, anyway. It's about letting an application shout out &#8220;hey, I need the Gentium font!&#8221;, and having a dbus service respond &#8220;right, I'm installing it now!&#8221;
I think that can be nicely implemented with Software Center as a front end to the latter half.

Here is Marks response ...
[I]
"Greg, thank you for your sincere and gracious apology.

When one cares deeply about something, criticism hurts so much more. And the free software world is loaded with caring, which is why our differences can so easily become vitriolic.

All of us that work on free software share the belief that our work has meaning far beyond the actual technology we produce. We are working to achieve goals that transcend the merits of the specific products we build: putting software freedom on a firm economic footing means that it can realistically become the de facto standard way that the software world works, carried forward by powerful forces of investment and return and less dependent on what feels like the heroic efforts of relatively few software outsiders swimming against the tide.

Red Hat’s success in proving a viable business model around a distribution was a very significant milestone in that quest, for all of us. I don’t mean to diminish that achievement when I point out that it’s come at the cost of dividing the world into those that buy RHEL, and those that can’t or won’t. Red Hat’s success is well deserved, and our work at Canonical is not in any sense motivated by desire to take that away. Red Hat is here to stay, there will always be a market for the product, and as a result, we all have the reassurance that our contributions can find a sustainable path into the hands of at least part of the world’s population.

Canonical’s mission is to expand the options, to find out if it’s possible to have a sustainable platform without that dividing line. We know that our quest would not be possible without your pioneering, but we don’t feel that’s riding on anybody’s coat-tails. We feel we have to break new ground, do new things, add new ingredients, and all of that is a substantial contribution in turn. But we don’t do it because we think Red Hat is “wrong”, and we don’t expect it to take anything away from Red Hat at all. We do it to add to the options, not to replace them.

We should start every discussion in free software with a mutual reminder of the fact that we have far more in common than we have differences, that individual successes enrich all of us far more in our open commons-based economy than they would in a traditional proprietary one, that it’s better for us to find a way to encourage others to continue to participate even if they aren’t necessarily chasing exactly the same bugs that we are, than to chastise them for thinking differently.

On that note, let’s shake hands.

Mark"[/I]
