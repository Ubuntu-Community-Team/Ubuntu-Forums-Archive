### Post by Flymo on 2011-10-14
Excellent thread, thanks!

I've voted for Enlightenment, since we have been trying Bodhi Linux in our testing partitions for a few months. It's been on this ancient Compaq C300 (Celeron 420) for a few weeks, and working pretty well. 

I used to be a 100% KDE3 fan after trying Knoppix way back when - until I tried the Fluxbox alternative in Knoppix - loved it but found the configuration learning curve too steep.  This changed with time.

A shiny new SuSE 10 box built for our CAD project arrived with Gnome installed, and we grew to know and like it - then early Ubuntu led to Xubuntu on our older slower machines (P3 with 256 RAM, eg), Fluxbuntu briefly, Geubuntu/OpenGeu, OzOs, ELive, in our testing partitions and Ubuntu as the mainstay with good old Xubuntu when there was that (brief) installer problem with Gnome.  

Latterly we've had good experiences with Mint flavours. My favourite for a long time was Mint Fluxbox CE - I finally learned the configuration tweaks, and had a ball.  The secret was to ignore the GUI config tools and just re-type the text files - easy when you know.

Bodhi's appearance early this year looked a bit too good to be true. The glossy lightweight Enlightenment riding on top of leaned-down Ubuntu Lucid Lynx? Yes!  Surely it can't be stable?  Well, I've had segfaults a-plenty in other E17 environments, so that would have been unsurprising.

In side-by-side testing over a month we conclude that Bodhi 1.1 ~ 1.2 has been substantially more stable than Natty.  And the Enlightenment interface is (typically) far less user hostile too!

Typically?  Yes. E17 is configurable in ways that Gnome etc can only dream of, including  scary set-ups! Some very like Gnome Shell or Unity.  Not all innovation is good innovation, we know this.

What Bodhi offers is a big bunch of desktop configs on the tiny (340MB) CD ISO - Laptop, desktop, netbook, tablet, Compositing, fancy, etc.  Marry any one of those with any one of the bunch of complete themes provided (with many more to download) and the choice becomes almost somebewildering - but easy to do.

No it's not perfect.  I do not install it for my posse of n00bs, that's what Lucid is for. But at this rate of development I can see that being a possibility before Lucid expires.

With that as background, here's my list of desktop environments in no particular order, since it's horses for courses - I notice no variation in font quality between them, but that's just my blind spot...;)

**Gnome 2** - stable, fairly configurable, flashy bling via Compiz, medium to heavy on CPU.
**XFCE** - stable, simple transparency on lower resources, fairly configurable, menus are a pain.
**Fluxbox** - so simple to configure once you know how, good transparency, very light, very fast & effective. Very stable.
**E17 **(Enlightenment) Highly configurable, pretty stable - surprisingly so with standard Bodhi packages from the web site. Less so if you try things at random from the Lucid repos.  Very light, very fast, even with the compositor on old hardware, getting better every Bodhi release.
**KDE3** - stable, fairly light, very configurable, good applications choice, but no longer my favourite.
**KDE4** - gave up on it due to obesity and sluggishness combined with that weird menu box and less than tasteful bling. Without those it's a slow version of KDE3.
**Gnome Shell** - better than Unity, but only just.  Porky, slow on our old hardware if it runs at all. Disappointing.
**Unity** - very disappointing if it runs at all on our varied hardware. Obese, slow, Flaky, crash prone, locks up, and frustratingly non-intuitive when it does work.
**Tiling** Managers - E17 also does this, sometimes very useful.
