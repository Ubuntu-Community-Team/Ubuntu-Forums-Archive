### Post by samjh on 2008-08-26
> **ICEcoffee said:**
> Even programs that are considered stable and at the centre of any good Linux desktop package list, have little development time and many of them have remained pre v1.0 for years.

I even found out, what seems to be, just ONE poor developer maintaining and developing KDE's Quanta plus IDE, he made a plee for help in June I think on his official blog, and he had two responses. F-spot remains at v0.4.3.1, Rhythmbox v0.11.5.... etc.

Be careful with version numbers.

In the FOSS world, 0.1 doesn't mean the project is immature or incomplete.  While some projects do use 1.0 as the baseline "stable" release, plenty of projects don't.  For example, Wine has been in development for 15-odd years, yet it only reached 1.0 this year.

Keep in mind that most FOSS projects are run by volunteers, who have busy lives outside of their project.  Families, real jobs, socialising, etc.  There are only a small number of projects that have people working on them full-time or enjoy financial sponsorship.

On the flip-side, a lot of the top distros have commercial support.  Ubuntu is sponsored by Canonical; OpenSUSE by Novell; Fedora by RedHat; Linspire and Mandriva have their own commercial entities; Debian is backed by HP and Sun, among others.

So major distros can afford to have more regular releases and business-like deadlines, whereas most application projects cannot.  Having said that, major projects like GNOME, KDE, Qt, Java, Mono, Eclipse and so on, have tightly-managed projects with more-or-less regular releases.
