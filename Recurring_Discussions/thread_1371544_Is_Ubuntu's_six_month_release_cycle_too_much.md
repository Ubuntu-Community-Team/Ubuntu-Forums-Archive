### Post by dr_dred5 on 2010-01-03
I don't want this to sound like a rant. I've been using Ubuntu since Dapper and I love it and I would never go back to any non Linux OS.

However I've had some pretty major issues with Jaunty and Karmic, issues that never seem to to get fixed. As a result I'm finding that I may need to stick with the LTS versions.

I have a pretty elaborate HTPC setup, using a Mythbackend server and three frontends.

Jaunty let me down with Mythtv's inablilty to display fonts with my hardware making it useless as a frontend See [https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/jaunty/+source/mesa/+bug/341898](https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/jaunty/+source/mesa/+bug/341898) This bug was never fixed and I began testing the later Alpha versions of Karmic, thinking that If I was actively involved in testing, any issues I had were more likely to be dealt with.

However, Karmic couldn't handle my 5.1 audio and now, three months after this was confirmed as a bug and two months into the final release, it is starting to feel like this may also get abandoned. See [https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/pulseaudio/+bug/445849](https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/pulseaudio/+bug/445849)
[B]
So the question is:[/B]

**Do you think Ubuntu should spend less time developing new releases and spend more time making their releases more solid?**

### Post by rdbowers on 2010-01-03
This was also my complaint with Windows over the years - they'd come out with new versions, rather than analyzing and fixing the errors in their old versions.  I refused W95 until it became necessary, Ditto for W98 until the second edition (I was tired of W95 problems), and didn't get anything until XP (and wasn't too happy with that).

I've always wondered how much of the Microsoft code is patched out and non-usable, but lazily left in place.  I once heard from a programmer that in at least one version, it was significant.

I would rather see a version come out and the focus be on fixing errors, rather than writing new code or major rewriting the previous version.  A new version would be best left for when there are new breakthroughs in technology or something equally significant.  One other reason I would consider valid would be if some sort of significant security hole found in a version that would require so many changes that another entire new version would be easier- and from what I understand, I doubt that would be the case.

I'm using 9.10... and I probably will avoid updating until it becomes necessary.  No use fixing what isn't broke.

I might add that I came to Linux because of the problems I've had with Windows over the years.  I'm fairly new to this OS- installed it for the first time 6 months ago.
