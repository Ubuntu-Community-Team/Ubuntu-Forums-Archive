### Post by DarkOx on 2009-04-03
> 1. Put a low price tag on it that anyone could afford

Your price sends a message to the market. If something is free, it may not necessarily be poor quality -- Google is free and very high quality, it just gets its revenues from ads, not the user -- but something that costs a small amount of money will be instantly perceived to be, well, cheap.

In some ways, if you were to charge money for it, it would be better to charge $200 bucks for it than $20. I'd worry about putting a $20 OS on my computer, but I'd feel that if they can charge $200 for it, then it must be good enough to at least not totally hose my system.

> 2. Get rid of all the distros and just keep the top 3 most used.
> 8. Too many desktop environments and mods. It may get confusing to the average point and click user

Only a huge problem if you come up and say, "Hey, try Linux, there are a thousand different options!" and then list each one. If you just say, "Here, try Ubuntu" and hand them a CD, none of these are a problem. The only people who dig into these kinds of options are those who are interested in them anyways.

> 7. Professional programs, such as Photoshop, do not work with it
> 6. Start getting manufactures to create games for Linux

The solution here isn't WINE, it's targeting people who don't need these things (initially). If at first you target people who just use the internet/email/word, eventually you can get critical mass and garner enough market share that demand for professional apps will build on its own.

I will say that I think Ubuntu's release cycle makes this difficult. As a user having to upgrade* every 6 months is a pain, and as a developer I expect having to re-test your program against a new version of Ubuntu twice a year would get old fast.

Honestly, what I think Ubuntu needs to go mainstream is to create an environment where people are making money on it. Where companies like Dell are selling hardware because of Ubuntu, and where developers (independent or large companies) are making money selling software that runs on it. Until that happens, we'll always have the hardware issues, and we'll never have apps as good as those found on Mac/Windows (though on the plus side, the apps we do have are still free).



* Ok, technically you don't *have* to upgrade, but if you don't, you're stuck on old versions of all your apps, which kinda sucks.
