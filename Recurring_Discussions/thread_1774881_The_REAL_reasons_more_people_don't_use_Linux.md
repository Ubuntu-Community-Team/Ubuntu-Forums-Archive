### Post by athenroy on 2011-06-03
While the debate rages over which Desktop and Windows manager to use, here are some cold, hard facts that not only Ubuntu, but most Linux distros in general need to face up to before Linux is widely accepted.  I installed 11.04 via Wubi.  In general, I'm pretty happy with it in the Classic Mode, but!  First of all, I'm not a big sound theme man, but every now and than it's nice to hear a pop, blip or whistle when you do certain things like close a window.  So, while exploring things, I notice it's supposed to have a default sound theme and than you see all these sounds like dog bark, bird chirp, whatever.  The only thing I heard was a little sound when the login screen came up.  Why?  After exploring the files and finding where the sound themes are, you find only one file in the default folder and all the rest just loose!  How can a new user be impressed by that??  Eventually, I downloaded a new sound theme and through sudo command installed it so I have some sounds to brighten my day.  Same thing with the Firewall, most people don't know it has ufw or how to set it up.  I installed Firestarter, myself.  Same with a network.  I have 2 Windows machines and one of those has a printer that's shared.  I downloaded Samba, thinking that's all I needed, but that isn't even set up.  You have to try and figure out the config file, than and than it has a setup folder besides!  I downloaded a live CD of a little know distro using E17, popped it in the DVD drive and when it booted I was shocked to click on network and actually see all my shared Windoze files and drives on the other machines!  That is the only distro I tried that set up the network for me, so, yes, it can be done!

It isn't the desktop, or the windows manager that keep people away from Linux, it's these seemingly small, but very annoying and frustrating things that keep people on Windows or OS X.  I consider myself an intermediate to advanced user and if I find it a challenge, imagine how a newcomer feels!

In other words, GET YOUR ACT TOGETHER, 'untu and all the other distros.  Fix the little things before worrying about desktops and windows managers!  That's how you'll get more users!

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-06-04
This is a little past hilarious to me. One of the most irritating things to me about Windows on a default install is all of those obnoxious little sound effects for doing normal things. To me, they seem almost *rude*; what if you're using a computer in a public place, like a library, coffee shop, or whatnot? Does everyone really need to know that you just booted up, or that you're opening a folder, or that you clicked a link?

The little details of the UI *are* important, which is why we have these Shell Vs. Unity and Compiz vs. Mutter threads and so on. There's a *lot* of focus on that sort of thing, and there's a lot of inconsistency between different visions of what metaphor or workflow or scheme needs to be *the* way that things are done. Consistency in behaviors for similar-looking things in different applications, for instance, or visual cues to tell the user that the computer is working on something - those are critical. 

Still, none of that has a *jot* to do with why Linux isn't more widely accepted, because they're no better under Windows than they are under any particular Linux distro. The actual things needed that presently keep Linux from broader use on the desktop are already quite well known and documented: support from hardware vendors for drivers, support from software developers for industry-standard apps, support from vendors in OEM installs, and users who can afford the switchover cost of learning a new operating system. 

In other words, whether you're a manufacturer, a developer, or a user, you probably already have a vested interest in using Windows, and that limits the extent to which Linux is actually developed for the desktop. Um, done.

OSX beta brought me back. I was in line for the first machine to ship OSX (it also ran OS9), and it was pretty neat. Very NeXT-ish, which for a person who had been a giant fan of NeXT Step and WindowMaker (which I still run as my desktop most of the time) was a huge perc.
It was BSD based, so I didn't have any OS-lexia (ps -aux vs ps -ef, etc). I was quite happy with it, got X11 installed, started learning Cocoa, etc.

I even belonged to .Mac because it was the most seamless sync/backup experience in the world, as of 2002. $200 some dollars a year it cost - but nobody else compared.

Well, the quirks of OSX soon became massive issues. NTFS volume problems. SAMBA issues, bugs from 10.1 stayed bugs up to at least 10.4, new bugs added in every release. Each one got more 'glittery', less stable (gray screens of death, crashing, issues), and the each ran slower than the last.
By 10.5 I bought all new Intel hardware to the tune of $3k (MBP Core Duo, first edition, 32bit). It was a disaster.
It got worse.
.Mac became Mobile Me, and was falling so far behind it was ridiculous. It was also down a lot. My MBP (when it worked) spent more time in BSD than OSX, and it was so unreliable a piece of hardware that I bought an Acer. That was 2007 or 2008, I think.

The new Acer came with Vista. It was pretty, but slow. It ran Debian and Ubuntu perfectly though, and PC-BSD 7 and 8 like it was made for it.

By this point I had gone through 6 factory replacements of my MBP. 10+ battery replacements (under warranty), and had a very real need for syncing my information between each version of my laptop. Apple doesn't do this for you. They just hand you a clean machine.

I spent ages looking for a WebDAV solution, then started playing with Writely and Google Docs. It was pretty lame at the time, so I went with MS Live Mesh (beta). Live Mesh saved the day - I was able to do everything that .Mac had once done and Mobile Me no longer did, and for free! I migrated at once, ditched the MBP, and turned my enormous Mac tower into a dedicated sound workstation.

Well, Live Mesh was great and all... especially under Win7, but I eventually ended up moving more and more into the Google ecosystem because the contact and doc sync simply worked. Far better than Live did.

...

Ok, so sorry for all that... but my point is - *none* of them are seamless, and if you truly want transparent, smooth, on AND offline sync, you have to keep hopping and working.
Nobody does it 100% right yet, and I will practically guarantee that Apple will *never* get it right, nor will any hardware vendor or OS vendor.

To get sync working right, you have to be platform agnostic. You have to deliver a mobile experience on any phone or tablet and a rich experience on any desktop or laptop (or capable tablet). You have to be available online and off, and you can't exceed some poor schmuck's data allocation.

Ubuntu One isn't even close. iCloud isn't even close. Live is hit and miss, and Google is not bad but data mines the *heck* out of it.


tldr; Tried Apple's walled garden, Google's mess, MS's mess, and Ubuntu's mess - none of them work standalone.
