### Post by blueturtl on 2010-04-20
What Canonical is doing is primarily marketing. Just think about it:

There have been all-free distributions out there for quite some time. Debian -- which Ubuntu is based on -- is very strict about software freedom. Linux on the desktop was quite stable for a long time, because only users who understood what software freedom meant would even venture into Linux. Few people would trade practicality for ideals.

Mark Shuttlesworth and Canonical are really being quite smart about it all. They know that to bring freedom to the masses Linux (or GNU) has to become a viable business on the desktop and that to make Linux a viable business on the desktop they need to increase the user base.

Ubuntu tries to combine user friendliness and freedom wherever it can, but will not sacrifice the end-user experience to preach. Instead they rolled out the restricted drivers manager which allows people to make full use of the capabilities of their hardware. They're making operating with the closed world more seamless which will lessen the resistance for people to switch. What this means, is that more people choose to stay in Linux, and inevitably further foray in to free/open products.

There is a reason Ubuntu is the most popular distro right now. Ubuntu is the Dark Knight of Linux. Ubuntu can do what the completely free systems can't: win over users who are too scared to make the switch to free in one go. Users can dip their toes in freedom, without fear of drowning. When (and if) they find they prefer freedom, the completely free systems are there for them.

Open Source comes from several distinct categories of communities:
[LIST=1]
[*]One guy who wrote a utility and wants everyone to have it.
[*]A group of people with a common interest, who get together to write software which is intended to be Open Source from the beginning.  Apache, for example.
[*]The United States Government.  The largest single organized contributor of Open Source software in the world.
[*]Commercial interests who wrote something and open sourced it for some reason.  Mozilla, for example.
[/LIST]

Money from Open Source comes from several places.  Some of them:
[LIST=1]
[*]The developer's own pocket.
[*]Books sold which were written by the developer or by the group.

So you may contribute to OS which is "greyware" in the sense that it might become commercial software again.

If a commercial interest "owns" the code, they can release a single version of that code under OS, but another version of it can be commercial.
