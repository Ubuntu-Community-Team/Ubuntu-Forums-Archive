### Post by beetleman64 on 2010-12-28
Canonical are involved in a number of ways, not least paying developers and other representatives to build and promote Ubuntu as a rival to Windows and Macintosh. They also provide paid-for support and taining for companies (or individuals) who choose to use Ubuntu.

They also work with OEMs to get Ubuntu pre-installed on new PCs. Also, if OEMs want their hardware certified, they have to pay Canonical money to do so. The Ubuntu One Music Store and the paid-for section of the Ubuntu Software Centre will also be a source of revenue for them.

### Post by MisterGaribaldi on 2010-12-28
Wow, the misinformation out there is truly staggering.

RedHat previously produced one big ol' Linux distro which was being used by businesses and individuals alike. As the corporate end of things became more lucrative and required more and more of their resources, RedHat decided to spin off the "distro" to the Fedora group, while maintaining a server- and workstation platform and renamed it, as mentioned above, RedHat Enterprise. They do all kinds of stuff and are on a lot of servers and so-called "big iron".

It's actually worked out really well, as the Fedora community is a very avid one, and between their efforts and whatever indirect support they get from RedHat, Fedora is a significant distro in the Linux community.

Canonical has made a LOT of in-roads with Linux through their own Ubuntu distro. Mark Shuttleworth has pumped a TON of cash into development and refinement efforts, starting with the Debian base, and arguably has made so much progress that Ubuntu is able to be though of in terms of being perhaps the most visible of the distros out there (especially to the general public) and is also probably the most friendly distro to use.

It's stable, relatively sane, and not bleeding edge. These are the reasons it is *my* choice of Linux distro, having also used Fedora, Mandrake (now Mandriva), RedHat (back when), SuSE and OpenSuSE, etc.
