### Post by BobvanderPoel on 2011-10-16
Add me to the list of "disappointed with 11.10" folks. Tried various flavors of gnome and kde4. I just see a buch of bloated and ugly stuff. 

So, I decided to give xfce4 a try. LOVE IT. Especially since I've started to run it with compiz! Apart from some blinking when I start a session, it's pretty much seamless. Biggest plus for my aging eyes is the magnify (compiz) function!

To try this from a running session:

  1. In case there is a problem, turn off session save (settings->session and startup->general->uncheck "automatically save session".

  2. From a terminal type:
     
         compiz --compiz --replace ccp &

  3. You should get compiz running replacing the existing window manager. 

  4. If this works, hit ctr-c in the terminal and then log out.

If compiz is working okay, set your system so that xfce4 uses this all the time. Use your editor of choice to change the file:

  /etc/xdg/xfce4/xfconf/xfce-perchannel-xml/xfce4-session.xml

Around line 14 delete the lines:

 <property name="Client0_Command" type="array">
   <value type="string" value="xfwm4"/>
 </property>
  

with:

 <property name="Client0_Command" type="array">
   <value type="string" value="compiz"/>
   <value type="string" value="ccp"/>
 </property>

Logout/in and you're in bliss!

### Post by houseworkshy on 2012-05-13
Is it ok to hold both extreem views at the same time?
For touch screen mobile devices Unity is wonderful, one can move the  playblocks on the left around with your finger and don't need the eyes  of a hawk to see things.  The biggest thing is the scaleability, one can  stretch the virtual keyboard to suit, and if the fortunate wealthy  owner of several devices have the same gui on all of them.  The privacy  feature is also nice as it helps prevent lint which on typically smaller  mobile things can be more of an issue.  For linux on mobile devices  unity's the best GUI out there.
On a desktop it may be the least intuative OS I've ever seen, really  hard to configure,  how does one remove, not hide, the playblocks on the  left for example or put a quick link somewhere so one doesn't have to  use several clicks or search by name to open a favorite app? I will give  it more time but couldn't recomend it to a newbie as I could gnome1 and  2.  For a desktop without a touch screen it works but doesn't shine.
So that's my tuppence as far as GUI's are concerned.  But the anger  whew, why?  Linux has always been like this.  There are two crowds with  respect to computers, the application users and the geeks who often  become developers ( thank goodness or we wouldn't have open source stuff  at all, I'll take this chance to say a huge heartfelt thankyou to all  open source developers ).  
Well think human minds and understand.  
From the application users point  of view they want a hassle free system which they can work and play  on.  Especailly work.  Many have bosses and clients breathing down their  necks about deadlines, or are students with projects which must be  finished in time or they'll fail.  There is also the fact that many  people don't have the cash to upgrade the hardware every couple of  years.  The app users are the majority overall I'd guess 99%,  for linux  probably more like 80%, I don't actually know but it'd be a very  usefull market audit and one which should be a prequil to any new distro  which wants to be popular. 
 There is also a big mixed area in the  middle of people who know enough of the command line to get by on and  fix the more common problems and maybe have some interest for the OS  itself and play and learn a bit when they have the spare time.  Well all  they want is to keep working and playing, often on the same machine for  several years because there isn't that much money around.  Whilst those  in the richer countries may be on duel cores and thinking about a quad  most in the poorer nations are on second hand machine and regard a  pentium as modern as they are on old reconditioned 4, 3 and even 285's.   No point sending 12.04 to any of them, it won't run but the gap has  been filled by the likes of slitaz so critism of Ubuntu for changing its  target market is unfounded.  It was there for them when there wasn't  much else and now that there is Ubuntu can move upmarket hardware-wise.   I notice that Nelson Mandela isn't included as a video example anymore,  quite right too, wouldn't look right on a system which needs newer than five  year old hardware which only a tiny percentage of computers in Africa  are.  

The app users want smooth workflow without interuptions and hate  having that disrupted.  The people on lower hardware want things that  always ran before to carry on running.  The thing about gnome for such  people is that is was so amazingly good, after windows and it's malware  horrors an absolute blessing.  It also tended to run trouble free.   Following gnome was never going to be easy when so many found it to be  perfect.  So they get cross.

Now look from the developers point of view.  A developer likes to leave  his or her mark so that means that they like to change or create  something.  How to do that when close to perfection has been acheived.   It's like finishing the last level in a game, there's nothing left to do  but minor tweeks and maintainence.  But heres all this new hardware on  the scence, ok so make something to fit the new stuff.  Which is a lot  of work even if it is one's prefered hobby.  Hearing it critisised is  bound to hurt, after all the majority have put a lot of hours into  something and arn't getting a penny out of it.
So change which the developers love and the app users hate;  "change for changes sake", "fixing what ain't broken" etc etc becomes a row.   It shouldn't.
Without the developers there wouldn't be any open source and of course  the app users want stability as for many smooth workflow is a matter of  livelyhood.
The realistic view is to remember that there is a lot of choice out  there.  The beginer linux system is now mint, just look at the  rateing on distro watch if you don't beleive me.  But that's ok because  without the development heavy systems there wouldn't ever be any  innovation, the novice linux systems always depend on earlier  developmental systems which have become stable.  First it was slackware,  then red hat, debian, and several others.  Just before Ubuntu it was  Gentoo that topped the charts and was going to be the linux that made a mass market.

It's a social process.  A group of people get together and design a system  which "just works".  It becomes popular, soon that populartity attracts  the biggest development team who develop and the system  changes, more innovations and what inevitabaly goes with them, at first, bugs.  The  app users rage a bit then switch.  Some of the developers, who feel that  much of their hard work has been binned, also rage and fork ( Mate ).  It's just how open source is and actually that's good for diversity and  choice, it's all the anger about it that I don't like.

I suspect the main reason that Debian has alway just been there, which  is why so many distos are confident basing themselves on it,  is good  psycology.  That strict division between "testing" and "stable" has kept  both app users and developers fairly happy.  Had the gnome team taken  that approach I doubt there'd be all this fuss. Had Ubuntu been able to release a 'Mobuntu' and have unity as a choice, like lxde,  along with gnome 2 on  the desktop edition all would be calm and they'd still hold the top  place in linux.   Ubuntu don't develop gnome/unity by the way so "love hate Unity is   really on the wrong forum" get the impression that a lot haven't sussed   that.  Still that's open source and how it works, it is made  and used by humans .. mostly

If you're an application user who for aesthic, workflow or hardware reasons don't like the change just use something else don't rage at the people who's work you've been happily using all this time.  If you're a developer who feels hurt by having ones freely given work critisised understand that many of the crits are from frustrated people who've just lost what had become a well known friend and don't really know the development score so don't take it to heart and keep develping.  If you want to be producing a popular system take it onboard though, call it rather over the top  customer feedback.  If you're a gnome2 developer who's angry about having ones work chucked out amble over to the Mate team and take your wastebin with you, they'll be needing what's in it.
