### Post by pbb on 2008-05-07
Hi all,

As a Ubuntu and Linux newbie, my learning curve is often artificially steepened by the use of Ubuntu codenames instead of version numbers.

Ubuntu by itself has a very clear version numbering policy; 8.04 means it has been released in April, 2008. Very quickly one learns there are two releases a year, at the 4th and 10th month, so it is easily understood that 6.10 is one and a half years old, or 4 releases ago.

However, very many forum users (sometimes it seems it's actually the majority) for some reason prefer to use terms like "Zippy Zoba", "Flimsy Fump", "Geeky Grok", "Hanging Hick-up" or whatever they are.

Take a look at the Ubuntu Homepage. Do you see any of these codenames there? NO! Go ahead, browse around -- you won't find them unless you really search for them!

Okay, I may be a bit overreacting. But please, hear this beginner's plea. The use of these codenames instead of the version numbers has a few bad results:
[LIST=1]
[*]Newbies don't know what you are talking about, because the version they are using only mentions a numerical version.
[*]Even if they manage to find out what the codename of their version is, they won't know if another codename is the previous one (so that examples probably still work) or if it is a 2-3 years old version which might be significantly different.
[*]The use of these codewords creates a sort of "in-crowd". You don't know what we are talking about, so you don't belong here.
[*]It makes things geeky, while my impression was that Canonical was actually trying to bring Ubuntu to the masses, instead of just the geeks.
[*]And finally: it's wrong! As I understand it, the codenames actually only apply to the pre-release versions. Saying something applies to "Ubuntu Gada Gada" would imply it does not concern the final release version, only the beta's.
[/LIST]

I hope I haven't offended anyone with this rant. And since so many people are using the codenames on the forums, and I haven't seen any complaints, I guess there are very few people sharing my problems...

The numbering scheme for Ubuntu releases was well planned from the beginning to help everyone know how new/old a release is simply from the number. Here is why.

The first number is from the year of release.

After the decimal, the number is the month of release.

Examples:

8.04 was released in 2008, in the month of April.

5.10 was released in 2005, in the month of October.

10.04 will be released in 2010, in the month of April (making a jump here, just to show how this works).

It is actually quite a useful scheme.
