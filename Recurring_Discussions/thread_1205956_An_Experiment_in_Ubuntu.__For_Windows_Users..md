### Post by Shibblet on 2009-07-06
The whole experiment is posted [here...  technopotomus.com]("http://technopotomus.com")

I have conducted a "simple" experiment.  I gave a copy of Ubuntu 9.04 Jaunty Jackalope, to two people.

Person #1 - Jack.  Jack is quite computer savvy.  He builds his on computers, overclocks processors, understands the in's and out's fairly well, and has been a Windows user since Windows 3.1.  We've talked about the performance differences between SATA and UDMA.  But Jack has never run Linux.  

Person #2 - Rose.  Rose is about as computer illiterate (to coin the phrase) as your great-grandmother.  If asked what she is running on her system, she would respond with "I don't know, it says 'Vaio' on it."  She asks questions like "If I got a bigger hard drive, would my computer run faster?" and "How do I get more hertz?"

So clearly we know the difference between these two people. 

My experiment was to see which one would respond better to Ubuntu Linux.  I told them to install it via Wubi.  (for my sake!)  And, I told them both the following information...

[LIST]
[*]Linux doesn't have virus problems like Windows does.
[*]It performs better on most systems.
[*]It is more secure.
[*]You can still use all of your movies, music, pictures, etc.
[*]And I explained a bit about the GNU/FOSS.
[/LIST]

After a week of playing around with it...

Jack was thoroughly displeased with the operation.  He used it on a Laptop that has a discontinued ATI Driver, and has spent most of the time just trying to get it to work.  He also didn't want to take the time to learn how to run Synaptic (which is pretty simple)  He kept going to Google search for programs to download instead of using Add / Remove.

One question he asked me was "Why does it keep asking for my password when I open a DEB?"  He also didn't like the way Totem played back media with image flicker or tearing.  His final complaint was that the sound was too low.

Jack's problems were...
[LIST]
[*]ATI Driver Support is better in Windows.
[*]Hard to find DEB's, EXE's are abundant for Windows.
[*]Media Playback wasn't as good as Windows.
[*]Sound is louder in Windows.
[*]Having to put his password in for root functions.  Unlike Windows.
[/LIST]

Rose was thoroughly impressed, and seemed to have the time of her life.  She liked the performance, the simplicity of customizing the interface, the menu, and especially Compiz.  When I first checked on her, she had changed her theme, font, and screensaver.

To her, it booted fast, she could web-browse, run her instant messenger (Emesene, she found it in Add/Remove).  Play games, for some reason, she loves Klotzki.  She even thought clicking on "Install" inside Update Manager was WAY easier than Windows Update.

Her only issue was finding an alternative for "Kodak Photo Manager".  Familiarity was the only issue.

So Rose came into this unknowing, and what I found with her was...
[LIST]
[*]Preferred the GNOME Interface.
[*]Enjoyed the simplicity of Add/Remove
[*]Okay, who doesn't like Compiz.
[*]Accomplished every task she does on a computer.
[*]Boot-Up and Performance.
[*]Doesn't have to worry about viruses.

[*]The only drawback was some familiarity.
[/LIST]

Well, I came to my conclusions.  Familiarity seems to be the drawback most Windows users have with switching to Linux.  I think we all kind of get "set-in-our-ways" with things we like or know well.

Obviously this was a personal experiment, for a psychology class.  But relayed to you, just for fun.  

If you have questions, comments, or feedback about the experiment it would help with my paper.

So here is a counter example to your - overly simple - theory. If you want to work scientifically then having heard this counter example you will discard your theory and start looking for a refined version of it.

If nothing else, then it shows that not familiarity should be considered as the major factor but the ratio between familiarity and adventurousness, not the existing knowledge is important but the readiness and pace to acquire new knowledge. Somebody who is more familiar with Windows will need to be more adventurous to like Linux than somebody who has no prior bias at all. But again, this is only based on a tiny sample and is not thoroughly tested (you need to come up with a good way of measuring adventurousness) so is useless for a scientific paper. There will be lots of other important factors. E.g. if you are very familiar with Windows and are dissatisfied with it then you will be more easily attracted by an alternative than if you know Windows well and like it.

That's just my 2-pence.

Why dowe take these little daily routines to an almost obsessive level, and don't even realize it on a day to day basis?

Humans are creatures of habit.  It's been said many, many times over the centuries.  And proven in many, many cases.  

For example:  Bob turns on the TV, and goes straight to Channel 4.  Ever time, sometimes when he's not going to even watch Channel 4, he still goes there first.

Daily routines:  Bob always has eggs, bacon, and toast for breakfast.  Then he reads the paper
