### Post by mb_webguy on 2008-08-05
As mdsharp24 said, you don't *have* to upgrade to the new release.  Releases are supported with updates for 2 years after their release, or 3 years for a LTS release.  And even then, those releases don't just suddenly stop working.  You can continue using a release as long as you want.  There are still people out there using Windows 95, after all, and Windows XP is the most popular version of Windows 7 years after its release.

Even if you do feel compelled to upgrade to the most current release of Ubuntu, the upgrade process is fairly painless, unlike in the Windows world where you essentially have to start from scratch with each new version of the operating system.

I think the Ubuntu life cycle is fine.  The regular releases mean that Ubuntu always has the best and most up-to-date technology, and the support is long enough that you can "wait and see" for two or three new releases before your release is no longer supported.  With Hardy, I can wait to see how Intrepid Ibex, Jaded Jackalope, Klutsy Kangaroo, Likable Lemur, and Mischevious Mongoose turn out before I feel a pressing need to upgrade in order to have a supported operating system.  Or I can upgrade to Intrepid the day it's released.  It's my choice, and it's a heck of a lot better than having to wait 7 years for a new operating system (e.g. XP to Vista), and between 2 and 4 years for just a service pack.

### Post by wdaniels on 2008-08-05
Sometimes it's too long, sometimes it's too short. Basically I think it's too predictable. A rolling release as mentioned would be an improvement, but actually what I'd prefer is for releases to be made as and when particular objectives are met and properly tested to avoid regressions. Sometimes 6 months is just right, but I think Hardy is a good example of when it didn't fall at the right point - it's still not finished IMHO - there were too many regressions, some of which are still yet to be fixed.

But any way you want to do it has its pros and cons. The main complaint I have is that the versions in the package dependencies usually seem to get elevated to the corresponding package's version from the same release, so that if you want to upgrade just one new package (at least if you want to do it cleanly using the package system) you have to start replacing half the entire system bit by bit. I'd like the package dependencies to reflect the actual minimum required versions instead.
