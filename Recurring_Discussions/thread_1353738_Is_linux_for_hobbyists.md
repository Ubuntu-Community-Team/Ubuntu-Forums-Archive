### Post by WitchCraft on 2009-12-13
First, it depends on how you define 'expert'.
It's an unprotected title, so even your cat could qualify as 'expert'.

Second, as always, it depends on the circumstances.
Most of these 'experts' have a MS qualification (MCSE, MCDBA, MCD, MCSA), and work in that kind of environment. 

First and foremost, if they would admit that Linux is just as good as Windows, it wouldn't be long before they lost their currently well-paid Microsoft job. And usually, they have no qualification/clue whatsoever in the Linux environment. Not at db's, not at administration, not at programming, etc. And usually, these kind of people doesn't understand the system. They aren't good at problem-solving, and they don't like the command line, they basically only know what they were told, nothing more. And they know it. And that's why they're against it, it's pure fear.

Second, it's convenience. It depends on how you define 'good'.
For me, an operating system is good if I can control EVERY detail, if I want to. It's good if it takes few resources and I can do everything from the command line. 

For an admin, this is a nightmare. 
Imagine, 100 people in an organization with completely different configuration. The amount of work and organization this requires is hughe, and usually, admins aren't the most stupid and industrious people, so they don't like to work, and for them, it's just so much more convenience to have a standard which everyone has, one they can't modify, not to mention that you can do everything with the GUI at the click of a mouse. 

And they also like the fact that windows computers/laptops require more performance, because they use them privately (office/multimedia/programming/virtualization/whatsoever) [and that legally], but the employer pays for it...

Another factor is simplicity and market penetration.
If you develop applications commercially ('expert'), you have to make a profit. Now, while it's true that you can make profit with opensource, it's also true that you can make a hell of a lot more profit without opensource. It's called vendor-lock-in.  It's very convenient if you are the only person who can offer support for your software. You have a monopoly. Besides, if you make a program commercially, you want to sell it as much as possible. With 90% of computers running windows, it's clear what the platform of choice is. You would be stupid to only develop software for a 1 to 2 percent minority.

If you are a hobbyist, you do it for pleasure, not for money. That's why in that case, people usually choose Linux.

That's why people say 'Linux is for hobbyists'.
It doesn't imply Linux is in any kind worse than Windows, actually, it's technically better.

But if you do it commercially, you will always go for the 90% first. Now, many vendors tried Linux, but it was just too much effort, and too less profit. So they gave up on it.

And that's the problem of Linux. It's technically better, but it has virtually no market power, except in distributed supercomputer applications (Google, NASA, CERN, FermiLab).

It's a sad reality in the commercial computer world:
You can be technically better than your competition, but ultimately, that doesn't matter. What matters is where can the money be made. And that's where there is quantity, not quality.

Unfortunately, quantity is there where you have corruption, the accompanying vendor-lockin, IBM monopoly, and adhesion contracts, not there where you have quality and freedom.
