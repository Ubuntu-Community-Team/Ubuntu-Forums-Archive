All those reasons are the effects of simple economics. If 90% of PCs had Ubuntu, all PCs would be advertised as "Ubuntu-compatible" and all PC games would be made for Ubuntu. But if Ubuntu cost $10000, (almost) no-one would use it.

If Linux in general makes advances, more people will use it, and that in turn gives more incentive for advancement.

Competition is the only way to get cumulative improvement.

### Post by MikeTheC on 2008-12-09
@O.P.:

Let us be *plain*: there is *no* solidarity amongst the Linux developer community, nor is there solidarity amongst the Linux user community (which, unlike on *any* other platform, these two groups share a *significant* overlap.) Without solidarity, you *will **not*** get much of a consensus of opinion on nearly *anything*.

About all that you can hope for amongst the super-majority of the Linux community (and even then, it's *still **not** 100%*) is for us to agree we like a free-as-in-liberty environment, and that free-as-in-beer at the same time isn't too bad, *either*.

Don't get me wrong, I would *love* to see Linux displace Windows as a desktop OS. And, it's not that *that* is a pipe dream. The chinks in Microsoft's armor have become both large enough *and* sufficiently numerous in quantity that even "normal" Windows users -- as well as various companies and organizations -- *are taking cognizance of this fact*. As an example, at the college I'm about to attend, while all the campus computers are running Windows as their OS, *Firefox* and not IE is the standard web browser, with all the necessary back-end components built to accommodate that fact. It may be a bit premature to say that Microsoft is crumbling and will "fall apart next Tuesday", but *right now*, Microsoft is in the slow transition *from* continuing their success because they are a dominant force *to* continuing their success ***due to inertia***.

Perhaps one of the biggest factors to which I can point, on a purely personal level, as to why *I* don't really care whether "everyone switches to Linux" is the understanding (and acceptance thereof) that not everyone on this planet should even *use* a computer, much less *own* one. As far as Windows is concerned, it will be providing a public service if it fly-papers all the idiots out there and keeps them out of our hair.

Speaking as someone who started out as a computer enthusiast in the era *of* the computer enthusiast (and watched the slow-but-steady transition of the computer landscape into a trendy, commercial and commoditized one), I would just as soon not have to watch that happen to Linux.

Just my 2¢.

n the 1990s Linus Torvalds used to give a talk called World Domination 101 on the early steps he believed Linux would need to take to achieve "world domination — fast" [1]. We've made a lot of progress since then, but Linux desktop market share remains stuck below 5%, which is too low to garner support from hardware vendors in some critical areas like graphics and wireless hardware, and too small a political base from which to effectively oppose software patents, hardware DRM, and other horrors. For those within the Linux community, World Domination 201 is about how to take advantage of an opportunity to go the rest of the way.
