### Post by Hwæt on 2010-02-09
> **Regenweald said:**
> Join date feb 2010. I've seen this thread many many times already and it always starts heated arguments. Tell you're 'friend' that the trolls feed the developers..

He is asking how the open source projects stay afloat, with no obvious revenue. Plus, he is obviously just a new user and curious about how open source works.


**KingKeith**, open source projects get money in various ways.

1. They can sell support for their open source software, or sell binaries. You see, open source does not automatically mean free of charge, and on the other hand, it doesn't necessarily mean freedom, either. You see, sometimes companies release a product's source code, but do not allow you to redistribute it. This is the case with some forum software, such as vBulletin. Specifically, the only open source projects that are free as in freedom are projects that classify themselves as "Free as in freedom" software, rather than just "free" or "free as in free beer". Also, sometimes, "Free" with a capital "F" means free as in freedom. Normally, just look for the project's license. If the project is licensed under these licenses:

GPL
MPL
LGPL
BSD
MIT

Then it is automatically open source. However, licenses like the BSD and MIT licenses allow redistributers to make the resulting product closed source, unlike the GPL and LGPL, which protect against such actions by stating that all modifications must be released under the same license, or one guaranteeing the same freedoms. This is better known as "copyleft".

**Note:** From here on out, Free as in freedom open source projects will be known as "FOSS" throughout the rest of this post.

2. Sometimes, FOSS projects run solely on donations from corporate users or average users. Wikipedia, and by association, the Wikimedia Wiki software, famously run on such a business model. Wikipedia raises an average of 6 - 9 million dollars every year in donations.


3. A lot of FOSS projects will use things such as Google ads to raise some revenue for their project. Sure, they won't pay the bills, but they might cover the costs of the web server, bandwidth, and domain name.


4. Most FOSS projects are not written by full time developers, but rather, by people contributing code in their spare time as a hobby. Now, don't get me wrong, there are quite a few companies that do pay people to work on FOSS projects. A famous example of this is with IBM and the Linux Kernel, who is reported to have hired 60% of the kernel's active contributors. This now leads me on to my next point.


5. A lot of FOSS projects have corporate sponsors, because sometimes, the FOSS project benefits the company so much, that the corporation using it gives yearly donations to it.


However, please bear in mind that losing sponsors won't necessarily spell the end of a FOSS project. FOSS projects often times have sugar daddies, but they don't die when they lose them. They just enter a period of less activity, which goes back up to its high activity state once sponsorship returns, it finds another source of revenue, or it gets a ton of people willing to just donate code.


I hope this answers all of your questions, Keith. Welcome to Ubuntu, and the FOSS world in general. It's great to have you!

By the way, you don't have to, but if you feel like giving a little back to Ubuntu you can always submit bug reports when something goes wrong on Ubuntu.


Have fun!
