### Post by Krytarik on 2011-04-03
Basically, you should run the desktop environment on which most of the apps you use are based on. Because, for example, when you run KDE apps in Gnome they use the KDE backend, which inevitably negatively affects the performance of those apps and of the system as a whole, although you may not notice them. Also, there are regularily issues with themes and such, because both DE are very different in that aspect.

Finally, it's also a matter of taste, some people, incl. me, don't like the *windowy* look of KDE. And of course, there are also people who like exactly the opposite. So after all, it's up to you which DE fits your needs/taste the best.

### Post by 1clue on 2011-04-04
> **chadmkidner said:**
> What is FVWM2?

<off-topic>
Stands for Fast Virtual Window Manager.  Virtual meaning you have desktops that you can page to.  Similar to the desktops in default Gnome on Ubuntu, you get 4 'workspaces' which you can page to.  It is a very NON-Windows, non-Mac approach to working.  It lets you get something completely different.

It's an old school UN*X window manager based in concept on TWM which was the first X11 window manager I know of, but more modernized in terms of memory use and features.  It is extremely customizable.  Very scriptable, but very manual configuration.  The way I always set it up is so that clicking on the desktop gave you the menu.  Right center or left click gave different menus.  Pick a function key and that modifier plus the click makes the desktop menus even in an application.

You can adjust everything from the thickness of the border of each window, height of title bar, position/width of scroll bars, adding buttons to the title bar (close, windowshade, zoom, ++)  and just about anything you want.

Look for themes to see what you can do.  Most of them have some variation of the default theme which looks really old school, but some people put a lot of work into it.

[http://www.fvwm.org/](http://www.fvwm.org/)

</off-topic>
