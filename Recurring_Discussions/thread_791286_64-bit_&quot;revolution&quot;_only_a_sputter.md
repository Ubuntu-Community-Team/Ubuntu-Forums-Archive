### Post by jamyskis on 2008-05-12
Is it just me, or has the so-called 64-bit revolution been fairly uneventful so far? The first processors came out 6-7 years now, and 64-bit is now almost all you can buy.

The problem is that most people are still using 32-bit OSs, and even 64-bit PCs preinstalled with Windows Vista are often preinstalled with the 32-bit version of it. I know why this is - compatbility problems, the uselessness of having 64-bit software because of its marketability and so on.

I think part of the marketing problem with 64-bit is that Intel and AMD were showing it off as being next-gen, fast as lightning etc. when in fact, 64-bit offers very few advantages in speed whatsoever. What it does offer is the possibility of addressing more than 4GB RAM, as 64-bit offers a larger address space. 

I suppose in that sense, 64-bit is really only going to take off when having more than 4GB RAM is going to be an advantage. However, considering people get by fine with 1GB at the moment (most modern PCs have 2GB), I can't see this happening anytime soon.

Any thoughts?
