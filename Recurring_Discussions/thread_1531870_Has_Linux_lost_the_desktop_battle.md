Steps to repeat:

1. Visit a local PC store.

What happens:
2. Observe that a majority of PCs for sale have non-free software pre-installed.

3. The system shall become more and more user friendly as time passes. That isn't the goal of *all* of Linux, but that is the primary goal of Ubuntu Linux.

### Post by Jay Car on 2010-07-16
It's interesting to me that so many people have an either/or attitude about "winning." I believe they aren't looking at the big picture. Here are the changes I've seen over the last 5 years. 

Five years ago, in the circle of people closest to me (i.e. family, long-standing friends, neighbors, and people I work with regularly) all of them used Windows. Today, only one remains a totally-Windows-only user - right down to Live mail, and an MSN home page using IE (shudder). 

Of the rest, several have switched to Macs, some are now dual-booting between Linux and Windows, others that still use Windows at least use non-Microsoft apps like Firefox, Chrome, Open Office or AbiWord, Gimp, Scribus and Inscape, but NONE of them are still using ONLY Microsoft software. Which means that, if (when!) they decide to move away from Windows, they won't have to leave their favorite apps behind.

In the last five years alone, FOSS has come a long, long way. I don't know what "winning" means to others, but to me, it's the idea of diversity that's winning. Few people are happily keeping all their eggs in a Microsoft basket these days. I'd say that's a damned fine outcome, and I can see this trend continuing to pick up speed. Small pockets of change, growing steadily, one computer at a time.

It's the kind of true grass-roots movement that all of Microsoft's billions of dollars can't buy...all they can seem to afford these days is astro-turf. :D
