> I understand it's a free implementation of Microsoft's .net, is that right?

In the same way that Firefox is "a free implementation of Microsoft's Internet Explorer". Mono is an implementation of two published standards, primarily from Microsoft (plus a few others like HP).

> I also understand that Microsoft "own" the patent to .net, and could sue any distributor using Mono whenever they feel like it?

There isn't a list of patents which Microsoft claim cover the two standards mentioned earlier, and Microsoft have never shared any information regarding patents they feel are infringed. They MIGHT hold some patents, in which case, they could indeed issue a cease & desist regarding their use. They might hold patents relating to their specific implementation, but not affecting the Mono or DotGNU Portable.NET implementations. They might hold patents covering all implementations which are easily invalidated through prior art, or worked around. Maybe. It's all a huge guessing game, with people on one side saying "show us the code" and on the other side going "don't use it or you'll get sued as soon as tomorrow!". Which you personally feel is the better course of action is up to you. Similarly Microsoft MIGHT own a lot of patents covering a LOT of things.

> My main question is, isn't Canonical based in London? There are no software patents in the UK (afaik) so surely Microsoft couldn't sue Canonical anyway?

They could certainly manage something if they decided on that course of action - e.g. Ubuntu is preinstalled on computers sold in the US.

> Basically I'd like someone to explain to me exactly what Mono is, and why the fanboyz think it's bad to be included in Linux distros.

As said, it's an implementation of the runtime defined in ECMA 335 (ISO/IEC 23271), a compiler for the language defined in ECMA 334 (ISO/IEC 23270). It also extends those standards with some of its own technologies (such as CPU acceleration, live injectable C# shell, and general libraries such as Cecil, GTK#, and so on). It ALSO implements clones of various non-standard libraries found in Microsoft's implementation, such as ASP.NET, ADO.NET, and System.Windows.Forms.

The extent of opposition varies between both what is scary and the suggested action to take, but the general short version is "eek, Microsoft!". Different people have different attitudes - fr'example, Canonical's position is at [https://lists.ubuntu.com/archives/ubuntu-devel-announce/2009-June/000584.html](https://lists.ubuntu.com/archives/ubuntu-devel-announce/2009-June/000584.html).

While it is true that **some** projects may entirely ignore patent issues, it is significant that in the case of Mono, it is the Project which initially raised the issue. The Mono Project were the ones who, in their licensing FAQ pronounced that Microsoft held patents on the core technology they were implementing; and then presented reasoning why those patents were inconsequential. 

The Mono Project itself chose not to ignore patent issues, deciding instead to address them with claims of promises allegedly being provided by the patent holders. Given that, it is perfectly understandable that people should strive to substantiate the validity of the reasoning being presented. 

Unfortunately, over the last six years or so, the Mono Project had eschewed responding to the concerns being raised other than to deride the skeptics and accuse them of "spreading FUD". I can't think of a single Free Software project besides the Mono Project which has taken such a philistine approach to the issue of patents. 

If you examine other Free Software projects, you will find that often patent issues are not being ignored, but instead being scrupulously and reservedly addressed. GIMP plug-ins have been removed because of patented methods; FFMPEG has separated out questionable codecs so that distro developers can easily exclude them; the SAMBA project has taken extraordinary measures to gain access to associated patents and thus been able to effectively work around them. The GNU Compiler Collection (GCC) upon which all major distros rely has foregone implementation of speed and memory size optimizations due to patent incumbrances. The Xiph project is currently pursuing legal counsel in an effort to alleviate patent concerns that have recently been raised with regard to Theora.

Before you buy into the Mono advocates' "persecution complex" rationalizations, do some research on how other projects respond to the same issues being raised. Nowhere will you encounter anything akin to the vitriolic, intolerant, and dismissive reactions that have been the hallmark of the Mono Project.
