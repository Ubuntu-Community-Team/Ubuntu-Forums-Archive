### Post by MikeTheC on 2009-08-12
@OP:

I would also echo the sentiment of my fellow UF members who've posted up-thread.

I think that perhaps what you think you're looking for is a kind of mythos, much in the same sense that the phrase "back in the good ole days" attempts to describe an earlier time as better than the present. But the truth is that all distros, like all periods in history, have their downsides as much as they have anything positive going for them.

You need to ask yourself what it is you honestly believe you're missing out on, and then see if you can find a distro that best addresses that. To be honest, unless you really want to just tinker (as opposed to being productive) I doubt you'll find much actual real-world practicality in using a different distro than Ubuntu or, to a lesser extent, Fedora.

While it was cool and all "back in the day" to be able to tinker a bit, or be able to do all kinds of "hands-on" work and repairs and troubleshooting and whatever else, I find I far prefer stuff that "just works" and can take care of and maintain itself, so I can get on with what I really want to use my computer for at any given moment.

In my estimation, on the Linux side of things, I believe Ubuntu represents the best compromise for normal users. It is based on what's arguably the most solid, stable and supported distro on the planet, yet it offers all the latest up-to-date services and applications. And unless you're sitting there a la Richard Stallman and denying yourself the benefit of the modern and robust computing experience on the basis of principle -- that is to say, have 0 bits of software on your computer that isn't 100% open-source -- then there's little point in using a less-supported distro.

I believe what makes Ubuntu so good for new Linux users is the very same thing which makes it good for experienced and advanced users. It's stable, supported, feature-rich, and feels well-polished. Why would you want to give that up?

### Post by JillSwift on 2009-08-13
Ubuntu is nothing more or less than a set of software tools that let you make use of computer hardware. That's it.

That Ubuntu is good at self-configuration is one of those tools. Because of that, it is often valued as good for those new to Linux based OSs. They can be off and exploring with minimal frustration. (mostly)

That does not, however, mean that's the only possible value you can attach to that particular tool set. It can also be valued by those who are not interested in going through the effort of setting up the OS, no matter that they possess the knowledge even so far as to compile a kernel from scratch and build their very personalized and highly tweaked OS of their own.

With that in mind, consider that Ubuntu has a tool that makes it useful to those interested in Linux but have little knowledge of it. That is, Ubuntu is a distro **useful** to new users. As opposed to being a distro **for** new users alone.

### Post by mcduck on 2009-08-13
> **Tipped OuT said:**
> 
This is the year 2009 going on 2010. I shouldn't have to be entering commands, and compiling programs.
Here's how I see command-line interfaces, might give you  bit different point of view:

Have you noticed how many sci-fi movies and stories view speech-controlled computers as the future? Being able to simply tell your computer what to do without having to use gadgets to work through the user interface..

Interestingly, CLI is very close to that, much closer than any graphical interface we've ever seen. You are simply talking to the computer and telling it what it should do.

Of course current technology limits us a bit, and we have to use keyboard instead of speech to give the commands, and the computer doesn't understand normal human languages (which are pretty bad suited for this kind of purposes any way, with far too much room for misinterpretations and badly suited for complex mathematical/logical tasks.
