### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-07-09
I agree- we shouldn't be making applications in attempts to match Windows, but to just make a good program from the very beginning that's entirely separate from the idea of what a GUI should be. However, it should generally follow the HIG for that DE.

I see your point, but one big problem is that many people don't bother to look for alternative software, so they don't realize there is something perfect for the job.

At this point, even though we're getting plenty more vendor support than ever, it'd be a good idea to focus some development on Wine just in case things don't turn out as well as we hope. Linux would then become a very universal OS, if Wine were perfect.

About the Flash bug- Flash is closed, so that is an issue. However, with alternatives to Flash and the rise of HTML5, it seems this may not be so large an issue. Also, most security issues found and fixed aren't flaws that have actually been exploited yet, but could be in theory, so they close it before it long before it becomes an issue. I however suspect the Flash thing wasn't the case.

However, to be realistic, malware of any form is extremely uncommon in Linux, to the point that when asked, people are very confused as to answer when or what the issue was. They have a list of Linux viruses, but most of these were run to seek out vulnerabilities and never got beyond the test machine, where they purposely ran it with administrator privileges. There aren't too many, either.

There are so few points of attack and so many layers to go through that, unless it's through an external plugin like Flash no one can see, it's pretty obvious that unless you're looking for malware, it won't find you on Linux. I think people become paranoid with the idea of minute possibilities.

It's far more likely you'll die from playing a video game than you'll get encounter malware in Linux. I don't think it's unsafe to tell noobs that this will most likely never happen to them.

### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-07-10
The most commonly found malware, known as spyware, is only found on Windows where programs are, by default, given the opportunity to run unauthorized code. Unless it's an installer program in Vista/7, which most viruses don't come through, Windows doesn't care what runs.

There are security holes, but they are found not because they've been exploited, but because they could be theoretically. Also, we have quite a lot more users than I think people suspect (just Fedora has about 20 million or more, due to their 'phoning home' to installations). But yeah, to keep this thread on topic, let's suffice to say that anyone who says Linux and other Unix (OS X, for example) security is down to a low user base probably hasn't read a lot about the security model, and if they have they should research it a bit more.

On another note, it's interesting to see, as Jane Silber recently noted, how much open source tends to integrate with open culture. Ironic that one of the Fair Trade products is called Ubuntu Cola, while it's interesting that labels like Magnatune and Jamendo are easily accessible by most Linux music players, and many people who have a moderate standpoint on open source tend to have more progressive ways of thinking.

Have you noticed that many Linux users in the mainstream tend to be the type for alternative thinking? I'm not talking about the 'classic' Linux geek, since I don't feel the majority of users are geeks anymore. In fact, I don't think the majority of Ubuntu users ever signed up to this forum.
