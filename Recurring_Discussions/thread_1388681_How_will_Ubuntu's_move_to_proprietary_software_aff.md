### Post by Lars Noodén on 2010-01-23
There have been online discussion petitions before to try to get a native version of Photoshop for Linux since at least 2000, way before Ubuntu.  If it were a matter of **adding** proprietary software for sale to the existing open source, then everything would be fairly safe.  

It's not.  The move has had several problems so far as we see in Jaunty and Karmic, weakening the distro.   Ubuntu has become popular, to make an understatement.  It has been on its way to becoming the fladship distro as Red Hat once was, and might be again soon.  A weakened flagship distro will not just hinder regional development but also the reputation of FOSS tools in general.  It's like the old marketing scam of bait and switch.  Some would say that is the goal of *substituting* proprietary software, rather than just *adding* it.  

**mono**

Take the controversial and unpopular insistence on using [mono](http://tim.thechases.com/mononono/), instead of open source like Java, is one example.  That one wastes a lot of time, perhaps intentionally, and reduces the usefulness, reliability and functionality of what has been a flagship distro.  

[http://tuxdeluxe.org/node/299](http://tuxdeluxe.org/node/299)
[http://blog.christophersmart.com/2009/10/15/mono-is-a-trap-evidence/](http://blog.christophersmart.com/2009/10/15/mono-is-a-trap-evidence/)
[http://www.groklaw.net/articlebasic.php?story=20090717043855128](http://www.groklaw.net/articlebasic.php?story=20090717043855128)

Again if it were a matter of **adding** to the distro, there would be little problem, practically speaking.  But what's happening is a removal of very useful tools and this will hit developing and underdeveloped regions the most.  

**discussion other proprietary**

The removal of GIMP is another example.  

A distraction has been offered by steering discussions to the mechanics of network package distribution.  The unspoken assumption there is that  all Ubuntu users and potential Ubuntu users have universal access to high speed broad band.  That is missing a very large portion of the world's populations which, by all indications, appear to be making and implementing plans to use ICT regionally.

Material not on the CD might as well not exist, when considering markets without good bandwidth.  For these areas, what is on the default CD becomes what must be used, due to lack of anything else.  What is not on the default CD might as well not exist.  So, for example, removing an image editor means that digital photography is out and that the skill is unlikely to be either learned, taught or used to the advantage of the community because it is unavailable.

These are regions which are beginning to grow rapidly, specifically Africa, Asia, and also rural and large parts of suburban North America.  Yes, note that large portions of the US, even near major cities, do not have broadband or even decent mobile phone coverage.  And this has not yet been mentioned in the debate is that the largest impact of the default CDs is in the regions largely without broadband or even serious dial-up access.  

Because of this real digital divide, the magnitude of the population affected over a period of three years make a significant global impact big.  Guidance from a global organization working to advance a Global Information Society could greatly increase the efficacy in bridging or removing the "Digital Divide" sooner than later.

[list]
[*] The primary audience to benefit from the default CD as a physical object is not online and is much larger than the audience that is online.  So far all discussions appear to involve only the online audience.

[*] Technologies that enable the free flow of ideas and ensure technological independence would have more benefit to the world economically, socially, industrially and scientifically than using the CDs as a vehicle for reduced function or, worse, vendor lock-in.

[*] Freedom, quality and safety must take the forefront rather than trying to retrofit any and all FOSS projects into being copies of a vendor infamous for the opposites.
[/list]

**What to do**

Sitting tight and being patient has stopped working, if ever once may have.  There is great value in putting effort into new distros like gNewsense to backup or replace Ubuntu, but Ubuntu has great momentum and should not be either discarded because of a small batch of bad apples nor allowed to become a vehicle for anti-FOSS strategies and technologies.

It is also not acceptable to listen to people mouthing vague promises who have so little regard for the experience and cognitive abilities of others that they would ask that we disregard the last [decades of IT](http://www.groklaw.net/article.php?story=2010011422570951) and IT politics.  

There are some problematic ideas regarding the choices for the default CD for Lucid Lynx, this next version.  It will be a Long Term Support version, which means that these choices will be the default for the next thee years, for the desktop version, and five on the server.

In areas where it is the First ICT to arrive, it will set the landscape for many, many years to come.  It will shape how the users think about and use ICT for a very long time and they in turn will shape the usage and attitudes of the people they train or work with.

Even for established areas with good broadband, what is on the default will set what will be used by the overwhelming majority.  The antitrust cases in the 1990's showed that the majority, back then when computers were more tinkerer's objects, left the default settings.  It is even more so the case now, as I have seen first hand with students paid to do such work, or anyone can see by asking people they see using computers.

So good or bad, the impact of the LTS version will be significant.  Some strong, assertive, corrective action is needed.  Allowing proprietary software to be **substituted** or allowing useful tools to be **removed** will cause a mess.  It's always possible to upgrade to [Debian](http://ebb.org/bkuhn/blog/2010/01/14/ubuntu-debian.html) or [Fedora](https://www.redhat.com/archives/fedora-desktop-list/2009-June/msg00003.html).  But it would be best of all to make a good distro better and have three top choices rather than only two.

### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2010-01-24
> **Lars Noodén said:**
> **mono**

Take the controversial and unpopular insistence on using [mono](http://tim.thechases.com/mononono/), instead of open source like Java, is one example.

I feel an overwhelming urge to scream when I see this sort of stuff. Granted, Java and Mono are similar except that Java was really only recently GPLed and is handled by one very controlling company. Beyond that, Java is *not* a Mono alternative; it does not make an effort to fit in the Linux environment and, while GTK+ bindings apparently exist for it, they are rarely used. Java apps on Linux are ugly, plain and simple.

However, it is interesting to point out that [Vala]("http://live.gnome.org/Vala/ValaForJavaProgrammers") *is* a sturdy alternative to C# or Java, with some similar conventions but also some strong differences (mostly to its benefit in everyday desktop apps, not so much when we are concerned about quick deployment across architectures). It's a compiled language, its source code turns into C with GObject, and (thanks to the GObject stuff) it fits this environment amazingly. It just so happens that a lot of new software uses Vala. Therefore, Mono is definitely not being chosen over alternatives. However, the alternatives are invisible, as any good software development environment should be.


Building on what Aysiu says, Ubuntu is a free *platform*. For a free platform to function and not fail miserably, it should support anyone who wishes to develop software for that platform.

It happens that Ubuntu users are trained to install software from official repositories, so if we want this thing to work, those official repositories have to host trustworthy software from third parties in a fair and balanced manner. If we don't do that — if all software that is not blessed by the free software foundation requires the user to jump through hoops — those third parties will go elsewhere. Probably Windows, but if we're really fortunate maybe a separate distribution. Either way, it achieves nothing.


I think it's silly that so many articles discussing the idea of a distro selling proprietary software throw up Photoshop and iTunes as examples. First of all, iTunes doesn't cost money. Secondly, just because Adobe can now put their software on someone else's online store doesn't mean they are going to start porting it to a foreign (and, frankly, work in progress) platform.

I see this starting off with small things in a way that will be quite beneficial if the doubters can try to understand. The fact is that certain types of software are harder to succeed with on totally free licenses, such as games. You can't attach a service, or a book, or a pay-for-perks online store to a small independent game like World of Goo, for example. It's a one shot deal.
It is simply more fair to customers to be honest, charge for that game, and provide a single, simple, high quality product.

A notable thing, perhaps, is that id likes to open source their engines, which is really cool, but the content remains totally commercial. In this way, users can keep the software alive quite happily on future platforms. I'm actually kind of curious what people think of that approach, so feel free to fire off any opinions about it.
