Sorry, not a workable suggestion. The UK Government Departments procure their IT systems from functional specifications of the desired services based upon the Interoperability Architectures devised originally by the MoD in the late nineties, and promulgated across Government by the Cabinet Office. These Architectures are, and always have been, strictly functional and are technologically neutral. Contractors are invited to tender for, eg, an e-mail communications system for a specified number users distributed across a specified number of locations for a specified period of time. The lowest bid in the open competition wins. If the lowest bidder offers an open-source software based system then that is what the Department gets, and what it uses.

So lets do a bit of futurology on this issue.

Increasingly knowledge-workers work from home - saves the organisations megabucks / lottazlotys on central office costs. At present Big Corps tend to offer their home-workers company IT kit to use at home on company business - like a company-owned and maintained laptop and a company funded broadband connection. It could be that when the end workers have to control their own costs they will switch to FOSS alternatives to the company provided products.

No. Not, repeat ***not,*** the way that the UK Government procures its IT capability, nor has it been for at least the last decade. UK Civil Servants do not, repeat ***not, ***specify software solutions. They specify ***capabilities***. The Bidders are free to specify any software solution they chose to deliver the desired capabilities. The bids, in open competition, are assessed and judged on cost grounds and the least costly solution must be contracted. The Contractor is free to deliver the capability by the software platforms of his choice throughout the lifetime of the contract. That is the way it works. UK plc does not, repeat ***not***, buy MS Office from EDS etc, it buys an OA capability. How EDS etc deliver that capability is not, repeat ***not***, the customers' concern. If the Contractor were to be directed, by political diktat, to switch from MS Office, or Lotus Notes, etc to OO.o then EDS etc would ensure that the taxpayer got a very big bill for the migration and would never see any reduction in the cost of the contract to reflect the reduction in software licence cost. That is the way the business works, sorry.

Difficult to see how the present procurement route for UK Govt IT capability can be changed, at least in the short(ish) term. In the last 15 years most of the big capability requirements have already been contracted out for the next 20years, or so. I don't see many major new requirements in the offing. Sure, a diktat could be laid upon the existing Contractors requiring a migration to FOSS in the near(ish) future, but you can bet your life that the taxpayers would be made to pay through the nose, and pay again and again by the Contractors who supply the capabilities at the moment. I don't see this happening any time soon.

As I have already suggested above, I think that the most likely roadmap towards the wider adoption of FOSS, in UK Government Service is through the growing trend towards out-working, where the individual provides his own IT kit and capabilities and, essentially, plays with his Organisation over the Global Internet. In that world the big proprietary software houses have a very small potential role. If* I* have to pay all the IT bills for my work for Department X then *I* ain't going to pay MS £300+ every two years when I can get OO.o for free and for ever. Department X will never even notice that I am not using MS Office. I did so for about 15 years and no one, but no one, ever did.
