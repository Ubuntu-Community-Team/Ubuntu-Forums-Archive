### Post by JeanRenault on 2012-02-12
NOTE: Please forgive my english.

Dear Ladies/Gentlemen,

I've been philosophizing a lot about our community recently, and I think besides all its peculiarities and exceptional cases, two basic things are lacking in it:

1-Compromise.
In various cases, the development of softwares ceases or stalls when the first real problems show up. People should plan before beginning the development, if the software will never reach an usable state, then it's better to not even begin to work on it. That's why Sourceforge is a huge graveyard nowadays.

2-Cooperation.
There are uncountable cases of softwares that has the same goal, but none of them reach it. Instead of making a new software that has the same goal as other, people should simply join the development of the most prominent one. That's why Sourceforge has hundreds of duplicates for popular types of functionality.

The effects of the lack of these things are preventing the FOSS to advance proportionally to manpower, because even though we have more people in the community, the actions seems proportionally more dispersed. In my opinion, the community is becoming "bloated". When the our number were far smaller, we witnessed almost the same efforts in terms of development, because the community were more coherent and cooperative, nowadays we have much more manpower and the advances should be substantially greater - assuming that it's true that only the Ubuntu community has 20 millions users.

I think I pretty much diagnosed the problem, however, I just can't understand why this happens - why people makes nonsensical decisions.

The software the community makes, -not generalizing, but a great part of them- lacks quality and exceeds in quantity. Even desktop environments, for example, historically we had only KDE and Gnome, the others were existent but had no 'real' efforts on them, if one wanted to develop a DE, I bet he would end up joining the development of one of these, however, the community grew a lot, and the others began to have great efforts, and then more DEs were created, in a few years we'll end up having 5 "major" environments (in # of devs) and a lot of smaller ones. But do we really need this amount of DEs? Some time ago the average user helped the development also, now the user is just a user and the developers are not working together.

The same happens for pretty much everything, from text editors to music players, too many alternatives, most of them extremely low-quality, some acceptable, but none actually "great". Not to talk about kernels and other OSs.

The community is throwing away a big part of its efforts.

Sometimes when I fire up a software like GIMP, I always miss something that the commercial alternatives certainly have, and I see that I have more 4 bitmap editors, and there are hundreds of others, and some of them have features the other's doesn't, but people developed them, and it was mostly just a waste of efforts. If people had worked to improve GIMP instead of making a brand-new software, it would be much better, but people just threw this effort away instead. It's a pity.

When will the community stop reinventing the wheel?

My words must be echoed all around the globe! I have something to tell you community! It's a circular thing with an perpendicular axis in the middle! Now that you already now how it is, please, don't reinvent it. Better invent something else like chains and pedals, but the wheel is invented already, I can send you blueprints to prove.

Next time you think to yourself "this app could benefit from some little developing effort" remember that it's almost certain that this effort has been thrown away on other app that will never reach an usable state.

### Post by JDShu on 2012-02-13
We need to distinguish between business vs volunteers and open source vs proprietary.

The advantage of a business compared to a group of volunteers is clearly that the top down management process allows for things to be done faster with less bickering. (Although that said, I recently read [an article by Joel Spolsky]("http://www.avc.com/a_vc/2012/02/the-management-team-guest-post-from-joel-spolsky.html") that argues it's not that simple in a company either). On the other hand, volunteers will implement new features to scratch their metaphorical itches which can extend the software in new and interesting ways.

Open Source vs Proprietary is a different dichotomy. Open Source just means that the source code is visible to everybody and everybody is free to modify it. This means that a company like Red Hat is free to direct its employees to focus on a certain aspect of an open source project. It can be argued that a certain amount of red tape is required in order to get the modifications integrated into the project compared to a company's own software project, but I think this is part of the trade off between code quality vs speed of integration which exists regardless of whether you are writing oss or proprietary software. The main point is that nothing is stopping a company's management from directing it's employees in a top down style to improve existing open source software.
