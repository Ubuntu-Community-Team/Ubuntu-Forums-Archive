### Post by ve4cib on 2009-10-09
> **BrokenKingpin said:**
> I have no interest in it personally. I am a power user, so I need a full featured OS. It will have it's purposes for some people though.

Technically-speaking one could be a "Chrome Power User" -- someone who is proficient with Chrome's features and squeezes the most out of them.  Power users are not necessarily people who use top-of-the-line, every-feature-available systems; they're simply people who get the most out of their particular system.  (Or that's the definition I've always heard.)

As for Chrome OS itself, it'll be very interesting to see how it pans out.  I can't see it going anywhere at any amazing speed; there are enough people who are wary of cloud-computing that a few vocal nay-sayers could have a serious impact on it.

Given that netbooks are becoming more and more akin to small notebooks, with sufficient power to handle most typical user applications the need for such an OS on said devices is diminishing.  But if Google can get themselves into some kind of new niche-market for ultra-low-powered devices (think a slightly suped-up Kindle, or similar internet-connected tablet-style device with very, very low specs) then Chrome may be able to beat out a lot of the competition.

I think the more interesting thing to consider is what impact Chrome OS will have on the rest of the Linux community.  Having a multi-national, consumer-known corporation visibly backing Linux (at least in part) will almost certainly give the rest of the community some free advertising.  And given Google's resources, and their penchant for producing either open-source products or at the very least open APIs, there's the potential for some very interesting new libraries for developers to start using.
