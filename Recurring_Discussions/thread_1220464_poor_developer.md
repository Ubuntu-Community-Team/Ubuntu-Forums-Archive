### Post by gnomeuser on 2009-07-27
There are many options. If you do a specialized program the best option is often to live off support contracts, remembering that while home users might be fine with filing a bug and waiting till you get around to fixing it, corporate users often need 24/7 access to tech support. Often they get this through a company like Canonical or Red Hat. This type of agreement is also incidentally how Microsoft makes the majority of their money, not selling boxed software.

Additionally you can use your software to advertise yourself, do something impressive and offer up your talent for specialized development. Effectively turning your program into your job application. Novell e.g. does a lot of specialized development for their partners and that is how they maintain a lot of kernel developers. Remember most software companies doesn't understand Linux, and being able to port their software or develop a driver for their devices for Linux users is big business. You could also be called upon for tasks like creating small to mid-sized applications this way.

Lastly, you can work off donations, this is a shaky method of development though and communicating your desire to get donations is hard, e.g. users tend to get pissy if the application nags them. Very few projects can sustain themselves this way. It isn't because Linux users are unwilling to donate, I e.g. ran a highly successful pledge drive for a driver project and we ended up with pledges spanning over 10.000$ with very minimal effort. I think if one integrated micro-payment based donations in the Help section of each application or say in package management frontends like PackageKit or Synaptic then we would see this means of survival in much better terms.

What mostly happens though, you develop an important piece of software, you maintain it and it's value is discovered. Some major distro or open source software house (e.g. Fluendo) notices that lots of their users or developers are depending on this library or program, and have they get bug reports or feature enhancements. There is only so long they can serve their users by having you be an external "spare time" developer so they will offer you a job. The Free Software world is filled with stories of this happening, some developer picking up a task he felt was important and it becoming his livelihood. E.g. David Zeutren started developing HAL after Havoc Pennington realized that there was a gap in the Linux space and issued a small spec for such a library. Quickly after David started doing this work Red Hat realized how important it was and hired him. Now David is a major player in shaping how the Free desktop is shaped, he has been for years.
