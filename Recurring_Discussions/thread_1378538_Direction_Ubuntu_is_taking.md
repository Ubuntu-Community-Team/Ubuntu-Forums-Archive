### Post by ooVoh9em on 2010-01-11
**Disclaimer:** [I]All views contained within the following forum post are my personal opinions and should be treated as such. I am merely an individual expressing my views - let us all (including myself) keep it civil and remain adults.
[/I]

I understand Ubuntu is a distribution that takes pride in being easy to use compared to other, more technical, software distributions that accompany the Linux kernel. That is all fine and dandy, but it feels like the developers are packaging what **feels** like beta software into the latest releases. This in itself is perfectly fine, because that is what the cutting edge releases are about. The most stable will always be the current releases that comes with long term support (LTS). Where the problem lies (in my opinion) is when the software restricts or takes away functionality. 

PulseAudio and the newest GDM both upset me. I feel that the version of GDM should have never made an actual release this early. To gain a minor performance increase (and a few other things no one really cares about) they literally cut out the previous GDM package that simply had more features. Especially in terms of easily adding themes, et cetera. 

I only complain about PulseAudio because it feels broken and creates problems for a lot of people. Personally it created so many problems, I uninstalled it. Now, my problems are gone. I properly configured PulseAudio and it still filled my logger with non-stop errors and would cause Firefox to lockup while playing flash videos. 

Both of these things were easy enough to fix to suit my taste and they aren't even that big of a deal - at all. Then again, I only think this way because I am a more technical person that enjoys fixing problems to pass the time. I also beleive that any Linux distribution is just as good as the next. In reality, individual distributions are nothing more than what software/optimizations you want to come with the Linux kernel. You can (generally) customize any distribution to do anything the next one can do, et cetera. I only raise concern because I wonder what the future of Ubuntu holds for their **target audience** if the developers keep following these trends.

Other than the few issues I had above, it was a good release. However, I thought this would be a good way for me to vent my frustration regarding these two pieces of software and spark an interesting discussion regarding the future of this specific software distribution.

Comments & feedback are greatly needed to make this thread worthwhile and not a simple QQ post :)

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2010-01-12
Ubuntu has to walk a thin line to stay popular.

On one hand, they want to make a Linux distro for "human beings" (read: Average User(TM)).  This demands stability and full functionality.

On the other hand, the reality of the Linux user community is that the people who are excited about Linux, talk about Linux, tinker with Linux, install Linux on other people's machines, and just generally move forward the grass roots desktop-Linux movement are not Average Users(TM) and don't generally put up with less than cutting-edge packages.  Witness the uproar that happens about 2-4 months after a release when some new version of firefox/openoffice/etc is released and it's not immediately available in the repositories.

Some releases get it right, some go to far in one direction or another, and of course depending on what packages you install it may feel like it's gone one way or the other.
