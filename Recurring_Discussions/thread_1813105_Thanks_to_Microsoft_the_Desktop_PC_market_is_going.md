### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-07-28
alexan, I don't think you're trolling, but you really don't seem to know what you're talking about, and I think you would get more out of this topic by asking questions rather than trying to score points in debate.

For instance, the image of the laser-projected keyboard you posted doesn't address the concerns you seem to imply it addresses. Projected keyboards are flat against the table, which is ergonomically a disadvantage, and they require a table surface. Both touch and projection keyboards are non-physical, of course, so they make touch-typing impossible, drastically reducing typing speed. Onscreen keyboards also require the display and the typing surface to be at the same angle, which is also ergonomically less than ideal. There's a lot of convenience and comfort sacrificed for no benefit on a desktop rig; if you're using such a thing at home, you're basically roughing it for no advantage.

Desktop and mobile computers are probably going to come to resemble one another more with time, and to an extent, that's already happened; look at the nettop, for instance. However, the wide arm motions required by a touchscreen on a larger workstation would be very awkward, and keyboards are still necessary for many reasons as I'd explained above. We're likely to see much smaller, sealed computer towers in the near future, and I still think touchpads like Apple's Magic Trackpad might replace mice in many desktop applications, with full multitouch support regardless of the OS. Those things don't make the desktop interface disappear.

A lot of the innovation you're talking about with mobile devices comes from a handful of factors that only apply to mobiles. Apps and hardware that take advantage of a platform that's always online and out in the world are of course very exciting, and the hardware capabilities of the phones and the software capabilities of mobile OSs seem to be moving very quickly simply because they're building up from nothing.

But even if the typical home computer in ten years is a Windows Phone in a dock, it's probably going to be attached to a keyboard and some kind of pointing device.
