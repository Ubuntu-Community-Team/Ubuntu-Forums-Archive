### Post by Nick_Jinn on 2010-08-10
So, most people feel that file sharing is a form of theft. Its actually copyright infringement, but close enough in a lot of peoples minds. However, I am thinking of a lot of examples where material is duplicated that gets a pass....so I am wondering where people draw the line. Could some of these attitudes be cultural rather than logical?


Recording a video on TV with VHS tape.
Recording a song off the radio with cassette tape.
Recording your favorite movies with Tivo.
Making a 'mixed tape' for someone you like, using the dual cassette function, copying songs from various albums that you have. 
Making the exact same mixed tape from CDs.
Making the exact same mixed tape from MP3s from CDs you owned.
(so far we are gifting to individuals)
Making a mixed tape and giving out several copies at a party....your 'party mix'.
Going to that same party and swapping MP3 files you have on your digital devices that you ripped from CDs you purchased.
Sending somebody you like the original mixed tape, but only sending it to them online from MP3s you ripped from CDs you paid for.
Sending those songs individually to your friends via messenger.
Sending those songs to people you dont know via torrent.


In my opinion, its very difficult to define a principle structure that separates things we have considered fair game since the 1980s, like VHS tape recording and making mixed tapes for your girlfriend.....these things are just common behavior. There is a slight quality reduction in older generation tapes, so its not exactly the original, but I hardly think that a slight hit in quality is a sound argument that defines whether something is piracy or not.......there is the practical attitude of how pervasive it is....it costs the companies more money due to the ease of sharing, but I am finding it hard to pin point how many of these activities are fundamentally different from activities most people consider legitimate, or have since the previous generation. Not concrete and easily defined in legal terms anyway. People have been making people mixed tapes or copies of cassette tapes, or cassette from CD for a long long time. Perhaps a mixed tape is fair use because it requires some original creativity where just making copies isnt? Most people who are against piracy probably dont frown on making mix tapes for close friends, or maybe some do.


Obviously modern file sharing is a bigger threat to a very wealthy industry. In practical terms it can cause more economic harm....though in some instances it has been argued that it might not hurt sales as much as people think, since many pirates couldnt afford the music they have so its not a loss of net revenue from what they would have spent....and it arguably helps lesser known bands by spreading their popularity, much like being heard on the radio. Some people also note that musicians getting taken advantage of, though that is definitely not a legal argument.....some disgruntled musicians upset with their labels have advocated piracy of their music to get back at their signers....but yeah, not a legal argument.


Still, I imagine a lot of movies were taped off the public airwaves, and a lot of mixed tapes and copies have been given out since the 80s, yet people for some odd reason dont view it as harshly as digital file sharing between strangers. Is this the result of media propaganda or is this actually a logically consistent argument?


I am not advocating piracy, just asking where you draw the line in that list.
