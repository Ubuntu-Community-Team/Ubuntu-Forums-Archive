### Post by RedRat on 2010-07-23
I would agree with those who say read and do.  Before you start, get a good book on Linux, try to understand where it is coming from. It will introduce you to the basic system and you learn about command lines and graphic user interfaces or GUIs. 

For a novice, try not to break the system. Get a good stable installation on your computer. I would suggest that you get a computer that is solely devoted to Linux (I recommend Ubuntu), download all the patches from the update manager and then experiment starting off small. Get the hang of the Gnome or KDE panel. I would suggest Gnome for a start, the KDE panel can get a bit overbearing, but then to each his own. Some will encourage breaking or trying more complex things, but take your time. Linux, unlike Windows or Mac, assumes that you know what you are doing, and heaven help you if you don't. As you become more proficient and comfortable with the system, then experiment and try some new things, but take small steps before you leap.

Get your system going and take small steps, learn as you go. Get your multimedia stuff together so that you can play DVDs, mp3s, .avi, .mpg, and other movie formats. This will take some time, since the necessary codecs don't all come with your installation.

Most importantly, be patient and have fun with it. Once you get onto it, you will not look back at Windows or Mac again.

### Post by malspa on 2010-07-23
I pretty much agree with everything said so far here.  Crazy, when I was first starting out, I stumbled upon a used Unix textbook at a second-hand store.  Cost me something like $2 bucks.  I looked through it for quite awhile and for some reason that book helped a light come on.  A lot of the basics are the same in Unix as in Linux.

I also found *Linux Pocket Guide*.  Taught me more basics, but I felt confused a lot of the time.  That book focuses more on Fedora specifics (I ended up using mainly Debian distros, except for PCLinuxOS, and few short runs with Puppy).

After those two books, most of my info came from online sources, and from trying things out, messing up, trying again.  At first I had my XP computer to fall back on, then I was dual-booting XP and Linux, and finally I gave my XP machine to my son, as I realized I wasn't using it anymore for anything.

I think books are great for getting started but in the end it seems better to rely on online sources.  The basics stay the same, but a lot of things in Linux books is outdated by the time they're published.  Things move fast in the Linux world.  So, books for the basics, but go online to find out what's happening with current distros.  Of course, others might disagree with that, but that's how I see it.
