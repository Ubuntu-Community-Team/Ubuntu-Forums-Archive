### Post by rg4w on 2011-05-21
While I don't agree with everything in Ms. Martin's article, I do think it's wise to question the 1% myth.

The most common source for the myth is cited as the stats at Statcounter, but where do those come from?

My understanding is that those are aggregates of a relatively small number of participating member sites who sell specific goods and services, mostly in the US.

Since we know that Linux is far more popular per capita in Asia, Eastern Europe, and South America than it is in the States, and we don't really know the full range of specific sites the StatCounter members are comprised of, what happens when we look at a more generalized source?

Wikipedia shows 1.82%:
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usage_share_of_operating_systems](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usage_share_of_operating_systems)

So just by changing our source the audience size has apparently doubled.

But again, keep in mind that not everything at Wikipedia is available in all the languages it supports.  Indeed, there's far more English-language content there than in any other language.

It seems safe to say that Wikipedia's 1.82% figure is arguably the lowest possible count of Linux users.

Now what happens when we go even further afield to find stats?

W3Schools is one of the leading resources for Web design and development, and while that's indeed a niche it's not a small one.

W3Schools shows Linux at 5.1%:
[http://www.w3schools.com/browsers/browsers_os.asp](http://www.w3schools.com/browsers/browsers_os.asp)

Again, that's not reflective of the general gene pool, but if you're a software publisher looking for markets and your product is at all related to Web design, it may be worth noting.


Another way to look at OS market share is to look at impact on the market leader, Microsoft.  

We know that Microsoft spends many millions fighting Apple, with ad campaign responding to Apple like "I'm a PC" and "Get a PC for under $1000".

Of course we've never seen a Microsoft TV ad responding to Linux, because there are no Linux TV ads so why would they bother?

Instead we could look at OEM pricing to get an understanding of how much of a threat MS perceives Linux as:

In the early Netbook days MS OSes generally added almost $100 to the cost of a computer, and manufacturers like Asus offered more memory and more storage on their Linux-powered netbooks as a result.  During the same time we saw a lot of large organizations and more than a few governments around the world questioning their MS annual licensing fees and beginning to adopt Linux as a standard.

Responding to this loss of market share, MS reduced pricing in many markets dramatically.  I haven't been able to turn up precise numbers, and we can expect they vary greatly between different orgs and OEMs, but I've heard they slashed prices as much as 50-80%, sometimes even more.

The collective lost income from this is almost certainly on par with what Microsoft spends combating Apple.

Whether this is a reflection of current actual market share or a fear of what market share could be if left unchecked, clearly Microsoft see Linux as a threat at least as significant to them as OS X.

It may be worth noting that over the last six years (coincident with both the netbook era and the awakening of international orgs to open source) Windows market share has dropped dramatically, from 96.36% in 2004 to 88.26% in 2009, while during the same period those stats show Linux going from 0.29% in 2004 to 0.83% in 2009:
[http://www.tgdaily.com/technology/41291-report-windows-losing-market-share-fast-to-mac-and-linux](http://www.tgdaily.com/technology/41291-report-windows-losing-market-share-fast-to-mac-and-linux)

While those numbers have all the complications of any single set of stats, they may provide a reasonable understanding of the relative shifts in overall market share.


In short, cgroza summed it up well:  There isn't any single reliable source for accurately measuring OS market share.  And since Linux is free and most often installed on systems that shipped with a different OS, Linux numbers will always be the most questionable among any set of statistics.

But I feel the lowest possible number that reflects actual worldwide desktop market share is at least twice as high as the mythic 1%, and perhaps likely to be three times that.

To put this in perspective, remember that Mac market share hovered between 2.2 and 2.7% for the better part of a decade until recent years.

While it may seem easy to dismiss for its lack of advertising, I think it's safe to say that Linux is at least half as significant to the desktop market as OS X is.

> What I want to know is does it really matter what percentages of computers use Linux? There is no way to measure exactly (as has been said), so to me, there is no point to this.

My organization has only one or two Internet-facing computers. The majority of installed computers never interface with the Internet. 

Internet counters would therefore only register perhaps 1% of my Linux installations. Furthermore, this does not even mention the number of Linux virtual machine and Wubi installations functioning on Windows-based hosts.

I'd wager the number of virtual machines using a Mac or Windows-based OS as the guest is miniscule, and I've never heard of anything similar to a Wubi-based installation for either of those OS's.

Further, I tend to replicate an established Linux OS installation rather than download a new copy every time. For this reason, there is no way that Canonical (or any other Linux OS provider) can possibly guess how many installations I have running, much less an Internet counter.

I have lots and lots of stealth Linux installations, and I like to keep it that way. Perhaps this capability of Linux is its marketing weakness, but the inherent limitations of the census takers shouldn't be pooh-poohed.

If one wants Linux marketing, look to NASA and the US military (using OpenSuse and RedHat), several French and German governmental bureaus (Kubuntu and others), WetaDigital studios, makers of the Avatar movie, (using Kubuntu), the Watson computer that won Jeopardy (using OpenSuse), countless Linux-based supercomputers (including Lawrence Livermore), Google and Android (Linux-based), and the majority of Internet service providers and webservice providers (based on RedHat/CentOs and other Linux OSs).

Focusing on penetrance of Linux OSs using an inherently flawed census mechanism is old school ("it's so 1990's," as my daughter likes to say).
