### Post by Bigtime_Scrub on 2009-10-18
Debian --> Ubuntu (same family) 

CentOS(Red Hat) --> Fedora (same family)


Someone asked what is the difference between Ubuntu and Debian. The short answer is not much. You can do what Ubuntu does with Debian, the problem lies in, do you have the Linux knowledge to do it? Ubuntu is already set up for you, Debian is not. You would essentially have to build Debian into Ubuntu yourself. That is why many people use Ubuntu and ironically enough, why many people use Debian (so they can build it however they like)

CentOS is just the free version of Red Hat Enterprise, which is the "stable" version of Fedora. (I know this is a bad analogy but its the best I can think of)

Whatever packages you put into Debian generally works with Ubuntu and vice-versa. I'm not sure how much compatability there is with CentOS and Fedora but I do know Fedora is a look into the future of what Red Hat, and consequently, what CentOS will become. 

I use Fedora and Ubuntu and I love them both, but they are from different families of Linux. There is no better or worse distro imo...it is all about choice and using what you prefer. if you are new to Linux you probably don't know what it is that you do prefer, but in time you will. That is what makes linux great.

A quick run down...

There are basically 5 major "families" of Linux. Most distros are based off these major "packages of families".

1. Debian (Ubuntu, Mint, Mepis, DreamLinux, and countless others)
2. RPM (CentOS, Fedora, you could also argue SUSE, Mandriva, and pclinuxos as they use RPM)
3. Slackware (Zenwalk, Vector, Slax, Goblin X)
4. Arch (Chakra)
5. Gentoo (Sabayon)

Almost all distros fit into one of these 5 families and they all function and handle packages differently.

Yes it's pretty stable. Let me put it this way:
I've had more trouble upgrading versions of Ubuntu than I have had in my entire Arch experience.

I like to think of Arch as Linux with a package manager (I've said this so many times). (Almost) All the packages are completely vanilla, just make, make install and uploaded to the repo.
So the potential breakage situations are when major releases to e.g. Xorg are made (like the hot plugging thing).

So it's always a good practice to keep an eye on the forums or the announcements after doing such upgrades, just to know what to expect. From personal experience, I always mess things up and see the warnings later :P But then again, even if such breakage occurs, it's pretty easy to fix (from my own experience).

This means, of course, that all packages on the repo are the latest "stable" releases/updates of programs. So you don't have the latest trunk kernel in the main repo, but if you want it, you can always grab it from the unstable/testing repo.

On a final note, if you keep your cache (or don't clean it very often) its always possible to downgrade packages. Imagine that you updated to the latest Xorg and something got borked. If you haven't cleaned your cache since you installed the previous version, then the package is still in your computer, which means you can install it (and probably downgrade all the dependencies as well).

PS: I forgot, installing is a breeze. There is a tool that ranks the mirrors according to your zone (basically pings them and measures the response) and you can choose the faster mirror for you. I usually make downloads of packages at ~400Kbps
