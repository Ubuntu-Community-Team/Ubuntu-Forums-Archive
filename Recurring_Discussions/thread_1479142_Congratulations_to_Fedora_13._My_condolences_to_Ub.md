FYI, the C# language is an international standard (ISO/IEC 23270).

The issue is not the programming language, it's the .NET platform, which is something totally different. In theory, one could write Assembly or Lisp for .NET provided the necessary interfaces are available. Think of .NET like Sun's Java Virtual Machine, which, by the way, is the real target of the whole .NET business.

Also, don't confuse patents with copyright. You can't copyright a programming language; you may copyright its specs documentation, code written in it, a logo for it or the implementation, not the idea. You assume property over ideas through patents.

There's a Novell-Microsoft patent agreement on this. Ok, this sounds like a pact with the devil, but it's what makes Mono perfectly legal as soon as Microsoft doesn't renders the agreement void... You may say one shouldn't ever trust Microsoft, but considering what .NET is about (knocking Java down) I don't think they'll do anything against Mono. Think it this way: they prefer to win less money favoring a volunteer-driven project like Mono rather than to lose money to Sun, a software and hardware monster corporation.

> 
GNOME was born because of the license of Qt in KDE


The GNOME case was totally different. The Qt licensing issue was a copyright one that mainly lead to mantainance issues, but don't forget that KDE was a legit Free Software project right from the beginning. It was just that not being able to modify the underlying library could become a burden for further development of the KDE suite and the GNU/Linux distros as a whole.

There were two responses, as you can read from the Wikipedia and other sites: one was Harmony, a Free Software Qt replacement which got obsolete when Qt was dual-licensed under QPL/GPL in 1998. The other one was GNOME, which had Stallman's direct support and therefore, it was "blessed" to bear the name of GNU; Stallman wanted "his" DE, not just to solve the Qt license issue, which Harmony could have easily done.

Another reason was the fact that KDE didn't followed (doesn't follow?) the UNIX tradition that much. GNOME also started as an effort to bring the UNIX ideals ("1 tool = 1 task", modularity, text files as universal interfaces, etc.) into the graphical world.

There were no patent issues and there was no danger of a lawsuit by Trolltech because of using Qt. It was just a convenience problem mixed up with some Free Software politics. The Mono issue was about whether it's legitime to even use it on a GNU/Linux system or not...

### Post by jwbrase on 2010-05-11
> **kahumba said:**
> So what, OOXML is also a standard, but the whole issue is (on purpose) far more complicated to "catch the unsuspecting and naive fish"

It may well be that. It may well also be that that's what they want you to *think*. They may judge an actual patent suit to be too costly (either in cash, PR, or some other resource), or too likely to fail, to be worth it, but may think that they can nonetheless scare people away from something by making a patent suit seem likely.

I think their strategy is as much about creating FUD about patent suits as it is about making actual patent suits

Think of it this way: They want to paint the map black. What they control, or can easily take control of, they paint black. Some parts of what they don't control they simply can't paint, these they leave white. Some parts would be costly to paint black, or would engender bad PR, or painting them black wouldn't stand up in court, or are for technical reasons difficult to paint black. These they paint gray. When they have the resources to paint them black, they do so. If somebody tries to paint a gray area white again, and ends up straying into a black area, they hammer them in court and manage to make the gray area black. If someone starts painting a gray area white, but looks rather timid and guilty doing it, they hammer them in court, which allows them to paint that gray area black. They hope that the fear of getting hammered in court will keep people out of gray areas. But if someone marches into a gray area boldly and starts painting it white without infringing on any black areas, they stand a good chance of making it white, or at least a lighter shade of gray.

Now it's good not to be overly dependent on the gray areas. Make sure your program can do things that have nothing to do with them, so that if you're forced to take those things out you'll still have a useful program. Don't use gray-area tools to do white-area things. Don't ever use black-area tools or go into a black area (in which case you will quite rightly get sued). When working in a gray area, use white-area tools whenever possible. And only work in gray areas when doing so combats one of their dirty tactics (such as putting together a standard and not conforming to it in order to create vendor lock-in, or carrying out "embrace, extend, extinguish" on someone else's standard). 

> later on as the lawyers are set to attack on the whole (Mono/whatever) stack and pop up submarine issues that were kept below the radar on purpose.

Our purpose should not be to avoid submarine patent suits (though it *certainly* should not be to provoke them either). Our purpose should be to make submarine patent suits too costly and unreliable to be a viable tactic. The point is to make it costly for Microsoft to paint the map gray, and to give them the choice of painting areas black or leaving them white. If they want to patent something, fine. If they want to make a standard, great. If they want to make one of their half standards with a not-exactly-compatible implementation and submarine patents, it ought to be challenged. 

> 
There's no _serious_ excuse to keep promoting Microsoft intellectual property and technology unless you're a Mono dev or a Microsoft apologist.

Certainly not to promote it or to rely upon it, but when implementing a gray area technology or using gray area tools helps combat things like MS's vendor lock-in tactics, it should be seriously considered. 

One thing though: Keep [this article]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/not-ipr.html") in mind and avoid the use of the term "intellectual property" without quotes.
