### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2009-08-11
A desktop *environment* is not the same thing as a window manager, first of all. A window manager is basically all you need to have a functioning system for your applications. Crunchbang uses a really awesome window manager called Openbox.

_Crunchbang does not have a desktop *environment* (Gnome, KDE, Xfce, LXDE, E17, etc) at all_. You get a gorgeous black desktop with a menu of key combinations to reach your applications, like Super+W for the web browser. OR you can *right*-click the desktop and select from a window that appears.

At first look a newbie would say, "Omygosh... there's nothing to click on!" 

That's because there is no desktop *environment*! And a desktop environment has a lot to do with the speed and functionality of your computer.

I loaded LXDE (the ultra-light desktop *environment*) into Crunchbang to make it "newbie friendly," but discovered that LXDE doesn't "play nice" with Crunchbang (even though LXDE relies on Openbox). I had forced shutdown issues, sound issues, etc. It turns out that the same persistent buggy issues seem to be common with all the Ubuntu/LXDE derivatives I tried, not just Crunchbang.

Crunchbang works best as is. I learned a lot in my attempts to add a desktop environment, but Crunchbang is not meant for that. It's gorgeous, versatile and powerful just the way it is - and you can customize Conky and wallpaper and all sorts of stuff even without a desktop environment installed.

-Robin

It's true you can install Openbox over an existing Ubuntu install, but the same could also be said for Xubuntu, Kubuntu, Lubuntu, etc... do you believe these distros are also "unnecessary"? Also, because Crunchbang includes a number of "non-free" codecs and applications that Ubuntu cannot legally ship, I believe a better comparison is CrunchBang and Mint, not CrunchBang and Ubuntu. Both Mint and CrunchBang are distros designed for end-user convenience, offering the "guts" of an Ubuntu system, without all the "Linux for Human Beings" baggage. I understand that many users find CrunchBang and Mint unnecessary, but the simple fact is that "ready to use out of the box" (which Ubuntu does not deliver) is a strong criteria for many users.

It's also worth pointing out that Crunchbang is currently #19 at Distrowatch (ahead of Xubuntu, Ubuntu Studio, Mythbuntu, Ubuntu Christian Edition, etc.).

There are two sides to this. At least... One side would argue that a distribution is nothing more than a CD download, ISO burn, install, and setup. Shouldn't be more than a 2 hour process. The other side would argue that decisions like that shouldn't have to be made. A user wants to install the OS and have everything work as expected. 

As someone who has grown up on Linux, I am ok with the choices. I can see from experience the difference between a desktop with Gnome and a desktop with KDE. The average PC user on the other hand cannot make those distinctions. 

My point is: for Linux to reach out and grab even more of the OS market share it has to be more approachable by the average user. To us these choices are great; they are basically a way to choose an ideal setup bases on our very specific knowledge and desires. 

My only goal for Linux has always been to offer a simple, free, and open source solution to outfitting any user with an operating system with the latest technology. 

In my opinion, one of the largest hurdles to Linux providing this is the confusions surrounding entering the world of Linux. I don't only want Linux for myself, or for it to remain a niche. I want it to become a contender in the operating systems market.

### Post by TeamRocket1233c on 2012-02-04
Crunchbang is actually really nice, I have it on my ancient PC and really like it, and the current #! release is actually based on Debian instead of Ubuntu.

However if you want a really lightweight Ubuntu, Lubuntu's a great option, and probably your only option.

Both Crunchbang and Lubuntu will run on 128 megs, although 256 megs or more is recommended for both of them, and they will both run laps around Windows Vista or 7, and even Ubuntu, Kubuntu, Debian, or Fedora on modern, high-performance hardware specs.

Basically, if you want a lightweight Debian with Openbox, Crunchbang's the way to go, if you want a lightweight Ubuntu with LXDE, Lubuntu's the way to go.

Puppy and Tiny Core are also good lightweight distro options.
