### Post by -kg- on 2009-04-05
Of course there is a lack of support from (some) vendors for use of their products with Linux.  Printers are a prime example (HP is one of the few that do support their products) as well as wireless cards and others.  Mobile phone vendors are not exempt, and probably one of the larger "offenders."

The major factor in my decision to use Linux for everyday use, while booting back into Windows for tasks that just can't be done any other way, is for the security.  Face it, the vast, *vast* majority of malware that is "in the wild" today is written for the Windows OSes.  They will not affect Linux because they are not written for Linux, and of those which are, one has to almost *try* to get infected.

Couple that with the fact that, as time progresses, Windows gets slower and even buggier because you must install all the antivirus, anti-malware, anti-this and anti-that to protect yourself, along with the continually running background tasks, both necessary and added by subsequent software installation.  Linux doesn't suffer from that quite as much.

On this laptop, Vista now takes *forever* to boot up, while Linux still boots in roughly the same amount of time.  Which is why I very rarely boot into Vista anymore and try my best to find solutions in Linux to handle my needs.  Only when I *just must* do something that I can't find a solution for in Linux do I boot into Vista (or XP on my desktop) to do it.  But you notice I still have Windows around.

Windows is not for everyone, and neither is Linux or any other Operating System out there.  I've gone through quite a few over the years, starting with CP/M and running a rather large gamut.  You'll find quite a few Ubuntu users right here who, like me, still keep Windows around for the few things they just *must* do.

It's absolutely your choice, and alternative OSes like Linux give you that choice.

### Post by Alterax on 2009-05-04
You know, I used to ask myself the same question.  I don't anymore.  There's a lesson in Spanish-American history here.

Cortez, on reaching the new world, burned his boats.  He knew that this was the only way he could ensure that his men would really get behind the idea when there was no other option.  Without a safe and easy way to get back to Europe, they had no choice but to fight or die.

I on the other hand tried the dual-boot option for a while.  Every time I ran into the slightest bit of resistance I'd run right back to Windows, claiming some deficiency in Linux.  Until finally I read about their business practices and decided it wasn't right for me to depend on them.

So I wiped the Windows partition on my desktop (and the mac partition on my laptop for good measure).  No more proprietary OS of any kind.  It meant that I was no longer elite like I was with Windows; I had to learn from scratch.  But the fact that I didn't have Windows or OS X to run back to meant that I had to get off my butt and learn to use GNU/Linux on its own terms.  In about two days I was fully functional as a user.  Another month, and my admin skills were back to what I could do after doing Windows for almost a decade.

Now I'm doing things that the MS-based IT crowd either considers incredibly expensive to downright impossible.  

Thing is I'm incredibly lazy and by no means a rocket scientist.  I know me well enough to know that.  And that's why I'm sure that had I stayed with a dual-boot solution, I'd still be coming up against insurmountable issues which could ONLY be handled by Windows.  I don't have those anymore.

My advice?  Burn your boats.  Fry the Windows partition.  You will be set back at first, but you'll find that in the process, you'll end up getting a lot better a lot quicker.
