### Post by issih on 2009-11-02
Market share is absolutely critical for driver support, and I have no understanding of anyone who claims otherwise.

The equation is very simple.

If the amount of money that a company makes from selling the additional hardware to the new markets made possible by developing a new driver exceeds the amount of money necessary to develop and maintain that driver, then it is economically sensible to do so.

That is only reason 95% of companies will ever make a linux driver.

For 5% there is prestige, marketing and other reasons for doing so, but as a general rule, the economics are the be all and end all.

The driver is not a service, therefore it is nothing to do with arpu. it is a one shot deal, you sell the hardware, if it works, then the user will not return it, and you get your cut. That is it. If you provide good hardware and good drivers, hopefully the customer will come back and buy your next model. In the case of Apple a small argument can be made that the average cost of Apple peripherals is higher than wintel peripherals and therefore the profit margins may be a mite higher, but it will be a small difference.

Now, motherboards, and other core PC hardware manufacturers need to support linux, because of its strength in the server market. Items that have a standardised interface similarly just plug and play - no shocks so far. For everything else, the driver will only appear if there is a profit to be made, and if it isn't used on a server, then chances are it'll never appear.

Market share matters, that is why it is bug #1, and it is Ubuntu's raison d'etre. 

Please stop kidding yourself, fine, what you have works for you, and you are educated enough to work around the limitations of the linux world as it exists....that does not mean that what we have is perfect or even anywhere near right.
