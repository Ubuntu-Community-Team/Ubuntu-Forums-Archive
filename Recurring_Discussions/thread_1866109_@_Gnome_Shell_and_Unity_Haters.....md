### Post by quequotion on 2011-10-22
> **3rdalbum said:**
> Unfortunately, computing is a lot different.

Indeed, operating a computer is very different from driving a car.

However, they both have both changed a great deal since the 70s.
Cars now employ computers for all sorts of functions, the carburetor has been replaced by fuel-injection, and gasoline engines are finally seeing competition from hydrogen fuel cells and hybrids. An expert mechanic from the 70s would need a complete reeducation to understand a car from 2011. While at the same time, car owners have had very little to deal with except to choose automatic or stick. The steering wheel is still in the same place, the signals and lights are never far from it, and the switches for the radio and the environmental controls are most likely in the middle of the dash.
Meanwhile, as computers have advanced by leaps and bounds users have been faced time and again with "adapt or die", sometimes with good reason, *and sometimes without good reason*.
Text mode interface never died. If anyone's used linux for more than a week you probably have met the terminal at least once. Winblow$ and MacOS have terminals too, although they feature less prominently.  There are plenty of system administrators out their using CLI as their standard (like some people drive stick) 
GUI theology has undergone some transformations, but the basic "desktop metaphor" still reigns supreme. Some changes had to come as computers became more multifaceted, like the notification area to keep track of background applications and panels to organize work in multiple windows. Other changes served no purpose, like WinXP's default hideous waste of resources and screen space theme, except to give an operating system a facelift.
Unity and Gnome-shell both feel like facelifts to me. Unity's functionality isn't nearly as well thought out as its look and Gome-shell looks oddly like LCARS.
But I don't need polish and I don't need science fiction. I need functionality that I can rely on and I need. an interface with as-close-to-zero-as-possible learning curve.

Luckily, there are always more choices in the open-source world, but I'd hate to start over with a new desktop only to see it's codebase abandoned or mutilated down the lIne.
