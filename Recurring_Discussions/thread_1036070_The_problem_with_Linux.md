And here's where the analogy breaks down: evolution and natural selection are not the same thing.

"natural selection" is "survival of the fittest" -- given a diversified group, those most fit for the environment will survive; and as they reproduce, the population will tend to that group.

"evolution", according to accepted definition, specifies that the method of diversification is unsupervised, random mutation.  There's where your glacial pace comes from, seeing as random mutation is relatively rare, and beneficial random mutation is even more so; and in any case, the changes don't become extant until the next generation.

Software development does not diversify by random mutation.  It's an intentional act by intelligent beings, targeted at specific needs.

Given that, I think economics makes for a better analogy than biology.  The free market is also "survival of the fittest", but it is diversified by intelligent agents making targeted decisions rather than random mutation.   

Even so, your point about dinosaurs and humans is excellent, and parallels the reasons why Vista is not being used on netbooks.  Netbooks changed the environment, and Vista could not be adapted.  The diversity of the Linux landscape allowed it to adapt.  The Xandros configuration on the eeePC, for instance, uses a customized icewm for the desktop.  If the only desktop environment for Linux was GNOME with compiz, would it have been as attractive an option for a 500 MHz sub-notebook with a 2GB HDD?
