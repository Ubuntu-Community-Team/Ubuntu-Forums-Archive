### Post by 3rdalbum on 2011-10-26
> **ade234uk said:**
> It will always make the desktop feel cramped and its even worse if you are working on a smaller screen.

I take it you haven't got it set up to autohide?

I would have thought on a smaller screen you'd be digging Unity, actually. It saves a lot of vertical space compared to Gnome 2:

Gnome 2: Two panels. A maximised window's titlebar. A menubar.

Unity: One panel which also doubles as the maximised window's titlebar and its menubar :-)

On today's monitors, the vertical space is lacking compared to the horizontal space. It makes sense to put the Launcher on one of the sides, to take advantage of the space you won't use when displaying text. Unity's "overlay scrollbars" help to regain some space lost by the Launcher.

Sure, the Unity Launcher isn't as flexible as the old-school panels. But it looks nicer and isn't prone to reordering itself on a whim. You can drag and drop files to programs on the Launcher to open them. The old Gnome panels weren't as accessible with the keyboard either, and the difference between notification area, indicators and panel applets was causing a lot of confusion in practice.

I think there's scope for the Launcher to improve in a couple of areas, but it's pretty good right now.

### Post by VanillaMozilla on 2011-10-26
**With Gnome 2 you can hide and move panels as you wish, so that covers most of the "added" capabilities of Unity with a few clicks.**

**With Unity, the basic operations are very clumsy.**  The dock takes up a lot of space, especially on a small screen, and you may have to scroll through the damned thing.  You can only get it out of the way if you shove it with a window, but then to get it again you have to rummage around through your windows and move them all out of the way.

**To find a program you sometimes have to scroll through screenfuls of icons** that are sorted only alphabetically, or you may have to scroll through the dock.  The screen is mostly occupied by views that you may not want.  With Gnome 2, my applications are neatly categorized and sorted into short menus, which I can rearrange.  I can also put shortcuts on the panel or the desktop.

With Unity this is especially problematic for small monitors.  **So if Unity makes finding everything more difficult with a large monitor, why is it supposed to be useful for small monitors?**  I suppose not using a menu might afford some advantage, but also at great cost.  Geez, guys, make the damned dock optional.

**Multitasking has all the finesse and power of a late 1980's computer.**  You can hardly start a new window of an application, and if you do, you have a hard time finding it again.

**Unity is also sluggish and requires too many resources**.  I have two computers that are identical except for OS and amount of memory.  The Maverick computer has 384 MB and runs well; the Oneiric computer with Unity has 512 MB and runs like a slow netbook.

Please **just give me back my Gnome 2.**  It would be nice if a couple of minor bugs were fixed, but that would be SO much easier than throwing everything out the way they have done.  And I have to say, Ubuntu Maverick is the end of the line as it stands.  I don't know what's next, but it isn't Oneiric.

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-11-03
I don't think Canonical's differences with Gnome were that enormous, and their resources aren't unlimited.

Forking Gnome 2 would be an enormous task, considering how many individual apps comprise the Gnome environment, and they'd still need support for GTK3, anyway. They didn't like the direction Shell was going in, so they evolved their own Gnome-2-based interface in a different direction. It's similar to their NBR interface, which they clearly had a soft spot for to begin with, and still looks and behaves a lot like Gnome 2. It has all the same parts as the Gnome panels, in mostly the same places; they put the bottom panel on the left. You couldn't say the same of Shell.

There's no sense in maintaining a huge, upstream project after it's ended, not when it means sacrificing effort that's more appropriately been applied to working in new features and fixing bugs. And even of the new features added, a lot of them seem like an attempt to keep up with Shell.

As for why Shell made the switch, I imagine the tablet market was a big consideration, and Gnome 2 is a very old project - older than Ubuntu, remember. Edit: I just realized that the implication is a bit unclear, there. I mean to say, it's an old enough project that there was some serious cruft to clean out and a lot of things designed around old computing conventions that don't apply anymore.
