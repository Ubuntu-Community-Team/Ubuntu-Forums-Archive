### Post by Copper Bezel on 2012-03-19
[QUOTE=Grenage]I assume the difference is down the Unity being an add-on, on top of Gnome 3. Gnome 3 is just Gnome 3 (good, isn't it?).[/QUOTE]
Unity and Gnome Shell are each provided by the compositor - Compiz for Unity, Mutter for Shell - which provides desktop effects, window decorations, and the panel graphics. Since Compiz replaces Mutter in Unity, there are still exactly the same number of layers at work. Compiz is slightly more graphics-intensive at its default settings (and can be configured more extensively, so that it can also be leaner than Mutter.) However, they interact with various graphics card drivers in different ways, and in fact, users may find that one or the other is "slower" on his or her machine, but not as a consistent trend across all machines.

Since I strongly dislike Unity (I revised that statement from, "Since Unity is a misshapen abomination") I'm lucky that my integrated Intel chip plays nicely with Shell, and I'm not affected by the fact that the Unity window decorator stutters on the same chip when moving windows. However, Compiz desktop effects are smoother than Mutter's when the windows contain video content (with a video playing, Compiz Scale is smooth, but the Shell Overview will stutter or skip the animations entirely.) These are anecdotal observations that have no bearing on an Nvidia chip, an AMD chip, or a slightly different Intel one. (Best performance on my machine, out of all the options I've tried, was Compiz / Emerald.)

There just aren't any useful generalizations. Sometimes Unity works better. Sometimes Shell works better. There's no pattern. They have the same structure and are theoretically roughly an even match for speed.

### Post by Mathor on 2012-04-26
> **ikt said:**
> Because they're on the side¿

Your typical Windows experience is devoted to simply that, Windows. The bottom, or left, or wherever you put the windows bar with the start menu, is more or less supposed to be for window management.  In a lot of ways, Gnome2 and KDE are a lot like this as well. With Unity, window management happens by bringing each button into focus, a more dock-centric perspective almost like Mac OSX. With the dock mentality, all icons exist from running icons and favorites, so there are really no need for shortcut icons on the desktop. 

Unity wins by ridding of a taskbar, having a usable dock, freeing up the desktop (I don't know if you know but filling the desktop uses more memory!), and having a dash to quickly open applications that are rarely used.  It is a wonderful desktop experience, and I've finally started to get it as the 12.04 experience has drawn to a close.

Gnome accomplishes many of the same goals, but I have to drag my mouse to the upper left hand corner to bring up the "launcher"  (though i do like the workspace management part of it!). What's worse is that every single time I want to deal with window management, I have to do this. That is one click (or two) versus a mouse gesture that can sort of hurt your wrist after a while, especially on large monitor screens.  I was a big fan of Gnome-shell and used it because I despised Oneiric and whatever that garbage was they called Unity.  In 12.04, Unity runs circles around my once-favorite Gnome-shell. :P
