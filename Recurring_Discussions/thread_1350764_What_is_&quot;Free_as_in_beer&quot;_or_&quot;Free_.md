### Post by Bachstelze on 2009-12-11
I'm surprised by how few people know where this actually comes from...

[http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw.html](http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw.html)

> "Free software" is a matter of liberty, not price. To understand the concept, you should think of "free" as in "free speech," not as in "free beer." 

This is an analogy used to explain the difference between free as in "no cost" and free as in "freedom". When you say "Hey, that store over there is giving out free beer!", you're probably saying they're giving out beer at no cost. On the other hand, when you say "In <insert your favourite country here>, people are guaranteed free speech", you're not talking about cost, you're talking about freedom.

It's not saying that beer is necessarily free, or anything like that. It's just a way of explaining that the word "free" can have different meanings depending on the context.

Sorry, open-source software can be proprietary. All you need for software to be open-source is that the source can be read by anyone who asks. If it can be read, but compiling it and/or deploying it and/or re-distributing it is not supported by the license, then it isn't free-as-in-speech software.

Software can be any combination of :
* Source : open source / closed source
* Freedom : free as in speech / proprietary
* Cost : free as in beer / sold or licensed for a price

The following combinations of the above are impossible :
* Closed source and free as in speech
* and that's pretty much all, I think. All other combinations are valid. The source to a program can be disclosed for security or ethical purposes and the program can remain proprietary, if enough restrictions on redistribution are imposed, for instance.

I refer you to the [TiVo case](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tivoization), where the firmware source was available and could be compiled, but could never be deployed to the actual hardware if in any way modified.

The reason for the beer/speech differentiation is that there appears to be no word in regular English (neither British or American, unless I am mistaken) to distinguish free (no cost) from free (freedom). Some advocate using gratis (no cost) and libre (freedom). I have a 7.10 CD here that says on the back of the paper cover "Ubuntu is *software libre*. You are encouraged and legally entitled to copy yada yada yada".

It's possible to make libre beer (you just need to disclose the recipe), but free beer makes one think more of the cost than of freedom.
