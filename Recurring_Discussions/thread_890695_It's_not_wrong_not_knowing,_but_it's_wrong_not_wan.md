### Post by pmasiar on 2008-08-15
I was thinking if it is worth to start this conversation, but then I found sig (in post title)... I believe in [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchronicity](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchronicity) so I just had to post :-)

If you are strongly offended by technical term "blub" stop reading now :-)

I strongly believe that reason why people come to this forum is to learn. And to learn you need to listen to people who have more experience and knowledge, and try to understand. And even if 1000 students thinks one way, single vote of the guru is decisive - it is not democracy but meritocracy.

Now it is little tricky because we do not have established recognized guru reincarnation, but we try to learn from each other: leading areas where you are expert, and following where you are not. And it is even more tricky, because I may not know if I am real expert in any area or not. 

And it continues to be trickier and trickier, because human brain has [Cognitive bias](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_bias) and not being aware of that fact will lead you to wrong conclusions. And one of such biases is [Lake Wobegon effect](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_wobegon_effect) ("all children are above average"), scientifically known as  [Overconfidence](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overconfidence_effect) or [Dunning-Kruger effect](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunning-Kruger_effect): **people who have little knowledge (or skill) tend to think they know more (or have more skill) than they do, while others who have much more knowledge tend to think that they know less.**

In programming, uberhacker [Paul Graham](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Graham) (which is beyond any doubt programming guru - if you don't agree, stop reading now) coined term [Blub programmer](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blub) (in article [Beating the Averages](http://www.paulgraham.com/avg.html)) which describes programmer with such cognitive bias.

"As long as our hypothetical Blub programmer is looking down the power continuum, he knows he's looking down. Languages less powerful than Blub are obviously less powerful, because they're missing some feature he's used to. But when our hypothetical Blub programmer looks in the other direction, up the power continuum, he doesn't realize he's looking up. What he sees are merely weird languages. He probably considers them about equivalent in power to Blub, but with all this other hairy stuff thrown in as well. Blub is good enough for him, because he thinks in Blub."

"By induction, the only programmers in a position to see all the differences in power between the various languages are those who understand the most powerful one. (This is probably what Eric Raymond meant about Lisp making you a better programmer.) You can't trust the opinions of the others, because of the Blub paradox: they're satisfied with whatever language they happen to use, because it dictates the way they think about programs."

I found with surprise in another post (thread now closed) that some people are offended by even using term "blub", compared it to "faggot". I see 'blub" purely technical term: programmer who is smart enough to understand that languages differ, but not learned features of more advanced programming languages (yet), so expresses (tricked by cognitive bias of overconfidence) that those features are not needed or "weird". 

My goal is to learn about features in more advanced languages than I use daily, to avoid being blub. And I am trying to help other understand and possibly reach that goal (so my wiki for Python beginners).

I am also surprised that some people are offended by often heated arguments - but as i said, I consider this forum conduit for learning, not "hang out and feel good" - a meritocracy, place there different ideas are compared and evaluated, "survival of the fittest" can be brutal but the only way to evolve.

So, my advice is: if you want to learn, be aware of cognitive biases which your brain is playing on you. 

Of course if you do not want to learn, you are free to use biases to full extent :-) but do not expect admiration from me :-) 

Surely CoC is still valid, and if I break it I deserve infractions - but learning is most important for me.

First, to base your argument on someother else's argument is not a fallacy. "Ad auctoritatem" fallacy occurs when you prove your argument just because someone else has said the same as you and not because of the reasons that other person used. Otherwise, a chemist writing a research on DNA would have to prove everything from the very beginning each time, from the existence of atoms, and subatomical particles... Absurd, of course.
