### Post by Copper Bezel on 2012-11-20
I don't think Nielsen's wrong in most of his assessment. It's obvious that he has a strong slant, regardless of what he claims about his objectivity, but he makes some solid points that would be worth addressing to someone who wanted to claim that TileWorld* was a sensible UI. 

Once it's actually out in the world and in daily use, especially on tablets, we can start to see whether or not the UI really fits together. As it is, I really think it's a brilliant concept in abominable execution, and I think it's too late for a service pack to fix some of the problems I see in it. The Charms Bar could support greying out unavailable features, for instance, in future versions, but the windowing convention (more on this in a second) probably isn't going to change within Windows 8's life cycle.

There's nothing stopping Microsoft from starting a new habit, like every other OS already does, of making iterative UI tweaks and adjustments between major releases. However, the standards that they've set for developers are roughly what they're going to be for the next couple of years. Oversights in those standards are real problems, even if they're relatively minor ones.

The biggest Windows 8 buzzkill for me in between seeing the initial demos and playing with a touch-enabled store display model was the windowing method in TileWorld. Initially, it seemed brilliant - something like Chrome's Aurora, but more native (as opposed to webappy.) Dock two windows, scale their respective sizes, and watch the contents and controls scale to fit. Sure, two windows at a time is a bit limiting, but it's all a person really needs for most tasks. Limiting the user to those two apps could actually reduce multitasking and increase productivity and focus. At the same time, the autotiling means no fiddling with window sizes. 

But it doesn't work that way, because TileWorld only allows one window to be usable (with 4/5 of the screen) while the other is generally reduced to a simplified UI, or even just an icon. There's no way to use this 1:4 split to look at two documents side by side (in different apps) or take notes from a video or web page. There really aren't any use cases that come to mind for me for this arrangement. 

What bothers me is that there's an easy alternative to the arbitrary 1:4 split, which is a 3:7 split. TileWorld apps will presumably have a portrait interface. That means that they can already adapt to a 9:16 aspect ratio instead of a 16:9 one. In a 16:9 screen, a portrait app on one side of the screen leaves a roughly 5:4 space on the other, which is a totally sensible aspect ratio for normal use. On Surface's 10.6" screen, the smaller window would be a bit smaller than the screen on a Nexus 7. 

Since one of those aspect ratios (small portrait) would come free, with no development hassles, I don't understand the motivation behind the 1:4 split, where *both* windowed aspect ratios have to be designed separately for each app. It didn't save them any time, it doesn't provide useful windowing, and it doesn't kill windowing, either, if that's what they were after, since the windowing itself is meant to be an advantage over iOS and Android.

That seems to me like a serious oversight. Use may prove me wrong. 

Comparing it to Unity or ascribing it undue influence on the success of Windows 8 is premature to non sequitur. Saying that the UI seems clunkier than it needs to be seems fair to me right now. Complaining because it doesn't work like a traditional desktop is shortsighted. There's a space in there for legitimate gripes for folks who actually want to see the best for the platform.

[size=1]* David Pogue coined this, and I think it's better than being called out on using *Metro* or using the highly polysyllabic and marketingy Microsoft-approved name.[/size]
