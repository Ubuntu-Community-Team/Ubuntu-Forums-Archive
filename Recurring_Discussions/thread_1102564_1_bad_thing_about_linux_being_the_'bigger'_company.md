### Post by Dr Small on 2009-03-21

2) White-hat crackers who break into other machines, but not for malevolent reasons.

The page had a really down to earth description of all them. I wish it was still up, or I had it.

Basically,

Whitehat, those who actively secure their systems, code, and servers. They search for bugs/expoits/hacks, fix the problem, and either notify the owner or publish the fix. They do attack and hack their own machines for learning purposes, but their ethics do not permit them to hack, destroy or ruin other peoples systems/servers/code.

Greyhat hackers are the middle line between whitehat and blackhat. They will help others, but are also involved in some illegal activity which can include piracy, system intrusion, using known exploits against targets, etc.

Blackhat hackers are the criminal type, and are most always referred to by the rest of the hacking community as 'crackers', as they give the hackers a bad name and reputation. They are involved in anything evil, including piracy, intrusions, DDoS, hacking government sites, cracking databases, selling credit card numbers, etc, etc.

Those are the three basic types of hackers. But the general media looks only at hackers as blackhats doing the world harm. Alot of IT guys are whitehat hackers too.

1) Tech "journalists" all seem to work for the highest bidder.  It's not uncommon to see "news" stories extolling the use of antivirus applications where the "evidence" is an interview with a Norton employee.

2) This is a symptom of what I see as a virus in the fabric of society.  Everywhere we look, we see subtle signs that a magic bullet of some kind is the answer.  Getting out what you put in is no longer the fad; now we are expected to do nothing and get more.  An antivirus is so appealing because it offers "security" with no work or learning required.  But that goal is impossible to achieve.  Another relevant quote (this one is from the novel Jurassic Park): **"Most kinds of power require a substantial sacrifice by whoever wants the power... once you have attained it, it is your power.  It can't be given away: it resides in you."**

Does this make the less knowledgable lesser people?  Of course not.  My mother knows almost nothing about computing, but that does not make her less accomplished or valuable as an individual.  However, she accepts a risk by using something that she does not understand fully.  It **is** her fault if she gets a virus.  ***Accepting responsibility does not need to be self-deprecating.***
