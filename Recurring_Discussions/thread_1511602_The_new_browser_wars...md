### Post by Helkaluin on 2010-06-17
Predictions made by a literature student, so you might just as well ignore them:

Midori/Rekonq/Arora and the like: 'Lightweight' is a growing niche. Two possibilities: these browsers might turn into mature products adopted for netbook-esque uses, or stay within the 'lightweight-alternative' niche.

Chromium/Chrome: I did switch to this for four months. The responsiveness and speed is still, to this day, superior to Firefox. And Firefox played catch-up on the feature of plug-in process separation. What makes Chromium/Chrome not a suitable replacement for Firefox, however, is that they are simply designed differently: the WebKit based Chromium is one single speed-demon, but the Gecko based Firefox offers extensibility. Heck, even the interface is written in XUL. But for its goals, Chrome/Chromium will serve the percentage of users who wants a responsive and simple web experience.

Firefox: What irks me is that Firefox seems to be drifting away from its original goals from the user's point of view. Rewrote from Netscape, Firefox should have continued a small core of essential functions, and offer everything else via the plug-in system. That's what makes the Gecko backend different from WebKit. Right now it just feels like one giant monolithic bloat if you run it on lower-end hardware. Hopefully Firefox 4 will address this. I heard they are revamping the core HTML rendering part of Gecko?

Opera: Will probably stay where it is, an interesting alternative that offers from time to time real innovations that are then implemented by others.

IE: Can die away, or embrace the standards. Heaven forbid another repeat of extending and exterminating will happen... I hope not. But HTML5 is, as we all know, still not a confirmed thing. And the web constantly changes, so who knows.

Lynx/Links/Links/eLinks/w3m/etc: Well, fun. But not for the majority of 'normal' people.

Uzbl: SRSLY?!
