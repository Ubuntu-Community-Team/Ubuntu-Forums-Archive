### Post by 3rdalbum on 2010-02-20
You say GNU and Unix as though they are two competitors.

Unix is a number of different "competitors", competing with eachother and with GNU/Linux. For instance, there's BSD Unix, HP/UX, AIX, Solaris and others. They are all different in many ways.

You'd probably find that some of the other Unixes are "better" than Linux at certain things where the vendor has developed software for a particular sort of task. But Linux usually has better hardware compatibility.

Ubuntu and Fedora are both GNU/Linux. The difference in MP3 codec support comes down to philosophy - Fedora doesn't really want you to add MP3 playback support, whereas Ubuntu wants to make it easy should you choose to add it. But neither distro will include MP3 support by default, due to philosophical reasons. Linux Mint is based on Ubuntu but it will include MP3 support.

### Post by Bachstelze on 2010-02-23
> **aeon.flux said:**
> [COLOR="DarkOrange"]is there any differences why to choose unix-based distro over ubuntu?[/COLOR] is the unix kernel more stable or faster (or something like that) than gnu?

There is no such thing as an "unix-based" distro or "unix kernel" anymore. Nowadays, Unix is not an OS, it is just a brand. In order to be called Unix, an OS must

1) Be compliant to a set of specifications (most of them are known as POSIX, there are maybe others), and
2) Buy a license from The Open Group, which is now the owner of the Unix trademark.

Most GNU/Linux OSes (including Ubuntu) meet the first requirement (at least for the most part), but are not willing to pay a large amount of money in order to be able to officially be called Unix, which is why they are sometimes referred to as Unix-like.
