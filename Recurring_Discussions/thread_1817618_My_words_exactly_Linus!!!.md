### Post by 3Miro on 2011-08-03
Gnome-shell and to a lesser degree Unity are just tablet interfaces ported to the desktop. I find Unity bearable, but Gnome-shell is simply dysfunctional. The thing with being dysfunctional is that you are guaranteed to find people that don't use the missing functions and will be happy with both GS and Unity. However, if you need the functionality, then you GS and Unity are objectively bad and this has nothing to do with "being afraid of change".

Desktops have been around long enough for us to have figured out the best interface. Open/close/minimize programs/windows, switch between tasks/windows and organize the windows around the desktop and workspaces. Those are the options that have to be accessible with minimal effort (mouse+keyboard strokes) and the best way to do this is to use menus + panels + docks (i.e. KDE, Gnome2 and XFCE ...)

GS may redeem itself with the ability to be easily modable and people are already trying to recreate the classic Gnome interface under Gnome-shell (those are at a preliminary stage yet). Unity needs a few options, but nothing that cannot be fixes. Until then, we can use Gnome-classic (available in both 11.04 and 11.10 as well as Fedora 15). I just hope that devs don't take too long to figure out that tablet interfaces don't belong on the desktop.

It's one click to focus an existing instance in either case, whereas multiple-instance-per-program users have to make two clicks to launch a new instance when the launcher space and taskbar are combined, but only one when they're separate.

Furthermore, whether you use just one instance per program or not, you're likely to switch focus between programs more often than you open new ones. You generally save more time by cutting the time it takes to do common tasks than uncommon ones. Meanwhile, [Fitts' Law]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitts'_law") tells us that you can point a mouse at a bigger target faster than at a smaller one.

Since you typically have fewer tasks running at any given moment than programs installed on your computer, if you give the same amount of space to a taskbar and a launcher bar, you'll be able, generally, to give more space to each task on the taskbar than to each program on the launcher bar. In the last paragraph, Fitts' Law told us that it's faster to point at a big target than a smaller one. So since we switch tasks more often than we launch programs, and since we have fewer tasks than programs, it makes sense to give each task a fairly large area of the screen that we can click on to bring it into focus, while keeping launchers small so that we can fit more of them in a given area (since we use them less often and thus lose less time in hunting down a small icon). And this is exactly what we see in GNOME 2, pre Win7 Windows, etc. 

When we combine task buttons and launchers into one item, we make it so that we have to hunt down a smaller target to switch tasks, which costs us time. Meanwhile, to make such a setup even halfways usable, we have to make our launcher buttons bigger so that we can aim at them faster for target switching, with the result that we can fit fewer of them in a given space. So our combined launcher-bar/taskbar ends up fulfilling neither of its functions well.
