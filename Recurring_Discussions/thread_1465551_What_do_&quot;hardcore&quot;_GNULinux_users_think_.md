### Post by srs5694 on 2010-04-29
Your question pertains to open source philosophy, not design aesthetics (in either a technical or non-technical sense), but I'm sure you'll get responses about both issues.

In terms of open source philosophy, OS X is built upon a mixed foundation -- significant portions of it are open source (especially from the open source BSDs), but other significant portions (particularly in its GUI) are definitely *not* open source.  Thus, open source purists are bound to have problems with OS X as a whole, no matter what they think of it from a technical or aesthetic point of view.

Expanding a bit, Apple has a mixed record with respect to open source ideals. On the one hand, they have used open source software and contributed changes back. A good example of this is CUPS, which is the printing system used by all major modern Linux distributions. It was originally developed by an independent party, but Apple bought them up, and CUPS is now developed by Apple as an open source project. (Check the [CUPS Web site,](http://www.cups.org) but note that the introductory sentence makes it sound as if Apple began the CUPS project. They didn't; see the [Wikipedia article on CUPS](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CUPS) for a brief history of the project.)

OTOH, Apple has frequently used very closed architectures. The original Mac was a very closed design, and current products like the iPhone and iPad are rather closed platforms. Mac OS X itself is tied to Apple hardware, at least in a legal sense (the EULA forbids running it on non-Apple hardware). All of this rankles with open source purists.

### Post by 1clue on 2010-04-29
FWIW, I think people who "hate" some OS and pick another one because it's "not the one I hate" are a bit extreme.  I can totally understand frustration and/or anger at what companies and people do in their own self interest though, when it affects what I can do with my product.

I take serious issue with Bill Gates and his business model, and I take very similar issue with Steve Jobs.  On the other end of the spectrum, I also take issue with Richard Stallman for exactly the same issues in the other direction.

In my mind, you need to give credit where credit is due.  You need to be honest with yourself about the reasons you pick something, whether it be Linux/Microsoft/Mac or Ford/Chevy/Lamborghini.

I've been a Linux user (meaning using it as my primary desktop workstation, not just servers, and not as an "I also have a linux box" situation) since well before RedHat got popular.  Don't even know when that was.  FVWM was hot stuff.

My third computer was a Mac SE-30 which was brand new at the time.  I was with Apple from fairly early on.  I have always thought they had the best user interface bar none.  They always had the crappiest backend though, and that's what made me investigate Linux and other OS alternatives.

I spent years using nothing but Linux, even at work when I could get away with it.  When I couldn't, for awhile I used IBM's AIX.  I consider myself to be "hardcore" UN*X, as much as anyone really.

On the other hand, I firmly believe that the very best computing environment contains both commercial and Open source software working in harmony.

When Mac OS X came out, my curiosity was piqued.  It wasn't long before I had a TiBook, with the PPC processor.  Apple desperately needed Unix under there, and the combination was great.

Really, the overriding reason for my choice of Linux as a personal operating system is openness and flexibility.  I can pick what I can put on it, and nobody is going to tell me otherwise.  Not Bill Gates, Steve Jobs, Larry Ellison or Richard Stallman.

Second in line for me is the stability of the core OS.  Also important are the configurability of the UI, the support that some commercial vendors give it (VMware for example) and the rapid response time for bugs and the ability to go in and tweak my own software.

IMO, Apple's user interface is second to none, and the second interface is a long ways back.  That counts for a lot.  I'm currently typing this on a pretty new and pretty maxed-out MacBook Pro, since I'm in between installations on my Linux box.  The mac is the work machine.  If you decide who has the best UI, you need to sit down and USE that UI for a couple months.  Apple innovates over and over, and everyone else copies them.

Apple has an ability to make the user more productive with less training.  That's huge.

On the other hand, Apple's closed architecture drives me absolutely insane.  I can't tell you how claustrophobic I get, stuck in an architecture where one company says what you can put on it:  The iPhone.  It infuriates me.  It's my phone, I should be able to put anything on there I want.
