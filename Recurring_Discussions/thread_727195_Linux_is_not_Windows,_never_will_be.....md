### Post by zcal on 2008-03-17
When learning a foreign language, the best thing you can do for yourself is to throw away your native language as a template.  Rather than associate new vocabulary words and grammatical rules with your native "equivalent," it is best to just learn them according to images or concepts.  Those who *don't* study this way will have a harder time thinking in the foreign language and speak/write unnaturally (in a "translated" tone, if you know what I mean).

I believe a lot of folks want to ascribe a similarly functioning Windows template to GNU/Linux.  The theory is, use ideas you're already familiar with to build your knowledge of a different computing environment.  But, as we've witnessed (abundantly) on these forums, using a Windows template only leads to problems and complaining because people expect/want things to work the same way.

Of course, just as languages can evolve together, especially if they're separated by little or no geographic distance, so too can computing.  Windows, GNU/Linux, BSD, Mac OS X (how many more do I need to list?)...all have pulled ideas from one another and implemented them with their own twists.  The internet's largely behind this, I suppose.

Anyway, I guess the point is that nobody should expect different computing environments to be homogeneous.  That'd be contradictory.  Some people just need to figure out that they can whine all they want that something doesn't comply with Windows standard, but it won't help them speak the language any more naturally. ;)

### Post by finferflu on 2008-03-17

> I think there are plenty of good ideas in Free Software, but most of them don't come to fruition.

That's exactly my point :D
What comes to fruition are for example Desktop Environments that resemble Windows, such as Gnome or KDE. I mean that they resemble Windows in the WIMP interface. Applications are mostly bound to windows, input and output are closely related to single applications, and there's almost no chance to pipe I/O between them. 
I will take a stupid example, just to make this clear: if I want to play some streaming music from Jamendo, I will have to parse the URL through the web browser before I can get the actual .ogg files to be added to the music player, in my case MPD. There's no way I can pipe Firefox's output to MPD. Not without going to the CLI. So what I do is copying the link, and feed it to MPD after being parsed by w3m, this way:
```
mpc add `w3m -no-cookie -dump <URL I copied from Firefox>`
```

I believe the interaction between most of the GUI apps in Linux is very poor compared to the classic Unix CLI tools (cat, ls, pipes, and so on). Perhaps we'll be getting there through desktop search engines and such, but I really wonder if we could have been there in a much simpler fashion, by designing apps to take advantage of Linux's modularity to start with.
