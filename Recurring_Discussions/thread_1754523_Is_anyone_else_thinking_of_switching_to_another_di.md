I share many of your sentiments, but have been encouraged recently by comments from Mr. Shuttleworth at UDS and elsewhere.

It seems from my reading that with Unity they've undertaken a very difficult task, and it's been encouraging to see the team's acknowledgments of its current shortcomings.

Canonical is not content to see Linux flounder with a 1-5% market share (depending on who's stats you read), and for the viability of both the company and the platform it must grow.  It's not enough to be stuck at [Ghandicon 3]("http://www.faqs.org/docs/jargon/G/GandhiCon.html") forever.

At UDS Shuttleworth noted a lofty goal: [200 million users by 2015]("http://news.slashdot.org/story/11/05/09/1820210/Ubuntu-Aims-For-200-Million-Users-In-Four-Years").

That's more than 15-fold growth in a four-year period. Big stuff.  Audacious even.  But not entirely impossible.

While a few have dared to make similarly bold suggestions in the past, none have done as much to make it plausible as Canonical has, like for example getting Ubuntu pre-installed on systems from Dell, HP, and others.

Such a radical change to the landscape can't happen without radical change to the platform itself.   Like the old saying goes, "If you don't change direction you're likely to end up where you're headed."  

For the last decade, [Linux adoption on the desktop has been pretty much flat]("http://www.netmarketshare.com/os-market-share.aspx?qprid=9").  That won't change by itself.  New rates of growth require new ways of thinking about the user experience.

Like you, I've had more than my share of concerns about Unity.  In Alpha 1 I thought it showed much promise, but when Beta 1 kept zany things like concealing menus by default I began to get worried.  Beta 2 addressed a good many things, but the final shipping version still has enough oddities about it (like requiring users to type to find apps, and not displaying all of their installed apps to show things they don't have installed instead), that I had to wonder, "Is this really what they wanted?"

Fortunately, [Canonical does not consider Unity quite done yet]("http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2011/05/mark-shuttleworth-talks-windicators-changes-for-unity-in-oneiric-and-whole-lot-more/").

There are varying opinions about the wisdom of making 11.04 the default download over the LTS, but it seems both the Unity naysayers and Canonical themselves agree that Unity needs work.

That said, it's important to put this work into context:

Canonical did not kill Gnome 2.  Gnome 2 was slated for EOL by the Gnome Foundation themselves long before the first line of Unity code was written.

Given this, the question for Canonical was never "Do we stay with Gnome 2?", since it was already going away.

Instead, the question was, "Do we wait and see what Gnome 3 looks like, postponing our goals in favor of an unknown, or do we forge ahead with our own design?"

Gnome 3 has far more in common with Unity than it does with Gnome 2.  Fedora, Debian, and others will, like Canonical, not be able to stay with Gnome 2 themselves.  This year many of the major distros will move forward to either Unity or the Unity-like Gnome 3.  Canonical has been getting a lot of the flak for a decision made by the Gnome Foundation, but ultimately all distros that had used Gnome 2 are in the same boat.

So how should a distro move forward in this brave new post-Gnome 2 world?

We could get into the design rationale behind Unity, but this post has gotten too long already.  If interested I could summarize Ted Gould's presentation at UbunCon in February, but for now let's just say it's an earnest attempt to make Linux more appealing to a wider audience than it's ever known before.

Whether it will succeed or fail cannot be judged by the current implementation in 11.04.   Canonical soberly acknowledges Unity's current shortcomings, and seems sincerely prepared to undergo more user testing and apply the results to redesigning the weak points in the UI.

The true goal with 11.04 isn't 11.04, but the next LTS.  Between now and then there's a lot of work to do, and there's much we can do to help:  file and triage bug reports, and participate in Brainstorm and Ayatana.

Ubuntu is as it's always been, a community effort.  Let's roll up our sleeves and make it the best distro we can.
