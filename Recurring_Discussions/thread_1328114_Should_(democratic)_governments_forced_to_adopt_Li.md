I don't think they should be forced to adopt Linux. However they should not as most governments really operate now, omit fielding offers from vendors and just go with a Microsoft contract. ### Post by Chronon on 2009-11-16
> **Redache said:**
> There should be a choice, as inferred by the word democratic. 

I think Governments should be held accountable for IT spending as the costs associated with IT spending can spiral out of control (like the NHS System in the UK). 

Open Source isn't always the best choice for the job, there are some proprietary software products that either have no Open Source competitor or are far ahead of the Open Source competitor.

Actually, in the case of government I think there are compelling reasons to adopt free software.  It enables the government to maintain stewardship over its files and to be in control over the means to maintain those files in an accessible manner.  I think it's an important point, maybe more so than feature parity.  With free software, a government can guarantee its future ability to access files because of the open-nature of the formats and the software used to interpret/encode them.  A proprietary package may offer more features, but it also shackles the government to the fate of the company that controls the software -- at least insofar as proprietary formats are used.

It makes much more sense to me to have a government body maintain control over the formats it uses.  I think government and industry should seek to avoid unnecessary dependencies on each other.  (Pardon the political statement.  I will venture no further down this road.)
