### Post by Welly Wu on 2016-04-10
This past Tuesday, an opportunity presented itself for me to purchase a new, sealed in the box, mid-2015 Apple MacBook Pro 15" with Retina Display [base model] starting at $1999.99 USD albeit I paid several hundreds of dollars less for mine. It took a couple of minutes to get my product registered with Apple, Inc. and to learn more about Apple Macintosh OS X 10.11.4 64 bit "El Capitan." I wound up downloading, installing, configuring, and setting up a mixture of free open source and closed source software apps that I already pay for.

This thing is wickedly fast. It's not the most powerful laptop in the world, but it is the fastest that I currently own. Downloading and installing software apps is incredibly quick. The other thing that I noticed is that Mac OS X is an advanced and fully featured desktop operating system and one that Canonical, Ltd. has targeted in developing Ubuntu over the past several years. While Mac OS X uses FreeBSD, Darwin, and their own proprietary software, Ubuntu uses mostly FOSS and the graphical user interfaces are strikingly similar if configured to be as close to each other as possible. The other major difference is that Mac OS X can install and run more closed source and proprietary software apps that usually must be paid for. It is a pay to play system and it encourages electronic commerce for digital goods. This is one of the most expensive platforms and ecosystems that I have encountered in a long time.

I like Mac OS X and the MacBook Pro 15" are their top of the line product models. It focuses on good user experiences and the community is far different than any GNU/Linux community that I have contributed to thus far. I find that some GNU/Linux communities to be a bit difficult to become a member of due to various personalities like mine and politics. It seems that the Mac community is more inclusive, diverse, and larger as a whole with a it takes a village type of ethos of community bonding and building. Mac users tend to look out for one another more closely and there is the sense that they go out of their way to build strong relationships and friendships over a longer period of time with fewer conflicts. I don't get that sense from here or other GNU/Linux communities. Put simply, I don't participate in any other GNU/Linux community because too many people go out of their way to ruin other people's days and nights.

I am interested in the June 2016 Apple Worldwide Developer's Conference and I am eager to see and hear what new products they will refresh this year. One of my aunts has a very old mid-2010 Apple MacBook Pro 13" laptop that she needs to replace but can not afford to do so soon so I might give her mine and get a top of the line 2016 Apple MacBook Pro 15" with Retina Display for myself as a replacement. Then again, I might not replace it and just continue using the latest Ubuntu 64 bit LTS GNU/Linux desktop operating system and keep upgrading or doing clean installations every two years in April.

One thing that I find to be different is that the Mac OS X system is not as secure as most GNU/Linux systems. There seems to be a strong emphasis on usability, tight integration, and fewer checks and balances in place to reduce the effectiveness of advanced persistent threats and attack vectors. For example, the Mac Firewall is not granular compared to GNU/Linux. As another example, installing software apps can be permitted from only three sources: 1. Apple products, 2. signed Apple IDs, and 3. anywhere. That's just not cutting the mustard in 2016. For instance, there is no Novell AppArmor or US NSA SELinux system in place to enforce mandatory access controls without paying a software vendor to add that security feature. In short, Mac OS X has decent security, but it's not too impressive coming from GNU/Linux.

The other major difference is that Macs are not designed and sold as gaming PCs. I already knew that, but it gets damn expensive if you want discrete GPUs to play PC game titles. While this is not a problem for me as I have other PCs to play games once in a blue moon, I find that the other major problem with Macs is that the curated App Store is quite a bit smaller than Ubuntu's main software repositories and especially when compared to third party PPAs. I understand that Macs are primarily designed for content creators and that's okay with me, but it seems to me that too much focus is placed on paid software apps to do just that. I'm more interested in content consumption and the proprietary Apple software has too many DRMs and other digital handcuffs to make it versatile to handle different audio and video codecs and formats in my massive private library.

Finally, it's an enjoyable experience using Mac OS X, but I think that too many Apple customers are living under a rock thinking that they don't have to think about security threats or attack vectors. They seem to be laisse faire when it comes to privacy and security. There's this sense that Apple will protect them no matter what because they own or use a Mac PC. That's almost never been the case with any piece of software code that connects to any network.
