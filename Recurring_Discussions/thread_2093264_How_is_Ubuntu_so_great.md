### Post by neu5eeCh on 2012-12-10
The OP *does *have a point. The deficiencies of the Linux ecosystem aren't, possibly, noticed so much in the Operating System proper, but in the wide variety of apps used on any given distro (including drivers). I don't think, for example, that the devs of GIMP are paid, and it shows. Last I read, there were only two of them. The developer of Ubuntu Tweak just recently had a "virtual" nervous breakdown. *He's* not getting paid. LibreOffice, on the other hand, has  more devs and contributors than any other application in the linux ecosystem including (I think) the various distros. 

It's features are improving by leaps and bounds, along with its stability and capabilities. It *may*be out pacing MS Office, but Libre **still** can't vertically center text without jumping through more hoops than a circus monkey. So even there, Libre Office seems to betray  the idiosyncratic interests of its numerous contributors. There doesn't seem to be a strong cohesive sense of development that one might expect with a more top down organi$ation. I notice, for example, that Impress and Calc are getting **way** more love than Writer. The answer to the OP's question might, in part, be that great software doesn't just require money, but passion and commitment. There are some apps out there -- No matter how much money is thrown at them, they're never going to amount to a hill of beans.

The place where one really sees the price for free development though, is when projects are abandoned -- projects like AWN and Synapse. Synapse works for now, but eventually, like AWN, this or that dependency might wreck it. Where money i$ involved, I expect that an organization will be more motivated to improve, change and adapt.

### Post by N00b-un-2 on 2012-12-13
Keep in mind that large parts of the kernel and some very useful software packages contained within Ubuntu (or any other Linux distro for that matter) are developed by large for profit organizations like Google, Intel, IBM, Novell, etc.  Non profit organizations like Mozilla have contributed some of the most important packages (Firefox & Thunderbird).
Even Microsoft has contributed code to Linux!  The CUPS system among several other things was developed in house by Apple.
Canonical does employ many developers and engineers.  Where do you think Unity came from?  It wasn't just a group of independent hackers who decided to rewrite decades worth of code; Canonical began an extensive project called "Ayatana" which aims to standardize, unify and improve the desktop Linux experience, and the result is Unity.
