First of all, non-techy users do not ever get a vanilla operating system, or they shouldn't. Usually, they buy a preconfigured operating system directly from an OEM, or they get their tech-savvy friends to configure the operating system for them.

Coincidentally, guess where Macs are most popular?  Ubuntu doesn't have this; no Linux distro does.  And frankly, as much as the detractors like to decry the lack of Photoshop and other specialty apps, or the supposedly-difficult-but-not-really-all-that-much-more-difficult-than-Windows setup, that lack of support network is the biggest thing that makes Linux "not ready for the desktop".  It's the chicken-and-egg problem: simply put, Linux is not ready for the desktop because it's not on most desktops, and there's not a huge corporation to buy it a presence like Apple does.
