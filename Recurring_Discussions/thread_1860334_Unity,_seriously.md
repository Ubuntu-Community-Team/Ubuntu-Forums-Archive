When I first installed Ubuntu 10.04 as an experiment on my old laptop I very quickly found myself booting into it instead of Windows. Within a week it was my default option. The [_link for "Unity"_]("http://unity.ubuntu.com/projects/unity/") on that page does describe a couple of features.

In terms of features Unity provides in a general sense, it gets out of the way and lets you think about it less, once you get used to it. The idea is that one way of getting to something is better than two equally good ways of getting to it. 

Think about the Launcher. It's replacing three things - panel launchers, the task bar, and desktop icons. All of those functions are immediately accessible in one place, instead of being organized in three different ways. With the new Alt+Tab, that's organized in the same way, as well. Navigating your apps follows the same pattern everywhere, and that reinforcement means you can think about it less. It's less about the number of steps in terms of the number of clicks and more about the mental steps you're going through.

Then there's the panel. Under Gnome 2's default shell, there are two lines of text taken up by the top and bottom panels, one by the title bar, and one by the menus. For a maximized window under Unity, one of those rows is removed and the other three are rolled into one, making the application essentially fullscreened. Even for non-maximized windows, it's still saving two lines of text over the old default interface. And because the menu bar shares space with the system tray, it's grouping like functionality; they all behave in the same way. There are even keyboard shortcuts to access the indicators, just like Alt to focus the menu bar.

It's not exciting stuff. It's just practical, functional stuff that simplifies the experience as much as possible without reducing the feature set. It's mostly working toward making things more intuitive, but in practical terms, that really does result in a faster workflow.
