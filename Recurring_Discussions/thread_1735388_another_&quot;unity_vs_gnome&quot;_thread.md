### Post by sanderd17 on 2011-04-21
I assume you are comparing Gnome-shell and unity.
[B]
app swiching[/B]:

Gnome shell doesn't have a dock or visible application list by default. You have to move your mouse to the top left corner to show your workspaces, your active and favorite programs and your recent files. If you press the super key (windows key), you also get that screen. Off coarce, hotkeys like ALT+TAB and CTRL+ALT+LEFT/RIGHT still work for app switching. It's a pitty that ALT+F2 doesn't have autocompletion anymore, you need to press TAB to complete the command. Therefore I use synapse instead and I gave it the shortcut ALT+F2.

Unity has a dock that gets out the way. So app switching is like the old gnome, it's just on a different place and it looks different. you can add apps to the dock and move them (you have to drag them to the right for that). When you press the super key (or click the ubuntu logo), you get the applications per category and a search field. 

So in app switching, unity is more like the old gnome and if you want to work with gnome-shell you would need to learn a little bit. It's not that the one method is faster then the other, you just need to get used to it. The advantage is that in gnome shell, your mouse is in the middle of your screen after switching an app, so you don't need to move your mouse too much afterwards, but you do need to move your mouse to the corner first.

**Search**:

search is well implemented in both DEs, not a real difference here.

**Goal**:

Both DEs want to "get out of the way". They want to give space to the user and the programs. They focus around the application.

**Integration**:

One of the main differences between gnome-shell and unity is that unity is made with a default configuration in mind (because it's made for ubuntu) while gnome is made more general, it has to work well with a lot of other distros. As a result, Unity has a better integration with the default programs like rhythmbox, the ubuntu software center, ubuntu one, a social menu ...  This is naturaly only for Ubuntu. If you take a distro which ships gnome-shell by default, the maker of that distro will make apps integrate with gnome-shell. 

**Social apps**:

Unity has a social menu, but the notifications could be better, when someone starts chatting at you, you still have to open the chat client to answer. In gnome-shell, the notifications are made bi-directional. That means that you can click on a chat notification and answer. when you don't click, the notification dissapears at the bottom but when you moveyour mouse to the bottom, it appears again. This is a really handy feature but I don't know how many programs use it. I've tried it with the chat client and that works well. Gnome-shell also has a social menu like unity, but I don't know how it can be used. I find no settings on how to input your login details, so naturally, I can't login via the social menu.

**Settings**:

This is the biggest problem with both DEs. They don't have much settings. Gnome-shell is themable with css, but you don't have a normal settings-menu for the appearance. In unity, there are also a lot of settings missing. I am sure this will become better when they grow older, but for now, it's still "you get what you get".
