### Post by Bazon on 2011-11-07
**1. It is fast **
Every window is just one click away. (Gnome3 and Unity: Minimal 2 clicks if there is more than one window per application open.) Positioned on the screen edge, the taskbar fulfils Fit's law perfectly.

**2. It works perfect with workspaces**
On every workspace I got just the things I need there, no other distraction. This gives you the opportunity to have a very good ordered, effective environment. 

**3. It provides good information**
To identify the taskbar object you are looking for is really easy and fast: the icon gives you clue of the application, the text helps you to identify the right window. (Specifically important for text documents.)



I really don't understand, why Gnome3 and Unity came to the strange conclusion to drop the taskbar. Just because the taskbar was invented by Microsoft? Or because MacOSX doesn't have a taskbar, too?
A taskbar is a key element in making a computer an effective tool!

I really appreciate Mint 12 including a Taskbar in Gnome Shell as an extension. ([http://blog.linuxmint.com/?p=1851](http://blog.linuxmint.com/?p=1851))
Meanwhile, I'm very happy with XFCE. :-)

Long life the taskbar!

> **Bazon said:**
> **1. It is fast **
Every window is just one click away. (Gnome3 and Unity: Minimal 2 clicks if there is more than one window per application open.) Positioned on the screen edge, the taskbar fulfils Fit's law perfectly.

Takes too much space, and it's a real problem to see what windows are open if you've got a lot of them. The space it takes is especially valuable if you're using one of these newfangled widescreen monitors that don't have much vertical space.

The Unity Launcher icons are clickable even when your mouse is pressed up against the edge of the screen.

I do believe Gnome 3 Shell requires only one click as it exposes the running windows when you throw the mouse into the top-left corner - there's Fitts Law for you again.

> **3. It provides good information**
To identify the taskbar object you are looking for is really easy and fast: the icon gives you clue of the application, the text helps you to identify the right window. (Specifically important for text documents.)

Not when there are a lot of windows.

The Unity launcher does something better - it gives you the icon of the applications, and when you click it you get a preview of the windows.

> I really don't understand, why Gnome3 and Unity came to the strange conclusion to drop the taskbar.

It's not strange at all. The XFCE that you know and love originally didn't have a taskbar; instead it had an "iconbox", which is actually a dock.

Risc-OS had a dock before Mac OS X.

Openbox and Fluxbox used a right-click menu for window management.

Mac OS 6-9 had no dock nor taskbar - instead it had a menu in the top-right corner of the screen.

Old-skool Unix users used keyboard shortcuts to switch between running applications, and actually so did MS-DOS users and Mac OS 1-5 users ("Switcher").

iOS and Android use a long-press of a button to display current running programs. On Android it's like an iconbox, not sure about iOS. On Symbian and Windows 8 you go back to the home screen to change programs.

The taskbar is only one solution. Ubuntu wanted to be practical and save space by having a vertical means of program launching/window switching, and taskbars just don't work well vertically. Use up otherwise wasted space on a widescreen monitor, and not waste precious vertical space. Interesting to note that Windows 7's taskbar works more like a dock now too.

### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-11-07
There's already the Mint taskbar and the Frippery taskbar for Gnome Shell. There's no need for tint2 there. Under Unity, I suppose it could be themed to match the panel, but it would still look a bit jankety, I'd think.

It would be hard to argue that task bars aren't convenient in light of the browser tab bar. I don't switch windows as often as I switch browser tabs, though, and I appreciate the cleaner interface. Of course they can autohide, but that has its disadvantages, too (which Unity's Launcher shares.) 

It's not all about pixel space or aesthetics, but that's a big part of it. I just really don't feel the *need* for a task bar. I'm not bothered by the need to click twice to switch apps.

Like AI is saying, I also appreciate the Super key in Shell, because it's a quick thumb motion to hit the key and a wide, sloppy mouse click to grab the corresponding window, while the labeled preview is ideal, because it gives both visual and textual identification. Compiz Scale used to do the same thing, but the labels have been broken since 11.04, and I've had well enough of that in the couple of months I spent using it.

I will say that docks are rubbish for task management. I'm a huge fan of DockBarX, and it contributed a number of things to my workflow - easy launching of common applications, visual feedback of what apps were running, and an easy way to close windows without bringing them up and finding their little close buttons, as well as a recent documents list for each app. But I get all of those things in Shell, too, without a dock (or at least as little of a dock as possible), and I like that window management is wholly done by windows, rather than by application and icon.

Alt+Tab / Grave / arrow is still madness, but it's so terrible that it forces me to use the Overview, which is ultimately smarter and doesn't require any repeated banging on to find a buried window. (Then again, I rarely have more than four windows on a workspace, so I'd have little room to complain on that point.)

Unity I can't help. Under Compiz, I still find task switching easier with a hotcorner for Scale, but I hate the loss of the captions, and it's so hard to go back to after using Shell's Overview for a bit. I don't find any part of Unity's task switching to be intuitive, and the combination of panel and application space is disorienting to me. I empathize with the common feeling that all of the background apps are "hidden" from me somehow and switching is being actively discouraged. I don't think that's the intention, and I imagine I'd get over it with enough use, but it's my feeling at first blush.
