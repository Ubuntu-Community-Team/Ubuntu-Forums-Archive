### Post by Mark_in_Hollywood on 2010-06-03
March 2010 I purchased a digital TV tuner card. That was after hours of research into the various makers: Hauppage, K-World, AVermedia, HomeRun, etc. I bought the card at Newegg as the user reviews are always helpful in decision making.

The AVermedia card I purchased is an A188R. It has no drivers although I knew that AVer had made Linux drivers for some of it's products. AVermedia techs do read and respond to posts at Newegg.

If Canonical could pull together with other forums such as LinuxQuestions.org, LinuxForums.org, LinuxHelp.net, and maybe 1 or 2 others, we, by which I mean, a large portion of Linux Users could pool resources in one place about Hardware. And Hardware only. This is the only weakness in Linux. Lack of driver support for hardware peripherals. (Are you saying to yourself, this post is just some selfish guy with a problem? Please read on.)

Some years ago, I suggested the UbuntuForums.org develop a section for hardware by device, Printers, Scanners, Webcams, etc. -that idea got no reception- but look at it my way. If I have a problem with a peripheral, I don't need the attention of the entire Multimedia or Dell, or Networking crowd. Yea, maybe it will be an OS problem but as of a year ago, Ubuntu's OS is pretty robust. I may more easily find a solution about a peripheral in a Forum about that specific manufacturer or device.

So what is gained if there is a .net or .org just for periperhals and hardware for Linux? I propose that all Linux users be asked to register their devices (serial numbers option, but quite necessary) and that through this, manufacturers would recognize why it's in their interest to work with driver-developers, such as kernellabs.com and linux-drivers.org. Linux user's would benefit from earlier adopters knowledge and this particular forum would serve as a *marketing arm* for Linux.

I would ask all Forum users to give information about the devices they have, down to the thumb-drive. Obviously some equipement won't have serial numbers, but if enough people say they have a Logitech Webcam 9000, for example, Logitech may want to take notice that there is a profit in helping Linux. I understand that some users probably want me to commit suicide for using terms like: Marketing. Well, bro' it ain't gonna happen. The Community could benefit from power-in-numbers.

thanks for your time.

### Post by Finalfantasykid on 2010-06-03
I actually find linux has pretty good range of drivers.  And since they are integrated in the kernel, there is almost no time when you have to go out in the interwebs looking for a driver for some hardware that you have.  It's either in the kernel or it isn't, and if it is in then the hardware should work without having to install anything.  This way of doing things is very easy.

I see what you are saying though.  Many companies do not make the drivers themselves, or they have very little support for them.  But many times there are drivers made by the community for these devices.  I mean if there is a device with no driver for it yet, chances are there is a programmer out there with the same device and is capable of writing a driver for it.  The drivers may not be there day 1, but in time most devices will eventually get a driver.

Remember, Linux was the first OS to support USB 3.0, so the linux guys must be doing something right.
