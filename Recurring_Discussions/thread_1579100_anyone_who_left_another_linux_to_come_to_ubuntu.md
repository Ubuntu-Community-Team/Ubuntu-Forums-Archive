### Post by WorMzy on 2010-09-21
[QUOTE="fuzzylogic25"]WorMzy, could you please tell me exactly what freedom Arch offers you at the cost of simplicity. This is what I hear all the time about other distros like Arch, Slackware, Debian but I still dont really get what.

If you could give me some detailed examples that would be really great. Thanks! [/QUOTE]

Well, if you open Synaptic and (don't actually follow through with this action) try to remove something like Plymouth or Evolution, you will, in effect, remove the entire system. This is because Canonical make certain packages false dependencies of other packages. It can get annoying because it means that you have to keep certain applications even if they're redundant. Also, the number of topics we get on the forums from newbies who didn't pay attention to the list of packages that would be uninstalled when they removed one of these "integral" packages is phenomenal.

Distributions like Arch don't do this, but they also don't pre-configure things for you -- they expect you to take an active interest in the contents of /etc/* and ~/.* config files, and some applications won't even work until you have done so. Also, Arch and (basic) Gentoo in particular give you practically nothing out of the box. You get a basic CLI system and that's it. You're expected to build the system that you want from the ground up. This includes setting up networking, getting your sound card to work, installing and configuring X.org, installing a Desktop Environment (such as GNOME), etc.
