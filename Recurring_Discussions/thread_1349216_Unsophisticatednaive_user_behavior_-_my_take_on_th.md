### Post by Jive Turkey on 2009-12-08
In my efforts to support friends and coworkers with varying levels of computer knowledge and overall sophistication I've noticed that in many ways microsoft has really done good work making an OS usable for people that have no idea what they are doing.  

Most striking are my friends who run mac OS's.

Case 1:
Helping a friend install some software on her mac, the instructions said to run some commands.
[COLOR="Blue"]Me: "ok, so open a terminal..."[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Magenta"]Her: "what's that?"[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Blue"]Me: "the program with the command line where you type commands."[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Magenta"]Her: <condescendingly> "It's a ***mac***, it doesn't ***have*** a command line!"[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Blue"]Me: "Move over let me sit there"[/COLOR]

Case 2:
Copying some music files from my thumb drive to her laptop (different friend).
[COLOR="blue"]Me: <inserts drive and sees that it is mounted>, "Ok, copy the xyz folder to where you keep all of your music."[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Magenta"]Her: <slack jawed look of confusion> "uhhh..."[/COLOR]

[COLOR="blue"]Me:  "You know, where iTunes puts your music files?"[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Magenta"]Her: "I just put it in iTunes."[/COLOR]

[COLOR="blue"]Me: <5 minute dissertation on the difference between applications and directories or "folders">[/COLOR]

[COLOR="Magenta"]Her: "I don't really know"[/COLOR]

[COLOR="blue"]Me: "ok, lets take a look..."<figures it out and gets it done>[/COLOR]


Most people here are likely quite familiar with the subtle dangers of user-friendliness gone bad. ie invisible misconfiguration, data-loss, data-redundancy, resource hogging, etc.

I don't evangelize for linux or any other OS, and I'm pretty ambivalent about it.  For those who want to see ubuntu gaining market share on the desktop, I think what you need to make that happen is a naive-user package that pops up lots of explanatory messages, like the windows balloon tips that provide ways to tell the user what is going on and very very basic information on what they have done or are about to do based on the user actions.  I'm quite happy to never see such things on windows or linux.

If anyone who reads this decides to undertake such a project please for the love of god, make sure that it never gets installed in such a way that it can't be easily removed and/or disabled, and that it is not depended upon by any other useful package.

I tell people that Windows (and the most common antivirus packages) aren't so popular because they are the best options, it is because they are the best marketed options.

/my 2 cents

### Post by toupeiro on 2009-12-08
Windows on a computer resembles a consumer test market.  They find fancy ways to overcharge you for basically the same crap wrapped in different colors.

OSX on a computer resembles a household appliance.   It's nice and shiny, and looks good sitting over there in the corner on a desk.  It does it's job and is low maintenance, but generally it doesn't reach much outside of that select job you bought it for unless you're very creative (artistically: e.g. graphics and audio production), or very rich, or in most cases both.

Linux on a computer resembles a professional car mechanic show on TV.  Everything is preset up for you by developers and coders, and most of them actually give you all the parts and tools you will need for free.  The pieces all fit, but they are all exposed for you to see, and you can fabricate them any way you'd like if they don't fit right *according to you.*  Doing so sometimes creates some bumps and snags along the way, but in the end, you'll get exactly what you've always wanted, an operating system that does **exactly** what you want it to do because you told it to do it, not what other people think you want it to do.

### Post by issih on 2009-12-08
Handy is exactly right...

Furthermore the limiting factor in these things is the user, not the OS.

A user who does not know anything about the machine could use any of the three OSes mentioned, and get stuff done.

They all have ballon tooltips, they all have built in help systems, they all cater to the novice user. The success of that catering may vary from user to user, but they will all manage to get a certain amount done.

In all 3 cases at the end of that time, the user will have a similar amount of knowledge of the inner workings of a computer, regardless of which os they used.

The person dictates how much they learn from the process, not the machine.

Quite separately from that, the age old argument that OS-X is somehow gimped and useless outside of being shiny and good for graphic artists, is utterly wrong. Same goes for windows.

All 3 systems can be used to achieve just about anything, and complex tasks and workflows are achievable on all of them. You just need to learn how to do it on that particular system.

Anyone claiming that any operating system isn't powerful is someone who has never learned to use it properly...end of discussion.
