### Post by christopher.wortman on 2011-08-04
I recently spent time with Unity and even more recently with Gnome 3 shell. Aside from technical issues which I won't go into here, I want to address some problems. The issue is not the user. In fact Windows 7 has a very sophisticated interface. It is simple at the start but you quickly find that you are using a seriously well designed product. There is nothing simplistic about the interface, in fact it is a cacophony of human emotion and chaotic mess organized into thoughts, and expresses this in the best way the developers could figure. Sure it catches a lot of flak, but it doesn't treat me like an idiot. The things it could never simplify it doesn't even try to, but assumes you know the product very well. This is the way Linux and computers used to be. It is the way Android is and look at how well that is doing. It tries to fix a broken network stack with an even more broken way of doing it, but that is besides the point. This is Windows and networking was never more than Internet anyway. KDE 4.7 treats me like a human not an idiot. Unity treats me like I know nothing as does Gnome 3. They tell me how to use my computer without allowing me the slightest bit of personalization. I get a cheap soul-less cookie cutter experience. I have looked at the new Unity+1, and while it is a step further, it further goes down this road. The next version of Gnome 3 with the new enhancements dont look to add anything I would want in a desktop.

LXDE, XFCE, KDE, and Windows 7 Explorer all empower me to use my computer. Unity and Gnome 3 tell me how without the slightest bit of leeway for those with a causality whom would otherwise need to change the position of something and have it stick that way. Or use the interface in a way that is more comfortable to them. This goes beyond a simple theme change. I am very let down with Gnome 3.1 and Unity +1. I understand that they are both unfinished products, but I can see the way they are headed as I have used Linux and computers long enough to figure out that people just cut off when they feel like it so Unity +1 and Gnome 3.1 could be cleaned up and released tomorrow but be in no better shape than they are now. Adding stack flows to Gnome 3 is like putting lipstick on a pig. You won't be able to flow through the interface because they removed what stack flow means to you and me. Changing the meme for the sake of being different will just earn you a ticket to being alone in using it. 

Being alone in using a product is a long standing proponent of free software, which is fine. However the issue arises when companies like Red Hat and Canonical decide to treat their user-base as if they only have enough grey matter to make up a brain stem. "Lets dumb down the interface because THAT is the problem keeping people off the OS" when it is not the case. Missing key features in applications that never get added, and things that should work without issue like sound and networking fail because of instability and instead of fixing it everyone is too busy nerffing the corners ignoring the gaping hole in the center.

### Post by FlameReaper on 2011-08-04
The problem is, many people are idiots.

While if I say that I'd be demeaning to my customers and I can be sacked by my management for it, the reasoning to "dumbing the desktop down" has sound reasons, and there's one too many cases that philosophy can apply to. Clearly that kind of desktop is not for you, but there are people who need it. And we don't need to be denied an idiot-proof UI for the masses.

Many people use Windows 7 yet they still have a hard time navigating through it, yet if I have to say anything they (Windows) have done a good job for their part and this has nothing to do with their domination of the desktop market. Many people just see it as more familiar to work around with rather than something that they think is similar but is not what they think it is (e.g. a Windows 7 user looking at a KDE desktop modded to look similar).

We also need more people to use things so that we can identify what features we might be lacking, but before we can make people start using what we use, we have to make them understand how to go around the UI and controls. Which is why there's this "dumbing down the UI."

Installing software for the end users, to them, is frankly something that is extremely complicated and they thought they should just ask us to do all the dirty work. Which IS our job to do it as the people who sold the computer to them. If I had to compare between how difficult it is to install programs, I think in terms of idiot-proof installation awards... that has to go to Apple's Mac OS X. You have the installation image (*.img) inside a folder, what do you do? Double click it, drag the icon into the rectangle which says "Install" or something (I forgot what was in the box), and poof it is installed.

Windows and *any* Linux distribution? Not so much on the easy part; although for the distribution which has binary packages provided (such as Arch, Ubuntu, openSUSE, Fedora, Debian and the like) this might be a non-issue and might even be contrary to the difficulty issues I'm speaking of, but if there's one thing lacking about it I'd say that the "average user" needs things to be done on a GUI not a CLI. No matter how much we like the CLI we still have to give the user a GUI option, because the CLI in general just freaks them out and spews things they don't understand when all they want is to see the program available in front of them and run the way they should.

But if the software only has a source package, it'll be like unleashing hell for these kinds of users. If they have already enough headache trying to figure out what the messages printed out while installing a binary package are, I will not be surprised if someone screamed "SOMEONE HACKED ME!! OR IS THIS A VIRUS!??" and passed out with a foaming mouth as they see lines of codes printed on the screen as the program is compiling... and either succeeds or fails due to dependency problems etc.

Windows ain't any easier either, but at least for the lucky ones they have someone right next to them that can either directly or indirectly show them how things are done.

The problem is there's way too many people using that one "derogatively-spoken-of operating system produced by a man who calls himself Bill Gates" that others, except for Apple maybe, have no choice but to invoke the "if you can't beat 'em, join 'em" approach to attract newer users.
