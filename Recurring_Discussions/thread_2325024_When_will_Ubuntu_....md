### Post by buzzingrobot on 2016-05-18
I'm skeptical about Ubuntu or any Linux supplanting Windows on home desktops.

First, because it is not in the retail market.  You can't buy it in a store because it isn't for sale.  And, as a practical matter, the FOSS licensing in Linux prevents anyone from standing any chance of sustaining a business selling retail Linux. Someone will just rebuild all the source and give it away with a different name and art work. (Linux was there and did that, ten years ago. Ask Suse and Red Hat.) 

And, there's the pre-install issue.  Only people who are already committed to Linux will order a PC with Linux pre-installed from one of the very few, very small, vendors. (Dell's an occasional exception.)  Everyone else goes to the Big Box stores, real or online, and they aren't selling Linux boxes.

Second, many people have bought their last desktop or laptop. Phones and tablets do what most of us use PC's for: communications and entertainment.   Many, many people will just never buy a PC at all. For them, a PC is the thing you use at work.

And, at work, the PC may very well run Linux, or Windows on Linux, or Linux on Windows, or some other combination. 

The forced updates, inability to disable the Windows Store, the telemetry and the monetization in Win10 are restricted to Home and Pro editions,  That's a very good clue that Microsoft has decided to treat that market the way everyone treats the phone and tablet market.  Enterprise customers, though, get to play a lot more by their own rules.  We will see what happens there,

(One crunch moment for Microsoft is the end-of-life date for Win7.  Enterprise users running Win7 will need to move elsewhere. It is no longer a given that they will move to another version of Windows.  *That's* why Microsoft is so focused on pushing Windows 10.)

### Post by user1397 on 2016-05-19
Yea there's nothing wrong with that setup.  If you only had 2 partitions (root and swap) like you have, then it wasn't necessary to make the swap in an extended partition, it could've just been a primary partition, but this doesn't matter at all.  

You can separate /  and /home, that can be a good idea because if you ever have to reinstall, then you are just overwriting root (/) but not your /home folder where all your personal files should be (and many program config files).  However, if you plan on putting more than one distro on the drive, or dualbooting between windows and ubuntu, then I recommend keeping / and /home together (so only a / partition) and just having a /data partition (you can call this anything you want) to store your personal files, as sharing a /home with multiple distros is a bad idea because of shared config files.  The /data partition should be ext4 if just sharing between linux distros (I mean you can make it other filesystems but might as well just use ext4), and either fat32 or ntfs if dualbooting with windows (fat32 has file size limits of 4gb, ntfs doesn't but fat32 is less buggy in general).

Cheers.
