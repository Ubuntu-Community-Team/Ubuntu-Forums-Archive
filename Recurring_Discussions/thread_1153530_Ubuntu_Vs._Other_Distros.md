### Post by dragos240 on 2009-05-08
If you find yourself with about 5 hours to spare and 86 pieces of paper, and enough ink, print out the guide for gentoo. Ultimate customization, the ability to compile everything from source, very handy when you have a slow system and need custom settings to fit your needs or just a nice custom linux for you.

If you want great customization, but do not need to compile from source and have a half-decent computer on your hands, you might want to check out arch. Known for its very simple design and the ability to get all your applications manually, of course this will help you to get a faster computer, and one that fits your needs for applications, it uses it's pacman package manager which is praised for it's easy to use CLI-interface. Arch is the choice of many linux users around the world.

Ubuntu is a handy free, well thought out distro intended for everyday use, with a nice free set of open source applications at your fingertips. It comes with years of official support from its sponsor canonical. This distro was made by Mark Shuttleworth and is by far the most popular distro, it can be used very easily, and can be very handy for the average windows-linux convert.

Debian is a free linux distro praised for it's APT package manager, it is also the distro that ubuntu is based on, a very usable stable release since 1993.

These are a few reviews I wrote at the top of my head.

### Post by dspari1 on 2009-05-09
> **sx66gns said:**
> Eh , Just another KDE distribution , I wasn't impressed.

Sabayon is a great and powerful distro that I would recommend to users that have mastered Ubuntu and want to step it up a notch. In many ways, it's easier than Ubuntu since it comes with Flash, Java, all the codecs(including DVD), ext4, and Compiz with all the bells and whistles turned on by default; like Mint, this distro wants everything to work out of the box. 

It has both a Gnome and a KDE version and is no longer a KDE centric distro like it use to be. However, Sabayon's KDE is one of the best you can get.

Sabayon, since it is based on Gentoo, is a great distro if you want to compile your packages using portage. With portage, you can compile tar.gz packages in the repository, or get a Gentoo ebuild anywhere which will allow you to download a tar.gz source code package and install it as a package found in the Gentoo repository. (You also have the option to use Gentoo compatible binary packages with Entropy to save time if you desire.)

Why is this good?
If you have ever compiled a program in Ubuntu, Synaptics(APT & Aptitude) is unaware that you have this new software installed. This means that if you end up not liking the program, you have to manually hunt and delete all the files that the program produced. This means that if you compile a lot of packages, it becomes hell to keep track of what you have installed and what you don't have installed and their dependencies.

Finally, Sabayon is a rolling release distro like Gentoo and Arch, so you always have the latest version.

The latest 4.1 version strips down the bloatware that plagued previous releases, and it is one of the best experiences that I've ever had with a distro.

To put it in a nutshell, Sabayon is what Ubuntu would be if it was based on Gentoo instead of Debian. Actually, to be more accurate, Sabayon would be the Mint of Gentoo due to its OOTB approach.

### Post by &#32 Greg on 2009-10-03
Possibly too much Arch in this thread... but for good reason, I guess.

What needs to be considered is what a distro is, and what Linux is.

Linux is the OS kernel. We've all heard that. It remains pretty much constant on every distro. X11 is also a constant on most distros (at least those that come with a graphics environment). Coreutils remains the same... in general, all Linux distributions are pretty close to each other.

Where they differ is in what DE/WM is used, what programs are installed by default, and how they manage packages. Yes, there are other differents, like how they do their init, but for the average user, that's what varies. At this point in time, package management isn't all too different amongst the major distros. Debs and RPMs are at least comparable. 

A Linux distro is nothing more than someone's idea of the ideal system. The major distributions tend to be pretty similar- they have either Gnome or KDE, and look and perform somewhere around the same. Ubuntu isn't significantly easier or harder then distros like Fedora, Suse, and Mandriva.
