### Post by handy on 2009-10-04
From my experience, most (but not all) dedicated Arch users that have gone to Arch from Ubuntu, have got seriously into Linux due to the Ubuntu community, which just happened to be here supporting Ubuntu.

Then after spending enough time becoming more familiar with Ubuntu Linux (in the care of this great community forum), they get to a point where they are confident enough to have a go at satisfying their curiosity with regard to some of these other distro's/BSDs & such that are freely available for them to install & use.

After some distro hopping, some have a go at Arch (usually thinking that it is a whole lot harder than it really is to install & maintain) & some of those find that it is just what they were looking for, some don't & keep hopping.

Many come here & help out technically as they have useful knowledge, & quite a few also have other systems or use multi-boot system that may be running Debian/Ubuntu or a Ubuntu derivative.

Some people think that the Arch enthusiasm is motivated by some kind of air of superiority held by the Arch user, but from my observations those kinds Arch users are few & far between.  I've noted some comments by Arch users that could be taken the wrong way from time to time, when in reality they are just being playful in forum dialogue. Use what suits you, help others out where you can, it is all FOSS in the end, the 100's of extant distro's are expressions of human creativity/taste & need. =D>

I still don't think you have got a handle on it.

Arch IS different.  

The differences suit some people perfectly. 

Those people that the differences don't suit, can't understand what the fuss is about. This is human nature at work, as we don't know what we don't know. :)

For those that the differences do suit, it is the end of the line as far as looking for the distro that is made just the way they want it.

Which of course Arch lets you do; apart from the inherently simple minimal core system install, you choose exactly what you add to that.  Many of us think this is just how a system should be, as it contains no more & no less than what we have chosen to be there.

Because of the different design of the Arch system, & the fact that it only has what the user/builder has chosen to be there, it is amongst the very simplest of systems to understand. This then allows it to be easier to maintain, repair, modify & upgrade than systems with layers of complexity between the user & the often over complicated core.

The rolling release upgrade system suits many of us also; we don't have to time our complete rebuilds or often dangerous upgrades in concert with a periodic timetable controlled by others, which is the way that most of the other distros function.

So most Arch users are very happy, content & grateful that Arch exists, as it has satisfied a variety of needs that they couldn't satisfy anywhere else.  This often generates a certain enthusiasm in an Arch user, particularly in those that haven't been using Arch for a long time, or are possibly quite young.

As far as the snowball effect is concerned, people may try a distro out because of the positive feedback surrounding it, but they won't keep using it if it really doesn't suit them. They will return to what they were using or try another new thing. 

I think it is a rare thing on these forums where an Arch user is actively trying to convert anyone to Arch.  They usually just point out things about Arch that suits them which may suit someone else too.  

Due to this some Ubuntu users try Arch & stick with it, others try Arch & don't like it at all & quickly leave it.  I'm all for freedom of choice & having lots of choices.

The last thing Arch wants is to dominate anything, as that would be far too complicated a situation, which goes against the Arch way which is founded on simplicity. ;)
