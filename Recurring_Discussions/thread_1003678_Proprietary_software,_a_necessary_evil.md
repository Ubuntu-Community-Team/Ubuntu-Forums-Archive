### Post by blakjesus on 2008-12-06
I just finished reading [this article]("http://www.catb.org/~esr/writings/world-domination/world-domination-201.html#id287535") (warning, its a long read) in which the author suggests what Linux needs to do in orded to succeed as a operating system and gain at least a significant share in the desktop market.

One of his suggestions i would like to bring attention is where he states that Windows, OS X, and even things like the Xbox have a "killer app", which means an application so great that it would cause users to adopt new hardware or an OS just to use this application. He says that in order for this application to help out Linux:

> Can't be ported off its home platform, whether for technical or legal reasons.

So this would obviously mean a closed source application.

Also he goes on to say that it is a good thing to push for open file formats, but he also says that Linux needs to make more of an effort at supporting closed formats (e.g. .mp3 .doc .docx .wav .mov dvds etc.). Honestly so far the only problems with closed file formats i have had are with .doc and .docx files, but this does pose somewhat of a problem with Microsoft and Apple constantly locking down alot of their software and the files they are associated with.

He does include alot of other points but these are the ones i want to focus on. While i love the idea of free (beer and speech) software, it seems that the Linux community needs to make somewhat of a 'deal with the devil' in order to increase its user base and win over influential software companies such as Adobe, hardware manufacturers such as Nvidia, ATI, Broadcom, and PC vendors such as HP, Asus, IBM.

So basically in order to receive better hardware support and better applications, the Linux community needs to recognize proprietary software as a necessary evil. I think some have accepted this fact, but some are still OSS purists (nothing wrong with it, i wish everything could be free and open too).

So basically i said all that to figure out what you guys think about all of this and start a healthy debate (please no flaming or petty name calling such as M$ and winblows for example)

:popcorn:

EDIT: Oh and also i don't know how old the article is; i couldn't find a time-stamp. Somewhere he refers to 2008 as the deadline for Linux to succeed, and apparently 64-bit computing is still kinda new in the article

### Post by dyous87 on 2008-12-06
The article seems to be from around 2007 since the writer speaks of Y2K as being from 7years ago.

All in all I don't believe most of the arguments present in the article really hold up. I think many times when faced with the question as to why Linux isn't taking a stronger hold, people look too much into it. It is really quit simple actually. 

The average person does not install, or even know how to install, their own operating system. They go to their local best buy, or to dell.com and they order whatever is readily available which more then 9/10 times comes pre-installed with Windows. 

This also discredits the argument that Linux is not yet as easy to use as Windows. That is not at all true. Try installing Windows on your own custom built computer and you will see that it is actually harder in many cases then setting up a distribution such as Ubuntu. 

Where can the average person go to purchase a computer pre-installed with Linux? It just doesn't exist to the masses, no one offers it and they have no way of even knowing about it. 

I strongly believe that if a company dedicated itself to pre-installing a solid distribution such as Ubuntu or Linux Mint on attractive and reliable hardware with everything tested and compatible, and then if they poured tons of money into advertising like Dell, and opened up tons of stores all around like Apple, then it would take a strong hold and people would like it for its simplicity, ease of use and non intrusive behavior.

Now with that said, while I do believe Linux needs more traditional closed source application developers such as Adobe to port their applications to Linux, this will not happen until Linux has a larger base, and as I have said above I believe this larger base will become a reality when Linux based computers are readily available to the masses. 

Just my two cents on this matter, what do you think?
