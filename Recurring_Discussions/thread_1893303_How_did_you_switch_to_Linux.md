### Post by N00b-un-2 on 2011-12-09
It's hard to believe that it's been so long.  Nearly five years has passed since I switched to Linux.  Everyone likes to talk about how much better Linux is, but it occurred to me that no one ever talks about the actual process of switching to Linux and how to jump the hurdles.
For me, when I switched I was bound and determined to ditch Windows out of spite.  Losing an entire hard drive worth of data because of a virus will do that to you.
I discovered Linux to begin with after getting off an hour long phone call with Microsoft tech support only to be told that my only option was to purchase another copy of XP despite owning a legitimate copy.  A quick Google search for "free operating system" led me to [www.ubuntu.com](www.ubuntu.com), and the rest, as they say is history...  
Partly out of sheer determination and partly out of necessity I took the most logical approach I could think of:  The scientific method.
I kept notebooks, writing down commands, URLs and step by step instructions on how to do things like configuring stubborn wifi cards, and forcing my awful Lexmark X1100 printer to work with Ubuntu.

So, how did you switch to Linux?  And how did you find out about Linux in the first place?

### Post by CryptAck on 2011-12-10
I cannot say in honesty that I'm a complete convert. I use the software that works for me and I give credit where it is due. There is a lot of good proprietary and open software, and I use both.

For the record though, I believe the OSS movement is the right direction for humanity. The concept that others can not legally build off a past idea, simply for the reasons of possible financial gain, imposes limits on human ingenuity and hinders technological advances. 

As for your question...

Around the turn of the century I had a lot of time to invest in educating myself on Linux. I spent almost an entire year reading about the various commands before taking the mke2fs plunge.

Getting my system to work was both pain and pleasure. After the installation--which was much more difficult than today--I had a lot of trouble getting X to work. My video card was not supported, and I had no good resources to determine how to make it work. Unfortunately, I had thought I could use lynx in a time of crisis, but to my dismay I could not even connect to the Internet since my modem was a WinModem.

Since then, I've actively used both Windows and Linux depending on my needs of the time. For the last few years though, I haven't invested much time into Linux, mainly because working with computers all day at work reduces my drive to do the same at home. Although, getting back into it has been very refreshing and I'm hoping to be more committed now than I was just a year ago.

As I used computers more, I grew to like them more, and I did this while being familiar with both Windows and Linux. reading about Linux really piqued the interest of the part of me that wanted to learn more about computers. So the parts that did it for me was getting used to it while I was young and before I had learnt all the parts of how Windows worked. Instead of growing up with one mindset, I grew up with two options (I used Windows and Linux just as much if you factor in time at school), and when I became interested and made a choice, I just went with the one I felt would be more fun and interesting.
