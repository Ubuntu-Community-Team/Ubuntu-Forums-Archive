### Post by Sean Moran on 2010-12-21
Back in 1999, we had to buy the magazine from the newsagent.  Magazines that were generally around 20% worth reading, but included a bonus two or three CD's, quite often with RedHat or Turbo Linux or SUSE for the price of $19.95, w/ free magazine, depending on how you might like to spend your free time.

I've still got around half-a-dozen CD sets of different distros from back then, but not much good for anything beyond nostalgia now.  Nowdays, what use is ANY operating system without good reliable Internet capability?  No computer is an island anymore.

---o0o---

In actual fact though, this is the crux of the extra stuff I've been working on as part of the endless rigmarole of the as yet unnamed 'SeanoBuntu' distro that seems to look like taking longer to finalise the more years I keep tweaking it 'round in circles.

It started when I had to revert to mobile broadband, and managed to get a 20kb/s signal for two or three hours every second or third day.  My thoughts centered on Peru, and I'll try to explain simply.

Imagine you're stuck with some small Internet cafè in a mountain village in the jungles of Peru, and when you can actually manage to get online, you pay by the hour, there are fifteen 14 yo kids playing war games non-stop evey Saturday (FIRE IN DA HOLE!) and you get around 10kb/s on the good hours, when you are lucky enough to get what you pay for - an hour online.

Now, suppose you have a good ten-pack of CDs to burn your Live-CD .iso, but the time to download Ubuntu at the going rate for Internet cafès in high mountain jungle villages in Peru means you'll take around 28 solid hours at best to download 700Mb, which would mean no days off in February if you only had time for an hour everyday to get your Ubuntu.  

Give up and revert to Windows.

What if, however, you could download a Live-CD with nothing except the GUI and the drivers to run the same stuff that the 700Mb Live-CD does, and burn that little tiny .iso to CD and have a look over the Ubuntu system in all its glory - sans OOo and stuff?

Better still, what if you could download a set of .zip files of around 50Mb each over four or five days, and then rebuild the little 200-250Mb .iso to get you started?

I'm thinking WITH an Internet connection, but WITHOUT the decent connection, in bandwidth and online hours, that most of us take for-granted.

If someone is really stuck, like I was with my Thailand mobile-broadband last month, then it might be a good start to be able to download Ubuntu in a smaller package (but with the Live-CD standard - Nautilus, gEdit, Firefox), and possibly that smaller rendition also split into pieces as .zips so that someone really handicapped by their geographical situation might be able to grab Ubuntu piece by piece over a few days.

That's the part I've been working on since late October, but because I've tried Xubuntu, Lubuntu, Bodhi, Puppy, DSL, and many more, and still seen good reason to keep the standard flavour of the authentic Ubuntu, the slimmest I've managed to trim KArmic down to is still a rather obese 395Mb, which would mean eight days at the cafè to download 50Mb per hour.

All in all, remember that the Peruvian jungle villagers of today who can't get a decent connection, might be the Peruvian Shuttleworths of tomorrow, and wise men never forget a good favour from long ago.

### Post by samalex on 2010-12-21
When I got into Linux in the mid to late 90's I knew one person with an ISDN connection in his dorm room, so we'd try to get software from him or from the college computers.  But outside of that either you just tied-up the phone line to get large programs, bought the CD at Best Buy, CompUSA, or some other computer store, or you subscribed to magazines that offered free CD's or floppies. 

For me I had an amazing script that would dial my college's Unix system and start a SLIP or PPP connection to get online, and if I needed to browse the Internet I'd jump into the GUI (anyone still do 'startx'?) but most things could be done via shell which even at dialup speeds shell apps (IRC, Usenet, email, etc) worked well enough.

The major change between then and now is distros didn't come out with new versions very often, so when Debian or Red Hat or whatever distro you ran had a new version you wanted to move to, it could be a chore to get the latest CD... but once you had it and it was installed you were set for a least a year or more.  And updates weren't all that common, or you'd just update the few apps you used instead of a blanket Update All.

But times have changed.  Most people are used to massive downloads, streaming audio/video, etc, and even the very basic websites depend on heavy, bloated Flash animations, large graphics, etc.  When I was a full time web developer I would take dial-up users into account and try to scale down the site size as much as possible, but anymore with so many mobile broadband options I don't know of anyone who uses dialup.

Sam

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2010-12-21
Linux arose at about the time the Internet became available as we know it. That was about 1991. Before that we used dial-up bulletin boards. They we basically servers for sharing software. The ones I used were local University servers. Long distance telephone calls were very expensive back then; therefore, I found local University servers. It was all text based and CLI type stuff. Nothing like Google existed. You had to have your own crawler program to search for things and it could take hours. Before there were Bulletin Boards, we used our computers for writing programs that we shared freely through various social networks; such as, computer clubs and magazines. You could get a UNIX OS for your home computer along with compilers for Fortran, BASIC, and so on. We actually used the computers for something other than entertainment. The world has changed. All modern operating systems rely on the Internet to some extent. If I lost my Internet connection tomorrow, I could still watch DVDs, edit documents, write programs, play games, and so on.
