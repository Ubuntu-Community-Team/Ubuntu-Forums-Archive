### Post by Lars Noodén on 2011-09-27
For US-based Ubuntu users, on the White House's official website for petitioning the government, there is a [petition to return sanity to the US software industry by overturning software patents](https://wwws.whitehouse.gov/petitions/!/petition/direct-patent-office-cease-issuing-software-patents/vvNslSTq). The petition calls for the patent office to cease issuing software patents and to void all previously issued software patents.  

Please consider signing.

The site is official, who knows what the results will be, but it's worth a try IMHO.

### Post by del_diablo on 2011-10-03
> **thatguruguy said:**
> The examples you give are fairly meaningless.  The Newcomen steam engine was patented in 1712.  The Watt steam engine was developed in 1775, over 60 years later.  The Trevithick engine was developed in 1799, and modifications were made to the earliest engines to avoid infringing upon Watt's patents.

The same thing could happen in software as happened in the development of the steam engine, **if you're willing to wait similar periods of time.**

You see, that there is the problem. 
There is no sensible reason to wait for another 20 years for the patent to lift before you can improve it or give it wide usage. 

The only reason it was bearable in the first place during the industrial revolution: was that the evolution of tech was a slow process. 
If tech had been advancing a little quicker, then there would have been a fat chance that Watts engine would have come around 2-3 years after Newcomens publishing, leading to a potential corporate patent warfare.
Another issue is that the Trevithick engine is legal because its a workaround of some minor mechanics, so it could be awarded its own patent. If Brittain in that age had used the US software patent standards then it would have been impossible.

So by their merits, my examples still stands, but we could have figured out even better examples if we searched around for a bit.
