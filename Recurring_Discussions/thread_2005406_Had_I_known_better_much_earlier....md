### Post by Welly Wu on 2012-06-17
Had I known better much earlier in my life and were it not for the fact that I live on a meager fixed monthly income budget, I would have not decided to switch to Ubuntu GNU/Linux. Yes, my data is extremely safe and it is extremely secure. Yes, I did wind up saving a fortune that I would have otherwise spent to purchase expensive, third-party, closed source, and proprietary hardware and software technologies to solve common problems. Yes, I learned a lot of hard lessons. Yes, there were a lot of bumps along the road especially as I literally changed from a Microsoft to a GNU/Linux mindset. Yes, it has been rewarding at times especially when I solved a unique and specific problem with the help of this community.

However, I have wasted a lot of my personal time by switching to Ubuntu. I have made myself unavailable to my family members and friends for very long periods of time. I have stayed up late at night working on the resolution of a problem and I have lost sleep.

Free as in beer is true, but my time is not free. I must have wasted at least 1.5 years of my life ever since I switched from Microsoft Windows 7 64 bit Ultimate Edition to Ubuntu 9.04 64 bit a long time ago. This is probably the greatest casualty that is not often talked about among GNU/Linux users or I have not seen enough of these topics pop up at Ubuntu Forums.

It is about the time. Once I got over the initial and very steep learning curve, the majority of my past time was spent trying to solve unique and specific problems associated with Ubuntu. This is partly why I have decided to stick with Long Term Support releases along with the fact that it is about the money for my situation to further compound the issue for me.

It is much more familiar and easier for me to use Microsoft Windows 7 than Ubuntu. I get fewer problems and headaches because Microsoft tightly controls their products and services. It is also true to say that a million different things can go wrong with Microsoft Windows. However, that was never the case for me. If there was a problem, then I could do a quick search for the solution or I could purchase a ready made solution by doing business with a company that sells a specialized piece of hardware or software to solve my problems.

My problem is that I literally ran out of money while using Microsoft Windows 7. It is a very expensive platform.

Time and money. Pick one of the two as your poison. I had little choice so I chose to sacrifice my free time. In retrospect, I have to concede that my time is worth more than my money and I can not get a full refund for the countless amount of time that I devoted to mastering Ubuntu. I am nowhere near mastery either.

Had I known better much earlier, I would have been more punctual at my former job as a Help Desk and Support Technician before I decided to stop showing up to work. If I could peek into a typical day that I spent much of my time and effort trying to solve yet another GNU/Linux problem, I would have been more diligent at work and punctual.

My best friend and another good friend of mine now use Ubuntu because of me. I offer technical support and my services to my best friend. In fact, I am going to install Ubuntu 12.04 64 bit Long Term Support on his brand new ASUS notebook PC toward the end of this June 2012. This will be his second notebook PC running Ubuntu. He is retired and he has no modicum of technical skill or relevant work experience in my previous line of work. My other good friend is becoming an Information Assurance manager for the US government, but his specific program does not require him to use GNU/Linux distributions as a job requirement. In fact, he is an avid Apple fan boy.

My point is that I am their nexus point and I offer technical support to them. This will consume more of my free time.

Done properly, Ubuntu is a way of life. It takes an entire lifetime to master its intricacies and complexity. I have not even begun to walk down this path yet. It is much too early for me and I know it. Ubuntu cost me a lot of my personal time which I can not reclaim.

It is hard to predict how this might be received by the community, but I thought that it was time that someone pointed out the amount of time that Ubuntu will consume in your life especially if you install it bare metal on a computer. There should be a standard disclaimer about this fact.

As for Canonical shipping Ubuntu on 5 percent of PCs shipped worldwide by 2015, I say good luck to them. I see a fair number of Ubuntu Forums members tell the community that they are leaving the community and they will stop using Ubuntu for one reason or another, but I rarely see anyone specifically state that it just took too much of their time.

I am in no financial position to purchase Microsoft Windows 8 this October 2012 without yet another financial gift from my best friend. I am seriously considering not to do this. Microsoft Windows 8 will be much more expensive to use and support than Microsoft Windows 7 and I can not afford that cost structure at this point in my life.

For now, I have decided to stick with Long Term Support releases. Fewer software applications will break or crash over the next two years until April 2014. Knowing what little I do know about Ubuntu and knowing myself, I will undoubtedly spend much more of an inordinate amount of my time in front of my computer fixing Ubuntu if I decide to keep upgrading every April and October each year. This has been the consistent pattern since Ubuntu 9.04.

I wish there was a software package that accurately tracks the amount of time that you devoted to solving unique and specific Ubuntu related problems and it could tell you whether it was well worth the effort and time or if it was a total waste. That would be a killer app. I could use that killer app every day before committing myself to another time intensive effort to solve yet another problem.

I try to tell myself of the fact that I had little choice in this matter. The amount of time that I spent chasing solutions to problems was enormous. I kid you not. I just hope that I can be honest and truthful with myself by telling myself that it was moreso worth it than not. But, I am not sure.

This has been the single source of my problems and this is what consumes most of my problem solving time and efforts. So, let us be clear here.

I thought that this was a fairly important topic to bring up in this community. The time factor that we spend using Ubuntu and especially toward solving unique and complex problems is significant. I just do not see that many threads devoted to this topic recur often enough.

It is for these reasons why I do not advocate Ubuntu as a viable alternative to working and traveling professionals. I understand and I appreciate the demands upon their precious time from every single front and I could not earnestly recommend GNU/Linux to them.

It just takes up too much time.
