### Post by buzzingrobot on 2013-11-27
All of us tend to equate what we prefer with concepts like "correct" and 'best" and "intuitive". 

I don't think terms like "best" and "correct" have any objective meaning when applied to software interfaces.  And, "intuitive" is almost universally misused. (It does not mean "easy".)

"Efficient" could be a legitimate standard if someone is counting mouse clicks. No reason, though, why simply being the most "efficient" should also mean an interface is the most appealing.

Almost all Linux DE's fall into two categories:  Some are panel-based and some are dock-based.  Panel-based DE's use a bit a graphical trickery to create the illusion of a window minimizing into a panel, where it sits until the user clicks on it.  Dock-based DE's  typically skip the minimizing illusion and replace it with the icon in the dock.  

The difference between panel and dock, then, really comes down to the shape of the things we click and their on-screen location.

Most of us, though, do get used to things.  So, when something we like is replaced or superseded by something we like less, we tend to tag the New Thing with all kinds of negative labels.  If we like something, it must be perfect and it must be loved by everyone else. Right?
