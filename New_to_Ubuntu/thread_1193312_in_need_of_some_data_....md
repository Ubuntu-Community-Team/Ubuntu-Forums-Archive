> **Samurai Penguin said:**
> would somebody please explain why a 1.5MB DSL download speed shows as 156kb/s when downloading a file? Thanks

Your DSL service provider uses the 1.5MB number to sell their service

This is their "theoretical" maximum attainable by customer ONLY if upstream load permits

There are other factors involved as well including throttling at any point along the transfer

> **Samurai Penguin said:**
> would somebody please explain why a 1.5MB DSL download speed shows as 156kb/s when downloading a file? Thanks

Actually, it is 1.5Mb (little b) and 156KB (big b).

1.5 million bits, is actually 187 kilo Bytes (eight bits in a byte).  It is just two ways of measuring, one counting bits, the other counting bytes.  You are getting your money's worth.

### Post by fifth on 2009-06-21
Not written by me but explains quite well ...

> Byte= 8 bits

Kilobit (Kb)= 1000bits
megabit (Mb) = 1000 Kb

KiloBYTE (KB) = 1000 BYTES
MegaByte (MB) = 1000 KB

So 1 KB= 8Kb
and 1MB= 8 Mb

So lets say your service provider says your download speed is 6 Mb/s. To figure out how fast this is in Megabytes/s, all you do is divide by 8. so 6/8= .75, or 750 KB/s. This works in reverse too. Lets say you are downloading a file at 780 KB/s. Multiply this by 8. So 780x 8= 6240Kb/s or 6.240 Mb/s, This works with Kb and KB too.
