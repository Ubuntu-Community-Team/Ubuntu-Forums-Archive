### Post by Letrazzrot on 2010-03-09
Another advantage, often overlooked, of building your own system are easier/cheaper upgrades and replacements.  Case in point:  when a friend wanted me change his power supply in an Alienware, it was a nightmare inside that case and I had to decline.  On my custom built computer, it took about 5 minutes to replace a faulty one, while he ended up spending $$$ for a shop to do it.   Also, cheaper pre-built systems often lack the upgrade paths that custom built machines can have (due mainly to the fact that pre-built machines often go cheap on the motherboard).

As to the wi-fi, I believe the latest and greatest are "n" type, though you need an 'n' router to take advantage.  An n card is backward compatible with b and g, however, so if you plan on ever upgrading to an n router, I'd say go for the n card if it fits your budget.

I do not have any brand recommendations for linux, sorry I'm all wired except for some laptops.

### Post by ridgeland on 2010-03-13
I built my first PC in December 2009.

I ordered all the components from NewEgg.  I spend days researching power supply connectors and mother board connectors and features. They don't all match. I downloaded manuals and read more.  I also checked out "Upgrading and Repairing PCs" from the Eau Claire Library.  It even had a video DVD showing what to shop for and how to put it all together.  For me the experience was a chance to learn a lot about computers.  And it's a thrill when you put it all together and it boots the Ubuntu LiveCD all systems go.

I have AMD with Gigabyte motherboard with on-board graphics and sound.  I use the AMD download (ATI Catalyst Control Center) for the graphics driver not the Synaptics one.

My opinion is CPU and RAM are way over-kill before you know it.  Even with excess capacity in both it's easy to keep upgrading to the next level. When I upgraded my Dell-Ubuntu from 2GB to 4GB I never saw an improvement. I don't play shoot-em-ups, just video editing, TV tuner, Flight Simulator in VirtualBox-WindowsXP, usual web and office stuff. 

I think just the opposite happens with Power Supplies.  I spent $80 for a power supply OCZZ550 550W RT with silver 80 plus rating.  A cheap power supply can cost you more than $80 in extra electricity usage over 5 years if you keep it on more than a few hours a day.  It's easy to buy on purchase price and not total cost with the power supply, the result being a "cheap" PSU with high total cost.  And with a 80 plus PSU you can pat yourself on the back for being more green even if the you aren't the one paying the electric bill.  Since I don't need a graphics card this PSU is over-kill for my needs.

My advice is keep researching and enjoy the learning before spending the money.
