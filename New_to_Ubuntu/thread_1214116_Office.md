### Post by Hospadar on 2009-07-15
I use OOo for everything pretty much, and that's what I'd suggest to you/your father.  It's true though that you sometimes run into compatibility and layout issues, especially with stuff that uses some of office's more advanced features.  I frequently have issues with pptx documents (and a lot of the .***x types).

For those cases where you really do need office, I'd strongly suggest running office in a windows virtual machine.  The user experience of a VM vs wine/crossover (especially since office isn't a 3d game) is a no-comparison win for VM's.  Everything works, nothing crashes, everything looks right.  Even on a lot of slightly older, less powerful machines (netbooks for example) I think you'll still have a superior experience running office in a VM as opposed to wine.

If you have performance issues, I'd suggest you use a copy of windows that has been slimmed down with something like nlite (you can do it yourself with any xp install cd and a free download of nlite).

That said, give wine a try, if office will work with minimal hassle, all the better.

My favorite virtual software is virtualbox, it's easy to set up and use and works well.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2009-07-15
Sorry to "break from the pack" here ... but ... isn't the question really based on two things: (1) the version of MS Office that your father uses and how dependent he is on the office features, and (2) how willing he is to learn a different tool for doing document work?

I'm an "older guy" myself, so I'm not slamming older folks, but people who have become accustomed to doing something one way or in one tool generally resists (and resent!) having to change their ways to do it differently or use a different tool.  This is especially true with folks that did not "grow up" with doing things a variety of different ways with computers.  I'm a long-standing IT person, so I jump at every opportunity to try out something new and do something different.  But most folks of my "generation" are not like that; they learn one way of doing something with a computer, and they stick with that.

The "easiest" thing for your dad would be to use MS Office via Crossover Office, presuming, (which I don't know) that it looks and behaves much the same as MS Office on the Mac.

If he's interested in experimenting, then by all means, introduce him to Open Office, but don't be surprised if you meet with a lot of resistance to that.
