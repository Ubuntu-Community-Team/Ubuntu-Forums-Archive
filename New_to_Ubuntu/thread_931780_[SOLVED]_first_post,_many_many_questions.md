That is a *very* good idea that will save you quite a bit of trouble.

As far as a few app questions:
* StarCraft - never got it to run right in XP, but it runs fantastically in [Wine]("http://winehq.org/")
* Kubuntu seems to be more Windows-like.  Whether it's easier or not is a subject of much debate.  I naturally gravitated towards KDE, although Kubuntu is my least favorite KDE implementation so far, and KDE4 is... well, I don't like it.  Good news, you can just install the kubuntu-desktop package after installing ubuntu, and you've got them both.
* Skype has a linux client
* Audacity works better in linux, in my experience
* [Pidgin]("http://pidgin.im/") is a great IM client, and works in Windows as well

### Post by Xiong Chiamiov on 2008-09-27
Although I had installed various flavors of Linux over my high school years, I never did anything beyond that (and boot into them occasionally to see if my network problems were windows-specific).  I installed Kubuntu over this last Christmas break, and now know more about Linux than I do Windows.

As far as basic computer usage, many things are actually the same.  The biggest differences are installing software (which is *much* better than Windows, btw) and proper permissions, but those don't take that long to understand, I think.  If you just check email, word process and such, give yourself a week or two.

And yes, the Ubuntu community is fantastic.  I don't use Ubuntu any more, but I still ask questions here oftentimes.

I'm 60 years old, and although I've been around computers for many years, I never took a peek inside, so to speak.

I've found little to no difference between the WinDoze programs and Linux programs other than they work faster, better and more efficiently.
In some cases, you have to get used to the different names they go under.  Like Open Office instead of msOffice, but they are nearly identical in all the functions.  A couple of things are listed in different columns which only takes about as much getting used to as switching from a stick shift to an automatic.

Mickey$oft considering no more support for XP and a bad XP-MCE release they dumped on me is my reason, in my old age, to learn Linux and the various Distro's. Just like with WinDoze, you will have some frustrating experiences when things don't go quite right, and as you learn how to get things humming along.
But this Forum is a Godsend for Noobs like us.  Populated heavily with excellent, informative tutors who can work you through nearly anything, often in minutes, not weeks or months as the Doze.

To help me learn things a little better, and faster, even though on my main computer everything is working fine.  I picked up an older used computer and a couple of old hard drives, just to practice installing and tweaking things in Ubuntu and other Distro's too, just to see what happens IF I DO THIS instead of THAT.  That's how I'm learning, plus reading a LOT of info here and through links provided here as well.

At first, stick to ONE distro, as there are many differences even among the various Debian based distro's.  And only Ubuntu can give you the support you need right now.

I went from using nothing but The Doze to not even booting up the XP machine much anymore.  Well, that's not true, all the machines are ON and networked together, so ALL of my needed XP files are on-line and being migrated over to my Linux machine as I use each one or update it.

I used Firefox on XP and now use Firefox on Linux, and play with some other web browsers as well.  I'm still out to lunch on which e-mail program I will finally settle into.  So for right now, when I do check my e-mail, I leave it on the POP server and fetch it with the XP machines Eudora Pro for safe keeping, since I'm still messing around a lot.

It probably has taken me a little longer than most to become accustomed to Linux, but a LOT of that is my own fault for trying to learn three totally different types of Distro's all in the first month.  Learned that's NOT the way to go.  Pick the one with the most support and learn it, the programs and how the file systems work.  Once you have one Distro down pat, then the others will be easier to understand and work with.

It's like changing from Windoze 3.11 to 2000 to XP or to Vista, each time it required a new learning curve and getting everything running the way you want it too.
What's under the hood, although important, is NOT what most users are using computers for, it's for running their programs, and taking them for a drive.
