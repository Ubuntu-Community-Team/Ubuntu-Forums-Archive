### Post by Doobie9 on 2010-07-26
Welcome! 

My first experience with Linux was a disaster: The very first distro I ever tried to install was Gentoo--on a Mac PowerPC. I had printed out  ~40 pages of instructions and I followed them to the letter. I believe that I got as far as compiling the system, but I think I screwed up with the yaboot bootloader. I didn't know enough at the time to attempt to fix it. I was in so over my head it wasn't funny.

It was about  two years before I tried Linux again--this time I decided to start with a 'newbie' distro like Ubuntu and then take a more gradual approach to learning. 

Even then, running Linux on a PowerPC (especially a Mac) was a difficult experience. There is very little in the way of third-party drivers; Apple's old hardware is very weird.

My experience with Linux has been that if I've got good hardware support, everything else can fall into place. So if you haven't had to change into a tty and change you xorg.conf just to boot to a desktop or something like that, then you're doing alright.

When I first used Windows (I've always used Macs or Linux PCs; I never needed a Windows computer) for an extended period I found it to be very confusing and disorientating. Not because it's all that difficult of an operating system, but because much of it just didn't make sense to me (and much of still doesn't).

If you find some of this stuff really arcane and confusing, don't worry too much. Linux is like the 90% OS from Earth's alternate universe; or like another country where people drive on the other side of the road and use the metric system (I'm American) and the 24 hour clock. The people there aren't any smarter or dumber than you are for being comfortable with these differences, it's the just the way things are done.

Above all else, just have fun. I may have been a bit too verbose here; sometimes I just lose myself in an open text field. Sorry if I've been a bit too loquacious. :)

### Post by slooksterpsv on 2010-07-26
Welcome to the Forums and welcome to the world of Linux.

A few things, and these are just my 2 cents:
Start out with Ubuntu or Kubuntu (Kubuntu has more of a Windows feel for it).

Linux Mint, I don't recommend personally, because Software Center isn't included in Linux Mint - with Ubuntu and Kubuntu, if you need any software, Software Center is the best place to install software from.

Ask lots of questions, even if they seem stupid like can I open a document that was created in windows? - answer yes

Linux is a rewarding experience - try it out in VirtualBox if you're unsure of installing it on it's own partition.
