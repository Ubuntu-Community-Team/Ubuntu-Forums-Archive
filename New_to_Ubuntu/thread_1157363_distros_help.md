### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2009-05-12
It really just depends on your computer (Ubuntu runs fine even on many older 'puters) and you personal needs.

For a reeeeeally old computer that has been left for dead, something more "lightweight" than Ubuntu (or any of the other 'buntus - Xubuntu is *not* as lightweight anymore as it was intended to be). There are plenty of lightweight distros to choose from. The beauty of Linux is that it can be as much or as little as you require. I took a stripped down, bare-bones Ubuntu variant called [Crunchbang]("http://crunchbanglinux.org") and customized it a very fast, lightweight, "Windowsish" environment called [LXDE]("http://lxde.org"), available in Unbuntu's repositories for my old, ancient machine. It flies along nice and fast!

If you're a total newbie and want point-and-click simplicity, any of the 'buntu distros are great for that. The major difference between the 'buntus is the desktop environment. If really pretty, splashy, fancy eye candy (but still easy enough for a newbie to learn) is what you're after, play with **K**ubuntu! If you want to explore a more "Linuxy" experience, **U**buntu and **X**ubuntu are great. For school, use **Edu**buntu.

Mandriva and Mint (mint is built from Ubuntu by the way) are s'posed to be newbie-friendly too. Arch is not so much for newbies like us, but is extremely popular with folks who know their way around the command line a little bit.

There are 'buntu flavors for media, arts, and even a couple of religious flavors (Christian Ubuntu, Muslim Ubuntu, etc) to be found!

Several distros are "based on Ubuntu" (which should tell you a *lot* about Ubuntu), and Ubuntu itself is based on Debian (not near as newbie-friendly as Ubuntu).

So:

How old and powerful is your computer, and what do you want to use it for?

Answer those questions, and choose a distro to meet those needs.

-Robin
