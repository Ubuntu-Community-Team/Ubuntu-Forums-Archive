### Post by kansasnoob on 2009-12-01
I'm a Newegg customer also. The first thing I do is read the Newegg reviews to see if anyone else has run that piece of hardware w/Linux.

This may also be helpful (be sure to look at the "Other Resources" at the bottom of the page):

[https://wiki.ubuntu.com/HardwareSupport](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/HardwareSupport)

Of course the case doesn't matter as long as it's compatible with the other hardware, but I really, really want to stress the importance of the power supply!

Many, many issues can arise from a poor or improper power supply. Make sure it has adequate connectors including a connector for a PCIE/AGP GPU if you should decide to upgrade at some point.

No power supply should be loaded beyond about 80% and many people forget that RAM can consume as much as 100 watts per GB of RAM under heavy load. So shop everything else first, then the power supply!

I've found this to be a common cause of problems with the newer mini-ITX boards where the system comes with 1GB of RAM and someone upgrades to 2GB but the power supply is under 250 watts!

Trying to save money on a power supply is like using cheap oil in a high performance engine!

Also consider airflow with the case and during assembly! Don't leave cables "untied" and "sloppy". If you're using any IDE components I strongly dislike the round IDE cables, it's best to just properly route a typical flat IDE cable (80 wire only).

### Post by Zoot7 on 2009-12-08
> **Chuckler3 said:**
> hmm.. that's an interesting idea too.. 

so regardless of my setup i should only need a wireless g card because i won't be able to use anything above it's capabilities even though i have a faster router?
Well picture this, you can only talk to the internet at the speed of your connection which is say for example 20Mb (in my case).
A G router gives a maximum burst speed of 54Mb between you and the router. Which means you're talking to the router too fast for your internet connection to keep up.
The only real reason for an N card is to talk to your router at speeds such as 270Mb, which is a lot faster than most internet connections today.

G cards are fine for anything up to 30Mb connections anyway. Plus the G cards are older and thus better supported under Linux in general.

### Post by Zoot7 on 2009-12-08
> **Chuckler3 said:**
> gracias. i hadn't though about cleaning the processor prior to assembly.. also. i've got some silver based thermal paste. the cooler that came along with the processor has some on it already. should i scrape that off and apply it myself or just go with what they have there?
Yes it's always best to clean the thermal paste whenever you change a cpu, normally the alcohol based solutions are what's used to do so.
Apply a bit of that, then clean off any old paste using that, then apply about a pea size blob of thermal paste and push the heatsink down on top of it. The paste will spread out anyway. Make sure it's easily distributed though, the last thing you want are air bubbles otherwise your CPU may very well overheat.
