### Post by jbrown96 on 2009-02-11
I have a Thinkpad T61p and it's fantastic. I imagine you're going to get some similar responses. They have fantastic hardware, espcially the keyboard, and great Linux support as well. The only downside is that they are expensive. Mine cost more than a Macbook, and while I think it's significantly more powerful and better at what I need, the price sounds like a major problem. 

I second the HP suggestion. HP models names are really confusing. My girlfriend has a dv6000 running Kubuntu, and it works flawlessly. 

Here are some suggestions.
1) Go Intel Centrino. This is a brand line that incorporates mostly Intel technologies. Intel has been great with Linux; they have invested over a $1B in development, so you get hardware that works great and a warm feeling for supporting a cooperative manufacturer. Like the previous poster said, Broadcom wireless used to be a pain, but it is much better now. I would still recommend getting Intel wireless though. 

2) You get what you pay for. If you buy a really budget system, it is not going to be that great. I bought a laptop 8 years ago that was insanely expensive and also fast. It was built very well and was future-proof (it was a gaming laptop, and I don't play computer games, so it had more performance than I needed). I took really good care of it, and it lasted 6.5 years. If you buy a budget laptop, you're going to get something that isn't as good for quality and performance.

3) You don't need tons of ram. I've never owned a Mac, so I'm not sure about it's long-term ram usage, but with Ubuntu you really don't need that much. I have 2GB, and even when watching movies, browsing flash websites, and listening to music, I can't get above ~800MB of usage. virtualization is the only place you need lots of Ram, but since you don't seem to be big on Windows and Mac won't virtualize, this doesn't seem to be a serious concern. Plus you can always upgrade the ram yourself and save quite a bit of money.

4) A netbook might be something to consider if you're really on a budget. And while the battery life will be good, performance will be lacking, especially if it is your primary computer. 

5) Linux, even Ubuntu, isn't always easy. There's a lot of "tinkering." I would imagine that most people here find that to be one of the great things about it. I am constantly changing things on my system, and it frequently gets hosed. But it doesn't have to be like that; my dad has been running the same installation for 2.5 years with zero problems (I would be the first to know). Prepare to get your hands dirty, and for the love of god, use a separate home partition, so you don't lose your data. 

6) Hardware support can differ between distributions -- types of Linux (Ubuntu, Fedora, OpenSUSE, etc.) -- so you may find that one works better with your hardware, but from a usability standpoint, I don't think anything comes close. It's nice to try different versions; again, see #5.

### Post by Vaedrah on 2009-02-11
I recommend Ubuntu - it's easy, secure and doesn't throw up a whole lot of security headaches to work around. This notebook runs Ubuntu 8.04 (acer aspire 5920) and my other one, and a PC also run Ubuntu. I also use VirtualBox (going to try VmWare soon) that lets me trial other OS platforms on the core OS - perhaps Mac has an option to let you try this also?

I have found that Ubuntu 8.04 works without any intervention (the wireless card needs a very small mod in "System" to allow the driver to be installed - but only in my older notebook; this one did it automatically!)

I don't mind windows (some of my best s/w friends are in windows) and many people would love to be as rich as Bill Gates I guess - but Ubuntu is the friendliest cross over from windows to linux. I have tried Fedora and Debian but I don't like these versions at all.

If you have the $ to buy a new computer/laptop it will probably come with Vista (aside from windows - I don't mind windows at all - Vista I don't like at all) but it may not have re-installation (boot) disks. Make sure you get these or make them before you reinstall anything (if you want to return to Vista - say for re-sell reasons perhaps?) 

You "can" dual boot Ubuntu on a Vista machine but I found this notebook "claimed" to offer the feature, but put to the test it was a very different story. I wiped Vista, installed Ubuntu and run other OS (XP, Fedora, Debian) on top through Virtual Box. My other notebook is dual boot XP/Ubuntu and also runs other OS's on top.

I admit I don't know anything about Mac's (I have a friend who "swears by them" but it seems like Ubuntu and Mac are probably on the same page. I hope I am not misdirecting here - if you do buy a new machine, I suggest you download Virtual Box s/w (free) and try other OS etc on top. The s/w works easily and doesn't affect your computer configuration. (you can erase it later if you want). 

[There is a small "trick" to make the USB ports work but it's trivial; unfortunately not spelled out adequately if at all in the documentation]

Other than that, I'd suggest dual boot as a first option (if your machine will support this) otherwise use a virtual machine on top of your core OS to run other platforms.
