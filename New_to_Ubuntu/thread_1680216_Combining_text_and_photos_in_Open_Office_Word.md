### Post by JKyleOKC on 2011-02-02
To make the screenshot show up in a forum post, on the message entry screen scroll down to the "Manage Attachments" button and click it. This gets you a dialog with a number of lines and a button at the right edge of each labelled "Browse." Click the topmost button, which will open a file dialog where you can navigate to your saved screenshot file. Highlight the screenshot file, click "Open," then back at the dialog click "Upload." You may have to enlarge the attachment manager window to see all of the buttons. After it uploads, click the "Close" area and you'll be back at the message entry screen.

I don't use Open Office so cannot help with details of using it, but I do have more than 60 years' experience in printing and publishing and almost that long with desktop publishing through computers (since 1967 to be exact) so may be able to help you translate between the two vocabularies...

If you have the choice to "Print to PDF" that's probably the simplest way to integrate things for distribution to others. "PDF" means "Portable Document Format" and was developed for this specific purpose.

Try using the ALT-PrntScrn hotkey combination to get the screenshot, instead of the program. This ought to bypass that screenshot popup window itself. If that one still pops up, click its OK button and hopefully it will capture what's underneath it.

My work has been almost all on the editorial side, with some 24 years of it being in the computer industry as a tech writer/editor. That's where I got involved with desktop publishing, working with a time-shared mainframe at first via a 110-baud connection. I programmed quite a bit of that early software after discovering the parallels between writing for humans to read, and for machines to read. I also did a lot of magazine writing over the years, mainly tutorials -- and 21 books, only one of which is still in print.

From what I could make out of the bottom layer of your screenshot, OpenOffice is giving you a read error when it tries to put the photo in place. That may be due to it being pasted in; at the machine level, an image file is totally unlike ordinary text. Think in terms of a halftone image with a 96-line screen, and each dot being represented by a huge number that represents the intensity of its red, green, and blue components. That will give you a general idea of what the image file looks like to the machine. The program has to be instructed how to reproduce the image, and OpenOffice may not provide the correct instructions when you paste the image into place.

Have you tried the suggestion earlier in the thread to use "insert" instead of pasting the image into place? When I used to do similar things in Microsoft's Word, using "insert" always worked. Just get the insertion point (sometimes called the cursor) where you want the photo to go, and use the "insert" command.

I found it worked best to put in an empty (blank) line, and insert the image onto that line. I could then place it centered, flush left, or flush right, as I wanted. Trying to put a photo in the middle of a paragraph of text always led to an exercise in futility since it would never go exactly where I wanted...

This response is not directly related to your immediate problem, but it is important in terms of system reliability, so here goes:

It is risky to stop the computer by using the power switch.  Not only does this put extra wear and tear on the hardware, it also has potential to corrupt one or more files and perhaps even the entire file system.  If you get into a locked-up condition then you can usually get out of it more gracefully:

It is likely that your system is set up so that ctrl-alt-bksp will stop X windows.  This will terminate your session and will lose any recent changes made to files, but at least it will allow the clean and safe closing of files.  Alternatively, ctrl-alt-delete will restart the entire system but this is a more harsh approach and is more risky.
