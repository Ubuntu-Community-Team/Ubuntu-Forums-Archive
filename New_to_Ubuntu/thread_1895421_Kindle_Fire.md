### Post by anewguy on 2011-12-14
I had one for about 4 weeks, then took it back this week.  The touch screen doesn't seem very good to me.  I had nothing but problems trying to tap, scroll, etc., reliably.  There are also some quirky things they've done to try to restrict you to Amazon's movies, books, etc..  As an example, I ripped a DVD to mpeg4 then via USB moved it to the Kindle.  Couldn't find it under Videos where the Amazon-purchased movies go.  Instead it was inside the Gallery app and just showed as video1 or movie1 (don't remember which).  The icon was just a generic icon, and is not changeable. 

All in all, probably good considering the cost, but even though I can't afford to spend more I took mine back because of the above and other little things that became quite annoying.  It just got to the point where I thought why should I own something I just get frustrated with?

Wireless - on the Fire side of things wireless is built-in, and had no problems connecting to my home wpa2 network.  I didn't follow the links you posted, but I imagine what is being said is that without 3G (cell service) you can't use the network everywhere - only somewhere that has internet access for you to use.

So, I passed on my 2-cents worth based on my experience only.  I'm sure there must be millions of users, considering the sales of the Fire, that enjoy it.  It definitely wasn't for me though.

### Post by Ishipaco on 2011-12-21
I've owned an original Kindle (now called the Kindle Keyboard) for many months and also recently bought a Kindle Fire. My wife owns an original (B&W) Nook and a new iPad, so I've had the opportunity to compare all of them. A few observations:

1.) I too had troubles with the touch screen at first and have read about others with similar complaints. What I found was that I was "tapping" the screen *_too hard_*! After I learned to *barely touch*, everything worked perfectly. I think a lot of people "thump" too hard and, when that doesn't work, they thump even harder, which only increases the problem.

2.) Despite all the pundits referring to the Fire as the "iPad killer," it's really a quite different animal. In particular, it's a lousy web browser. The screen is way too small and webpage links often don't work. But it's also less than half the price of an entry-level iPad, and cheaper than the Nook. It's a pretty good product for its price and intended purpose.

3.) The Kindle is, basically, an improved ebook reader and a portal to Amazon products, just like the iXxx products being proprietary portals to Apple offerings. That's why Amazon is selling the Fire for $2 less that it costs to manufacture! They'll make their money on the other products they'll sell. For a while there was even a rumor that Amazon might give away Kindles for free; it's an important marketing tool for them.

4.) The Kindle Fire is, in my opinion, a huge improvement as an ebook reader over the original Kindle, mainly because of its slightly larger screen, its touch-screen controls, and its full color features. After months of comparisons, my wife and I both agree that the Kindle is a better ebook reader than the Nook; it weighs less and has a better range of font sizes, among other features. By comparison, the iPad makes a very poor ebook reader, if that's the primary function you need. Try laying back in bed at night next to a sleeping spouse and hold an iPad up with one hand. If you try that, you'll soon start wishing you had a Nook or a Kindle.

5.) If you want a minimally functional web browser, need built-in web cams, need 3G capability, or have already been captured by proprietary (and expensive) Apple products, then you should consider shelling out hundreds of dollars more for an iPad. For about the same amount of money, however, I can build a state-of-the-art desktop machine with a monitor that would make an iPad screen look like a postage stamp! Of course, I can't carry a desktop machine around with me, or carry it to Starbucks to show off to my friends.

Bottom line: it's probably obvious that I'm not a big Apple fan (or Microsoft fan either, for that matter). I guess that's why I run Linux on our four computers and am participating in this website, eh? BTW, I spend much of my ebook reading time on my desktop computer. Amazon's Kindle for PC ap runs well under WINE. Is there an "iPad for PC" ebook reader app for Linux offered by Apple? I'd be interested in knowing about it if there is.
