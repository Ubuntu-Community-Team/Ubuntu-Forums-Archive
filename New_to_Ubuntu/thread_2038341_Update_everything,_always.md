### Post by ub07 on 2012-08-06
Here's what I do. Every couple of days I check for updates, and I install only the critical (security) updates. Then once a month or so, I download the "recommended updates." Now I realize that the recommended updates may contain bug fixes for my installed software, but if I am not affected by the bugs, it's no big deal.

### Post by audiomick on 2012-08-06
I would say let it do all the updates it wants to. If you want to strip it down a bit, you could try de-installing programs you don't use. I don't mean system relevant stuff, but any "normal" program that is installed and which you do not use. Then you wont get any more updates for that program.

I don't give it any thought, myself. I figure the updates are there because someone somewhere found something that was broken, and it got fixed. The people who develope FOSS software generally have enough to do keeping up with serious developement work without writing updates just for the hell of it. ;)

### Post by ptn107 on 2012-08-06
Most updates are necessary.  Keep in mind you are only offered updates to things that are installed.  It will not offer you an update to apache2, for example, if it's not already there.  If you don't see the need to update a particular package why not just remove it if it's safe?

### Post by ajgreeny on 2012-08-06
You shouldn't actually need to check for updates manually as the system will tell you one way or another that there are some available.  On my system it is set to check daily, but you can change that in **Software-Sources->Updates** tab.

I always look through what is about to be updated, just out of interest, but then I always let everything go ahead and update the lot, like most people, I suspect.
