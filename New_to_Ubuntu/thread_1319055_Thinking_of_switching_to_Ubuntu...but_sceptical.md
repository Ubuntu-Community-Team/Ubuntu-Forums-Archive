### Post by fhmanas on 2009-11-08
Moving from Win may seem daunting at first even more if you only knew Windows. Having said that, Ubuntu is successful in making the transition less painful over the years.  You may try the Live CD without the fear of the transfer since it would have most of the Ubuntu experience with a slight performance hit and without committing yourself to the change.  It also allows you to see if your hardware is compatible and will work.  One thing you have to understand and why most people encounter problems, is that Ubuntu and the rest of the Linux world are left to fend for themselves when it comes to hardware drivers.  Most hardware like network cards, webcams, printers, bluetooth devices, etc. were really developed for the Win or Mac environment as was intended by the Hardware manufacturers, Microsoft and Apple, hence the supposed notion of an "easier" experience with Mac and Win.  Linux had to rely on the brilliance of a lot of contributors to make these hardware work but it is not 100% perfect but so far most devices have been successful in being implemented, so do check if all your devices will work before moving to avoid further disappointments.  Another is the expectation that software will be the same on Ubuntu/Linux as it is in Win or Mac, again, the same as with Hardware, you will be hard pressed to find certain apps implemented on linux (ie. iTunes, Adobe, MS Office, etc.), of course, there is an application layer named WINE that you may use probably implement your native Win apps, but it is in constant development, and might not be succesful in running all Win apps.  But there are tons of alternative apps for most needs, you just need to break from your comfort zone and learn them.

After all that, is it worth the transfer to Ubuntu/Linux?  You betcha, I started in version 6.06 (Dapper) and I couldn't be more happier with the transfer.  Furthermore, you will discover after a while that Ubuntu/Linux is more powerful than what it is perceived.

Enjoy the exploration and adventure.

### Post by YosefKaro on 2009-11-08
One of the remarkable things about ubuntu is that it is designed for gradual assimilation.  You can start of with the live cd which will make no changes to your system.  That will give you the chance to see just how well ubuntu works on your system.  That will also give you the time to feel more comfortable with ubuntu.  Next, you can install it in Windows just like any other application and which can be uninstalled just like any other app.  Then you will have the ability to choose at start up which OS you want to use. ### Post by Ji Ruo on 2009-11-09
Ubuntu and other Linux distros generally require putting in some time and effort before it will work for you. However, they are getting better at a rapid pace, and may be easier to work with than Windows in just a couple of years.

If you want to try it out, start slow and use the live CD. Check what works with your hardware and peripherals - your printers, scanners, mp3 players, cameras, phones, internet connection, wireless. This will give you an idea of how much work you will need to do.

If you are satisfied with this and want to take the next step, I would strongly advise that you keep Windows, and use WUBI or dual boot rather than going straight to Ubuntu. No matter how well Ubuntu works for you, as a seasoned Windows user you are going to want to be able to play your games and use your other software that will only run (or run well) on Windows.

Before you install you should research your hardware - your Graphics card(s) and TV Tuners especially. So if you wanted to use MythTV for recording and watching TV, do an internet search for your tv tuner to see if someone has implemented support for it. It could be very well supported or non-existent. With graphics cards, search to see if people have experienced problems, and whether the driver is still being supported by the chip manufacturer. NVIDIA will probably be supported, but ATI will probably only be supported if the card was released in the last 2 years or so.

And then research the install - make sure you know which one you want to do (dual boot or WUBI), how to do it and what could go wrong if you do the wrong thing (EG. choosing the default install will wipe out your Windows installation and you won't get it back).

Having said all that, I really love Ubuntu and it does what I need really, really well. You have to be a bit more choosy with the hardware you buy, that's about the only drawback I can think of for a dual user.

A couple of links that will help you with 1) software you might want to use, and 2) installing -

1) [http://ubuntuguide.org/wiki/Ubuntu:Karmic](http://ubuntuguide.org/wiki/Ubuntu:Karmic)
2) [http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/index](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/index)
