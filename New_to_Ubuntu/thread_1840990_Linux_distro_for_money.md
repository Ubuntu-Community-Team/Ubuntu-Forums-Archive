### Post by The Cog on 2011-09-08
My understanding is that you can charge money for a distribution. However, since you cannot add restrictions on redistribution, anyone who buys a copy from you can sell as many copies as they want. I think there would be a round of undercutting to the point where suppliers were barely charging more than their reproduction costs. That would appear to be the price that Linux CDs have already reached. Charging a higher price will result in zero sales as everybody shops elsewhere. And the free downloads readily available for most distros mean that most people don't even think about buying a CD at all. In summary, charging a significant price for a distro doesn't really make sense.

Charging for support does make a lot of sense though. Support means man-hours and as such is not in essentially infinite supply.

### Post by Dragonbite on 2011-09-08
Technically, if somebody wants to pay you money there are no legal restrictions to it.

Because of the licensing with Linux, though, you have to be able to supply the source code for what you distribute, upon request. You also have to include the license agreement and not restrict HOW the user will use Linux.

Therefore while somebody can pay for it, it doesn't mean somebody else can't come along, take your source code and turn around and sell that.  Nor that the person who paid for it isn't going to get mad and scratch up your car either!

You have to provide it free of restrictions, so you cannot do anything about them taking their "purchased" Linux and putting it on more than one machine, offering it online for others to download and use or even selling it.

Trademarks, though, is another matter govern by different laws.

That is why Red Hat provides their source code for free, but leaves it up to whomever is going to use it to remove their logo and all Red Hat references.  If you don't, _then_ they have legal grounds to sue you over the trademark!

This is what CentOS and Scientific Linux does; take Red Hat's source code, remove all Red Hat trademarks, replace with theirs, re-package and provide it for download.  

It sounds easy, but it isn't a walk in the park.  It takes CentOS weeks or longer for their dedicated dozen or so programmers to turn around a release.  Oracle hoped to cash in on this by doing the same thing CentOS and Scientific does (taking RH's code) and selling support. I think they've found out it is tougher than it looks.

What Red Hat does is give you a handy-dandy installable media with a paid support subscription, as well as other company services.

If a company provides Linux on one media, that doesn't mean they cannot offer non-FOSS software (proprietary blobs, codecs, etc.) to be sold in a separate media (or download).  That is up to the licenses of those products.

That's my rough understanding.  Please feel free to clarify if I am incorrect.

Another method of making money is to sell extensions.  These extensions can be proprietary and licensed under a non-foss license but made to "plug in" to an open source project. I think the Eclipse (a foss program) IDE does this too.

It's simpler than you're making it: If you want to sell something without the GPL license applying, then you can't include any parts that use the GPL license.

You can sell Linux. I can build my own Linux distro ("Totally the best Linux X²") and sell it for one million dollars (sure I won't sell many, but if I sell even one...) and that is completely legal and allowed by the license. But because I am using things covered under the GPL I need to comply with them by making the source code available for all of my customers, so they can take it and modify it without restriction and redistribute if they feel like it (which is what I'm doing).

I could get upset from my customer taking "my" hard work assembling the Linux distro and giving it away to others, but you have to realize that is what I did to begin with: Assembling a linux distro is taking the work of literally thousands of other people who said "use this however you want but give it to others with the same terms". If I don't want my customer to re-purpose my work, I can't re-purpose other people's work either.

Note: an operating system is more than just a kernel, and many different licenses are involved: BSD, GPL (v2, v3), apache, X11.
