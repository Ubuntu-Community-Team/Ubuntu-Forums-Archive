### Post by mathom on 2009-10-08
Stepping from the canned spam world of Windows into the "Have it your way" world of Linux has me feeling out of my depth.  With so many distributions of Linux to choose from with all sorts of purposes and proponents, I'm finding it hard to figure out which fits my needs and restrictions.  I have, as it were, walked into a Chinese restaurant where the entire menu is in Chinese and all I want is some egg rolls.

So I have turned to people who "speak the language" to find out which distribution of Linux is right for me.  Here is my story:

I have inherited an old laptop computer (see [http://www.chemusa.com/chembook_7200e.htm](http://www.chemusa.com/chembook_7200e.htm) for specs) with an Intel Celeron processor, 64MB of RAM (with the possibility of making that 192MB), and 5.*GB of hard drive space.  I don't have any other options for upgrading.

It currently runs Windows98 slow as a glacier and has lots of stuff on it that I don't need.  I've been wanting to try Linux for some time and I think here is my chance.

I plan to use this computer as a work/school computer.  My work and school are currently the same thing, being in the sciences, and I wanted to set the laptop up for some light numerical computation (compatible with MatLab, Mathematica, etc.), document and presentation preparation, and some light programming (Java, HTML, C++ maybe).  I don't need internet (though I will need a browser to view HTML), or email, or iTunes.  I do need something that will be fully compatible with the Widows machines that I have to deal with.

I don't want OpenOffice.org as I can't pick which portions to install.  I will need spreadsheets, but I use LaTeX heavily (with the intent of using it more heavily) for word processing.  I need a drawing program that produces .eps files and a presentation program compatible with PowerPoint.

Also, the whole system must be user friendly.  I am coming from Windows and while the command line doesn't terrify me, my typing skills are not so stellar that I would want to depend on them for navigation.

I've been advised to use Xubuntu, but while looking at the spec requirements, it looked like this laptop just barely made the cut.  The last requirement is that the whole system cannot run glacially slow or I know I will use it seldom to never.  I've heard that there are some pretty lean Linux distros out there and that Linux is the best bet for resurrecting old computers.

I know I am addressing the Ubuntu forum and am most likely to get people suggesting Ubuntu, but I am hoping to find someone with knowledge of the pros and cons of several distributions who can point me toward the Linux distro that is most likely best for me.

Thank you.
