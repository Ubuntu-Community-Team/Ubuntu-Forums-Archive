### Post by snowpine on 2010-04-22
In my opinion, the main advantages of 10.04 over 9.04 are:

1. 9.04 will be supported through October 2010; 10.04 will be supported through April 2013.
2. All applications and software packages in 10.04 are approximately one year newer (but remember newer is not always better).

Since 10.04 has not been released yet, and 9.04 will be fully supported through October, I would wait a little while before deciding. Definitely try a Live CD of 10.04 before committing to an upgrade, to make sure that it supports your hardware and that you like the changes. :)

Otriades, you are like me: You want something to just work. I recommend that you do upgrade to 10.04, because it's long term (LTS), so you won't need to worry about upgrade for another three years. (I still use 8.04 on my computer, but I will upgrade to 10.04.)

But, before you upgrade:

[LIST]
[*]Wait until 10.04 has settled in. I suggest that you **wait until June**, because it seems that new releases take about a month to settle in.
[/LIST]

[LIST]
[*]Do a **full backup** of **all your data**.
[/LIST]

[LIST]
[*]Do a **full backup** of **each one of your partitions** (root, /home and /var). I use [CloneZilla]("http://clonezilla.org/") to back up mine. That way, if the upgrade is a total mess, you can restore right back to where you were with hardly any hassle. (You'll want a separate drive, maybe an external hard drive, to do this.)
[/LIST]

[LIST]
[*]I recommend that you do a **fresh install of 10.04** instead of an upgrade. It's a little extra hassle, but the clean installation means that you won't carry over any problems lurking in 9.04. You can install in your current root (/) partition, without overwriting /home or /var, so you should -- in theory -- be able to just start using it without having to restore anything. (Just ask on the forums how to do this if you're unsure.)
[/LIST]

### Post by mcduck on 2010-04-22
The way I see it that over time all the programs (and the system itself) *will* improve, bugs get fixed and nice little features get added and so on. Sometimes you might even get a big upgrade on some program.

Of course nothing drastic will happen in the six month period between two Ubuntu releases, it's still improving, little by little. Most of the changes might even be too small to notice if you aren't looking for them, but over time and several releases they make a big difference.

So whether you should upgrade to latest version as soon as one gets release or stick with your working system and perhaps skip over couple of Ubuntu versions before you upgrade is simply a question of if you like small, incremental changes with a little improvement here and some small change there, or great big upgrade that changes lots of things at one go.

Unless you have some hardware compatibility issues with the new version there really is no reason not to upgrade, but on the other hand there isn't that big reason to upgrade either if you are happy with what you have now and don't have the need for new versions of all your programs.
