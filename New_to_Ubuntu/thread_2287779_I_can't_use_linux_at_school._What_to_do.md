### Post by grahammechanical on 2015-07-22
Your situation is one reason why some of us recommend dual booting with Windows rather than removing it from the machine. A person never knows when they might need it. I do not have Windows but if I brought a machine with Windows pre-installed I would not remove it as I paid for that OS in the purchase price of the machine.

The router is a piece of hardware and so it is neutral as regards to the OS running on the machine being connected to it. Problems may appear when it comes to logging in to the school network and accessing and exchanging files on that network. And it is in the area of connecting Linux machines to the network that the IT assistant is lacking the necessary training.

Does this school teach IT subjects? You now have a subject for study. You could enlist your parents to respectfully ask the school administrators to give you practical lessons on computer networking with machines having different operating systems.

### Post by mystics on 2015-07-22
Chances are, it isn't impossible to connect, they just won't guide you through it. Connecting through Linux is different than connecting through Windows or Mac, so it is possible the IT people (or at least some of them) just don't know how to do it or don't care (or aren't allowed) to help as it isn't part of their job. There's a similar situation at my school, and it took me a little bit of time to get things working. I learned a lot, though, and it turned out to be an...interesting...introduction to running Linux on my computer.

But as others have said, view this as a learning experience! I would advise looking at what is available to Windows and Mac users and putting that together to at least get a basic idea of what the settings are. If possible, find some friends who have those two and look at their settings. There might still be a little more work on your part, but it's a good place to get started.
