### Post by snowpine on 2011-05-12
I recommend you burn a Live CD of each and take them both for a test drive. 

10.04 uses year-old software applications (10.04=April 2010) including the classic and familiar Gnome desktop.

11.04 (April 2011) is brand-new. It has the latest applications and uses the innovative Unity desktop, which has a completely different user experience than previous Ubuntu versions. Some people love it, some hate it, so you'll have to decide for yourself it it's right for you. :)

Just to complicate the issue, don't forget about Xubuntu, Kubuntu, and Lubuntu... they all have different "user experiences" so it should be easy to find one that works well for **your** needs! ;)

### Post by aphatak on 2011-05-12
I was in exactly the same position about four years ago - after years of using Windows, I converted to Linux.

Initially, I installed Ubuntu 7.11, and then upgraded every six months till I reached 10.04.

I apparently have a 'non-standard' set-up - dual monitors on one desktop, a projector serving as a monitor another, an HP tablet with a stylus-driven screen in the third and so on.  I also have three headless servers, which basically run services such as file storage (with LDAP coming Real Soon Now), but use network card teaming.  I had to go through the pain of figuring out how to make this work every six months: the proprietary drivers I needed wouldn't be available, and VMWare would have to be re-patched and re-compiled.  Such activity, for someone in my age- and weight group, is contra-indicated.

I made the decision to stick to LTS when I reached 10.04, and have lived happily ever since.  If I were to do it over again, I would stick with LTS versions only - I am not in love with sexy GUIs, but I *am* in love with stability!

My conclusion:  if you have time on your hands, and use linux for fun (and therefore system availability is not a concern), by all means try every new version, including alpha versions:  it's a great lot of fun!  However, if you have mission critical applications (such as your better half demanding to know why she can't watch her favorite movie ***_now_***, or lovingly enquiring she's trying to access the photo she wants to e-mail to her friend for the almost *30 seconds, what on earth's wrong?!?*), stick with LTS - in fact don't upgrade unless someone is pointing a gun at your head.

2 cents worth:

The varous LTS versions are intended for people who need a computer that just works. As has already been said here, they are the stable versions. I am still fooling myself that I will leave 10.04 on this machine an d on my netbook. It works well, I don't have any complaints, it will be maintained for 3 years, I won't have to teach my girlfriend how to use it again. (That sound really male chauvinist, but it isn't. I don't know about Linux being painful to learn - in my experience, re-training is minimal.  Sure, it looks different, but you click an icon to launch a menu, right-click to open item-related menus, keys such as ctl-c and ctrl-v work the same. I have seen a point being made of command line in Linux. An example, typing 'ipconfig' at the command line is easier than going to the control panel, then to network, then to properties and so on.  My point is, the choice os interface is almost always yours, whichever OS you are running.

I wouldn't want anyone to be discouraged from trying Linux out - especially Ubuntu - thinking it is somehow more difficult to use than Windows - it is not.

### Post by Bugsy_malone 666 on 2011-05-13
I think a question you should ask yourself is why do you want to switch to linux? I may get shot down for saying that but its for a good reason.

If your going to run Ubuntu as your only operating system then its probably wise to get something with long term support thats actually looked after and works well for a long time, something along the lines of win XP which has had support for 10 years and people find pretty good.

If your going to use dual operating systems with side by side boot (I am doing this at the moment as I am still technically a Noob getting used to Linux) then you can afford to just install whatever lastest version to see what toys and changes they have added to the new version, initially though this can result in the equivilant to windows Vista, something that looks alright but isnt as bug free as you would like/need. Depending on what PC setup you have can also play a part in it, I have a custom build and so far I have run through 8.04/9.04/9.10/10.04LTS/10.10 and now on 11.04 as I am still working bugs out with my graphics cards and update as and when in the hope that it might actually work (so far I am struggling), with some machines linux is fault less, sometimes the less standard machines need a bit of help which I think is where LTS can be a benefit.

Personally I think as mentioned it may be worth installing an LTS version first, dual boot on your machine so you can learn to adapt and see what things you need to use windows for to work with (battlefield heros online gaming is something I cant get to work on linux) and then if you want to see how a new version looks, download a live CD and give it a whirl as if it proves to be unreliable you havent mashed your working operating system.
