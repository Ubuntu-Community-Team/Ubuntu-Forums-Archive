### Post by QIII on 2010-04-22
Being the blasphemous fella in goat hair that I am...

You need not get rid of Windows.  I don't use it, but that's me.

You should use what works.  Sometimes what works is Windows, so it is not anyone's business if you keep it around (but make sure it doesn't crap on your carpet by keeping it in the garage.)

Unless you are deeply, madly, truly willing to never have anything ever to do with Windows again, dual boot (plenty of tutorials.  If you need help, just post back.)  That will give you a chance to find out what it is that YOU think is good about Linux.

you will also have a chance to really get to know the nuts and bolts of Linux while you have a safety net.

Reasons I use Linux?

1.  I'm not chained to Redmond's vision of what the "user experience" should be, but can choose my own.

2.  Although I have no qualms about Windows as an OS (hack, cough... blasphemy again!), I have a huge problem with its purveyor.  I do NOT appreciate market hegemony, monopoly or the fact that Redmond has such a choke hold on the entire industry that everyone has to bow and grovel at the Redmond alter.

3.  The relative safety from viruses and malware.  Don't think nothing exists.  You can be the victim of social engineering and install something malicious on your machine if you trust the wrong people.  But you won't have to worry about "drive-bys".

4.  There is a plethora of software for anything you could possibly imagine you wanted to do on your machine and there are several to choose from in each of those categories.  You get to pick what works best for you and your style.  If you install them from a package manager like Synaptic, you get updates as the software is made better.  Did I say most of that stuff is free and you don't have to buy an upgrade version all the time?

5.  Canonical is committed to making Ubuntu be free, always.  You don't have to pay for the next version.

(A note:  In this world, "free" does not necessarily mean "free" as in beer.  Some software does have to be purchased.  But you are free to make changes as you see fit -- provided you follow whatever licensing arrangement is made by the developers.)

### Post by laffinet on 2010-04-22
+1 for dual booting.

You don't need to go out and buy a linux specific laptop, which might be more expensive.
Select a few win7 laptops that you like, check the hardware compatibility with linux on the internet (or post it here and get feedback). That way you might save a few dollars and still get the laptop that does everything you want.

Install Ubuntu (or some other distro) as a dual boot. From that moment on, try not to boot into windows unless you really really have to and can not figure out how to do something in Ubuntu.
You will find that this will rarely happen, if at all, certainly less as time goes bye. Eventually you might erase windows altogether.

Side note on CLI: people always say that you don't need CLI to run Ubuntu. While that might be true you will find over time that some things are just easier done in CLI. Certainly if you are the one maintaining the computer. Don't be afraid to try it. It's a gradual process.
It'll start with someone posting help in the form of a few lines on the forums. Over time you will get used to some commands and procedures and keep using them. Sometimes CLI is just faster and easier.

Why use Linux: you got a plethora of great applications at your fingertips, that can do almost anything. And they are free. Can't beat that

Why not use Linux: You're not willing to try something new and different. Since you already use Ubuntu on your netbook this shouldn't apply.
