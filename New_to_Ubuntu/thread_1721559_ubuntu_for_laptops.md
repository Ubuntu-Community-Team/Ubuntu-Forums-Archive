### Post by Kixtosh on 2011-04-06
> **wep940 said:**
> ...  I am curious to see if maybe they might run on my notebooks better since they have 1gb and 768mb respectively and a P4-2.44ghz and Celeron 2.7ghz ...With those specifications, it is unlikely you will gain much speed with lighter versions such as Lubuntu, Xubuntu or Peppermint. Even a hard drive installation of Puppy might not be faster than regular Ubuntu.

In my case, on a Pentium M, 1.2 GHz, with a maximum allowable 1,276 MB of RAM, I found a very slight difference in start up time (less than ten seconds faster, probably more like five seconds), but with regular Ubuntu, it's still about 50-55 seconds, and the whole desktop experience is so much better than any of the lightweight solutions I have tried - they took about 45 seconds to boot. Basically, I don't think I'd bother with any of the LXDE based environments unless you have only 512 MB of RAM, or less, and a slower CPU than 1 GHz.

On much older machines than yours, with just 256 MB of RAM and a PII or PIII of less than 1 GHz, then yes: Lubuntu, Peppermint, and Xubuntu with LXDE (not the default XFCE, which is nicer perhaps,  but much slower) have all worked very well for me, and were much better than Ubuntu.

(*P.S. Yes: they are all just as easy to download and install as Ubuntu.)*

### Post by I2k4 on 2011-04-06
> **wep940 said:**
> Compared to "regular" Ubuntu, how hard is it to download and install xubuntu or lubuntu?

Download the .ISO file you want (personally suggest Ubuntu 10.10 Desktop or Lubuntu, but haven't tried Xubuntu) and then step by step, with illustrations:

[http://www.ubuntu.com/desktop/get-ubuntu/download](http://www.ubuntu.com/desktop/get-ubuntu/download)

If you want to compare them ON THE LIVE USB without risking installation to the hard drive, be sure to install "Persistence" at the end of Step 2 "Show Me How" - otherwise the USB installation won't save your settings and preferences between boots.  I suggest using a 4GB USB drive and setting 2GB of "Persistence" during the installation.

I'd start with Ubuntu Desktop 10.10 just because as a complete "solution" it's fairly easy to learn and preinstalls a lot of software - really runs quite well off the USB, even on a weakling netbook.  I find Lubuntu much faster and lighter on RAM and battery life, but with less user help and requires installing most of what you want - so it's good to know a bit before using it.

Have fun.
