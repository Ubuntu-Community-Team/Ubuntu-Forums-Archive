### Post by Coreigh on 2009-04-24
That there are many MANY ways of doing something is one of the core principles of Linux. Perhaps not intended but it is true just the same. The Unix way of operating a computer is based on the idea that there are many small simple programs that a user can string together in any way needed to complete a task. The open source ideology invites people to create and share their solutions with others. These two systems together (Linux) will naturally lead to multitudes of solutions. The only down side in my eyes is figuring out which works best for you.

The ONLY thing I have found that I can't do as well with Linux as with the other two (MS and Mac) is speech to text.

The biggest issue with hardware is lack of support from manufacturers. Linux drivers, if thought of at all, are often an afterthought. They are also very reluctant to share the source code of whatever they do have.

This leaves the Open Source community of coders to try and figure things out the hard way - by figuring out how it works on their own and writing drivers for it.

Add to this, as you noted, there are literally dozens of variations of Linux, often with disparate and independent groups figuring out ways to make things work. It's not like they are all working in concert.

The advantages, of course, is that everything anyone comes up with is open for anyone else to use - no NDAs to sign, no proprietary nonsense, etc.

So, eventually, things get worked out, but there may be a dozen ways to do it.

Personally, considering that it is all volunteer effort, I think it's an amazing thing. For that matter, you have hundreds of people on this forum willing to help if they can, and many of them will go out of their way to do so, by doing web searches, for example. Again, the users of this forum are doing it all for free.

Stick with it, and things will start to click into place, at least to the point that when you have a problem, you'll know how to find out if anyone else has and if/how they fixed it.

If you find a bug no one else has, report it, and then you'll be part of the process that helps this great big juggernaut keep rolling. You'd be surprised how often and how quickly you'll get feedback, often directly from the programmers themselves.
