### Post by TheFu on 2023-10-10
Nothing replaces trying the software yourself.  Youj'll need to get used to not paying for software, not having to track license keys and not getting to spend 2x more on hardware because it is "compatible" with Apple's stuff.  This can be a huge effort for some people.

You'll also need to get used to the idea of a bazaar for all software.  The menus will probably all be different. There's no mandated, enforce, GUI standards.  The GUI is just another program, like a file manager is just another program.  There are lots to pick from and your should try a few before deciding.  Choice - is a strength of Linux, but it can be overwhelming.

For example, there isn't 1 file system on Linux. There are 20 that are popular, each with different strengths and weaknesses.  That's probably new to you.  You seem to have grouped "Finder" with a file system.  That really isn't how they work.  "Finder" is a GUI file manager, not a file system.

I would say that you should plan on converting all your Apple Music to open forums.  In the F/LOSS world, we use vorbis in an ogg container for audio which isn't encumbered by patents. All web browsers can play this type of music ... except I guess Apple stuff.  Most audio players can handle it too.  Vorbis is more like Apple's AAC and provided excellent quality around 196 Kbps.  Of course, MP3 can be used too, or FLAC, if you like to waste storage, but FLAC support in portable audio devices is spotty.

If you can, use Linux daily for 4+ months inside a VM before actually deciding to change.  Try a few different distros, a few different GUIs, and figure out the other things you need.  You seem to be deeply ingrained into the Apple stuff.  When I had a MacOS system, moving from Linux to it was very difficult. I wanted more control over everything and found it very frustrating that I wasn't allowed to control things.  I lasted just 3 weeks, before returning the machine. The last week, I wanted to throw it against a wall.  I wasn't being flexible and my expectations weren't fair.  The reason to choose MacOS is for the centralized, consistent, design.  That doesn't exist in Linux.  At the time, nobody was attacking MacOS systems either, so it was perceived to be safer. Then we learned that Apple was being lazy and running really old BSD code full of security issues.

BTW, if you posted text, not an image, I'd be more likely to make suggestions for other things because I could copy/paste.
I assume you know about Alternativeto.net, right?

### Post by ian-weisser on 2023-10-11
Switching from Proprietary to Open Source requires a change of perspective.

Most of your power-user skills no longer apply. You will be a beginner again. Be willing to learn and experiment. VMs are great for that.

You will not be a "customer" anymore. Your relationships with others in the community won't be customer-vendor. You will be a participant, and your relationships will be different.

Since you are not paying for the software, responsibility and liability work differently. That's why , for example, open-source wallets haven't been popular -- if your money is stolen, you cannot sue the vendor (there isn't a vendor). It's YOUR responsibility to safeguard your data and your valuables. You cannot delegate that responsibility to community-created software.

Some of your data simply won't be exportable from the Apple ecosystem. Not a shortcoming of Linux. You paid Apple for that feature.

Your workflow will change. Some features that you might love in the Apple ecosystem simply won't have a corresponding feature outside, particularly various forms of data-sharing that are linked to your AppleID. You can build new data-sharing networks, but it's not quite the same.
