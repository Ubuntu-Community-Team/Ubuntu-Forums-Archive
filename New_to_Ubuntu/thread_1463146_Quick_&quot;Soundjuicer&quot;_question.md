### Post by Bucky Ball on 2010-04-26
The simple answer to this is that there is a CD quality standard known as the Red Book Standard (PCM also), which is 16bit and sampled at 44, 100 Hz.

Consequently, saving to anything better than this would make no difference because you can't improve on the quality of your source material.

For more detail:

[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Book_(audio_Compact_Disc_standard]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Book_%28audio_Compact_Disc_standard"))

No doubt this standard will change as the way we listen to music does (MP3s are in fact poor quality and if you have the opportunity to test this by listening to a high quality FLAC from vinyl (or live performance), make an MP3 at Red Book standard and compare). The way MP3s get so small is they basically squeeze the 'air' out during compression. Long story.

If you were making recordings from vinyl, of course, all this changes because vinyl has no such standard and the quality you get is as good as your stylus, amplifier,  specifics of the file type you are saving to and the settings (24bit at 48, 000 will make a difference, as will an MP3 beyond Red Book, say 24bit and 320Kbps). 

Hope that helps a bit. Bottom line: ripping to a higher quality than Red Book from a manufactured CD from the local music store is a waste of space. You can only get as good as the source. ;)
