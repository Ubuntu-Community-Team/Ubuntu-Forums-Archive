### Post by Cypher on 2009-01-04
The easiest way to see where you'd stand with Linux is to grab the Live CD for Ubuntu Intrepid Ibex 8.10 and burn that. Boot with the CD in the drive and after some time you will eventually (hopefully) see the Gnome desktop. 

Here you can see just how well all of your peripherals got recognized. See if your sound works. You might also want to see if the wireless also work. 

You'd be amazed to see what just works with Linux, now if certain things are indeed not working, there's likely drivers/steps available to remedy that.

But with the Live CD, you get a chance to play around with Ubuntu/Linux and experience all the cool things people have been saying. Should you choose to go ahead with a Linux installation, you can do so using Wubi or dual-boot just so that you have your XP installation available should you change your mind..

### Post by jiangshi on 2009-01-05
There are many advantages to using Linux over Windows, but most are below the surface, especially given the tasks with which you use your computer. I think Scorp123 answered that best. Like the old saying: If it ain't broke, don't fix it.

I am also a long time Windows user. My switch to Linux came because of two primary reasons. The first is technical curiosity. I've been a tech-head all my life (62 years!), and after working on Windows platforms for 25 years, I became bored with Windows. The second reason is costs. Over the years, I have spent a small fortune on software and books for Windows. The only money I have spent on Linux is a couple of small donations and buying a couple of versions of SuSE from Novell. Well, to be honest, I still buy computer books, but much less with Linux.

Under the hood, there is no comparison between Linux and Windows. Windows is a toy operating system when compared to the capability of Linux. However, those capabilities are not something the single end user is going to care much about. If you don't have a network or work in a multi-platform environment as an admin or are in similar situations, there is not much need to change.

On the other hand, if you have some curiosity and would like to experiment a bit with using a different OS, then Linux is a fine way to do that. Find an old machine at a garage sale that's 500 mhz or faster, has a 20 gb drive, and about 256 megs of memory and load 'er up with Ubuntu. SuSE is very excellent too, especially when it comes to hardware recognition. Get youself a book. I recommend Ubuntu Unleashed by Sam's Publishing. You can find them on 3rd party Amazon or your favorite used/new bookstore. It generally comes with CD's or DVD with the OS on it, and these books are great at taking you step-by-step. You'll be a wizard in no time.

Another alternative that has been suggested here is to use a live CD. Just stick it in your drive and boot up your machine. You might have to adjust your boot device order in your BIOS, but that's easy. You can sample Linux that way. The only drawback is speed. You'll find that operating from a CD is a bit slower than if the OS was installed on the hard drive. You can also dual-boot your single computer. That way you preserve your Windows installation and share the drive with Linux. When your machine boots up, you can choose which system to go to.

Whatever you decide to do, the best of luck, and most importantly, have some fun with this stuff. Otherwise, it gets way too serious.

~jiangshi
