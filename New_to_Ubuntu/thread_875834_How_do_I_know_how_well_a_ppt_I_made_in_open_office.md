### Post by darthmob on 2008-07-31
I converted the few presentations I had to do for university so far to pdf (both latex beamer and open office are capable to do so). it may look less good sometimes (especially when using open office) but you can be sure it will look the same on every system. there can be strange side effects with ppt-presentations on different versions (for example wrong positioned symbols)! I'm not sure though what it is like with sound & video in pdf (most likely it will not work as it is intended to).

PS: in general you should not put too much fancy stuff into the presentation! those transitions seem fine when implementing them, but they only hinder you when you are actually presenting. for example when there are questions and you have to scroll some pages back you always have to wait for the effect.

PPS: it does not automatically seem more professional, if one slide is warping into the screen with a "fsssshh" noise and the next one "ploing"s into view.

PPPS: I think it is a bit risky to put sound and video into a presentation you are not going to show on a system you tested it with. I'm not sure how literate your teacher is with pcs, but it may be not so good if he does not get the videos to work for whatever reason.

PPPPS: I'm one of those guys who always expects the worst. keep it in mind when reading my post. :)

### Post by scorp123 on 2008-07-31
> **barbedsaber said:**
>  I made a presentation in open office impress, with music and sounds and slide transitions, (I think they honestly make it look more professional Honestly: Leave those sound effects and those transitions away. They are annoying. Let the data you present speak for itself. And keep your document at under 20 slides ... unless you want to risk your audience to fall asleep. And don't do any "overkills" with colors and varying fonts etc. ... your presentation should be pleasing to the eye and easy to read even after a long long long working day. Avoid drastic color combinations (e.g. yellow letters on a pink background ... YUCK!) ... as I said: it should be pleasant to the eyes, short and sweet.

> **barbedsaber said:**
>  What parts are likely to work properly on Powerpoint, and what parts should I just take out?  If you want a 100% guarantee that the document will really *REALLY* really look 100% on your teacher's desktop the way *YOU* intended it to look then: **convert it to Adobe PDF!**
[U][B]
Only with PDF's[/B][/U] can you be sure that the document will *absolutely* look the same on anyone else's screen as it does on your screen. End of the story. With PowerPoint or any other format?? **Not possible.** It *definitely* WILL look different. Even if both machines by coincidence happen to have the same fonts installed (a missing font is a guarantee that a PowerPoint file might look ***very*** different!) it's already enough if he uses a screen resolution that just happens to be different from your's. 

**If you want your document to look on your teacher's screen the way you intended it to look ... _use PDF._**

Believe me, I spent more than enough time in phone conferences and business meetings with PowerPoint slides to know that this format should be best avoided altogether. I have seen people lose business deals because their PowerPoint slides looked like crap ... and only because they had to present the documents on other people's laptops ;) ... No thanks, I convert my stuff into PDF and I know for 100% sure that it will look 100% the same as it did on my screen back at home.
