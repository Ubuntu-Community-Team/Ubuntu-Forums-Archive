### Post by DuckHook on 2013-10-01
Torrent speeds are a black art. There are so many factors that it is almost impossible to isolate enough of them to definitively say that one client is faster than another. It depends on your HW, the settings of both your client and your router (some clients work better with large maximum port settings on the router, but past a certain point, the router starts choking), your NIC, your cable modem, your peers/settings/options and the intricate interplay between each and every one of these components.

I've read tons of arguments on various threads claiming that one client/OS combo is better than another, but I don't place any credence on any of these claims, not least because, over time, they usually start sounding like religious arguments. The best way is simply to time them yourself.

My experience is atypical. I torrent using an ancient laptop on which I've installed a minimal CLI version of Ubuntu and *rtorrent*. Since it happily chugs away in the obscurity of my basement closet, I've never bothered to time it. All I ever torrent are Linux distros anyway (my contribution back to the community), so I have no idea if they are fast, slow, lean or fat. You may get better answers over at the gaming subforum where such expertise may be more, shall we say, sought after.

EDIT

P.S. The biggest factor affecting speed is ISP throttling, of which my provider swears they don't do, and of which I swear they are full of beans. I think all discussions about which client is superior pales in comparison to ISP throttling.

/EDIT

### Post by Lars Noodén on 2013-10-02
Though some ISPs throttle torrents, they have a big advantage over FTP and HTTP in that they do checksumming and error correction as they download.  That means when you are finished with the download, you have a perfect copy.  With FTP and HTTP you have to check afterwards and if there is an error, the whole thing needs to be downloaded from the beginning.
