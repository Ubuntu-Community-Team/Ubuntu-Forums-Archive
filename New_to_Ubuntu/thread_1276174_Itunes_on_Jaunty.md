### Post by Brian Vaughan on 2009-09-26
Downloading digital restrictions management media is a bad idea, for several reasons. Among others, several former DRM merchants have shut down their services, rendering the DRM media they sold unusable. You have less access to DRM music than to music on a CD you've bought in a shop. More broadly, DRM is a technique of using software to prohibit people from exercising their legal rights, such as the "fair use" reproduction of media that is necessary for cultural criticism and scholarship.

See [DefectiveByDesign.org]("http://www.defectivebydesign.org/").

DRM is completely at odds with the principles of free software. It's one of many reasons why I switched to GNU/Linux.

I agree that you're better off downloading music from Amazon, since it comes in the form of a high-quality MP3 that's not crippled in any way. eMusic has an odd subscription model, but I found their prices low and their selection good. And there are other services as well that offer uncrippled media. I wish more would offer Ogg Vorbis or FLAC as options in addition to MP3.

I find the idea of trying to install iTunes via Wine strange, for three reasons. First, there are *lots* of good music players available for GNU/Linux, such as Rhythmbox, installed by default in Ubuntu, so it seems odd to choose a non-native application over a native one. Second, there's the DRM issue. Third, iTunes will rearrange and relabel your music collection without your authorization, often inaccurately, which I find highly irritating. (Windows Media Player does this as well).
