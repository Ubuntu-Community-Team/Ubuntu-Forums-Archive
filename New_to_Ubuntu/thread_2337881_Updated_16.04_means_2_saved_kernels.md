### Post by harfin on 2016-09-22
Back in November 2014 I opened a thread regarding my inability to update my system which is running 16.04LTS.

Apparently because I have encrypted my laptop, there was insufficient room to have any more than three saved kernels. The recommendations made within the forum *(CoffeeCat and StickyMaster I seem to recall were two of those who pointed me towards the solution back then)* were that if the error message appeared again, I should identify the oldest kernel using the command **'ls -l /boot' ** and (using Synaptics) delete that oldest kernel.

A couple of weeks ago I was advised of a significant update of 16.04LTS, and subsequently I downloaded / installed the new version.

Now I get the "Insufficient Space" error message when there are only two saved kernels. 

I am no expert, but it seems to me that only having two saved kernels may mean that smaller number may mean an unacceptable vulnerability.

I would appreciate any guidance.
Alan

Your /boot is not full (54%) with two kernel sets.
There is no extra cruft in your /boot.
Your /boot should easily be able to hold a third kernel.
You don't seem to have the same problem in that old thread.

You have *four* sets of kernel headers installed, so 1% inode use seems rather unlikely. Are you sure about that output?
Kernel headers fill inodes quickly.
Use apt to remove your -31 and -34 header packages and see if that solves the problem.

### Post by ian-weisser on 2016-10-12
Changing that setting enables unattended-upgrades. u-u will automatically run every 24 hours. It will automatically install new kernels for you. In 16.04 and later, 24 hours after installing a new kernel, u-u will delete the old kernel.

There are two possible outcomes:
More likely: If apt continues to work, new kernels are installed, and old kernels deleted without apt throwing errors, then the problem was an erroneous disk-space calculation bug by Software Updater or some dependency.
Less likely: If apt fails due to out-of-space, then your problem is a /boot that is too small, and your partitions must be resized a bit. This is rather a pain to do on LVM systems, but can be done.

Run 'sudo apt update' and then 'sudo apt upgrade' in two weeks to discover which outcome has occurred..
