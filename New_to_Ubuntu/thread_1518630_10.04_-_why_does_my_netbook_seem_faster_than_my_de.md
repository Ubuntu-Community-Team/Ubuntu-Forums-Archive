The kernels are the exact same in UNE and UDE, there is no hidden optimisations. There used to be an LPIA architecture toolchain that was aimed for Netbooks. But due to Atom CPUs continuing to support IA32, and kernel configurations in LPIA and i386 configurations being identical, it was dropped.
Since then, a number of those LPIA optimisations have been brought into i386.

The reason why your Netbook is faster is most likely for the reasons you stress in your OP.

As 80% of Desktop activity is cache oriented, the size of RAM and Disk speed matter a lot on the apparent speed of your Desktop.

Of course, a faster HDD == faster access speeds - sort of. If you were to replace your Desktop HD with a SSD, you'd probably notice the huge difference in boot and application load performance. However, write speeds may be hampered. ;)

What is more interesting, however, is that your Netbook has 256MB more RAM available. As 256MB is pretty much what is needed to load an Ubuntu Desktop, your Netbook should have 786MB of free memory available for cache, whereas your Desktop will have 512MB (in approximation).

Because Linux is still quite tiny (in comparison to other systems) - chances are that your Netbook will happily be able to keep all your applications in the system memory, so it doesn't need to touch the hard disk - which has slower access than RAM - to be able to re-read and load them.


Some people have mentioned hyperthreading - I don't have much to say about it, other than I think it is over-hyped and the wrong way to go about solving a problem. Whether or not it actually makes things quicker, I disagree in this case. For reasons I state above - Desktop being cache oriented rather than CPU oriented systems. :P

### Post by cascade9 on 2010-06-28
They do, sort of. Those names are just codenames for the CPUs that got into common usage for various reasons. 

AMD used to make just 1 line of CPUs, then decided that they needed a 'budget' competitor to the Celeron, originally called 'Duron' but then changing to 'Sempron' later. 

The main line for the socket A was the 'Athlon' (P3/P4 competitor) with various codenames (Thunderbird, Palomino, Thoroughbred A and B, Barton, Thorton). The Durons had thier own chip types (Spitfire, Morgan, Applebred). Semprons used the Athlon chips ( Thoroughbred B, Barton, Thorton), but were rerated because the XXXX+ was against a Celeron, not a P3/P4. So what was a Athlon 32000+ (Barton, 2200MHz, 512k cache) became a Sempron 3300+ with the same spefications. 

*thinks* I probably should have used socket 754/939 as a comparison, but they just get more confusing.
