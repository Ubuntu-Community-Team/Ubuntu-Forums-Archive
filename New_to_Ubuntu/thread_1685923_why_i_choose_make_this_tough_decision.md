### Post by rg4w on 2011-02-11
As a long-time Mac user migrating to Ubuntu, I'm quite the noob myself.  The upside is that I can very much appreciate the learning curve you're climbing, but the downside is that I'm of little practical value in answering specific questions like ad hoc networks.  :)

That said, one of the things that attracted me to Ubuntu is its critical mass, the size of the surrounding community.  For most questions I've had I find it's been easy to just use Google when I put "Ubuntu " before whatever the issue is I can find the perfect answer for this distro easily.  When that fails these forums are a godsend, much as Windows forums can be very helpful for Mac people getting started learning that OS.

I was able to turn up what looks like a helpful page addressing your issue on ad hoc networks:
[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/Adhoc](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/Adhoc)

Best of all, the instructions show that finding your way to where to set that up is just a right-click away, just like Windows; but like Windows, you first have to know where to right-click. :)

Hope this helps.  If not I'm sure some of the wizards here will chime in with good advice.  This is a remarkably helpful forum, so I'm confident you'll have that up and running well in no time.

On the command-line stuff:   

I appreciate that for many newcomers it's a total turnoff, but given the long history of Linux it's going to remain a useful way to solve problems for some time.

Even still, one of the things I like about Ubuntu is its emphasis on usability, its slogan being "Linux for human beings".  Sure, it's not quite CLI-free, and probably will never completely be, but I find myself using Terminal less in Ubuntu than with any other distro I've tried over the years, and each release of Ubuntu adds just a little more fit and finish to make the necessity of the command line ever smaller.   It'll always be there for admins and pros who need it, but most end-users rarely need it at all once their system is set up for their particular hardware, and sometimes not even for that.

Ubuntu is a long way from perfect, but its usability is growing by leaps and bounds each year.
