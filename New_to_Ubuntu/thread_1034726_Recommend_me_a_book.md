### Post by NewJack on 2009-01-09
I would recommend Ubuntu Kung Fu by Keir Thomas.  The second chapter is titled "Administration Crash Course" and gives a lot of good starting ground for new users to work with.  It's presented well. 

The rest of the chapters give lots of great little tips for new users and some not-so-new users who haven't learned all the ins and outs of Ubuntu.

[http://www.amazon.com/Ubuntu-Kung-Fu-Tricks-Hints/dp/1934356220/ref=pd_bbs_sr_1?ie=UTF8&s=books&qid=1231479355&sr=8-1](http://www.amazon.com/Ubuntu-Kung-Fu-Tricks-Hints/dp/1934356220/ref=pd_bbs_sr_1?ie=UTF8&s=books&qid=1231479355&sr=8-1)

On sale on Amazon for $23.07 (that is what I got it for).

PS:  There is also the Official Ubuntu Book too.

### Post by vikigal on 2009-01-09
I find myself reaching for the Linux Phrasebook more than any other book. I use it when reading online tutorials, the man pages and everything else. As a fairly new user I often find myself asking what the heck is that command for and do I use it? The Phrasebook makes using the terminal command line understandable. You can also look-up commands asked for alot here in the forums and find out what they are for. It is from Safari and you get online access with the purchase of the hard copy: the best of both worlds.

Over the years I have developed an inherent distrust of internet information that has sometimes hindered me in following the instructions online. Using a book it is easier to read ahead and find out what to expect and too look for solutions just in case.
