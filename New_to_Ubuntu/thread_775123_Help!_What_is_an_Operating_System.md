Well kinda...  in short form any way.  Here's the long version:

My local church has a religious school (free tuition FTW!) that uses a software package called "Switched On Schoolhouse" to teach their lessons.  They have just gotten an agreement with another local school (public) to buy up that school's old computers to replace the older machines they're using now.  The church is funding the purchase of the older machines by selling off the older ones plus some of the new (used) machines.

I managed to convince my business partner who is also the school's administrator to offer to put Linux (Ubuntu) on these older computers for an additional $50.00.  So that's (hardware) $25.00 + (labor) $50.00 = $75.00 for a functional legal machine.

Otherwise, people will have to buy the machine AND a copy of Windows AND **still** pay to have someone install it...  That's [at our low low prices--lowest in town AFAIK] (hardware) $25.00 + (Windows) $99.00--$199.99 + (labor) at $40.00 an hour or our $100.00 WINstall special = Too #### expensive for a $25.00 "cheap" computer!

The problem is trying to explain to the church people (who get first pick) what exactly an Operating System is.  They just don't get it.  It's not that they don't know about Linux--it's more fundamental than that--they don't know what an Operating System IS even.  And i'm not sure even if I showed them a picture of Ubuntu, a video of it in action or even my laptop running Hardy Heron they'd really understand what they were seeing isn't...

They don't understand the abstraction of an Operating System.

They don't even understand that Windows is an Operating System--they think the GUI **is** the computer...

It isn't a Windows vs Linux thing.  It's an gap in understanding that I'm not quite sure I know how to bridge...

I used to think I could just explain it's "just a free way to run your Internet browsing, music playing, video watching, and word processing that is also more secure against viruses and doesn't crash very often."

It's clear to me now that isn't enough, but I don't know how else to explain it....

### Post by Calash on 2008-04-30
Think of your computer as plumbing

The pipes are the hardware, faucet is your interface.  The water is the operating system.  The fixtures sure look pretty, but without water in them (OS) they don't do much.


You have a choice of what water you use in your plumbing.

City water (Windows) has a lot of chemicals added but it is always there and works, though the chemicals can cause problems if not monitored right.  

Well water (Linux) is from your own back yard, and only has what you put into it.  There are kits you can buy that will purify the water for you, or if you want to you can go out and drill yourself (Gentoo).


Or...you can skip it all and get a water cooler (OSX).  The hardware is similar to the more generic pluming, and you can run regular water through it but it comes with it's own special water designed for the pluming.  It is a bit streamlined, but great for people who just want to push a button and get a cup of water.
