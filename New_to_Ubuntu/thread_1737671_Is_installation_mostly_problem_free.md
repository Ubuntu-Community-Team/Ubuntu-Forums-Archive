### Post by Longstreet on 2011-04-23
I'm fairly new to Ubuntu myself. I recently set up a dual-boot on my Aspire One. The process can be simple to fairly elaborate, depending on what you want the final result to be. I wanted to be able to access documents, photos, music, etc from both operating systems, and so had to set my system up a little differently. It wasn't hard, just had to be careful, and I found some excellent advice from great folks on here to help out on the way.

As it turned out, I didn't need to worry. I kept the Windows partition because I had two programs that I really needed to keep, and they both run fine in Wine. In fact, I think Kindle for PC works better in Wine than it did in Windows, though that's purely a subjective call. My Bible study program looked a little odd initially, but I picked a different font and now everything looks, and works, just like it's supposed to. In the month that I've been running Ubuntu, I've found exactly one reason, one time, to switch to my Windows partition. Otherwise, everything I need or want to do I can do in Linux. I'm betting you'll find that to be the case as well.

The links in mwl's post are two that I used to get an understanding of the process. This place is full of good info, and people that are willing to share it. Once you get your drive partitioned (not as scary as it might sound), installation is basically a matter of putting the boot CD in and following the prompts.

If you decide to dual-boot so you can still use Visual dBase, or anything else, don't worry that one OS will affect the other. If you set up your partitions properly, the Windows partition won't interfere with the Ubuntu partition, and vice versa.
