### Post by rootbear on 2010-03-06
> **His said:**
> I just wanted to get some opinions about what you think are must have apps for Ubuntu and Linux.

Simply naming apps and utilities that are already in Ubuntu/Linux is missing the point unless one believes that they are sub-standard.

My main gripe with Ubuntu/Linux is mainly the the poor implementation of features and facilities readily available in commercial software such as Windows and Macs. The current implementations can only be described as "lacking", in my opinion, and relegate the operating system's user experience  to a mere curiosity.

To answer the question, some "must-haves" which come to mind include:


[LIST=1]
[*]tight integration of workflow in the areas of mail, calendaring (esp. group calendaring), instant messaging.
[*]proactive support for newer forms of hardware on the market.
[*]seamless, rock solid connectivity with existing external hardware such as Blackberry, Palm devices and iPhone, webcams.
[*]rock solid wireless connectivity.
[*]end user extensibility with easy-to-use programming tools.
[*]document management and workflow tools, esp. accurate OCR technology.
[*]support for a wide variety of scanners.
[*]rock solid multimedia capabilities.
[/LIST]

... just to name a few. Anything less and wide adoption of Ubuntu/Linux will always remain just a dream rather than a viable choice.

On a related note, the current penchant for Ubuntu developers to tinker with trivialities such as the user interface (i.e. Gnome and KDE) and themes/skin rather than tackling real issues that matter to users is self-defeating, bordering on the absurd and ridiculous.

Just my two cents worth!  :)

### Post by PhilGil on 2010-03-07
A few of the extras I install:

ubuntu-restricted-extras: Installs the Microsoft core font package, flash, and a slew of essential audio and video codecs.

DigiKam: Outstanding photo organization and editing tool.  Well worth installing the KDE dependencies if you're using Gnome.

KeePassX: Encrypted password database.  Cross-platform compatible with Windows.

GnuCash: Finance manager.  It takes a bit of effort to learn, but it's a very capable piece of software.

VirtualBox: Run Windows or any other Linux distro inside Ubuntu.

GRsync: Graphical front end to the RSync backup/synchronization tool.
