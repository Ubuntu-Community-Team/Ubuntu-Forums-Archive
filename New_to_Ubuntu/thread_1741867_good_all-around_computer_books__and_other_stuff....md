### Post by Zill on 2011-04-28
> **RememberWhenItRained said:**
> ...I figure with OSs though, it's a lot more of a hands on thing, so while i will be reading these, my main idea is to just keep dual booting and using Ubuntu as default. Still need to take an active approach to learning the terminal and shell, i suppose...
You are on the right track here.  There is no right or wrong way to learn - just try things out on a system you don't mind breaking and you will "learn as you go".  I do suggest you have one system for your "real" work and one to play with - this one doesn't need to have a great spec, particularly if you are using a shell rather than a GUI.  Also, don't forget that the MAN pages are a very useful resource built right into your system.

There is so much to learn that it may help if you try to narrow down your area(s) of interest.  Having said that, *everything* you learn about different aspects of computing will add value to your knowledge base.  I suggest you keep a logbook (either hard-copy, soft-copy or a wiki-type app) detailing all your experiments, showing what works and what doesn't.  This will be a valuable personal resource for you in future.

My own experience: I have been using Linux systems since around 2005 - and still learn new stuff every single day. ;-)

Good luck.
