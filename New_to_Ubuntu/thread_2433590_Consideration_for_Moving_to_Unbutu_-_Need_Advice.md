### Post by TheFu on 2019-12-21
For those requirements, stay on Apple.  You will not be happy with the tiny differences and will find it terribly frustrating.

Apple teaches users to know paid products, not protocols and standards.  Unix people think in terms of protocols and standards so different devices and programs can inter-operate.  That is against the Apple business model.  How many non-Apple users can use iMessage?   ZERO.  

Android to Linux integrations are many.  
[https://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2018/11/connect-android-ubuntu-gsconnect](https://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2018/11/connect-android-ubuntu-gsconnect)
[https://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2019/03/kde-connect-app-sms-features-removed](https://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2019/03/kde-connect-app-sms-features-removed)

There is good news. When you switch to Linux and Android, you'll never need see another lightning cable or be forced into buying some $50 apple-specific cable again!  All my HDMI, microUSB and normal USB-a cables still work just fine.  No more "Apple Tax" either and you won't be stuck running 5+ yr old versions of Unix software. OSX routinely has ignored patching Unix software.  Saw they were running a 5+ yr old version of bash last weekend when I helped someone wipe OSX and install Ubuntu on their Apple laptop.

"good decent computer" is highly subjective.  For many people, a $70 raspberry pi v4, connected to a used keyboard, mouse and spare TV is a great desktop.  Is that "decent"?  Other people aren't happy with a Ryzen 9 Threadripper with 128GB of RAM and 10 TB of SSD NVMe storage.  I'm running a multitude of different systems, most built using new parts merged with old cases, PSUs, monitors, etc. My current Asus Core i5-8250U laptop was $405.  I've had more expensive machines many times previously, but none with this much battery life, the same capabilities, good keyboard, and good-enough screen (assuming you can live with 1080p).

Linux is about options and choice.  Coming from Apple, that might be overwhelming to you.  BTW, lots of people wipe their Apple computers and run Linux on them, happily.

If you expect things to magically work between an iPhone and non-Apple computer, I think you've been smoking something - not vaping.

You don't HAVE TO BUY anything, but there are easier phones which work with Linux than Apple.  But you shouldn't expect the same level of integration that Apple OSX and iPhone provide. That just isn't possible AND Apple is really great at making all Apple products just work between each other. They deserve credit for that ... and charge 2x more than others for it.

I suspect you should load up an Ubuntu-Studio variant to see if that meets some of the needs.
For finding similar software, check out [https://alternativeto.net/](https://alternativeto.net/)

While Linux has a number of professional video editors, I think 5 last time I looked, most people who come from Adobe tools aren't sufficiently impressed to change. For my video editing needs, I've been disappointed with Adobe and Linux solutions. What a creator needs is very different from my needs.

Said you didn't want an Apple or Android phone.  Did you want a $20 flip phone like from 2005 or is there some other type that is currently made and popular?  Blackberry and MSFT don't make phone OSes anymore.

If you don't like Android due to Google, you can run Android devices without any google integrations. There are a number of android devices that are popular with alternative firmware and OSes, but those aren't end-user friendly. You'll need an unlocked boot loader to load these alternative OSes and only a few specific device models will work.  And **all** the letters+numbers in the device model matters.  I have a device with xyz-L24n as the model.  There are lots of xyz-L22i and xyz-L20e models which appear to be identical on the outside and have firmware and alternative OS compatibility, but not my xyz-L24n version.  There are hundreds of different models.

I figure that's enough feedback.  Some might even be helpful. Perhaps.

### Post by TheFu on 2020-01-03
"decent" is vague and subjective.  What each person thinks is "decent" is different.  Linux isn't Windows and doesn't demand similar resources unless you demand to use all the cheese (fancy graphics) that a bloated Ubuntu with Gnome3 wants.  There are Linux distros that run in 100MB of RAM.  These can easily handing email, web surfing, lite word processing on a $50 used PC or Raspberry Pi v4.   Or you can spend $4000 and get a system that is a little faster.  You haven't said anything that conveys really what you want or consider "decent."  We aren't you.  We don't use computers the same way that you do, so "decent" means something very different, I suspect.

Why not just use an Apple desktop for your Linux system?  Run a virtual machine and learn what "decent" means to you?  Spending money when that isn't needed seems like a waste to me.  You shouldn't need to spend $0.01 to try this out.

I wrote: 
> If you don't like Android due to Google, you can run Android devices without any google integrations. There are a number of android devices that are popular with alternative firmware and OSes, but those aren't end-user friendly. You'll need an unlocked boot loader to load these alternative OSes and only a few specific device models will work. And **all** the letters+numbers in the device model matters. I have a device with xyz-L24n as the model. There are lots of xyz-L22i and xyz-L20e models which appear to be identical on the outside and have firmware and alternative OS compatibility, but not my xyz-L24n version. There are hundreds of different models.

Android is just an OS.  It can be configured NOT to use anything from Google.  Today there are 2 smartphone base OSes.  iOS and Android.  One is completely proprietary and the other is open for knowledgeable people to modify.  China has taken Android and made a Chinese-specific, non-Google, version.  There must be 20 other non-Google versions of Android OSes you can use.  Or, I'll say it again, did you intend to use a flip-phone from 2005?

Look for a playlist converter so you can drop apple stuff.  m3u and pls are the standard formats. Any player that doesn't support one of those isn't a player you need.  It isn't like there aren't 50 different audio players.  Most of these playlists are just text files. Unix systems are masters at text manipulation.  There are hundreds of text processing tools built-into the OS.  I would be surprised if OSX didn't have most also pre-loaded too.  I will admit, I've never looked at any itunes playlists and don't know anything about them.  I had an OSX machine for 3 weeks - it drove me crazy.  Nothing was configurable.

As for media players - you seem to think in the Apple way still.  Devices support protocols.  When you don't choose proprietary Apple stuff, then all the different devices tend to support a protocol that is widely supported.  Every MP3 player I've ever owned either looked like USB storage when connected to my computers or they supported the MTP protocol for transferring files.  My smart phones usually let me drop rsync onto the device, then sync files/music/videos however I liked.  Think protocols, not "what does apple support."

The only way to know for certain is to try it with our individual equipment, doing the things we need to accomplish.  For point-n-click users, if what they use is available, it will be great. For example, I had to edit & convert a lot of family videos from DVD format to MPEG4. In Windows or Mac worlds, this would cost $$$. I found out that with Ubuntu, I could use OpenShot to edit my videos and use Handbrake to convert my videos to MPEG4 ... and all that costed me zero dollar !!!
 
Isn't handbrake cross-platform?  [https://handbrake.fr/downloads.php](https://handbrake.fr/downloads.php)  Yep.  Windows, Mac and Linux.  Of course, when loading it under Linux, please use the PPA and do not download the .deb file directly.  Hopefully, you aren't really using mpeg4 video still.  .MP4 is just a video file container, but inside using h.264 or h.265 video codecs has been the standard for 8+ yrs.  All those consumer playback devices have supported  h.264 video inside either an mp4 or mkv container for a very long time.  Divx and mpeg4 video was popular pre-HiDef, so from 2005 until about 2009.  Anyways, most people should be using h.264 video and mkv for video files today.  In a few more years, h.265/AC1 will take over as average, cheap, devices gain support.  OTOH, my roku3 player doesn't support h.265 and probably has at least 5 more yrs of working.  Same for a Chromecast and sub-$80 androidTV or Kodi hardware machines.

When I purchased video capture hardware, it always came with a cheesy Windows video editor.  Think the last Haupauge 1515 capture machine included some SONY program.  OpenShot isn't bad, but it is overkill for many people.   Hasn't Windows Movie Maker been included with the OS for a long time?

My point is that there are lots and lots of F/LOSS programs available for other platforms if the users know to seek them out.  F/LOSS doesn't have any advertising budget and won't be on the aisles in stores.  Many people get their computer knowledge from the local computer store, which isn't going to point anyone towards F/LOSS solutions for reasons that should be obvious.

To figure out if any Linux will be good for any specific user, take notes for a few weeks of every program used.  Then look up alternatives to each of those programs, being certain to check that the specific features needed are covered.  After about a year of Linux use, it would be time to rethink how things are accomplished.  Often, the Unix way can be accomplished much more efficiently than the Windows/OSX point-n-click techniques.  

For example, I've automated getting news in the morning.  It is waiting for me when I look for it and can be downloaded to a tablet for off-line reading with just a down-swipe.  It is my personal daily newspaper. Longer articles are pulled down too, so sitting in a waiting room is still productive reading time if my tablet is there.  That's just 1 example.
