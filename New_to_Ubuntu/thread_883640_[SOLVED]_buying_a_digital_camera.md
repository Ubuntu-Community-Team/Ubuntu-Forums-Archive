### Post by Frenske on 2008-08-08
I am not sure what you think the problems going to be. If you want to use the software that comes with the camera itself, I don't think any software will work on Linux (correct me if I wrong).
Are you going to use photo organizing software e.g. f-spot to transfer your photos, there might be problems. Maybe you should check their websites.
If you are going to use card readers there should not be problems at all. Actually the cardreader I use is causing problems in XP but works fine in Ubuntu.

I am serious hobbyist and I take a lot of pictures. Beside the DSLR I have a Canon Ixus 50 which has been superseded by many others models by now. It has a very good lay out, looks gorgeous and it takes nice pictures. 
I would recommend to spend a bit more on a camera which has lens that starts at 28mm or wider (equivalent to a film camera). This is important when photographing buildings and landscapes. I also recommended to get a camera with stabilization to reduce the blur caused by shake.

Don't fall far the Megapixel advertisement. A camera with 6MP is enough for most use and cameras of 10MP and higher often exhibit strong noise or artifacts due to the noise suppression.

Check [www.dpreview.com](www.dpreview.com) for independent reviews.

### Post by unutbu on 2008-08-08
Just to add a wrinkle to things: 

If you hook up a digital camera to your Linux box, and type:
```

gphoto2 --capture-image        # requires gphoto2 package
```

It may or may not be able to snap a picture remotely. (On mine, the camera snaps a picture but the gphoto2 process hangs...) Anyway, I thought this was neat because you could do time-lapse photography by writing a simple script to snap a picture periodically. (For example, to watch a flower open)

I unfortunately don't know which cameras support this feature fully.

### Post by WRDN on 2008-08-08
When purchasing a camera, the best manufacturers, in my opinion, are Canon and Nikon. If you're looking for a higher- end camera, then the Canon 450D (12mp) and the Nikon D80 may be good choices.
If you're looking for a good all-round DSLR though, with high quality, and a lower price tag, the Canon 400D (10mp, over 18 months old, and still great) and Nikon D40 (6mp) are great choices.

### Post by radi0j0hn on 2008-08-08
I am the author of "How To Use The Digital Camera You Just Bought" and also teach buying/using cameras at a local tech school in the evenings.

Your first question should be: "What kind of photos am I planning on taking?"

If it is general snapshots, then most point & shoot cameras will be fine. I lean towards ones made by camera companies, as they have good experience with users. But others such as Sanyo have worked well.

If you are trying to shoot sports, work in low-light or shoot nature shots, then you need to consider something better, perhaps an SLR. That is because you will need a faster response time and larger aperture.


Try to find one that has the most used features available from a button or dial, not from an on-screen menu.  For example, the +/- exposure control should be very easy to use.

Ignore "digital zoom."  It is just in-camera cropping.  Optical zoom is the only one to consider.

Try to find a camera with an optical viewfinder as well as screen.  Outdoors, most screens can't be seen in bright light.

If the camera uses a proprietary battery (as opposed to AA cells), plan on buying a second battery.

And speaking of batteries, make SURE the battery door has a decent hinge!
I cannot tell you how many cheap cameras just have two plastic pegs as part of the cover.  They break quickly and then you have to tape the door shut!

Ignore "digital anti-shake."  That just raises the ISO to allow a higher shutter speed.  Any camera can do that via the settings.  Optical anti-shake is another thing entirely.

Right now, 7 MP cameras are very cheap. There is not that much differnce between 7 and 10 MP, except price.

If you buy a card reader (very cheap), you can plug any card into it an Ubuntu will read it as simple a new drive.

Note that the newer high capacity SD cards (4 GB+) only work with newer cameras that are marked for HC SD.

Most low end cameras come with low end editing software for Windows.

GIMP for Linux is good and several of the free Windows programs listed on my site play well with WINE.  Photofiltre (the free version) is one of them.

More info on my tutorials and podcasts are at acpress dot com.

Good luck!

John
