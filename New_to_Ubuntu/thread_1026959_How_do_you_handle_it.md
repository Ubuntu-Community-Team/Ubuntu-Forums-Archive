### Post by chrisod on 2008-12-31
Linux not supporting proprietary hardware devices is not the fault of the Linux community. The faulty party is the cell phone manufacturer, EVDO card provider or whoever that does not provide Linux drivers for their hardware. People in the Linux community donate their time and talent to try and make proprietary hardware work. However, volunteers can only do so much. The fact is many drivers for Linux are far more stable than their Windows counterparts that are developed and supported by dozens of paid developers. Yes, there are some some paid developers at Red Hat, Canonical etc but it pales in size compared to the paid Windows developer community.

I've been using Ubuntu for 2+ years on a Dell 600m and in that two years I've spent far, far more time fixing problems with my kids or wife's XP boxes than I ever have with my Ubuntu laptop. The laptop just works.

### Post by InfectedWithDrew on 2009-01-12
I think I'll say the old canned phrase that it's not really Linux, it's driver support.  It's not Canonical's fault that you can't get an easy install - in fact, they do their best to make sure you can.  It's the fault of the companies that built your hardware.

Quite simply, a Linux environment is better for those who want to do a lot with their computers.  By virtue of its contributors, it's superior to commercial OSes.  That doesn't mean it's easier to understand, especially under a paradigm that expects things to just work because someone has done all the work for you and you only need to open a package.  It just means it's better.

I sympathize with your woes, however.  I have had a lot of grief with broadcom drivers, especially.  That's just how life is right now.  What's important is we have pioneers who are willing to push the market to give Linux more of a voice, so companies will start coding for this platform.
