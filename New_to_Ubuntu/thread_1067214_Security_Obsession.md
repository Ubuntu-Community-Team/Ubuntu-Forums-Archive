### Post by Vaedrah on 2009-02-11
I guess this post is somewhat idle - I have been trialling other OS's (Virtual Box) and it strikes me (at least) that Ubuntu's great strength is to be open to everyone. Fedora and Debian are not in this mindset at all - this "sudo" and "sudoers group" mentality is counter-productive to anyone that wants to try these alternate OS's.

There is a place for protectionism - perhaps for shared computers in a company or University environment perhaps. But does this obsessive protectionism make sense for an owner and user of a PC or computer.

I have heard/read arguments that an owner needs "protection from her/him self" but this seems very condescending to me - as an attitude it "sucks". You are far more likely to have a notebook, for example, stolen at a mobile setting than having someone "hack" into it. 

Also, if you use windows (I don't mind windows) then every now and then it may be a good idea to fresh start your PC - some updates (SP3) can cripple older s/w (e.g. Frontage Web editor) and no amount of back-tracking will solve crash errors - reinstall OS is the only solution in this case!

So I "like" Ubuntu a great deal and to be "open" and "friendly" I hope it never becomes "protectionist" to the obnoxious extent of Fedora, Debian, etc. The truth is in the pudding I guess, These forums / Ubuntu are great and really tie in a fantastic community. Alternative OS groups don't seem to share such humanities :)

### Post by Xiong Chiamiov on 2009-02-15
I don't understand why you think Fedora is more restrictive than Ubuntu.

You can enable the root account and log in as that, but you *really* shouldn't do that.  Running as a superuser is just asking for trouble, which is why we get so angry at Windows making that the default.

It's less about protecting you from yourself (although there certainly is that) than allowing arbitrary code to do whatever it wants.
