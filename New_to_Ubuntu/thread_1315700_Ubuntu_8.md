### Post by mikechant on 2009-11-06
And I always feel compelled to add the following:
If having your Ubuntu machine working pretty much at all times is important to you I would *always* recommend a 'side-by-side' dual boot install for new release unless you are *really* short of disc space.

That way you are just a reboot away from your fully working existing system, if you need to get some important work done or are just fed up with the problems of the new release. You can update/test the new release; if there are serious problems you can carry on using your current release for normal working, meanwhile periodically applying updates to the new release or tinkering with it until it works. 

When you are fully happy with the new release you can switch your /home over (or - my preference - make and use a new partition with a copy of my current /home) and still have the old release as a backup.

And if you just can't get the new release to work at all or get fed up with trying, you can carry on using the old one until the next release comes out. I've done this twice - installed and tested 8.04 and 8.10 but never actually switched because of sound problems. Finally switched to 9.04 but kept my 7.10 install until last week. Now I have 9.04 running as my normal OS and 9.10 on test.

Although it's a bit of extra effort - dual boot setup, systematically testing all your important software/hardware etc. I can't recommend this method highly enough for its robustness. I come from an IBM mainframe environment and this is the sort of thing (very roughly!) we do in an environment where having a working system is essential and so quick reversion is always required.
