### Post by Dedoimedo on 2008-10-26
Hello,

1. Resolution, for one, the compression used, the audio track. For example, the movie can be an avi, but sound can be raw uncompressed or mp3, resulting in hundreds of megs of difference in size.

2. Errors can be caused by many things. In most cases, the source is troublesome, i.e. not readable. With CDs, this is usually irrecoverable damage.

3. Cookies are just text files. AVs like to hype the threats to boost sales. Safely ignore and enjoy.

### Post by CatKiller on 2008-10-26
> **John Macnab said:**
> 3. Can viruses be implanted into cookies from sites? Why does the anti-virus (AVG) say that 'tracking cookies' have been found? I mean, are they something I should keep a sharp look out for?

No. Tracking cookies aren't a virus threat, but they can have [privacy implications]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracking_cookies#Privacy_and_third-party_cookies"). If you're worried about advertisers tracking which sites you visit, use your browser settings to block third-party cookies and you'll be fine.

### Post by hyper_ch on 2008-10-27
you generally don't need any antivirus product on a linux installation.

normal uses for av on linux are:
- if you run a mail server to scan attachments
- if you run a file server to check files
- if you share files between your linux and windows -- also it should be Window's job to protect itself against malware and not linux's job to protect windows...
- if you don't want to forward virus-infected documents to other people -- although I think again each one should be responsible on their own to prevent malware getting on the system
