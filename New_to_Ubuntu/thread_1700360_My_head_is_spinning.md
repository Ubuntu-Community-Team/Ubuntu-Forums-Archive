### Post by bikodog on 2011-03-05
The difficult part is the _un_-learning of the way things are done in the proprietary OS world.

The proprietary companies have done a good job establishing a bias in most people's minds that their way is the only way to interact with a computer, phone, tablet, etc.

The user buys a machine with a basic layer of software and over time adds to the stack by installing one proprietary executable after another (usually for a price). "...double click, click some more, reboot..." if there are conflicts, etc; call tech-support. When layer after layer of hidden proprietary or so-called "malicious" code begin to conflict, the system bogs down and the user becomes frustrated. The likely outcome is that the user buys a new machine, and of course, a new OS license every 2-3 years. The prop software firms "upgrade" their offerings on the same timetable, forcing users to think they need to upgrade their applications as well, and the cycle starts anew. The revenue stream is secure.

Yes, there may be a steeper learning curve with open-source, but once that "bump" in the road is overcome the acquired knowledge stays. Eventually you will wonder why you ever put yourself in the proprietary jail cell to start with.

Advice is to just "jump in" and start using. Ubuntu has come a long way to make the change over as easy as possible. When you come across a problem, search or post in the forums, google, etc and you will very likely find a solution.

My mother switched from years of using windoze to Kubuntu with virtually *NO* help. No tutoring, no explaining, nothing.
