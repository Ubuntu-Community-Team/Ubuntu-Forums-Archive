### Post by 1clue on 2011-03-08
I haven't had to buy since I got my Laser, but in the past I've found that I have best luck buying "middle of the road" printers for Linux.  Don't ever buy the cheapest one in the category.  Probably don't want to buy the expensive one either, unless it's a mainstream business printer.

If it's a mainstream business printer, you want a bigger one that handles standard printing protocols.  Then you have all sorts of options for drivers.

Since I got the laser, I stopped paying attention to the ink jet market.  I don't know exactly what is out there, so you'll need to ask those other guys for that.

Be really careful on brands.  If everyone says stay away from something then stay away.  My Canon works but it was a PITA on Linux.  But my laser working in no way means that if you buy an inkjet it will work.  Not the same driver.

When you read somebody's recommendation, find out if that printer is the type you want, and if not then ignore it.

That said, my arguments looked a lot like your arguments.  Ink jet printer ink is incredibly expensive.  It was more so because I go for months without printing anything, and my cartridges would dry out.  Sometimes I print a lot, but usually it's like 3 sheets and then I stop again.  $80 for 3 sheets is outrageous.  I went through 3 printers in a year because it was literally cheaper to buy a printer than it was to buy just the ink.

So I went with the laser.  Laser printing is a fraction of the cost per page for toner, but the printer itself is quite a bit more expensive.  That's been over a year now and still the same printer.  I've gone through quite a bit of paper now relatively speaking, and I don't regret getting the laser at all.  At last year's rate, I'm 60% done paying for my printer.

Good luck.

### Post by 1clue on 2011-04-06
The link I showed was made by a guy with a Canon with a lower number than yours, presumably that makes it older yet.

Mine is network connected as well, using dhcp from my wireless router.  The printer isn't wireless, but it hooks into one of the ethernet ports.  Nothing special on the printer side, just plug it in and it works.  Special drivers are needed in any OS I have tried to use, including Windows 7.

I don't buy into the logic that a home printer should be hooked to a single host and only available when that host is running.  It takes a minute or two for your list to populate when you set up your printer, so have patience.

Believe it or not my printer was an austerity measure.  I print a few pages, then I turn the thing off for months at a time, and my inkjet printers were drying out.  So I would shop for cartridges, and I'm faced with the choice of paying (for example) $80 for cartridges or $100 for a printer.  Or more recently, I actually would have had to pay MORE for the cartridges than the same brand printer, better model.

Considering that one of these iterations got me maybe 5 pages of printed output, I had enough of that.  $20 a page is just too much, and it's more considering the driving around and reading specs online.

So the laser works really well for me.  All-in-one, although I don't use fax, and the cartridge doesn't dry out.  I don't print photographs with it so I don't care that it's only 600dpi.
