### Post by catrin on 2017-01-17
I am a Ubuntu, indeed a Linux newbie due to a monster sized Sony all-in-one desktop. I bought it in 2009 with the best resources that I could buy at the time - so good that in 2017 it still does quite well though I wouldn't attempt to turn it into a gaming machine. The problem is a common problem with all-in-ones. The hardware was customized so much to fit behind the monitor that it became proprietary and no driver updates were ever released which made it eventually impossible to upgrade Windows. Eventually it became a 50+ pound brick after a failed upgrade and I reformatted/wrote over the hard drive to eventually recycle the computer and bought a much smaller hybrid computer for daily use. I do like the little thing, but it's too small to comfortably use to remote into my work computers at the office.

A friend encouraged me to give Ubuntu a try to see if the necessary drivers were available, and much to my pleasant surprise the live CD worked and the old brick is up and running, and even faster than it was with Windows 7. All for $8  :) For some reason my wired mouse doesn't work well, but a new mouse is far less expensive than a new computer!

Suggestions for a good reference book? While electronic references are great, I do like having both. I need to figure out how to install Citrix Receiver for work. I've found the Ubuntu Community page that describes the process. I've a learning curve for sure. I appreciate any references for a good Ubuntu/Linux book for a novice, there are a good number of choices and it's hard to know how to choose.

### Post by TheFu on 2017-01-17
Depending on what your learning goal is, would determine the best way to start.
[http://blog.jdpfu.com/2014/12/28/learning-linux](http://blog.jdpfu.com/2014/12/28/learning-linux) is what I tell people who want to either be a programmer or system admin.  

For end-user types, start with #1, 2, and 4 in my list, then moving to "the ubuntu desktop guide" is probably best. 
[http://ubuntuguide.org/wiki/Ubuntu_Trusty](http://ubuntuguide.org/wiki/Ubuntu_Trusty)

Most paper books are out of date before they get to print. Do not discount the built-in help system.  Run **xman** to see it.

### Post by TheFu on 2017-01-17
If you care about security, DO NOT USE bluetooth.

Also, IMHO, using the GUI only gets access to about 10% of the power in a Unix system.  The other 90% comes from the shell, automation, networking and remote access.

Citrix is proprietary, so I would expect challenges in getting it working. I looked up the Citrix Receiver link - you'll want to become a power-user to understand much of that. There is a free PDF book in my link - #5 (I think) - which will get you the learning and background to get to that point in about a week.  There are also EdX and similar training classes and training videos on amazon prime which can help with a few hours of effort.
