### Post by aaaantoine on 2011-01-28
The best way to learn is to practice doing what you want to do.  As soon as you hit a road block, look around for a solution.

If you want to learn more about what does what, try putting together a more complex installation.  Ubuntu does everything for you, and has a great set of default packages and settings.  By contrast, a distribution like [Arch](http://www.archlinux.org/) comes with basically just the kernel and a terminal shell by default, and it's up to you to decide what you want it to do from there.  If you want to try this approach but with Ubuntu packages instead, there also exists an Ubuntu [Minimal CD](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Installation/MinimalCD) which is more or less the same thing, barring other fundamental differences between the two distributions.

If you're curious about how you want to customize your OS (as far UI is concerned), have a look at the Screenshots threads in the Community Cafe (alas, the Cafe is closed to new posts while the forum admins try to deal with performance issues, but you can still look in there and post questions about a particular set up in a support thread).

### Post by charlie_b on 2011-01-28
One of the most important things to realise when switching to Linux and open source software is the need for problem solving skills. Using Windows and proprietary software encourages dependence; using open source encourages independence. Switching to Linux you will find yourself confronted with a succession of problems, many of which will initially seem insurmountable. It is at this point that  a lot of people give up. 
Be assured that there are solutions to nearly all the problems, there is lots of support from this and other Linux forums. 
Using Linux fosters indepent free thinking and develops high level problem solving skills and creativity.
Good luck with making the switch!

### Post by lisati on 2011-01-28
Welcome to the forum.

Some of the things you already know will be useful. For example, "point and click" is pretty much the same, regardless of the OS.
Other things you will need to learn as you go. For example, programs written specifically for another OS won't work without help, but there are often equivalents and other options.

If you get stuck, we have a large community of volunteers here who will be able to offer suggestions to help you with your learning experience and any troubleshooting you might need to do.

### Post by wormyblackburny on 2011-01-28
One of my favorite books is [Ubuntu Kung-fu]("http://www.pragprog.com/titles/ktuk/ubuntu-kung-fu"). It is a series of tips and tweaks that you can use to "customize" your desktop, add useful programs, and generally familiarize yourself with the operating system. All of the tutorials are really short and separated so you could think of them as "labs" to do. Look through it, find the topics/tools that interest you, and do the tutorials. It's an excellent source of opportunities for hands on learning.... Other than that, Google is your best friend :D
