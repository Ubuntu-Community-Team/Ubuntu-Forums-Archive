### Post by HittingSmoke on 2008-10-20
Well, I wouldn't consider myself an avid Linux user quite yet, but I'm in about my 3rd month using Ubuntu as my exclusive OS after switching over from being a lifetime Windows user so I can tell ya exactly what happened with me.

If you're the kind of Windows user I was, you're going to screw some things up. I fell in love with Kubuntu after just a few days of installing and followed random guides and tutorials around the net to customize it just the way I wanted it. After so long I had some serious problems from following guides that didnt work quite the way they were supposed to and decided to try out Ubuntu instead after reading about how Kubuntu isnt the best implementation of KDE (just the desktop environment).

After doing my homework I realized after doing a decent job of keeping my first ever Linux install running after heavy experimenting and customizing I should just do a clean install. I tried out Ubuntu and haven't looked back, and haven't had a single problem.

So to summarize a post that probably could have been much shorter, I would just play around with an initial installation. Get comfortable with the basics of reversing certain changes, editing the more common of the settings files (xorg, sources, fstab. You'll find out what these are soon enough) and after you're confident you can at least get your common tasks up and running to your expectations, go ahead and do a fresh install and get things exactly the way you want them without any experimenting or guess work. After you are secure in your ability to do that, you can usually fix any problem one step at a time without any serious changes. The basics are really the biggest leap in the switch.

As for Adobe programs. From what I've read they do work with WINE (the program that comes with Ubuntu allowing some Windows apps to run) but they don't work particularly well.

Most Windows games I've tried with WINE work fine with a few small exceptions. Some games may run with some glitchy graphics here and there, but I havent had any real problems.

So yea, this isn't coming from a Linux guru, or even a proficient Linux user. I'm just a guy like you who decided to make the switch a few months ago and I haven't looked back.

This is all assuming you don't want to dual boot. I tried it but I found myself too impatient to switch my OS when I needed a Windows program, so I went full Ubuntu to force myself to find alternatives. If you dont mind that, you can load Ubuntu and Windows onto the same PC and choose one at boot.
