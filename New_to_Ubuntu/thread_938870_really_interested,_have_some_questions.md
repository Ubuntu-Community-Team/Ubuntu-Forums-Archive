### Post by billgoldberg on 2008-10-05
**firstly, does ubuntu support adobe photoshop? **

No. Adobe does not support Linux for most of it's applications.

However you can get Adobe Photoshop CS2 and earlier working using Wine.

Ubuntu comes with the Gimp (gnu image manipulator).

**how about additional hardware such as my wacom tablet? does it have drivers for things like this and my webcam?**

Don't know about the Wacom tablet (google is your friends).

Ubuntu (linux) has support for loads of webcams. Google for "ubuntu + your webcam model".

**another thought that occured to me is games and their compatibility. can i run steam on ubuntu?**

Again Steam is windows only software.

You can get it working using Wine on some machines. 

If you are a gamer, trust me stick to windows or dual boot.

### Post by Kellemora on 2008-10-05
Hi SNA

There is another option that I rarely see mentioned here, and was the PERFECT solution for my needs and tight quarters.

The problem I encountered was, it takes TIME to dual boot a system, and you only have access to the current system you have running.

You can pick up a second computer, box only, no keyboard, mouse or monitor for fairly cheap.  If it comes with XP you can still set it up as a dual boot and to learn on, without messing up your working system you use everyday.  

There is a device called a KVM Switch, they can be had for as little as 29 to 39 dollars.  You just plug your existing keyboard mouse and monitor into the back of the KVM Switch, then connect some cables between your two computers.  That way you can be on XP or Ubuntu at the push of a button.
Also means you can have a program RUNNING on either machine while you are looking at the other machine.  

Not only is it a time saver, but you don't find yourself sitting around twiddling your thumbs while one of the machines is busy.

Another feature of Ubuntu and most Linux Distro's is that you can have 2, 4 or more windows running separate programs, to whatever capacity your computer can handle.

I don't know if this is true for all KVM switches, the model I have is a Cables To Go, Tru-link 4 port with separately switchable USB ports.
But I have a program running on the XP box that requires a mouse click every 7 seconds, because of this, the monitor would never sleep.
Using an auto-clicker to press the mouse key, I can use the KVM and switch away from that computer, and the mouse clicker keeps doing it's thing, even though no mouse is really connected to the machine anymore.
And the screen saver on the Ubuntu machine will turn off the monitor if I'm away from the desk.

Also, you can share the files between both computers quite easily too.

And, something you can do on Linux that I've never figured out how to do on a Doze machine, is you can run programs regardless of what machine they reside on, because in Linux, a hard drive on another machine appears to your machine as being on your own machine.

I just wanted to let you know about these normally unmentioned features because, as a total noob, I was just about ready to give up, since most of what I did required the Doze, but I like the speed and functionality of Linux that you can't get on Doze.

I've only been using Linux now for a tad over a month and already 90% of my business affairs are running on Ubuntu, with far less problems.

But like everything NEW, there is a learning curve you will have to experience.  Even between different Doze versions there was a learning curve to master.  Ubuntu became my choice over CentOS or Debian simply because of the WONDERFUL support and hard working teams that made Ubuntu climb to the top of the charts in record breaking time.

Ubuntu IS the Winning Team!!!!!  Hands Down!
