### Post by mapes12 on 2012-06-03
For me, I adapt a policy of thinking "if someone hacks this account what damage could they do?". The answer to this boils down to 2 types of sites. The first is financial i.e. banking or anything where my cash could be compromised. The second is general log on accounts like this forum where no financial info is available.

I then set up 2 passwords. One for the financial sites and another for the none financial sites. Use a strong password generator then you only have 2 passwords to remember. 

To automate the logons I use the FF addon Lastpass. Been using it for ages without any issues. You will find that many financial sites like banks circumvent lastpass but you with just the 2 passwords you will know the password to manually enter.

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2012-06-03
I have a system for creating passwords that are easy for me to remember. I have different passwords for everything and I don't need to rely on an application to remember them for me. I am always afraid that I will loose the applications data due to disk crash or a faulty backup.

I have a couple of base passwords; then, I add special characters, numbers, and site or application identifiers.

This is an example of the password schema:

XSnSa

where 
[LIST]
[*]'X' is the base password. I have two for my job, one for financial sites, one for personal sites, one for forums, etc. I have one that is 4 times as long for encryption pass-phrases. 
[*]'S' is special characters, such as, /_$-.&#%@. I use the same special characters for the same base password.
[*]'n' may be a number; such as, the month and year the password changed if I change them every 3 months, or just some number if I don't change the password regularly. I can also use the three character month nomenclature plus the year. I have rules that determine when I use numbers or the characters based on the level of my paranoia. 
[*]'a' is an application or site identifier; such as, "ub" or "ubf" for ubuntuforums.org. 
[/LIST][INDENT][/INDENT]

For this site my password could be something like these:

 teJUFv%0412$ub
 teJUFv_%0412$#UB
 teJUFv_Apr12@ubf

### Post by BLOODBANKER on 2012-06-03
Yes KeePass is good, but if you decide to use whatever backup scheme for your passwords, be careful.  Moving the file where the data is kept can change the encryption and render it impossible to open.  I've done this accidentally.  Always record your passwords by an alternate method - and secure them in a safe place.
