### Post by scratman on 2013-02-24
It depends on what exactly you intend to use the machine for.  The benefit of an SSD is that it can locate, cache and deliver a file more rapidly than a standard magnetic hard drive.

If you intend to use 100GB of space for media, ie music, video or photo, then frankly there will be no benefit other than kudos in having a larger SSD.  You will not notice any speed difference, with the exception of potentially saving up to a second in load times for the initial file.  If you have a playlist the software will actually cache the next file while one is already playing, so you will not notice any difference with an SSD.

As long as the Operating System itself is on the SSD, and specifically an SSD with lower seek times and higher transfer rates than a 10000rpm SATA hard drive, then the system will be quick, and storing large quantities of Data on a separate SATA drive will provide you more storage for your money, with no discernable reduction in speed.

### Post by audiomick on 2013-02-25
> **incurablegeek said:**
> ...Into the Ubuntu computer I will transfer from this work computer my slightly older SAMSUNG 830 128GB SATA III MLC and a Gen 2 (much older Intel 80 GB X25M SATA II SSD, which I can use for overflow programs should the need arise...

When you install programs onto a Linux install, you don't get to choose where they are installed to. The system puts all the bits and pieces in the various directories where it needs to have them. Your install will "grow" with the number of programs you install.

Having said that, my feeling is that Linux programs tend to take up a lot less space than Windows programs. For instance, on this install, I have a little less than 5GB in my / partition. The install is a few years old, and I do tend to install things just to have a look at them, and forget to remove them. I don't have any games, though.

I did have to enlarge the / partition on a friend's computer recently. She had got up to around 8GB in the / partition.

In your case, I would do the following: set up about a 20 GB partition on the 80GB drive, and a swap partition at the end of it. If you want hibernate to work, you need to make the swap a little bigger than your RAM, otherewise 2GB, or even only 1GB should be enough. A computer with a modern amount of RAM generally doesn't get into the swap space much at all in normal circumstances.

I would then set up a /home partition on the 128GB drive. I would consider dividing the drive into 2 partitions, one as /home and one as a data partition.

The leftover space on the 80GB drive could be left empty against future experiments with other Linux versions (multiple installs...) or set up as a spare partition. I have an 80GB SSD for my system (and a couple of HDDs with 2 different /home partitions on the, and a few data partitions.)
The SSD has one install on it at this stage, some swap, and a partition that I have mounted at /home/basement. The intended usage is obvious from the name, I think, but I haven't actually got much in there. A couple of .tar files, I think, from various backup attempts.
