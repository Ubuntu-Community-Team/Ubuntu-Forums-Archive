### Post by QIII on 2012-03-22
I have used testdisk/photorec on many a mistakenly borked disk -- once overwritten but no more.

If you have a lot of music for you iPod, however, you are out of luck.  You can recover the files, but the iPod software has a proprietary and inscrutable database that keeps track of arbitrarily named files scattered around as only the database knows.

DO NOT try to reinstall Windows for the time being.  Go to a DIFFERENT machine, download testdisk and burn the ISO.  The more you mess with the overwritten disk, the less likely you are to be able to retrieve anything.  As jerrrys said, if you have overwritten it twice, you are very unlikely to retrieve anything of value.

Have an external drive attached to your machine to copy your files to and start pulling things off of the overwritten disk and putting them in a safe place.  The process is not quick.  Be prepared for what might take several days of detailed work.

You will not get 100% satisfaction even if you can salvage anything, unfortunately.  Some of your files are surely completely lost.

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2012-03-23
If you can remove your drive and can connect it to an MS Windows machine, you can try scanning it using MS Windows utilities to see if they find anything.  If you're interested, read  on ... 

In my experience, Windows filesystem utilities have been best at recovering data from former Windows partitions; Linux filesystem utilities have been best at recovering data from former Linux partitions.

Since your data was on a Windows partition, based on my experience at doing this successfully, my suggestions are the following:
[NOTE: If your PC has a working copy of MS Windows on it, skip to step 4]
1) Find someone with a working MS Windows PC
2) Remove your drive from this PC.
3) Connect your old drive to the MS Windows PC.
4) Download and install the trial version of GetDataBack for NTFS from Runtime Software in MS Windows.
5) Run GetDataBack and select the option to find Deleted files.
6) When done, browse the filesystem in the app to see if your directories and files were found.  If so, view some to confirm the contents are intact.
7) If they are, you will have to purchase GetDataBack to do the actual recovery. You won't have to reinstall it; instead, they will email you an activation code which you can use to turn on the recovery feature.

And, last time I checked, GetDataBack for NTFS was $80, USD.

There's also a FREE MS Windows file system recovery app known as Recuva.  You could try that first -- but my experience in MS Windows is you get what you pay for.

Your data ... your money ... your choice.
