### Post by davidmohammed on 2010-08-06
what do you mean by "will it still work"?

Do you mean, do I get 3D effects etc.  That depends upon the graphics card.  If its an old card then you have to use the standard drivers that ubuntu supply - i.e. you will not have to install anything.

If its nvidia or ati, checkout "administration - Hardware drivers" and see if any hardware drivers are offered to you.  If they are, just click the activate option.

If its a new ATI graphics card, you'll need to install the latest ati catalyst driver.  Plenty of threads around describing how to do this.

### Post by furialis on 2010-08-06
Changing your IP depends on a few things. Sometimes they're static and you can't change them.

In my case, my computer is hooked up to my router which is hooked up to my cable modem. I have installed custom firmware on my router that allows me to change the MAC address of the router. All I do is change the MAC of the router and reboot the cable modem and I get a new IP.

You can plug your computer directly into the cable modem and run a program called macchanger.
```

sudo apt-get install macchanger

```

Then run
```

sudo macchanger -A eth0

```

Reboot your modem and you just may have a new IP.

### Post by cloyd on 2010-08-07
Ubuntu is good for not having to buy windows. Ubuntu is good for ditching and not having to pay people like Norton. Ubuntu is good for word processing, spreadsheets, presentations, email, ripping cd's playing mp3's, video, even ripping dvd's. Open office is perhaps  75% compatible with Microcsoft Office. Some things just don't come across the same, but you can exchange documents. Highly formatted Word Processing documents don't always come across right. But then, it is free. There is a world of free, trusted software. There is a community to help you. And I guess, if you really needed professional support, you could buy it from a third party like Canonical. After all, you don't have to pay Norton or McAffee. 

Ir is not good for gaming. Just no compatible games. Though you can use Wine. No commercial software like Microsoft Money (I think there are a few open source one around, but they will be different from Money) No CAD programs as far as I know, but I don't use those. 

I personally don't need Windows anymore. You can dual boot to make the switch. But you may find that you never use Windows if you have both of them on your machine. 

Couple of added bonuses. No Microsoft looking over your shoulder to make sure you are not bootlegging software: no "Trusted Computing". No Microsoft changing your browser without asking. No microsoft starting updates without asking you.
 
And not the least. Fast booting. Faster shutdowns. Fewer crashes. 

And the last unintended bonus. You'll learn more about your computer. It's fun.

Am I over-zealous?
