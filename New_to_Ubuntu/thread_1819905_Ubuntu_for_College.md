A goodplace to start reading would be [here]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Beginners/FAQ"). The Ubuntu Wiki in general has great information. The best way to learn is jump on in, and if you have a question Google it. There are thousands upon thousands of articles out there on Linux.

I have found google (or other search engines) to be an invaluable tool for beginning linux users. 

For instance, when I was thinking of buying a Lenovo X61 tablet computer a few years ago and installing Ubuntu on it I googled "X61 tablet ubuntu" and got a lot of results about how to get Ubuntu running on that computer. ### Post by hailtothethief on 2011-08-07
The reasons you mentioned are some of the very same reasons I first switched to Ubuntu.

First and foremost, using Ubuntu isn't an all or nothing deal. It can be installed along side a current Windows installation, letting you gradually wade into the warm waters of linux.

Some common pitfalls to watch out for are wireless compatibility, video driver issues (especially regarding Flash and video playback in your browser), and sound issues (which are not a problem for most users).

As far as resources for learning linux, I recommend the following:

[LIST=1]
[*][Ubuntu Guide]("http://ubuntuguide.org/")
[*]Google
[*]Ubuntu Wiki
[*]Ubuntu Forums
[/LIST]

The Ubuntu Guide gives a great overview from the very first steps onward.

At it's current state, using Ubuntu is generally a pain-free process. But never be afraid to ask for help (after searching Google, the wiki, and the forums of course).

The single most important thing to keep in mind when first switching to Linux is the generic problem solving process. Applying skills you have and leveraging your available resources to gain new ones will ensure that your transition to Linux is smooth and gratifying. Good luck with school and Linux!

### Post by I2k4 on 2011-08-07
I'm very much enjoying my Lubuntu thumbdrive tryout experience, but for a serious student who will want to work on documents over a period of several years:

1. Depending on your program of study and your school, decide if Linux/Ubuntu supports all the software you will need for your coursework;

2. It's silly to discount Windows as "unreliable" - XP is a mature OS that will run well on lightweight systems, Windows 7 has greater hardware requirements than Ubuntu for satisfying performance, but "reliability" as between Windows and any Linux OS is not an issue.

3. I'm no Mac fan for business purposes, because Mac tends to leave long term users in the lurch with useless document files whenever Steve Jobs gets a "better idea" (something Windows has not done) but it is a solid OS likely fine for school.

You can do just about anything with Linux/Ubuntu for yourself, but you are going into a collaborative environment in school that may well involve document sharing.  Just make sure you are not setting yourself up for needless compatibility problems with teachers and students using other software.
