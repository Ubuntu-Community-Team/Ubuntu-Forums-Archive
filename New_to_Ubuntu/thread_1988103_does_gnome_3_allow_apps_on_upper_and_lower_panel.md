### Post by 3rdalbum on 2012-05-27
> **Phil Smith said:**
> Hi Community:

The Gnome 3 desktop and Unity both have a top panel. These have limited customisation options.

Both Gnome 3's desktop and Unity have a space on the side of the screen that handles all your quick application launching duties. This is now the officially-mandated way of placing application launchers close at hand on your screen. Unity's is especially neat as it can store more items than what fit on the screen - surplus items "stack" when not in use, and "unstack" themselves when you move the mouse over them. Try doing that with Gnome 2!

I'd definitely recommend giving Unity a try for a week. It's very different to Gnome 2, but I find it a very "helpful" desktop. It uses very little screen space until I call on it, and then it's packed with nice little features when part of it appears. If you're going to try Unity, try it for a period of time; at first when I saw overlay scrollbars I didn't like them, but now I've been using them for a while I understand why they work like that, and find them useful.

If, at the end of your week with Unity you still don't like it, and Gnome Shell doesn't float your boat, there's no need to change distribution. Install Gnome Session Fallback and select "Gnome Classic" at the login screen. You'll get something that works very similar to Gnome 2. You can even customise its panels if you Alt-RightClick.
