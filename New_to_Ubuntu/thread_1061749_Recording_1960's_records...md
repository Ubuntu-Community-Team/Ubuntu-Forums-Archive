### Post by Kellemora on 2009-02-07
HI Donald

My cousin used to work for a company that remastered songs to digital format.  Most often they were working from original masters which greatly reduced the work involved to get the job done.  But a lot of great old songs, no master still existed and they had to work from the best LP's or 45's they could find.  Or in some cases Carnuba Wax (inside joke for shellac records).

They had some mighty expensive equipment for sure!
Nonetheless, it could take them nearly a month to produce a perfect ADDRD reproduction.  In other words the result was a Master Quality digital multi-track remastered file.

Although using computers, most of the work was still done by hand, on a computer though.  Sorta like remastering a photo one pixel at a time to restore it.  Using tools not much unlike an eraser to remove extraneous marks on the sine waves that were displayed on their consoles.

I sat an entire morning watching my cousin do nothing more than remove tiny lines from the image on the screen.  He was only doing one function of many that needed accomplished.  Come lunch break he said the boss would be proud, he managed to complete 7 seconds of cleanup in only 1/2 day.  He was very proud of that fact, so it must normally take much longer.  And that was just one of the steps they did.

I know today, a lot of this can be done electronically and not require all that tedious hand work they did back then.  But it would be nice if such a feature WAS available in Audacious for those who DID want to spend that extra time.

### Post by henry cow on 2010-07-08
Come on, you guys! I am a record lover and have ripped several hundred choice pieces of vinyl to digital. I have been at it for several years, you listen as you record in real time, then spend 5-20 minutes cleaning up the result. I record entire album sides, clean up the bad pops (easy to see and kill) and live with everything else. If you listen to album sides you don't even have to break up into songs, if you don't want to, for a lot of jazz or classical.

Get yourself a good card (I have a Creative X-Fi semi-pro level that was about $400, but they have decent ones for $75), a fresh stylus (you can spend any amount but $50 will get you a surprisingly good Audio-Technica and a few hundred will get you a better cartridge than your ears, if you are old enough to remember records anyway), and a decent pre-amp. Don't waste your money on those garbage "USB-Turntable-Combos" good grief! 

Almost any record that was made before CDs were invented could probably be improved by a good sponge bath in warm soapy water at the kitchen sink. This will wreck 5% of your labels but will be well worth it in sound. I would not even consider playing a record from the 60s or before without washing it, and then they get fresh new plastic sleeves.

I use Audacity exclusively and it works like a charm. I have a couple of very minor complaints with parts of the display but it beats the pants off everything else. I record everything at high quality levels and save to both .WAV and .MP3 (takes a lot of disc space but .WAVs are SO MUCH BETTER ! And I have a 1.5T hard disc that barely set me back a C-note.

Audacity is a spectacular program and I have barely scratched its surface. NEVER NEVER use any of those stupid automatic noise-reducing programs, they kill the sound. If you aren't hearing hiss you aren't hearing cymbals.

Of course, sound itself is an analog phenomenon, all musical instruments, including the human voice, are analog devices, sound waves in air are analog, loudspeakers are analog, and the human ear is an analog device. Please remind me, where is the "digital" component of that scenario? That mile-long groove in black plastic is where it's at, no doubt.

But, on the other hand, I can now listen to any of the nearly 30K "songs" I own without even moving my fat butt off my chair at the computer desk, so that is something too.

This is easy. If you have no gear at all, you might have to invest a few hundred dollars, but if you care anything about music, that is precious little to spend.

Gone is the horizontal scroll bar which seemed snappy on a 1.2 GHz Athlon netbook in both screen and smooth scroll.  The new version lags and only operates in a screen scroll in three slow, jerky segments even on a 2+ GHz multi-core athlon.  The Zoom in and out is also slow and lags from the button 'press' even on the faster machines while the old netbook with 10,04 seemed instantaneous.

I have tried compiling the old Audacity source on new systems with no success as well as compiling several Audacity versions and WxWidgets versions also without success.  To anyone now wishing to process old vinyl, I would recommend staying with 10.04 along with its version of Audacity.  If anyone has a fix for this on newer systems, I would be most grateful.
