### Post by marin123 on 2013-01-16
Fact: Ableton won't run faster on Ubuntu than on XP.

As for sound programs, the best two Linux natives are definitely Ardour and LMMS.

Google them. Ardour is used for sound recording and arranging and may be the program you are looking for. LMMS is a DAW program with preloaded VSTs and you can also run other DLL VSTs like NI Massive, Sylenth, Nexus, Z3ta, etc.

I also recommend Audacity, which is a complete audio analysis and tweaking tool with so much plugins and you can do almost anything with it.

If you don't REQUIRE Ableton, check out this programs first. Maybe start with Audacity (it's simple to get started) and then try Ardour.

Good luck and if you have more questions, feel free to ask :)

### Post by alexii on 2013-01-17
Learning Linux = highly encouraged! Serious audio production on Linux = good luck!


To be brief, audio codecs can be buggy and get broken by updates, and hardware support is slim pickings and also buggy. Wine is asking for trouble.


I dont mean to be a downer, just saw a need for this sentiment here. You may spend more time fixing problems than recording music. Dont take my word, please do try - check out Ubuntu studio, mixxx, lmms, audacity (your best hope) and others, and i think you will see what I mean.

### Post by DracoMaille on 2013-01-17
Just to *** my two cents (as a relatively new Linux user) with Ubuntu you also have the ability to install Windows on the side of Linux, so that you can be able to use your preferred program while you find / learn a new set of Linux programs.  at bootup you'll be able to choose which one you want to use.  Or if you don't have the hard drive space, Ubuntu also has a live cd version that you can run without having it take up the space (it does run slower, though). 

I recommend learning the Linux programs in general due to the relative ease of personalizing your sound programs for yourself.  Have you ever found something in Ableton SO annoying that you almost switched programs?  In Linux you could eventually correct those types of issues as everything is open source and editable (and I am ABSOLUTELY sure you could find help doing so through these forums).  So far I have found that the people you meet through Linux connections tend to be very nice, very friendly, and very helpful -- it's a great community :-)
