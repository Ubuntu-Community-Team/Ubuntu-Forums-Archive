### Post by Rex Bouwense on 2012-02-12
You betcha an old dog can learn.  My wife and I are also RVers (caravaners) and we travel full time with an old Dell Inspiron 1000 and an ASUS netbook.  Both are using Ubuntu or Lubuntu.  Linux will scare you initially but once beyond that it is rather simple to use.  Soon you find out that you know what you are doing and not long after that you are giving advice and providing solutions to others on the forums.
One problem that you might have with the Dell is the WIFI card but if you hook it up to the Internet with an ethernet cable when you install, it will be able to search for and install any WIFI driver that you might need.
I am using both Ubuntu and Lubuntu but would recommend Lubuntu.  It is simple, not bloated, and from your description of you use will do the job very nicely.  Try it from a CD or flash drive brfore you install to see if you will have any problems with your hardware.  Enjoy.

I would recommend that you download a "live" CD image, which you can then reboot from to have an initial 'look around in Ubuntu'. You will likely need to change the boot order in the BIOS (F2 while starting up) and set the CD ahead of the hard drive.

To answer more specifically - yes, Ubuntu will have apps to cover your needs day one, out-the-box.  The travel apps may be something more specialized, so I can't answer on those.

ADSL - Ubuntu should detect and connect effortlessly. No need for another modem. In fact it may leave you wondering why Windoze made connecting so complicated.

Of course from a quick tour from a Live CD you don't get the full effect of Ubuntu Linux.  As you have more than one machine, make a reliable backup of your data and test it on your Toshiba.  At that point you may decide to dedicate the Dell fully to Ubuntu.  But please ensure you have your important data like photos and emails all backed up, as a clean install will wipe out everything!

I hope you have success.

### Post by Vaphell on 2012-02-14
also worth mentioning: picking the right one to install is not that crucial, all desktop environments (kde, xfce, gnome, lxde and few others) are available in repositories, you install them just like any other program. At the login screen you can pick the environment you want to use.
 Flavors of ubuntu simply use different defaults but share the common software base (repositories) and you can freely transform any flavor into any flavor with few mouse clicks.

Install any _ubuntu for testing purposes and go on an installing spree. Check the environments, check the apps and when you more or less figure things out and have a crystallized vision, install the system from scratch and mold it to suit your needs.
