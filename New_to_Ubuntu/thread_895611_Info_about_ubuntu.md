Well.  As alien a concept as it might seem, there are people who believe that software is more like maths and knowledge than physical property.  As a consequence, such people believe that software should be Free (as in freedom to use it and learn from it, free speech), although that does not neccesarily imply free (as in gratis or no money) even though the 2 things appear to go together frequently with respect to downstream receivers.  This by the way, does not mean "no payment" at all - you'll find most free/libre software have been paid for, in one way or another, whether monetarily or non-monetarily, e.g. by big companies like IBM paying developers to improve open source/free software they use on their machines, or perhaps by you as a developer being able to freely use other people's code to build upon, for example. You pay for that use by making your own contributions also freely available to everyone else using the software in question.  The botom line is that you and every other third party free software user (and humanity in general) benefit from this outlook.  Aside, companies like IBM spends literally billions of dollars on free/open source software as it makes economic sense for them to build on the work of others as well as anyone else, rather than reinventing the wheel themselves.  

Anyway, I hope that helps clarify whats behind "free" software a little... So in Ubuntu's case, while the code that you receive gratis in Ubuntu has been paid for in various ways, you have the benefit of not having to pay for it monetarily as well.

### Post by xeth_delta on 2008-08-20
There isn't really any catch, or trap for that matter. This is one of the beaties of Open Source software. The End user is treated in equal terms to the developer. A lot of the work put in Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) comes from altruism and love for programming.

As has been mentioned before, the "free" does not have to be interpreted as in "free soda", but in free from freedom.

Most FOSS comes under the GPL license in its different versions, and so long as you respect it, you are free and welcome to use the software, modify it and make it suit your needs. If you want, the least form in which one can pay to the FOSS community is by simply respecting the License.

Maybe some other people who know more about it can shed more light, but one of the main ideas, is that if you redistribute the software or a derivative based on FOSS, you have to offer also the source code, so that other people will benefit from the same opportunities and rights you did. That does not mean you can't make money out of it, if you want, but you ought to respect the GPL. Many companies have shown it is possible, e.g. RedHat, IBM, etc.

[EDIT]Something you must be aware of, is that there will be a learning curve, when switching to Linux. Many things will be done differently, which is in itself not bad, again, just different, some easier, some more difficult. A bit of patience and an open mind are always appropiate for the change, and whenever you get stuck, the community will be glad to help.

Hope it helped, corrections are welcome :)
