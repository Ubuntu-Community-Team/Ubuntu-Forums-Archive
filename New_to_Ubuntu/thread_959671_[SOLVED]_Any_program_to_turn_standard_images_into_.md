### Post by Kellemora on 2008-10-26
Hi CJM

Actually, using PhotoShop you can do everything 3DMax can do!

There is another program made by AutoSketch/AutoDesk where you can set back dark tones and raise lighter tones so that it gives the appearance of being sorta 3D if viewed on a computer screen.

I've actually used stitching programs, like Panorama Maker to assemble a series of photo's and then used Photo Shop to make a presentations that also gives a 3D effect.

In essence, all you are doing is zooming in on a photo slowly, moving from point A to point B on the image as it zooms in, which is fairly easy to do in PhotoShop.

I haven't seen the program itself, but the results of some 3D imagers, but they take supercomputers and at minimum 3 photo's to make a 3D model image that can be turned around a little on your computer, with 6 to 8 photo's you could make a persons face rotate all the way around, but naturally, not as smoothly as if you took a dozen or more photos.  Stitching is the big problem when doing this.

It would be a whole lot easier just to revert back about 40 years or so and do Stereo Imaging.

There is also another old method of shifting the RBG to layers and then offsetting the layers, and then use 3D glasses to view the final printout.

Not sure of what you are wanting to achieve here!

I have a very very olde program called Stereo Workshop, it was designed to make those 3D images you view by learning to stare at a picture just right.  But the picture looked like a bunch of SNOW or pixelled all to heck until you learned to focus on them.  You may remember them from the newspapers a number of years ago.
However, that same program would let you assemble what the image was supposed to look like before it was converted, and the cool part was, the restoration always caused it to appear 3D looking to a certain extent.
Like the handles on a car or it's headlights would look like they were standing in front of the car instead of attached to it.  Wouldn't exactly call that Stereo, but it was interesting to see just the same.

### Post by justchange on 2008-10-29
This may be the program you were referring to in post #3:
I was just looking into this a few days ago after coming across this video at [ Metacafe (link)]("http://www.metacafe.com/watch/807300/make_3d_pictures_from_any_digital_camera/").

It shows the use of a small, free program [(Callipygian)]("http://www.callipygian.com/") for Windows *(pardon me)*, but its [homepage (link)]("http://www.callipygian.com/") lists links which give helpful tutorials for DYI *(Do It Yourself)*.

As other posters have mentioned, one typically moves the camera (to replicate the spacing of two eyes, which send slightly differing images to the brain, which has learned to process that difference into 3D spacial relationships), rather than moving the subject (which will yield the appearance of a moving subject/object, *like stop-motion animation.*)

One of the things I've learned from this is that not all "3D glasses" are alike... The [anaglyph (link)]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaglyph_image") image process superimposes offset images in differing colors (red/cyan is popular) and the 3D glasses _must match_ the colors used in a particular image.

Another thing is the realization that the resulting image can only be processed from the available information. In other words, one can't make a 360-degree 3D image of say, the moon, without images of the reverse side of the moon. 
To accomplish this, one would need to follow Crandom's information in post #2.
*(And the only way to render this as a single image is with a hologram.)*

Anyway, good luck and have fun!
