### Post by Spiritof76 on 2010-01-15
I have to state an oposing view.  
There are several componants in a system. Some of those componants are called electrolytic capacitors. They have liquid in them and they tend to dry up up over time. As temperatures increase, the expected life of the capacitors decrease.

Electrolytic caps are found on every major device in a computer system. But those found on the motherboard and power supply are the most likely to fail. 

Modern Hard drives spin pretty rapidly at over 7800rpm on very small bearings. these are mechanical devices and and are subject to wear.

Leaving a unit on 24/7 subjects the fans early failure also. A PS fan going out will often cause an almost instant powersupply failure. 

All this being said I still prefer leaving my systems on 24/7 and keeping an close tabs to any new noises. Try to use as much electricity as I can to help make our winters a little less extreme than they have been the last couple of years.

### Post by -kg- on 2010-01-16
I would have to agree with those who have advocated turning the computer on and off as little as necessary.

I have been in the electronics technology field for around 40 years.  At the time I got into the field, vacuum tubes were still widely used.  It was advised not to turn equipment on and off because every time you turned this equipment on, there was a power surge through the filaments of the tubes, which would shorten their life, and hence the life of the tubes.

The initial power surge also affects other components.  Resistors, capacitors; even solid state devices are negatively affected by these power surges upon turning an electronic device on.  The P/N junctions are put under a strain and eventually will cause a breakdown of the junction.

A friend of mine recently had his motherboard fry.  He was an advocate of turning his computer off while not in use.  His computer was newer than the newest of my desktop computers (only my laptop is newer).

I do let my computers run 24/7.  Of course, they're not sitting idle, either.  I run BOINC distributed computing projects, which put max strain on them, running the processor at or near 100% duty cycle.  I've not had a one of them go out yet.

I built my newest desktop (AMD Athlon 64 3200+) 3 years ago.  I have run it near 24/7 for all that time.  I gave my next newest computer (AMD Athlon Thunderbird) to my brother, who runs it nearly 24/7 (also running BOINC projects).  It is around 9 years old and has never had a problem other than a hard drive going out (that eventually happens with hard drives and has little to nothing to do with heat).

Yes, there will be wear and tear on moving parts, but moving parts are easy and not real difficult (or expensive) to replace.  Of course, I know that people turn their equipment on and off and they last for years.  I'm just relaying information that I've read, learned, and experienced over the years of being in this field.

It's basically up to the owner.  I personally would have no problem with the schedule you're using.  I leave mine on 24/7 for a reason.  If I had no reason, I might revert to turning them off more often than I do.
