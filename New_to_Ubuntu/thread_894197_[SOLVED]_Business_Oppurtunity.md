### Post by VeeDubb on 2008-08-19
To be honest, I really don't see there being any meaningful market for this.  Linux is at a stage, IMO, where many people use it for many different things.  But, from what I've seen, everybody seems to fall into one of two groups.

1. People like me, who are willing and able to configure for their own hardware, given the very robust set of configuration tools provided by ubuntu.

2. People who are purchasing linux systems which are pre-configured for their purpose.  i.e. preconfigured linux servers and budget PC's.

Not only do I see the market for something like this as being incredibly small, but the work/profit ratio sucks.  Redoing ubuntu for each customer would be a nightmare, and mostly not needed.

### Post by Cheesehead on 2008-08-19
Non-IT companies *will* pay big money for Linux...if it does what they want.

If you can provide custom solutions or products such as embedded system programming, robot control, CAD-to-production solutions with short turnaround, guaranteed system uptime, and low reprogramming/retooling hours, manufacturers would love to talk to you. They don't give a damn if you're selling Linux or Windows or ToothbrushOS as long as it does what they want without problems.

That's what you're really selling every month - no problems.

You can also make money providing value-added services to companies with Microsoft and other products that simply don't meet their needs. For example, one company kept customer information in a proprietary database in Quickbooks and used Word to prepare invoices and envelopes- my solution used a generic file export, python, OpenOffice, and Evolution to save days of clerical work in Word every month.

I sell them convenience and accuracy twice a month - they don't care how I do it, and they're happy to give me money for it because they still save money from the old Word Mail-Merge method that took hours.

The money is in custom software products, tested hardware with preinstalled (and debugged and tested) software, ongoing service/support agreements, and 'no-hassle' business services...often just scripting.

In other words complete life-cycle support, and the promise of no distractions, with a smile.
