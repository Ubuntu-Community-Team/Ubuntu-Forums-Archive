### Post by Mark Phelps on 2009-06-14
> **dh436 said:**
> 1- As a newcomer to linux, is 10GB enough to experience the best that linux has to offer (or at least enough to convert me)?

While Linux doesn't put the same resource demands on hardware as does, say, Vista, nonetheless, to "experience the best that Linux has to offer" is going to need more than a "crappy old HP' brings to the table.

For example, I have a bunch of older machines, all of them running Windows of some kind (originally).  Upgraded them over time to dual-boot with various flavors of Linux.  Experiences were quite mixed.

Some upgraded well, meaning, all hardware was detected, got full-screen resolution, wired and wireless internet worked, audio worked as well.  Others hardly worked at all, meaning, poor screen resolution, not able to get internet working, not able to get audio working,

Generally, the older and lower-powered the machine, the worse the Linux "experience" -- which is not a criticism of Linux, per se; but rather, it's what you'd expect.  Meaning, you're not going to get the high video resolution, high-def audio, lightening fast response typical of a machine running on a modern high-end, dual-core, 64-bit processor, with 1GB of DDR3 memory on a DX-10.x compliant video card, and 4GB of ddr3 system memory, when compared to experience on a 256MB, Coppermine PIII 600MHZ, 32MB video card machine -- regardless of the OS that is running on the box.

Just saying, Linux is not a "miracle cure" for older machines.  So, if your "Linux experience" is less than stellar, consider other options:
1) The hardware and drivers aren't up to the demands of a modern Ubuntu distro and try something "older" -- like 8.10 or 8.04.
2) Try other Linux distros that have different combinations of kernels and Xorg versions, and may be closer to what you are used to in MS Windows.  See the Distrowatch.com web page for a huge list of alternative Linux distros.
3) Try a more lightweight distro, like Puppy Linux or DSL -- again, see the distrowatch page.

You see, that's the really great thing about "coming over" to the Linux community -- you get a huge choice of options.  And, you don't have to spend a fortune to try them out.

It sat like that for two months until I finally fixed it. I got a new laptop about a month later and got Ubuntu on it and love it. > Just saying, Linux is not a "miracle cure" for older machines.

That is very true. However, you will get better performance out of an old computer than with Windows.

Don't worry if it takes you a while to migrate into Linux. It took me almost 6 months.

Just be warned, trying a livecd or a liveusb will be much slower than actual performance from your hard drive. If your only complaint about Linux when trying it with the liveusb is that it's slow, install it and it will speed up.
