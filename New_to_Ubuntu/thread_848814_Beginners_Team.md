### Post by bodhi.zazen on 2008-07-03
[FONT="Times New Roman"][size=4]**Beginners Team**[/size]

First, Welcome to Ubuntu. Starting a new operating system can be challenging and the **[Beginner Team]("http://www.ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=215")** is here to help :p

_The goals of the Beginner Team are to assist new users to _:
[LIST=1]
[*]Evaluate Ubuntu to see if it will fit their needs.
[*]Obtain and install Ubuntu.
[*]Transition into Ubuntu.
[*]Deepen their knowledge of Ubuntu/Linux.
[*]Become members of the Ubuntu community.[/LIST]

See also : [uhelp]community/Beginners/MissionStatement[/uhelp]


**New users** :

First, welcome to Ubuntu.

The Beginner Team is here to guide you, help you become familiar with a your new OS and the Ubuntu community.

Specific support questions are best directed to the [Absolute Beginner Talk](http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=326), [General Help](http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=331), or one of the specific [Main Support Categories](http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=327). Within those forums you will receive assistance not only from the Beginners Team, but the Ubuntu Community as a whole. The best way to use the forums is to get involved by asking questions.

Forums FAQ : [ Guide to Forum features ** INCLUDES: Thanking, beans, titles, searching, and more](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=726219)

Additional sources of information include :
[list=1][*][The Ubuntu wiki](https://help.ubuntu.com/) ~ The wiki pages are a great place to start learning more about Ubuntu.
[*]IRC ~ The main support channel is #ubuntu (you may use Ubuntu Servers or Freenode).
[*][Launchpad](https://launchpad.net/) ~ Bug reports, projects, questions.
[*]Help is built into Ubuntu. Go to System -> Help and Support.[/list]


**Working with team** :

The Beginners Team is an extension of the Ubuntu forums and is a community of volunteers dedicated to assisting new users. Although the primary focus of the Beginners Team is the forums, the team also contributes to IRC, Launchpad, and the Ubuntu Wiki.

If you are interested in helping with the Beginner Team, please feel free to share you ideas with us on our sub-forum [Beginner Team]("http://www.ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=215"). Please do no use the Beginner Team Sub-Forums for technical support (support questions are more visible in Absolute Beginner Talk or General Help).

If you are interested in joining the team, please see this link :

[Joining the Beginner Team](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=385084)

To those who have submitted a join request, please understand I receive an almost overwhelming number of join requests and can not personally attend each request, thus the link.


[size=4]**Unanswered Posts Team**[/size]

In addition to the BT, we also have the Unanswered Posts Team. This team focuses on, well, unanswered posts. These teams are not mutually exclusive and some people devote time to both teams.

[Unanswered Posts Team](http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=216)

Posts can go unanswered for a variety of reasons from insufficient information to (esoteric) requests for specific technical support. Threads requiring specialized knowledge pertaining to hardware, kernels, servers, applications, etc may take longer to answer. In the event your thread goes unanswered, please understand that you are not being ignored, it is just that it may take a little time to answer some questions.

Please be aware that if you "bump" you own threads, they are no longer unanswered, and so they can not be identified easily as needing assistance.

Additional information :

[Unanswered Posts Team FAQ](https://wiki.ubuntu.com/UnansweredPostsTeam/FAQ)

### Post by bodhi.zazen on 2008-09-11
> **feinar said:**
> HI.
Just wondering. Why is it so critical that you dont operate as root in linux? Isn't it the point that you'll be free to do what you want? I guess there's a very good reason, just courious!

Regards

It is not so critical and you can do as you wish. I log into (some) servers as root on a routine basis and some os use su rather then sudo.

On these forums, however, we have a lot of new users. These new users may not understand the security implications of running as root and so , rather then learning a new way of sys admin, they deactivate security features, log in as root, and change permissions of system files.

This results problems ranging from total system failure, having their system cracked, or posting requests for support.

So there are two groups then. If you know what you are doing you are more then free to run as root. This group does not need to ask how to sys admin because they know what they are doing.

The second group is new users. Rather then circumventing security features we teach people how to sys admin. This involves teaching tools such as sudo and gksu and the 'Ubuntu way" of sys admin. Proper sys admin, especially during the learning phase, is critical to proper system functioning.

So, for this second group, **if you have to ask (on the Ubuntu forums) you will be given the standard Ubuntu methods of sys admin**. Same with the Fedora questions on the Fedora forums (or any other OS for that matter).

What I am saying, when in Rome do as the Romans. It makes no sense to install Fedora, rip out selinux, install sudo and run Fedora like Ubuntu. Likewise it makse no sense to run Ubuntu like Fedora.

If you can't find an answer to your question, you make a post by deciding which forum would be most appropriate. You can see the list by clicking the main Ubuntu Forums icon at the top left of the page. Take a look at the available forums and decide which one will fit best. Please use a descriptive title that gives a good indication of the nature of the post.

Enter that forum and select "New Thread" near the left side of the page.

A word about a couple of icons which will help you and those providing responses:
When your questions have been answered or problems resolved, it is helpful to mark the thread solved. You do this by using the link at the top right of the first post marked "Thread Tools". Only the original poster can mark a thread 'Solved', and that poster can also change it back to 'Unsolved' if the need arises. Marking posts solved helps other users looking for threads which were successful in resolving a problem, and allows helpers to concentrate their efforts on unresolved issues.

Finally, there is also a 'Thank You' icon in the lower right corner of each post. If you find a reply particularly helpful and you wish to thank the poster, you can click on the gold/red medal icon to place a thank you in the post. Doing this not only provides feedback but can eliminate the single line "thank you" posts.

There is a sticky in this section that will provide you with additional information:
[New to Ubuntu? Start here...]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=801404")

And as bodhi.zazen mentioned, there is a Forums Help & Feedback Section.
