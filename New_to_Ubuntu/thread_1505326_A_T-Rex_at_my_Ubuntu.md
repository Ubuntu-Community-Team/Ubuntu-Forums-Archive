### Post by Calmor on 2010-06-15
Regressions, unfortunately, do happen while trying to solve other problems or improve code.  And, likely, the testing teams do not have the hardware to test every possible thing.

The best thing that you can do to help is submit a bug on Launchpad noting as much detail as you can, and continue to follow it if others ask questions.  Moreover, you can install beta versions in other partitions to improve the quality of future releases.

Not everything is solved this way, or at least not overnight, but it is the most effective way to get your problem noticed.  The Intel video fiasco is still ongoing after a regression in 9.04 from 8.10, but there was a reason for this regression, and it continues to be an ongoing discussion of how best to fix it.  If nobody with your TV card reports a problem, nobody will work to fix it.

You can try searching Launchpad for the model or hardware ID of your tuner (which can found running lspci | grep *insert some identifying factor of the tuner here, such as manufacturer, or simply tuner*)

As others have said, this is a completely free OS.  Part of its strength (specifically Ubuntu, but other distros as well) is the strong community it has surrounding it.  Not trying to come off as rude, but filing a bug report (or even throwing in a "me too" to an existing one) is more constructive participation in the community than complaining of a regression to a few folks that stumble upon your thread.  Paid support is also an option if you don't want to do the searching and bug reporting yourself.

Another place you might check for supported tuners is the Mythbuntu sub-forum - a section dedicated to MythTV installations, and therefore directly with TV tuners.  I personally run a Hauppauge PVR-150, and I hear QAM is working on the Hauppauge HVR-1600, so I might try throwing that one back in as well.

### Post by anewguy on 2010-06-16
I know it's a lousy "excuse", but I doubt any 1 TV tuner is going to draw a lot of attention from the developers/debuggers, so I wouldn't expect anything much in the way of quick attention.

That being said, I must admit that sometimes I'm frustrated by changes in the next release.  One the things I need that I guess the more usual user wouldn't is to define rules in udev.  It was very frustrating when the syntax for the rule statements changed but no mentioned was made.  I had to search the net and find someone else who had found another distribution to give me the new syntax.

However, as someone else mentioned, given the vast amount of hardware out there, the developers rely upon US to test the next release while it is alpha and beta.  That's the only way it can get exposed to a large array of hardware.  Back in my old mainframe days, we didn't have these types of problems, but it came with a huge cost (several thousand dollars a month for a maintenance contract, several thousand dollars each year for software support - and each piece [like compilers] were priced seperately).  We didn't have those problems because the HARDWARE manufacturer controlled EVERYTHING, including the OS.  With the advent of PC's years ago (even back to the CP/M and pre-CP/M days), hardware moved to the background and the OS came to the foreground.  Now move forward in time to open-source and free OS's, like Ubuntu, and you need to rely on others out in the real world for real world testing.  So, and I'm guilty of not doing this yet myself [mainly because I don't have any extra hardware], the best way to avoid things like this is to participate in the testing of the releases.
