### Post by -kg- on 2010-06-14
> I guess my problem is one of principle. If an open-source distro is heading in the direction of being more amiable to a corporate cell phone racket or the two biggest pieces of trash on the internet, then maybe it's not the distro for me.

I can understand that.  The main reason that any distro might head in that direction is customer demand.  The demand for these capabilities is very high, and those companies who want their products to succeed either try to convince their potential users that they have a better way (and provide it), or accede to the popular demand and provide that service.

It's like the mp3 codecs.  The Linux community in general would sincerely like to see the rest of the world convert to the .ogg format, but 99.999...% of the compressed music available is still in the .mp3 format.  So the vast majority of the distros out there provide the means to install the .mp3 codecs in their distros.  Ubuntu doesn't provide them by default...you have to install them.

> most of what I've read says the same thing...that Lucid was geared towards being more friendly to iPhones, facebook, and twitter.

As far as the last two things are concerned, more user friendly access to net-based services aren't a matter of "free" or "non-free" GPL licensed software; they're merely a more user-friendly access.  Again, if you're not interested in them (or even if you hate them) just don't use them.  The demand is there for them, so Ubuntu has provided an applet to make access easier.  The applet is removable.

As far as iPhones, that's also a matter of demand.  If you don't have an iPhone, then you're not going to have use for that capability.  If they provide the ability to access iPhones by default, then I'm sure this capability is covered under the GPL license.  Otherwise it will be provided after-market or through 3rd party repos not connected with Ubuntu.  These 3rd party software and repos are also provided to other Linux distros, and are not controlled by them, any more than the distros control what the individual user does with his or her installation.

You might find [this article]("http://interviews.slashdot.org/interviews/06/08/17/177220.shtml") interesting.  It is an interview of Fedora Project Leader Max Spevack on Slashdot.  Fedora (the open distro of Red Hat Enterprise) is a very good distro of Linux; I have it, as well as OpenSUSE, installed on my laptop alongside Ubuntu.

> After three years of using Ubuntu, I don't think I've come to understand as much about it as I should have. I sense that there is some kind of knowledge base about Linux in general, which I'm lacking, in order to get the most out of one's distro, or even to choose the right distro... 

If you ***really*** want to learn Linux, you might consider something like Slackware or Arch Linux.  These distros (especially Slackware) require you to build your installation piece by piece, putting in only what ***you*** want to be there.

With those, you'll definitely learn Linux...you'll ***have to*** in order to have a working installation that does what you want it to and has what you require.  I would suggest putting Python on the back shelf and start on C and C++, though.  You're likely to have to recompile the kernel a few times.

As far as learning about Linux itself, there are many on-line resources.  A couple I particularly like are:

[The Linux Documentation Project]("http://tldp.org/docs.html")
