### Post by Brandon Williams on 2010-01-10
The best way for you to figure out whether linux will work in your environment is to try it out. You can easily install a linux live CD to a USB thumb drive in order to get persistent data data storage, and then boot your computer off of the thumb drive. That way, you can experiment with linux without losing your current windows installation.

In my experience, linux fits just fine into a windows-centric offic environment. There are native apps that fill the role of each piece of the MS office suite (open office for documents/spreadsheets/presentations; evolution for exchange). And if those apps don't work well enough for you due to the specifics of the document contents or exchange configuration, then the whole MS office suite can easily be installed under linux using a windows compatibility layer called wine.

Where hardware is concerned, it tends to be true that older hardware works just fine with linux. You're more likely to have trouble if you're using the newest, most cutting edge hardware. Like I said above, it's easy enough to test hardware compatibility without having to overwrite your windows install, so that's the best way to verify that all your required hardware will actually work.

### Post by Methuselah on 2010-01-10
I am nervous for you too if you use your computer for business and depend on Microsoft.
While there is some interoperability (mostly by dint of persistence and reverse engineering) Ubuntu is as far from MS as one can get.
Windows applications do not run natively and Microsoft protocols/standards are not the native/default standards.

In addition, I am certain you have a particular workflow that would positively be disrupted by any abrupt switch.
So I would definitely not encourage you to just switch to Ubuntu cold turkey or in any way jeopordize your current setup.
If you have not done dual booting before there is need for caution in setting that up as well.

However, I would definitely encourage you to try Ubuntu out on another machine or in a virtual machine installed within windows (if your windows machine is powerful enough).
In addition, sticking in the downloaded and burned CD but choosing 'try ubuntu without a change to your computer' will launch a demo environment.
It will not be as fast as an installation but will give you an idea of how well your hardware will be supported.
I am optimistic about your printer and scanner working but wireless depends entirely on what particular chip you have.

So yeah, your next step should be 'try' not 'depend on'.
That's my 2c.
