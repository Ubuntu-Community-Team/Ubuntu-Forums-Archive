### Post by mikechant on 2009-02-23
A lot of the serious development on large Free/Open source applications such as Linux itself is actually done by well-paid developers at IBM, Novell, Sun etc.
Those companies find that they benefit by (e.g.) getting Linux to work on the hardware they want, adding features that they need etc.; yes, they have to feed these changes back and their rivals may benefit from these changes for free; but IBM don't have to maintain the whole of Linux themselves for example - they just work on the bits they are interested in and leave the rest to others. IBM are a hard-nosed company that only do things that will make them money - and they are very happy to pump significant amounts into FOSS.

### Post by candtalan on 2009-02-23
An additional factor is that the regime of open source and free software - the fact it is necessarily open - means that yes you can pay for support or specialised development, however, significantly, you do not find you are forced to go to a particular company for the work, nor the information. 

This has the advantage that many more companies and businesses can create wealth and knowledge perhaps locally, and wealth is not channeled only towards a single company, which is perhaps in a different country.

So there is a de-centralisation of knowledge - a spreading of knowledge, and wealth. 

Crucially though, the user has a choice (worth emphasising) about when and where money is spent. And also a considerable choice about becoming self informed. An authoring company then will find that their customers are well informed, and may probably assist the fortunes of the author company.

Oh and not least - open source is -not- the same as free (as in libre) software, although there is a lot of common ground. It is worth looking at the ethos and motives of both.
