### Post by TheFu on 2023-06-13
Linux/Unix has a very steep learning curve.  I remember being frustrated trying to install SLS long ago.  I'd been using Unix for about a year at that point, but Linux was very, very new and there really weren't installers.
I remember when Slackware came out and it was the "easy" linux to use.

So, my only recommendation, Grasshopper, is to relax and learn things from the beginning, in the proper order.  Linux/Unix mandates that we learn to lift our head, learn to rollover, learn to crawl, then stand, then wobble-walk, walk, walk-faster, jog, run, run-faster, then sprint.  Don't expect to run the hurdles on day 1 - or to jump.

If you are having issues getting Linux installed correctly, and it read like that is part of the issue, I'd strongly suggest you get help from a LUG. Most Universities will have a LUG that is open to everyone unless you aren't near a University that is technical.  Some less populated areas might have a LUG that covers half a province with people driving 2 hrs to the monthly meetings.  Some LUGs have online meetings.

The first thing you need to do when there is a problem is to look at the log files for the cause of that problem. There are guides online for how to look at Linux log files.  I usually search them all for errors/warnings when I don't have a clue where to start. That gets me to the specific logs and some subsystem with an issue to begin my troubleshooting.

**ls** is a very basic command. Don't know what it has to do with anything here. It doesn't fix anything.  Running **ls** can be useful to get specific information, but the arguments matter. 'ls' alone probably isn't very useful.  Most CLI commands require arguments to be useful as the defaults provide limited, minimal, information for just 1 directory.

If there are issues booting from a flash drive, it could be the flash drive is incompatible or wasn't setup correctly or that the USB port is incompatible or the USB-port and USB-Flash drive are incompatible.  Try a different port. Try a different flash storage device.  I've seen expensive flash drives be incompatible and cheap ones work.  I've seen the opposite too.  I have a flash drive that works in 2 of my systems, but not the other 5.  Some flash drives can't be used to boot at all.  I think it relates to the USB controller, but who knows?  These sorts of failures aren't as common as they were in 2010.

Did you follow these instructions to create the flash drive for installation? [https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Installation/FromUSBStick](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Installation/FromUSBStick)
Ubuntu isn't designed to be run from a USB drive, though people do.  It is best to install onto an SSD or HDD internal to the computer.  I tend to seek out guides from *.ubuntu.com domains first.  Those are the more official documents which can usually handle most situations.  Every computer and every user is a little different.  When google provides an answer that is just a few lines, be careful.  The person who wrote it probably meant well, but was ignorant about possible complexities which may or may not apply to your situation.

Anyway, I get that you are frustrated, but insufficient details have been provided to help.
