### Post by th1rt3en on 2011-04-30
I'm no Linux pro, but I've been using Ubuntu since 8.10, and my general rule of thumb would be to wait a month after each update to allow the major bugs to be sorted out if you don't want to deal with them. I always update immediately though, and that's just so I can help sort out the bugs with the rest of the community. If you don't have the time or feel you have the experience to do this I would recommend waiting at least a month.

And just a tip, I always do a clean install of the next Ubuntu version rather than upgrading; only so I can get rid of all the accumulated errors and glitches I pick up over time rather than having them carry over. This is just personal preference, however.

### Post by beew on 2011-04-30
I will go for option C.

You are not obligated to upgrade your OS based on Ubuntu's release cycle. I am skipping 11.04 and probably 11.10 as well. Why should I risk breaking my system while 10.10 is rock solid and pretty close to perfect on my machine? It is hard to think of any improvement that would make me take the risk to upgrade in less than 6 months (I installed 10.10 almost two months after it came out to make sure all major bugs were fixed) I have the latest softwares from ppas so I don't need to risk my OS just to get up to date software. (IMO this is the most ridiculous thing about Ubuntu. On the one hand they expect you to upgrade every 6 months to get up to date software--actually they are not that up to date as the repos freeze before release, so they are only less outdated,--but if you f-up your system as a result they would tell you all non LTS are in a sense beta so upgrade at your own risk)

I really have a good thing going and I want to enjoy it for as long as possible. From what I see about Natty it would actually be a downgrade if I go ahead to replace 10.10 with it.

On the other hand, I do a full install of 11.04 on an external hard drive so I can test and experiment as much as I want. I have been testing since alpha and the beauty of it is that the system is a full fledge install so it is no different from running 11.04 from my internal hard drive, yet it doesn't put my working system at risk, what is more, it is fully portable so I can use it on different machines and test different hardware as well.

So I get the best of all worlds, I  fully enjoy the cutting edge new stuffs yet without risking my highly customized work machine which is as close to perfection as possible. :)

P.S. I am making a general point and it has nothing to do with Unity. By "upgrade" I mean in a general sense of replacing your existing Ubuntu with a newer release, it doesn't mean "upgrading" in the technical sense like upgrading with the update-manager, it includes a clean install as well (and it is preferable to "upgrading" in the technical sense for many reasons)
