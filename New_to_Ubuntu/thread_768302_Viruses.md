### Post by MeanEYE on 2008-04-26
> Sure, there are Linux viruses. But let's compare the numbers. According to Dr. Nic Peeling and Dr Julian Satchell's Analysis of the Impact of Open Source Software (note: the link is to a 135 kb PDF file):

"There are about 60,000 viruses known for Windows, 40 or so for the Macintosh, about 5 for commercial Unix versions, and perhaps 40 for Linux. Most of the Windows viruses are not important, but many hundreds have caused widespread damage. Two or three of the Macintosh viruses were widespread enough to be of importance. None of the Unix or Linux viruses became widespread - most were confined to the laboratory."

[Linux vs. Windows Viruses]("http://www.theregister.co.uk/2003/10/06/linux_vs_windows_viruses/")

### Post by marquee moon on 2008-04-26
Been using ubuntu & other linux distros for a couple of years with no viruses. 

As well as the reasons mentioned in previous posts why its not a problem, here's another really important reason: * viruses have nowhere to hide in linux *. There's no restricted access directories or files that you're not allowed to look in. You can manually clean up every bit of your system. You can re-install whole chunks of your OS in under an hour. So whats the point of making a virus that can be stopped at first base? 

My router has a firewall. That's been enough for me for a couple of years now.

### Post by Kevbert on 2008-04-26
You're only likely to encounter viruses if you are using a windows or dos emulator (eg DOSBox).  These will be DOS/Windows viruses which are unlikely to affect linux.  If you are using a dual boot PC with shared directories you may want to take a look at ClamAV which will find the viruses.  An article on installing can be found here: [http://www.ubuntugeek.com/howto-install-clam-antivirus-with-gtk-frontend-gui.html#more-344]("http://www.ubuntugeek.com/howto-install-clam-antivirus-with-gtk-frontend-gui.html#more-344")
Please note this will only find DOS/Windows viruses.

### Post by The Cog on 2008-04-26
> **unclejac said:**
> Is it worth having Firestarter?

Not for a default install - there are no listening services for anyone to connect to, so nothing for a firewall to prevent happening. However, if you install any services yourself, openssh-server for instance, then you may want to install Firestarter to limit which IP addresses are allowed to try to connect to that service.
