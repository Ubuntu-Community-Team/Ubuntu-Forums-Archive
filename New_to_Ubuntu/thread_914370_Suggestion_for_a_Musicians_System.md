### Post by Charvelguy on 2008-09-08
'Best' is subjective of course. Do you want to spend hundreds if not thousands on computer and software, OR you could go with what you prefer and feel comfortable with working on... which tools are best for the creative process. There's always a learning curve with whatever you work with.. if money is no object, well... Power Mac G5 Pro with dual quad core 3.2 Intel Xeon processors & dual 640gb hds running in raid and at least 4gb ram would be a trick setup. 
.[http://www.apple.com/macpro/](http://www.apple.com/macpro/) 
...a formidable digital recording tool.

I would say Pro Tools and Mac has been the industry standard for over a decade, BUT... there's so much available out there.

Personally, I have been using Riffworks because I like to work with it. No latency recording when using a toneport. Riffworks with Audacity, and Reason with a UX2 toneport and the gold plugins....its a little buggy yet still but its getting more stable and I've had good results with it overall and good tones as well.  If its good enough for the Runt,(see the OCT issue of Guitar Player with Todd Rundgren) its good enough for me too. Runs on a PC loaded with at least an upper Pent 3 over 1 GHZ and a gig ram. Runs best on dual or quad core, though a single core system works ok too.  

Ideally, I like working with a quad core and 4 gb ram nowadays for more apps, but a decent dual core or OC'd dual with 2 gb will work just fine too. AMD or Intel?  I use both, I would have to give the edge to the Intels, but an AMD dual or Quad, depending on the processor, mbo combo..will suit one just fine as well. 

Your interface also depends on your needs.
I have Motu,
M-audio, E-mu, Digidesign but I still use Riffworks with a Toneport just for ease of use and/or cost factor. Some people may think its a joke, but one can get pretty impressive results for such a low cost and export your master to an external program.  

USB speeds will be upgrading here in the next year and I imagine firewire will also be going up too. 
Firewire 800mbs cards can be somewhat pricey but if you are running Firewire, its not a bad consideration, otherwise USB 2.0 is fine.

### Post by Sorivenul on 2008-09-08
I would also suggest an Apple/Mac for production.  I've done production work on Mac and Linux and have to say the Mac offered me much more of what I personally needed (at a price).  However, if you are serious about using Linux for a studio setup I would definitely suggest the real-time kernel.  It is the successor to the low-latency kernel, and is available in the Ubuntu repositories.  Learn more about the pros and cons of the real-time kernel here:

### Post by Sam the Wizer on 2008-10-06
I'm currently running a Presonus Firepod through a 1394a (Firewire) card and it works very well.  I don't have a screeming system (Intel 2.4GHz processor, 512Mb ram) running real-time kernel with ubuntu studio package.  The programs I use primarily are Jack audio server, Ardour Digital Audio Workstation (multi-track recording) and Hydrogen drum sequencer.  I have friends that are audio engineers trained at MI in Hollywood and the Conservatory of Recording Arts and Sciences in Mesa, AZ and have had experience recording with them on both Mac and Windows.  The programs they use are Cubase and ProTools.  Protools seems to work best (but is very costly) but Cubase and other Windows applications never have worked correctly for me.  I was surprised when moving to Ubuntu Studio that it works much better than similar programs in Windows.  The only issue I've have had is that the plugins in Linux aren't anywhere near the quality of those in Protools or even Cubase.  Honestly though, if you listen to recordings from the 70s when the technology was analog you'll find that they got some amazing sounds.  If you have a basic Ubuntu setup it will allow you to spend more on hardware such as good mics and a good soundcard which will probably have the biggest impact on the quality of your recordings.
