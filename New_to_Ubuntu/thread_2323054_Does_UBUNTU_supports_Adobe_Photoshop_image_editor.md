### Post by Mark Phelps on 2016-05-02
That depends on which version you want to use.

Photoshop requires using Wine, because it is a MS Windows program, and the WineHQ databases publishes a rating page for the different versions:  [https://appdb.winehq.org/objectManager.php?sClass=application&iId=17]("https://appdb.winehq.org/objectManager.php?sClass=application&iId=17")

As you can see, the ratings are all over the place, and general rule is, if not Gold or better, you're wasting your time as the lower ratings mean loss of functionality.

Good Luck

### Post by buzzingrobot on 2016-05-04
GIMP is FOSS. As a practical matter, it's almost impossible to sell a FOSS product while simultaneously making its source available.  Someone else will just rebuild it and give it away. (There are exceptions, but few.)

The "Is GIMP a replacement for Photoshop" debate is endless and pointless. They are two different products.  PhotoShop's dominance exists because it is a good product, it is a very old and established product, and it has fostered the development of many commercial software products to supplement it. Professionals who have spent years and thousands of dollars accumulating Photoshop expertise, have archives of perhaps hundreds of thousands of Photoshop files, and clients who are prepared to only deal with Photoshop.  They have no incentive to change. The gratis nature of GIMP is not relevant to them because they pass software costs along to clients.

Amateurs, especially, amateur photographers, should look elsewhere altogether, Both GIMP and Photoshop have as their primary purpose something other than editing photos. Graphic artists should look at Krita, in particular.

### Post by leunam12 on 2016-05-05
> **peeng said:**
> 
S0 what you think why is it due to free of charge or some other matter behind this no doubt Adobe Photoshop is king according to my knowledge and experience.
Adobe has the financial resources to hire better programmers; also Adobe receives a lot more input from users that help make it a better program. If you are used to Photoshop you will have to learn to think differently to use GIMP since concepts are not the same. A layer in Photoshop, for example, is not the same than a layer in Gimp; they are both layers in the technical sense of the word but they behave differently and have different properties. Also tools in GIMP are not as precise as in Photoshop; as a matter of fact, I stopped using GIMP when I noticed that a simple color correction with the Color Balance tool using the same settings in both programs, rendered a non-desirable result in GIMP while it looked perfect in Photoshop. I know that there are some plug-ins and tweaks that you can add to GIMP that can make it more functional (such as the layer-styles plug-in, since GIMP doesn't have that feature natively) but I just don't have the time to research and try them out; for me it is a lot easier and way faster to press one little button on my KVM switch and use Photoshop in Windows. Besides, when you have to edit 300 or more photos having recorded actions can help cut the time needed to do all you have to do in half. And even if you don't use actions it's faster in Photoshop since the tools are more precise and render the desired results with less effort.
