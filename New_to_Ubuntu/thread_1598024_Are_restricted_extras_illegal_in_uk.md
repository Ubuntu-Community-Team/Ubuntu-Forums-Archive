### Post by Old_Man_Unix on 2010-10-16
Some of the material in the ubuntu-restricted-extras metapackage is released as  "free/libre" but is licensed under terms that differ from the GPL/LGPL  in ways that Debian (and by extension, Ubuntu) consider to be restrictive.  Therefore, Ubuntu won't include it in the distribution.  You can use that material for almost all purposes with no problem.

Some material is "free/gratis" but is closed source and is encumbered by a EULA (e.g., Adobe Flash).  Again, Ubuntu won't include that in its distribution.  However, you can generally use it for all private purposes. 

Finally - and this is probably what you are most worried about - some of the material is or may be encumbered by various property rights and - from the POV of  the rights holder -  cannot be released in any "free" form (e.g., the MP3 codecs).   Obviously, they are not trying to enforce that at this time.

If you are bothered by any of this, you don't have to install those parts that concern you.  The meta-package actually consists of individual packages and you can always install  the packages individually if you want to,  and leave out the ones you feel you shouldn't use.

As far as it being "illegal",  if you are going to use *any material or any derivatives of it* for other than private use, e.g., commercial purposes, and the material is not covered by the GPL/LGPL,  I would definitely get a legal opinion on the licensing/rights issues involved.  Otherwise, I wouldn't worry about it.
