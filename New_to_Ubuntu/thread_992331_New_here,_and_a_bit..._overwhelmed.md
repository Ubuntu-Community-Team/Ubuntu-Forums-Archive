### Post by wonderbriefs on 2008-11-24
It's great that you're interested in switching over, but I want you to know that despite what you may have heard, it will not be easy.

If a failed video driver is was the final straw that turned you off of Microsoft, then I question whether you'll have the patience to deal with Ubuntu which is notorious for having bugs including video driver issues on many systems. These bugs often require the user to edit sensitive system files such as xorg.conf using the terminal. This is not for the faint-of-heart newbie, but any complaining will usually cause the Linux-elitists to say things like, "if you can't use the terminal than GTFO."

Anger toward Microsoft is warranted because you had to pay for Microsoft. Microsoft pays their employees to make their software work, and pays customer service people to handle your problems.

In the Linux world, everyone is a basically a hippie, developing software for the greater good of humanity and all that jazz. If the software doesn't work, no one is obligated to help you. You might be lucky enough to find someone who knows how to fix your problems, but if you upgrade your video driver and the screen goes black, you don't have anyone to help get it fixed. Well, there's Canonical, but they cost about $250 a year for tech support. I'd only recommend them if you're a business.

I've been using Ubuntu off and on for about a year and feel fairly comfortable with it, although I've never contributed any source code myself. I personally dual-boot between Ubuntu and Vista on my desktop.

Here are some tips.

1. If there are any must-have apps that you cannot live without, then Linux might not be for you. For instance, if you're a professional audio technician who uses Propellerheads Reason which is only designed for Windows and Mac, then Linux will not work. There are opensource audio production programs available, but none are as functional and polished as the high-end, high-dollar, Windows and Mac programs.

There is a program called Wine, that will attempt to run Windows-based programs on a Linux machine. This is not for the faint of heart. Some programs work fairly well. Others won't work at all. You must be ambitious.

2. If you are the typical home-user who is simply looking to use the web, use an office suite, download mp3s, share photos, etc, then Ubuntu can meet your every need.
OpenOffice is your Microsoft Office replacement. It's compatible with reading and writing to nearly all Microsoft Office formats.
Gimp is your Photo editor. It can do some of the same things as Adobe Photoshop but is by no stretch of the imagination a replacement.
Totem is a fine basic video player.
VLC will play nearly any video you throw at it.
Pidgin is a great Instant Messenger that handles AIM, GChat, MSN, and more.
Cheese is decent for recording videos through your webcam.
Rhythmbox is your default music player. If you're more familiar with iTunes, then you might try Songbird.

All the programs you might need are found in System -> Administration -> Package Manager. Once Package Manager is loaded, you can search for programs that are written to run on your computer. Some are pretty good. Others are honestly no good at all. This is because this is because almost everything you find here was developed for free by a community.

Welcome to the wide open world of Ubuntu, where you will have to bravely use your own discretion in everything and have no company to blame anything on. To survive, you'll definitely need to use these Ubuntu Forums. I know I have.
