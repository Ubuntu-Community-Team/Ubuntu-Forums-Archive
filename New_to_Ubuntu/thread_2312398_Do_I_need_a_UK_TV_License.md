### Post by matt_symes on 2016-02-04
Understand that if you've had a TV license, they will come around.

They assume, rightly or wrongly, that, if you've had a TV license then you'll never be of the opinion that the "content" they broadcast is tripe and formulaic nonsense and not want to renew it.

Watching catchup TV from IPlayer is currently allowed, but they are expected to close this loophole at some point.

Detune any TV you may have. If it you an aerial to an antenna on the roof, cut the plug off.

When they call around, invite them in straight away. Explain that you don't watch TV and explain what your antennas are for.

If they ask you to turn on your TV (and they will), do it straight away and show them that it's detuned and/or there's no aerial in the back leading to an antenna that can receive broadcast TV. 

Head them off at the pass by getting in contact with them and telling them that you don't watch TV.

They'll still send someone around though and they wont tell you when it will be.

Remember they get many people trying to dodge payment of this TV tax, so almost everyone they visit will be watching TV illegally.

The above is in no way advice on what you should do. Heed howefield's warning and advice carefully.

If your interested, i don't watch any broadcast TV and recently had a visit from TV licensing.

I was able to demonstrate to the satisfaction of the inspector that i was not watching any broadcast TV.

They come around, on average, once a year after the first inspection to check that your not breaking the law. All visits are unannounced.

You have to take every measure to demonstrate that your not watching broadcast TV because, if they can, they'll get you even if your not breaking the law.

### Post by The Cog on 2016-02-05
My understanding is that if you only have computers but no TV you are OK. iPlayer and the like are technically not "broadcast" services and therefore not covered by the license requirements. 

I think at some point this will change. One reason is all the students in lodgings who used to pay a license fee for their TV now tend to only have computers instead and the BBC is seeing reduced license income. The BBC's sense of entitlement is palpable - there was recent discussion about the fact that people aged over 75 don't have to pay for a license, and the BBC were arguing very clearly how much this was "costing" them.

I believe that possession of equipment capable of receiving TV broadcasts is enough to incur the need for a license. De-tuning a TV or storing it in the loft/cellar with other old furniture and stuffed animals and forgetting about it are not enough.
