> How do I dual boot?

Dual booting is easy. Grab the livecd version from [www.ubuntu.com(I](www.ubuntu.com(I) believe it's the default download) and put it in your computer and reboot. The livecd acts like a hard drive but does not touch your hard drive at all and runs the operating system in memory. It is slow this way, but it's mostly a demo, or system repair tool.

If you like what you see and would like to install it, click the "Install" icon, and follow the steps. During the install, make sure **_not_** to select "guided -use whole disk". As long as you select "use free space" or similar it will automatically set your computer up as dual boot.

### Post by -kg- on 2009-04-03
Most certainly, [back up your current information]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/HowtoPartition/EditorsAndBackup") before making any changes to your hard drive partition(s).  Also, before performing any partitioning operation (which is what will happen when you install Ubuntu or any other OS in a dual/multi-boot configuration other than with WUBI) it is highly recommended to defrag your Windows partition.  This will make it much easier to resize the partition, since the vast majority of the files will be near the beginning of the partition.

Read the ["How To Partition"]("https://help.ubuntu.com/community/HowtoPartition") page in the Community Documentation before you begin so that you understand what you face and what can happen (and what to do in case that happens).  There are also numerous sources elsewhere that will give you much the same information and sometimes more.

And if you do run into seemingly insurmountable problems, check back here and post.  There are a great many on this site who can get you through most problems; even those where you think you might have hosed your hard drive.

And if you _do_ run into one of those problems, *[COLOR="Red"]**STOP!**[/COLOR]* and don't do anything else.  Come on this forum and make a post asking for help with it.  A great amount of the time, these problems can be corrected, but if you try to recover by yourself without understanding the problem, you can truly hose it and make it unrecoverable.

Really, in reference to backups, you need to have a relatively current backup of your system whether you are doing anything with the hard drive or not.  Hard drives crash...OSes crash...virus can come in and hose everything, and if you don't have a backup, you're just as screwed as if you had hosed the hard drive through a partitioning operation.

I make frequent backups, and have multiple drives on which to do it.  I have an internal 1 TB SATA hard drive for that purpose in my desktop, and an external 1 TB hard drive for my laptop and for redundancy.  I probably don't have to tell you that you can also back up your hard drives to CD-R and DVD-/+R.  That is probably the best, because all your hard drives can go bad (not too likely) but it is unlikely that a set of CD-Rs or DVDs will.

> I have a supposedly 256Mb laptop (currently showing 222Mb for some reason that I haven't fathomed out yet)

Likely, because it's a laptop, the "missing" RAM is assigned as VIDRAM, since laptop video chips rarely have RAM dedicated to video.
