### Post by TheFu on 2022-08-29
> **dmacpeak said:**
> I have purchased the book and I am a bit anxious it will be over my head....

Any suggestions on how to approach dissecting this fascinating tool of potential empowerment aside from very carefully and with a highlighter?

I've used this specific book to teach beginning Linux.  
Forget the highlighter.
**Read the chapters in order.** Concepts are taught in a specific order for a reason.  Prior concepts are important later. DO NOT SKIP chapters - well  at least until about 300 pgs in.
Work the exercises at the end of each chapter.
Work the "extra" exercises at the end of each chapter.
Have a plan. Spend at least 5 hours a week on the book, reading, exercises.  Use Linux as much as possible, daily, in addition to the book stuff.

TLCL is available legally for free here: [http://linuxcommand.org/tlcl.php](http://linuxcommand.org/tlcl.php) as a no-hassle PDF file. It has been translated into a number of languages.

Keep a link to this thread, so when you ask questions directly from the book, we will know it isn't a school assignment and can offer explanations knowing what you've already read.  Answering homework assignments is against forum policy, but helping to understand for someone doing self-study is really why I'm here.

There are other linux learning methods and trying to do it alone isn't the easiest.  Join a LUG so you can interact with other Linux people. Sometimes a 10 minute post here can be answered in 2 sentences by a human in the same room or over a video conference.  Many LUGs have switched to online conferences. Take advantage of those meetings.

### Post by col48 on 2022-09-06
I think a lot depends on what your ultimate aim is.  

If you are very ambitious in what you want to do by way of changes to the guts of your system then you will surely break it eventually and will rue the day you did not follow the advice offered earlier in this thread.

But if you are cautious and do your research well and MAKE GOOD BACKUPS and stick largely to messing about with files and folders you created yourself and are particularly careful when handling sudo you may well wonder what all the fuss was about.  My personal approach includes having all the files and folders of my own making inside a master folder within my user folder in Home - unless they are in the grey area which the OS needs to know about, like the Desktop.  

It sounds like you are making a good start, not diving in without laying a good foundation of understanding.

There's a lot I am not interested in understanding more deeply that I have to - but for me this forum is a wonderful resource to cover what to me is cutting edge and to others is bread-and-butter.  For me, some exposure to Unix some 40 years ago gave me some understanding of what went in the engine room and when I first took the Linux plunge I was given "The Pocket Linux Guide", 2004 edition, predating Ubuntu but a reasonable gateway.

One of the delights I found with Linux, inherited from Unix, is that there are often many ways to achieve a particular goal, in contrast to (at least the earlier versions of) Windows where there was often only one - which did not quite do what you wanted as often as not.

Go to it, and MAKE GOOD BACKUPS.
