### Post by Geoffrey_Arndt on 2015-09-09
I'm assuming you are using Ubuntu 12.04 "with the Unity" (normal, standard desktop),  but from your post, it sounds like you might be using a different desktop environment (re "applications" . . no such icon or option in Unity launcher that I'm aware of).

Anyway, the problem with Dropbox for some users is that it's basically "too simple" . . . so those users wind up looking for a complicated solution that doesn't exist.

In other words, if you open two file windows (nautilus file manager), and then drag and drop any combo of folders and files into the other window into the dropbox folder (either drag it on top of the icon, or open the dropbox folder first and then add other files to it.   You don't have to drag/drop.    You can also just copy/paste the normal way.    You can also manually create new folders within the dropbox folder (in a variety of ways).

Once you have new content within the dropbox folder - - that content will be uploaded to your dropbox folder in the "cloud" (e.g., the dropbox server).    ALso, you should have the Dropbox widget/applet in your top menu panel.   By clicking that, you get more options including going to your folder at Dropbox's website.    By going to their website (assuming you've created a profile there by registering), you get more options for things like sharing files with other folks.    Also, if you have dropbox installed on your smartphone (or other PC's or certain digital camera models) - - you photos will be automatically uploaded to your dropbox folder in the cloud . . . AND THEN, downloaded or synced to your dropbox folder on your PC.

Here's a thread that talks about a recent install of dropbox - - that may help if you closely read all the posts.

[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2293397](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=2293397)

By the way, the file with the "green tick" containing a PDF on how to use Dropbox.   Read it again - more carefully this time.   It doesn't just apply to windows or mac.   It also applies to linux.
