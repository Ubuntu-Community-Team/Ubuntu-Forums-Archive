### Post by lflindsey on 2010-03-06
I think Gnome is more stable than KDE, and having tried both, I think Ubuntu-Gnome integration is much better than KDE-Gnome integration.  I like KDE, in fact I'm using it right now, but I'm afraid that anyone new to Linux might get soured by how unstable it can be.

As far as Windows interoperability, I have XP running inside of Sun's (Oracles?) VirtualBox.  It's pretty excellent.  Windows boots faster in VirtualBox than on the native hardware. ### Post by cacycleworks on 2010-03-07
No, no need to pay $ for codecs for mp3s. You enable the "restricted extras" in the software center and then install just like every other package.

When you see things called "non-free" that means they have a restricted license and that it could be theoretically a violation of license depending on where you live, etc, etc. Kind of like the way microsoft says you can't have a license on two computers, etc etc. 

Another example is when you download a precompiled binary and have no source available. Video drivers, Adobe Reader.

You will see reference to FoSS, Free open source software. This is completely free, they share the source with the community under one of the friendly licenses (gnu is one).

"PAY for software" are called commercial software and normally for business or enterprise offerings. And even in these cases, there are often provided a free version which might have some features disabled. An example is the open source enterprise accounting software we use in my business, xTuple.
