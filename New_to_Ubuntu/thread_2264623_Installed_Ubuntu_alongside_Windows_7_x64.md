### Post by Mark Phelps on 2015-02-09
>  To an end-user, how exactly does Ubuntu differ from Microsoft besides the look of the UI? 

Quite frankly -- to an "end-user", the two things that make the most difference between OS have nothing whatsoever to do with the open-ness of the source, but instead, are the GUI and the apps.

The GUI is the "look and feel" of the OS -- what the end-user sees and how they interact with the OS.  Modern Windows OSs have two different GUIs -- the Modern (once called Metro -- the tiles), and the Desktop.  There are apps you can use to make changes to the colors and textures in the desktop, but the basic layout does not change much.

In stark contrast, Linux distros come with a variety of different GUIs, the default one for Ubuntu being Unity.  Without changing the OS, you can install and try out different GUIs to get a very different look and feel.

As to the Apps, the biggest mistake new folks make in coming over to Ubuntu is exhausting themselves struggling to get their Windows apps working; when instead, they should be learning to use native Linux apps.  Some apps, especially Browsers like Firefox and Chrome, look and behave essentially the same between Windows and Linux; while other apps, most notably Internet Explorer and Microsoft Office, work well only in Windows.  If Linux has the apps you need, and they work well for you, you won't miss being back in Windows.
