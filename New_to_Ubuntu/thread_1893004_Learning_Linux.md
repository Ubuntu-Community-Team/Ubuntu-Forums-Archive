### Post by agillator on 2011-12-09
First, forget about becoming an 'expert'. Break the word down into its parts: ex - old, has been, over the hill; spurt - a drip under pressure. Becoming proficient is another story. The best way to become proficient in Linux is to use it and use it and then use it some more.  Start with simple tasks such as changing the prompt or changing the colors used in a terminal. When you see something you would prefer changed, change it. There are many good reference books out there - your choice. There are also many good references and tutorials online. Again, your choice. It doesn't really matter where you start. You will find some suit you and some don't. Eventually you will find you have settled on a few that form your core library. Online references have the advantage of being free. You will also find that some references are good in some areas and not in others, so you will use more than one. 

As to a language, change that to 'languages'. They all have their uses, their strengths and weaknesses. It depends on what you decide you want to do. You need skills in both scripting and programming. A rudimentary knowledge of bash is almost a necessity. For scripting and simple programs I like perl. For more robust programs take your pick. There is java, C, C++ and a number of others. You will probably have some facility with more than one. What you are doing with it will become the deciding factor. Pick one, start to learn it and use it for a purpose. If it is not suited to the task at hand it will lead you to another and another. You will naturally settle on one or two .

Above all, be curious. Don't accept that something works or doesn't. Ask yourself why and how, and then set out to find the answers. Forums such as this can be a fantastic learning tool. Participate and be active. Be patient, with yourself and others. 

There are not many specifics here, but then there are at least thousands of ways to get to where I believe you want to go. Don't worry about how you are going to get there, just start. As you learn you will naturally pick the roads that are right for you.

### Post by edm1 on 2011-12-09
As you said there is not really a quick way to learn linux. The best way i have found is to try to do *something*, probably break your machine, then spend half a day figuring out how to work out how to fix it. You dont necessarily have to break anything but if you try to understand exactly what you are doing and look at different ways that you can achieve it.

Just think of something that you would like your computer to do differently and use these forums and google to work out how to do it, e.g. putting your home folder on a separate partition (without doing a fresh install).

And to be honest, you don't need to know linux in order to be able to start programming. I have just started learning Python for an MRes I'm doing next year. I have learnt the absolute basics and now like to think of a problem that i could try to solve using it. I suppose it is slightly easier that i have a context in which to think of problems to solve. So what is it you are most interested in doing? Find a project that you may be able to get involved in and take it slowly, understanding why and what you are doing each step of the way.
