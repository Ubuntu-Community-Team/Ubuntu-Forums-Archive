### Post by ClientAlive on 2011-07-01
> **Hwæt said:**
> Thanks! So I guess that's a no-go without a new motherboard. If I buy a new motherboard and put it in, will all the old drives, hard disks, and graphics card just be plug in or will I need to replace some of them?

EDIT- Are the positions of ports on the back of motherboards standard, as well? I want to make sure it will fit in the case


The drives (whether optical or or hard drive, whatever) should just plug in. I think that if the drives are sata the mobo needs to support sata but sata is been around long enough it is really common. As far as expansion cards (NIC, sound card, graphics card, etc) I'm pretty certain they should all just work in any mobo.

Another thing you need to consider is the type of ram you would be putting into that new mobo and the quantity. Motherboards are designed to support both certain types or ram and certain speeds or ram and also certain max quantities of ram. They will a a particular number of "slots" on them for ram to be plugged into and the board itself will be what has to do with how much max, the type, the speed. Some examples of how ram is named/ labeled are: "ddr3 1333 mhz 4X4 GB sticks" or "ddr2 800 mhz 2X512 MB" etc, etc. You need to look on a stick of the ram you have and see if there is a sticker or some other label on it that tells you this info. You may have to google some part number you see on there or something.

So, in summary, the two biggies for compatibility are going to be ran and cpu centered. With ram a few considerations come into play. With cpu, mainly just one (the socket type and whether it accepts the type of cpu you want to put in it). Maybe two if you count bus speed (see below).

Just for kudos, some of the things about a mobo that help determine it's quality and performance are:

FSB (front/side bus speed)
The chipset it has on it
The bios it comes with

Those are the bigger things to look at when evaluating a mobo.

HTH
-----------------

Edit:

By the way, there are some really good, inexpensive mobos out there. Just because it only costs $80 or $100 doesn't mean its slack at all. Just do your homework, pick something out that suits your needs and lay in wait/ watch for a good deal. Newegg and tiger direct aren't too bad sometimes.
-------------------

Edit 2:

If you're going to run Linux on the machine (and I hope you do ;) ) watch out for compatibility issues with the expansion cards. Especially the Broadcom cards are know to be an issue with their NIC cards. After that I think there's a couple graphics cards that can be a challenge. There's workarounds and there's always a way but might be something a person likes to avoid when possible. There are very few that it is an issue though and the ones that are seem to be fairly well known about. Linux is pretty good with drivers and compatibility.
