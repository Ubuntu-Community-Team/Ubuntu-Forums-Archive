### Post by DuckHook on 2013-01-30
It is a new netbook, so it should be able to run whatever you want with it.

As to choice of distro, you must decide based on your own criteria. Will you be maxing the netbook out? If so, then all of the eye-candy of Ubuntu/Kubuntu is at your disposal. Are you more interested in the older-style menu and couldn't care less about 3D desktop effects? Then Xubuntu is better. Do you want to run lean and mean? Try Lubuntu. You may even wish to try completely different distros like Fedora, OpenSUSE, Debian or even something as light as Crunchbang.

Since USB sticks are so cheap and reusable, download everything you are interested in, install to USB and give each distro a testdrive. You will quickly discover which ones are not for you. Whittle them down to the one you like.

### Post by DuckHook on 2013-01-30
Many of the programmer types don't like Ubuntu because they feel the new Unity DE makes it more difficult to find the tools they use. I imagine the same would be said for Kubuntu and KDE. Many on this forum have expressed a preference for Xubuntu because the XFE DE is traditional and makes all apps available through an old-fashioned pull down menu. Lubuntu uses LXDE which has a menu system similar to Windows XP. If you are using for a production machine, then an additional factor is long term support. 12.04 is an LTS whereas 12.10 is not. However, Lubuntu has no LTS, so if this sort of stability is important to you, then by elimination, you've narrowed your choice down to Xubuntu 12.04.

I'm not a programmer, so the above are just observations based on the history of threads in this forum. Would love to hear from the gearheads for their perspective.

### Post by DuckHook on 2013-01-30
As an occasional non-bleeding-edge gamer (mostly simulations) let me give you my perspective:

1. Though I prefer not to use proprietary code, it's a compromise I'm willing to make if it will get me through the day without trouble or bother.
2. Therefore, I've used the proprietary NVidia and ATI drivers on most of my machines. I find the performance gain to be so dramatic that it is impossible to forego.
3. The gain is not just limited to games but also to things like CAD work and vector graphics rendering: anything which relies on the GPU more than the CPU.
4. I've never had a spot of trouble with any of the proprietary drivers I've installed. Even though I try to research thoroughly before buying new cards, often I work with legacy machines that are what they are. The ATI and NVidia drivers have always worked right out of the box upon install.
5. However, this forum is filled with users who have had lots of trouble getting the proprietary drivers to work, or conversely, who have had trouble with the generic nouveau driver and couldn't solve their woes until they installed the proprietary. Yet I don't recall a single such complaint with Intel chips. Such problems are all (or almost all) NVidia or ATI.

Conclusion:

Intel graphics chips are better supported by the native Linux drivers. However, Intel graphics is significantly behind the sheer horsepower of either NVidia or ATI. Since getting the proprietary drivers to run is a one-time pain for ongoing gain, I feel that it's a worthwhile trade. However, if you absolutely never use GPU features, not even for the occasional simulation game, and will never do any CAD or graphics work, then the Intel route will be the least bother.

To answer your question directly: the ATI/AMD graphics chip will not hurt you. If it causes you any initial trouble, this forum is always ready to help.

P.S. the other area where the proprietary vendors can't be beat is in multi-monitor support. I recently splurged on a new machine for Christmas with a beast of an ATI card that is now running 4 monitors (and can run up to six). AFAIK, Intel does not make a graphics card that can do this. However, this is not a consideration in a netbook, so doesn't apply in your case.
