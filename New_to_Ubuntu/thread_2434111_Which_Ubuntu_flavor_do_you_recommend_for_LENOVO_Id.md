### Post by guiverc on 2019-12-30
What DESKTOP best suits your, only you can really decide.


GNOME is not my *cup-of-tea*, but lots of users love it. The software you end up using can influence which will perform best for you, ie. if you want to use Qt (Q toolkit) apps then a Qt DEsktop maybe best, if you want to use GTK+3 apps then a GTK3 based DE will perform better for you (or at least waste less RAM by having two libraries needed in RAM that do the same thing, ie. one for your desktop, one for your app). 

Personal preferences are a huge factor, what DE best suits your workflow (at times I do like gnome, and select gnome at login on my box; though that's rare; I have multiple DEs installed on my system so I can choose at login). 

My best advice is download some flavors, and try before you install.

[https://tutorials.ubuntu.com/tutorial/try-ubuntu-before-you-install#0](https://tutorials.ubuntu.com/tutorial/try-ubuntu-before-you-install#0)

(this link applies to Ubuntu proper with GNOME desktop, but works equally well with any flavor)

I would normally strongly recommend an LTS release, but 18.04 was released before that hardware, and support for it wasn't put through the pipeline in advance, and so it can be a bit of work getting that hardware to work with the older release. 19.10 or the future 20.04 will probably be a smoother ride. 

> I'd like it (the interface, window manager?) to be stable, fast, reasonably power-efficient when surfing the web, watching videos, editing documents, transferring images from phone.  

Gnome is also not my cup of tea. I'd recommend starting with KDE; it's polished and fast, well-integrated, and has configurable bling of the sort that Compiz had. Not having wobbly windows and the desktop cube would feel weird to me, and they're just a checkbox away on KDE. 

I'd also second the recommendation of trying out a bunch of desktop environments from a live USB or in a VM to get a feel for the kinds of thing that are available. Everyone's tastes are different. 

>  Could it also support playing (mild) games? Something like Minecraft or games popular among 10-year olds. 

My much weaker Skylake laptop runs Minecraft fine. Stardew Valley runs on anything, too. Phoronix does linux gaming benchmarks often, and I think they covered the Ryzen integrated graphics. You could see there what performance might be like with games similar to the ones you might be interested in.
