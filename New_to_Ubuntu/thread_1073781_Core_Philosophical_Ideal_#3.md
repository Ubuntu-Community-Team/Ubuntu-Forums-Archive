### Post by DaveLH on 2009-02-18
Hello --

I am a Self-Employed Computer Technician providing support primarily to home users, many of whom are low-income, elderly, and/or disabled.  My goal is to provide computer support that is more friendly, more compassionate, and more in accord with the special needs of my clients than most of the "geek" franchises offer.  For this reason, I am very impressed and excited to read about Ubuntu's vision, especially the third of the "Core Ideals": *"Every computer user should be given every opportunity to use software, **even if they work under a disability**."*

I would be very interested to learn further how Ubuntu can help me better support my low-income/elderly/disabled clients for whom Windows has proved to be in many ways unsatisfactory.  My most immediate concern is a user who is totally blind and would like a computer environment which includes:

-- Software in which *every* command she needs is accessible by keystrokes; no mouse required.
-- A screen reader that *accurately* reads *everything* that is onscreen.
-- A suite of productivity apps (Word Processing, E-mail, Calendar, etc.) that will not eat much into her small income, even if she installs copies on both her desktop and her laptop, and which will adhere to the other requirements above.

If anyone can offer me feedback, such as experiences anyone has had with Ubuntu for elderly/disabled persons, or who can point me towards some good resources, I would appreciate it.

Thanks,

Dave

### Post by avtolle on 2009-02-18
With no experience using the same, Orca is (IIRC) designed to visually handicapped individuals (it is on Ubuntu 8.04 and 8.10, at least) and to my recollection, has a screen reader. As to its accuracy and totality, I don't know; have not done anything with it.

Productivity suites: OpenOffice comes with Ubuntu, no charge; calendar/email; Evolution is default, again no charge. What I'm trying to get at is that the basic "stuff" that comes with Ubuntu has no out-of-pocket license fees, etc., associated wtih the same, again from the Ubuntu philosophy.

Key strokes vs. mouse clicks; I need to think that one through (although when operating from the command line, surely is possible).
