### Post by TSS on 2008-07-13
> **suprsalami said:**
> Thank you hyper_ch, and to WRDN, I use the adobe creative suite CS3 and some morphing apps. there are too many to list but I also read somewhere that you can use windows in ubuntu

You can use Windows in Ubuntu, and you may be thinking of one of two ways.

First, you can use a program called Wine, which does its best to run windows programs.  See the Wine Application Database for programs it can run ([http://appdb.winehq.org/](http://appdb.winehq.org/)).

You can also run Windows in VMware ([http://www.vmware.com/products/player/](http://www.vmware.com/products/player/)) as long as you have a copy of Windows.  This involves running an operating system on top of another operating system, and obviously that involves a performance hit.  

Unless your apps are really, really specific, like IDA Pro, you probably can find at least a passable alternative in Linux.  Switching to Linux usually involves more then switching for what programs you have available to you, it's about what type of freedom you want with your own computer.  What are the reasons why you are thinking about switching to Linux?  There are many good and bad answers to that question, and it is important to figure out which type your answer is :-p.

Well there is no doubt about it that Linux is here to stay.  Ubuntu is a distro that wont be going anywhere anytime soon either.  Obviously I feel that it's worth it in the long run or I wouldn't be using it :-p.

I think what it really comes down to is what you want out of your computer.  Using Linux gives you flexibility and customization simply not available in Windows.  The support community for Linux, and Ubuntu in particular, is fantastic.  This forum can pretty much answer any question that you have, as long as you can give them enough information.  There is no point in lying about it though, Linux does take a little bit of extra effort to understand what is going on with your computer.  That extra effort can go a long way, and will eventually result in you viewing computers in a completely new light.  You will be able to tinker with them if you'd like, optimize your operating system for your hardware, whatever you'd like.  On Linux, the things you can do with your computer are only limited by your time, effort, and creativity.  

If you are not into the whole tinkering, coolness aspect of computers, there are still some practical advantages.  You can update every piece of software on your computer with one click, and usually you don't even have to reboot.  And the software and customer service is all basically free.

Just some food for thought!  Like a few have suggested before, why don't you download the LiveCD and play around?
