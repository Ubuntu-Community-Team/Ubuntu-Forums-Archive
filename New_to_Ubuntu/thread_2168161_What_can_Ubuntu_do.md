### Post by tgalati4 on 2013-08-16
Ubuntu will run on just about any hardware, but you need RAM:  1GB is probably minimum and 2GB is better.  It can do all of those things, but it has a learning curve.

Mac's run a proprietary operating system Max OS X, based on BSD Unix.  There are some similarities, but the graphics environment and user interaction is quite different.

LibreOffice can open your old Office files and you can edit them and save them.

Your surfing speed will depend on your internet connection.  Dial-Up is outdated.

### Post by grahammechanical on 2013-08-16
Ubuntu won't make coffee for you or get you a donut. Leaving that aside Ubuntu does what any OS does and it comes with Libreoffice.

[https://www.libreoffice.org/features/](https://www.libreoffice.org/features/)

Couple of tips. During the installation you will be asked to confirm that you have enough disk space and are connected to the Internet. You will see a box that you can tick to install third party software. Tick the box. Included in third party software are the free to download Microsoft core fonts set. At some point you will need to tick that you accept Microsoft's licence. In this way your Office documents may have the same fonts in Ubuntu as in XP.

A second tip is to open your Office documents in Libreoffice and then copy and paste them into a new Libreoffice document which you can then save as a Libreoffice document. You can then easily convert them to open source fonts and it is goodby to Microsoft.

In regard to hardware apart from the power of the CPU and the amount of RAM (as much as you can afford of both) think also about the graphic card. Again it should be as powerful as is reasonable with as much of its own memory as possible under the circumstances. That is if you are thinking of buying new. 

I am running the very latest Ubuntu on an Intel Core 2 Duo 2.4 GHz with 1 GB Ram and an Nvidia GT 220 with 1 GB of memory. None of this was top of the range 6 years ago when I brought it. Today, if I was planning to build another machine I would want to do a bit better than that but not because Ubuntu demands it.

### Post by r_avital on 2013-08-16
Hi Peter, welcome,

Regarding MS-Office files -- Ubuntu by default installs the "Libre office" suite, formerly known as Sun's Open Office, which is compatible with MS-Word, MS-Excel, and the MS-PowerPoint.  They open MS-compatible documents eith extensions like .doc, .docx, .xls, etc., and save into those same formats as well as their own native formats.  As a bonus, export to PDF is also available.  As far as MS-Access (since you mentioned databases), there may be a Jet-compatible engine somewhere, but I haven't seen one.  Same for an ODBC layer.  However, the free version of My-SQL is part of the Ubuntu distribution, and I've been able to manipulae MS database files with it (forget about MS-Access forms, reports, VBA/Macros, strictly SQL DML/DDL etc).  I've even been able to install and work with an Oracle engine in ubuntu (yuk).

Hardware-wise, the installation of the current version (13.04 a.k.a. Raring Ringtail) recommends a minimum of 5.6 GB or so of available disk space, and I believe 2 GB of RAM as a minimum.  Graphics and sound cards compatibilities used to be difficult, but are much easier to configure now, almost everything works by default.

One thing you need to be aware of, coming form the world of Windows;  Windows has one single "Window Manager" which is the single desktop environment.  You have themes that you can play with all day, but the metaphor of launching windows for apps that have their own menus, the task bar, the system tray, "shortcuts" on the desktop etc., is always they same.  Different colors, fonts etc,. but the functionality is the same.

In the Ubuntu world, you have your choice of the default Ubuntu, gnome-shell, xubuntu, xfce, Kubuntu (ubuntu with KDE), cairo-dock, cinnamon, mate (pronounced mah-tay, as in the South-American bitter brew "Yerba Mate"), and probably something else I'm forgetting.  Don't let all these names overwhelm or confuse you, it's just lingo, the point is they offer alternative ways to do the same things, to suit different tastes or preferences.  They each install and uninstall separately, and at login-time you will have your choice of which of these "sessions" you want to use.  [I'm deliberately leaving out the "gnome-fallback" environments, because if you did not know the old gnome and never worked with it, it won't mean much to you, and there's no indication of how long anything called "fallback" will be supported in the future.  But they're available as well.]

Most important, don't be afraid to experiment with any part of it to the point of breaking it.  Because that's the best way to learn.  The fact that all of the above let you burn a "live CD" or DVD means you can boot into one, or into the other, and test it to your heart's content, and use the same optical media to install on your system.  You could also make installations on USB drives/sticks, if they're large enough, which will in fact allow you some permanence, saving your settings, documents, etc.

Have fun!

### Post by Mark Phelps on 2013-08-17
> Can Ubuntu bring up all my old Microsoft Office files?

Truthful answer is -- it depends on the files.  Office files created before Office 2007, or current files created using the non-xml format will generally work OK -- but these are limited to Word, Excel, and Powerpoint files.  Office files created using the xml formats don't work well, especially the Word docx files.

Also, Office is a very large set of apps, and the full suite includes things like Visio, Project, and Access -- NONE of which work well in Linux (some don't even work at all).

Your best bet is to look up details on the Office "equivalent" apps and see what file formats they import.
