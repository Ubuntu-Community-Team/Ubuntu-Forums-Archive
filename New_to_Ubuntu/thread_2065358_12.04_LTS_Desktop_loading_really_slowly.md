### Post by houseworkshy on 2012-10-02
The gimp is my favorite for image manipulation, however there is another way  of easily finding and posting system specs.  You could install a program  called "sysinfo" which is in the standard repositories.  Use any of the  graphic installers or "sudo apt-get install sysinfo" in the terminal.
Once it's in, open it and choose "save file" from the file menu and  decide where to put it, your desktop probably, this will make a text  file with your hardware specs, then just attach that.  In 10.04 a link  is put in the Applications>System Tools menu.  In 12.04 I'm not sure  but entering "sysinfo" in a terminal will open it anyway.

Another question you've been asked is about your boot.  There is  another tool with a graphic interphase, details on this can be found  here [https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Boot-Repair](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/Boot-Repair).   I'm not suggesting you use it to do things, don't in this case, but it  does have a diagnostic button.  You could use this to find and post an  answer to what is booting up.
A look at your boot log may be useful too, you can find it in /var/log with the other log files.  Use the log viewer or you could use "gksu gedit" to look at them, gksu because they are in an admin area.  Some of the other logs may have something interesting in them too, 'errors' for example.
This would save fiddling around with image files and hosting services.

The command "top" entered in the termial will give you an idea of any  processes which are eating up CPU.  It will output into the terminal  window.  Watch it run for a while to in case you are getting quick  blasts of high use from some process or other.  If you are make a note  of which one it is.   Then enter "q" to stop top running and exit back  to the command entry cursor.   You can then just copy and paste top's  last output from the terminal window.
I think Unity and Gnome3 use a bit more RAM than gnome2.  It could be that you were on the cusp RAM-wise.  What does "free" entered in the terminal show?  The swappiness could be a factor too.  

This is a wild afterthough.  A chugging machine can be a symptom of heat  putting the resistance up, often due insulating layers of dirt.  On the  improbable chance that your upgrade happened to coincide with dust  getting into the box, you could check the temperature.  
If you install "lm-sensors", which is another command from the standard  repositories so just install it in any of the usual ways, you could then  find out how warm the chip is with "sensors" entered into the terminal.   Don't panic if the result is dramatically higher than the room  temperature your machine is in, it usually is.  
Sysinfo will have told you what your chipset is so search for the  temperture range recommended by the manufacturers.  If you are at the  top end of the range maybe a clean would sort things out.  It would be  an amazing coincidence but such things can happen.  
Always worth taking an occasional peek at temperature from time to time  anyway to foresee, and prevent,  dirt induced low performance and   fry-ups.
Only diagnostic stuff as I'm not familliar with gnome3 either which is why some of it's commands.  Bless them for not depreciating the familliar so much as GUI's do.  Hope some of it helps.
