### Post by decoherence on 2009-07-02
Linux is a multi-user system because it was designed to do the following

1. Allow a single computer's resources to be shared amongst many users.
2. Allow those users to work in a private, secure fashion. In other words, User A does not know what User B is doing, can't read their files and so on.

All of the popular operating systems these days are multi-user: Linux, Mac OS X and Windows.

The last single-user systems to be widely used were Mac OS 9 and Windows ME. While they had support for user-specific logins, they were not designed with the above two goals in mind.

Hope that explains it!

### Post by mcduck on 2009-07-06
..and just to clear things a bit, there is no limit to the amount of users. Linux/Unix system can have hundreds or thousands of users at the same time. :)

Unix has always been the system used for mainframes and central computer systems, in the times when computers were too large and expensive for everybody to have a personal computer on their desktop. So companies and large organizations had a single large computer and a terminal for every user. Linux is very close to Unix and works inn the same way, so there's decades worth of background in multiuser/multitasking systems (while at the same time DOS was singleuser/single tasking and Windows has only recently gained multiuser abilities and still doesn't do it very well).

It's actually possible to plug in multiple keyboards, mouses and monitors to a Linux system and that way share the hardware to make separate workstations for many users (this kind of setup is often called "hydra")..

And the central computer idea has raised it's head again recently, at least here in Finland there are now quite many schools that have discarded the idea of having hundreds of PC's and instead use a single server machine running Linux, with lots of thin clients that users can use to connect to the main computer. Pretty neat idea, as you can walk to any machine and have your personal desktop and all your files available, and the staff doesn't need to maintain hundreds of computers, just one. :)
