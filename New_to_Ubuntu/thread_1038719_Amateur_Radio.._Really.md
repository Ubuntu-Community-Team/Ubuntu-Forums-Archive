Exactly.  I hold an Extra Class Amateur Radio license, and have been licensed since 1964 (at that time, my call was WN/WA9VPF...it is now KG5UC).

Amateur, or Ham, radio may not be what you think.  You are not allowed to broadcast, except under certain circumstances, and you're never allowed to broadcast music.  It is a communication medium, not unlike a bulletin board or IRC/IM chat channels on the Internet.  In fact, some of our bulletin and message forwarding is partially done over the internet.

Ham radio is very useful for emergency communications.  Ham radio is used for a lot of emergency communication during emergencies and natural disasters.  It is a radio experimenter's hobby, covering frequencies from just below the AM Broadcast bands to far up in the UHF and SHF bands.

Hams also experiment with various modes of transmission, from the simple voice and morse code transmissions to many digital modes of communication, from simple ASCII to CLOVER.

It's not extremely hard to get a license anymore.  The FCC has recently dropped all morse code requirements for any grade of license, and you only need to demonstrate the ability to use and maintain the radio equipment.

In my day, the tests were far more difficult, and for my class of license, you were required to pass a 20 wpm morse code test (which I passed...at my peak I sent and received well over 40 wpm).

Is Ham and CB close to same thing or are they WAY different?

Ham radio and CB are somewhat the same, but Ham radio is more technical and tilted towards technical and experimental aspects of radio communication, as well as providing a community service in times of emergencies.

While the CB band can be used by businesses for business communication, Ham radio cannot.  CB radios are FCC approved (called Type Accepted) for legal operation on the CB band.  Ham radios are not Type Accepted, and may be varied and modified.  The Ham radio operator is responsible for their legal operation.

As such, CB radios may be modified to operate on Ham bands, but Ham radios may not be modified to operate on the CB band.  Once a CB radio is modified, it is no longer Type-Accepted for the CB bands and may not be operated there.

You can only use AM (Amplitude Modulated, with a carrier frequency) or SSB (Single Side Band, with the carrier and one side band filtered out) voice communication on CB radio, while the sky's the limit on types of communication on Ham Radio.  Computer digital communications, FM (Frequency Modulation), Pulse Modulation, Digital Spread Spectrum; whatever you want to experiment with.

You need a license to transmit on the Amateur Radio Bands.  The tests required for your license will demonstrate that you understand the legal operation of Amateur radio trasnitting equipment on the Amateur frequencies.  You may listen and monitor freely, as long as you have a receiver that will receive the frequencies and the ability to receive the modes of communication required.

Some of the programs in the Repos will aid anyone who wishes to monitor and listen in on such communication.  No license is required as long as you don't transmit on those bands.  Some of the programs do require a license, as they are specifically used for transmission.  And some of them would be useless to you unless you had a license, since they aid in activities in which you would be involved running a Ham radio station.

And a few of them are virtually useless.  The morse code training software is a prime example.  Unless you wish to communicate using morse code (the absolute original method of communication way years ago), passing a morse code test for your license is no longer required, so the practice programs are useless for what they were originally created for (i.e., passing the code test)

However, I personally really enjoyed communicating using morse code.  As I said, at my peak I was sending and receiving over 40 wpm.  Back in the early '90s I was driving 18 wheelers over the road, and would communicate using morse code while driving down the highway.  I copied morse code that fast in my head, just like listening to speech.

Before I was ever on the internet, I was sending and receiving messages and bulletins over the Amateur Packet Radio network, kind of like a large forwarding bulletin board network.  Packet radio and digital communications over the radio were the things that I were into, as well as morse communications, and antennas were something that I specialized in.

The ARRL link provided by Jim is the American Radio Relay League, which is an organization that has been around since the very early days of Amateur Radio communications.  I found a much more abbreviated and concise explanation of what Ham Radio is at [Wikipedia.]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amateur_radio")  That page gives a brief synopsis of what Ham radio is, and some history behind it.

If you're interested in the hobby, then look for a local Amateur Radio club in your area.  They will be more than happy to get you started and to help you out.

A CB radio and a HAM radio are two entirely different animals in terms of FCC licensing and usage.

OFF LINUX TOPIC explaination;

A HAM radio user MUST carry a valid FCC license with assigned call letters such as WKMH, KSOC, W5RGT, etc., which would allow them to transmit on certain frequencies. There are certain levels of "frequencies" available to different licenses. The person (kg) that posted earlier has an EXTRA class license will be able to broadcast on frequencies that a "beginner" class license would not be able to broadcast on. You can obtain "higher" classes of licenses as your proficiency in radio communications increases and you are able to pass the test for that license. There are MANY different frequencies available to broadcast on. FM broadcast radio stations will broadcast in the ranges of 88 Mhz - 108 Mhz. AM stations licensees will broadcast in their assigned frequency range, which is in the Khz range. HAM radio frequencies can be on the 146 Mhz or can be in the 3 Ghz frequencies. The space shuttle broadcasts their transmissions back to NASA on a certain frequency that a lot of HAM radios can receive and listen to.

A CB radio user is not required to have a license. But, they are unable to legally broadcast over a certain wattage of power. This limits it's effective useage range. CB was widely used in the 70''s for cross communications between truck drivers and others that used the highways as a means of earning money. It has since abated in use, but is still used.
I forget how many channels there are for CB set aside by the FCC for that type of radio. Anyone can go to a local truck stop or Radio Shack and purchase one, wire it into their vehicle or take it home and power it up and begin transmitting.

### Post by kencoe on 2009-02-01
If you are looking for a little info on what HAM radio can do, I suggesst checking out [http://www.wedothat-radio.org/](http://www.wedothat-radio.org/) on the "interweb" (I just love saying that).

As far as a few comments on this thread, here's the short of it; or my 2 cents...

Yes there is software that can basically turn a soundcard into a "software radio," but you cannot use it to **broadcast** communications. In a non-technical way of explaining this; ham radio is designed for point to point, direct, communications between two licensed radio amateurs. You cannot broadcast to the general public. There are caveats to this, but the basic rule stands.

Yes a CB is the same thing as a HAM radio (it is actually generally an 11 meter rig). It is in a radio frequency now considered open public spectrum, hence the name "citizens band radio." It does not require a license anymore in many countries, but does come under the same rules of operation (in the US that is FCR [Part 97]("http://www.arrl.org/FandES/field/regulations/news/part97/")). 

No, morse code is no longer required, [but it IS a very important thing to learn]("http://www.qsl.net/wy3a/Why_Morse.htm"). The reason it was used originally was that it was an extremely robust communications method which often works when nothing else will. It is also used very commonly for ID info on devices called "repeaters", and is used for long distance and low power communications.

Hopes this helps answer a couple of questions...
