### Post by bredman on 2010-05-11
Reading a book or a tutorial will only help so much. Real life experience is much better.

Luckily, you have real life in front of you. Keep an eye on the Ubuntu forums like this one and watch the commands that users recommend.

For each new command, read the manpage for the command. For example if somebody recommends to use the command ls -tl, use the command 'man ls' to read the manual, and figure out for yourself why the -t and -l were recommended in this case. Figuring it out for yourself will burn the information into your brain. It will also make you more confident reading manpages. Until you can read a manpage you will always need somebody to hold your hand.

Note: To exit from the man command, press the q key.

A word of warning. As you gain confidence, you may be be tempted to help others when you don't really understand the consequences. A little knowledge can be very dangerous.

You might want to take a look here for some things NOT TO DO.

[http://ubuntuforums.org/announcement.php?f=326](http://ubuntuforums.org/announcement.php?f=326)

Before you try anything you may get from the web (that is, you don't know the author from Adam), it might be worth your while to ask here before you run it.  The commands you find on trusted sites, like from your video card OEM or Sun or other well-known sites are also likely to be safe.  Safe.  That does not mean that they always work as you expect them to.

When suggestions are made here, you can be fairly confident that they are not malicious.  The Forum Staff does a good job of sifting malicious commands out.  However, that does not mean that someone might not make a typo in a suggested command.

Sometimes it is best to wait a while to give others a chance to look at a suggested series of commands before you charge off and issue them in the terminal right away.

### Post by lolzwut on 2010-05-11
Honestly, reading online command cheat sheets is not what I would recommend. I don't claim to be an expert or anything but all the commands I learned was just from reading books. If you try to learn commands by reading a big list of them chances are you'll forget them. Not every linux command is really practical anyway. You have to narrow it down to suit your needs, some commands you'll probably never use while some you may use all the time. Either way just reading them and trying to memorize what they do is pretty pointless. Running into a situation when you need to use those commands is the best way. So I recommend reading a book & trying out some of the examples in it. And just use linux a lot. The more you use it the more often you'll need to use the terminal.

### Post by bredman on 2010-05-11
Don't get too hung up on the command line. Being a wizard in a terminal is no longer the only way to strut your stuff.

Modern distros bring a whole new outlook to Linux. In some cases, being a modern Linux guru means that you have to know your way around the GUIs too. I have lots of tricks and tweaks for Ubuntu that I only do through the GUI, I dont care how to do them on the command line. Other concepts in Linux, for example directory structures, are easier for beginners to understand in a visual environment.

So keep your mind open and learn all about Linux, not just the command line. If you focus too much on the command line, you may find that modern Linux has left you behind.
