### Post by DocWu on 2008-07-27
The easiest way I can think of is to take the cover off your case and look at the label on the PS. The power supply doesn't interact with the motherboard in any way that would communicate any model information, so it can't tell you much about the power supply.

If there is no label, or it's fallen off, you're going to have to guess, or just assume it's too small and replace it. Many power supplies that come with a case are pretty cheap anyway and even if they are rated high enough in power, often don't deliver. 

You'll rest easier with a good quality, name brand power supply that you know can handle your system. You might as well install one while you're upgrading things.

On the other hand, you can just go ahead and try to use what you have. If it's underpowered you might experience unexpected shutdowns or reboots or other odd prblems. Then you'll know if it's big enough...

 That would be nice but I don't think any Power Supply on the market has a feature that allows it to communicate with the system.

 I'm afraid your only option is as hrod beraht said, open the case and look at the sticker on the Power Supply


**edit:** there are some good power supply calculators out there although I can't find one at the moment. Keep in mind even though you don't need say 500 Watts it doesn't mean you're using 500 Watts, your system uses what it's drawing. If you need a minimum of 200W I'd get a 300W and even then you have to consider a 300W power supply is not going to be capable of delivering 300W since you have to consider losses due to heat and inefficient components, I'd add a good 25% margin of error to the size you will need.
