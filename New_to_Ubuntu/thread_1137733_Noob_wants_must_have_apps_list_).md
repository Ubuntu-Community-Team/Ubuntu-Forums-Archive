### Post by bennachie on 2009-04-25
Nice summary, and all the packages listed work well. You can stick to Skype if you want (not open-source, but it is free, and readily available). If you don't use Outlook for mail, you might prefer to try Mozilla Thunderbird which, of course, is both free and cross-platform (that can be convenient for moving settings and archived items from Windows to Ubuntu). In a similar manner, Evolution can now import Outlook data, and has generally become a pretty solid package.

Ubuntu comes with, or can access, open source and/or proprietary drivers for most printers and scanners. However, if, like me, you use a scanner a lot, you might like to note that VueScan (although not free) is, like Thunderbird, cross-platform, and that your existing licence will be valid for both Windows and Linux (so long as you're not using more than one instance at any one time).

If you care to nominate any more specific Windows packages that are important to you, you'll probably be inundated with good advice as to suitable equivalents.

That's one thing you'll notice now, the number of choices you have. You didn't really say what sort of applications you use and are looking for but I suggest you perhaps taking time reading through the packages in the repositories, you'll be amazed how much is in there. That's the great thing with OSS, if you don't particularly like an app or it doesn't do what you need,  there's no need to persevere with it, just look for something else that works for you (takes time I suppose to try different programs and deal with the learning curves, but worth it in the end as you can find stuff you really like using and which suit the way you work).

Open Office will look after your wordprocessing, spreadsheets etc., or if you don't need that level of functionality, then you can use Abiword. I use another app called Notecase 'coz it's a lightweight app similar in function to OneNote in MS. 

You've got a heap of audio and video players to pick from, same for email clients, chat, browsers et al. 

As I said, if you want to do something on your computer, you can be pretty sure someone else has wanted to do it as well, and has probably even created some open source software to do it with. I find the forum here excellent for pointing me in the direction of applications I'm looking for (and I've even found some I'd have had no idea about, let alone used, but I learned of them here :) ).

And glad to hear you've found it a good experience making the switch, I'm guessing you won't regret it once you give Linux a fair try out.

### Post by s3a on 2009-04-25
If you'd like Ubuntu to be even faster click Applications-->Accessories-->Terminal and type **sudo apt-get install preload**.

What it does is it judges what you tend to use the most and preloads that in your RAM so that when you choose to start your applications, they will launch even faster!
