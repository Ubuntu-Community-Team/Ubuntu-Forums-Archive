### Post by JKyleOKC on 2011-01-20
A little gadget I bought quite a while ago has proved to be quite valuable in troubleshooting and in backing up data. It's a USB-to-IDE adapter, which has a cable with a USB plug on one end and the adapter on the other end, plus a small power supply. The adapter plugs into the 40-pin socket of a PATA/IDE hard drive. You then connect the power, power the drive up, and plug the USB into a USB socket on your machine. This is all with the drive out in the open, not in any machine. 

If the drive is any good, you'll get it automatically mounted and all of the partitions it contains will be displayed, each in its own File Manager window. You can copy files to and from the drive by simply dragging them to where you want them, and dropping them there.

When I scrap a machine, I pull its hard drive and any other salvageable parts before putting it out for the scavengers to swipe. Currently I have four of them on my shelves. I keep them for the old data they contain, and use their free space to hold backups.

I recently bought another adapter that does the same thing for SATA drives, so I'm now able to use those also, even on machines that do not support SATA natively. The adapter provides the compatibility. I got both of mine from Cyberguys, a mail-order outlet in California that has all sorts of unusual gadgets for us geeks; they were around $30 each, plus shipping costs. Google for the address; it's well worth scanning their web pages.

### Post by JKyleOKC on 2011-01-20
I don't see anything there that should cause a loss of video sync, unless it might be the attempts to get rid of the Google stuff. Sometimes when removing stuff, the package managers (Synaptic, apt-get, dpkg, all have the same back end) go way too far and remove all of the desktop packages including the X-server. That will leave you with a black screen and a command-line prompt, but ought not get the "no sync" message (that's done by the monitor itself, not by the computer, and indicates that the computer isn't sending valid video signals to it, or at least it's not getting any). That might be something like a bent pin in one of the connectors...

When I'm removing stuff using Synaptic, I always click the "details" button after I click "apply" and study the list of packages that will be removed. Every now and then I find it has gone overboard, so I cancel out of the "apply" dialog and unmark the package.

As for the gadget when connected to a bad drive, what happens is simply nothing at all. If nothing appears on screen within a minute or two, the drive is probably dead. You can open a Terminal window and type "mount" to see if the drive is listed at all.

I've not yet used it to connect to a totally blank drive, although I do have a 1-TB SATA drive still in its original box (a thank-you from one of my data recovery customers) that I plan to try it with one of these days.

### Post by JKyleOKC on 2011-01-22
Yep, it can be lying out in the open, if the cables are long enough. Just be sure that no metal object is touching the exposed circult board on the bottom of the drive. I usually put a sheet of paper down and set the drive on that, when using the USB adapter gadget. Avoid plastic, especially in winter, since it can build up a static electricity charge and zap the chips on the board -- which kills the drive.

"No sync" doesn't necessarily mean a bad drive. It could be a bad video card (or integrated video circuits on the motherboard). If you have a spare or known good video card to plug in temporarily you can check for that. Just be very careful about static electricity when handling cards.

Cyberguys used to sell a "grounding power cord" that looked like an ordinary power cord except it was green, that had only one wire in it -- the ground connection. You could replace the normal cord with this one, plug it in, and the box would be grounded (if your electric socket was properly installed, not always true in older buildings). I always intended to get one but never did, and it's not in their catalog any more. I just take extreme care when handling circuit boards and drives, and so far I've been lucky. Only killed one system by static discharge and that was a TRS-80 Model 3 almost 30 years ago!

### Post by audiomick on 2011-01-25
Hi Chris. You've been having fun, haven't you...;)

Jim's tip about intermittent connections in cables is good. That can be a very frustrating problem that is very difficult to isolate. The worst of it is that when you start to look for the fault, it doesn't show up.

If you suspect a dodgy cable, you can do the following:
Check that all the connectors are properly connected, as you said yourself.

Take a hold of the cable, one hand on the connector and one on the cable behind the connector, and move the cable around whilst trying to hold the connector as still as possible. Do that for each connector to see if you can  provoke the fault into showing up.

When you have found a suspect connector, try moving the connentor in its' socket in comparison to moving the cable in relation to the connector to try and see if the problem is the contact between connector and socket, or if there is a broken wire inside the connector.

Intermittent connections are nearly always inside the connector, or at the clamp where the cable is held into the housing of the connector. If you have a broken wire somewhere along the length of the cable, you can nearly always see physical damage on the cable.

Don't be too gentle when doing this. If the cable is good, you are unlikely to damage it, if you do cause a fault with these tests, it was probably going to happen soon anyway.

edit: Oh yeah. Periodically I encounter a machine that has a problem that can't be explained. Sometimes just turning it off and on, or even just fooling around with a bit, will make the problem go away. It is as if machines need attention like small children do, and sulk when they don't get it... ;)
