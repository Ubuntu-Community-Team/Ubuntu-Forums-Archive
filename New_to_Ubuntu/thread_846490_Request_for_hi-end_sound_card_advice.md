### Post by bluepowder7 on 2008-07-01
some motherboards (i've only used ASUS since the mid-90's) have a connector on the motherboard that sends out the actual digital audio signal, though that's typically just the plain 2-channel (Left + Right) stuff.  S/PDIF is Sony/Philips Digital Interface, same as CD players, and most likely to be there (if anything IS there).  AES is Audio Engineering Society, and it's the professional digital standard like you'd see in music studios.

anyhoo, find out if your motherboard has an S/PDIF (spdif, digital, whatever else they call it) output, and if you're OK with just the regular left-n-right stuff, then find out how to connect to it, and send it to a separate DAC (Digital-to-Analog(ue) Converter).  being a hobby musician, i already had a DAC (a Behringer Ultramatch-Pro, costs around $200, maybe less), which handles a whole lot of different input / output signal formats.  being a stand-alone piece of hardware, linux / ubuntu doesn't need drivers for it - the audio chipset on the motherboard just routes it like it always did.

for added fun, i also have a 4-channel headphone amplifier that's housed in my home-made monitor stand.  it's yet another piece of pro-audio gear, and it lets me send the analog signal to either a pair of headphones, or to a pair of speakers, or both, or patch in my portable mp3 player, or mute everything, or mute just the left or right channel, or....  it's a Behringer Powerplay Pro-XL if you wanna see what i'm talking about.
