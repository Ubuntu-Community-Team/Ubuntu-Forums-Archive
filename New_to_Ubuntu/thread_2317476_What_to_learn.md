### Post by goofprog on 2016-03-16
This was my problem in college.  I learned some linux and wanted to go into programming so I tried some webdev and some other stuff.  It is too broad.  I suggest just to write maybe three things down to specialize in.  Three things not one.  Everything else should fall into place for your goals that you set.  Maybe just not create extra projects on top of college work too.  That is also a discipline.  The projects can be saved for when college work calls for a project of interest and it will motivate you more and you will get college credit for it too.

### Post by ian-weisser on 2016-03-16
Follow your interests, the rest will follow naturally.
If you are interested in wheelbarrows, then look at wheelbarrow-related applications. What do they do? What languages are they written in? What are the issues those projects are having?
Those answers are what you should learn about.

Following the path of those answers may take you different places - coding, triaging, leadership, documentation, translating, packaging, systemd, dbus, networking, graphics, installer...who knows
 Enjoy the experience and make friends.

### Post by ian-weisser on 2016-03-16
'an apprenticeship in web development' means you want to create pretty websites and their database backends?
If so, then learning the databases and languages and how to put them together is appropriate.

If you enjoy installing LAMP and tinkering with infrastructure more, then web servers and networking and the LAMP stack variations are appropriate.

The code is imporant, but your ability to communicate with other humans (like clients), to understand what they are saying...and not saying, to collaborate, to lead or follow are just as important. A fabulous coder who cannot follow direction or collaborate is not worth hiring. In many higher-salary-bracket careers, communication and relationship-building are more valued than technical skills.
