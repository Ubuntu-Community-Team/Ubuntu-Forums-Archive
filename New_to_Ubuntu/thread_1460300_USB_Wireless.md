### Post by ScarySquirrel on 2010-04-23
Short Answer: [NETGEAR WG111v2]("http://kb.netgear.com/app/products/model/a_id/2557")

Boring Answer:
I have done this a lot recently. There are several sites where you can check device compatibility. Read on to find out how you pick a piece of hardware for linux compatibility, in three not-easy :( steps: 

0. Look up the history of companies and their linux support. For example, when picking a video card, look into the history of NVIDIA, ATI, and their old linux loving competitor, 3dfx. 

1. Find out, generally, some good brand names. For example, with wireless routers, a name that comes up a lot for linux is Linksys. 

2. Finally, pick a few cheap products offered by this company, maybe also one other, and check their GNU friendliness on web sites for to this, like those to which these link [start with those about to usb wireless adapters]: 
[[COLOR="Blue"]Adapters List[/COLOR]]("http://www.seattlewireless.net/ClientAdapters/802.11g")
[[COLOR="Blue"]Linux Wireless LAN Support Online Checker[/COLOR]]("http://linux-wless.passys.nl/")
[[COLOR="Blue"]linux-drivers.org[/COLOR]]("http://www.linux-drivers.org/")
[[COLOR="Blue"]Official Linux Wireless Wiki[/COLOR]]("http://wireless.kernel.org/")

Good luck. You're going to need it.

The brand wont tell you much. All these brands rely on chipsets made by companies such as atheros, broadcom and many others. Its the chipset that will define how well the device works with linux, but it nearly impossible to tell that from the package (unless it actually mentions linux compatibilty, but dont hold your breath for that)

Best way is probably checking here:
[http://linux-wless.passys.nl/](http://linux-wless.passys.nl/)

You definitely want one with kernel support
