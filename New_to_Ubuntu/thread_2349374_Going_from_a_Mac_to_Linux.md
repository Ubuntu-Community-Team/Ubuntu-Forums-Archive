### Post by DuckHook on 2017-01-16
@shanebunting

I've been a Linux user for years, but came into the OS much the way you did, seeking refuge from the high cost of Apple/Windows. I no longer use Windows at all. It is turned on every month or so inside a virtual machine so that I can waste a few hours on an old game that won't run on WINE, but I have found that I no longer need Windows for *anything* (and I used to be a Windows power-user). But despite considering myself a Linux enthusiast, a mild caution:

The "freedom" that Linux offers, though real enough, also has an associated cost: you will need to unlearn old habits and learn new ones. Linux is a very different beast from MacOS or Windows and some of the accepted ways of doing things in those OSes will get you into trouble in Ubuntu. Example: installing things from outside the repositories. Moreover, as much as various FOSS developers have tried their best to make documents interoperable, the reality is that documents generated by LibreOffice, GIMP, Inkscape, etc will never be completely compatible with their proprietary counterparts. Following a variant of Murphy's Law, any such breakage will usually occur the evening before that big presentation you have to make to your assembled management team/professors.

You need to go into this with a realistic idea of what is involved. These forums are filled with people who blithely install Ubuntu on their systems only to find themselves in trouble, or with some element of their install dysfunctional. Although it *can* be a painless process, it frequently is not, and this happens often enough that you shouldn't assume that things will go just peachy. And when they don't, you won't have some form of technical support to turn to except community-based help like these forums.

The idea that Linux is free as in beer is attractive, but the reality is that what you save in upfront cost will have to be invested in the form of time. Both time learning, and every now and again, time solving.

I encourage you to read the link in my sig: *Linux is not Windows*. Don't fall into the trap of thinking that it is.

Having stated the above caveats, I can say that my life with Linux has been incredibly rewarding. I've discovered a world of power and possibilities that was closed off to me back in the proprietary days. For some, these freedoms have a meaningless impact on their lives, but for me, it's been intellectually and philosophically satisfying, in addition to the sheer use that I get out of this OS.
