### Post by Rocket2DMn on 2011-02-04
Ubuntu is a completely standalone operating system.  You can set it up to dual boot with another, but during the install phase you can select to install it using the entire disk, which would wipe the Windows XP partition that is currently there.  You will probably want to get the latest stable release version, which at this time is Ubuntu 10.10 Maverick Meerkat.  Download site: [http://www.ubuntu.com/desktop/get-ubuntu/download](http://www.ubuntu.com/desktop/get-ubuntu/download)

There are a lot of websites out there with screenshots of Ubuntu, either for use during setup, or just informative and fun screenshots.  You can see some screenshots of Ubuntu with applications here - [http://www.ubuntu.com/desktop/features](http://www.ubuntu.com/desktop/features)
There are many more on the web, and a simple search should find you some more. Other forum members may have their own favorite sites as well.

Welcome to Ubuntu!

### Post by Tyggna on 2011-02-04
From my experience, here's what I have to offer:

Ubuntu makes a very good effort to be usable by everyone.  If you consider yourself not very tech saavy, than Ubuntu may be the best thing that ever happened to you.  It'll take a bit of struggle and probably about two months with help from the people on the forums to get it working the way you want it to--but after that it'll ALWAYS work the way you want it to.  You'll be done with the days of your computer crashing for no reason, and you won't have to do another OS reinstall ever again.  All your software will be free and readily available, and it'll be awesome on every count.

If you consider yourself tech saavy, then switching over to Ubuntu can pose a pretty big challenge.  It'll take about an hour a day for about a month and a half to really learn the ins and outs and get it the way you like--but when you're done you'll wonder why you didn't do that years ago.

If you could go work on tech support for Windows tomorrow, then you're really gonna struggle.  You'll try to do things the "windows way" and get frustrated when nothing (or very little) happens.  You'll be constantly trying to reboot your computer when something bad happens, and make a good number of mistakes that will be difficult to fix (often resulting in you feeling like reinstalling Ubuntu would be the quickest way to fix it).  You'll fight with your OS for about a month, and eventually things will click.  When the dust settles, and the pieces start coming together, you'll realize that computers are fun again and your life will never be the same.


This isn't true for everyone--granted--this is just what I've noticed.  If you've had experience with Macs then it usually is a very easy transition to make.

### Post by stderr on 2011-02-04
Welcome

Honestly, the best way to satisfy your curiosity in this respect is to give it a whirl and see what you think. It really depends what your background is, what you're looking for in an operating system, what you want to do with it, etc, as to how you'll find the experience. For many people, a world of free software that can all be installed at the drop of a hat can completely change their perspective on computing; others may bump into an odd hardware issue during installation, get frustrated and give up before even seeing the interface. C'est la vie. I'd very much advise giving it a go though - you're highly unlikely to regret trying it, at least.

With regard to how it looks - like many Linux distributions, it can look like moreorless anything you like depending on what you install and how you configure it, hence why people are reluctant to post screenshots. Googling something like "ubuntu maverick screenshots" should give you a bit of an idea, but do remember that Linux is incredibly customisable...

As Rocket2DMn says, you can set up a dual-boot setup with your existing Windows installation, or just overwrite your Windows installation entirely. Alternatively, you can follow nothingspecial's advice and give a "live CD" a go first to get a taste, without modifying your computer at all. 

And do post any questions you may have throughout the process here - it's a very beginner-friendly community!
