### Post by Daveski on 2008-05-07
Distros are all about what is 'pre-configured', what is in the 'approved' repo's, and what the community is like. Stick any flavour of Linux on your PC and customise at will. A distro is about getting a set of apps, utils and libraries with a little pre-config in the mix and a group of people to make sure it all hangs together.

### Post by Iowan on 2008-05-07
Different distros (might) use different package management schemes (RPM's, etc). Some are permutations (Mint>Ubuntu>Debian). Some have been configured for specific purposes (firewall) or for specific hardware (Pupeee).  Some are designed for security, some for configurability, some for ease of use.  I saw a joke somewhere that announced that the number of distros now exceeds the number of users.

### Post by Dougie187 on 2008-05-07
There are also differences that are based on what the developing community thinks of as "stable" Some distros might not package up to date software or keep their repos up to date because they don't consider they stable. One good example of this is Centos. It uses firefox 1.5.0.12 (at least I am told it is default and not in their repos) because they consider it to be stable. But in reality the main difference is preference. Some people sit at home and think "Man it would be cool if ubuntu did this, or if fedora did that" and then they decide to come up with a new distro and make it work to their liking.
