### Post by Scheater5 on 2008-10-28
There is an unfortunate ambiguity in the word "free."  There is free as in no cost (often time referred to as "gratis," or "free as in beer"), and there is free as in freedom (often referred to as "libre," or "free as in speech").  
Software that's "free" may be one or the other or both of these.  Software that is free as in speech is indeed sometimes not free of charge (take Red Hat Linux, for example).  But if you didn't have to circumvent anything to play these games, and they called themselves free, then you're pretty safe to assume they're free of charge.

### Post by snova on 2008-10-28
The trouble with free software is that the "freedom" part of it allows you to give it away. You technically can charge for it, but there's almost never any point in it, since anybody who buys it can sell it for a lower price, or give it away themselves, with no legal repercussions.

If at any point they ask for money, they probably want to be paid. :) But if there's an alternate method of getting it not involving money (and it's not a different version, but the whole thing), there's nothing wrong with it.
