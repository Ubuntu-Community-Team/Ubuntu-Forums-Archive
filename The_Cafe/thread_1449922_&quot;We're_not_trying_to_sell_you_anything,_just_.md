(Phone Rings)

me: Hello?

voice: Hello. I'm from [some company] and we're doing some market research on mobile phones. Could you help me with a short survey? It will only take about 10 minutes.

voice: What type of mobile phone package do you have?

voice: OK. Do you have broadband internet access?

voice: Right... and do you have a phone on a landline?

me: No, sorry, I'm not on the phone.

(at which point I hung up. Ask a silly question, get a silly answer!)

I just wonder, has anyone on here had a similar experience from cold-callers?

Actually, real and actual [market research]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_research") by phone is quite common and legitimate. Also, since [number portability]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_number_portability") is becoming more and more common, it's not such a strange question for them to ask if you have a land line even though the number they called is a land line exchange.

### Post by dmizer on 2010-04-08
> **Grez said:**
> voice: Hello. I'm from [some company] and we're doing some market research on mobile phones. Could you help me with a short survey? It will only take about 10 minutes.
This is an introduction to a genuine market research study that can make you real cash by participating.

> **ubuntuman001 said:**
> Random sexy voice: Hello! Our records indicate that you signed up for a raffle to win a free $1000 amazon gift card, and I'm happy to inform you that you have been randomly selected as one of the prize winners!
This is an introduction to a telemarketing scam which will most likely cost you money.

Notice the subtle but important differences. The the phrase "market research", the specific target of the market research, the length of the survey, and most importantly a verifiable company name are all mentioned in the genuine study.]

On the other hand, a raffle you probably never signed up for, the word "free", the mention of a large sum of money, and "randomly selected" are all earmarks of spam/telemarketing.
