In that sense Apple are directly using their monopoly to keep down those they don't agree with. As people move to the iPod Touch and iPhone this problem only gets worse and Apple are even more aggressive here protecting their protocol and specifications for these devices, going so far as issuing DMCA takedown requests for harmless reverse engineering sites. 

E.g. if Linux does not full support the iPod/iPhone for media, we will lose customers. It is harmful to our business and they know it. They actively work to prevent competition using this knowledge and a boatload of lawyers.

They then also use this monopoly to force which codecs and services people have available for their benefit by disallowing 3rd party additions to iTunes.

Here is what should happen, iTunes should be forced to a certain level of openness to allow other vendors to add support via plugins. It would be unreasonable to expect Apple to support these plugins though, nor should they be forced to open their code. Secondly they should be forced to open their specifications for interacting safely with the current and future Apple devices under terms reasonable for open and free library implementations. 

That would go a long way to ensure fair competition with Apple and prevent them from abusing their monopoly without depriving them of what makes their products special and desirable in ways harmful to their business.

As for the iPhone app store specific problems, given that I don't own an iPhone nor have I have spend sufficient time understanding the problems involved. I don't feel qualified to give an opinion.
