### Post by Corfy on 2009-03-06
Oh, there are lots of Linux conferences.

The one I have gone to the last three years (and am planning on attending again this year) is Ohio LinuxFest ([http://www.ohiolinux.org](http://www.ohiolinux.org)) in Columbus, Ohio. This is the only one I have actually been to, but it is close enough for me to drive to.

My favorite experience is simply being in a room with 1,000+ other people who are all familiar with Linux. That is a welcome change of pace from my normal day-to-day experience where almost nobody has heard of it (and if they have heard of it, it is through me). But there are vendors, community groups, speakers, socializing, and people from all walks of life getting together to learn about, discuss, and share ideas about Linux.

A decent list of Linux Conferences from all over the world can be found at [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_conference](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux_conference).

### Post by Corfy on 2009-03-07
> **medya said:**
> nice , do they accept atendees from other countries , like Iran ? how much does it cost to attend ? 
were you a visitor or a you was a speaker or something ?

Oh, they'll take people from other countries, that isn't a problem. Of course, you have to get into the country first. That I can't help you with.

Each conference is different, but Ohio LinuxFest is free to attend, but they do have some paid "supporter" packages. Last year was $65, and people who paid it got a t-shirt, box lunch, after-party drink tickets, and a few other bonuses.

And I was a visitor all three years. Last year was the first time I paid.

> **medya said:**
> can you tell us more about how was that ohoio conference , what did they do, was it fun , did they give anything or sell anything? how long was the speaking times

There were a lot of vendor booths. Some of the vendors gave things away (everything from a small stuffed Tux to small "No Windows" buttons to information... it varied depending on the vendor). Of course, several of the booths representing Linux distros gave away copies of Linux. Last year, I got OpenSuse, Fedora, and Foresight. I could have had Ubuntu as well, but I didn't really need that.

They have several speakers talking through the day. They have a keynote speaker at the beginning of the day, and another at the end, but then they have three talks going at once through the rest of the day lasting approximately an hour each, and you have to decide which ones you want to attend and which you don't. Some of the talks are targeted to beginners, some are targeted to more advanced users, some talk about various issues (which can be for anyone).

My favorite talk last year was "Through the Looking Glass: Open Source From a Teenage Perspective" given by Elizabeth Garbee. Jon "maddog" Hall has spoken all three years I have been there, and he is always worth listening to. 

Last year, they started an "open source theater" showing various videos. I was busy with the topics and so I didn't really look at what was showing.

Still, before attending my first LinuxFest, I had never met anyone in person who had used Linux, so it was an experience to be around all those people. Seeing lots of users online is one thing, hanging out with them for a day is something else.

### Post by gnomeuser on 2009-03-07
I see you are in Kurdistan, so I assume travelling is a problem.

If it isn't then the UDS is a quite nice conference I hear. You can also go to FOSDEM which is a great experience. In Denmark we have Open Source Days (used to be Linux Forums) which I have attended a few times, it is always a great experience though in the recent years the talks have become uninteresting to me. Then there is the GNOME User And Developer European conference which is filled with good experiences, year time it was held in Turkey next time I believe will be in Gran Canaria, maybe you can get lucky and it will come close to you.

Generally the format varies, some places stick to a barcamp style presentation which mixes elements of unprepared presentations and hack sessions, then in the other camp you have the more keynote minded style conferences. I tend to enjoy the latter more.

I really enjoy a well prepared talk, 1-2 hours with questions.

You can seek out conferences near you to start with, your local Linux User Group should know what is available.
