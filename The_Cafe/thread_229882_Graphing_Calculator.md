### Post by teet on 2006-08-06
I bought a Ti-86 when I started undergrad (I was a math major).  The TI-83/84 were both like $100 at the time and the Ti-86 was like $110 so I just went with the higher number.

The Ti-89's are good because you can do some calculus on them...but you can do some simple calculus on the Ti-86 as well (integrals, derivatives, etc.) if you know how.

I can only recall one math professor ever telling me that I couldn't use my graphing calculator, and to be honest, we didn't need it for the test anyway.  

A graphing calculator is a very wise investment if you're going to college.  I used it in my math, biology, chemistry, stats, and physics courses.  Yes, it's good to learn the theory behind the process, but sometimes you just want the answer.  For example, we spent a good 20 minutes one day in my Abstract Algebra class using calculus to sketch the graph of a polynomial (we were all a bit rusty however) when we could have spent <1 minute by using a graphing calculator.

### Post by KaeseEs on 2006-08-07
I took pre-calc two years ago, and BC Calculus ( counts for 8 credit hours this year in college ) last year in high school.  Throughout, I used a TI-83+ ( the regular model is deprecated and hasn't been mf'd in some years IIRC ), and it served me well.  Know that most courses nowadays require a graphing calculator - the AP & BC Calculus exams have 2 calculator sections each! - and though your teacher will almost certainly have several available during class, this is a rather sub-optimal solution, as these calculators are unavailable during homework, study halls etc. [ duh! ] and are often unreliable during tests due to battery uncertainty, and some in the future certainly will, and it is best to familiarize yourself with one now and keep it through later high school and college work.

  I would recommend a TI-83+ 'Silver Edition' if you can find one, or a TI-84(+) if cost is not prohibitive, as these models both graph complex equations a good bit faster than the plain TI-83+ ( second derivatives, complex integrals etc. - the TI line uses a long Taylor series to approximate to a precision of 1/2*10^13 , which takes time on small hardware ).  However, a TI-83+ will do fine if that's what you can swing.  In any case, and especially if the TI-83+ is your choice, practice mentally simplifying equations so that the calculator is only needed for tedious bits of computation, as it's generally faster to burn through algebra and calculus on paper or in your head whenever possible.  Also, use functions that spit output to the terminal before graphing things, as it's much faster.

Finally, if you get a graphing calculator of any model, get a set of Ni-MH rechargeable batteries and keep a set of spare alkalines on hand in case your main rig dies during a test.

I think the distinction is that a "university" is a large collection of "colleges" under one bureaucracy.  For example, I go to the University of Tennessee and I'm in the College of Engineering.

Then there are other "colleges" that are not part of a "university", and they are schools that focus completely on one topic.  There's a growing trend toward labeling technical schools "college" according to this meaning.  For example, Bob goes to the local "College of Technology" where he's taking 2 years of classes on Microsoft Office, Windows Networking, and Xbox Repair.

As far as standards go, you're right.  There is no national standard, so just going to college doesn't really mean anything.  Instead of a national standard, we have "acredidation" societies (groups of industry and educational representatives) that oversee the curriculum, work, grading techniques, etc. and then decide if it deserves their stamp of approval.  That ends up being very important, because no serious company is going to hire someone from a non-acredited university.  So the schools all have to at least keep up with the acredidation requirements.

And finally, "freshman" means year 1, "sophomore" means year 2, "junior" means year 3, "senior" means year 4+, and "grad" means you've already got a degree but you keep going to school anyway.
