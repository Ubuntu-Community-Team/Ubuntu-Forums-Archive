### Post by Emily on 2007-04-30
I'm another American with no car. Neither my husband nor I has *ever *owned one, which I think makes us pretty freakish by American standards. :twisted: 

As you might guess, we live in NYC. One of the main reasons I moved here after college was that I'd spent some time in Germany and got used to walking a lot, and didn't want to move back to the kind of suburbs where I grew up. 

I like walking and taking public transportation (so that I can read while commuting). However, I've never had a job close enough that I could walk every day as my commute. When the weather is nice, I deliberately get off the train a few stops early and walk about 1/3 of the way home. Commuting on the NYC subway is definitely more entertaining than riding in alone a car. A [recent article in the New Yorker]("http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2007/04/16/070416fa_fact_paumgarten") suggested that New Yorkers are happier with their commutes than other Americans because there is more of a social element to it. I sometimes run into people I know and ride the train with them to or from work. Also, because most people get around by subway, safety isn't as much of an issue--there are always other people around. 

Part of the reason we don't have a car, though, is that the insurance, parking, gas, etc. would be really expensive. We can't afford it because our housing is so astronomically expensive. By not having a car, we're making a choice about our lifestyle, but we're not saving any money.

### Post by Miguel on 2007-05-07
FWD is indeed *front wheel drive*. Four wheel drive is either AWD or 4WD, to save the customers from the confusion. Prizrak probably hates FWD cars due to their ultrastable (read: hard to make turns) and and bloody understeery nature. However, these cars do make sense in some situations:
[list][*] It's the cheapest way to build a front engined car.
[*] If you want to maximise boot space, FF (front engine, front wheel drive) is the answer.
[*] Most people can't cope with oversteer*
[/list]

This is the reason most cars in Europe are FWD. They are smaller and thus, to be more space efficient they end up being FF. There is also the "little" issue with the roads. European roads are much more twisty**, which increases  the risk of driving errors. They are also narrower, so your margin is also smaller. Parking in the cities is also scarcier than in the US. The end result is that very few brands make RWD family cars. Curiously, the AWD system from the VW cars is basically a FWD car  with a differential to switch to AWD only when needed.

* Even if you handle oversteer nicely, one distraction can be very scary.
** I've only seen Maine and Florida in the US. It's surely different near the Rocky mountains, but even then,  european motorways rarely have miles-long straights (Spain and France, I don't rememeber british or italian ones).
