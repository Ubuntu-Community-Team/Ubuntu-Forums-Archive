### Post by Sunforge on 2007-09-29
This is what happens when your study gets too cluttered.

I was rooting through my old CD collection, which is a grab bag of all sorts of distros and I came across a version of Knoppix. I cast my mind back to the first time I saw it and I can definitely remember one of the network guys coming into the data centre where I was working with this "wonder CD" that worked on pretty much every machine we tried it on. Linux! On a CD! you could browse the web! Hmmm, geektastic. 

Nowadays, apparently, a disto can't hold it's head up unless there is an accompanying live CD.

And something I hadn't come across before, a live CD distro site:

Then I began idly wondering how much of the recent success of Linux could be attibuted to these live CDs? What's your take on it?

### Post by popch on 2007-09-29
> **Sunforge said:**
> Then I began idly wondering how much of the recent success of Linux could be attibuted to these live CDs? What's your take on it?

For me, it changed everything. I have been a Linux user for years, meaning that I always had at least one computer in my household running Linux, and that usually but not always was the main one.

Two simultaneous developments pushed me over the edge:

1: Windows became increasingly difficult to re-install on my computers. That is a bit weird because I always bought those computers with Windows, and usually I did the re-installs with the original material supplied with those very machines. The emergency recovery disk supplied with my latest hp laptop even failed to find the image of the OS on the media supplied. The path to the image on the DVD was incorrectly given in the batch file on that very DVD.

2: At the same time, I could use live CDs of several Linux distributions to check if any given Computer would run reasonably well with that distribution before performing the installation and even before buying the machine. This gives you some nice bargaining edge in some shops, as you can always walk away if they do not let you do the test. Besides, in some shops the personnel also would like to know if their stuff works with Linux.
