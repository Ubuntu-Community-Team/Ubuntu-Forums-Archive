### Post by ve4cib on 2009-11-23
> **ZankerH said:**
> You guys obviously go to wrong unis. It may take a while, but in the end you're better off in a university that acknowledges and respects Free Software instead of spreading misinformation and lies about it.

Preface: The following is a somewhat broad generalization based on my personal experiences and those of some local friends of mine.  The following may not be true in all locations, and some deviation from my observed trends is to be expected.

I've generally found that universities (i.e. places that issue Bachelors/Masters/PhDs) tend to be more open-source friendly than colleges (i.e. places that issue diplomas and certificates).

The necessity of peer-reviewed publishings in universities means that computer science profs are very often forced to release code (or detailed pseudo-code at the very least).  Universities are naturally geared towards academia, and academia is geared towards openness.

Colleges on the other hand are much more focused on the practical side of things, and preparing people for "the industry".  And, unfortunately, "the industry" tends to be much more closed-source.  Its in colleges' best interests to expose their students to what the majority of businesses use, and for the time-being that is generally Windows.

A friend of mine did a three-year network administrator certificate course at a well-reputed local technical college.  Over the whole three years he took one course that dealt with Linux and Unix.  Everything else was Windows.  And I don't even think that one course was required.

Compare that to a four-year Computer Science degree at the university I attended.  First-year was all Java, so pretty platform-independent, but the labs were all done in Windows.  Second-year was a 50% Unix/Linux/Solaris, and 50% "use whatever you want".  In third-year and fourth-year every single assignment except for one or two *had* to run on the department's Linux boxes (running Fedora 7 at that time).  If you wrote your code on Windows/Mac you had to make sure that it would compile and run under Fedora or you would fail.

Obviously a CS degree and a network admin certificate have to cover different materials, but the general trend of universities leaning towards Linux and colleges tending towards Windows is pretty well-illustrated.
