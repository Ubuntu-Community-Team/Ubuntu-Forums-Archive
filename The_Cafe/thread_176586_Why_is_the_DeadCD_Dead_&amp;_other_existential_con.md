4. refer to articles of interest that will deepen this discourse, starting with the following article from:
[http://news.mymyah.com/Top/Mandriva/](http://news.mymyah.com/Top/Mandriva/)

"Mandriva, Friend of foe?"

Mandrake linux after almost running itself into the ground. Has not only been able to rebuild but has also merged with a Latin company called Conectiva. With their new and bigger company called Mandriva, they also took over Lycoris.
     Now at first I was happy to hear that Mandrake was able to save itself from the scrapheap, because Mandrake was a nice distribution, and I didn't want to see any linux company go down. But I was thinking about how they are going about doing business, and I think it might not be the best thing having them around.
     First of all the reason Mandrake was able to stay afloat was because of their outrageous prices. Now 99% of Mandrake or rather Mandriva now is completely free software that anyone can use. Mandriva does have some programmes that they have written like Harddrake, their hardware management utilities, but other than a few utilities like that, everything else is open source. So, why are they charging so much? Because people will pay the price just to stay away from Windows. Now that is silly, people want to stay way from Windows because of their greed, yet Mandrake is 2 to 3 time more expensive. What do you get out of this? A free software and some customer support that believe me you will need.
     The second reason they didn't go under is because you can actually subscribe to their communities. People subscribe for free downloads and other goodies, plus just to help a linux distro out. But this is also very, very expensive, I couldn't believe how greedy these people are. People are trying to give their money to anyone but the big bad Microsoft, not seeing that they're feeding this monster that is taking more than Microsft would ever take.
     Now this make me very mad, because we really don't need another Microsoft or worse, and on top of that you're getting FREE software for it. Now I would understand charging a reasonable amount that is fair, but Mandriva has no concept of that. I fear if Mandriva and other companies like Linspire are going the way they are, eventually they will try to take something like KDE and pull it out of Open Source and charge people for it. Then, what will we have is Gnome. I don't really want to use Gnome. And that will be the end of free linux. And free is what linux is all about.

*btw I don't agree with everything from this article I love Gnome*

Seriously, any way that a company could make hardware with linux would be a godsend. What a wave it could set off if successful.

A hardware company, that was maybe too ambitous and impractical, going down, being bought out by a wealthy entrepreneur, one much like Mr. Shuttleworth, and building linux boxes. 

There are a lot of things stopping this from happening though, like

Market market market. Yes, there are a lot of linux users, very assertive ones. But who can say for *sure* how many? And who can say that they will purchase these products in sufficient number's to make it a worth while investment? How much money does the average linux user have? I personally think that a lot of linux user's would go out of their way to buy a linux-box. But is this standpoint enough to convince a curious CEO somewhere to hop on? 
It's too fuzzy, too many maybe's. 

The real ticket is when the current major manufacturer's realize there is a market for linux computer's, and sell linux boxes themselves. In widespread fashion. Then you might see new mp3 player's that support ogg! 
(...then 100 years later, apple releases a build for itunes on linux...)
Then a CEO has number's to look at, and might be more inclined to take the risk and venture into this arena. It could be a boom, if set off right.
Naturally, it could be a bust too, and further proof to that CEO that this isn't ready yet.

At this point I can see why it's just not convincing enough to take such a risk. Not a CLEAR enough market.

Ubuntu has, and continues to change this though, as regular, end-user's are picking up the software, and creating a demand for hardware compatible with it.
"I love ubuntu to bits. It's all free, I feel safe and secure, it's stable, friendly helpful community, but unfortunately my Lexmark printer is a complete bust with it, and my USB wireless too. 
I have to use windows for x, y, and z.
I can't play my favorite game, even after paying for Cedega"

A regular end-user, introduced to free software, impressed, but wanting more. What does he want? His/her devices to be compatible. It's demand being stirred up. It's happening now.

But at what rate, and to what degree? Just how reliable is "google trends" and distrowatch and other stat tickers.. Subjective, fuzzy, unclear.

The CEO is curious, his attention captured, but he's not convinced. It's just not clear enough yet.
