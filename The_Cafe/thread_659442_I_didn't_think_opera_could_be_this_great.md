### Post by urukrama on 2008-01-05
I also love Opera. What I especially like:
[INDENT]
* The speed! And the neat pop up progress bar (you have to enable this) where you can see the speed at which the site loads, how much more it has to load, how many more images, etc. If the images take too long to load, just switch the images off for this page with one click.
* The 'forward' and 'rewind' button. If you have a password saved with the 'wand', it enters it automatically when you press forward. Great also for browsing pictures: just click forward and you go to the next one (for most sites).
* Right - Left mouse click brings you back in history; left-right goes forward (or same as the forward button). It makes browsing so much easier! Very frustrating if you're using another browser that doesn't have this feature, and you're used to it.
* you can choose how to start Opera: load homepage, no pages, sessions (!), or just what was last opened.
* the trashcan (in the upper right corner): any tabs you have closed (including its history) can be easily opened again. If you just want the last closed tab, type Ctrl+Alt+Z and you are back there.
* The 'duplicate tab' option (right click on a tab). Again the history and all is duplicated.
* Site preferences. Have a site that doesn't recognises only IE? Edit your site preferences, and make Opera identify as IE, or even mask as IE.
* Press spacebar to go down the page (standard, no?). When you reach the end of the page, press spacebar again and it takes you to the next. Great for Google searches!
* Disable gif animation!
* Block unwanted ads.
* Asign shortcuts or nicknames to bookmarked sites
* The transfer (download) system. See how fast things go, where you are downloading from and where to, option to retransfer or pause transfer, or use the quick download option (just enter a url). Torrents will also appear there.
* the option to save particular file types (mp3, pdf, etc) to particular locations on your hard drive
* 'Work offline' option
* 'Links' panel: see where the site you are on links to.
* The notes feature. Just select some text, right click and select 'save to note'. A new note is created and the url is automatically preserved. Just double click on the note and the webpage is opened.
* the built in search engines. Type 'g ubuntu' and you do a google search for Ubuntu; z for amazon, etc.
* 'Fit to width': is the image to big and do you have to use the horizontal scrollbar, or is the page just badly designed for your small screen? Click the fit to width button and it is automatically resized or adjusted.
* Zoom easily in and out.
* Its easy to customise the look. Move all buttons around until you like where they are.
* etc.
[/INDENT]

### Post by Praadur on 2008-01-06
I think all browsers have their pros and cons.

The downside of Firefox (or Swiftweasel, as I prefer) is that one will have to pay a *heavy* toll when it comes to memory, and sometimes even CPU, this is something I'll admit as I've seen it myself.  Not to mention that I understand that the extension system can be confusing/irritating, with so many versions of Firefox out there, so many extension versions, and all the flags in about:config to allow or lock-out extensions from different places or within certain version brackets... well, not all users will want to put up with that.

What i gain, however, are a number of things that I wouldn't want to be without (and keep in mind that I run an amd64 optimised build of Firefox, and I'm comparing against the most recent Opera I could find)...

o Fast page loads, with proper rendering: I found that Opera was visibly slower than Swiftweasel when it came to that (and Opera messed up a couple of pages that Swift got right, too).  I do a lot of research and sometimes I have a lot of things running around in my head, having internet pages load as quickly as possible is a real boon as it often means I'll be less likely to suffer an aneurysm whilst waiting (and even nanoseconds can seem like a bloody eternity then).

o Powerful visual control: I can really get into the guts of Firefox and change how it looks.  Thanks to the CSS back-end, I can place any bars or buttons pretty much anywhere I like within the UI.  This allows for unprecedented control over the visual browsing experience, and can help with shaving unused pixels off a minimalist theme to make it even more minimalist to having things which only show on mouseover.  By comparison, I couldn't really do anything to stop Opera feeling so bloated (is there anything as powerful as Firefox's CSS control in Opera?).

o Open extendibility: Firefox is like a canvas which I can paint as I desire.  If there's functionality that I want then often I can find it as an extension, or even create my own extensions with great ease.  Even better is that Firefox extensions are basically zip files, so if there's anything I don't like about how an extensio works, I can change it.  It's simply a matter of going into the zip and editing the Javascript/XUL files.  This allows for a more community-orientated atmosphere aswell as people can pick up dead projects and udpate them, and folks can submit patches to projects which are still alive.  Not to mention that if an extension doesn't work exactly how I like, I can change it (and even I'm surprised at how often I dive into extension files to change little things).

My conclusion is that most end-users won't give a damn about anything I've covered here, maybe the page loads in some cases but I doubt it, so Opera really *is* a good browser for the end-user.  But when speaking for myself alone and of my own opinion, it's just not a suitable browser for a developer/tinker.
