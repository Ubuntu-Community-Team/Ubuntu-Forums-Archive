### Post by Dixon Bainbridge on 2010-06-13
Digital SLR camera guide - Updated

Some time ago I wrote this:

[Earlier thread]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=888577")

I've decided to revise and update with some more experiences.

I'm a full time freelance photographer, which means I'm a professional photographer. Professional means you derive 100% of your income from a profession, and should not be used as a measure of someone's ability to do a job. Many amateur photographers are better than professionals in terms of skill. Pro is no indication of quality.

If you do not derive 100% of your income from a profession, any profession, then you are an amateur. Amateur is often used in the same way professional is to denote someone that isn't very good or substandard. This is not necessarily the case.

*I need to buy a camera, what do I buy? Bridge? Pro-sumer, Entry Level, Pro?*

Ok lets clarify a few things:

1) Bridge, Pro-sumer, Pro, Entry level are all marketing terms and are therefore meaningless.

2) Here is a list of consumer camera makers:

Nikon, Canon, Leica, Olympus, Panasonic, Pentax, Sony

3) Here is a list of professional camera makers:

Linhoff, Phase One, Mamiya, Leaf, Hasselblad, Tachihara, RED

A lot of professionals use consumer cameras. Nikon and Canon don't make cameras for professionals, they make them for rich amateurs. Without rich amateurs, the likes of Canon and Nikon would be out of business. 

Professionals that use consumer cameras: Wildlife photographers, Paparazzi/journalists and sports shooters.

Despite all the marketing guff about Professional cameras, Nikon and Canon et al, make cameras for consumers and amateurs. Professional for these companies just means "costs more".

A lot of people measure a photographers worth by the gear they own. This is an amateur attitude. A lot of professionals, ironically, define themselves by the amount of consumer gear they own (ie, thousands of pounds of Nikon gear). People are strange. I define myself by the quality of the work i produce and the awards I win. The equipment I use to produce it is irrelevant to me.

Medium and Large format, digital or film, are professional formats. 35mm and its equivalents is an amateur format.

A Nikon D3x is a consumer camera aimed at rich amateurs. Its not made for professionals in mind, even though some pros may use one. (see above). The Canon 5D Mk2 is another example of a consumer camera. Its called Pro to make it sound more sexy. Its an amateur camera. Don't be fooled into parting money for a camera called Professional under the illusion that its better than one thats called consumer. Its all marketing toss engineered to upsell. Ignore it.

*How much should I spend on a camera?*

As much as you can afford, and no more. There is no discernible difference in image quality between a bridge camera (lowest end of the price spectrum to the most expensive (what camera companies call Professional). The more expensive pro cameras are built better, are compatible with more lenses and shoot faster. They also have better high iso performance. But thats it. Most of these issues, and certainly the high iso performance/noise issue, can be corrected with NR software afterwards. (with the usual caveats of loss of dynamic range)

All consumer cameras are much of a muchness. Ignore reviews from places like Luminous Landscape, Camera Labs, DP review, they are all sponsored by the camera industry and are biased. Reviews are just another arm of a camera companies marketing division.

Don't take advice off a salesman either. All DSLRs will take the same image. So get any that you like the look and feel of. They are all identical in image quality. Ignore pixel peeping comparisons. ALL DSLRs take the same image. People that tell you otherwise have just spent too much on a camera and want to look superior about it. My Olympus E-520 takes as good an image as the Canon 5DMk2. I know, because I shot both toe to toe in a studio for giggles. When I finished processing the shots from both, nobody I showed the shots to could tell me which came from which camera. Put that in your pipe and smoke it, Mr Reviewer Pixel Peeper. In real terms, there is no difference in any digital SLR. Your ability to use it is far more important than anything else. Most people are let down by their talent and technique, not by the camera. A Canon 5d Mk2 is a great camera. Its also an awful camera. What makes it either/or, great or awful IS THE PERSON USING IT.

*What about lenses?*

Spend your money on lenses not on bodies. Digital bodies die quickly, lenses live forever. An expensive piece of glasses value can increase with time. I have 3 lenses that are worth more now than when I bought them brand new 10 years ago.

Glass>bodies.

*More megapixels means bigger prints*

This is the single biggest lie in digital photography. I've never heard such rubbish in my life. I've printed 10MP images 6ft wide for exhibitions. You can print a 10MP image to the size of sky scraper and it will be pin sharp as long as you are standing far enough back. The issue is viewing distance, not pixel count. Pixels  50 cm high will look pixelated at 6 foot away, but stand 100 meters back and it will look sharp. Stand further away and they will look even better. People never take into consideration viewing distance in anything. Image looks pixelated? Stand further back. Problem solved.

You only ever need to print an image at 240dpi - so if you have an image 7x5 inches, then print at 240dpi. If its 6ft wide, make sure thats 240dpi - the human eye cannot resolve anything more above 240dpi, so even printing at 300 dpi is pointless. That way, every thing will look nice and sharp at a normal viewing distance. Obviously the bigger the print the more pixels you'll need to keep the image at 240dpi… but thats what genuine fractals is for. Genuine fractals has made pixel counts in cameras irrelevant. As long as you have at least a 4MP image, you can blow it up as large as you like with no loss in quality. When printing stupidly large, just stand further back to view it. No one looks at a 6ft wide image from 6 inches away.

Forget pixels when buying a camera, its a non-issue.

[I]What about noise?
[/I]
Digital shooters are obsessed with noise. I do not know why. Its much better to have a noisier but sharper image, than a noise free and smudgy image. Don't obssess about noise, and don't be too aggressive in removing it. Concentrate on composition, light, expression and emotion instead. Its far more important for an image. I hate super smudgy noise free images. They look worse than HDR, and thats saying something.

[I]Digital has replaced film, film blows and is irrelevant. DSLRs are better than any film camera.
[/I]
Recently I sold all my digital gear (Nikon) and bought a Mamiya 645 Pro TL instead. With the left over money I bought an E-520 body so I could use my old OM lenses. So far, apart from handling, I've yet to see a difference between the E-520 and the D300 I had previously, in real terms (real terms mean shooting live in proper situations, not shooting charts and comparing images at a pixel level, which is something that only berks do or are interested in).

The Mamiya I bought to replace the D300, a camera Nikon markets as Professional (see their website), is superior to anything Nikon or Canon make for the following reasons:

1) It shoots film, That means I have more artistic flexibility in my shooting. Artistic flexibility is more important to me than anything else. Digital filters are all well and good, but film types and speeds give better artistic effects than any digital filter. Black and white rocks on film, it sucks on digital (at the moment. that will change in time)

2) It has a dedicated mirror-up switch. Very few DSLR have this and most bury it in a menu - not convenient. This is vital for studio shooting as I can operate it easily.

3) I can replace the focusing screen in about 10 seconds. I timed it actually and I did it 8. Trying replacing your DSLR focusing screen in under 10 minutes. I didn't need any specialist tools either, just my finger. Win.

4) The meter is infallible. No DSLR has a meter as accurate as the Mamiya. I switch it to Av/Sp mode and the camera takes care of the rest. Its reflective meter also matches precisely my light meters reflective readings. I've yet to use any DSLR that has managed to do this, regardless of price. They are usually a stop to half a stop out.

5) Lenses are relatively cheap, and are superb. You'd need to spend 4 or 5 times more than a Mamiya lens to get the equivalent performance for a DSLR.

6) Its built like a tank. I dropped my Mamiya, back down, onto concrete 2 days ago. It got a scuff and thats it. Works great. I shudder to think what would happen to a DSLR dropped back down onto concrete. Humpty dumpty springs to mind.

7) I have slides that can be scanned in 30 years time when scanners are good. At the moment, even drum scans look poor for the 120 slides I shoot on. Scanners get better and better every year. Once you  captured something digitally, thats it, you are stuck with that images quality at that point in time. With a slide, I can extract more and more year on year as scanners get better and better. And I have a file format that will stlll be around in years to come. Good luck opening your camera model specific raw file in 20 years time.

8) The Mamiya has a switch to block out light entering the viewfinder. No DSLR has this, you have to stick in a plastic thing yourself. If you lose the plastic thing then you have to botch it. Not convenient.

9) The Mamiya can use any flash ever made. I wouldn't stick an old flash gun on the latest DSLR for voltage reasons. Ive seen DSLRs fried form using the wrong flash gun. I've used 6 different legacy flashguns on my Mamiya and all work perfectly with no voltage frying problems.

10) The viewfinder is nearly twice the size of any 35mm viewfinder. A huge viewfinder is perfect for composition and focus accuracy. This is absolutely critical for a professional. 

11) The mamiya shoots 120, 220, 35mm, polaroid or digital, depending on what back I choose to put on it. Win.

All these points matter to a pro. They are important and critical. The mamiya has no menus to negotiate to change things. I just change the aperture, thats it. The camera sorts everything else out.  Good cameras let you take great shots easily and consistently. Bad cameras get in the way of doing this. Image quality, so often the judge of a cameras worth, is an irrelevant measure. All cameras take the same image. Not all photographers can. That's the critical difference - you. Film is great. Its just different to digital, and cannot be compared to each other at all, just as film cameras cant be compared to DSLR cameras. Film is dead for consumer use, unless you are an artistic photographer. Otherwise, don't bother, shoot digital.

*Should I buy a DSLR then?*

Dunno. How much money have you got to burn?

I'd buy a DLSR if I was:

1) Rich and have lots of money

2) Poor and needed a cheap affordable way to get into photography. DSLRs cover a huge price range, so get one that suits your pocket not the invented level camera manufacturers use to define their marketing demographic.

3) You plan on becoming a wedding or sports photographer. DSLRS are great for these areas, and for wildlife. If you want to shoot landscapes, I'd suggest a medium format film camera - its a far cheaper and more rewarding option. But get what suits you. Convenience should be king.

4) You have kids and you want to get those moments when they are running around. In that case, get a cheap DSLR and a kit lens. Bingo, job done.

In conclusion: digital is the option for amateurs and a lot of journalistic professionals.  Film is for printing, black and white,  and for artistic professionals. I wouldn't bother with film unless you have a compulsive urge to use it.

All cameras are sharp, even the crappest lenses ever made are sharp somewhere on their aperture scale. I've seen sharp as a tack shots taken on £35 plastic Holga's, which have plastic lenses. Every camera ever made is sharp. Sharpness comes down to the photographer, NOT the camera.  Better technique improves sharpness.  So forget about sharpness. Get a the DSLR that suits you, and suits your needs. They are all equally sharp.

If you want to go for a pro camera, then don't bother unless things like I've mentioned above, like  a huge viewfinder, are absolutely critical to your needs. 

Remember, as Flavor Flav himself once stated: Don't believe the hype!

Consumer DSLR makers talk so much crap, its untrue. Happy camera hunting.

Thanks for the write up. Pretty accurate except you sound like a salesman yoruself! :P. I love the way black & white film feels and I haven't found the digital equivalent. I shoot a DSLR for my work. I also shoot 35mm film camera for some personal projects but most of my hired work goes through my DSLR.

I will say that everyone should experience film cameras & go through the development/printing process to really appreciate what the digital interpretations of these processes are. Not only that but it's a much more self satisfying process than sitting at your PC and doing edit after edit (at least for me it is).

That said, DSLRs are incredible for learning since they have instant feed back. I think that's why there is such a large crop of people buying camera's saying "hey I can do that too". It's pretty easy to copy, but not so much to create.

You have the benefit of over two decades of experience and gaining capital and you also had the benefit of working when the swimming pool wasn't so crowded. I understand that technology has made things easier, but accessibility creates noise & an opportunity to really stand out.

It is what it is and some of us really do want to become professionals as you said but it's a slow crawl when you're wading through a mountain of photographers.
