### Post by laurenbanjo on 2010-11-14
Grain. Grain is easy to plant, all you have to do is spread some seeds and a LOT of grain grows. You don't have to bend down and dig holes. It can be planted just by the wind blowing. It's also easy to store. Most of our diet revolves around grain.

In places where they started growing grain, they had lots of time to invent other stuff because they didn't spend time planting food. They experimented with lots of stuff.

They also had animals, so they were used to the diseases. When they came to the Americas, the germs were very deadly because the natives weren't exposed.

In a place like Papa New Guinea (not sure how it's spelled), the soil isn't great for grain. The kind of plants they eat, you have to spend a lot of time planting so you don't get that much, and not much of what grows is edible.

### Post by drawkcab on 2010-11-14
The dialectical engine that operates behind civilization is the notion that "Well, if we don't do X then someone else will."  A really simple answer would be that many native american cultures lived sustainably such that competition over scarce resources wasn't as large of a factor in shaping the culture.  Hence nature was to be revered and respected rather than mastered, the latter being the major metaphor behind Indo-European rationality.

### Post by CarpKing on 2010-11-15
> **weasel fierce said:**
> My point was not so much that natives are peaceful and in harmony with nature. But there was a lot more space to expand into, compared to Europe.

Actually, prior to the arrival of Europeans, some areas of the Americas were very densely populated.  Areas that were not, such as the open plains before the arrival of horses, were that way for good reasons, so there really wasn't "space to expand into."  The Mayans cleared huge areas of rainforest for agriculture (which likely led to their collapse).  Vast cities existed especially in and around the Mississippi valley and delta, to the point where the first Europeans reported that you couldn't leave one city without another being in view.  Disease brought by the first Europeans quickly killed millions, though, so by the time settlers arrived the place appeared to be sparsely populated.  

The pilgrims got along so well with the natives in part because the place they landed had recently been cleared out by smallpox, so they were new neighbors instead of invaders.
