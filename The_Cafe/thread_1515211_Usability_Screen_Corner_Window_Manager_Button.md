### Post by inaneframe on 2010-06-22
**[SIZE="4"]Fitts' Law[/SIZE]**
Human-Computer Interaction

[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitts's_law](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitts's_law)

[INDENT]Edges and corners of the computer display (e.g., Start button in Microsoft Windows and the menus and Dock of Mac OS X) are particularly easy to acquire because the pointer remains at the screen edge regardless of how much further the mouse is moved, thus can be considered as having infinite width.[/INDENT]

[INDENT]
It applies only to movement in a single dimension and not to movement in two dimensions (though it is successfully extended to two dimensions in the Accot-Zhai steering law);
It describes simple motor response of, say, the human hand, failing to account for software acceleration usually implemented for a mouse cursor;
It describes untrained movements, not movements that are executed after months or years of practice (though some argue that Fitts's law models behaviour that is so low level that extensive training doesn't make much difference).[/INDENT]

Whether or not you like the OP's tone doesn't make their point any less valid. Fitt's law has been the cornerstone of good UI for three decades. Apple have always employed it in their UI to great success, it is one of the fundamentals of the Human Interface Guidelines. That's why they've only have one menu on the top of their screen going as far back as Lisa, so that you could just throw your mouse up and hit the menu you need, without having to decelerate. Remember the Windows 9x start button? It started one pixel to the right and above the lower-left screen corner, so one could never hit it without actually aiming. You would notice that in XP, Vista and Windows 7 the Start menu goes all the way to the corner. If you switch any of those to Windows Classic view, it mimics the old looks BUT they've improved the behavior so that the corner is actually clickable. In Windows 7 (now mimicked by KDE 4.4), all four screen corners and three out of four screen edges serve different functions. There's a good reason for that and it's called Fitt's law. It helps. ### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2010-06-22
Fitt's Law is not just about making whatever button you happen to be thinking about clickable from the edge of the screen. Computers don't read minds, so we shouldn't build them with that expectation.

In fact, Fitt's Law provides some pretty good reason for why we don't want the close button to extend to the corner of the screen: closing a window is a destructive act, so it shouldn't be so easy to do by accident.

In addition, applying that behaviour to the edge-most button creates some inconsistency. Every other button's clickable area is just the button itself. Why should Close be different?
This is all made worse by the reliance on very minimal pre-lights for window buttons. It's theme specific, but with Ambiance and Radiance it is not particularly easy to know if you are clicking, or about to click, the Close button.

Finally, there *is* a function which can be accessed from the very edge of the screen. That's the one which is least destructive: moving the window! It's quite logical, too. Observe that you move the window by clicking the title bar anywhere except those few well defined spots that hold buttons. The title bar also extends to the furthest edge of the window, or the corner of the screen.
