### Post by aysiu on 2009-08-03
I do think new users should be willing to do a little legwork, but I'm also against the whole "Why don't they just Google it?" mentality, for a few reasons: [list][*]Sometimes when new users are overwhelmed by migration problems, they are in too much of a panic to get things working that they can't think straight enough to Google a problem.[*]New users do not always know *what* to search for. I've gotten pretty good at Googling Ubuntu problems now that I've been using Ubuntu for a few years, but in the beginning, I didn't know what the best searches were for my problems.[*]New users do not have a way of validating what information is best. When I first started using Linux, I thought I was being self-sufficient by Googling how to do a dual-boot. Well, instead of picking a tutorial in the search results that just said "Use the defaults and the Linux boot loader will automatically add Windows to the boot menu," I ended up with some complicated tutorial about modifying the boot.ini file and installing Grub to some special location. It was ridiculous... in hindsight. Of course, if I'd swallowed my pride and just *asked* the best way to set up a dual-boot, I probably would have gotten a better tutorial.[/list]

### Post by t0p on 2009-08-03
> **EDavidBurg said:**
> My main gripe is that if something is harder to fix than just googling or using a man page, most people here don't know how to handle it, as their support mainly entails googling something for someone and then giving them a link to and/or summary of their findings.

We can only give quality advice on stuff we actually know about, right?  And few people know lots about lots of stuff.  But I think the majority of 'helpful' users here give as much as we can.

Also, on my desktop machine I have bookmarked a number of tutorials that helped me learn something.  So if I'm on that machine and I see a query about one of those subjects, I can provide a link that I think has value.

But newbies can use google constructively.  They can check out the results, scan some of the sites, and if they see a number of tutorials covering stuff similarly they can make an educated guess that maybe there's something in it.

Another thing.  I often see newbies asking about encryption, password-protected directories and the like.  I'll usually recommend truecrypt, as I find that app to be very good.  But the newbie doesn't need to post the question.  If he searches the forum for keyword 'encryption' (preferably by using google with search term 'site:ubuntuforums.org' added - the forum's search utility is rubbish) he will see a large number of posts recommending truecrypt.  So why ask that question again?  Because the newbie *hasn't* searched.  He wants to be spoon-fed.  And I understand that a bewildered newbie will feel much happier about being spoon-fed rather than striking out alone.  But if we don't encourage him to learn, he might never learn.  Which is no good thing.
