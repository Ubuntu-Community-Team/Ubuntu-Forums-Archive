### Post by Lil_Eagle on 2007-01-30
Well, I'm the old fashion kind of guy who likes a printed book.  You think you guys in the States have it bad, try living in Mainland Europe.  Sure, there are books, but most of them are not in English and quite frequently poorly translated.

The other problem with the books is that most of them are quite old.  That's why I bought a new printer recently.  So I can print the books I find online (although many of those are old as well).  I can be selective and only print certain chapters that I find interesting and can dump the basics like "This is how you read e-mail" type things, which I must admit about all you find specific to Ubuntu.

However, books and online resourses about Debian are quite applicible to Ubuntu.  There are minor differences, but on the whole, you can apply 95% of what you learn about Debian to Ubuntu (no matter which flavor of it you run).  The same can be said about most distributions.  There are only a handfull that don't stem from one of the majors.

One caveat is that while there is a standard linux base, distibution specifics can cause problems.  For example on Ubuntu (and all Debian derrivitives) you'll find your network cards configuration in /etc/network/interface, but where is it on Red Hat (and it's derrivitives)?  /etc/sysconfig/network.
