### Post by QwUo173Hy on 2011-08-04
If you play to it's strengths, it's a great device. If you try to use it for other things, it's a heart breaker.

I connect it via usb cable to my machine and put saved webpages (thanks to Instapaper), downloaded ebooks from various sources as well as my own compositions. It's great being able to read, for free, material from so many sources like archive.org, project gutenberg and many more. Also, not having a backlight has improved my ability to get to sleep at night as I no longer sit in front of a lit display in bed at night. But I'm a little sensitive on that so it won't necessarily be a problem for you.

I don't use it for PDFs unless I reformat them on the computer to suit the small display better. PDFs can't reflow their text on the Kindle you see.

Also, it's not a good device for material you need to jump around inside - eg. hopping around various pages, chapters etc in a textbook. It's best for linearly read material like fiction and things you will take notes on and never read again. But if you get the notion 'oh there was something about that somewhere in such a book, I'll have a quick flick through and see if I can find it!', then forget it, unless you had the foresight to underline or bookmark it in the kindle at the time or you have the correct words to find it with the search tool.

The wifi is handy for *basic* web browsing. I use wikipedia and sometimes a blog or two, but not email or anything that requires more than the most basic keyboard input.

I love my kindle. I has saved me so much space and I am now getting rid of all of my books that I don't need to keep to hand as a reference (everything except college books (psychotherapy) and computer books that I reference).

The Oxford dictionary included adds major value to it as well IMO. Also, as a tech-phile, the device encourages me to read more :)
