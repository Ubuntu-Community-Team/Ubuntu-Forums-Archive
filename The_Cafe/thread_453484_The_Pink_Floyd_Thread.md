### Post by sebbouckaert on 2008-05-21
Hey, I'm also a big fan!

 I discovered “Wish You Were Here” (the album) when I was 16 or so, and I just loved it ever since. I had a fairly liberal upbringing, so around the time I was 17 I used to hang around the house with some friends, as we enjoyed some pot while we listened to that album. 

Later on, I discovered all the other Floyd stuff and had a huge trip on their early stuff, from “Piper At The Gates of Dawn” to “Ummagumma”. I was really intrigued by Syd Barrett and his song writing abilities, as well as the whole story of his mental state, forcing him to withdraw from the band and public life and so on...

With the quarrel and departure of Waters, and the legendary status of the band, I couldn't believe the news in 1986 that they would go out and tour again! So during the summer of 87 I saw them twice, first in Rotterdam and then a few weeks later in Werchter festival ground (Belgium). But I never liked the way Gilmour acted in that period, and I must say that “A Momentary Lapse Of Reason” is my least favourite Floyd album, in fact I agree with Waters who then called it “a pretty fair forgery”, 'cause I mean, that's what it sounds like, very dull and mechanical stuff (plus Rick Wright isn't even credited as a full band member, so...°

Anyway, the nice thing about Pink Floyd is that they have so many periods: the early Barrett stuff, post-Barret psychedelia, their US breakthrough with “Dark Side” followed by a major seventies period. Then the Waters domination (Animals/The Wall/Final Cut) and finally the Gilmour “I can do it on my own” period. Not to forget the short, but moving 4-man Floyd reunion on Live Aid.

And as a guitar player, I must say that Gilmour's playing  has been a big inspiration for my own playing. Although he's not really a fast player (neither am I) he has this magnificent tone. Producer Bob Ezrin once said: "You can give him a ukulele and he can make it sound like a Stradivarius." And who am I to disagree!

Shine on,

Seb

First of all, Syd was a great and innovative songwriter. He used his vivid fantasy to mix elements from Victorian English literature with psychedelic weirdness and witty things.
