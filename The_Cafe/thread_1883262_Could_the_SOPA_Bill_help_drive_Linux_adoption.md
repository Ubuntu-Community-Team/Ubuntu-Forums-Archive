### Post by Dr. C on 2011-11-19
SOPA and is related bill Protect-IP bills are more about protecting obsolete business models and stifling competition to traditional "big content" than they are about protecting the rights of artists and creators. What many forget is that a band will sign with a major for promotion and exposure, which was vital in say 1961, and not for royalties from record sales. They typically make money from touring and live performances not record sales. The trouble is that this 1960's business model simply does not work today. Get an album prominently featured on *TPB* and one gets far more promotion and exposure that any major can deliver at minimal cost to the artist and furthermore the artist still *retains control of the copyrights!* 

What makes SOPA and Protect-IP so objectionable is that while they may provide a very small benefit to old "big content" they do this at a huge cost to perfectly legitimate Internet based business both large and small. 

To look at the original question how does SOPA and Protect-IP impact GNU / Linux, I would say the GNU / Linux would actually benefit somewhat from this legislation in that it would make it somewhat more difficult to obtain pirated Windows. It is fair to say that pirated propriety software is the single biggest obstacle to the widespread adoption of GNU / linux and FLOSS. It is also important to recognize that companies like Microsoft and Apple are far more vulnerable to a SOPA attack than say Canonical. In fact even the MPAA itself is not immune to a SOPA attack.
