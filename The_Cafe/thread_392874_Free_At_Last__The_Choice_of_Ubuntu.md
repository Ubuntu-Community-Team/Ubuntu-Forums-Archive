>  FREE AT LAST
                
                                           Monday, March 19, 2007                         
                
                     Wallace, Idaho - This rant isn't about silver at all; we figure once or twice a year we're entitled to a tirade of a different color (although in a strange way this one is connected to silver, too, because silver bulls by their very definition are a contrary lot). It has instead to do with our love-hate relationship with the personal computer, which is chock full of silver, gold, lead, rhodium, indium and other delightful metals, and our liberation from a software monopoly.
 We are just a tad harder on our personal computer than a NASCAR driver is on his Ford, Chevy, Chrysler or (this year, for the first time) Toyota. We frequently crash it and we're always pushing it to the max, for the simple reason that we make our living off the damn thing. As such, typing daily, we wear them out. There comes a time when you can't sharpen the old blade any more: there's no steel left, and it's time to get a new one.
 We have no particular bone to pick with Microsoft, except that we have fed the Redmond, Washington maw for years going back to Windows 2.0 and MS-DOS on our first new real desktop machine, an IBM clone with the Intel 80286 chip. It was a screamer for its day, boasting a 20-megabyte hard drive and 128 kilobytes of RAM which we later upgraded, for something like $1,000, to 2 megabytes of RAM with the addition of an AST Rampage board and a slew of Micron chips. After it finally blew up, we purchased an 80386 "laptop" machine by Toshiba, from the Costco store in Anchorage, and loaded it up with Windows 3.1 and Word 4.0 that ran under DOS. Another several hundred bucks off to Microsoft - 33 floppies all in as we recall. 
 After two years' duty, our laptop went up in a ball of smoke one morning and there was no salvaging it. What we had, we explained to our computer guru, was quite enough. We liked Word 4.0 for DOS, couldn't we just replace? But no, Windows 95 was just out, and a new word processor, WinWord, part of a monstrous Microsoft Office installation, would be our salvation, along with an 80486 chip and an Internet browser. WinWord immediately insulted us with its efforts to correct our grammar and diction, but we found, after hundreds of dollars of "free" technical support, how to turn those miserable features off, even as we were unwittingly infected with virus and spyware and malware programs every time we dialed up to our Internet host at the screaming speed of 26 kilobaud. 
 Which at the time was a screaming speed, up from the 75 k Bps we got from the acoustic couplers on our Radio Shack TRS-80 Model 100 laptop, which to this day remains our favourite computer. The Trash-80 Model 100 ran on four penlight batteries and had an eight-line LCD screen. It had 24k of RAM (and 24k of ROM software). You could create a fairly large Sunday-style newspaper story on the thing, save it, tweak it, then zap it to the Mother Ship's computer over the telephone line where some editor could pull it down, bugger it up, and stick it in the next day's editions below a headline. When this tiny dream machine landed in our lap in 1984 - courtesy of a $1,000 loan from Northern State Bank, co-signed by our then-editor at the Spokane, Wash.-based Spokesman-Review, Doug Clark (Doug is still a fine columnist there) - we knew we had found Nirvana. You could type up a story whilst sitting on a sailboat in the middle of Puget Sound or Coeur d'Alene Lake - or maybe at the actual scene of a crime or a breaking news story hundreds of miles from the office. Bang the thing out, then go looking for the nearest tavern that had a telephone, and zap it over to the Mother Ship. Gawd, we thought, if somebody would only invent a telephone that didn't require wires, we could do this thing from the center of the Pacific Ocean, and no-one would be the wiser that we hadn't been in our cubicle at work. The Trash-80 Model 100 remains to this day the most important breakthrough for newspaper reporters who couldn't endure the tightening rules about personal office behaviour, as it related to chain-smoking and whisky abuse.
 But we digress. Last December, watching our trusty Windows XP- and MS Office-powered SonyVaio laptop teeter on the brink of disaster on a Transportation Safety Administration body-cavity-search table at some airport, realizing that if it hit the floor and exploded, so would most of our recent intellectual efforts (c'mon, who backs up more than once a month?) we realized a second computer would be in order. We looked at our options, deciding upon a nice new machine from an Idaho supplier with a 64-bit chip and a gob of RAM. For another couple of hundred dollars or so, we would get the latest permutation of Windows XP with it, and for a few hundred more, the latest permutation of Microsoft Office.
 But we were prepared for this Microsoft gambit. Why, we asked, after thousands of dollars already expended, should we feed the Microsoft maw again? Why this kilobuck penalty because we're getting a new machine? Made no sense.
  And here we need to digress again. It started with a nasty-gram from a guy who could not read our website, which was written for Internet Explorer, with Firefox. What, we wrote back, was Firefox and who cared? A browser is a browser, we figured. Oh, No, he said, this one's better, and it's free. We tried [Firefox]("http://www.mozilla.com/en-US/firefox/") and we liked it. None of the Internet Explorer bugs, more features, blocks the pop-ups, gives you tabbed browsing, faster running. This was two years ago. Internet Explorer is still catching up, still buggy, still virus-vulnerable, still a memory hog. Meantime Firefox has learned how to read websites built with Macromedia and to run all that Java-script stuff. Without bugs and viruses. We installed Firefox on our Windows machine and have never looked back.
 Then a geek friend of ours turned us on to something called [Open Office]("http://www.openoffice.org/"). It's a collection of word processor, spreadsheet, dBase, web-page editor and power point stuff that differs from MS Office in only one startling way: it's free. Although we had installed a full-purchase version of MS Office Pro 2003 on our laptop, we downloaded and started fiddling around in Open Office and now use it exclusively. It's just a nicer, quieter version of MS Office, and fully compatible with all of Microsoft's file nomenclature, be it Word, Excel, Power point or whatever. In other words, neither the recipient nor the sender need beware that you are cheating.
 Open Office and Firefox also run under the several zillion Linux installations, so our next experiment, on a cold snowy Wallace morning, was to go looking for a Linux version that would enable us to function without learning anything as dreadful as new computer science. By happenstance more than wisdom, we landed upon Ubuntu, downloaded the disk, and installed it on our Windows XP Sony Vaio laptop, where these operating systems now peacefully co-exist. Every time you start the laptop up, it asks if you want to play in Windows or if you want to play in [Ubuntu]("http://www.ubuntu.com/"). We choose Ubuntu every time.
 So down it came to the nut-cutting time. Brand-new computer, sitting here on top of the desk. Chicken-out, go with Windows, or take the Linux plunge. Let's see: $800 for Vista and Office 2007, single install, or Ubuntu, Firefox and Open Office, all for free. The Ubuntu CD slid smoothly into the drive. It asked us a couple of dumb questions, primary of which, what would our password be, because anyone wanting to shove a virus into this machine or modify its operating system (including us) would have to know this password from here to eternity. Ubuntu installed Firefox and Open Office, went to the web for updates (they occur daily, not every year or two in the form of some gigantic service patch), cleaned itself up and went to work.
 We like Ubuntu's philosophy, to wit: Ubuntu is an African concept of "humanity towards others." It is "the belief in a universal bond of sharing that connects all humanity." Put another way: "I am because of what we all are," or, "A person is a person because of other persons." Or in yet another way: Somebody stuck their hand out to me, and I grasped it, to our mutual benefit.
 No real reason to get spiritual here. There are plenty of other Linux distributions that apparently work as well as Ubuntu, though we have not tried them all. The important thing is that Ubuntu does work. Ubuntu is the South African striking of the silver coin that is Linux. It works. Try a Chinese, or South American, or European version. It's still good; it's still Linux, it is still universal, it is still silver. 
 There are Federal Reserve interest-bearing notes that you use as dollars, just as there is Microsoft. And there is Linux, struck of pure silver. These are choices. You don't need to be a geek, or a hard-rock miner, to appreciate the difference. Further, affiant sayeth not. Free at last. Selah.
