### Post by VeeDubb on 2010-05-23
I was all ready to tear it apart, because I'm like that, but I was disappointed when I actually looked at it because i couldn't actually find anything to tear apart.  I actually think it's pretty good.  I do have a couple of pointers though.

1. Hosting from your house is perfectly fine for testing purposes, but you can't send a potential client to a dynamic DNS site, especially for an IT service.  Pretty much screams "not professional" and hosting services can be very inexpensive for people capable of building and maintaining their own site.  $80/year will get you domain registration, hosting, email, and all the basics.

2.  ".....taking advantage of deals we have in place...."   sounds sleazy.  You want everything you say to imply openness, and trust.   So, instead of "taking advantage of deals," try something more like, "The other way, is by building and maintaining strong relationships  with other small businesses in our local community."  It's all in how you say it.

3. Kviero I.T.  How about K.I.T.?  Big text/logo that says KIT, subtext that says Kviero IT.  Just a thought.  In addition to my day job, I have a photography business.  Originally, I went with Steve Coons Photography, but changed it to Stephen Michael Photography a couple months ago.  (My name is Stephen Michael Coons) because somebody reminded me that my last name happened to be an outdated racial slur, which explained why I had so much trouble with my emails and my web address getting filtered.  In business, the name is everything.  

That's really the most I can come up with, and that's being nit-picky.

### Post by kamaboko on 2010-05-27
OK...here we go.  I'm going to give you a bit of a spanking, but think of it as constructive criticism.  I only say this because I've helped a number of startups.

1.On the home page you claim that you're an expert.  Proof?  Do you have certifications? (If so, post them).  A work history?  How about testimonials from companies you've performed services for?  You want some sort of “blurb” on your main page from a few clients (not family members or friends).  How long have you been doing this professionally?  What is your standing in the IT services industry in your area?  How is that verifiable? 
2.To say that you service small businesses is too broad.  You need to identify specific types of small businesses.  For instance, you work specifically with accounting firms and architectural firms.  You know their industries, the support they need, and the software required.  For instance, what are you going to say if an accounting firm calls you up and says, “yeah, we need you to set up a server to support MAS90.”  You say, “Sure, we'll be there”.  Once you get there you discover MAS90 won't work on a Linux server.  My point is, you need to identify a target market and have canned solutions for all their needs. 
3.Scratch the entire “open source” software pitch and infomercial.  That will only confuse potential clients.  
4.You mention building relationships with small businesses to keep hardware costs down.  Give quantifiable examples.  For instance, a Dell Vostro desktop with “x” specs costs “x” amount of dollars.  We can provide the same desktop locally w/the same warranty for “x” saving you “x” percent.  As it stands now, there's nothing compelling.  
5.Don't write “various email services”.  Stick to “email services”
6.Your example pricing plan is confusing.  You state $969 for labor and $22 per hour.  Huh?  So are you saying that particular setup will take around 44 hours and anything additional is $22/hr? If so, say it.  “The following package plan is $969.  That includes.....”  Then, “our hourly rate is $22/hr for non-package related services” or something along those lines.  Also, does that plan include the servers, desktops, etc?  You need to point that out.  And just for out of curiosity, would it really take you 44 hours to set up that system?
7.And for GOD's sake don't refer to Linux as an unusual system when that's what you're trying to sell! You mention that under "support".
8.You're going to lose your *** on your support packages.  $499 for three years?  What if you end up making 30 trips over a three year period?  That's $16/trip.  So if you have a one hour commute, and two hours on site, that's just over $5/hr.  You'll chew that up in gas alone.  Dump the support services. You're not a big enough company to support it.  Go with the hourly rates.  Offer a one-year on parts and 90 days on software.  
9.Since we're on the subject of support.  How did you come up with 98% availability?  Cut it. Just say something like “All support requests will be responded to as quickly as possible.  We will reply within 24 hours of all messages and support ticket requests” or something like that. You get the point.   
10.You have to explain the value of a “trouble ticket”. 


I'm tired of typing.  I've given you enough to work on though.
