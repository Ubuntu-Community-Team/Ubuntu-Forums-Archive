When most people have never tried something "big" and unknown, like switching their **primary OS**, they don't just happy-go-lucky up and try it.  They first look at who are the people who've **already** try it and what they have to say about it.  Only after getting this info, do they start weighing options.

Let's call one such "normal person" Bob.  Bob is a fairly typical Windows user.  Most likely Bob knows some Windows users and some Mac users, who he sees as people "just like himself".  But does Bob even know any Linux users?  If so, those Linux users are usually seen as 'techie computer savvy geek' type people - often for reasons unrelated to Linux-vs-Mac, but the reasons become irrelevant once the label is stuck.

Now Bob wants to migrate away from Windows.  What's Bob going to do?  Something he's seen someone just like him do, or something he's only seen 'computer geeks' do?

Well, of course, whichever he *thinks* is easier, safer, and more doable...for him.  He'll do what he's seen 'people like him' do.  So, off to Apple he goes to order that new computer.

Yep, it really is that silly.  But I'd think there ought to be a way you could overcome this, no? :-k

### Post by montag dp on 2016-11-07
The advantages of Linux are things that most people simply don't care about, at least not enough to make the switch. Let's face it, for most Linux users, computing is a hobby. We like the technical aspects of our computers and the power and freedom that FOSS affords, so we don't mind putting in a little more effort from time to time to understand the system and fix things when they go wrong. Most people don't care about that. They just want something familiar that lets them run their applications, and they deal with the types of problems, annoyances, and abuses that vendor locked-in software entails, often unaware that it doesn't have to be that way.

There's also the perception that FOSS is low-quality or dangerous. I've heard that sentiment plenty of times. I think it is part of human nature to correlate cost with value, so people who don't know better think "free open source software" means "junky software that anyone can modify to harm you," despite the fact the proprietary software is historically much more likely to be malicious or do things behind your back (cough Windows cough Google cough cough).

If they ask, answer the question directly but don't elaborate unless asked. Short answers. One sentence if possible. Don't talk FOSS philosophy unless they're fellow nerds who are really interested in that sort of thing. If they're programmers then talk to them about development tools.
