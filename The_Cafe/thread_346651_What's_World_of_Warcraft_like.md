My opinion is that MMORPGs are over-rated. All of them.
They are not in any way individual experiences; they feel more like single player games than singel player RPGs.
Why do I say this? Sure, you can "quest" with other players, but these "quests" lack any after-effects. You as a player lack any impact, and the world that you are in lacks any logic.
(Hm... there's one farmer, maybe 5 guards, and about 1 million heavily armed "adventurers" who seem to serve no purpose except to do the same task for that same farmer Every Single Day).

If it was a truly persistent multi-player experience, as they all claim to be, then wouldn't a player be able to change something? Wouldn't one player be having a different experience from another?
In WOW, that definitely does not happen. Actually, for that matter, nothing happens. A quest consists of finding an object that 7 million other people have already found, and giving that object to a person who has already been given that object 7 million times before.
Telling someone about one's experience in finding that object always consists of the same answer: "Oh, yah, that was a funny quest. I did that one just yesterday! The object looked weird". The end.

Persistent? No. You picked that object up and it reappeared as soon as you left. As you were leaving, you saw another guy go in and come out with the same thing. It's a bit like a giant line-up to level 60. (Or, rather, 70). The kind that seems like it will be quick, then gets slower and slower at the end and keeps getting interupted by hooligans who budge in front.
Player-driven? No. Again, you have changed nothing. You gave it to the NPC farmer guy, and then you went on to do something else.
Follow-through? Anything happening as a result of your doing that quest? Unless you are very lucky... Nope.
Unique experience for everyone? Okay, you *could* have not done that quest, or you could have done it. Does anything change? No: The world of WOW is stagnant. No gate opens, no NPC disappears, no trees move. If anything changes in WOW, it is because the developers said that it would happen on a particular date after a month of planning. (Either that, or you have changed a very small thing indeed).

Sure, that's no reason for why WOW is not a decent game; they seem to do very well at hiding the sad truth, just like a linear first-person shooter such as Half-Life. But the point is: Why spend $15 a month to play WOW when you can simply spend a single lump sum to play a good RPG that doesn't have a monthly fee and which gives players a reasonably unique experience -- or at least an experience that makes some attempt to seem unique?
WOW is stagnant, but a single-player RPG is not.
In a single-player RPG, entire cities can fall to the ground and emperors can turn evil as a result of your actions. It is all the doing of *you*, the player. You do not have 8449198 clones running around doing the exact same thing as you. Your character changed the universe, and thus actually produced a storyline and maybe even an activity aside from "find that thing for me, will you?".
After all, it's not an assembly line as WOW so strongly wishes it was; it's a role-playing game.
Something happens. It is quite interesting when something happens.
"But it's not massive multiplayer!", I hear? Actually, it can be just as massively multiplayer as WOW. Open up a message board or a chat room for whatever RPG you happen to be playing, and you probably won't notice a difference. ("Oh yah, I did that quest! | Haha, me too! | ur still doing that You noob!").

(Rather than NPCs delivering information unceasingly, there are actual players trying to spread it).

### Post by orionsbelt on 2007-02-23
WoW can be a very fun game...for awhile.  I've never been a big gamer, so I don't know how it compares to other games.  I enjoyed it for 6 months and got to level 38.  There is a reason, though, why leveling up quickly is called "grinding."  It can be grindingly boring--until you get that coveted item, finally complete the damn quest, or have a funny moment of camaraderie with another player (assuming they're not spamming the chat channel).  

It does have addictive qualities.  Yes, you have control over how much you play.  However, the game does punish you for not playing it often.  Want to sell your auction items at the best rate (so you have money for learning new spells)?  You have to check the auction house.  Want to get the cool seasonal items?  You may have to be on at specific times.  Not to mention the fact that if you plan to play with people -- either in a guild or IRL (in real life) -- then you're going to be levels behind those of them who do play often.  Trust me, it's no fun to quest with someone who's 10 levels ahead/behind you.

So, if you want possible fun, an interesting community, potential addiction problems, and the risk of repetitive stress injuries, then go ahead.  It is YOUR choice.  Just know that there are a lot of fun things to do *outside* the virtual world.
