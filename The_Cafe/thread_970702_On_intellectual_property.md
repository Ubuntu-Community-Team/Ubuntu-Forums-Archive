### Post by brunovecchi on 2008-11-07
I don't think that Intellectual property applies to mathematical formulas, just as it doesn't apply to scientific facts. Formulas make factual relations that are restricted by logic/physics, not by the mind that "created" them. In this sense, formulas are discovered rather than created, hence there is no intellectual property in a formula.

### Post by Capt. Mac on 2008-11-08
I like the [Free Software Foundation's definition]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/words-to-avoid.html#IntellectualProperty") of intellectual property:

[quote=FSF]&#8220;Intellectual property&#8221;

Publishers and lawyers like to describe copyright as &#8220;intellectual property&#8221;&#8212;a term that also includes patents, trademarks, and other more obscure areas of law. These laws have so little in common, and differ so much, that it is ill-advised to generalize about them. It is best to talk specifically about &#8220;copyright,&#8221; or about &#8220;patents,&#8221; or about &#8220;trademarks.&#8221;

The term &#8220;intellectual property&#8221; carries a hidden assumption&#8212;that the way to think about all these disparate issues is based on an analogy with physical objects, and our ideas of physical property.

When it comes to copying, this analogy disregards the crucial difference between material objects and information: information can be copied and shared almost effortlessly, while material objects can't be.

To avoid the bias and confusion of this term, it is best to make a firm decision not to speak or even think in terms of &#8220;intellectual property&#8221;.[/quote]
