### Post by TheFu on 2023-09-26
If your office isn't actually a city, state, or federal building, then it isn't "public". It is private and he doesn't have any right to film there, except from outside on a "public" sidewalk. The owner of the office gets to decide who can and cannot enter and what they are allowed to do.  Many buildings have a "no photography" sign at the entrance, just to keep it clear. That wouldn't stop you and  others working there from snapping a bday photo.

Rights are very different around the world.  For example, taking a photo of a private home is illegal in Spain or taking photos of children, if the children are the center of the photo.  Children in the background, not the main focus of the photos are fine, whoever.  In Greece, taking photos near airports that have military equipment is illegal. Guess that's left over from wars when gathering information about military assets was important. 

In general, I'll ask a person if they mind being filmed, unless they are working for the city, state, or federal govt, then anything they do as part of their duties can be filmed, provided I'm not obstructing them ... on public property.  We don't have a "right" to follow a state police officer and film them in a restaurant or at a crime scene or when they are grocery shopping on the way home from work, if they and the private location do not both approve.

This sort of misconduct arises because the depressing trend in our society is to adopt only binary positions.

Courtesy (and its sibling, civility) is, by its nature, the attempt to navigate life's grey zones by erring on the side of restraint and accommodation. But this requires the understanding that there ***are*** grey zones. When simpletons do not admit of greys—when actions consist of nothing but on or off—then anything not strictly prohibited by the rules becomes fair game. This is why binary thinkers (if we can even dignify them with the term "thinkers") neither understand nor practice courtesy.

So the answer to your question is sad but simple: binary thinkers do not consider themselves to be "pushing the limits" because the concept of limits has no meaning to them. Limits require a continuum with "acceptable" on one end, "unacceptable" on the other end, and varying shades of acceptability in between. Such sophistication is completely alien to binary thinkers. With binary thinkers, if it's not prohibited, it's open season. Left or right. Open or shut. On or off.

BTW, this is the same problem with three&#8209;letter agencies and commercial data slurpers. They too take the attitude that what isn't expressly forbidden by law is expressly permitted. The idea of "norms", "values", "propriety" and "courtesy" make no impression because such concepts are qualitative: requiring nuance, appreciation for grey zones and a commitment to self&#8209;restraint. Can you see any agency or commercial data entity practising self&#8209;restraint?
