### Post by czr114 on 2010-11-12
> **KiwiNZ said:**
> 
Bottom line is , price and affordability is no excuse to pirate... period.  Either buy cheaper or use FOSS alternatives.

In reality, that's a tough sell to many students and autodidacts trying to learn Photoshop for later use in a commercial context, as well as part-time graphic artists, or those living outside Western markets.

When I was at university, almost all of the design students had CS, and it was 100% pirate. University labs were not sufficient for those studying in their own time.

How much harm that caused is debatable. Well-trained students with honed, vendor-specific skills are what creates much of the demand for Adobe products in the real world. Had they not learned CS, including on pirate installations, they wouldn't have the skills required for creating commercial demand for Adobe products following graduation.

CS piracy is also prolific throughout Eastern Europe. In many situations, the choice is between using pirate CS to create some benefit for someone, somewhere, and not using CS at all, which results in no benefit, for anyone, anywhere.

It's easy to bemoan piracy as being wholly without merit. Certainly, somebody who pirates a game is looking to get out of paying for the game. In the real world, though, commercial software producers are neglecting key demand segments, which results in lost benefits to humanity as a whole.

The products themselves have a near-zero marginal cost of production. CS can be pirated for a mere few cents in bandwidth and electricity.

In a perfect world, GIMP would be a viable substitute, and everybody would have enough money to pay for all the software they need to make the world a better place.

The commercial software producers can alleviate some of this misallocation through alternate distribution channels, such as academic licensing.

The Western-centric commercial price point is hindering the creation of value in non-Western, or non-commercial situations, such as a student in the dorms, or a part-time graphic artist in Moscow.

There really isn't a solution to that problem. CS is priced to maximize profit from the Western commercial markets. There isn't any way to enable price discrimination based on underlying utility differentiation.

This is why I've been an advocate of rewriting copyright law to  incorporate the principle of no harm, no foul. If there's no lost sale,  then there is no harm. If there is no criminal or civil sanction for causing no harm, then at least somebody can draw some benefit from the marginal creation of a few cents worth of data transfer and electricity.

FOSS is supposed to allow everyone to derive benefit from software, to the limits of his or her ability and market, by not charging for an extremely cheap act of creating an additional copy at the margin.

### Post by czr114 on 2010-11-12
> **Paqman said:**
> His point was that with a digital product the cost of duplication is near zero, so your costs don't increase with the number of units you ship. So selling two for $500 will give you the same profit as selling one for $1000.
The demand curve doesn't work that way.

At $0.01 per copy, Adobe goes broke. At $1,000,000 per copy, Adobe goes broke.

The maximization is somewhere in the middle, and it isn't based on a linear function. Adobe is going to set the price to extract as much money from the commercial, Western-centric market, but not so high that it causes defection or commercial piracy.

If the price were dropped to $500, they'd be halving their revenue from the existing commercial base who is going to buy anyway, but only marginally increasing it from part-timers and smaller outfits using pirate goods, less-effective alternatives, or nothing at all. To convert many of the part-timers using pirate software, or the current non-users, they'd probably have to get the whole suite down under $100, and even then, that won't abate the pirates who prefer the completely free pirate copy, which lacks the nuisance of activation, and doesn't require getting on a mailing list when purchasing a license. Pricing it at that level would devastate their revenue, and hinder future development.

Our classical theories of price were based on tangible widgets made from scare materials, or inherently scare man hours.

Software distribution over the Internet has a scarcity profile that is so minimal as to be effectively zero.

FOSS and SaaS are newer ways of thinking about how to distribute digital goods.

Piracy, be it right, wrong, or somewhere in between, is the manifestation in reality of gaps in the classical distribution models. It's manifestation should be seen as a symptom of untapped opportunities. Comparing it to a kid shoplifting at the mall does a disservice to trying to think about how to distribute a capital good with negligible marginal production costs in a way which creates the greatest benefit for humanity.

### Post by czr114 on 2010-11-12
[LEFT]Since CS is a capital good, as opposed to something such as a video game, it helps to understand its economic role like any other capital good.

A tractor is an example of a capital good. They're expensive to produce, owing to the labor required to design them, the raw materials necessary to manufacture them, and the labor necessary to conduct that manufacturing.

The good is thus inherently scarce.

Subsistence farmers typically don't use tractors, as the benefit gained from additional capital is outweighed by the cost of that capital. Thus, it can be said it is inefficient to allocate tractors to subsistence farmers, in a scenario in which the input costs of the tractor are not outweighed by it's ability to create useful goods.

If said farmer steals a tractor, he is depriving another of his property, as well as that person's ability to apply that capital to creating an economic surplus. Thus, the benefit to the thief is dwarfed by the loss of benefit from the victim of the theft. Theft misallocates resources, as it bypasses the rationing and allocative functions of price. Typically, society's net position is decreased through theft.

If we had the ability to create an infinite number of tractors at negligible cost, every farmer, subsistence, small-commercial, and large-commercial, would all be using them. Every farmer would benefit, as no farmer would be without that useful capital good. Society would also benefit through the increased aggregate output of food.

Software, unlike tractors, can be replicated and distributed at near-zero cost through protocols such as BitTorrent (support or physical packaging is still scarce).

High-priced software prevents individuals or irregular businesses from reaping the creative power of a digital capital good, in those situations in which said producer can't overcome the price point.

There is no perfect solution to this problem. With FOSS, useful software is put into as many hands as possible, allowing anyone who can use that software to create benefit to do so. With FOSS, however, the best we can do is GIMP, which I don't think any reasonable person would argue is a comparable substitute for Photoshop. The difference is even more pronounced with more expensive software, such as commercial CAD or engineering platforms.

Without the high price point, specialized software won't be produced (absence an incredible upswell in FOSS development volunteers). With the high price point, only some producers can use the software to enhance the creative power of their labor.

There is no straightforward solution to this problem. Commercial software will always underallocate, and as long as FOSS development energy remains scarce, FOSS won't achieve the degree of specialization necessary for certain commercial tasks.
[/LEFT]

Take, for example, our understanding of Shakespeare. While his works were preserved, many of his contemporaries and influences were lost to history, thus limiting our ability to fully understand historical context and influence.

For the first time in human history, we have the capability to ensure that no intellectual work product is lost. Our technology can preserve, compile, cross-reference, and make redundant all of today's notable knowledge, in all its forms. What stands in its way is a copyright paradigm created before the digital age.

While I'm not hostile to the idea of copyright, or of people being able to make a living through creativity, I find it disappointing that we're not leveraging this exciting opportunity solely because of outdated ways of relating to information.

I see technologies such as DRM and activation as standing in the way of this opportunity.
