> 
--1000 Years Later--

News Reporter: Hi everyone, I'm Amy Tucker. In today's episode of 20/20 we are going to be talking to Dr. Arnold Reese about life in the 21st century. Dr. Reese got his doctorate in Internet Analogy from Yale in 3011. This is going to be a long broadcast with a lot of interesting information. So doctor, without taking up any more of your time, what is the most interesting thing you have uncovered on the 21th century computer network?

Dr Reese: Recent discoveries on an older part of the Internet (the predecessor of the Virtuality Network) have shown a shocking trend among the people of that time. Apparently, People would spend long hours day and night uploading hundreds of terabytes of personal information about themselves. It's through their relentless dedication and sacrifice, that we are able to recreate the 21st century to an almost scale model today.

Amy: Now doctor, when you say dedication, what do you mean by that?

Dr. Reese: Well, the historians of the Internet had to put up with numerous issues, such as lack of social life, lack of exercise, poor diet. Most of them would fill up on pizza and Coca Cola (a historic foaming drink which contained caffeine to allow them to continue doing their jobs unrelently. It's through this dedication, that we are able to reconstruct their lives on (what is a first time for that era) a personal basis.

Amy: How personal are we talking doctor?

Dr. Reese: If their Internet had a brain scanner (back in the day) like our Virtuality Network, we would be able to build a 95% profile for their characters in the network. Now, there are certain essential characteristics which were left unposted to the Internet in the name of privacy, but today, everyone owns their own brain scan within the network, and is able to pick and choose which parts they want to share and with whom to share them.

Amy: Doctor, you still haven't answered my question. What do you think the most interesting thing about the 21st century Internet is or was.

Dr. Reese: Mrs, it's ok to call you Mrs, or miss?

Amy: Mrs. is fine.

Dr. Reese: Mrs. Tucker, I would have to say, the most interesting finds I found on the 21st century Internet were the numerous ways they found to occupy their time on it. It almost seems as if they were addicted to it. They would spend large quantities of hours during the day on it just looking for a reason to be on it. Why, I recently found an online text based game called "The Last Place Trophy Game" on Ubuntu's old question and answer services page.

Amy: Ubuntu still keeps it's records from that era.

Dr. Reese: Well, you're talking minute amounts of data. Small amounts of Terabytes. Nothing overly populative. In fact, all the data we have on the old Internet could fit on 5 flash drives.

Amy: Wow, I was thinking something a little larger, anyway, get back to what you were saying Dr. Reese.

Dr. Reese: Well, in this game called "The Last Place Trophy Game", the winner had to be VERY agile. They would often wait up at wee hours of the night, just to steal a pretend trophy so they could claim ownership of it and call everyone who had the trophy before them losers for allowing it to be stolen from them.

Amy: Interesting. . .

Dr. Reese: Yes, well this fueled the majority of teenagers at the time to become historians to this great era of human history.

Amy: Well, we're out of time. Dr. Reese, It was great having you on this show. Maybe you can tell me a little bit more about your archaeological finds back stage. Good night everyone and good luck.
