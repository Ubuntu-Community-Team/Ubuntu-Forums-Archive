### Post by TheFu on 2015-04-21
Without a degree, you will be paid less - perhaps $20k less per year - is that "a little?".  Many jobs will not be open to you without a degree too. Larger corporations require degrees in "a technical field" to hold a technical job.  Startups seem to care whether you can do the work, fast, and pivot with any new challenge.

Sure, you can work freelance once you prove yourself and you might make lots of money that way with many small clients.  Do you have a friend who owns a consulting company and will hire you? Are you enough of a self-starter to become active in a F/LOSS project, rise to the top of the contributors and will your skill outshine everyone else on the project?  Then you may be better off skipping college.

Both methods can lead to rewarding careers.  I don't use anything today that college directly taught me as an engineer. What college did was teach me how to think critically, not believe everything people in power say, and, most importantly, it taught me how to teach myself new things over years.

College degrees open doors and you may or may not choose to enter. Without a degree, 40% of those doors will be closed until you have 10-15 yrs of a proven track record.

Getting a CS degree doesn't always lead to a programming job.  Most of the people I've met who work as programmers do not have CS degrees. I don't.  Some of the best programmers I know have degrees in English, Art History, political science because none of them could find jobs in their fields and had to retrain to pay rent. Lots of math, engineers, physics majors work as programmers too.

If you just want to learn to code, in my metro area most of the popular scripting languages have FREE training classes on the weekends for about 8 months of the year. Ruby does and there is a separate RubyGirls group since women appear to learn better in segregated classes.  BTW - RailsConf is going on this week. Live streaming on youtube now.

Some countries have special immigration quotas for technical people - The Netherlands does, for example.  Other countries limit the work that foreigners are allowed to take to household or manual labor. i knew a French Medical Student forced to be a waiter in Switzerland, because he wasn't allowed to take a better job.  There are many rules like that around the world.

More and more there are jobs we can do from anywhere in the world.  I had one where it was conference calls and file transfers with a face-to-face meeting about once a quarter.  I never had to meet directly with clients. It sucks to be in Istanbul tied to a hotel network to do work when just a yards away is 4,000 years of history waiting to be explored.  Plus having to shift my schedule to be on Pacific time while there isn't fun.  Of course, some of the most interesting places in the world don't have good internet connections or even stable power.  Internet connections without power 6 hrs at a time is hard, just sayin'.  Did that for a few weeks in Nepal.  Of course, in places like Tokyo or Seoul internet connectivity is better than at my house, but the 12-13 hr TZ shift can suck on work days.

### Post by user1397 on 2015-04-22
Having been in a similar situation as you, I felt compelled to reply to you.

I'm 24 now, and working on a Computer Information Systems degree.  It's a bit of a mixed degree, as it is located in the business college, so I have to take several business courses as a requirement, but as far as computer courses I have to take programming and IT related courses.

For a while there, I was a bit lost and didn't know what to do, and almost dropped out of college.  I actually posted on these forums a while back asking people pretty much the same thing you just asked haha.

From everything I was able to gather, it seems that although yes, if you teach yourself computer skills such as programming, you *can* get a job and work your way up that way, but it is by FAR a WAY better idea to have a degree as a base, and go from there.  You maybe won't even learn much through your courses for whatever reason (maybe the teacher sucks, or the way the class is structured sucks, or the material is out of date, etc) but that doesn't matter too much because you should always be teaching yourself and studying on the side, even as a full time student.  From what I hear, this is especially true if your end goal is to be a programmer.  I've heard from many programmers themselves that they didn't really learn much in school, and were largely or fully self-taught.  However, SO many employers won't even look twice at your application if you have no Bachelor's degree or up. Is it a bit sad that this is how the world works? Maybe.  Can you do anything about it? Probably not.

A point of contention: not everyone in the tech world who has a degree has one in a computer-related field.  I personally know a few people like this.  One guy I know only got interested in programming his senior year (he was a journalism major), and he taught himself and is now working for a software company as a programmer.  So just because you have your degree in biology or fine arts or history, it is STILL valuable in many situations.  Many employers see a Bachelor's as a minimal base standard, and a lot of companies job application systems probably disregard applications that don't have college degrees (not sure about this but I would bet money on it).

If you have difficulty with hard sciences like chemistry and physics then some of these Computer Science or Computer Engineering majors are going to be difficult because many have some pretty hard courses as requirements (not to mention Calculus and Linear algebra etc).

I know I am *capable* of passing a physics course for example, but I would need to study literally 24/7 to pass (whereas to some people subjects like this come more naturally).  This is why I ended up going for the major I'm doing (CIS) because it is a little lighter on its requirements and is more of an all-around major which lets me see what area I'm more interested in.  Not saying this is the way you should go, but maybe you might like a major like this as opposed to strictly Computer Science; it is definitely worth it to at least check it out.

Now, if you are a programming prodigy and found bugs in the linux kernel when you were 7, ignore all of the above and just keep doing what you're doing :)

### Post by TheFu on 2015-04-22
In Asia, being a native English speaker is often enough to get the lower paying jobs.  Getting a cert, raises the pay and having a formal teaching degree raises the pay even higher.  Of course, English teachers in Asia generally aren't paid well by what I'd be willing to accept, but if you are used to hostel life, perhaps it would be possible.

Don't expect to work remotely programming.  That is still the exception.  Most companies hiring programmers want to see them daily, in their seats, inside the building.  If you are able to prove your programming skill is exceptional, only then will you be able to set the rules.  Generally, it takes 7-15 yrs to get to that level. Some people do it quicker, very few. If you are realistic, 10 yrs is a normal estimate to get to expert level in 1 language.  Plus, don't forget that there are colleges and schools world-wide putting out new programmers with extremely low living expenses.  Would I pay an American $30/hr or someone from Kenya $7/hr to write a DB app in python?  Well, if I were an American company and expected the person to be in my building ...
