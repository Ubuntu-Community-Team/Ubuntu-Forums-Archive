### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2009-04-09
If I see a new user compiling from source, I usually assume they don't know about repositories or package management and made the common faux-pas of going to the developer's website and downloading something that looked like an installer (which was actually source code).

About 2/3 of the time I'm right, the rest of the time it's someone trying to get functionality that can only be got by compiling.

The thing about open development is that there will ALWAYS be functionality you can only get by compiling from source.  Because new functionality is always being developed, and by the time things are released there's a newer version out there which can be compiled.  Sometimes newer versions can't be put in the repositories due to dependency issues and version conflicts.  

As I see it, compiling will always be something people will want to do, but it doesn't always have to be something people HAVE to do.  It will be mitigated 
 - when Linux is popular enough that it isn't playing catch-up to every new feature/program/hardware in the proprietary world.
 - when there are enough "average joe" users of a distro that developers see a point in releasing packages for that distro directly.

### Post by Stupendoussteve on 2009-04-09
I have no problem with people compiling their own packages. I do not think it should be forced on users, unless the distribution decides to go that route.

It is important that people recognize and are aware that installing packages independently of the package manager, on any distribution (even Gentoo), often leads to issues with competing files, broken dependencies, etc. It would be better if people learned how to make their own customized .deb packages which could be managed through the existing tools, just like local overlays allow in Gentoo and AUR allows in Arch. I would encourage this vs straight up compile and install.
