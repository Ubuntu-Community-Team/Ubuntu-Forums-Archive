### Post by Tuna-Fish on 2007-02-24
> **louis_nichols said:**
> It is true that implications are great. Frankly, I can't understand this concept of patenting ideas. We'll soon have mathematics professors patenting formulas. Writers patenting stylistic methods.

Certain kind of formulas and algorithms can be and have been patented. 
> 
It makes me almost wonder that nobody has patented the idea of idle time on a computer. And then take money from all application vendors whose applications leave unused CPU cycles. I hear MS wanted to patent the idea of double click. I would have patented the idea of making the mouse for right-handed users, then let everyone learn how to use their left hand or cache in.

While you can patent pretty much anything, if someone you try to sue for patent fees can prove there is "prior art", or someone else has done it before, the patent does not hold.
> 
How about patenting every single stupid idea that comes to mind? That asks for NO R&D INVESTMENTS WHATSOEVER. Then we keep quiet about it and in 5 years time see who has implemented something similar to one of our ideas. Then we take them to court and cache in. Now THAT would be a business model. Hell, we wouldn't even need programmers for that.

There are really only 2 requirements for getting a patent, 1st, nobody can have patented it before, and 2nd, with your patents you must provide specific enough instructions so that someone can replicate your work using them. Patenting every crazy idea hardly satisfies nr 2.

To understand patents, one must remember why they were originally created. Before patents, people and companies that invented something great concealed it to profit from it, severely slowing progress and sometimes taking the inventions with them when they died. Patents were invented to increase speed of technological progress by providing people with monetary compensation (in the form of a monopoly on the patent for a set number of years) in exchange for teaching everyone else how to use their invention. In it's job, patent has been largely successful.

Software patents are not inherently flawed, but they are plagued by 2 problems: 1st, the people granting patents and deciding on their use have absolutely no connection with the technology the patents are for, resulting in massive numbers of patents covering so trivial things they should never have been patented, and 2nd, the speed of technological progress in software world is so fast that the patent timeouts are far far too long.
