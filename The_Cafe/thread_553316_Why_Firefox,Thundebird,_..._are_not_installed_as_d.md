### Post by t0p on 2007-09-17
I'm always a little puzzled when people complain about how "difficult" or "complicated" it is to install apps in Ubuntu.

Okay, so Synaptic isn't a "one click" operation.  But it's still pretty damn simple to operate.  You decide which repos you want open.  You type the name of an app or a description of what it does in the Search field.  And Synaptic presents you with a list to choose from.  If your choice has dependencies (other apps it needs in order to work) Synaptic asks if it should fetch them too.

A few mouse clicks.  Synaptic downloads the packages, configures them, installs them, then tells you it's done.

I understand there may sometimes be problems - broken packages for some reason - Synaptic itself may break.  But I don't think these problems are very common.  They haven't happened to me yet.

The only time I've had problems installing apps has been when I wanted something that *wasn't* in .deb form - a game on a CD.  And I got it sorted in the end.

But with Windows - and its wonderful InstallShield wizards - I have had a few problems.  Not lots of problems, I admit - but between the two, I prefer Synaptic.

### Post by igknighted on 2007-09-17
You don't get any of those things installed by default in windows, but yet you demand them in linux?  Examples:

1) Firefox does not integrate with KDE well and repeats the functionality of an already excellent browser (Konqueror).

2) Thunderbird, while a great pop/imap mail client, does not have the calendaring and other more PIM specific features of Evolution or Kontact.

3) Non-free codecs do not come on windows or linux in a complete set.  For example, DivX must be installed separately on windows, and more than likely also ogg and flac and other high quality codecs.  In Ubuntu there are other sets missing (mp3 for example), but they are left out because of legal restrictions, rather than a simple oversight by the software provider.

I don't know why I bothered to address these specifically, you are missing some very fundamental ideas behind linux and Ubuntu.  I suggest you read the articles on Ubuntu.com that describe the freedom of software they are trying to promote in order to gain a better understanding of why ubuntu (and other linux distro's as well) does things the way it does.
