### Post by userundefine on 2006-08-13
> **Jh00 said:**
> 1- Is Open Source the same as Free Software (in the linux sense?)
No, not necessarily.  'Free Software' refers to free speech, not free beer.  Therefore, automatically there is the possibility to make money off it.  Check out the [FSF's differentiation of the terms]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-software-for-freedom.html").
> 2- How can someone make money out of it?
See above.  Also, you can make money providing support.  Cy**gnu**s was one of the first companies providing support.  [They merged with Red hat in 99.]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_Solutions#Cygnus_Solutions")
> 3- Let's say that Ubuntu fixes bug n.1 (microsoft domination): wouldn't it throw out of the market a great number of computer professionals?
Anyone basing their entire business model on the work of another company risks losing their business should it go under.  Microsoft trying to make their OS more secure puts anti-virus, anti-spyware, anti-malware, anti-anti-anti-Windows-exploit software makers out of business.  Is this a bad thing?  Well, when you base your business on the faults of another business's product, and they fix that product, how is that a sound business solution for professionals?  But honestly to answer your question more directly I don't think it will.

Companies that base themselves entirely on Windows solutions (which aren't open-source) will probably have a harder time converting their work over to X operating system, and they may find a market already saturated with products similar to theirs or irrelevant (AV on *nix, for example).  And at that point it's simple supply/demand.  Programmers, however, would likely not be put out of work by it, since they could just apply their language skills to other projects (save VB code, maybe).

> 4- Most work done in Linux, I think, come from people that usually code for a living. In the future, if most of the coding goes to open source software, wouldn't it mean lots of unnemployed programers?
I'm not sure I understand the question.  Are you saying that most GPL software is a direct result of people being *paid* to code it, or that it comes from spare time of programmers who code for a living (on something other than GPL perhaps)?  Either way, the truth is the latter.  Most work done in Linux come from people that usually code for a living and work on 'Linux work' in their spare time.  But, like Stallman says, [no one has to be a programmer for a living.]("http://www.gnu.org/gnu/manifesto.html")

> 5- My friend develops a commercial product that is sold in its binary form. How could he "adapt" to the future (according to Mark's vision) and still make money?
Release the source code and make money supporting the program.  Release the source code and still sell the product.  Other ways I probably haven't thought of.  You'd do well to watch [Revolution OS]("http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0308808/"), which not only details the history of Free Software, Open Source, and Linux, but also the businesses that sprung up around it.

### Post by Erik Trybom on 2006-08-13
Let us point one thing out - it is impossible to make money selling GPL'd software. Since both the binaries and the source code are free to share, you cannot get any money that way. Sure, you could charge 100$ for each CD of your program, but the license would allow the store next to yours to sell exactly the same CD:s for 5$ each (or some site to offer it for free). This is different from piracy since the GPL explicitly allows it - and since you've signed it, you allow it too.

Support is a viable way for some companies. If you sell server software or other corporate tools, it might pay. However, you'd be mad to release a computer game under the GPL hoping to make money off support. The number of people needing support for a game, and willing to pay for it, would be minimal.

There is a difference between Open Source and GPL, though. I believe there could be written a license where you pay for the software and still get to play with the source code. However, I think the best we can hope for in the long run is a mix between open and closed source software.

### Post by peabody on 2006-08-15
> **Jh00 said:**
> Hi there.

...

1- Is Open Source the same as Free Software (in the linux sense?)


No, though Free Software can be considered Open Source, Open Source is not necessarily Free Software (confusing I know).  Open Source is simply a design methodology, while Free Software is a social movement and doesn't really care much about the economic models of software (well, it does care, but the morals of Free Software trump all economic concerns).

> 
2- How can someone make money out of it?


Software as service is the wave of the future.  Even Microsoft is going in this direction with Microsoft Live.  It's not the software itself that has value, it is its maintanence and upkeep that has value.  If you need the software to do something you either hire your own programmers, or pay a company that performs service on the software for you.  Very often these kind of service companies are the people who created the open source product.  JBoss is an example of this.

> 
3- Let's say that Ubuntu fixes bug n.1 (microsoft domination): wouldn't it throw out of the market a great number of computer professionals?


Maybe, but this sort of thing has happened in the computer world all the time, NT overtakes Novell and all the Novell professionals are out of work, etc, etc...  If Ubuntu overtakes Microsoft's market share it just means more money for Linux programmers and less money for Windows programmers :).  It's a dog eat dog world.  I know there are a lot of old and bitter OS/2 programmers out there in the world...

> 
4- Most work done in Linux, I think, comes from people that usually code for a living. In the future, if most of the coding goes to open source software, wouldn't it mean lots of unnemployed programers?


No, again, "Software as Service".  It means a lot more Linux programmers will be hired to perform work for corporate firms so the software does what the firms need it to do.

> 
5- My friend develops a commercial product that is sold in its binary form. How could he "adapt" to the future (according to Mark's vision) and still make money?


Depends, right now he probably will make more money if it is closed source.  However, if a competitor produces a competing product down the road and releases it as freeware or open source and that product begins to undermine your friend's product's marketshare, then your friend might as well go open source because it maximizes the competitiveness and mindshare of his product and helps keep his investment maintained.  He then sets up a service model for the product that he can sell to people who use the software.


Software is increasingly becoming more of a commodity and less of an expensive luxury.  Some people may see this as the market being undermined but it's inevitable.  The bleeding edge will probably always have a proprietary nature to it, but eventually the demand will drop and what's new will become dirt cheap or free and at that point, why not go open source?  If these sorts of things didn't happen, we'd still be paying $30 for the latest web browser and software innovation would be at an all time low.

I've always seen open source as the great normalizer of proprietary software.  Either you're as good as or better than Open Source equivalents, or you can't even compete, at which point you might as well try contributing to an open source product and setting up a service model.  Think of it as an investment.

### Post by IYY on 2006-08-15
> 1- Is Open Source the same as Free Software (in the linux sense?)

Free software usually means it is licensed under the GPL. All Free Software is Open Source Software, but not all Open Source Software is Free. For example, the BSD license is also open source, but it's not at all similar to the GPL.

> 3- Let's say that Ubuntu fixes bug n.1 (microsoft domination): wouldn't it throw out of the market a great number of computer professionals?

It would throw out some computer professionals, but a very small portion. The reality is that very few software developers make commercial applications. Most, like myself, make software that's used only inside a specific company and is never meant to be sold commercially: engineering, medical, military, government, business stuff... That's where the money is. Free software makes this kind of business easier, because you can use any GPL code in your project (and you don't have to release your changes if you're only using your product inside the company).  

But of course, it is highly unlikely that closed source, commercial software will disappear. Even if the entire world uses Ubuntu, companies like Microsoft will be able to make binary releases of their software (like MS Office) to be used in Ubuntu.

> 4- Most work done in Linux, I think, come from people that usually code for a living. In the future, if most of the coding goes to open source software, wouldn't it mean lots of unnemployed programers?

It depends on the product, but it's quite unlikely that your friend will be able to make money according to Mark's (actually Richard Stallman's) vision. > I've been thinking about this for a while, so if you want to make a linux programme, and don't want to reveal the source code, you just send the binary version and nobody can see the source code, is this right, so can Adobe sell Linux software that is closed without anybody getting to the source.

Yes, a developer could do that.  In fact, there's nothing wrong with selling proprietary software to Linux users, but it is a problem with many Linux users, because they want the source to be open.

You could always sell the binary, and make sure no one gets to the source.  If people have free access to your source, you obviously can't do much, because someone will create his/her own binary, and distribute it to others.  So, how could you make money off of this when someone is distributing a binary of your program?  You can't.

> I think there is arguments for both sides, free software and get revenue from service contracts,

Making money off a service is FAR less profitable than selling a product.  That's a given.  This is why the tech industry is so profitable.    You simply sell a CD with your program, and make lots of money off it, because you spend only a little money on making copies.

I, although a proponent of capitalism, am caught somewhere between allowing proprietary software, and at the same time asking for a program to be open source.
