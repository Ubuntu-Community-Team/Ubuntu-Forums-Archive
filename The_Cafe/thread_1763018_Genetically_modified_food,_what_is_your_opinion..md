### Post by snowpine on 2011-05-19
In general I oppose GMO food because I support organic agriculture and a "slow food" lifestyle. 

That being said, most of the foods we eat are "genetically modified" from a certain point of view. The animals and plants that we raise are often very different from their wild ancestors. This has been accomplished over thousands of years through technologies including selective breeding, hybridization, seed/sperm banking, grafting, etc. 

Non-GMO agriculture has had tremendous successes but also spectacular failures (Irish potato famine, American dust bowl, etc.) and it is logical to assume that GMO agriculture will have its successes and failures as well.

Is the author the one who wrote Gulliver's Travels and stuff?

And regarding genetic foods I have no objection in it's consumption, but I think it's too early. A gene has can have roles in multiple processes and since we don't know each and every of other biological processes, it's quite a stretch to say we know what the exact effects will be when eliminating and adding DNA sequences.

There's also the matter of genetic diversity. I'm not talking about the wilderness but the diversity of the food we eat. Normally without GM, we used have a variety of a particular food, say corn, that would only grow in certain areas during certain time of the year. But with the appearance of GM, the variety we can feed on has decreased dramatically. The farmers switches to GM corn for the potential profit it carries, eventually decreasing the choice available to us.

The following is targeted to the other readers of this thread. Patent trolling isn't restricted to computer industry, it's the same in farming. I don't exactly remember the details, but it seems if the patented gene spreads to a neighboring farm growing regular crops and the company finds out, you'll be suspected.

And there's another thing - GM is *unnecessary*. At least in the Western first world countries. ON the contrary to the global trend, there's been a tremendous amount of food wasted due to oversupply. We dom't need *more* food, we need *cheaper* food. GM doesn't always result in cheaper food either. There are some GM that simply makes the crops resistant to certain pesticide, and the farmers need more of that with every passing year because the weeds starts to become resistant too.

Continuing of the need of GM, yes, there's a need in Africa and, to a certain extent, Asia but who's going to bear the cost?

My conclusion is, playing with genes for research and knowledge is all fine and dandy, but there's not much practical application the agricultural sector. It's easier to cross-breed tough variants which can be done by the farmer himself avoiding patent trollers.

### Post by tumbes2000 on 2011-05-20
Genetically modified food is essential to feeding the planet and not just by growing food faster.  For example, Round-up ready crops are good for the environment in a number of ways.  1) Round-up ready crops do not require the earth to be tilled for the plant to grow saving millions of gallons in diesel fuel and preventing sediment runoff.  It also requires less crop maintenance over the course of the growing season, again saving fuel.  

There are also new genetically altered crops that have much better moisture management, in other words they can grow with less water or can store water more effectively to survive dry spells during the growing season.  In asia and india where rice is a staple crop they are developing rice that can survive under water for weeks at a time so instead of losing 50% of the rice crop during the rainy season, only 25% is lost.  

Regarding animals, antibiotics, growth hormones, etc are again beneficial to us.  First, no large population of animals can live close together without the threat of disease (see all of human history until the infection of indoor plumbing, antibiotics, and vaccines).  With antibiotics farmers can stop the spread of disease (such as mad cow) and preserve the herd.  Growth Hormone increases the amount of meat generated per pound of food ingested decreasing the amount of grain necessary to reach a desired weight in animals and increasing the amount of grains available for human consumption.  

At the end of the day we have an increasing human population (mainly in poor countries) and we have to become more efficient at feeding ourselves (read lower food costs for poor countries). It would be nice to have all of our food grown in a less "corporate" manner, but that will lead to drastically higher food costs, which means more malnourishment in developing countries.  You cant ask someone to go hungry because genetically modified crops "seem" bad.
