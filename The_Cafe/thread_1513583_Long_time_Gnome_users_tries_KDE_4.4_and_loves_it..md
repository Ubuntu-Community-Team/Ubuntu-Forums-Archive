### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-06-19
So, been using Gnome since the beginning, and every time I'd try something else, like Xfce4, Openbox, e17, I'd find a lot of neat things and improvements, but I'd also run into a few things I would miss from Gnome. Much of it seemed to be kinda'... hit and miss.

In Xfce4, I really liked how fast and slim it was, had its own compositing manager that took up less RAM, Thunar is a beautiful file manager, but a the menus didn't seem to integrate well with each other and it all seemed sort of slipshod in some areas (don't get me wrong, LOVE Xfce4, but not gonna' use it every day).

OpenBox is great, but I like to have a little beef to my DE (I know it's a window manager, but bear with me as I call whatever I mash together my DE).

e17 is beautiful, and has ecomorph, but still doesn't blend with compiz so much.

So, after realizing it's hard to get rounded menus in anything GTK-based, I went to take a look at KDE 4 - I tried it when it first came out, and it was a bit rough, but here's what I like about it now that they've smoothed it out.

In the beginning, a lot of functionality was hidden or placed with other functionality less likely to be used in KDE 4's settings- now it seems that everything is in the right place, and it's easy to get to features you'll more likely want to tweak/customize.

It's just gorgeous. Gnome can be just as "pretty," (it's subjective), but usually people wanna' change their themes. KDE 4 is pretty by default, which reduces the competition for window borders and the such by a great deal, which seems to give the developer base more unity in how they want to code programs and how they should feel. It really does help a lot when you think about it.

Which brings up my next point- everything just feels very integrated, just like Gnome. It's great that you can use GTK in KDE and QT in Gnome, and I'm glad the theme compatibility functions so well in both DEs.

A great thing about Amarok, Rekonq, Plasmoids, etc. is that this stuff's all included. In Gnome, there's plenty to try out in these areas and it's great to find high quality software, but it's all third-party addons when you think about it. Screenlets, docks, tons of duplicated effort (which as I discuss in a moment is a good thing) for similar programs, etc. It's nice to have some focus, especially when you're new to the DE.

Also, one thing beyond polish, integration, rounded menus, blah blah blah is just the matter of convenience it offers. When there's a new theme/addon, no matter what program/setting it is, there seems to be a "Get New/More" button that automatically goes to KDE-Look or related sites and lets you preview and install additions on the fly.

In Gnome you have a link that sends you to the site to get a tar.gz to drag on top of your Themes window. If you think Gnome makes it easier to do these kinds of customizations, please do explain.

Gnome is great, it's beautiful, and its themes reflect its design- simple, clean, easy to look at. I love Gnome.

KDE is great, it's beautiful, and its themes reflect its design- a little over the top, a nice kick of spice, interesting to watch. I love KDE.

And this brings me to the big point I've noticed that I cherish between Gnome and KDE that I don't see so much in Xfce4, e17, etc. Other than e17, most of those other window managers and setups seem more like a slimmed down Gnome experience.

KDE, rather than imitating, is something vastly different. Gnome and KDE represent two sides to a coin of visual design in computing, and by that virtue both become exceptional in their given areas. This diversity is important to users, to give them choice.

KDE does some things better than Gnome, Gnome does many things better than KDE, but in the end they're both awesome and great buddies. I don't see why they need to fight, and I hope our community continues to foster an air of openness and friendliness towards any programming that is done for the sake of the users (developers are users, too XD).

I hope this topic will be about the good values of desktop environments, and in what ways we can improve them. I'd also like to discuss Gnome 3 and what features we can look forward to that further explore these differences between the environments.

I'm sorry, but KDE and Gnome are both very high quality, and Windows environments don't seem to offer much improvement on them. I must be crazy, but I find it all much easier and simpler to use than I ever found Windows.
