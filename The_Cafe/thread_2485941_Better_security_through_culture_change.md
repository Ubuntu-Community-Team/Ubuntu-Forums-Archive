### Post by Topsiho on 2023-04-17
This attitude towards accidents is not new. In my younger days I was a young ship's officer, and had to read the investigations after some accidents that had happened to ships, just to learn from it.

One was the investigation of a ship that sank after leaving port: it capsized very unexpectedly, after the umptieth time it had transported the same cargo, from the same port.
Such an investigation had the SOLE intention of finding out extensively what had happened, and did not involve any criminal investigation.
I forgot the name of what was the cargo, It was considered safe, and nothing had indicated so far it was not.
It appeared however that during  the loading (it was before containers were used) the cargo was on the quay alongside the ship in a slight drizzle, nothing to get worried about.
After experiments it appeared that when this type of cargo was very slightly wet, within a small margin, it became quick as water.
Now, there is one thing that is dangerous to a ship's stability, and that is a large surface of water in the hold of a ship: the water flows to the lower side of the ship and the ship rolls over.

After that this sort of cargo was never left standing in a slight drizzle on the key and was never loaded without checking it's water content.
Saving the lives possibly of later sailors. And other measures were taken, such as minimizing eventual free surfaces of this type of cargo.
No one was punished, for losing their ship, and an important fact had become known. Also internationally.

Edit: the cargo concerned was pyrite.

Topsiho

### Post by haqking on 2023-05-05
The blame culture is actually tested on ISACAs CISM and CRISC certification tracks. It is a real 'threat' to the organisational security stance.

There are so many things wrong with 'security' - organisationally speaking it is not about the 'vulnerabilities', but the risk. 

For risk to be fully understood the culture needs to be mature. A blame culture is a childish trait and not a mature one. Security maturity will foster a security and risk aware organisation that makes risk aware decisions. Blame should not exist, only lessons learned and continued improvement.

Security should be a business enabler and not an inhibitor. Security should be an input to the corporate risk management/assessment function.

'Security is a process, not a product' - Bruce Schneier
