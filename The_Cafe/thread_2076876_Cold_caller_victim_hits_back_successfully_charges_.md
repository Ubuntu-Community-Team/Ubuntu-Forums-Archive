### Post by 3rdalbum on 2012-10-27
I thought he could have done it by running one of those virtual "premium" lines. There are several companies around that allow you to create a phone number that redirects to yours, but the caller is charged a premium rate per minute.

One of the problems is that the government departments behind these "do-not-call" lists are overwhelmed with complaints. Most of the complaints either have insufficient information:

"I'm on the do-not-call register and I got a phone call the other day from a telemarketer! I want this stopped!"

Or mistaken information:

"I got a call from XYZ Insurance a month ago, and then I put myself on the DNC register, and then someone else was calling about insurance yesterday! It must be XYZ Insurance!"

Or insufficient knowledge of the law:

"I got a call from this company called Synovate but I'm on the DNC register! They claimed they are except because they're a registered market research company but I want you to fine them $10,000!"*

Or they try to complain about companies calling from overseas, obviously not bound by legislation.

All these kinds of invalid complaints take up too much of the government's time, so they can't go after the real illegal operators.

*I was in market research for a while, and yes people believe that market research companies aren't allowed to call - I even had one lady claim we'd get a $10,000 fine. Obviously trying to scare me, it didn't work.

### Post by Paddy Landau on 2012-10-27
> **3rdalbum said:**
> I thought he could have done it by running one of those virtual "premium" lines.
In the UK, that is possible, but the cold-callers will not call those numbers. They call only landlines and, occasionally, mobile (cell) numbers. The landlines are often obtained by calling randomly-generated numbers.

> **3rdalbum said:**
> One of the problems is that the government departments behind these "do-not-call" lists are overwhelmed with complaints. Most of the complaints either have insufficient information:
The complaint form here in the UK explicitly asks you information about the call in order to reduce those problems. It gets you to think twice before making the complaint.

> **3rdalbum said:**
> I was in market research for a while, and yes people believe that market research companies aren't allowed to call
The UK has stronger laws. If you are on the TPS (the equivalent of the DNC), cold callers are not permitted to call you, period. This includes charities and market research. You have to have a prior bona fide relationship with the caller.

In the UK, cold callers often pretend to be market researchers in order to find out more about you before calling back another day to try to sell you something.

As you might have gathered from the initial post, the UK callers can be terribly unethical. For example, the ones claiming to help you with PPI carefully avoid letting you know that you can reclaim free, easily, without their costly "help".

I used to be fine with cold callers, and indeed made a couple of useful purchases from them. But now, being inundated with unethical sales (even when registered on the TPS), they are entirely unwelcome. I am even considering cancelling my landline because of the harassment, and using only mobiles in the house. The cold callers that I'd welcome do not call because I'm on TPS, and the cold callers that lie to me call me all the time.
