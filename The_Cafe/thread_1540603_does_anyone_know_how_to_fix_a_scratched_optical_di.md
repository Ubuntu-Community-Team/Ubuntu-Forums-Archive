### Post by theraje on 2010-07-28
How deep is the scratch? If it's too deep, there's really no way to fix it, because while the outer surface may be preventing data from being read, there's no way to "fix" any data that was damaged. CDs/DVDs/optical media are like records... once the pits and grooves become worn or damaged, there's no way to restore the data. This is especially the case since the read method for optical media is digital, so there is no real room for error.

So, even if you do get a resurfacer, there is no guarantee you can fix it, if the pits/grooves in the data layer of the disc have been damaged. Might want to see if you can find a replacement copy of the game you have... it's probably the most practical thing to do (assuming the game isn't unusually rare).

### Post by handy on 2010-07-28
Just as an END note:

CDs store ALL of their data on the underside of the label. (is that hard to comprehend?)

DVDs store all of their data on the underside of a layer that is held between 2 layers of plastic. So in this case (as opposed to the method used by CDs) it is protected somewhat from the label being scratched.

Therefore, if you have a *valuable* to you, optical disk, that has had the plastic on the read side scratched, & if all other home cleaning methods have failed, you can get the offending scratches polished out by someone who owns the right equipment for the job. 

Often the local DVD rental store can do this for you quite cheaply. :)

Bottom line: All optical media that is scratched on the read side can be easily salvaged. (I found this out years ago when I *almost* pulled a $600- disk from a closing optical drive; the *almost* of course had the expected results.)
