### Post by ve4cib on 2011-07-08
I've been a lab demonstrator for some introductory computer science courses, so I know what you're talking about.  People who think science fiction is reality, and that computers should just magically "know" what it is we want them to do.

Whenever I talk to non-programmers I always like to start off with a basic example of something humans can do easily that computers simply cannot do:

----------
Show a person a picture of a room.  Any room.  Ask them to count the number of chairs in the picture.  For a human that's a very easy thing to ask.  A 5-year-old could do it.

But show that same picture to a computer and it just won't work.  Or not well.  It'll probably get some of the chairs.  Maybe even most of them if it's lucky.  But there is no magical program that can do what any 5-year-old child can do.  Computers are stupid.

Think about how you would describe what a chair is to a computer in a way the computer understands.  Humans have an intrinsic understanding of ergonomics.  We know what we find comfortable, and we remember other things we've sat on.  A computer has none of this background knowledge.

To make matters worse, things that look radically different could all be chairs.  Like a bean-bag chair, an inflatable pool chair, and a Victorian dining room chair.  They have almost nothing in common, except that they're all designed for a person to sit on.  But how different is a bean-bag chair from a pile of blankets tossed on the floor?  They look pretty similar.

...
----------

It's amazing to watch people's expressions as you explain to them just how mind-bogglingly stupid computers are.  Technology seems to be kept on some kind of a pedestal, and when you rip it down people seem to get disoriented by the concept.

### Post by linuxforartists on 2011-07-11
I had a similar experience, as a web designer building a site for a nonprofit.  Mostly they left me alone, which was great.

The nonprofit centers around throwing a big event, and people have to apply to attend.  The head of the organization thought having a regular contact form was enough.  I strongly argued to have online forms that automatically captured the applicants' data into a database.

I knew that a lot of people wanted to attend.  If the nonprofit had used a regular contact form, it would have generated ***hundreds ***of e-mails.  Some poor clerk would have had to open each e-mail and manually enter the information into a database.  Whereas by connecting the applications to a database, all that grunt work could be done automatically.  

Basically, I took a hard stance and said I wasn't doing data entry.  Naturally, no one else wanted to do that either, so in the end they deferred to my suggestion.  Whew!
