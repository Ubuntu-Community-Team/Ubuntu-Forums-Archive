### Post by em3raldxiii on 2009-12-05
Hey dude, I haven't checked the other thread out yet, but I will after I post this reply,

1.  Maybe, see #2
2.  No, there are a few different sockets that were used by P4.  Luckily, the motherboard that you pulled it out of should have the socket type marked on the harness mechanism.
3.  The powder is most likely the thermal paste they use, though it's hard to tell by your photo (try taking the picture again, but set your camera in "Macro" mode, have the zoom all the way OUT, and put the chip less than 8 inches away from the lens).  Yes, you should wipe some of it off with a soft dry cloth.  Don't worry if it leaves a very slight residue, but if the COOLER has "chunks" on it, you can very carefully remove those with a flat razor blade.  Some people swear by polishing the bottom of your cooler, others swear by roughing it up.  I don't find it makes much difference either way, I just make sure both surfaces are clean and free of chunky bits.

When you re-install it, you will want to put some new thermal paste on it.  I have found the best method of applying thermal paste (I often use Arctic Silver II) is by putting a single line along one edge of the COOLER and then using a piece of thin cardboard (inside a panty-hose package has perfect cardboard for this actually LOL) to spread it thinly and evenly across the surface of the cooler.  Ideally you will make a note of where the processor lined up on the cooler so you can get just the right amount of paste on there without making a mess.

I'll see if I can find some further info about the processors; also, there may be markings on the top of the processor die itself that will make sure we have the right info.  AFAIK, if the processor drops smoothly into a socket, then it's the right socket.  Be VERY careful not to bend any pins at all ... it CAN make the processor useless, even if you get them bent back into shape.

### Post by em3raldxiii on 2009-12-06
Excellent.  I look forward to seeing how it turns out!!

Here is a link for a quick vid on applying the Thermal Compound:  [http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fRRWbQUqW1Y](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fRRWbQUqW1Y)

I generally trust those guys, but just to let you know, i don't actually use their methods, and I can't really tell you if it's a problem or not because I have never had a CPU overheat.  I must be doing something "not wrong" LOL!

My method is this:

I put the new CPU in the socket and close the locking mechanism.

I remove remaining thermal compound from the CPU and Heat Sink using a soft cloth.  I understand their reasoning for recommending 99% isopropyl alcohol, and I recommend following their advice.   I generally don't use anything on my CPU or Heat sink, rather, I rub it off with a soft cloth (like their microfibre cloth).

I apply a very thin line of thermal paste along an edge approximately equal to the width of the CPU (slightly smaller actually), then using a piece of cardboard (a business card might work), I carefully spread the compound to a very thin, somewhat even coat, so that it is somewhat translucent.  Too thick = bad & messy & potentially damaging.  Too thin = no big deal, maybe not very good heat transfer.

Place the heat sink, close the clips and off I go.

If you are not totally comfortable with where you put the paste, and are worried you might have overshot it or made a mess, you can do it twice:  The first time being a trial run to see where the footprint of the CPU is in the goo.

### Post by The Real Dave on 2009-12-07
Overclocking doesn't always mean higher heat. For example, I have a 2.4Ghz Celeron OCd to 3Ghz. 

What that means is that say, I left it at 2.4Ghz, and used 25% of the CPU at idle. That would give out quite some heat.

However, overclocked to 3Ghz, it uses only 10% at idle, giving a lower heat output.

That said, run the thing at 100% and it'll be hotter. But sometimes, day to day usage, it can actually cool things down.

In my experience If your TIM is applied correctly it should just appear to be a light residue or film covering the heat spreader. It's only job is to fill in minute imperfections in the surfaces of the CPU heat spreader and the contact point of your heat sink.
