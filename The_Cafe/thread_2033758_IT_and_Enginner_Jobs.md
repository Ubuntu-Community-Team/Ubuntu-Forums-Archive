### Post by TheFu on 2012-07-27
It depends on the location, your skills and how well you present them in your CV AND in person.

Top people are always in demand, so if you want to stand out, you need  to have a passion for the work, for constantly learning, and it needs to  come across in all communications with potential employers.

I've been a professional programmer, professional sys admin and  enterprise architect. When I needed a job, it tool less than 2 months to  find fantastic positions.  Sometimes I had to relocate for the  position. Those have been some of the best jobs in my life.  I've worked  at 8 9 different companies in many different industries and I've been  able to travel all over the world.  

I don't think I'm mediocre, but there are positions where I was not a  good fit and the only way to know is to have an interview.  The people  on the other side of the table probably thought I was completely wasting  their time.  I've been to a few interviews where I felt the same way.

Good people are always in-demand.  I think that "passion" is the start  of becoming good in a field.  WIthout it, you will become a mediocre  employee looking for 5pm on the clock to head home. You don't want a job  like that and most employers don't want people like that either.

I don't believe that IT and CS degree choices really matter very much.   My impression, which could be very wrong, is that CS would learn more  about programming and how computers work inside while IT would be more  about operations techniques.  I've worked in both fields. Operations is  something that very few people know before they must. I know lots of CS  people that made the transition to operations, but I don't know many  operations folks that write much code. They code just enough to be more  efficient and "elegant" is never used to describe their code.  Learning  to code efficient, elegant code is not easy.

OTOH, your location may be producing many CS people, so standing out with that degree in the work force will be difficult.

I have a passion for both operations and writing code. Being flexible has been my key to success.

You will make the right decision for yourself. Trust in that.

### Post by MSPdwalt on 2012-07-27
The biggest challenge you'll face going into a career in technology (hardware, software, management, or other) is that 9 times out of 10 the people you work for won't have a clue how to evaluate or validate a technical hire.

I've worked in IT for 6 years now and I've been disappointed to find that there are no standards for performance/excellence.  Morons are consistently hired based on likability and fast talking, talented people are stuck into roles they don't fit into, it's a mess.

Hands on training at a 2-year school may serve you better in the short term (4-6 years) it worked well for me.  I had less student loan debt and I was ready to work immediately.  As far as programmer v. systems do what you like.  Passion is the most important, you won't get the good jobs if you can't show them that you love what you do.

I've had a blast on the systems side, but it's starting to get stale now.  The fun and the money seems to have moved to development.  I've been exploring ways to learn to program, specifically in the mobile arena.  I'm not sure going back to school is worth it, but if I could teach myself maybe I could come up with the next angry birds or something.

### Post by Kirboosy on 2012-07-27
I'm a Linux System Admin and the biggest thing I can say to anyone when wanting to decide what degree to get is this.

**Find what you love and do it.** If I listened to those stupid advisors I would be a Bus Driver (no joke either)

### Post by QIII on 2012-07-27
Whatever you decide to do, understand that the most detailed technical knowledge is not enough.  You may be the world's best technical expert in the mechanics of a language or hardware, but you are of little value if you cannot *solve problems!*

Take courses outside of the technical aspects of your chosen field.  Literature, classical Philosophy, Logic, History.  There are thousands of years of history about how people have solved problems.  Read Sun Tzu.  His wisdom reaches far beyond war.

What employers want are elegant solutions to problems, not technically correct fluff.
