### Post by VeeDubb on 2012-07-10
Keep in mind that almost none of the words tossed out so far are English.  Concierge, claviger, conocer, dilettante, infophile, biblioholic, and logophile all have non-English roots, or are modern contractions of English and non-English words.  Also, a thesorus isn't likely to be a lot of help, because the origianl meaning of concierge has very little to do with what a modern hotel concierge does.  Originally, it was used to refer to a doorman or even a janitor for an apartment building.  (I do love etymology) 

Since it was Canadian I strongly suspect that the word was either French, or a French-English composite; even if it sounded English.

In the mean time, how about ambassador, docent, advocate, consigliere (that one's a joke), "comte Des cierges," conservius, commissionaire, hotelier, conveyor, emissary, internuncio, or porter?
