The problem with analogue TV is not storage, but rather transmission. Digital transmission is more robust because small changes to the signal will not alter the final data received. With analogue, all small changes will propagate into the final data, showing as artifacts in the sound and video.

### Post by NovaAesa on 2010-03-01
> **cariboo907 said:**
> A digital cable tv signal is no better than an analog signal
Maybe that's where things differ in countries other than the US. In Australia, we don't have "cable TV". There is free-to-air digital TV which is transmitted via radio waves. This radio wave transmission is always analogue in nature whether or not DTV or analogue TV. But when the receiver picks up the signal, if it knows it originated from a digital source it can round off the analogue signal to the predefined digital quantization levels. This cannot be done with analogue TV (because by its very nature there is no quantization levels in analogue - analogue signals are never quantized).

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2010-03-02
I used to sell TVs and other electrical appliances for a living.

A very small percentage of people here get a 'perfect' analogue signal on all stations. A very large percentage of people here get at least one station that is a little snowy or worse. But even a 'perfect' analogue signal can be a little fuzzy, just your TV probably doesn't have the definition to show the fuzziness.

Digital TV does not suffer from "snow" or fuzziness, and it's more resiliant to interference. It's like the quality jump from VHS to DVD. Very much so, in fact, because digital TV signals look a lot like DVD pictures (when you're talking about standard definition).

Most people can get all digital TV stations "perfectly", or perhaps when they replace the RF cable from the wall to the telly/STB.

Standard Definition Digital TV is only the first step. High Definition is much superior in picture quality than analogue TV (which is standard definition, by definition :-)  ).

I hate to be branded a picture-quality-Nazi, because I previously expressed surprise that some people don't see an appreciable difference between DVD and Blu-ray (eyes clouded by their dislike of DRM?). But really, a 40% signal strength SD digital broadcast pwns a 90% analogue broadcast. And an HD digital broadcast dances on analogue's grave.

And the switchover is not anything to complain about, either. You Americans have been given government subsidies to switch to digital; most other countries have not had such a program. Digital has been out there for long enough, the STBs are cheap, the TV stations have worked out how to get the best out of their bandwidth (earlier SD digital broadcasts weren't terribly good) and all new flat-panel TVs have digital tuners; there's not really any reason not to switch over on at least your main TVs.

This is an argument that never seems to go away. It's interesting for many reasons, principally for me from an anthropological point of view.

There are advocates that are firmly digital, others that are firmly analogue. Within these camps you have the vehemently digital and the vehemently analogue, and then the second group that aren't so strong in their opinion but just prefer one over the other.

Myself, I use whatever. As a photographer, the digital v analogue thing never goes away, and from a photographic point of view, I just shrug and say "both are good." And thats the real answer. Both have their faults, both have their strengths. I'm a great believer in using whats best to fit the task, or in artististic terms, using whats best for whatever you aim to create.

On a shoot, I use a DSLR and a manual film camera. I take alternate shots with both cameras.  I usually load the manual camera with black and white film, and shoot colour on digital. Black and white on digital never looks anything like black and white on film, no matter what product I use, and what filters I use. That does not mean that digital is inferior to film in this aspect, it means its **different** and I **prefer**, and thats the key word here, **prefer**, the look on film. Just as I prefer the sound of vinyl over cd.

People take preference for superiority. I *prefer* this, therefore its *better *than that. No, its your preference. Technical issues don't count, especially in the production and creation of art. It's what suits the medium that works best. There is no better, there is only what someone prefers.

### Post by whiskeylover on 2010-03-02
Simple explanation

Digital is 0's and 1's. So, even if there is slight signal noise, it might change the 1 to 0.9. And it would still be interpreted as a 1. There is no middle-ground in digital. Most errors get corrected by built-in error correction mechanism of the hardware.

In case of Analog, even a 1% change in the signal means that it cannot be corrected without very complicated algorithms. 

In short, digital is easy to store and transmit. But Analog, if transmitted within an acceptable margin of error, gives the best quality audio and video.

In short, digital is easy to store and transmit. But Analog, if transmitted within an acceptable margin of error, gives the best quality audio and video.
Because it's on the actual spectrum instead of converted into 1s and 0s.

### Post by audiomick on 2010-03-03
Something else occurred to me relating to digital/HD television.
I have a regular job doing the PA for the studio public for a German political satire show. A couple of months ago, the show was considered to be stable enough that the studio that produces it as a sub contractor decided to start investing in digital HD stuff to replace the up to 20 year old existing gear. They have got as far as changing the cameras, but not the video mixers or the rest of the stuff after that, but the sender is already committed to broadcasting HD.
For what it is worth, what you see in the camera viewfinder on the new cameras is orders of magnitude better than the old ones, which were very good analogue cameras. I am not talking about some cheap garage broadcaster; the show is for one of the German State owned senders.
The thing is, the digital signal from the camera gets converted to analogue to go through the video mixer and associated signal chain, then at some point back to digital to be broadcast. The effect on the quality should be obvious.
The reason for this situation is purely economical. The cost of converting a TV studio from analogue to digital is simply astronomical. One digital camera is several hundred Euros, and the show I am talking about uses 6. And then there is the mixer and all the rest of the stuff....

What I am getting at is that there is no guarantee at all that the picture quality that you are receiving on your new digital HD TV is anything like as good as it could be.

### Post by Macfunky on 2010-03-06
Digital has so many advantages over analogue in the way of convenience. I have a background in sound engineering so i can't say anything about visuals but with sound, analogue is better. Someone said something about the sound of the needle on vinyl? Bottom line is vinyl has a MUCH better frequency response. Tape always had a background hum so for me the quality was outweighed by that but vinyl (considering it is in good condition) is fantastic. Digital audio has limitations. CD audio is encoded at 44.1 Khz at 16 bit which is good. Quality wise it sounds crisp clear but that's besides the point. Analogue hasn't these limitations. Even audio at higher bitrates (don't fool yourself that recording at 48 Khz is much better. It's much more beneficial to stay at 41.1 Khz and use 24 bit) digital still has limitations. I do use digital and record audio in digital, because it is cheaper and more convenient, but analogue is better quality wise. Our ears can't hear above 20 Khz but frequencies above this DO affect how we perceive sound. Multiple frequencies do affect ones below them i.e. we can't hear 88.2 Khz but it will affect lower frequencies that it is a multiple of. Analogue, for this reason has a better frequency response because, provided you have the proper equipment, it can produce these frequencies that we can't hear but that we do perceive. Digital cannot do this because it only samples audio frequencies up to a certain point. While it does fool most people into "hearing" full quality, it actually could be better. Analogue does not have limitations that digital has and this is why it is considered better. Bear in mind you have to have the right equipment but provided you do, playing vinyl on a proper system will have a fuller sound and better bass response than CD on the top end system.
