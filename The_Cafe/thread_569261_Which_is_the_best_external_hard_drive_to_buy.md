### Post by jpkotta on 2007-10-07
Build your own.  

I like Western Digital Caviars, because they're very quiet.  I don't have long term experience with them though; my oldest ones are about 9 months old.  I inadvertently let them overheat several months ago to the point that they automatically shutdown, and no problems yet.  

I have a Metal Gear Box enclosure.  It's quiet and keeps the drive cool.  Looks good too.  My friend got one too, and we're both happy with them.

You're actually probably better off with a internal HD and a USB enclosure on Linux, because then it will work the same as a USB Flash drive, something that's very common and well-supported.  I have never used an "external hard drive", but I would suppose they have extra "features" that may confuse Linux, a system they were almost certainly not designed for.  Hence the problems some people have mentioned.

### Post by popch on 2007-10-07
> **n3tfury said:**
> which model(s) do you have?  they're nice looking although a little pricey compared to similarly equipped NAS.  and you say they work "reasonably well" - what don't you like about them?

I used to have a 20GB 2.5" one. This one used too much power for most notebook PCs I tried to use it with. It came without its own power supply but with some kind of plug with which you could connect it to one. Windows usually told politely that there was a problem with the drive. In some cases, it also added the useful bit that the problem was that too much power was being consumed. With all variants of Linux I tried at that time, the drive worked some times, some times not. Once it stopped working, it would resume only after a restart of the PC. Also, the linksys NAS server would recognize one of those drives but not another one we had bought at the same time. 

AT work, some colleagues tried to use the one Terabyte 'drive' when it was still rather new. In 'principle' it worked all right. It was, however, very noisy and became alarmingly hot. It also took so long to power on or to wake up from power saving mode that the computer which tried to access the drive normally had stopped waiting for the drive by the the drive the drive had become ready. That was, however, one of the earlier products of that size, and some of the problems were presumably to do with the number of discrete disk drives in that unit (four).

Those two I use in my living room (with the unspoken permission by the missus) are 3.5" 160GB drives. I use them with a linksys SAN server and they have been working flawlessly for more than a year. They are not particularly quiet, but not so noisy as to be a disturbance. In other words, you can hear them from time to time.
