### Post by JDShu on 2010-03-11
> **phrostbyte said:**
> It's not such the "cost of producing software", but where this money is being spent, which is entirely in the "capital" area of expenses, which does not even scale logarithmically, it's a *completely* constant expense.

It might be a difficult concept to grasp so let me explain it in a narrative. It might seem kind of an inane narrative but bear with me.

You may (or may not) have heard Windows Vista cost Microsoft $1.5 billion or so dollars to produce. That's a lot a money, don't you think? But you could go on eBay and buy a copy of Windows Vista for something like $200 (high estimate). How in the world is Microsoft making any money? Vista cost them $1.5 billion, and you buy it from them for $200?!! But Microsoft is very profitable, but how can they sell something at such an extreme loss?

All that means is that the fixed costs are extremely high and the marginal costs are constant, even negligible. As you produce more copies of Vista or whatever, the average cost of producing Vista tends towards marginal cost. The price in competition is therefore still marginal costs. Now high fixed costs do make it hard for businesses to enter the market (one of the reasons why we have so few operating systems). However, it does not follow that a monopoly handles development costs better.

Now we throw FOSS into the picture where we force MS (or somebody) to open source their stuff while transferring money to them. Forcing MS to open source causes a positive externality and then giving MS money internalizes it. MS effectively becomes a governmental organization that creates software with FOSS principles.
