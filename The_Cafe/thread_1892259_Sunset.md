### Post by Paddy Landau on 2011-12-09
> **Dry Lips said:**
> I never remember the difference between a.m./p.m
That's easy.

[LIST]
[*] a.m. = _a_nte_m_eridian, i.e. "before midday" (*ante* means *before*; *meridian* = *midday*).
[*] p.m. = _p_ost_m_eridian, i.e. "after midday" (*post* means *after*).
[/LIST]
 Also:

[LIST]
[*] Midday = noon = 12 in the _mid_dle of the _day_
[*] Midnight = 12 in the _mid_dle of the _night_
[/LIST]
 Thus:

[LIST]
[*]12:30 **a.m.** means 12:30 *before* noon, i.e. half an hour after midnight.
[*]12:30 **p.m.** means 12:30 *after* noon, i.e. half an hour after midday.
[/LIST]
Words:

[LIST]
[*]*Morning* = before noon (so 12:30 a.m. is 12:30 in the morning).
[*]*Afternoon* = after noon (so 12:30 p.m. is 12:30 in the afternoon).
[/LIST]
(Bizarrely, the word *noon*, which now means midday, originally derived from the Roman word meaning the ninth hour after sunrise, i.e. about 3 p.m. Its meaning changed to midday somewhere around the 13th and 14th centuries.)

Dealing with 12 o'clock (*o'clock* is an abbreviation for "of the clock"):

[LIST]
[*] 12:00 **a.m.** means 12:00 *before* noon, which means midnight as the day starts.
[*] 12:00 **p.m.** means 12:00 *after* noon, which means midnight as the day ends.
[/LIST]
 Thus 12 a.m and 12 p.m. are both midnight!
Midday is neither 12 a.m. nor 12 p.m., but 12 noon.

However, watch manufacturers have adopted a de facto standard whereby:

[LIST]
[*]12:00 a.m. means midnight
[*]12:00 p.m. means midday
[/LIST]
 To avoid any confusion, use either "noon" or "midday" for 12 in the middle of the day, and "midnight" for 12 in the middle of the night.

HTH
