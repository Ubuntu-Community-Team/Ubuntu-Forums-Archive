### Post by mybunche on 2007-06-10
Folks, please know that the heading for this arcticle **has been taken out of context**. If I was Mark I would be appalled that this title has been used for the transcript of the interview. Not only that, even a Linux website, Linux Today, even repeated the title. It should be promoting Linux not discouraging its use and letting people have doubts. It's taken out of context so much that it's totally wrong, you could say the title is a lie. Unfortunately, most people will probably never read the acrticle and will believe something that's not true.   

Please read the whole transcript. Mark is a great ambassador for Linux and you should know this title is a lie.

Here is the question and answer the title is taken from:
So you wouldn’t do a deal?

No, absolutely not. But the time will come when the folks at Microsoft who have a clear vision for the company as a participant in this community, rather than as a hostile antagonist, will win. At that point I’d love to work with Microsoft. It’s not an evil empire. It’s just a company that is efficiently grounded in the 1980s. New leadership and new thinking might make it a more effective partner for us.

More importantly, people need to understand that the risk of a patent suit associated with Linux, which is a real risk, is far greater from someone other than Microsoft. Signing a deal with Microsoft doesn’t solve the patent problem at all. It doesn’t give you patent immunity. It gives you immunity from a suit from Microsoft and I really don’t think that’s the threat. Microsoft won’t win a patent war, so I don’t think they’ll start one.

### Post by G Morgan on 2007-06-10
It's too simple to say that he would or wouldn't love to work with MS. Naturally there are things right now that people in the OSS community could get around the table with MS about and strike a deal, there are other things that could be done with changes of business practice on the part of MS, there are further things that would require more trust and also there are things that obviously can never be done.

Not even patent deals are beyond question but they have to be under the right terms. This is what GPLv3 is partly about, establishing the terms that we would accept patent covenants under (i.e. redistributable patent rights, not limited to the company that signs the deal).
