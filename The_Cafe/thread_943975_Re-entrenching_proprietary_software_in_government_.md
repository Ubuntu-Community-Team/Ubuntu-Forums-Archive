### Post by Forrest Gumpp on 2008-10-10
The Australian government is embarking upon a program of providing laptop computers for every student in years 9 to 12 in secondary schools throughout the nation.  This program involves the projected expenditure of $A 1.2 billion over the next five years.

A consultant to the Australian government's Department of Environment, Water, Heritage and the Arts (DEWHA), Tom Worthington, has posted an article titled '[COLOR="DarkOrchid"][COLOR="DarkRed"]Digital education revolution is not sustainable[/COLOR][/COLOR]' on Onlineopinion, Australia's foremost e-journal of social and political debate, here:  [http://www.onlineopinion.com.au/view.asp?article=7984&page=0](http://www.onlineopinion.com.au/view.asp?article=7984&page=0)

In the General Discussion area of that same forum, Tom Worthington had earlier opened a discussion titled '[COLOR="Purple"][COLOR="DarkRed"]Reducing computer greenhouse gas emissions 50% by 2020[/COLOR][/COLOR]', see:  [http://forum.onlineopinion.com.au/thread.asp?discussion=2165&page=0](http://forum.onlineopinion.com.au/thread.asp?discussion=2165&page=0)  .  In this discussion Tom stated that he intended recommending to DEWHA that it be mandated for all Australian government procurement of computer hardware that it meet certain reduced energy usage criteria in order to be eligible for consideration in procurement.

In a post to that discussion I said this:

"The requirement reeks to me of the use of a smokescreen of 'environmental responsibility' as a vehicle for the securing of selective corporate advantage for a favoured few. I reckon its all about forcing upgrading of hardware in such a way as to attempt [COLOR="Red"]re-entrenching proprietary software as the only option in government procurement[/COLOR].

Mandate Linux compatibility for all hardware."

Tom has stated it is not his intention to lock out open source, but remember that it is DEWHA that is driving the energy saving initiative that may, in this case, be a 'green fog'.

I don't know whether I have read between the lines correctly in assessing both a threat to open source, and an opportunity for the advancement of Linux in connection with this government program, but I thought it was worth mentioning on Ubuntu Forums in the hope that the many far more technically competent users here may both be alerted to possible implications of these two programs, and be able to contribute timely responses to the discussions on Onlineopinion.  Those discussions will be seen at government decision making level.  I have seen Australian politicians, High Court justices, and former Governors-General viewing, and sometimes posting, on OLO.

Both Onlineopinion users and the Australian education sectors have been predominantly Windows environments.

Can some Ubuntuans please help hot up the discussion on Onlineopinion?  I suspect Tom may be blowing a whistle.

### Post by Forrest Gumpp on 2008-10-12
[SIZE="3"]Background to Public Policy in Australia[/SIZE]

Climate science can still be described as an emergent field of study.  It is more likely that in such a field of endeavour theories untested, and perhaps untestable, may become the bases for populist movements and belief systems, and through such movements come to influence the formation of public policy.

These days, at least in Australia, anthropogenic global warming (AGW) has acquired almost the status of an official state 'religion', with embrace of the term 'environmental responsibility' being a shibboleth thereof.

I am not seeking to contest what has come to be the current public policy within Australia, which is to reduce GHG emissions wherever possible.  I am trying to distinguish between genuine, and merely cosmetic, achievement of that goal.

Much of Australia's electricity generation is coal-fired, so consequently, reductions in electricity demand are seen as contributing fairly directly to reductions in GHG emissions.  DEWHA doubtless sees the energy consumption currently attributable to desktop PCs and monitors as an area where energy consumption, specifically that of electricity, can be easily attained, and thus runs get to be shown on the scoreboard of bureaucratic and political achievement.

The information and communication technology area of the economy contributes 1.52% of Australian GHG emissions.  This 1.52% overall contribution is virtually entirely able to be classified as part of the **stationery energy** contribution to emissions, in which case ICT's share comes to 2.84% of total emissions.

[SIZE="3"]The 'Green Fog'[/SIZE]

Virtually all computer hardware purchased in Australia is imported.

The move, in the market at large, from desktop to laptop and from CRT to LCD displays, is well under way.  The move to laptops alone, was it to be undertaken by most present desktop users, would probably achieve a 50% reduction in electricity usage from the baseline figure within maybe five years rather than the 12 envisaged.  The proposed $A1.1 billion Australian government purchase of a computer for every student in years 9 to 12 is for laptops.

**So what is the problem?**

The problem is twofold.

First, the terms of reference is prescribing a goal of 50% reduction in emissions from the baseline level **in absolute terms** in the face of whatever growth in computer usage takes place over the next 12 years!

Second, in his OnLineOpinion article, the consultant is in fact **calling into question the Australian government's focus upon the purchase of current state-of-the-art laptops** on the basis of some claimed unsustainability.  He is emphasising other requirements that he sees as requiring funding; by inference funding from within this particular budgetary allocation.

Why are these two things a problem?

They may be a problem, as I see it, because the consultant is also advancing thin client or 'cloud computing' as one of the strategies to attainment of the somewhat concealed and unstated true emissions reduction that will actually be required.  Once locked into the 'cloud computing' concept, irrespective as to what OS is on the inferred alternative low cost student hardware, **the national education program will be locked into the web OS and web applications business model**.

When you look at the potential providers of web-based computing do you not see only a handful of global corporations with proprietary software as being the basis for it all?  **A business model where the user starts paying all over again for what has been paid for already, and in the case of open source has been and remains available for free without the need for the user to ever be forced to climb another learning curve**.

[SIZE="3"]A Misconceived Energy Saving Goal[/SIZE]

Whilst the pursuit of greater energy efficiency in computer hardware is a commendable end in itself, so too is the pursuit of what amounts to the recycling, or extension of the service life, of existing hardware.  However, the retention of older hardware in service is instead portrayed as a potential obstacle to achievement of the arbitrary goal of 50% reduction in electricity usage in absolute terms by 2020.

Both the proposal to acquire the several hundred thousand laptops, and the seeming alternate 'cloud computing' recommendation being advanced as a remedy for the perceived funding shortcomings of the government program, to my way of thinking, **amount to a forced hardware upgrade**.  The first perhaps to accommodate existing proprietary OS bloat, the alternative being to introduce an entirely new line of hardware relative to that used within the market today as a necessary means of tying the market into a new business model.

The fundamental misconception in prescribing mandatory energy efficiency targets upon computer hardware is that, quite apart from placing a handful of corporations in a position of **effectively being able to emplace incompatibilities with respect to open source OS's within their chips**, the greatest and most immediate substitution of GHG-emitting electricity generation with eco-friendly renewables is in the stationery energy area.  There is very real prospect in Australia for renewable non-GHG-emitting electricity generation, both grid and stand-alone, being brought on stream by 2020 to an extent more than sufficient to completely overshadow any energy savings otherwise achievable in computer hardware.

Once the power for computers is clean, given the relatively small proportion of total consumption they represent, the advantages of reduced energy consumption is far outweighed by the capital savings of avoiding either forced upgrade.

**Either forced upgrade can immediately be avoided by mandating Linux compatibility** in much cheaper, relatively only slightly lower specified laptops.  Ones that could handle Windows XP, for example, but not Vista, in the world of proprietary software.

[SIZE="3"]True Sustainability[/SIZE]

In the educational context, true sustainability of computer hardware would appear to be most advanced by securing, and if necessary mothballing, sufficient replacement units and spare parts to keep the envisaged Linux compatible computers supplied under this procurement program operational well beyond the from two to four year life currently typically imposed by forced upgrading.

Should there come to be degradation of internet service throughout the world outside Australia, the intra-networkability of the existing stand-alone PC would still remain.  With 'cloud computing' it would not.

Should there come to be shortages of supply of critical components on the world market, Australia would have sufficient stocks on hand to insulate its educational program from such problems.

In the event that upgrading of equipment is nevertheless seen as being desirable, a mandated Linux compatibility of the existing hardware stock (plus guaranteed availability of replacement components) **would ensure a ready market for the old and familiar equipment to students graduating** from their secondary education courses.

### Post by Forrest Gumpp on 2008-10-14
I have attempted to get to grips with the effective **price per laptop** in the Australian government's one-laptop-per-student program.

In the references quoted in the opening post, it is claimed that $A 1.1 billion has been allocated for the purchase of the proposed laptops.  It is further claimed that **116,820 laptops will be purchased in the first round of procurement.**

I have assumed that this first round of procurement will be for approximately one quarter of the eventual total requirement for laptops.  116,820 would be an approximate fit with the 14-year-old cohort in the Australian population; attendance at school being compulsory at that age.  The program is indicated as being intended to be introduced fully over a four year period.

Given that $A 1.1 billion is to be spent on the laptop hardware over the whole period, and that 116,820 may represent one quarter of the total acquisition, **the price per laptop would appear to be going to be $A 2,350 or thereabouts**.  If it is anticipated that there will be significant reduction in numbers of students continuing into years 11 and 12, and the 116,820 laptops initially to be acquired represent maybe 40% of the eventual number required, then **the unit price may be as high as $A 3,370**.

For a bulk government procurement **this seems to me to be an extortionately high price quotation range**.  That is the sort of money that is being asked of retail customers in Australia for Vista-capable laptops.  That is why I question whether the Australian government has been conned into what amounts to a forced upgrade of specification just so proprietary software can continue its stranglehold on the future market.

Those schools within the State education system that have had an IT orientation are currently using Windows XP.  **All that is required is that a laptop specification capable of running XP and compatible with Linux be made a mandatory requirement of suppliers** wishing to be considered in this procurement.  Such laptops have been selling, retail, for less than $A 1,000 each over the last two years in Australia.  All that has been lacking in them is thoroughgoing Linux compatibility.

For the size of the order in prospect, and the ongoing implications for sales beyond the scope of the education hardware program, it is reasonable to expect prospective suppliers to meet at least the Linux compatibility specification.

If Microsoft wishes to sustain its erstwhile 'Prima Nocta' status (ie. Windows pre-loaded) with hardware manufacturers, then it will have to agree to supply the requisite number of supported licences with any such procurement of Linux compatible hardware.  Or miss out completely.
