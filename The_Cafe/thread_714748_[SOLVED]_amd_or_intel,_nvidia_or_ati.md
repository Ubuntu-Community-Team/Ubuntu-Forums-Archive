### Post by julian67 on 2008-03-10
I'd go for Intel CPU and either Intel or nvidia graphics. I'd also be very careful to find a laptop with Intel wireless. In the last year  I have run 3 laptops with various distros and set up my gf's laptop too as well as helped out a few other people. 

Reasons: Intel CPUs are incredibly efficient and ideal for a laptop. I am not talking about the awful old Pentium dual cores, but the Pentium-M (if you are on a tight budget), Core Duo and Core 2 Duo are all superb for low heat, long battery life and excellent performance. AMD are way behind the Core 2 Duos. I never though those laptop cooler pads were anything but a gimmick until I used laptops with Turions.....you could fry an egg on those things if the battery would just last a little longer...... 

Graphics: if you want a completely free software machine then Intel graphics are  extremely well supported (generally) and surprisingly powerful. I even was running Beryl on a Centrino pentium-M 1.6 GHz with Intel 855 GMA (this is the lowest of the low in terms of graphics ability). My other laptop is a 1.6GHz Core Duo with Intel 945 graphics and it easily runs Compiz and has enough 3D capability for any of the stuff I do ((simple games like AssaultCube, Sauerbraten).  If you're going to be doing intensive 3D work/gaming then go for a model with nvidia graphics in preference to ATI because the nvidia drivers are generally trouble free and the ATI drivers are basically awful. 

Wireless: Intel wireless generally works out of the box, the best supported chips are ipw2100 and 2200 though these are old now and only found on budget specs. The 3945 is also fine in Ubuntu right out of the box. 

Other things to look out for: Avoid fancy sound systems on laptops. Many use Intel ICH7 and ICH8 sound controllers and they might be controlling any of a fairly large number of codecs. Manufactures like ICH because the one controller can be used in a huge variety of hardware configurations. unfortunately many of these hardware configurations are poorly supported outside of Windows and problems in Linux might range form no sound at all to cruddy stuff like being unable to mute the speakers when headphone is inserted. There are lots of workarounds and I'd research some specs of likely models and then start searching these forums, ubuntu community docs and also gentoo wiki. Personally i'd just go for the simplest sound set up possible, i.e. stereo pair, line in, line out, headphone socket, microphone. 

Other things to research before you buy: find out if people are having trouble with suspend on any model you're interested in. Also power management and cpu frequency scaling. A nicely compatible laptop should dim the screen and scale the cpu when you unplug the power but not all of them work in practice.

Special function buttons: some of these are hardware buttons and some are done in windows (by design). it can really be annoying if you have something like a wireless on/off switch that doesn't work. 

Best thing is to make a shortlist and go to a few stores and find one that will let you boot your Ubuntu live CD and start checking stuff out. I did this and it's not foolproof but it is useful.

### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-03-10
I don't see a reason to buy AMD processors unless you are building for someone else who is on a tight budget. The CPUs are only a bit cheaper, but there's a really cheap ($59 AUD) motherboard for AMD processors.

Intel's processors are quite simply BETTER. They beat AMD for performance-per-watt, raw performance, performance-per-dollar at the moderate-to-high end, and heat generation. AMD's cheapest processors are cheaper than Intel's, and AMD's new 65nm Phenoms use slightly less energy than the 65nm Core 2 Quads, but really. The Phenoms also have a now-infamous bug that causes crashes, and the workaround BIOS upgrade causes even lower performance.

I also hear that the top-of-the-range Phenom is beaten by the entry-level Intel Q6600 (you know, the processor that is advertised like "OMZG! Quad-core computer for just $1300!").

I had an AMD Sempron in my previous computer, which was reasonably snappy, but when I came to build my new computer the answer was obvious. Intel CPU. The hardest part was deciding whether to spend another $300 to get a half-decent Core 2 Quad or just stick with the E6850.

A friend of mine who should know better, reckons that he'll always use AMD processors because "some games don't work on Intel".  The scary thing is, this guy is an expert at computer-building. So don't get sucked into the FUD. I also hear that "Intel processors aren't very good at 64-bit". That's just more FUD from people who can't admit that Intel makes the better CPUs at the moment.

AMD really has been caught napping, and until they get their act together I wouldn't recommend their CPUs for desktop or laptop. I'll be happy to recommend them again when their CPUs deliver the same performance for less price, or better performance for not much more cost.
