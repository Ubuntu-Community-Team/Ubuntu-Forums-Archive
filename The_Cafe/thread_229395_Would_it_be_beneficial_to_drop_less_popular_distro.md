### Post by Brunellus on 2006-08-04
You have completely misunderstood something that is fundamental to free software.

while the Linux kernel may be monolithic--in its structure, mind you--the distributions and operating systems which run on that kernel are anything but.  There is no "Linux International" that controls, directs, and allocates resources across all those various distributions.  

The variety in distributions exists because of the freedom inherent in the licenses under which the software is distributed.  In other words, distros exist because their maintainers conceived of them to serve certain needs. 

There is always a great deal of moaning among new members of the community that there should be some sort of 'consolidation' or 'unification' of Linux.  Perhaps they have a mental image of the combination of distros as a kind of Free Software [Voltron]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltron") that will combine and slay Microsoft.

It just isn't so.  Again, there is no central organizing authority to compel "standardization."  Should one distro gain prominence in terms of userbase, that becomes the de facto "standard."  One can argue that Red Hat Enterprise Linux is that standard, based on its success in the corporate server market.  

Even so, the nature of the GPL means that all developments on the Linux kernel and other GPL-licensed parts of Linux-based operating systems find themselves into the larger community.  In this, very real sense the Linux distros are ALREADY consolidated.

The very clause "would it be beneficial" is quite ambiguous; beneficial for what? Saying "beneficial for the whole FOSS world" would imply that the person saying this sees a "big picture" that is the whole FOSS world where dropping certain distros will bring immediate benefit. The fact is that we don't see such a big picture; we only really see our close proximity of the whole sphere and have a rough idea of how it will evolve over a fairly short period.

A new Linux user may "feel" that it would be more beneficial to have less distros since workforce would concentrate on a smaller area, but once they gain the knowledge that development happens in a distributed fashion and all that distros do is put the mess back together, they'll get to know this isn't really true. If, say, Ubuntu and MEPIS were to merge, this may be beneficial for GNOME development, but not really matter for the development of HAL, and it may hamper the development of KDE. So who is to judge in advance the overall "benefit" and loss from this change to the whole FOSS world? Noone really can.

This is why FOSS governance usually counts on the power of matters working themselves out rather than force them to go certain ways in a top-down fashion.

### Post by Third Thoughts on 2006-08-06
What I found strange is that no one (or at least not so far as I have noticed) has raised the fact that this is a "pick the winners, skip the game" sort of argument. Those arguing for a Linux merger are implying that the top twenty distrobutions somehow represent the best of the Linux community. Even if we take that assumption to be true and ignore the issues of a relative term like best, just because a distro is the best now, doesn't mean its always the best. Here's a theorhetical game. Let's imagine this is a SuSE forum and its the year 2000. Let's imagine that somehow, the idea of a mega merger catches on and all distro work is consolidated into the big 20. Whoops... there goes Ubuntu. 

The whole merger argument ignores the fact that you can't figure out which distrobutions are going to end up being the best. A small time player can always have a meteoric rise. We of all people should know, after all, Ubuntu hasn't even made it to it's second birthday yet. Most arguments about "re-inventing the wheel" can only be made in hindsight, because that's the point when we can tell which wheel works the best. GNOME and KDE are a perfect example. If you research the history of both you realize why there are two DE's. KDE came first, but there were concerns about its legality because the Qt toolkit was not liscenced under the GPL. GNOME stepped in to make an IDE fullly compatible with the GPL. Eventually Trolltech made a version of Qt available under the GPL, eliminating the concerns about KDE's legality, but only in hindsight can we say something like "GNOME was re-inventing the wheel."

Some may say, well that was then, why can't we just merge them(or pick one) now. Besides ignoring that time and effort that folks have invested in each project, that argument also once again ignores the issue of the future. KDE and GNOME have very different approaches to the desktop, as any KDE vs GNOME flamewar will inform you. To merge them would be to pick, or create through compromise, a single vision. But we lack the knowledge to come up with a vision that will definitively respond to the future of computing. We can't pick the winner, without letting the game play out, so its better to leave all the player in and see who comes out on top. Of course since the game never really ends, there's no real winner, just distros that adapt, change and respond to what users are looking for. Which sounds like a  pretty good system to me.

### Post by BuffaloX on 2006-08-06
Short term having fewer distros might be beneficial,

1: It would be easier for a distro to penetrate the market.
2: Easier to agree on standards between fewer distros.
3: A distro having more developers, would be beneficial to that distro.

**[COLOR="RoyalBlue"]In reality counting the human factor the exact opposite would happen![/COLOR]**

1:
Distros targeting special market niches, helps Linux within those niches.
this is beneficial to both Linux and the Distro.
Linux rules within some of those niches, clustering is probably the most obvious example.
Likely candidates are Music, office, movie making, generel IT development. 
Maybe some day even games.
Even Microsoft understands this, and is lining up more choises than ever before. But as usual with Microsoft they get it all wrong.

2:
Fewer distros would likely make it even harder to make standards, because the motivation would be less. Also the need to work with the rest of the community would be less, because each distro would have a larger share of the total number of developers, and would to a higher degree be able to "stand on its own".

3:
If it's less fun/interesting to develop, there would be fewer developers in all. Maybe each distro would have more developers, but the total would likeky be less. I think Gnome and KDE are very good examples of how it makes it more interesting to get involved. You choose the one you like the most, if the other camp has something you like, maybe you try to make it even better.

All in all I think it's quite obvious that having a multitude of distros, and the option to make your own, helps Linux in the long run.
