Dell buys enough hardware from component manufacturers that they're able to drive their unit costs down to pretty low levels. ### Post by aysiu on 2007-03-13
I don't see what's so wrong about this.

**Publicity for Dell?**
What exactly is "free publicity" for Dell in this case, anyway? No one I know in real life has heard anything about Dell in the news. Most of the buzz about this is in the Linux or technology communities. Turned on the evening news--nothing about Dell and Linux. Opened my morning paper--nothing about Dell and Linux. Talked to my wife and cat--no mention of Dell and Linux. Haven't seen any *major* publicity about this apart from within Linux forums and Digg.

If this kind of publicity does *any* good, that's good. In other words, if people who actually frequent Linux forums and Digg actually buy Dells because Dell preinstalls Linux, that's a reward system working the way it should be. Customers ask for preinstalled Linux. Dell provides it. Customers buy it.

If Dell doesn't provide preinstalled Linux, do you think the Linux/Digg crowd is going to say, "Wow! They didn't do it. With all this buzz about Dell, I'm going to buy a Vista Dell laptop anyway"? Nope. In fact, I'd wager to guess a lot of people who have been "voting" for preinstalled Linux won't even buy it if Dell provides it, even if Dell provides it under "ideal" conditions (prominently displayed as an alternative to Windows, cheaper than the equivalent Windows system, and on laptops--not just desktops).

**Email harvesting**
If you provide an email address that you frequently use, you're just not being savvy in general--this has nothing to do with Dell. Many sites ask for your email address, and if you're afraid of spam, you should set up a dummy spam email account right now!

I don't really agree with Rav Tux on this whole dichotomy (Dell good or Dell bad). I think Dell is good and bad. Should you support System76? Sure. Does that mean you have to think Dell is evil and offer it *no* support whatsoever? No.

**Doing the right thing?**
The way I see it, if you want to do the right thing, give Dell a junk email address, buy from the little guys, but if Dell preinstalls Linux, you can advise friends and family to buy from Dell. That way, they have a safe middle ground (unfamiliar OS, familiar manufacturer) and support Linux (in the minds of OEMs) as a viable product, while you support the little guy who's doing the "right" thing.

Whenever possible, I try to avoid an us v. them mentality. Dell can possibly be doing a good thing. That doesn't make the company a good company. It could also be doing a bad thing. That doesn't make it a bad company.

**Possible outcomes**
I see this going one of three ways:

1. Dell does essentially nothing, sticking with its current offerings--Windows Vista and then an obscure N-Series only "geeks" know about and won't buy because it's $50 more than the equivalent Vista system. There's a backlash against Dell among Linux users, who then suggest to friends and family (if they must use Windows) to buy their Windows computers from HP or some other OEM.

2. Dell makes a half-*** effort. Linux (OpenSuSE) comes preinstalled on one model. It's obscurely placed on the Dell website (only in one country) so that someone has to link you to it or you have to do a Google search to find the product. It's significantly more expensive than the equivalent Windows model. It's a desktop and not a notebook. Dell comes away saying, "Well, no one wanted to buy Linux even though we made the effort." The Linux community says, "Well, you didn't really make the effort." No one is happy. Dell loses sales. Linux users lose what little respect they had for Dell.

3. Dell makes a genuine effort--making a Linux distribution (say, Ubuntu or Fedora) a major choice on lower-grade notebooks, prominently displayed on their websites (several countries). The Linux notebook is the same price as the equivalent Windows notebook. A bunch of Linux users buy the laptops. Many naysayers don't because they prefer to buy a barebones notebook, a Windows-preloaded one for a dual boot, or a System76 laptop.

No matter what "publicity" Dell gets from this event, I don't really see how Dell comes out ahead in this game unless #3 happens. And if #3 happens, I would like to think Linux users would reward Dell for going out on a limb.

Just a few ideas. I hope these are already what Michael Dell is thinking.
