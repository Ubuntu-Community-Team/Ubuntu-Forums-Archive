### Post by Autodave on 2017-02-06
With a quality tape and recording on a *very *good machine, the quality was there or at least *could* be there assuming the person doing the recording knew what they were doing and the equipment used was in proper working order. Keep in mind that your $100 cassette recorder was *not* a quality piece of equipment. *Good *maybe, but not quality. And then you had the signal-to-noise ratio to consider. If recording using analog mixer, more noise. If you $100 cassette machine came with mics, they were NOT quality mics.

Manufacturers loved to spout the specs on the machines. Like, 20-20K frequency response. That was good, but what no one understood was the part right after that that said + or - 30db. That pretty much meant that the lower or higher ends of that 20-20K were going to be barely heard if heard at all.

Also, you had *wow and flutter* to consider. The tape just simply did not move at an extremely constant rate.

I did a lot of live recording with good equipment: $400 recorder and $100 apiece mics. Paid $12-20 for one blank tape. (This was 45 years ago!) 

While I got (for the time) very good quality recordings, I would never go back to tape. Your frequency response today is limited pretty much by your sound card alone (as far as the recording machine is concerned). Wow and flutter don't exist in digital format.

I still do live recording through a digital mixer hooked up to my PC via a USB cable. Mics still about $150 apiece. The PC is nothing special at all: 2.8 dual core with 4 gig RAM running Xubuntu and recording up to 18 channels at once. The quality that I get now blows away anything I ever got on tape.
