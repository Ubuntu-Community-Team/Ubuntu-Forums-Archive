### Post by dixonstalbert on 2007-09-17
Bell Canada which owns the Canadian Business TV channel (bnn.ca) and the largest Canadian sports channel (tsn.ca) has been posting high definition rebroadcasts of their shows on their websites.

Unfortunately, when you click on the shockwave link, it starts a java script which checks your OS ( /broadband/scripts/detect/os_detect.js  ) , and if it isn't Microsoft Windows or Apple MacOS it won't redirect you to the video, and you get the 'Sorry' message.

This type of thing is really what is going to keep Microsoft's monopoly; whatever the European Trade Commission rules.

Large media owners will insist that their content can only run on proprietary OS's with locked code as a way of protecting copyrights and guarding a present or future revenue source. 

Government legislators will probably side with Microsoft, because the potential locked code has for keeping tabs on the population.

It seems to me it is technically possible for a script residing on the providers computer, which I have to give execution rights to if I want to watch the media, could check what OS I am running, then check my IP address, then scan my harddrive for al-queda links, then check  for mp3s I dont have sales receipts for, etc. And, this whole process could go on  without my knowledge because my local OS has little code bits that allow all this and nobody but Bill Gates and Homeland Security even know they are there.

It is difficult to see how any open source software OS will ever get anywhere as we move closer to the era of internet  movies and television as our main media source.

### Post by dixonstalbert on 2007-09-18
ctv.ca and comedynetwork.ca are also owned by Bell Canada and are using the same server script.

Again, the script checks the Operating System, not the Browser, so Firefox  User Agent switcher or even Windows IE6 under wine won't help you. (you can try them yourselves)

I read in a forum a few years ago that the OS detection is by design; Bell Canada felt that open source OS's would prevent them from having complete control of their content. As such, they will never offer a linux compatible version of their video clips. 

It is not so much the prospect of spyware etc, I object to, it is the fact that in the future, if I want the kind of content that Bell Canada offers, like Canadian Football highlights, like Canadian Financial News, things that no one else in the world would have any interest in providing, I will have to run the OS that they tell me I have to run.

And, I think they have a valid point. Although the content is free at this point, they are almost certainly going to charge for it in the future as the technology improves and they have the means to provide more and more streaming video and television sets are a thing of the past. 

How else can they prevent theft of content unless they can control the local OS?
