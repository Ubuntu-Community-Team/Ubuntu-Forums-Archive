### Post by murderslastcrow on 2010-11-27
I'm getting the impression that not many people responding to this thread have the following. Either 1. A computer made since 2001, or 2. Tried KDE 4 for more than a couple minutes.

I will acknowledge a few things, first that much of the new technology in KDE 4 does require in some cases a bit more updated hardware than KDE 3, most notably a video card from Intel, ATi, or NVIDIA is preferable. But I should also note that KDE 3 has its faults, as well, compared with KDE 4. Application loading time, integration, and simplicity are not always so easily found in KDE 3. The truth is that, without compositing, KDE 4 has, at least on my hardware, been equally as responsive as KDE 3. The compositing is lighter than Compiz, so if you're going to use anything other than KDE 3, it might as well be KDE 4.

However, if your hardware really is so much of an issue, I encourage you not to use KDE 4. Just because it's a tiny bit heavier doesn't mean it's worse, unless resource use is the only thing you care about in an OS or DE. Of course, KDE 4 has been optimized quite a bit since its early days- if you haven't tried KDE 4.5 I encourage you to do so.

I don't think we should make a trend of using more resources than are necessary to create a modern DE, but I think KDE 4 makes a better example than Gnome 2 of how to best utilize these resources. For example, this computer has 400 MB of RAM and a wimpy video card, but it still runs KDE and opens its applications more comfortably than Gnome with compiz. And it manages to look very nice while doing so.

So I guess what I'm really getting at is that, if you look at KDE 4 for what it is, without thinking so much about the slightly higher system requirements for the compositor, you may understand just how much of an improvement KDE 4 really is. From a design perspective, it's pretty obvious the improvements (notice I didn't say from a 'prettiness' perspective, but design).

And if you like KDE 4, but your computer can't handle it, I suggest hashing out the fifty dollars to get one that can.

### Post by blackbelt_jones on 2011-08-19
I thought that I had surrenedered, but I went all the way back to KDE3 after about a year and a half of KDE4.  I had a computer malfunctions so that it cant read hard drives anymore, so I could only use a live CD.  I chose Slax, the most customizable, which still uses KDE3, and KDE3 *felt so good.*

It just doesn't work for me.  I appreciate that some people, maybe most people, want all those options... but for me, they're always in the way, and now that I've got Trinity/Kubuntu installed on both my machines, I'm starting to really discover the power of the Desktop metaphor for managing tasks. With KDE4, the Desktop became an apartment, that I had to furnish and decorate, even though no one ever visits me there.  I know that you can run KDE as if it was KDE3, except taht you can't, not quite.   The panel for KDE4 just isn't as agile.  

I understand that KDE5 is reportedly on the drawing board, and there's always hope that they'll manage to get it all together.  But I think we're going to have to face up to a problem that may be unique to FOSS.

Linux is "technology driven", instead of "market driven" like Windows.  In the Windows closed source business model, the user is "the customer", and therefore is supposed to matter, though in a monopoly he/she matters much less.  In Linux, users who are not developers are pretty superfluous.  Again and again, users who weren't happy with KDE4 were told to go learn C++.  The developers have all the power, and you sometimes get the feeling that if desktop developers ran the world, we'd all be eating our cornflakes with an electronic spoon.

And now I hear Linus Torvalds has stopped using Gnome, calling Gnome 3 "an unholy mess".  I wonder if Linus has heard about Trinity.
