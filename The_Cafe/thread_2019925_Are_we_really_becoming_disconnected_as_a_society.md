### Post by Tombgeek on 2012-07-07
I recently saw this [video]("http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZN79S1VGgrI&feature=g-u-u") by Lack76. In it, he discusses his views on Google's Project Glass and the potential negative impact it has in a world that already is divided by social media and technology.

I agree with him for the most part, although him comparing it to George Orwell's prediction of a totalitarian society in *1984* might be a bit of an overkill (though it seems everyone uses the *1984* metaphor without having actually read the book). He's not far off though.

I can personally comment about this. In South Africa, Blackberry (hold on to your seats because this may rock your world :P ) has the majority market share of all portable phones sold. This is partly due to Blackberry Internet Service (because Internet is quite expensive here compared to first-world countries) and Blackberry Messenger. In fact, majority of Blackberry users in South Africa are youth. The irritating part about this is, as a young guy, I cannot speak to my friends without them checking their phones every five seconds and breaking off the conversation to reply to their messages. As much as I love technology, this is ridiculous.

And if it's not BBM, it's people on Facebook constantly feeling compelled to update their statuses, "like" everything and comment on the status update of some moron who thought it cute to tell the world that he had a ham sandwich for lunch. While I do check Facebook numerous times a day, I never really post and comment unless I really want to.

Apart from that, studies have shown the Internet is making us dumber by forcing us to adapt to a negative form of speed-reading, which causes damage to our long-term memory, concentration and factual comprehension. While I do read a lot on the Internet, I try as often as I can to balance it out by reading a book. 

One thing I both agree and disagree with Lack76 is his belief that fanaticism of tech products (from either Apple, Microsoft, Google, etc.), almost treating it as a religion, will cause more damage. While I do agree it causes damage, what he doesn't realize is that this isn't a new problem. We get obsessive over everything: be it politics, religion, celebrities or consumer products. An obsessive Apple zealot is the same as a person who thinks everything Stephen King wrote it brilliant. They're both equally credulous and narrow-minded.

Of all this, it seems Google's Project Glass my further divide us. After all, Google doesn't care about their customers or long-term consequences, which can be said for almost every tech company, including Apple, Microsoft and even the Linux companies: Canonical, Red Hat and Novell. All said companies are after money, not customer satisfaction.

Sorry for the long post, but I thought I'd just share my personal opinions and wish to hear yours. Do you agree or disagree?

I think the bottom line is personal responsibility, ones own choices about how tech is used.

For example, Call Waiting isn't exactly a new feature (it's been around for decades), but I find it inherently anti-social. Just as tech can be used to isolate oneself socially, it can also be a positive augmentation to F2F.  Through Facebook, Meetup.com, and other services I've met many good people, and have used those services to plan get-togethers in the physical world.

Like anything else in life, tech is what you make of it.

### Post by Dry Lips on 2012-07-09
Of course the new information technology is changing society, in the same way as the invention of the printing press and television changed society. This &#8220;brave new world&#8221; certainly has many advantages, but I believe that we have to admit that there are downsides too of plugging into the matrix.  
 
This is what I believe is the two most important challenges of our time:
 
**Privacy:** Our activity online is already harvested by companies for profit, and I think we are overly naïve if we downplay the mind-boggling possibilities for unlimited surveillance that the new technology offers governments and snoopers. People nowadays tend to accept that we have less privacy than before, which in turn makes it easier for powers benefiting from snooping in our data to get things the way they want it. In an age where cloud computing and online storage are buzzwords, we need to be more vigilant than ever before.
 
**Are we amusing ourselves to death?**
 Information technology makes it possible to get information about a wealth of different subjects, but people increasingly use computers and the Internet as yet another source of entertainment.  
 
 *&#8220;Internet users of all ages are much more likely now than in the past to say they go online for no particular reason other than to pass the time or have fun. Some 58% of all adults (or 74% of all online adults) say they use the internet this way. And a third of all adults (34%) say they used the internet that way &#8220;yesterday&#8221; &#8211; or the day before Pew Internet reached them for the survey. Both figures are higher than in 2009 when we last asked this question and vastly higher than in the middle of the last decade.&#8220;*
  [http://pewinternet.org/Reports/2011/Internet-as-diversion/Report.aspx](http://pewinternet.org/Reports/2011/Internet-as-diversion/Report.aspx)
 
 Though Internet access and laptops are becoming widespread at home and at schools, we haven't become more knowledgeable than before:
 
 &#8220;*Since the late 1980s, the emergence of 24-hour cable news as a dominant news source and the explosive growth of the internet have led to major changes in the American public's news habits. But a new nationwide survey finds that the coaxial and digital revolutions and attendant changes in news audience behaviors have had little impact on how much Americans know about national and international affairs.*&#8221;
