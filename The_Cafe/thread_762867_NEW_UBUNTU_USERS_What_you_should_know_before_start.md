### Post by Mazza558 on 2008-04-22
I'm writing a short-ish guide for people who are even vaguely interested in exploring Ubuntu. It's designed to be even easier than the last guide I wrote, which was aimed at people who were well on their way (see signature). This guide tries to clear up some common misconceptions about Ubuntu and Linux - users on the forums can send this to friends to set the scene. It's a work in progress, and I'll add more as I go along.

[SIZE="5"]**NEW UBUNTU USERS: What you should know before starting...**[/SIZE]

**Linux/Ubuntu are not Windows!**

- The absolute first thing that you should know (and this is constantly emphasized by many other users), is that you are potentially trying something which is as alien to you as an unknown foreign language. You have been brought up to learn routines, not principles. In short, you know, but you don't understand (yet). 

- You have learned how to use Windows, not a "Computer" in general. Microsoft, in their rush to make computers "easy to use" for everyone, have ignored a lot of more sensible and logical ideas in relation to how computers work. You may feel that Windows is easier to use, but this is entirely subjective and depends on how ingrained the "Windows" way of doing things is for you. 

- Windows comes on nearly every PC because Microsoft have deals with a huge number of companies to keep it that way. You are, by and large, considering the underdog, a **hidden gem** of technology that is generally out of the public consciousness (except in the I.T world). This may be a boon or otherwise for you, depending on whether you love non-mainstream things, or prefer to go with the popular. Note that this does not make it any harder, it just doesn't receive publicity. 

- It is the general perception that self-proclaimed "I.T Experts" seem to have the most trouble with trying Ubuntu out, as they are absolutely locked in to the idea that computers should work in one way, and **only** one way.


**You may have to think for yourself!**

- When you use Windows, it comes on your PC with everything set up for you. There is generally nothing you need to do to set it up, as you are a consumer, not a thinking human being. The companies want you to be satisfied with your **product**, but don't expect you to want anything better.

- On Ubuntu or Linux, some things may not work straight away. It depends on your PC entirely. Some companies simply do not want to co-operate with us for various reasons. Therefore, you may have to ask questions and follow guides to get things set up. But, if you consider it, a single CD of software (Ubuntu) can often support more stuff straightaway than on Windows, which is a pretty amazing thing.

- Fixing the more complex problems you may have with Ubuntu can sometimes involve using what is called a terminal. This is a window into the core into your PC and will probably scare you. However, it is easy to understand! See [THIS POST! (click here)]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=714338")

**Nearly all of us are volunteers!**

- The forum members here are extremely helpful, and, in my opinion, more so than professional tech support. We don't get anything out of it other than the satisfaction of helping more people discover the hidden gem awaiting anyone interested.

- If something breaks for you, don't start raving and ranting at the other forum members. Consider that you have paid none of your money to try Ubuntu out, you have paid nothing for the tech support, and you have learned that Ubuntu did not work for you out of the box. The common argument is "I've wasted time!" - well, I ask you, what would you have done with that time otherwise? Watched TV? Even if you had something else to do, why didn't you think about your priorities? Either way, you've had a learning experience, which is invaluable. You're of course welcome to wait 6 months for the next release, which may work better for you. 


---------------------------------------------------------------

This is all I'll do for now, and I'll add more at a later date. Thanks for reading!
