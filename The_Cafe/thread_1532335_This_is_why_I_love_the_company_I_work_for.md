### Post by donkyhotay on 2010-07-16
As someone who is not deaf but has taken an ASL (american sign language) course (I foolishly thought it would be an "easy A" for my college language credits), I will be the first to tell you that ASL is not "english using gestures". It is a completely different language with it's own form of grammar that is actually based on *french* rather then then english (there are historical reasons for this). If you were born deaf and learned ASL growing up (even if in an english speaking household) chances are you know nothing about english. While reading/writing single words may be possible (signs and stuff), trying to stuff all the information ASL gives into words is very difficult. I've seen someone born deaf "write" on a piece of paper and there were as many diagrams and arrows to emphasize what they wanted to communicate as they had actual words. The word order / sentence structure (such as existed) was very disjointed too(by my perspective at least). I don't think I would have been able to understand her at all if she had been limited to a regular keyboard trying to text.

Technically yes, ASL (American Sign Language) is used with two hands. However, deaf people have become quite proficient at signing with one hand when the other hand is occupied. Also, any skilled sign language interpreter can understand a deaf person that signs with one hand. :-) We have to be able to. I knew lots of people in my ASL classes (before I became an interpreter) that thought the class would be an "easy A"...hehehe...not so "easy" especially if you have a deaf teacher. ;-)

Fortunately my teacher wasn't deaf, unfortunately she had a *very* strict no talking rule. Essentially the first day she gave the standard introduction/syllabus and a brief history on ASL verbally. She then told us that starting next class there would be *no* talking whatsoever for the remainder of the class by either us or her. It was rough but after going through the class I understand the reasons she did that because by making it an immersive environment it really pushed all of us to learn what she was teaching (or at least get really good at charades). I was glad she wasn't deaf though because it gave us a chance to occasionally ask questions before/after class for clarification of things that may have been hard to understand from direct sign/pantomime. It wasn't the "easy A" I was expecting but I did learn quite a bit and although I rarely use it these days (I don't know anyone personally who is deaf so I don't get much practice) I still have "survival" level knowledge of ASL which does come in handy from time to time.
