### Post by orlox on 2009-10-09
My take on this:
[LIST]
[*]Openffice missing to perfectly support .doc: Different office versions sometimes fail to do this. So, you really can't expect perfect compatibility if the guys who actually have the specs of the format can't get it done. It would impress me that the guys reverse engineering it could...
[*]Photosop alternative: Gimp is gettig there, though it misses important futures like CMYK color support. I guess it will be 1 or 2 years from now till that is solved. Also, the new single window interface will surely convince many photoshop users.
[*]AutoCad: Well, here to begin with, it's the fact that dwg is an industry standard, perhaps even more than .doc in several fields. Besides, professional cad software is not only related to 3d modeling, but to precise 2d plots. Getting a tool as fully functional as autocad is certainly a very difficult task, since there's no clear OSS right now that could match up to it anytime soon...
[/LIST]
Also, remeber that photoshop and autocad are sold at prices ~$500 dollars and above, and there's a reason to it. There are very complex pieces of software, and constructing and mantaining equivalent opensource apps is a huge task.

These issues have been in existed for quite some time now.  And they are touted as major reasons why more Windows users don't migrate to Linux.

So I want to know: why haven't developers solved the OOo .doc problem or written drop-in replacements for Photoshop and AutoCAD?  Is there some major difficulty in doing these things?  Please explain.

The reason that they are not compatible is because Microsoft does not WANT them to be compatible. If it was up to them, they wouldn't want OpenOffice to be able to read it at all. The real question is why Microsoft Office can't natively open and save odt files. I can understand them not being able to read .sdw (StarOffice 5.x), or .sxw (StarOffice 6.x) files, which are rare, but ODT is an open standard. OpenOffice has tried to become compatible, but Microsoft needs to come halfway. I should make a point though. Sun Microsystems has created an addon for MS Word that allows it to save in ODF with near-perfect conversion. So all you need is XP on a virtual PC with MS Office 2003 and you can convert to your heart's content. 

My main point is that it's not OpenOffice's fault that MS keeps its source code secret and doesn't collaborate with OpenOffice to create interoperability.
