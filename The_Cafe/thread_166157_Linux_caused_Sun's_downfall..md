### Post by drogoh on 2006-04-26
Nobody but Sun caused Sun's impending demise.  The same goes for Cray before SGI was forced to buy them up, the same goes for SGI now.  These are niche companies that have not evolved to keep ahead of technology and instead have rested on their laurels while "low end" technology caught up and hit them in the face.

Linux isn't to blame for Sun or SGI falling from grace.  It's more appropriate to say that AMD and Intel built the coffins for these once goliath computer companies.  I don't need a multimillion dollar Sun Fire server when I can put together something that works comparable for a fraction of the cost.  By all means, the Linux kernel or even the Linux umbrella term to mean "operating system" is not the David that slayed the mighty Goliath.

The only way Sun would have been able to save itself is to lower its prices once the x86 caught up to SPARC.  Linux has no role in Sun's downfall.  My dual 450MHz Ultra 60 running Solaris performs quite well compared to an Athlon XP 2200+ I have running FreeBSD, and the Ultra 60 was manufactured in May of 1999.

The fact of the matter is that corporate beancounters can't justify spending $30,000 for a single Sun Blade 2000 these days when their purchasing manager can call Dell and get a pallet of OptiPlexes with a comparable loadout for the same price or even less than the price of that nifty Blade.  It's not Linux that killed the supercomputer giants, it was Intel's little 8086 from 1978 that gave rise to the most successful processor architecture that did.

### Post by nocturn on 2006-04-26
Here's how I see it if Free Software pushes out the commercial vendors, and keep in mind that this is a process of years, not days or months.

Windows is a MS product, therefor benefitting the US economy the most off course.  If you move to Linux in bulk, there will be a large shift from the old model.

Companies would either rely on local support companies for their distribution or build a more comprehensive in-house support group for it.  This means that the money spent will flow back to the local economy.   The use of Open Standards in Free Software makes it easy to shift to a new distro or program (instead of being locked in), so the IT landscape will probably be made up of a lot of smaller to medium-sized companies instead of the giants we see today.

In the end, I see the demise of giant corporations such as MS and even Sun a good thing.  These giga-corporations have become slow and arrogant over the years, while you see smaller businesses like Canonical innovating.

The shift to a more localised spending pattern will have the greatest impact on the poorer countries, where extra jobs will be created instead of the money being spent on pricy licenses from a foreign country.

That's just my 2 cents.
