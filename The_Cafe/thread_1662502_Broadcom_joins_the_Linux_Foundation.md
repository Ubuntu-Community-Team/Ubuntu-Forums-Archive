### Post by Dr. C on 2011-01-08
> **ugm6hr said:**
> Companies care about their customers wishes, if they coincide with could fiscal sense.
The Linux community has always known this - hence Bug #1.

This is right on the money. When Bug #1 was started in 2004 GNU / Linux on the desktop was around 0.1% to 0.3% of the market. Now it is more like 1% to 3% of the market. Now consider the scenario of a manufacturer of USB wireless dongles. Walk into a big box retail store and ask if such a device works with GNU / Linux. The most likely response is: > We don't know, but we have a customer satisfaction policy. If it does not work we will give you your money back, if you bring the item with your receipt within x number of days If the retail return rate for these devices among Windows and Mac users is typically say 2%, The impact of a 2% market share for GNU / Linux is to literally double the return rate for those chipsets not supported by GNU / Linux, as 100% of GNU / Linux will be returning the device, while keeping it the same for those chipsets that are compatible. Returns are very costly for the industry. The choice for the manufacturer then becomes obvious. 

Another example: As recently as 6 months ago we were looking at some laptops. Under Microsoft Windows 7 one goes to ```
Control Panel\All Control Panel Items\Device Manager
``` and looks for Broadcom chips. If any were found the laptop was eliminated from consideration. The market share of GNU / Linux is now large enough to get the attention of manufacturers.
