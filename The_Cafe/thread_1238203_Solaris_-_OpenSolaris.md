### Post by Fallen_Demon on 2009-08-27
Sorry to necro, but I used to work as a Sun campus ambassador. 

To make it simple: OpenSolaris is Sun's attempt to get Solaris back into the mainstream. It emphasises technologies that are useful to enterprise servers (comon, a fully redundant RAIDz 5 array isn't going to be in my lounge room for a while) and other cool gadgetry like network virtualization through CrossBow and the fast reboot (my record is 10s from "system is down" back to the GDM login :D). At this point, I think it's very safe to say it's aimed at DEVELOPERS, it is unsuited to the typical n00buntu (sorry, but let's face it, with increased popularity comes the people who like to think they know a system. This is not a stab at Ubuntu, just the people who use it without really knowing how it's different to Windows or Mac OS X) fantard who can't appreciate the beauty and power offered by a mature, rock solid and expertly engineered system. Ok, maybe that was a bit harsh. Linux is awesome (I use openSUSE as my main laptop OS and have even done LFS), but it's a world away from Solaris and *BSD, I can't emphasise that enough. 

Basically, it comes down to preference. I love both Linux and OpenSolaris, but for different reasons. Linux has mountains of support and is very nice for development work due to it's lean nature, but OpenSolaris has absolutely amazing technologies. And, I would ask, so I don't post something as pretentious as this in the future, you just Google it. [OpenSolaris.org]("http://www.opensolaris.org") has info on a lot of the backend differences and plenty of discussion about differences between the systems while [OpenSolaris.com]("http://www.opensolaris.com") gives you the marketing spiel and shows off all the whizbang features of OpenSolaris

EDIT: Anyone interested in testing out what will eventually become Solaris 11 can download and install Solaris Express Community Edition from opensolaris.org. It's released every couple of months, but I wouldn't recommend it as a production platform
