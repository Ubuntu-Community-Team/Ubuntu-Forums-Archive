The kevlar in chainsaw resistant boots, chaps, and pants works differently than that in bulletproof kevlar materials.  In the case of the chainsaw protection what happens is the chain sucks the kevlar fibers out of the boots/chaps/pants and into the main body of the saw, where they jam up the sprocket that spins the chain.  This happens very quickly as the chain is moving at around 60mph at max rpms.  However, a powerful enough saw will cut through kevlar chainsaw protection given enough pushing force.  

How do I know this?  I am an arborist.  I use chainsaws for a living.

I can also tell you that cleaning all of those kevlar fibers out of the chainsaw is a real pain.

### Post by Paqman on 2011-04-18
> **eriktheblu said:**
> 
The vest I was issued was there to protect mostly from indirect small arms fire (extended range or ricochet) and fragmentation (from nearby explosives or or small arms strikes). The preferred defense against rifle fire is a large quantity of dirt.


Sure, but there are several levels of protection available. Ones designed to be worn under clothes don't generally offer a particularly good level of protection, whereas ones issued to front-line troops will stop 9mm at 5m or less, almost all frag, and have a good chance of deflecting a lot of other stuff if it hits a plate.

Nothing is really designed to offer you guaranteed protection against rifle bullets. There's a reason why high-velocity rifles are the weapon of choice for combat. At the moment the weapons are still well ahead in the armour-vs-penetrator arms race, but that might change in the future.

Kevlar is a brand name btw, and only applies to aramid made by Du Pont.
