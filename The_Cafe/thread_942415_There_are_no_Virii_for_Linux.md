those "in-jokes" are an example of grammatical creativity, and they are always "correct", in that they're either over-generalizations of correct language usage, or can be rationalized. -en (or -an) is a common plural in most germanic languages (including English) and it still occurs in English (eg oxen, bretheren) so it's 'acceptable'. Likewise, the plural of Unix could be "Unices" because Latin words on -ix form their plural that way. The over-generalization here is that you apply Latin grammar to a word that isn't Latin (but could be, judging by the looks of it). 
Likewise, people have argued that the plural of caboose is cabeese, and the plural of mongoose is polygoose


for virii, non of the above applies, it is just wrong, there's no way the latin word 'virus' can form a plural 'virii', no matter how many rules you bend, stretch or over-generalize.
In stead of showing an understanding of pluralization mechanisms in languages, it shows a lack thereof. So it sounds really silly.

### Post by igknighted on 2008-10-09
> **leg said:**
> Me too and even after reading [this]("http://www.askoxford.com/asktheexperts/faq/aboutspelling/affect") I am not that sure!

Easiest way to think of it is if you are using the word as a verb, it is almost always "affect".  When it is a noun, it is "effect".  The verb "effect", is quite different and means, essentially, to get something done (most often seen as "effect a change").

Examples:

The weather affected the picnic.
     // here affect is the action in the sentence

The effect of the bad weather was that the picnic had to be canceled.
     // here effect is a noun, and actually is the subject of the sentence
