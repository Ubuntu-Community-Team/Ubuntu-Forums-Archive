I think eventually issues like DRM will result in massive legal reform.  It's generally a trend in legal policy that when a law or set of laws become so lop-sided that rulings become ridiculous, "judicial activism" starts to swing in or otherwise governments legislate to keep things in control.

Negligence law for example, started with Donoghue vs Stevenson [1932] and expanded so much that people were being awarded millions for silly injuries.  Well that got curbed pretty sharp when Civil Liabilities Acts were introduced in Australia in years 2002 and 2003.  I think other jurisdictions may have adopted similarly heavy-handed legislation to limit such claims as well.

Right now the legal environment is leaning towards conservative observance of intellectual property rights, hence giving more and more power to record and movie publishers.  Once the bubble gets big enough, it will pop.

As much as I hate to defend recording companies and movie publishers, it's not the fault of the businesses or the government that copyright laws for electronic media are so restrictive.  It just makes sense for them to be so restrictive from a legal point of view.

Electronic media is easily copied and distributed with minimal effort.  That characteristic alone, distinguishes it from most other consumer products like home furniture, appliances, vehicles, etc.  If you purchase a motor vehicle, you cannot create copies of it and sell those copies without investing massive amounts of money, effort, and time.  But you can easily purchase a CD or DVD and sell copies of them while investing very little money and time.  So physical products just cannot be treated in the same way as electronic media.

From a legal standpoint, electronic media needs to be as restricted as print media, if not more.  At least with print media, it requires substantial physical effort to reproduce books, magazines, etc., and physical effort and money to distribute them.  Electronic media can be copied, stored, and distributed, entirely in electronic form requiring almost no effort and very small monetary investment, so this ease of copying and distribution needs to be offset by further restrictions.

People who put the effort and investment into producing and distributing electronic media - as with any other works - has a right to profit from it.  How much and at what cost to consumers?  That's the contentious issue! :)

If we really want the laws and the market environment to change, opponents of current copyright laws (regarding electronic media) need to create an alternative form of publication and distribution which is equally or more profitable than the established method, while liberating consumer rights.  If such a method can be created and proven to be effective, then businesses and legislature will turn their heads and adapt this more liberal form of electronic media publication and distribution.  Until that happens, laws are unlikely to get easier for consumers.
