### Post by bashveank on 2009-04-09
Make sure you get a monitor with LED backlighting. LED backlights provide much deeper blacks and much more even light distribution than traditional florescent backlighting.
Also, generally, the higher the contrast ratio the better, but it's not really a major issue.
If you're doing graphic design, photo editing, magazine layout, etc... you'll also want to get something that can render AdobeRGB as close as possible.

### Post by Miguel on 2009-04-09
My suggestion is going for either a PVA or an IPS (if you can afford it) panel. Most TFT panels use a technology named TN (meaning "twisted nematic", a term only scientists and engineers working with liquid crystals know what it means). For some information, check [wikipedia](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_film_transistor_liquid_crystal_display#Types). 

The reasoning behind this is that TN panels (all the cheap ones) have poor colour reproduction, poor viewing angles and the colour changes depending on the angle. This doesn't happen at all on IPS panels and in PVA panels you only have a slight gray shifting.

### Post by Ralex1098 on 2009-04-11
Contrast ratio is supposed to be the difference between the darkest color (black) and the lightest color (white). The problem is that the environment really skews your perception of the screen, and since there is no regulation regarding the conditions in which these measurements are taken, it's impossible to compare contrast ratios. 

I do agree with you though and just get one that looks good. Be sure that it at least has a good operating record though (won't break down in 8 days or something crazy).
