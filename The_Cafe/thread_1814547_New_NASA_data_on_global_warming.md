### Post by Kimm on 2011-07-29
First of all, I'd like to point out that there is more than once model for predicted climate change, some of them for more extreme scenarios than others. 

Secondly, even though the change is not as extreme as was predicted by some models, it still does not invalidate global warming, if anything it strengthens it (if we disregard my third point). As the new knowledge shows that global temperature change is occurring, although not as extremely as some would have predicted.

Thirdly, one of the previous posters was completely correct when he stated that 10 years is not even close to long enough to get accurate data on global climate change. Even though the overall trend is positive there will be periods that are warmer and periods that are cooler, the prediction models are based on data collected from various sources that span extremely long periods (something along the lines of 50,000-200,000 years).

Finally, even though the earth had a past climate that was warmer than current models predict, this is not any reason to ignore the warnings. Its not just a matter of temperature, its a matter of the time-scale. If the temperature increases too quickly, nature may simply not have enough time to adapt (i.e. species will not get enough time to evolve or relocate to a new suitable habitat). We have examples in the geological record where sudden climate change has caused global mass extinction.

About temperature records...

Suppose you start observing temps now. If there is no global increase, then next year, the probability of a record is 1/2 on average (across many different pairs of years when one supposedly starts). It's either lower or higher, thus 1/2.

At year 3, probability of a record is 1/3 because that year can have a highest, middle or lowest temp with the same probability.

Thus, the number of records in N years is on average
1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + ... + 1/N. (N. Glick wrote on this in "The American Mathematical Monthly")

In 10 years since the records started, the number of highest recorded temperatures is expected to be 1.9

In 100 years, there would be about 4 records.
In 1000 years, there would be about 6.5 records.
In a million years, there would be about only 14 records.

It's hard to beat records if there is no trend! (we see records in sports precisely because there is a trend of increased performance over the years)
