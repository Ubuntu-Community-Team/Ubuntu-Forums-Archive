### Post by beew on 2011-07-26
> **armageddon08 said:**
> 


I don't think it has optimus. It has nvidia gt 525M. What is optimus for?


Optimus is a Nvidia technology which dynamically switches between the Nvidia card and a onboard Intel card to save battery life but it works only for Windows7 (not even Vista or XP)

The problem of Optimus with Linux is that not only the switching doesn't work, with Optimus you can't use the Nvidia card at all even though it has Linux driver because the card becomes a hybrid under Optimus and the Linux driver only works with the stand alone Nvidia card. So with Optimus you are stuck with a low performance Intel card (which perform worse than the Intel card working alone) but the Nvidia card is still sitting there generating heat and draining battery.

If you search this forum there are a couple of threads about the XPS15 which are marked "solved" and their so called solution is to turn off the Nvida card and turn it into an expensive paper weight. It is not a solution at all, just the lesser evil.


There is some work recently that seems promising, but it is still very alpha. Vdpau still doesn't work and probably never will (from the bumblebee site)

[http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2011/05/bumbleebee-brings-nvidia-optimus-gpu-switching-to-linux-users/](http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2011/05/bumbleebee-brings-nvidia-optimus-gpu-switching-to-linux-users/)

The problem is many new laptops with Nvidia comes with Optimus. If there is an option in the BIOS to turn off Optimus to use only the discrete graphic card then you will be able to use the Nvidia card(examples Thinkpad T410, T510), otherwise your only option is bumblebee. I read somewhere that there are several models of the Dell XPS 15. the core-i5 models all have Optimus but the core-i7 one doesn't, but I can't confirm it.

### Post by pelle.k on 2011-07-26
+1 on avoid optimus. With ATI, switchable graphics might become easier to live with,  thanks to the upcoming changes done to the kernel and userland, in future releases of ubuntu.
Still, switchable graphics presents another problem (even with a working solution in linux), and that one is a hardware limitation i'm afraid. Most laptops/notebooks with switchable graphics come with the intel IGP connected to the D-SUB out, and the high-end gpu connected to the HDMI/DP port. That presents all kind of problems, should you ever want to connect an external monitor to your laptop. Even if you accept that, and go with the intel IGP on the DSUB output, the DSUB output is analog, and can't deliver a signal of enough quality for say 1980x1080 resolution. It's a damn mess if you ask me.

There's one notebook with a decent GPU, connected to both a DVI and HDMI output (no analog, antiquated dsub), no optimus, and that's the clevo P150HM (15 inch) and the clevo P170HM(17 inch). You can get it with nvidia gtx 480m, gtx 560m, gtx 585m and also thr ati 6970m.

If you can live without a good GPU though, the built in intel HD 3000 in sandy bridge have none of the problems with switchable graphics (or external output configurations), of course.
