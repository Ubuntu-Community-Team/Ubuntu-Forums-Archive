### Post by tgalati4 on 2010-06-06
That's a tough one.  

A better strategy is to take a job with the first offer.  You will probably be under a "probationary status" for three months.  They may or may not want to keep you.  Then at the 6-month or 1-year point, put out feelers for something better.  If you are due for a raise after 1-year, you can compare your raise with an offer to go to another job.

Playing mind games with the HR department is not wise when starting out.

### Post by GregBrannon on 2010-06-06
An offer will typically come with an expiration date.  That date may be negotiable, but if the prospective employer has a real opening in this economy, that means there is work that needs doing.  Either somebody else is trying to do it, or it's not being done.  The prospective employer is not going to wait so that you can gather multiple offers for the best one.

But you can help yourself by scheduling multiple interviews as close together as possible, hoping that the time to respond to multiple offers will have some overlap.

It is a tough market, there are lots of "recent college graduates," and the starting salaries for approximately-equal candidates vying entry-level positions fall within a fairly narrow band.  As suggested earlier, it is best to get started at something to develop special skills, talents, and a reputation that you can market elsewhere.

You're focused on salary but consider the benefits as part of the whole package.  Something you need - like health care - that costs you less at one company means less money out of your salary at that company.

Good luck!
