### Post by wersdaluv on 2008-10-16
The Dust theme has always been popular in [http://gnome-look.org](http://gnome-look.org) but I didn't like it before because it's not polished enough for my taste. Now that the version 0.2 is out, it's a different case. I always liked the idea behind the theme and in this version, the idea is closer to reality than ever. They achieved a modern tango-compliant look. I really like it. 

It's just that, this theme still has to be developed and a good theme to compare it with is Shiki-Colors. It still isn't as polished as that hybrid theme (a hybrid theme has the advantages of a dark theme and the usability of a light one). There is a number of bugs that still have to be squashed here in Dust while Shiki-Colors only has two known bugs.

Overall, I think, it's a great theme. It's what I'm using now with the Gnome-Colors icon theme.

### Post by benerivo on 2008-10-17
You should be able to install the deb file safely. Ubuntu will check the version is more recent than your currently installed murrine, and then install it as an upgrade. When the repository version becomes 0.61 or something more recent then it will upgrade the package again. All of this will be visible in synaptic, so at any time you can check what version you are on and see if it is a 'local' package (a .deb) or a 'repository' package.

### Post by Giant Speck on 2008-11-27
> **wersdaluv said:**
> Is this for real? LCD monitors _actually_ use more power to display darker colours than bright? Wow. Any proof? :)

Here is an excerpt from a Scientific American article about the subject:

> The notion that black screens save electricity certainly makes sense when you're talking about cathode-ray tube, or CRT, technology that works by moving an electron beam back and forth across the back of the screen. "The front screen is covered with red, blue and green phosphors," says Bill Schindler, vice president of electrical engineering for Panasonic Plasma Display Laboratory of America. To produce white, the electron beam is directed at the phosphors. However, "when the screen is black, you don't have to fire the beam," he adds.

CRT monitors, which until a few years ago were the predominant models among PC users, consume more power when a computer screen is white. To confirm this, Schindler measured the energy output of an 18-inch (45.7-centimeter) CRT monitor and found it used 102 watts when the screen was white but only 79 watts when the display was black.

This is not the case, however, with LCD monitors, which have no phosphors and represent the lion's share of every new monitored purchased in the developed world, including those used by laptops. Instead, LCD displays rely on an array of thin-tube fluorescent bulbs that provide a constant source of light to create a white screen. To make it black, LCDs rely on a diffuser to block this light. As a result, LCDs use more energy than CRTs to display a black screen. Measuring a 17-inch (43-centimeter) LCD monitor, Schindler found that white required 22.6 watts, while black came in a tad higher at 23.2 watts. With a 20-inch (50.8-centimeter) LCD, black required 6 percent more energy than white.

You can find the entire article here:  [http://www.sciam.com/article.cfm?id=fact-or-fiction-black-is](http://www.sciam.com/article.cfm?id=fact-or-fiction-black-is)
