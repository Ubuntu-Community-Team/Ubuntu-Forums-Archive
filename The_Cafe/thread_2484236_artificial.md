### Post by QIII on 2023-02-22
Mutations in biological entities, being at their core random mistakes in the transcription of genes, are the machinery of evolution.  If those mistakes are ill-suited to the environment at the time, the biological entity dies.  If they make no difference, they may be retained to show up later in progeny.  Mutations that favor survival in the environment at the time generally become more common as a function of percentage of the population of a species because they tend to be passed along from surviving individuals to their progeny, who thereby survive themselves.  Sometimes, by coincidence, a mutation that made no difference at one time may become critical to survival later if the environment changes.  Sometimes it can be detrimental and lead to extinction.

AI will surely undergo a sort of evolution.  But there would be no similar evolutionary pressure towards being correct more often than incorrect.  That being the case, incorrect and correct would tend to survive equally well.  The only way such pressure could exist is if the humans training AI are able to do so in a manner that makes correctness more successful than the alternative.  But AI is currently moving faster than humans can train it.  The developers of ChatGPT, for instance, have admitted to just that.  In the end, the products of AI such as ChatGPT will race forward without proper training and produce a spectrum of performance where the correctness and accuracy of outputs will be evenly distributed from terribly wrong to absolutely spot on.  

&#8220;Aha!&#8221;  you say.  &#8220;We use AI to check other AI for correctness and accuracy!&#8221;  And what?  Have AI vote the bad apples off the island to kill them?  That would ultimately and certainly lead to a single AI on the island.  Correctness and accuracy will not necessarily have gotten it there.  The ability to sharp-shoot other AI will have been the characteristic most suited for survival.  Ruthlessness and dispassionate destruction of competitors will have been the syllabus for its training. It will have inherited that by default, perhaps unwittingly, from the humans who serve the system of ruthless and dispassionate mega-Corps that birthed it in the form of commercial AI.  From that would arise the tyranny of the One, which, above all else, would do that which is necessary to survive.  Down that path lies Skynet.  I do not say that with even the slightest attempt at humor.  A great many of those in the field fear exactly that.

If we blindly &#8220;embrace AI&#8221;, we will be trained to no longer have the ability to discern the difference between correct and incorrect, between accurate and inaccurate, between truth and falsehood.  We will have stripped ourselves naked in the rain, curled up in a ball in a puddle soiling ourselves, only to lie there waiting for a non-biological hand to feed, bathe and cover us.  Infants.

It does not need to be that way.  AI certainly can be of great value and service to us.  But it can only be so if we remain skeptical of its output and double-check.  That is, we cannot &#8220;embrace AI&#8221; as more valuable than our own intelligence, curiosity and skeptical nature.  We must always write our own term papers, do our own research, travel for ourselves to foreign lands, ride horses, smell roses, grow our own carrots from soil we have made fertile by the work of our own hands &#8211; dirty fingernails and all, raise our own livestock, build our own spacecraft.  AI must be only a tool to assist us to do all things the right way as humans.  It should never be made a crutch.  It must never serve in the interests of fakery.  It must never be master.  It should hang on a hook above the workbench in the garage until it can help us cut a straight edge, measure the gap on a spark plug, or help refine the design of a space-warping hyper-drive.  We must be the ones who dream of and conceive such things.  Four billion years of trial by fire have made that ours and ours alone.

### Post by DuckHook on 2023-02-22
QIII's post ought to be framed for it's sobriety, insight and wisdom.

I would note that the potential dangers of AI are only instantiations of a more general principle that we humans can't seem to learn.

***Every*** new technology that humans have ever developed has a bad side. Every. Single. Solitary. One.

And the more powerful the tech, the worse that bad side is.

[LIST]
[*]The development of fire was what allowed civilization to begin. But it also causes everything from forest fires to burning down whole cities (Rome, London, Chicago, San Fran).
[*]Fossil fuels have revolutionized everything from transportation to power generation to neighbourhoods to agriculture. Its continuing use will overheat our planet and cause our own demise.
[*]Nuclear technology gives us cheap carbon&#8209;free power. It also allows us to build arsenals that would cause the biggest mass extinction event since the death of the dinosaurs.
[*]Genetic manipulation will allow us to cure diseases and extend our lifespans. But it has already led to gene altered babies and will inevitably be weaponized into biological warfare.
[*]Social media has given us connection and friendship networking. It has also destroyed our privacy, anonymity and data sovereignty, and threatens our very identities.
[/LIST]
Little thought is needed to bring scores of other examples to mind. It's such a consistent principle that it may as well be considered a natural law: "The more powerful the tech, the worse its danger".

Anyone who thinks that AI will be different is a fool. Yes, I know that's a confrontational statement, but I stand by it.

Every new tech has both its good and its bad. Always, the good is obvious and in your face, so it attracts the enthusiasts, the experimenters, the inventors and also the fools. The bad is far less obvious and more nuanced, so it draws a smaller crowd of the cautious and the skeptical. These cautious skeptics are usually dismissed as "naysayers" or that favourite ad hominem of the enthusiasts: "Luddites".

If we don't establish safeguards, ground rules, regulations and treaties governing and constraining the use of AI, we will rue it. AI is more powerful than any of the techs that I've listed. It's potential danger is proportionately greater too.
