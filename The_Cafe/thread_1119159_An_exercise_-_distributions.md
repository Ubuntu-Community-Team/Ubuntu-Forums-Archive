### Post by ryaxnb on 2009-04-07
I found an old post in a thread and it reminded me of a good exercise.
Try explaining in simple terms what the differences and similarities between the following distributions are:
* Mandriva
* SuSE
* Fedora
* Ubuntu
* Linux Mint
* Debian
* PCLinuxOS
* Puppy Linux
BONUS POINTS * Sabayon
BONUS POINTS * Sidux
Using anywhere from 1 or 2 sentences to 2 paragraphs per distribution.
Keep terms so a intermediate windows user could understand them. 
:popcorn:
I'll start:
*Mandriva: This distribution tries to be something for everyone, from beginners to advanced users. It offers Graphical Control Panels and has a friendly user community. However, there has been turmoil inside the compaany lately, and the future outlook is uncertain.
* SuSE: This distribution, designed for enterprises and businesses as well as the average user, Includes plenty of tools useful mainly to businesses, as well as some functions for Microsoft compatibility, and iPod support. It also offers a graphical control panel and a wide variety of install options, but can be a little harder to install then some distributions. Finally, SuSE can be sluggish to adopt to new trends due to a long length between updates.
* Fedora: This distribution is fast and modern, but can be a little unstable and harder to use than some. It adopts the latest trends early on. It can be a little more difficult to administer than SuSE or Mandriva. It does feature a good installer, and a friendly community.
* Ubuntu: With one of the best communities of any distribution, Ubuntu is well known for being community-focused. It also offers a simple desktop that is focused on functionality and ease of use, not aesthetics. Ubuntu is often considered the distro with the best support, and it has a balance between modern and stable.
* Linux Mint:
This distribution doesn't have the community or inertia of Ubuntu, nor does it have the wide variety of support options, but it offers ease of use and install not matched by any other distribution. This distribution works easily with common formats such as Windows Media, Mp3 and Youtube.
* Debian: This distibution is designed for stability and to be truly free. What free means is it is free to use, modify and inspect, and free for developers anywhere to do the same. Because of the fact that the distribution is purely "free", some hardware and files may not work as well out of the box, but this can be fixed. This distribution is typically updated only every 1.5 to 2.5 years, so that stability is maintained. The community can be a bit aloof.
*PCLinuxOS: This distribution is designed to be simple to configure and use, and compatible with most hardware. The Downside of this distribution is it lacks the inertia of bigger distributions like SuSE or Ubuntu. Furthermore, the community can be a bit aloof. Also, the amount of available programs is smaller than some. However, this distribution has many upsides as well, like a graphical control panel, easy wireless configuration, and a fast update cycle called "rolling release".
*Puppy Linux: This distribution is designed for lesser-power computers like the netbooks and older computers from the 1990s and early 2000s. It lacks some common features in other distributions, but places much less demand on hardware. It contains some graphical configuration tools. 
Now you try!

### Post by cardinals_fan on 2009-04-07
Mandriva: a popular Red Hat derivative focused on ease of setup and use.  It has lost much of its former glory in the past few years, but the last couple releases have garnered a lot of praise.

openSUSE: Novell's community distro and testbed for SLED.  Also Red Hat based and also aiming for easy setup.  Well-known for bulky graphical config tools.

Fedora: Red Hat's testbed for RHEL and community distro.  Focuses on the latest technologies, freedom, and ease of administration.  Lots of graphical tools.  The packages are more vanilla than with most big distros and they maintain a close relationship with upstream.  Fedora provides a ton of testing that other distros benefit from.

Ubuntu: a Debian derivative that has become hugely popular.  Aims for ease of setup and use.  Many graphical tools and a gigantic community.  Financed by Canonical, which in turn was founded by millionaire Mark Shuttleworth (who is the project's absolute leader).

Linux Mint: I don't consider Mint a distribution.  It is Ubuntu with some well-thought-out tools and many codecs/players preinstalled.

Debian: an old and very popular distro.  Has no corporate backing and is led by volunteer committee.  Stable releases are extremely stable, while unstable branches are more up-to-date.

PCLinuxOS: a new distro that gained a ton of attention and popularity but is now experiencing growing pains.  Based off Mandriva, but with more codecs and is rolling release.  A number of PCLinuxOS devs have just defected, and the project is somewhat stagnant.

Puppy Linux: a popular live CD distro often used on older machines.  Features many graphical tools, but can be a bit cumbersome for installed use.  Longtime developer Barry Kauler recently stepped down as leader.

Sabayon: a Gentoo derivative targeting those who want an out-of-the-box system.  Known for including just about everything on the giant DVD image.  Aims to bring the power of Portage to less experienced users.

Sidux: a newer distro based on Debian Sid.  This is a rolling-release system and provides the latest from Debian's unstablest branch.  Aims to be somewhat lightweight.

There are many more that I would have put on this list.  How were these chosen?
