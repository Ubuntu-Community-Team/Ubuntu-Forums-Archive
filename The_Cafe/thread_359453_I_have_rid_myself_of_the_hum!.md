### Post by SZF2001 on 2007-02-12
A little while ago I posted a topic about buying a refurbished computer, or a cheap one, you know. With a little help from the forums, I got my computer to stop humming.

A little bit of backround story - I bought this computer two years ago, and before that the school had gotten it for free and it was like three years older, so it's a bit of an older guy.

Anyway, ever since I had the computer, there has been some kind of humming. I usually just use headphones and try to ignore it - except in the past few days it for REALLY loud and I couldn't take it anymore.

I was planning on buying a new power box, since that's what I through the humming was coming from, when I got a little bored and twirled the fan inside the computer on the motherboard. I watched and listened as it twirl, and guess what? BBBRRRVVVVVV, it said. I can't believe it - by screwing around I actually found the problem!

I rubbed my finger against one side of the fan and looked at it - pure black. Oh God, it was gross. I don't know if what I did was a good idea on cleaning it - I got a Q-tip, dampened it, dried it a little bit so it was just a little soaked, and rubbed around and inside the fan.

The first run of cleaning, when I pulled the Q-tip out, I'm not even kidding - strands of **hair** came out, along with other greasy black gunk. What in the hell did these people exactly do with this computer for the past few years?!

I cleaned and cleaned and cleaned the fan until I could tell it was spottless. Suddenly, I realised I just used water inside on my mother-boards fan, and if I remember right, water and electricity aren't exactly the best friends.

Regardless, I plugged everything back in, and here I am now. It's not a super quiet computer, I mean you can obviously tell it's on - but *damn*, I'm loving the humless computer right now! In fact, I've noticed a bit of speed performance when I open and use various apps. I can't believe it.

Like I said before - who knew that screwing around with something would end up showing you the way? I just saved myself like $50... Imagine my rage, if you will, after thinking that you've pin-pointed the problem and it pursists after you use all your money...

Best night of my life, lemme tell ya. Thanks Ubuntu members for encouraging me to poke and prob around in my box!

### Post by mips on 2007-02-12
For future reference use a smallish paint brush (about 1.5-2cm wide) and brush of all the dust on fans, motherboards and other components. Follow that up with compressed air from a compressed air can, blower or hair dryer.

If you have to use a liquid then use pure alcohol as it evaporates very quickly. Also useful for cleaning electrical contact surfaces.

### Post by glotz on 2007-02-12
Another useful trick is to spray compressed air at the heatsink to blow off the dust if you don't feel like actually taking it off and giving it a shower. The heat will be conducted faster and your fans will run slower and thus quieter. Also, you don't always need active cooling, i.e. the fan on the heatsink.

### Post by tagra123 on 2007-02-12
Here's something else that helps.

This should only take 5 to 10 minutes and might make what appears to be a dead fan work again.

I did this with all my fans  Originally I had a CPU fan that would spin for a few minutes -- make a lot of noise and stop than make some intermittent noises and start again.


This is what works well:

   Sewing Machine Oil or 3 in 1 oil
   Graphite Powder (Ususally near the lock & keys in hardware)
   Paper Towel
   A couple q-tips
Total cost < $5.00

Turn off the computer (shouldn't have to say it)

Remove the CPU fan from the heatsink. This can usually be done without removing the heatsink from the computer.  Most have 4 screws, some just slide off.

Dust off heatsink with a small art type of brush. Blow with some canned air if you have it.

Now for the fan

Find the base of the motor (where the wires disappear) and carfully remove the sticker.  You'll want to reuse the sticker.

Next, on some fans there is a gasket and O-ring.  If present scotch tape will help pull it off.

Take the graphite powder an give one or two squirts until the parts inside the hole look dusted -- you don't need a mountain of graphite here.

Next, put on or two drops of the sewing machine oil in the same hole. spin the fan a couple of times.

Wipe the top of the area with the paper towel so the sticker will stick again.

Place the  O-ring and gasket in the hole. Place the sticker over all of it and if the sticker doesn't want to stick scotch tape is fine.

To really clean the external parts of the fan place a drop or two of the oil on a q-tip and start wiping the blades.

When finished put back in the computer.  If the fan was making noise before then it might make noise for a few seconds but it will quiet down and the only sound you should hear is the air moving through it.

As far as lubing the motor  -- you should only have to do this once.  

By the way -- never blow compressed on the fans without restraining the blades with a pencil or q-tip.  THe air can make the fan spin faster than it was meant to and damage it.
