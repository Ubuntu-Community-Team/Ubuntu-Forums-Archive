### Post by QIII on 2022-05-23
Even the "truth", taken out of context, can be misleading, either by accident or deliberately.

Unfortunately, people can be hurt by misinformation.  Snake oil salesman can influence purchase of dubious products by saying "clinically tested to ..." which  is not the same as "clinically proven to ..."  "Clinically tested to ..." may be absolutely true.  Someone could have tested.  But was it proven?  Add to that the question of who did the clinical research.  We have the whole saga of the tobacco industry long before the internet to show that.

Even short of deliberate misdirection, people can mislead others even with the purest of intentions if they are not careful themselves.  The "information age" has magnified that to a truly scary extent.  I'm afraid that battle is lost.  And trying to teach people to fact-check and apply reason may be a lost cause.

A very simple example of non-malicious, non-deliberate misinformation is boiling or freezing water.  There are two misleading preconceptions:  one that cold water boils more quickly than hot, second that boiling water freezes before cold.  This doesn't stand up to the scrutiny of Physics.  However, it is difficult to convince others that such deep-seated myths are not true.  Each is a simple equation of the heat energy contained in the water.  Hot water needs less heat energy input from the stovetop to raise it to the boiling point.  Cold water needs to shed less heat energy to freeze.  In each of those cases, time, as a function of rate of addition or extraction of heat, is shorter.

But nobody who makes either statement has any malicious intent.  Just a misunderstanding of basic Physics.

### Post by QIII on 2022-05-23
By the way, for anyone interested in the boiling/freezing question, the Mpemba Effect is flawed here.

Mpemba noticed that hot ice cream froze faster than cold.  This is due to the lower density of hot ice cream with all of its entrained air volume.  Let it melt a bit and the volume of the entrained air decreases, so it takes longer to freeze.  We're talking water here, not a thick liquid with entrained air.

Further, evaporation continues with hot water even in the freezer, so the mass of hot water soon becomes less than the cold, thus that container freezes faster.  Hot water is not freezing faster.  *Less* water is freezing faster ... apparently.  However, consider the water vapor rising from the hot water.  It freezes very quickly -- your freezer might frost up.  Not to mention the cooling effect of the evaporative process.  The freezing time of the entire mass of hot water is relatively the same when you combine the vapor portion and the liquid portion.  Frosting is a good reason to let hot items cool down a bit before putting them in a freezer.
