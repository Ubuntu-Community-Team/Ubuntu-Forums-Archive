### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2007-10-20
Miro is a slow one, and currently a tad buggy (lots of downloads seem to add two videos to the database), but it's pretty nice for what it offers. Hopefully, some day, it will have video support and an option to play media externally, since its media player is really an unnecessary feature; Miro is about sharing complex content over the Internet.
Always a +1 from me: It allows user-configurable menu accelerators! That's really great for a media player since I can select a menu item, press the corresponding media key on my keyboard. Then, I can just press that button as the shortcut to that menu item. (Which is why Every function of a program should be in the menu -*COUGH* Totem *cough*, and the GNOME keyboard shortcuts thing shouldn't need to exist!). Unfortunately, I have to give it -.6, since it does not remember those accelerators or the size of the vertical pane.

There are some really nice channels on the channel guide. There's one for TED presentations, which are always quite exciting. There are a lot of channels about Linux. National Geographic's channels are excellent, The Discovery Channel has some decent content from time to time, and NASA has a bunch of channels...
I created a "Google Tech Talks" channel, which is also quite often very exciting. (It's just Google search RSS feed, aimed specifically at English tech talks and sorted by date. I take no credit except finding how to get the right URL, which was a bit tricky at the time). Google Tech Talks are little discussions, posted online surprisingly often, about all sorts of computer-related fields. (And sometimes not, which begs the question: What the heck is Google doing?!). Quite fun also is that the technical discussions are, 99% of the time, involving free / open source software and Linux.

Really, Miro is an excellent program, once you get past its flaws. There are faster, sleeker programs which do a similar job (minus the guide), but they just aren't as pretty!
