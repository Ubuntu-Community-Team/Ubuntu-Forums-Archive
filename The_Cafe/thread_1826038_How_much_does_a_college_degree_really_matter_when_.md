### Post by handy on 2011-08-16
I taught myself over a period of roughly 10 years without having any intention of going into business. During that time I was motivated by personal interest only. These days I see that 10 year period as my apprenticeship.

The time came when I swapped from the Amiga systems to Windows 3.11 & shortly after to 95. A while after that I was fixing peoples problems that the pro's in town couldn't & I had people telling me I should go into business.

So I did.

In the following 10 years I was in business, some of my customers were:- two legal firms; two car dealerships, one operating in two different towns; an accountancy firm that was operating in two different towns; another accountancy firm; a hardware/agricultural supply business; a medical doctor's business; a canoe hire/adventure/accommodation business; a country club (golf course/club); a school; a daycare centre; a stock & station agents firm; a surveyors firm; two building firms; a steel manufacturing company; many farmers; a lot of home computer users & these are just what come to mind after being retired for heading for 6 years now.

I have no formal IT qualifications whatsoever.

I found working with hardware to be (usually) easy (& easier as the years went by), the other stuff varies from easy to really hard depending on the circumstances. 

Charge reasonable rates. If you are learning on the job, don't charge whilst you are learning, other customers down the track will pay for the time you spent doing your unpaid learning in previous jobs.

If you make yourself a good technician & charge reasonably, then word of mouth will create a business for you. If you charge **un**reasonably &/or are a poor technician your business will fail as word of mouth will kill it.

After I retired I dropped Windows & went to Linux, where I will always be just a beginner. Which is fine by me, I'm well & truly over having to know how to fix everyone else's problems...
