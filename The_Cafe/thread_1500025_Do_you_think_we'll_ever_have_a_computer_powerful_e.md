### Post by undecim on 2010-06-02
Division by zero doesn't work. 

Why?

Short answer: 

If x=y/z then y=z*x.

Therfore, if x=1/0, then 1=0*x, and there is no number that when multiplied by 0 is equal to -1.

Moreover, multiplication is commutative and associative, which means it doesn't matter what order your multiply numbers in, so suppose there was a number (lets call it "w") that when multiplied by 0 was equal to 1. In that case, w*0*10=10, right? But only if you multiple 0 and w first. If you multiple like w*(0*10), then you get 1! That gives us a contridiction. And since the only new assumption we have is that 1/0=w, that assumption must be wrong, since it leads to a contridiction.

Further explanation:

The reason mathemeticians originally found the need to use imaginary numbers was because there was no solution for the quadratic equation for a=1,b=0,c=1 (x^2 + 0x + 1 = 0 or x^2 + 1 = 0)

Guass (I think...) proved that with the square root of -1, you can solve not only that, but any algebraic equation of any order. That's why we can use "i" to represent the square root of -1.

The reason we can't make up a number to represent 1/0 is because there is no algebraic equation that requires it. As a direct result or that, 1/0 is not a number.

### Post by Hyporeal on 2010-06-02
Computers do divide by zero. It has already been said that some computer processes use NaN ("Not a Number") when dividing by zero. Therefore these processes use an algebra in which division by zero is allowed, and does not abort the computation.

But that is not what we mean when we speak of dividing by zero. We want to retain the useful properties of division, not resort to what amounts to an error code. For example:

A) 0/0 = 1
B) 0^2/0 = 0
C) (1-0^2)/(0+0^2) - 1/0 = -1

Unfortunately, (A) and (B) above appear to have contradictory notions of what 0/0 ought to be. Therein lies the source of contradictions in derivations that utilize division by zero in this way.

Fortunately, division by zero is not a new concept and has been solved, to a certain extent. Newton realized that he needed infinitely small values for his calculus, so he introduced special variables to stand for zero and performed algebra similar to (A), (B), and (C) above. Today we choose to formalize calculus with limits, but directly manipulating infinitesimals gives the correct results as well. Here's a correct version of Sporkman's derivation, with "h" as the infinitesimal part of x (so h effectively equals zero) :

x = 5 + h
x^2 = 25+ 10h + h^2
x^2 - x = 25 + 10h + h^2 - x
x^2 - x - 20 = 25 + 10h + h^2 - x - 20

### Post by McRat on 2010-06-03
There is another error like it that weird, and that's when you are trying to "intercept" a moving value.

Let's say you want to hit an airplane with a missile, but the airplane's movements are not linear.

You aim at it, then find you are too far left.  So you go right.  Now you are too far right, etc.

This can have 3 outcomes:

Your guesses get better and better, until you hit.

Your guesses get worse and worse, until you lose contact.

Your guesses are equally bad in both directions, and you go into an infinite loop.

You see this in engine controls, guidance systems etc.  Ever have a fuel injected car that "hunts" for it's idle RPM?  Then you've seen the last case.  It's hard to always spot, and sometimes you there is no "step" value that is correct if the target is moving.  You need to identify when it goes into that phase quickly, and use a second type of algorithm to get you past that lump in the data.  You may have several different schemes.

Not divide by zero, but something to think about when doing loops.  You need to evaluate progress even when the data "looks" like it's making headway.

x^2 - 4 = (x+2)(x-2)

### Post by StephenF on 2010-06-12
If we sensibly define division as the reverse of multiplication:

a * b = c

so 

c / b = a

Now, let's suppose b is 0 and a is 1 or 5.

1 * b = c
5 * b = c

The value of c will be 0 in both cases so when we do the reverse process of division we have:

0 / 0 = 1
0 / 0 = 5

Two identical sums with two very different answers. The first answer is correct for the reversal of the first multiplication and the second answer is correct for the reverse of the second multiplication. It's not that you can't divide by zero so much as multiplying by zero causes a loss of information.

In conclusion, you are free to specify a somewhat arbitrary answer when presented with a division by zero. Even more so when zero is divided by zero.

Point is, you can't divide a pie into root two pieces, into -3 pieces, into 2i pieces either - but division by these numbers is mathematically well defined. 

Some mathematical operations are closely connected to certain physical operations. That connection is probably how maths got started. Adding, subtraction, division by integers - all related to certain kinds of quantities. But sometimes mathematics is interested in idealisations, abstractions and extensions of the simple physical operations. As well as counting, there are geometrical constructions that go beyond counting; complex numbers were originally discovered/invented simply by postulating that negative numbers possessed a square root, then assuming that those quantities obeyed standard mathematical axioms, then manipulating them algebraically, to get true results about real numbers that couldn't have been derived otherwise; in the original formulations of the calculus, Newton and Euler talked of infinitesimals, numbers that were not zero but were smaller than any finite quantity. 

Ok ok - none of this shows that sense can be made of division by zero. Time dilation - the phenomena that time runs more slowly for moving things - is a genuine, effect which has been measured and is a consequence of relativity. Of course, relativity might be disproved and all this turn out to be wrong - but that's true of any physical theory. At the moment, it's one of the best and most highly confirmed theories we have.

### Post by yossell on 2010-06-17
The photon is massless. See [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon) esp footnote 1.

But that doesn't mean that photons carry no momentum. Photons *do* carry momentum, and they carry energy, they can interact with other things. So you're right about measuring recoil. But you can't infer they have mass from this. Although, in Newtonian mechanics, the momentum of an object is mass x velocity, this is not the *meaning* of momentum; other kinds of objects, photons, electromagnetic radiation, can also carry momentum, and there are equations that tell you how much momentum in terms of, say, wavelengths and frequencies.

The way in which photons interact with other things is very different from the way in which things interact on the Newtonian billiard ball picture. In fact, the concept of a photon is really one that belongs to quantum mechanics rather than relativity per se. Before QM, light was believed to be a kind of wave and a Non-Newtonian wave mechanics was developed in order to explain and account for interactions between light, and electromagnetic waves and classical Newtonian massive particles. 

Although Einstein, in his original paper on special relativity, was focussing on Maxwell's wave mechanics where light was treated as a wave and there is no photon, while, at the same time, in another paper, putting forward the revolutionary idea that light was a particle.

In quantum theory, wave equations/concepts are combined with particle equations/concepts but, while the mathematics and the physical predictions are impeccable, there's still quite a lot of debate about the correct physical interpretation of these equations.

I don't know if it's what your calc prof had in mind, but one trick is to change the bracketing. Without brackets, this reads:
1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1....

As you've bracketed them we see the sum is zero, as each bracket is zero and an infinite number of zeros is zero. But if you bracket the second and third terms, the 4th and 5th terms, you'll get:

1 + (-1 + 1) + (-1 + 1) +....
(I'm rewriting "1 - 1" as "1 + -1" here)

In this case, each bracketed pair equals zero, and the sum is now 1 plus an infinite number of zeros - that is 1.

More complicated rearrangements can be found to give other numbers. 

Of course, what the argument really shows is that one has to be careful about treating infinite sums as satisfying the same algebra as finite sums. In special cases, you can use the same algebra and you won't go wrong. 1 + 1/2 + 1/4... does seem to make good sense - and adds up to 2. 

But these examples show that these rules are not generally valid on sums with infinitely many terms.
