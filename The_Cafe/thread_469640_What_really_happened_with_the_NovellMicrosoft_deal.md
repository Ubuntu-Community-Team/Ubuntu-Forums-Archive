It's a very bad deal which Novell had hoped to capitalize on patent FUD concering Linux, in order to get people to buy SUSE.

Think about it:  MS agreed to give ~200 million dollars in marketing for that agreement.  I think they've certainly funded all their patent FUD with that, but has it done any good for Linux or Novell?  200 million is NOTHING to Microsoft, they make that in an hour.  Here's a good Eben Moglen (Columbia law prof, former FSF chief council, now at SFLC) quote on the situation:

> Eben Moglen: Oh, I beg your pardon, certainly, I, the question was so obvious that it needed no repetition: &#8220;Could I explain the threat posed to GPL&#8217;d software&#8217;s freedom by the Microsoft/Novell agreement?&#8221;.

And I&#8217;m going to speak in slightly more general terms than that, beginning with: Imagine a party which wants to eliminate Free Software&#8217;s freedom or at least hobble its developers in serious ways, so as to inhibit their ability to compete. Imagine that such a party has patents of uncertain validity but in large numbers, which it could conceivably use to scare developers and users. Imagine that such a party then begins to make periodic threats in the form, &#8220;Gee, we have a lot of patents. Never mind how many. Never mind what they are. Never mind how good they are. We have a lot of patents, and someday something terrible will happen. Don&#8217;t use that software.&#8221;

Imagine that that&#8217;s a strategy that the party adverse to freedom engages in because it&#8217;s better than suing. Suing is expensive. Suing is irreversible. And suing might actually cause you to have to explain which patents they are and why they&#8217;re any good. [Laughter] So threatening is better than suing, OK? Imagine a party who engages in recurrent threats every summer time, for years on end, on a sort of annual &#8220;be very afraid&#8221; tour, okay? [Laughter]

I know, it sounds absurd.

Imagine now that what happens is that the annual &#8220;Be very afraid&#8221; tour starts creating terrible pushback, because people call up who are the CEOs of major banks and financial institutions, and they say, &#8220;Those people you&#8217;re threatening are us. We&#8217;re the largest, richest, most powerful people in capitalism, and we determine the value of your stock. We think you should be quiet now.&#8221; OK?

That happens if you do this thing of saying &#8220;be very afraid&#8221; to people who have lots and lots of money and lots and lots of power and who control the value of your stock. They will push back. The business model of threatening to sue people works if the people are 12-year-olds. It does not work real well if they are the pillars of finance capitalism. So as a party engaged in annual &#8220;be very afraid&#8221; tours, you&#8217;re going to start to get pushback by enterprise customers who say, &#8220;That&#8217;s *us* you&#8217;re threatening.&#8221;

Now what if you could reduce their sense of being the people who are made afraid? What if you could find a way to give them quiet and peace -- and make a little money on the side -- so that the only people who are left quaking when you did your annual &#8220;Be Very Afraid&#8221; tour were the developers themselves? Now you would have given yourself a major ecological boost in swinging your patents around and threatening to hurt people.

Deals for patent safety create the possibility of that risk to my clients, the development community. If enterprise thinks that it can go and buy the software my clients make from some party who gives them peace from the adversary in return for purchasing a license from them, then enterprises may think they have made a separate peace, and if they open the business section one morning and it says &#8220;Adversary Makes Trouble for Free Software&#8221;, they can think, &#8220;Not my problem. I bought the such-and-such distribution, and I&#8217;m OK.&#8221;

This process of attempting to segregate the enterprise customers, whose insistence on their rights will stop the threatening, from the developers, who are at the end the real object of the threat, is what is wrong with the deals.

So what you ought to do is to say to parties, Please don&#8217;t make separate peace at the community&#8217;s expense. Please don&#8217;t try to make your customers safe, if that&#8217;s going to result in the destruction of the upstream rain forest where your goods come from. We&#8217;re an ecological system. If you undermine community defenses, you&#8217;re undermining the whole ecology. And doing that for the benefit of your customers at the expense of your suppliers is not a good way to stay in business.

So that&#8217;s the fundamental discussion about the problem created by such deals.

[http://www.groklaw.net/article.php?story=20070517083516872](http://www.groklaw.net/article.php?story=20070517083516872)
