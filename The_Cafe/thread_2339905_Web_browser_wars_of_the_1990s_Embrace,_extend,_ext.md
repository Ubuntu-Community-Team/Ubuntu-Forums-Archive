### Post by Drone4four on 2016-10-13
I’m working on a small research project on the web browser wars of the late 1990s. I’ve got a terrific white paper from Microsoft from 1994 in which Microsoft execs lay out their embrace, extend, extinguish agenda in general terms. The paper was initially confidential but became public during anti-trust court proceedings in the year 2000.  This now public white paper is helpful in terms of shedding light on Microsoft's EEE business strategy. 

But I am curious as to what specific competing specs were introduced by Microsoft and Netscape around this dark (and thankfully relatively temporary) chapter in the history of the world wide web. [Wikipedia says that]("https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embrace,_extend_and_extinguish") “in 1995, Netscape implemented the "font" tag, among other HTML extensions, without seeking review from a standards body.” In what other ways did Microsoft and Netscape try to out maneuver each other? Could someone give me an example (or multiple examples) of specs which Microsoft attempted to viciously implement ahead of Netscape back in the mid to late 90s?

Thanks for your attention.

For sure, **speedwell68**, the browser wars is an ongoing saga.  The web today is rife with widespread proprietary standards and DRM.  But with the miracle success of civil society and community run web browser, Firefox, along with the establishment in 2004 of WHATWG by Google, Opera, and Apple, the open web today is far better off than the dark, unfree and coercive order of the Microsoft monopoly in the year 2000.    According to Google, Blink’s mission statement reads: “To improve the open web through technical innovation and good citizenship”.  By ‘good citizenship’, Google is referring to the goal of ensuring that the features Google ships by default have **open standards**.  Google is more friendly to the web and to the open source community than Microsoft ever was.  We should be grateful to Google (and the open source community) for a more open order which we all take for granted today.  Now we can look back and be grateful that Trident was just a passing nightmare. Would you agree?
