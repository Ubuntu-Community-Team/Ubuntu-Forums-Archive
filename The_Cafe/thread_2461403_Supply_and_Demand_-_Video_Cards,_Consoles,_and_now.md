### Post by Shibblet on 2021-04-29
Everyone is aware of the Video Card shortage.  This is causing the price of back-inventory to reach levels almost triple retail cost.
The claims are that there is a chip shortage... which is also causing the issues for consoles as well.  They also claim that "Data Miners" are buying up all of the video cards for making Crypto Currency.

Now, there are claims that people can do Crypto Mining with Hard Drives and SSD's.  This is going to make Hard Drive prices go up.

In other words, there are a number of reasons as to why the cost of back inventory is through the roof on things like Hard Drives, SSD's, Video Cards, CPU's, and all computer related items...

But I have a different theory.  And it's not a conspiracy theory.

Most home (micro) computers technology hasn't really changed that much in the past 10 years.  I mean, yes, CPU's are a bit faster, and a few more cores... Video Cards are faster, and can do more "effects."  But we may have reached the technology plateau.

How many of us have 7-10 year old computers that still work just fine, and can do mostly what we need them to.  How many of us have 5-7 year old Video Cards that still play current games, with a few features turned off?

So, I wonder if the "supply and demand" problem really stems from distributors who have a truck-ton of back inventory that they need to move, when companies like Nvidia/AMD/Intel keep coming out with new tech, that isn't "that much" better than the previous generation.

### Post by Shibblet on 2021-04-29
> **grahammechanical said:**
> I have often wondered what happened to all the stock on the shelves when new products get released. Yes, I know the price drops. It is why I do not buy the latest but the less than latest. But there must be tons of hardware left unsold as fashions change.
I think that's the thing...  Think about it.  If you have a shelf-full of GTX2060's that retail for $499.95, and Nvidia releases their new GTX3060 that retails for $329.95...  How then will you move your old inventory?

You clearly won't if you have the newer/less-expensive card in stock.

Secondly, tech-savvy people will look at your old inventory and say to themselves, "Nah, I'll wait a few weeks for the new card.  It costs less and performs better."

What is supposed to happen, is a new product comes out... then the previous generation prices go down, and that's how you move back-inventory.  This has always been a source of contention for dealers, if they have to move old inventory below cost, just to make room for the new inventory that has a lower cost.  i.e.  GTX2060 Cost the dealer $399, so they can sell it at $499 (MSRP), but the new GTX3060 costs $249, so they can sell it at $329 (MSRP).  I'm speculating on the actual prices, but you get the idea of cost vs. retail.

I mean, this does create a major problem for dealers who get stuck with back inventory.  No one wants to move product at a loss.  It will actually put dealers out of business.

### Post by rbmorse on 2021-04-29
In a perfect system this is where distributors earn their place in the food chain and, working with manufacturers, provide a "cushion" for retailers in the form of returns, credits or allowances on the purchase of new stock or marketing "incentives" to help move old products off the shelves.  Take a look at Newegg's "deal of the day" or "shellshocker" promotions (and God help you if you ever get put on one of their mailing lists).  Most of what they are really pushing are last gen products distributors have pulled back from retailers and bulked out to Newegg et.al. at discounted wholesale prices.  Amazon does this, too...Costco and Sam's Club in some cases (U.S. market).  Alibaba used to be a notorious dumping ground, but I understand now they prefer to work the problem from the other end (Factory surplus that may or may not be part of the "regular" OEM channel rather that distributor/retailer surplus).
