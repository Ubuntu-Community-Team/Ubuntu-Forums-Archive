### Post by toupeiro on 2008-04-07
I guess I've just become modest, or maybe humbled  in the midst of the people I've come to know and work with over the years, and  perhaps even a little cynical to the "title" based on how it gets used (**see above post for example**).  I know one thing for sure.  The more I know, the more I realize I don't know.  I've been around computers all my life.  I installed my first OS the same year I learned how to write cursive.. :)  Between then and now, I've probably forgotten more than I've retained about different systems. A title only has as much merit as you decide to put into it.  I think once anyone has been around IT for any decent length of time, working amongst very skilled peers, you realize you don't need, or even want,a label to tell you if you're good or not good.  By that time, you will likely have some idea whether or not you are, by reflecting on what you've learned, and done, and what you can do, but probably moreso, by the level of confidence amongst you and your peers. 

Self-proclaiming or associating yourself to be a hacker in a technical community is usually one of two things:

1) A sign of self-confidence in your knowledge amongst your social peers. (It take quite a level of self-confidence to associate yourself with a label placed on technical minds  the caliber of Stallman and Torvalds.  Not that there is anything wrong with doing so)
or
2) a cry for attention

There is nothing wrong with number 1, but too often number 2 is the case.  Unfortunately, the shear number of cases I've encountered number 2 have desensitized the label greatly in my mind. In any regard, I'm just saying my opinion on the matter and I am probably being overly cynical :).  Although I have not read your book, I'm sure theres nothing wrong at all with your definition.

### Post by original_jamingrit on 2008-04-07
I've always thought that the terms "white-hat" and "black-hat" got the point across nicely to the layman.

Although, a script-kiddie is still not a hacker.  A script kiddie is only as powerful as his googling skills.  They follow others blindly, sticking to a well-beaten path, without actually doing anything clever.  A script-kiddie may become a hacker, and a hacker may sometimes behave like a script-kiddie, but they are still not the same thing.

The best definition of hacker I've come across is this:  "A *hack* is any display of cleverness using an existing system.  A *hacker* creates *hacks*."
