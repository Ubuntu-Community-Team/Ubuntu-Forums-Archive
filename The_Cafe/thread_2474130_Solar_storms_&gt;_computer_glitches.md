### Post by TheFu on 2022-04-22
As with most of these things, there's little to be done in advance beyond what we do for a big regional storm ... or covid lockdowns. It isn't like we can do anything about it.

The Van Allen Belt protects the earth, but a few, very few, high energy particles can get through. The higher latitudes are more susceptible, since there magnetosphere is less strong there. If your locations sees Aurora Borealis, that's a good hint.  If you don't, I wouldn't worry.  Just to make up a number, anyone south of, perhaps 65 deg N latitude doesn't have much to worry about.

But ... these events do impact GPS and electrical power systems.  GPS signals are degraded and power systems have failed previously.  Quebec's power grid was knocked out in 1989.  I don't think any other electrical devices were impacted, since size matters. Larger power systems will be impacted much more.

And just think about all the airliners that fly over the far north daily using normal computers, ipads, etc. How often do we hear about those devices being destroy?

OTOH, I tend to be very calm in emergencies and plan ahead, so issues don't do more than a slight inconvenience.  Last month we didn't have internet for almost 3 days because a neighbor ran over the ISP's box and only a few people were impacted. Minor inconvenience. Not a big deal.

### Post by QIII on 2022-04-22
Although high energy cosmic ray particles are usually handled by our magnetic field and atmosphere,  cosmic ray hits do happen.  Typically, error correction designed in handles the lion's share of problems.

Computers in spacecraft, however, can be catastrophically damaged.  So a lot of redundancy is built in and dies are not made with ultra small production methods.  

Cosmic ray hits are a real thing even down here on the surface.  

However, it is not likely that a coronal mass ejection is the source of issues on the forums.  More likely software and infrastructure maintenance issues.  Power distribution can be affected because of the truly immense effects of electrical surges.
