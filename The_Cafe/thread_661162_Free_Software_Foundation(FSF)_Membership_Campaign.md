### Post by hayalci on 2008-01-07
Free Software Foundation is the organization behind GNU GPL licence 
and Free Software movement, which inspired and drove many great projects,
like Ubuntu. 

They also aim to protect the rights of free software developers
against any companies that like to use the successful software,
but reluctant about contributing back according to the licence.

Remember, free software is not about price, it is about **freedom**.
To learn basics of free software, read [this article]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw.html") at [GNU Philosophy Page]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/").

To learn more about FSF, and to join, go to [http://www.fsf.org/]("http://www.fsf.org/")

"Happy Hacking" ;)

### Post by frup on 2008-02-08
When you look at Free software in the long term it will be the one thing that does prevail. When you look at proprietary formats especially in relation to organisations like Government where long term digital archival is important you can begin to see how important open formats are. 

The reality is that GPL is a beautiful license and it doesn't stop you being able to link proprietary software in to your own computer in anyway you want, you just can't redistribute it like that. Isn't that freedom? What is wrong with that?

The comment posted earlier that more hot headed free software advocates would have tried to stop proprietary software even touching a GNU/Linux machine is pretty offensive in my opinion. Look at the GPL, look what it allows, that is the basis of this radical free software movement that is being accused of something that it isn't.

Sure RMS can't give Ubuntu for example a tick of approval, does that matter? Why should he? He isn't campaigning against it. He silently accepts it's existence fully aware that in time more and more of what is proprietary in the GNU/Linux world will have an open counter part. 

In someways I believe that allowing certain proprietary parts only gives us more reason to create an open counter part as it proves an area where GNU/Linux is in fact lagging behind. Others argue that it gives us less incentive to do so. The issue I guess is moot.

But really to criticise those who only want a more freedom for us all is pretty ignorant of what everything is all about. Nothing GNU would exist if it weren't for the GPL. With out the GPL most things that did get formed would probably be using BSD and so only companies like Sun and Apple would really benefit and Ubuntu would not exist. You could argue that with out Linux, GNU would not be a functional system too, but the fact is that Linux itself probably destroyed HURD. Once Linux was released under the GPL, there was no need for HURD. Personally I would love to see the HURD be completed. I like the idea of a monolithic and micro-kernel competing (although HURD has 17 years catchup to do!). I also like choice. It is important to think about what could have been if GNU did not exist though, Solaris would not be open as it would have no competitive incentive to do so. Apple would probably be using Internet Explorer as their default browser too.

If you take a step back and look at what FOSS has achieved and created, directly and indirectly, it is amazing. When you realise how vast it is how can you not see it as the future?
