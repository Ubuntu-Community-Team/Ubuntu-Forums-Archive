### Post by RAV TUX on 2007-06-06
> **[SIZE=5]   Censorship 'changes face of net'[/SIZE]** 
                                                                 ** Amnesty International has warned that the internet "could change beyond all recognition" unless action is taken against the erosion of online freedoms. **                                                             The warning comes ahead of a conference organised by Amnesty, where victims of  repression will outline their plights.                               
                               The "virus of internet repression" has spread from a handful of countries to dozens of governments, said the group.                               
                               Amnesty accused companies such as Google, Microsoft and Yahoo of being complicit in the problem.                                                    
                                                        **                              Website closures                              **                              
 When challenged on their presence in countries such as China in the past, the companies accused have always maintained that they were simply abiding by local laws. 
                               Amnesty is concerned that censorship is on the increase.                               
 "The Chinese model of an internet that allows economic growth but not free speech or privacy is growing in popularity, from a handful of countries five years ago to dozens of governments today who block sites and arrest bloggers," said Tim Hancock, Amnesty's campaign director. 
                               "Unless we act on this issue, the internet could change beyond all recognition in the years to come.                                
 More and more governments are realising the utility of controlling what people see online and major internet companies, in an attempt to expand their markets, are colluding in these attempts," he said. 
                          

                                                                               According to the latest Open Net Initiative report on internet filtering, at least 25 countries now apply state-mandated net filtering including Azerbaijan, Bahrain, Burma, Ethiopia, India, Iran, Morocco and Saudi Arabia. 
                              **                              Egyptian blogger                              **                              
 Filtering was only one aspect of internet repression, the group said. It added that increasingly it was seeing "politically motivated" closures of websites and net cafes, as well as threats and imprisonments. 
 Twenty-two-year-old Egyptian blogger Abdul Kareem Nabeel Suleiman was imprisoned for four years in February for insulting Islam and defaming the President of Egypt. 
 Fellow Egyptian blogger Amr Gharbeia told the BBC that the internet was allowing people to express themselves: "The web is creating a more open society, it is allowing more people to speak out. It's only natural that upsets some people." 
 The Amnesty conference - Some People Think the Internet is a Bad Thing: The Struggle for Freedom of Expression in Cyberspace - will have some well-known speakers including Wikipedia founder Jimmy Wales. 
 It marks the first anniversary of Amnesty's website irrepressible.info, which is being relaunched to become an information hub for anyone interested in the future of internet freedom.

### Post by Tundro Walker on 2007-06-06
Freedom is like the "boiling frog" analogy.  If you toss a frog into boiling water, it'll jump out.  But if you put him into normal water, and slowly bring it to a boil, he'll stay in and get boiled to death.  (Note, I'm not sure if that's Biologically true or not, but for the sake of this argument, let's assume it is.)

When you take a lot of freedom away from someone, they will fight back fervently.  However, if you erode it over time, perhaps even generations, then it will be imperceptible to the average person, and they will submit slowly but easily.

I'm torn on this issue, though.  I live in the United States, and sometimes I think we need to keep our nose outta other peoples business.  But, we can't deny that we are moving more towards a unified world, and it's difficult to see other countries still repress their citizens...citizens who could otherwise be connecting with others around the world and creating major achievements and innovations.

My personal opinion is that government and citizenry are like a marriage...they should support each other.  But we have these cases where the government acts like an abusive spouse, keeping the citizenry cowed and submissive.  I feel a government should have power to keep things organized, but shouldn't have power to censor its citizens.  If they're mad about something, they should have the freedom to express it.

That's why, as much as I hate them, I think KKK folks should still be allowed to protest and march in US cities if they want.   Like Voltaire said "I may not agree with what you have to say, but I will fight to my death to defend your right to say it."

### Post by Dr. C on 2007-12-09
> **sloggerkhan said:**
> I think there should be a movement for the Internet to secede from the domain of national governments and claim that computers and the internet are their own 'nation' with different rights, rules, and freedoms than those enforced by any other nation on Earth, and which has rights, rules, and freedoms which any users have a right to in any other country when using it.

This assumes that government censorship is the greatest threat to free speech on  the Internet, and while that may be true in China or Saudi Arabia there is another far more sinister threat from private industry in the United States, the European Union and many other developed countries. 

This comes from two sources. The MPAA, RIAA and IFPI who are pushing for all sorts of filtering (read censorship at the ISP level) in an attempt to prevent piracy using some of the very same techniques used in the "Great Firewall of China". This when combined with ever more draconian copyright laws  and DRM laden operating systems like Windows Vista can really chill free speech. 

ISPs who want to give priority to their "partners" the classic net neutrality debate.  With the advent of broadband Internet the number of ISP's serving an area can be as few as 1 or 2 (The phone and cable companies) creating great potential for abuse. In the days of dial up there were many ISPs serving an area so the market could take care of this, but this is no longer the case.

I see two ways to preserve free speech on the Internet.

1) Promote Free as in Speech software (for example GNU / Linux) particularly at the Operating System and Internet related application level. The growing presence of GPL v3 code in GNU / Linux will keep the DRM at bay.

2) Push for strict network neutrality laws where the providers of "content" must be not be able to influence in any way the provision of Internet access.

For those living in the United States, the European Union and countries like Canada, Australia, etc that wish to preserve free speech; starting with the threats to free speech at home make much more sense than worrying about the Chinese or the Saudis.
