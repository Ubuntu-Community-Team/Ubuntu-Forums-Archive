### Post by Skripka on 2009-03-14
Drift in language is something that has been fought for 100s and 1000s of years.  

This drift was only initially noticed in old Frankish chant, when Charlemagne (of whom you may have heard) ca 800 or so -hired church musicians from Rome to come up to his place to teach his church/monks the "right" way of doing chant....well 20 years later the romans came up again-and found that the Franks were doing things entirely wrong from what they were doing....the Franks were so embarassed that they set down to lock down on the drift and codified things....the amusing fact being that by ca1200, Frankish (by then "Gregorian" chant) was more kin to the Roman chant at 800AD, than the Roman chant was at 1200AD compared to 800AD ;)

The catch being that for this drift to happen-it requires populations to be separated geographically and linguistically.  We can still find people today who speak "proper" English a la Shakespeare as a vernacular-and NOT in Great Britain-but in the US-in the isolated Appalachian regions actually, as they have been so separated culturally, geographically, and linguistically that they were never touched by the movements in vernacular elsewhere.

It is unlikely that "American" would become it's own language as such--especially with anal grammar teachers and libraries on the subject.  All these stabilize drift, and lock it down-much as Charlemagne and the Franks did.

Heck, American style English has become the modern Latin of our day, and most countries have their schools teach their national tongue as well as English.

It's kind of debatable. Technically it is classified as a language. Historically it is descended mainly from middle english with french, norse and gaelic influences, was spoken in lowland Scotland, and used to be quite distinct from english. 

Nowadays, with modern transport and communications the language spoken in scotland is on the whole very similar to english, although with many words and phrases that wouldn't necessarily be understood by all english speakers. There are quite a lot of people who would not be easily comprehensible to an english speaker when talking broad scots or ulster scots (spoken in parts of northern ireland) but they can usually change their tone to a more "standard" type of language.

Make sure you don't confuse Scots with Gaelic, which was the historical language of highland scotland and has completely different roots. There's probably less than 100,000 people able to speak this today though.
