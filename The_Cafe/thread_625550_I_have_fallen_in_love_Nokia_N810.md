### Post by ssam on 2007-11-28
the N810 is not a phone. like its predecesors the N800 and 770 they are 'internet tablets'

they have wifi and bluetooth for connectivity. so you can get on the internet in most places (if you have a phone with bluetooth).

they run a derivitive of debain, with an interface called maemo. (actually it gets very confusing what precisily terms like meamo and hildon mean). porting a GTK application to meamo is not too much work (and should be less in the latest OS verion). there are ports of big apps like abiword and gnumeric.

have a look at maemo.org for list of some of the applications.

i use mine for writing lecture notes (using xournal), reading the web or pdfs, listening to music, offline wikipedia (sdictviewer), remote control for rhythmbox ([rhythmweb]("http://web.vee.net/projects/rhythmweb/")).

have a look at the N800 aswell. it does not have a keyboard or GPS, but it does have 2 SDHC slots and FM radio and is cheaper.

### Post by maniacmusician on 2007-11-28
The N810 is great if you're a bit of a geek and plan on doing geeky stuff with it, but overall, at this point the Eee-PC is a better value for the amount of things you get. The Eee-PC is also linux based, and I believe it comes with a terminal and everything you would need to get your geek on, and it's actually cheaper than the N810. 

In my opinion, it also offers a better experience, since it's a full blown laptop with USB/SD card slots, and it's very light. I guess the N810 is more polished, and has a touch screen; probably why it's more expensive. edit: I rescind my statements. I was just rationalizing. The N810 can do pretty much anything the Eee PC can. The Eee PC has a faster processor and more (and upgradeable) RAM, but it also runs a full KDE desktop. In terms of hardware, the Eee PC is ahead, but it all depends on how well they can utilize that hardware, and if they can build an open source community around it like the N810 has.

### Post by Rallg on 2009-05-04
I got the N810 a few days ago. I use the Dell mini 9 with Ubuntu (replaced XP) as my usual laptop, since it fits in my bicycle bag. But this summer I plan to make an extended bike journey, so I wanted something handheld. I did not need a phone.

Originally I had thought of the iPod Touch, which is very common where I am. Nice product, but it seemed to be very good at doing things that I never needed to do (such as play games), and inadequate at doing some things that I might want to do (browser with Flash, ability to type a letter and upload it for printing). So I looked for alternatives.

I did not need GPS, but the N810 has it. I expected it would be hard to get a fix, and that seemed to be the case for the first two days. But I was indoors in a large building. The location fixed quickly once I loved to an open area. Keep in mind that since I had no prior interest in GPS, I did not think of the obvious solution, and of course I did not read instructions. I find that Maemo Mapper and QT Mapper are more useful than the built-in map system, although it is necessary to be online to get the maps. I do not intend to subscribe to the available travel route planner.

I was happy to see that Abi Word was available. It is in an experimental repository (for this product) so you will have to find it by Internet search. I doubt that I will take the trouble to put a large Debian distro on my removeable media card, but it would be nice if I there were a way to make a PDF from Abi Word, without installing 100Mb of CUPS. Still, if I have to, I can write an Abi Word document, upload it via FTP (zip it, if necessary), then read it elsewhere using the protable version of Abi Wrod on a USV drive. The N810 has a USB mini-port, but I haven't tried it yet.

I like the slide-out keyboard. Now all I need is a couple of thimbles for my two index fingers, so I can peck the keys without using the stylus. Understand that I am not young, so I do not have the thumb agility of younger people, who grew up with handheld games.

I purchased a metal (mostly) case specifically designed to fit this product. The case allows the device to be used as an audio-video player, but of course blocks access to input.

### Post by Vostrocity on 2009-06-04
The N810 is seriously a dream device, especially for Linux fans. And it's dropped to just $200 since Nokia is clearing up stock for the next-gen which is coming in a few months. It's got a VGA screen, camera, GPS, built-in memory plus mSD, WiFi, BT, Qwerty. On the software side, the default Maemo OS is completely open-source with a good number apps. It can also run desktop Ubuntu as well as Android with Cupcake (in fact it was the first device to ever run Cupcake, that was back in January).
