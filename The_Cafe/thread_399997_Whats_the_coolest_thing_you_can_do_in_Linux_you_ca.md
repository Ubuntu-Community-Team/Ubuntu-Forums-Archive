I've never used Mac OS X, so I'm comparing Ubuntu to Windows. My list:

* When I login as a normal user (without root privileges), I can't mess up the system. When I run a new piece of software, I don't have to worry that it changes system configuration files or writes DLLs or configuration files in a system directory somewhere.

* Great, easy to use package management and automatic updating of not only the OS itself but of all software installed through the package management system.

I hate being controlled by a commercial company like that!

* Installing Ubuntu is easier than installing Windows. I recently re-installed Windows Vista and Ubuntu 7.10 on my new laptop. After installing Vista, most hardware doesn't work immediately: I had to manually install drivers for my chipset, wireless network card, bluetooth, video, webcam, built-in card reader etc. On Ubuntu, everything worked out-of-the-box; the only thing I had to do was enable the restricted nVidia driver, which was just a few mouse clicks.

### Post by Forrest Gumpp on 2008-02-11
I have never owned or used a Mac, so cannot comment in respect of this system.

I do own a second-hand Dell Optiplex G X 240 desktop computer, which I purchased on eBay for <$150 AUD, with a (genuine) OEM version of Windows 2000 Pro installed on its 20 GB HDD.  Its specifications are:  P4  1.7 GHz Intel  768 MB RAM  integrated graphics and sound  FDD  CD ROM.  A fairly robust old commercial unit.

After modifying it such that two externally mounted (and powered) drive caddies now sit on its lid, I now have two easily removable 3.5" PATA (IDE) HDDs connected by cable-select ribbon cable to the primary IDE channel connector on the mobo.  (The additional HDD is of 80 GB capacity, purchased separately.)  I can very quickly remove or insert a HDD on this computer.  Caddies cost <$15 AUD each, and are designed to fit in a 5.25" drive bay.  They can also be obtained in SATA versions.

I have installed Ubuntu 7.10 on one of the HDDs for this computer.

A cool feature of Ubuntu Linux is that I can then transfer that HDD to my son's desktop computer (P4 3.06GHz 1.5 GB RAM  Gigabyte MB FDD DVD/RW) of different manufacture (also fitted with caddies mounted in two 5.25" drive bays) and my Ubuntu will run perfectly in every respect just as it did in the Dell upon which it was first installed!  Try doing anything like that with any Windows installation!  No driver installation issues, just swap caddies, and away you go.  'Sneakernet' on steroids!

I believe I could also do the installation in reverse, installing Ubuntu using his desktop computer and then tranferring the HDD to mine, if I wanted, but I must admit I haven't actually attempted it in this direction yet.  The point of this is that should there occur a hardware failure in either computer, it is possible to stay in operation with your own Ubuntu installation by simply crossing the room and swapping caddies.  Fault finding and repair can then proceed at leisure.

But wait, there's more!

Should it be that my son was to install Ubuntu on his desktop computer (which he has), and then run virtualisation software (freely available), and install his XP thereunder, an interesting situation would come about.  Due to the evident transferability of an Ubuntu Linux installation from one computer to another, it would seem that his HDD with the virtualised file image of XP on it could be transferred to my desktop computer and then be routinely used.  XP would not know that it now resided in an entirely different hardware environment!

The bonds that tie an OEM version of XP to the particular hardware configuration with which the license was sold would be broken.  And provided a backup copy of the virtualised XP installation file was always kept, any re-installation of XP on the computer to which the HDD is transferred could circumvent the hardware change recognition triggers built into the software that would otherwise disable the XP OS and require its re-activation!

In like manner, if with somewhat less spectacular commercial implications, the full functionality of older Windows installations once installed under virtualisation can be preserved into the future long after the original hardware upon which they came pre-installed has become obsolete or worn out.

Now it may well be that running XP in a virtual environment may be contrary to the terms and conditions of any EULA, but enforcement of a EULA with respect to a home use situation is entirely impractical.  Should such enforcement nevertheless be attempted it will be largely commercially self-defeating:  it is not a good business model to rely upon suing or threatening those whom you want to be your repeat customers.  The only other 'enforcement' pathway would appear to be degradation of the ease of use and privacy status of users who desire, or are trapped into, continued use of XP or any successor to it.

Linux, it seems, has inadvertently become an enabling technology for effectively defeating Windows Activation.

In this household we never violated the EULA for XP, but we have suffered the degradation of its ease of use.  We were FORCED to migrate to Linux.  I am now so glad that happened.  Linux is the last learning curve that will have to be climbed, and I can learn on the net without spending a dollar for applications or worrying about viruses, or the performance hits of anti-virus software.  Largely thanks to communities of users like those found on the Ubuntu Forums!

This is a good question, and it leads to an important point about Mac OS X. Mac's under-the-hood UNIX has these things, AFAIK most of the tools are BSD-based, although Bash is the default shell.

I'm not knocking BSD systems, because the way they are packaged they are truly engineered by competant developers. I have used BSD and Linux, and lived between the two worlds: the gui and the command line. Mac OS seems to have a little of BSD and GNU tools, but where is the integration? I know if I make a backup with dump/restore or GNU tar things will be "Iry." I don't like the idea of clicking a gui button and hoping the developers took some time off of all the hallucinogenic interface programming to make sure data is copied with integrity.

I used to fiddle with BSD until I realised I was going through hoops to GNU-ify it--GNU sed to run a script that required it, a Bash shell, Emacs... but when I realized I like the GNU stuff I thought why swim upstream and I stuck with Linux.

So.. Windows? Not even close.

Mac OS X? A good shot, but too much effort on the wrong things. Linux or BSD is the way to go.

### Post by tobiasly on 2008-05-29
I can tell my computer to reboot (on those rare occasions when it's necessary in Ubuntu), then walk away to get a cup of coffee with the comfort of knowing that when I return, my computer will have rebooted and be ready to go.

On Windows, it's more of a "reboot request". You tell it to reboot, then click through all of the separate apps you have open that ask "are you sure?". Then you sit and wait to make sure that there isn't some background process that refuses to shut down, prompting more dialog boxes. After all that babysitting, if no other app has vetoed your "reboot request", then your computer actually reboots.

In the same way, if a process hangs in Ubuntu, I can kill it instantly. Boom, it's gone, not a single additional CPU cycle wasted on it. In Windows, you issue a "terminate request" from Task Manager, and maybe it kills it a second later, or maybe 30 seconds later, or it just silently ignores your request. Or maybe it says you don't have permission to kill it, even if you're logged in as Admin.

Same way with deleting files, renaming directories, etc. No app has exclusive control of a file while it's open (it's just another filesystem pointer), so I can delete a file without some app saying sorry, you can't do that, or rename a folder without the app saying sorry, I read a file from that folder 5 minutes ago and you can't rename it until you close me. I can also tell Ubuntu to delete an entire directory tree without clicking through a dozen confirmation boxes for each system file, or hidden file, or hidden system file, or read only file, or executable file.

In short, I'm in control of my box and my OS is there to serve me. With Windows, it's not your computer; it's Windows' computer and if you're lucky it will grant you permission to use it, as long as you use it the way it wants you to.
