### Post by linux18 on 2010-09-05
a "little" history courtesy of wikipedia:





**Quake engine, 1997&#8211;1998**

 *Duke Nukem Forever* was officially announced on April 28, 1997,[[14]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-wernicke-1-13") with the intention of releasing the game no later than mid-1998.[[15]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-14") Barely a year after the release of *Duke Nukem 3D*, the game's graphics and its game engine, Build, were antiquated. Id Software's new [Quake II engine]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Id_Tech_2") was far superior to Build, so Broussard decided to license it.[[14]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-wernicke-1-13")[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15")  The price spent for the licensing rights was steep&#8212;estimates were as  high as $500,000&#8212;but Broussard reasoned that it would save time used to  write a game engine from scratch.[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15") Broussard and Miller were flush with cash from sale of *Duke Nukem 3D* and other games, so they decided to fund *Duke Nukem Forever* themselves, turning marketing and publishing rights over to [GT Interactive]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GT_Interactive").[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16")
 In August and September, the first screenshots of *Duke Nukem Forever* were released in *[PC Gamer]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PC_Gamer")*.  However, 3D Realms did not receive the Quake II engine code until  November 1997, and the earlier screenshots were mock-ups with the Quake  engine that the team had made in their spare time.[[18]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-reed-17") 3D Realms unveiled the first video footage of *Duke Nukem Forever* using the Quake II engine at the 1998 [Electronic Entertainment Expo]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo") (E3) conference.[[18]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-reed-17")  The trailer showed Duke fighting on the back of a moving truck and  firefights with aliens. While critics were impressed, Broussard was not  happy with the progress being made.[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15")
 **[[edit]("http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duke_Nukem_Forever&action=edit&section=4")] Unreal engine, 1998&#8211;2002**

  [[IMG]http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b2/Duke_Nukem_Screenshot2.jpg/220px-Duke_Nukem_Screenshot2.jpg[/IMG]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Duke_Nukem_Screenshot2.jpg")  [[IMG]http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png[/IMG]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Duke_Nukem_Screenshot2.jpg")
 Screenshot of *Duke Nukem Forever* from 1999.
 
 
 Soon after the release of the Quake II engine, [Epic Games]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic_Games") had unveiled its own [Unreal Engine]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreal_Engine").  The Unreal Engine was more realistic than Quake II and was better  suited to producing open spaces&#8212;3D Realms had been struggling to render  the Nevada desert. Soon after E3, a programmer suggested that they make  the switch. After discussions, the developers unanimously agreed to the  change, which would mean scrapping much of their work so far,[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15") including significant changes 3D Realms had made to the Quake Engine.[[19]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-wernicke-2-18")  In June 1998, 14 months after the Quake II announcement, 3D Realms made  the switch announcement. Broussard said that the game would not be  "significantly delayed" by the switch, but that the project would be  back to where it was at E3 "within a month to six weeks". Broussard also  said that no content seen in the E3 trailer would be lost.[[20]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-3drealms-engine_switch-19")  Chris Hargrove, one of the game's programmers at the time, confided  that the change amounted to a complete reboot of the project.[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15")
 By the end of 1999, *Duke Nukem Forever* had missed several release dates and was largely unfinished; half the game's weapons remained concepts.[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15") Broussard shot back at criticisms of the game's lengthy development time as the price paid for developing complex modern games:[INDENT] Today's games are MUCH more complex. In *Duke Nukem 3D* all the characters were sprites. Today they are polygonal models, have to be skinned, and then animated or motion captured. A *very*  long, tedious and complex process. Also games today are "deeper" and  require more than key/door stuff. That means more time to develop. Note  all the so-so [first-person shooter] games that didn't sell well in the  last two years and you will see that they were simple compared to more  complex efforts like *[Half-Life]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-Life_%28video_game%29")*. A game like *Duke Nukem Forever* is probably 5-10 times more complex than *Duke Nukem 3D* was.[[21]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-ign-duke1999-20")
 [/INDENT]A significant factor contributing to the game's protracted  development was that Broussard was continually looking to add new  elements to the game. A running joke at 3D Realms was to stop Broussard  from seeing a new video game, as he would want to include portions of it  in *Duke Nukem Forever*. Later that year Broussard decided to upgrade to a new version of the Unreal engine that was designed for [multiplayer]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplayer") matches. Former employees recalled that Broussard did not have a plan for what the finished game would look like.[[16]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-2-15") At the same time, GT Interactive was facing higher-than-expected losses and hired [Bear Stearns]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bear_Stearns") to look into selling the company or merging it.[[22]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-cnet-gt_interactive-21") Later that year [Infogrames Entertainment]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infogrames_Entertainment,_SA") announced it was purchasing a controlling interest in GT Interactive.[[23]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-22") The publishing rights for *Duke Nukem Forever* passed to [Gathering of Developers]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gathering_of_Developers") in early December 2000.[[24]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-23")
 To placate anxious fans, Broussard decided to create another trailer  for E3 2001&#8212;it was the first public look at the game in three years.[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16") The video showed a couple of minutes of in-game footage,[[25]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-ign-e32001-24")  which notably showed the player moving in a what appears to be Las  Vegas and a certain level of interactivity (the player buys a sandwich  from a vending machine and pushes each individual button on a [keypad]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keypad") with Duke's outstretched finger). The trailer was impressive, and *Duke Nukem* was the talk of the convention;[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16")  IGN reported on the games graphics that, "Characters come to life with  picturesque facial animations that are synced perfectly with speech,  hair that swings as they bob their heads, eyes that follow gazes, and  more. The particle effects system, meanwhile, boasts impressive  explosion effects with shimmering fire, shattered glass, and blood spilt  in every direction [...] Add in real-time lighting effects, interactive  environments, and a variation in locales unequaled in any other  first-person shooter and you begin to see and understand why Duke Nukem  Forever has been one of the most hotly anticipated titles over the last  couple of years."[[25]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-ign-e32001-24") *Duke Nukem Forever*  looked as good or better than most games, and staff at 3D Realms  recalled a sense of elation after the presentation; "The video was just  being eaten up by people," one said. "We were so far ahead of other  people at the time." While many of the staff expected Broussard to make a  push for finishing the game, however, he still did not have a finished  product in mind.[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16")  Following the death of one of Gathering of Developers' co-founders and  continuing financial problems, the publishers' Texas-based offices were  shut down and absorbed into parent company [Take-Two Interactive]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Take-Two_Interactive").[[26]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thorsen2003-25")
 **[[edit]("http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duke_Nukem_Forever&action=edit&section=5")] Conflict with Take-Two**

 By 2003, only 18 people at 3D Realms were working on the game. One  former employee said that Broussard and Miller were still operating on a  "1995 mentality", before games became large-team, big budget  development affairs. Because they were financing the project themselves,  the developers could also ignore pressure from their publisher;[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16") their standard reply to when *Duke Nukem Forever* would ship was "when it's done".[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16")[[27]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-morris2003-26") In 2003, Take-Two CEO Jeffrey Lapin reported that the game would not be out that year.[[28]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-27") He further said the company was writing off $5.5 million from its earnings due to *Duke Nukem Forever*'s lengthy development time.[[27]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-morris2003-26") Broussard shot back that "Take-Two needs to [STFU]("http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/STFU")  ... We don&#8217;t want Take-Two saying stupid-*** things in public for the  sole purposes of helping their stock. It's our time and our money we are  spending on the game. So either we're absolutely stupid and clueless,  or we believe in what we are working on."[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16")[[27]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-morris2003-26") Later that year, Lapin said 3D Realms had told him that *Duke Nukem Forever* was expected to be finished by the end of 2004, or the beginning of 2005.[[26]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thorsen2003-25")
 In 2004, video game website [GameSpot]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GameSpot") reported that *Duke Nukem Forever* had switched to the *[Doom 3]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doom_3")* engine.[[29]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thorsen2004-28")  Many gaming news sites mailed Broussard, asking him to confirm or deny  the rumor. After receiving no answer from him, they published the rumor  as fact, but Broussard explicitly denied the rumor soon after.[[29]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thorsen2004-28") Soon after 3D Realms replaced the game's Karma physics system with one designed by [Meqon]("http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Meqon&action=edit&redlink=1"), a relatively unknown Swedish firm. Closed-doors demonstrations of the technology suggested that the physics of *Duke Nukem Forever* would be a step up from the critically-acclaimed *[Half-Life 2]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-Life_2")*.[[30]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-29") Rumors suggested that the game would appear at 2005 [E3]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E3_%28Electronic_Entertainment_Expo%29"). While 3D Realms' previously-canceled *[Prey]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prey_%28video_game%29")* made an appearance, the rumors of *Duke Nukem Forever*'s appearance proved false.[[31]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-30")
 Broussard reported in a January 2006 interview that many of *Duke Nukem Forever*'s elements had been finished; "we're just basically pulling it all together and trying to make it fun".[[32]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-31") Later that year Broussard demonstrated samples of the game, including an early level, a vehicle sequence, and a few test rooms.[[33]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-carless2006-32") Among the features seen was the interactive use of an in-game computer to send actual e-mails.[[34]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-33")  The developer seemed contrite and affected by the long delays; while a  journalist demoed the game Broussard referenced note cards and  constantly apologized for the state of the game.[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16") In filing with the [United States Securities and Exchange Commission]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Securities_and_Exchange_Commission"), Take-Two revealed they had renegotiated the *Duke Nukem Forever*  deal, with the developer receiving $4.25 million instead of $6 million  on release of the game. Take-Two offered a $500,000 bonus if *Duke Nukem Forever* was commercially released by December 31, 2006.[[33]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-carless2006-32") However, Broussard denied the rumors that *DNF*  would be released, saying that 3D Realms never cared for or asked for  the bonus. He stated that he would "never ship a game early."[[35]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-34")
 Some of the staff were tired of the delays&#8212;*Duke Nukem Forever*  was the only 3D game many had worked on, giving them little to put on a  resume, and as much of 3D Realms' payment hinged on profit-sharing after  release, the continual delays meant deferred income.[[17]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-3-16") By August 2006, between 7&#8211;10 employees had left since 2005, a majority of the *Duke Nukem Forever* team (which in recent months had shrunk to around 18 staff).[[36]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-4-35")[[37]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-36") While [Shacknews]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shacknews")  speculated that the departures would lead to further delays, 3D Realms  denied the claims, stating that the employees had left over a number of  months and that the game was still moving ahead.[[38]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-37")  Creative director Raphael van Lierop, hired in 2007, played through the  completed content and realized that there was more finished than he  expected. Lierop told Broussard that he felt they could push the game  and "blow everyone out of the water", but Broussard responded that the  game was still two years away from completion.[[36]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-4-35")
 **[[edit]("http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duke_Nukem_Forever&action=edit&section=6")] 2007&#8211;2009**

  [[IMG]http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e0/Dukenukemforever2007.jpg/220px-Dukenukemforever2007.jpg[/IMG]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dukenukemforever2007.jpg")  [[IMG]http://bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.5/common/images/magnify-clip.png[/IMG]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dukenukemforever2007.jpg")
 *Duke Nukem Forever* 2007 teaser screenshot
 
 
 The long delay strained Broussard and Miller's relationship, and by  the end of 2006 Broussard appeared to become serious about shipping the  title.[[36]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-4-35") On January 25 and May 22, 2007, Broussard posted two [Gamasutra]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamasutra") job ads with small screenshots of Duke Nukem and an enemy, which he later confirmed were real in-game screenshots.[[39]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-38")[[40]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-39")  The team doubled in size within a short timeframe. Among the new hires  was project lead Brian Hook, who became the first person to successfully  resist Broussard's requests for changes.[[36]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-4-35")
 A new game trailer was released on December 19, 2007, the first  teaser in more than six years. The video was made by 3D Realms employees  as part of holiday festivities. While Broussard maintained the release  date would be "when it's done", he added that "you can expect more  frequent media releases [and] we have considerable work behind us".[[41]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-40") While the *Dallas Business Journal*  "confirmed" a 2008 release date for the game, Broussard later reported  that this was based on misunderstanding "off the record" information.[[42]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-41") In-game footage of the game appeared in 2008 premiere episode of *The Jace Hall Show*. Filmed entirely on hand-held cameras but not originally expected to be publicly released,[[43]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-42") the video showed host [Jason Hall]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jason_Hall_%28producer%29")  playing through parts of a single level on a PC at 3D Realms' offices.  The footage was confirmed to have been shot 6 months prior to the  episode air date and according to Broussard, contained outdated particle  and combat effects that had since been replaced.[[44]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-43")[[45]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-44")[[46]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-45") The game did not make an appearance at E3 2008, an event which Miller described as "irrelevant".[[47]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-46")
 While the game neared completion, the funding began to dry up. Having  spent more than $20 million of their own money, Broussard and Miller  asked Take-Two for $6 million to complete the game. According to  Broussard and Miller, Take-Two initially agreed, but then only offered  $2.5 million. Take-Two maintained that they offered $2.5 million up  front and another $2.5 million on completion. Broussard rejected the  counteroffer, and on May 6, 2009, suspended all development.[[36]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-thompson-4-35")
 **[[edit]("http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duke_Nukem_Forever&action=edit&section=7")] DNF team fired and 3D Realms downsized: 2009&#8211;2010**

 3D Realms laid off the *DNF* staff on May 8, 2009 due to lack of funding, but inside sources claimed it would still operate as a smaller company.[[48]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-official_release-47")  Development on DNF halted, and its fate was unknown. Publisher Take-Two  Interactive, in response, stated that they still held the publishing  rights for *Duke Nukem Forever*, but they were not funding the game.[[49]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-48")
 On May 7 and May 8, 2009, unreleased screenshots, concept art,  pictures of models from the game and a goodbye message from 3D Realms  were posted by alleged former employees. Similar leaks occurred on May  9, 10, 11, and 12.[[50]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-misc_link_1-49")[[51]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-misc_link_2-50")  On May 9, 2009, an unofficial Duke Nukem Forever gameplay video was  leaked by a user of the Duke4.net forums. According to the user, the  video was to serve as a demo reel for animator Bryan Brewer (who had  been working on the game with 3D Realms), and Brewer had been waiting  for approval from George Broussard, former co-owner of 3D Realms, at the  time of the leak.[[52]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-51")  On May 10, 2009 a second demo reel showing some Duke Nukem Forever  animation was also released. The same user of the Duke4.net forums later  proceeded to release 28 screenshots and documents outlining the plot of  the game. The 28 screenshots were taken from a employees portfolio that  was on Linkedin.
 On May 14, 2009, Take-Two filed a lawsuit against 3D Realms over their failure to complete *Duke Nukem Forever*, citing that they paid $12 million to [Infogrames]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infogrames") in 2000 to acquire the publishing rights.[[53]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-amon-bloomberg-52") 3D Realms argued, however, that they never received that money, as it was a direct agreement between Infogrames and Take-Two.[[54]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-53") The lawsuit seemed to be over a contractual breach, but not regarding the $12 million mentioned above.[[55]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-lawsuit-54") Take-Two asked for a restraining order and a preliminary injunction, to make 3D Realms keep the *Duke Nukem Forever* assets intact during proceedings.[[56]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-lawsuit2-55") The court denied Take-Two's request for a temporary restraining order.[[57]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-56")[[58]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-57")
 In June 2009, Apogee court filings stated "[3D Realms/Apogee Ltd.]  admits that it has continually worked on the development of the DNF for  many years, and continues to do so" as well as revealing that  development halted on 'Duke Begins'.[[59]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-58")  In December 2009, Apogee CEO Scott Miller clarified that "we've never  said that Duke Nukem Forever has ceased development," explaining "yes,  we released the internal team, but that doesn't correlate to the demise  of the project."[[60]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-59")  According to a recent interview with magazine Gamesauce, "3D Realms has  laid off the game's internal development team, but still plans to most  likely work with external development studios to develop the game."[[61]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-60") An unofficial compilation of gameplay footage was also released in December 2009.[[62]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-61")
 On June 11, 2010, it was announced that [3D Realms]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_Realms") and [Take-Two]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Take-Two") had settled the lawsuit and dismissed it with [prejudice]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prejudice_%28legal_procedure%29") on May 14.[[63]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-lawsuitSettled-62")[[64]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-Settled-63") According to the [United States Patent and Trademark Office]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office"), 3D Realms remains the owner of the *Duke Nukem* [intellectual property]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_property").
 **[[edit]("http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duke_Nukem_Forever&action=edit&section=8")] Revival**

 At [Penny Arcade Expo]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penny_Arcade_Expo") 2010 on September 3, 2010, it was announced that development of the game is being continued by [Gearbox Software]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gearbox_Software") and is going to be released by Take Two Interactive sometime in 2011, one of the developers confirmed in an online interview.[[65]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-64")  Playable demo booths were held on the first day of the convention. The  demo was reported to be a build from 3D Realms and Triptych Games before  involvement from Gearbox Software and Piranha Games.
 Gearbox president [Randy Pitchford]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randy_Pitchford") confirmed his studio was finishing the game since 2009 and that they now own the Duke Nukem IP.[[66]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-65") Furthermore Take Two subsidiary [2K Games]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2K_Games") announced that they have secured the exclusive long-term publishing rights to the game.[[67]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duke_Nukem_Forever#cite_note-66")  Duke Nukem co-creator Allen Blum and his studio Triptych Games are  working with Gearbox, as well as independent developer Piranha Games.
 Gearbox creative chief officer Brian Martel said the booth was simply  set up without making an announcement, making it a surprise for  attendees.
