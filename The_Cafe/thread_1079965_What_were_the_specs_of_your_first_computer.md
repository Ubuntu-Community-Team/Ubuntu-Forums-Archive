### Post by MikeTheC on 2009-02-25
Macintosh Plus
System 3.2 / Finder 5.3
8 MHz 68000 (actual clock speed: 7.8336 MHz)
1 MB RAM
Integrated 512x384 black-and-white (1 bit) 72dpi display
Unknown integrated video RAM (probably 64-128KB)
1 Internal 800KB Floppy Drive (Sony powered-ejection unit)
Provision for 1 External Floppy Drive (DB-15 dedicated port)
No Internal HDD, no provision for one
On-Board SCSI interface (DB-25, external device support only)
2 Serial ports (Mini-DIN 8 pin, RS-422 compliant)

The Mac Plus shared a nearly-identical case design with the original (a.k.a. "128K") and subsequent 512K (a.k.a. "Fat Mac") Macintoshes. The differences were the nature of the Apple logo on the front of the case and the ports on the back of the computer. The 128 and 512 were identical in this respect, having a pair of DB-9 serial ports, a DB-15 floppy drive port, a dedicated DB-9 mouse port, and a front-mounted RJ-11 keyboard port. The Mac Plus traded the DB-9 serial ports for a pair of Mini-DIN 8 pin ports (which took up far less space), and gave room to add the DB-25 SCSI port. These were also the only three Macs to have externally-accessible CMOS batteries. Starting with the Mac SE and moving forward, the batteries were mounted directly to the motherboard.

The rear of the 128, 512, Plus and SE cases had each design team's signatures embossed into the interior of the rear panel of the case.

The Mac Plus, like the 128 and 512 before it, had no active cooling (i.e. "fan") and relied exclusively on convection. Most Mac Pluses failed eventually due to heat damage; it's more uncommon to see a Mac Plus power board *not* serviced. Eventually, Kensington put out the System Saver Mac, which featured forward-mounted system and accessory power buttons, and a fan which would draw air up through the vent holes in the integrated handle/grip point on the top of the case.
