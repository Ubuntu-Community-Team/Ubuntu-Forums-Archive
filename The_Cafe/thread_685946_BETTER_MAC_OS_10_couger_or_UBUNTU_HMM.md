### Post by pytheas22 on 2008-02-02
I dislike Macs hugely and think that in many respects Apple is worse than Microsoft when it comes to stifling innovation and putting down open-source software.  The reason that Macs work more reliably than PC's, I think, is due largely to Apple's absolute control over both the hardware and much of the software on its machines: it's not hard to design a good operating system when the range of hardware that it's going to run on is very narrow and you are able to control it completely.  Even Windows would probably work well if Microsoft manufactured the PC's that it ran on.

This Apple monopoly not only leads to exorbitant prices, but makes Mac users to a large extent dependent on Apple's wishes to decide what they can do with their computers.  On Ubuntu, since we have access to the source, we can do whatever we feel like; on a Mac, you can only do what Apple's restrictive API's will allow.

I also don't like that Apple tries to market itself to people who want to express individuality or cultivation by owning a Mac.  Buying an overpriced computer from a company that wants to stifle your freedom even more than Microsoft doesn't make you cultured.

### Post by pytheas22 on 2008-02-03
> The only thing i can see why would be security, but it's possible to keep a secure windows box.

Another issue to consider in security is whether you know what's actually going on in the background, instead of just being handed a binary blob and promised that it's secure.  Running OS X, you're trusting Steve Jobs not to be spying on you through backdoors and things.  True, it's extremely unlikely that he's doing that and there might be ways to tell if he were, but why deal with that concern when you could get Ubuntu for free and be assured that there are no backdoors or other nasty things in the code? Not to mention that when general security holes are found in Linux, they get well publicized and corrected quickly, whereas with OS X, as with Microsoft, you have to wait for those companies to disclose the hole and offer a fix, and hope that no fifteen-year-old in Belarus finds the hole and writes a script to exploit it before it's patched.

### Post by aysiu on 2008-02-03
> **pytheas22 said:**
> Another issue to consider in security is whether you know what's actually going on in the background, instead of just being handed a binary blob and promised that it's secure.  Running OS X, you're trusting Steve Jobs not to be spying on you through backdoors and things.  True, it's extremely unlikely that he's doing that and there might be ways to tell if he were, but why deal with that concern when you could get Ubuntu for free and be assured that there are no backdoors or other nasty things in the code? Not that I think Ubuntu has nasty backdoor things, but just to play devil's advocate for a moment here:

Many Free software advocates who are also programmers like to say that people can be confident that open source software doesn't have nasty backdoor stuff because the code is freely available, but the code being freely available means nothing to non-programmers. If I examined the code, I wouldn't be able to tell you what it was doing to my system or reporting/not reporting back to some company.

It's tantamount to handing a non-Star Trek fan a legal document in large type entirely printed in Klingon, "Here. All the terms and conditions are clearly laid out here. Nothing is hidden if you know Klingon. Just sign right here." Uh, I don't know Klingon. (Or, in the operating system context, I don't know any programming languages.) As a matter of fact, I don't even have to use Klingon as an example--most legal documents appear to be written in a human language, but they are incomprehensive to laypeople and usually written in "legalese" to obfuscate the real meaning. Most people don't have the time/energy to read through completely to the point of total comprehension all legal documents they sign.

Likewise, most people don't have the time and energy to learn all common computer programming languages in order to examine source code to determine the intentions of the program's author. Most end users put their trust in a person or organization, not code. I am not a programmer, and I have never looked at Ubuntu's source code, but I trust Mark Shuttleworth more than I trust Steve Jobs when it comes to privacy.
