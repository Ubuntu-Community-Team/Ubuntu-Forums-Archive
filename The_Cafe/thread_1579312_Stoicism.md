### Post by NMFTM on 2010-09-21
I've been studying and attempting to put into practice the teachings of stoicism for about the last 4 or 5 months now and so far it seems like almost everything they said or advocated is correct. Recently I've been reading a website that gives various quotations from Marcus Aurelius's Books of the Meditations and gives an explanation of what he probably meant by them in modern English.

The only shaky area is stoic physics. Which claims that fire, water, air, and dirt are the only elements that exist. But that was only a major concern for the original Greek stoics. The later Roman stoics seemed to focus almost entirely on the psychological aspects of stoicism. So, I don't necessarily see that as being a problem of stoicism as much as it was people's attempts to understand the world in an age before "modern" science. Every philosophy has to evolve to suit the times. Just like the founder of stoicism (Zeno of Citium) took many of the ideas of cynicism and altered them to form what he considered to be a more logical philosophy.

I was just curious if anyone else is interested in or is a practicing stoic? Also I got bored with the usual discussions that go on in this area of the forum and figured that something new and fresh was in order. I don't think anything I've posted is in violation of the ban of religious debates. Since stoicism isn't a religion unless you count the idea of logos. Which is really more of a way of thinking of the laws of nature than a theological statement.

> **agnes said:**
> However I am not sure about a physical universal rigid determinism myself and think that living by the laws of something you consider deterministic, can be dangerous.
Stoicism says that while Humans are still animals, we're different from bears or moths in that we have a much higher capacity for exercising the ability to use logic/reason. It also advocates living in "accordance with nature". Which means using the ability to reason in every aspect of life. When you put those two things together it logically follows that Humans are social animals and social animals are built for and thrive when they act in cooperation with other social animals of the same kind. So, people shouldn't do things like steal, kill, rape, etc because doing so would be going against our social nature, which means going against logic, which means going against nature. It would also follow that as a side effect, we'd be happiest if we lived in accordance with nature instead of against it.

As far as determinism goes, the stoics seemed to have a [weird way]("http://people.wku.edu/jan.garrett/stoa/seddon1.htm") of combining cosmic determinism with Human free will which I'm not exactly sure I'm in total agreement with. But as far as determinism in general is concerned. If people were created by the cosmos which follows, as we've observed a fixed set of deterministic principals. That every event is connected to every other event, than there are no random events. For example, if everything from the big bang up until the creation of Earth was the result of casual events then it would seem to logically follow that the creation of life was also a casual event. Which means that our lives today are also casual events. The laws of physics didn't simply stop when life was created. The sensation we call "consciousness" is put into motion by the same laws of physics that result in asteroids flying through space or the sun giving off heat. It's just that our consciousness is a different manifestation of those same laws of physics. The Human brain and the sensations we regard as thoughts and ideas also only exist in the physical universe and follow those same laws. Most modern people seem to regard a person's physical and mental properties as being different things. But really they're the same things, because the decision making processes the brain does are made up of chemical and electrical reactions, which exist according to the same laws of physics everything else does. But as far as our lives are concerned, whether or not we're free agents or not doesn't really matter. It's just like playing Super Mario Bros on an actual NES console with a Motorola CPU vs on an NES emulator on a x64 CPU. If the game had an intelligence it wouldn't care whether it's instructions were being directly processed by the NES's CPU or translated into other instructions which are run in a virtual environment that the an x64 CPU could process, it would still play almost exactly the same. So, if we can agree with the first statement that Humans need to cooperate with other Humans than I don't think believing in casual determinism can lead rational people into doing bad things like stealing or killing.

 It might be determined that you will die getting hit by a run away ice cream cart while crossing the street. That event would also have be co-fated to happen along with the person who was pushing the cart loosing control of it. And would also be co-fated to happen along with you not taking as much time as you should have to look both ways before crossing the street. Which would also be co-co-fated to happen along with you deciding to leave the house in the morning, etc. The ice cream cart didn't just come out of nowhere.

Just because quantum mechanics and general relativity present two incompatible laws of physics that concern how the universe works doesn't mean that there isn't one law of physics that explains everything. It just means that we haven't discovered it yet.

 Logos is just another name for the (singular) natural laws that the universe operates by. My knowledge of this area is pretty rough and it's likely that I'm in error. But some quantum mechanics theorists believe that the reason objects behave the way they do is because of their physical properties as manifested in subatomic particles. I don't just mean what gives objects their "physical" properties. But that even things such as why anything that possesses mass also possessed a certain amount of gravitational force is the result of various subatomic particles much smaller than even protons/electrons/neutrons. The subatomic particles that create gravity are called gravitons. Although, this is all just theory and theirs no reason to believe that there aren't particles even smaller than gravitons for example. I think it was one of the stoics that proclaimed that there are just as many parts making up his thumb as there are that make up the rest of the universe beyond his thumb. That everything is made of infinitely smaller and smaller particles.

So even if it's true that an objects gravitational pull for example is because of it's physical properties and not some non-material force that goes through the universe, which would seem to be at odds with the stoic theory that there is matter, which is always stagnant and stays forever stagnant until it's acted upon by logos, which gives it motion and it's various physical properties. I still don't think it's really a bad metaphor for thinking about the way the universe works. Especially at the macro level which we experience every day and much more often than we (or, at least those of us who aren't scientists working at the Large Hadron Collider) do the movements of sub-sub-sub atomic particles.

Which would seem to prove the theory that everything is just a matter of opinion and that we should try to shape ourselves to fit into the workings of the universe instead of trying to shape the workings of the universe to fit ourselves.
