### Post by chillicampari on 2010-03-20
Some of it (at least for those in the 35 year old + range) might be a holdover from the days where the computer was the mysterious big gray box where one false keystroke could wipe out hours or even the whole days work of work in a lot of data entry jobs. In several places I worked at, if you could type, you ended up doing data entry at some point. Even if you were not assigned "computer operator" duties there was a good chance you'd still hear the screams from those who were when they realized all that data just got eaten up. :P  

It's only in recent years where the concept of "recycle bin"  "undo" and "are you sure" type confirmations became the norm. Even 15 years ago or less you were likely to see something like:

COMMT CUR RECD TO DLY BATCH RECN [APPLY] [YES] [HOLD] [MRGE/WKY] [Z]

The example above is really close to one of the many dialogs from a legacy system at one of my former workplaces that was in use as recently as five years ago. Yikes!

The wrong choice at the wrong time means at minimum reentry of that stack of paperwork you just entered (lucked out there!) or worse, management intervention and a discussion of what went wrong. And your time usage is monitored also so you've gotta keep up. Too slow or too many errors meant you're looking for another job. 

And so I'd figure that mindset to be extra careful would holdover to using a personal computer at home, but this time, they don't actually know exactly what a lot of the options do (at least at first) since no one has trained them. Or even if someone has, the user has the safe area they are familiar with and they stay only with that. It becomes like a tunnel vision thing- if you see something unfamiliar, that usually means something went really wrong.   

We've come a long way, but to many people, even if something "looks" safe and has a friendly interface- it just might not be, since they know from experience what happens when you hit the wrong thing. So they tend to not mess with it 'cause that one wrong move might make for a really bad day.

### Post by lisati on 2010-03-21
A couple of trains of thought going on for me at the moment. Hopefully this won't be too incoherent or rambling.

Sometimes the fear is not from lack of skills but from lack of knowledge, combined with a hint of misinformation.

I recall seeing a tutorial program for a word-processing program from the Wordstar family of programs many years ago. Near the start it put up a message on the screen asking something along the lines of "Will the computer explode if you press a key?", and inviting the user to  press a key. When they did there was a nice little text-based animated graphic sequence "Kaboom!".... 

Next rant: Sometimes the error messages didn't help. Back in the day when I was using mainframes a problem that came up from time to time was that people sometimes forgot to put in the batch job control card that assigned the file stream used by a program to a real file. The common consequence was that when the program tried to open the file, it wouldn't open, and an error message popped up that sort-of made sense if you were familiar with the system. Trouble was, some of the programs would then blindly bumble on as if the open had worked, and try to access the file. This would usually result in the program crashing magnificently with an error code signifying "Invalid operation", and the printout of a totally unnecessary core dump that would mean next to nothing to the typical user. The stupid thing was that if you were programming in ASM, the crash could easily be avoided with a dozen less lines of code - one to test if the open worked, one to jump to a "die gracefully" routine if it didn't, and a handful in the "die gracefully" routine to end the program nicely, setting the OS's exit code in the process.

When I was about 9, my dad got fed up with me asking how to do this, and how to do that, and handed me the manual for DOS 6.22, which is about the size of a bible, and told me "It's all in there.  Figure it out."  Sounds harsh, but my dad always knew that the best way to learn something is to get your hands dirty.

Meanwhile, my mom had a mythic fear of "breaking" the computer.  When I built my parents a new computer in 2000, to replace the DOS machine they had been using for 14 years, my mom was even worse.  She wouldn't install anything without help for fear of breaking it.  She had to have everything backed up in 6 places so she could never loose anything.

For years I've told her that the best way to learn how to manager her system, is to dive in, break it stupidly, and then learn to fix it.  Every time you break the system, and then fix it yourself, you learn more about the way the system works.  And it doesn't matter what system you're using.  This is almost universally true.

Lately, she's started reading PC magazine.  (That's right, my 69 year old mother is reading PC magazine)

She's started tinkering with the system, trying to do maintenance, and occasionally, breaking things and having to fix them.  The reason is that she has finally realized that the VERY few irreplaceable things on her system, are all easily backed up, which means that the worst case is a minor inconvenience.

Ultimately, that's the difference between a person who's system always runs well, who is happy with it, and enjoys it; and a person who hates computers, yet buys a new one every 2 years because that's all the longer desktop windows can run without a reinstall.
