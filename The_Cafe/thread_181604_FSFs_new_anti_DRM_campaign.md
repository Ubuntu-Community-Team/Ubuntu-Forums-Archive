### Post by andy101 on 2006-05-24
Just wondering if anyone had heard much about the FSF (Free software foundations) new campaign to try and stamp out the evil that is DRM.

Their website is: [http://www.defectivebydesign.org](http://www.defectivebydesign.org) 

Not wanting to start flame, what does everyone think of DRM?

I am personally highly opposed as it is often used to try and stamp out competition.
The reason many people can't legally watch DVDs or listen to certian music on Ubuntu is not a technical problem, there are many people willing to write some code but a legal problem. Or a problem associated with the hidden standards used by some companies (*cough* Apple *cough*).

Many people cliam DRM is only enforcing the rights of the contents creator. This is untrue and th person who tells you this requires an education.
DRM prevents fair use, where in the law does it say I can only make 1 CD with the file on it? or I can only move it onto 3 devices? The law says I can't give it to someone else, but DRM software rarely if ever makes choices about permitting coping based on identity.

Further more it appears to only be targetted against legitiamte users, pirates can and will crack DRM technology, it has a major flaw in its design. Compare it with encryption, we are quite good with encryption, I can encrypt a file and send it over an insecure medium, how does this work? well to get the orriginal message you need 3 things, the encypted data, the decryption algorithm, and the key (assuming some kind of keyed crypto), an evesdropper has the encypted data, and may well have the knowledge of which algorithm was used but does not have the decryption key! he gets nothing.

DRM on the other hand is differant. My DRM enabled player can decode the content and play it, I have the encrypted file, the player also contains the decryption algorithm, it may be obscured but not well, after all my pentium 4 can understand and execute it (its why you really can't hide code if it actually executes something). As for the key, well either the player has it or the file has it, or its unkeyed either way it can be extracted from where its kept eventually, after all its on an untrusted machine. After all the machine must be untrusted, f it was trusted why have DRM? If your machine can play the file its surely possibly (but possibly illegal) to find out what it does and just replicate it. So DRM does not protect against thoose willing to break the law, and as illegal copying is,well illegal then we have no reason to think that pirates would obey they law saying don't break DRM any more than they would copyright law.

DRM does of course prevent competition, there was afrench case over this involving apple.

This also poses a problem for Linux aswell, we have people who have reverse engineered propriatery formats, and although this is less than desireable, some have managed it (I would like to say a huge thanks to Open Office here!!!), however certian DRM laws would make this kind of action illegal.

Say no to DRM, Say yes to open standards, say yes to the right to choose.
