### Post by mivo on 2007-10-04
> **abadtooth said:**
> but I guess after you use Linux for a while and get to know it, you might want something a little more like... erm well.. something that would be a little harder for us Linux newbies to get used to 

Why though? I mostly want an OS that "just works". Sure, I want to be able to configure it, tweak it a little, and sometimes I feel like tinkering (all can be done with Ubuntu), but more than anything I want a stable, well-supported system that works with my hardware out of the box without having to spend nights of fiddling around with arcane config files. I love to learn, but at my own pace and when I want it -- and at other times I want to be able to get work done when I have to. :) I get this from Ubuntu. In fact, you can set up an Ubuntu system and start to be productive two hours later.

I used FreeBSD a few years ago, and it compiled all new packages locally, much like Gentoo. I loved that, and when I have time, I will set up a hobby system just for learning purposes, but I would not want that on a system I use for working. The older I get, the more appealing "just works" becomes, and the less I am interested in "random adventures" that keep me up all night. :P

### Post by Sethalos on 2007-10-04
I started using Ubuntu when 7.04 was released and after receiving my new laptop configured with Vista. I used Vista all of one month and that was it. I formatted the drive and installed Ubuntu (a friend suggested it) rather than pay the 250+ bucks for WinXP.

Since then I have installed Ubuntu on every computer I own, my cousins computers, my neighbors computers, and am in the process of convincing my workplace to switch over and they are currently testing doing just that.

Is it the best in the world, in my opinion it's darn close. The other distro's will appeal to those with far more Linux know how than I so I can't comment on them, but Ubuntu has done everything right so far in my opinion and is only getting better.
