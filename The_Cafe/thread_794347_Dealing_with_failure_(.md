### Post by beercz on 2008-05-15
Hi Kernel Sanders

Sorry to hear about your "failure" (think positive - your "learning experience").  I hope things improve for you soon.  Failing an exam is not the end of the world.  However, I hope the following is helpful for you and others who are taking exams.

As someone who has done lots of exams in the past, having done part time study for 13 years, as well as going to school/college etc. I find the following tips I have learnt along the way useful:

1. Time the questions.  Assuming, say, it is a 3 hour paper, and you have to answer 5 questions.  I allow 10 minutes to read the paper at the beginning, 20 minutes to read through, review and correct my answers at the end.  That leaves 2.5 hours.  Therefore I know I have 30 minutes per question.  Don't spend longer than the time allocated per question.  Calculate how much time you have for each question and stick to that time in the exam. This timing can also be applied to papers containing many very short questions or multiple choice questions.

2. Read the question. Read it again. Make sure you know what is being asked, not what you *think *is being asked.

3. Answer the question.  Make sure you do this, and not add unnecessary waffle, or deviate from the question.  Write clearly and legibly, so that the person marking your script at 3am may give you the benefit of any doubt!  You never know.

4.  If you are stuck, leave that question and move onto another.  Make sure you keep to your timing, then you can go back later.

5. During your first read through of the paper, scribble short notes next to the question (on the question paper if you can), so that you can refer to the note later if needed.

6. If you are sitting an annotated exam, make as many notes on the supplementary material as you can.  Make sure they are clear and legible.

7. When revising, revise in small chunks, say 30 minutes at a time, then have a small 10 minute break - this is also a good way of preparing yourself to keep to time.  Focus on one topic at a time.

8. Plan your revision time, and then plan a reward for yourself when you have done that time.  For example, I used to say to myself, I am going to revise for 2.5 hours then I can watch that DVD afterwards.  All revision and no play is not good.  I preferred silence when revising, some prefer music.  You have to do what is best for you.

9. I used to write salient key points (e.g. formulae or facts) on a number of address cards and keep them in my pocket.  When I had a few minutes (e.g. waiting for a bus, coffee in a cafe etc.) I used to get them out and go through them.  I went through the series of cards several times, it is amazing what sticks!

10.  When revising, tell your loved ones, or those around you, that you are going to revise for x amount of time at whenever and you are not to be disturbed.  You can agree to make time for them later, and stick to it!

11. Arrive at the exam in good time.  You could even read those cards whilst you are waiting outside the exam room!  Go the the toilet beforehand, make sure you are not hungry or thirsty.  Some exams allow you to take a bottle of drink and sweets etc.  If you are permitted take some, it helps to make you feel a bit more comfortable.

12. In the exam, try and stay calm.  If you feel panic coming on, stop, take a deep breath and hold for a few seconds, release slowly.  Pause doing the exam for a short while if you need to.

13. Ensure you have all the necessary materials for the exam, including spare pens (that work!!), sharpened pencils, sharpeners, erasers etc etc ....

14.  Practice, practice, practice.  Use past exam papers, buy books with sample questions relative to your exam.  When using past papers, time yourself on each question.  If possible, go through the past/practice papers with others who are also due to sit the same exam *after* you have all done the past/practice papers, compare answers, learn from what you did good and what you did bad.  I used to keep one past/practice paper aside, and about 2 days before the actual exam, I used to 'sit' the past/practice paper in a small room, alone, and do the paper as if it was a real exam.

15.  Read and understand the exam instructions on the paper, follow those instructions.  Same applies to instructions from the exam invigilators.

Hope that is helpful information - it was for me.

Remember:

Exams are testing what you know (or what you should know), but mainly about what 
can remember!

Exams are about preparation - the key to passing them is about preparation, sometimes weeks, or months in advance.  Get into a routine of preparing for them.  Stick to it!  "Prepare to fail, fail to prepare".  Exams are a doddle if you prepare properly.

Almost finally, exams are just that - an examination of your ability in some particular field.  They are NOT life or death.

And finally we all need a bit of luck with exams, so to all of you who are doing exams - GOOD LUCK!!!
