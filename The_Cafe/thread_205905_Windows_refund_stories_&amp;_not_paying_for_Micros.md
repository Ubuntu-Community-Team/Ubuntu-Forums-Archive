Dear Mr. Ferguson,

Thank you for your email dated I7 June 2006 to the Consumer Officer. This email relates to the possibility of purchasing PCs without a preinstalled Windows operating system.

The Commission is aware of the difficulties encountered by consumers to purchase PCs without an operating system on them or alternatively with another operating system than Windows.

As regards the possibility of purchasing a PC without an operating system, I understand that Microsoft's licensing agreements with PC manufacturers encourage (through the grant of rebates) the pre-installation of operating systems on PCs. ### Post by OttifantSir on 2006-12-03
> **3rdalbum said:**
> The computer I bought was the display model. It had been turned on in the store for the past 8 months, and the EULA had been accepted by the staff of the store.

Apparently you know there is an EULA, so by not asking the shop to do a clean install or reset the OS, be default you accept it.

 > **3rdalbum said:**
> When I got the computer home and booted it, it went straight to the Windows desktop, so I was not given the option of refusing the EULA. I've just checked the paperwork that came with my computer - no EULA there.

However, any legal contract requires of you to read it before accepting. Since you were not given a paper-copy of it, you had no way of knowing, before booting the computer with the pre-installed system and use the system to view the EULA, what it contained.

> **3rdalbum said:**
> I still need Windows, so I'm not trying to get a refund for it, but I'm wondering whether I'm still bound by the license agreement since I was not given a copy of it or even asked whether I accept it!

Since you know it exists, and you did nothing to ensure you were presented with a copy, like a paper, or even setting up a "clean" computer simply to boot it up and read the EULA, Microsoft, Dell, HP, Lenovo, etc. have enough lawyers who knows how to tweak the laws to make sure you are bound by the agreement. And if you should win, it will have costed you a lot, and you have the possibility that they will simply deny paying you, just keep on appealing to the courts. And there are many. The PC manufacturer has its headquarters one place, Microsoft another, the shop who sold it to you a third place. Then you have city-courts, state-courts of varying degrees, several federal organs that can be appealed to, several federal courts, lobbyism, politicians "on the take", Supreme Court (which you won't get into if your case isn't of beneficial worth to the public). Trade treaties also come into consideration.

If you wanted to make a case of this, I predict it being a high-profile case for a few weeks, then lose interest as it never gets resolved. Then, some years down the road, it would be announced, one way or the other how the case had been decided and people would look at the screen and ask themselves: "What the f*** has this got to do with me? I wish they could send real news for a change, like what happened to that quarterback that got sacked in the game between the Raiders and the 49ers (I am Norwegian. Don't know American football. Apologies if it's incorrect)

So, to sum up: You will have to be highly motivated to pursue this, and you will have to come up with some truly innovative arguments.

PS: I tried to see this case from three perspectives: The user's moral rights, the legal way, and the reality. Therefore, it's messy.
