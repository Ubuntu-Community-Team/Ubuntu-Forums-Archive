### Post by Objekt on 2010-04-15
The article was light on details.  Are the movies going to be in full HD format?  That would be quite a change for the movie studios.  Generally, they will not do anything to make it even remotely possible to copy one of their precious optical discs.  Putting HD movies in files on a hard drive, even if the files are locked down in some manner, is a huge step for them.

For those speculating about AES encryption, digital watermarking etc: Highly unlikely.  Sounds like way too much work.  The movie studios are all about making a quick buck.  They'll make it a pain for the average Joe to watch without paying, but there will be some kind of crack/unlocker out within a week of these things hitting the market.

I expect the locked-up movies will use a "phone home" DRM scheme, of the type infecting PC games and commercial OS's lately.  They will require proprietary player software, likely included on the HDD alongside the movies.  Nothing more.  No AES encryption.  Certainly no attempt at per-copy tracking.  That would be way, way too much work and therefore too expensive for the studios.

It's not as if every movie in question wasn't already available for download.  However, the folks who supply those copies will see Seagate's scheme as a challenge.  I, for one, salute our digital restrictions management-breaking fellows!
