### Post by Thelasko on 2008-10-24
> **gn2 said:**
> Why is that?
Emissions standards.  There is an effort to force diesel engines to meet emissions standards similar to those for gasoline vehicles.

For a while, the US was the only county to have such strict standards.  Knowing that diesel cars were never vastly popular in the US, car manufacturers decided it would less expensive to stop selling diesel vehicles in the US than to develop engines that could meet the standards.  This year the EU standard was made similar to the standards in the US and we now have a few diesel cars available for sale.  All of which are made by Volkswagen.

Trucks have slightly different emissions standards, but they too are extremely strict.  The diesel truck market is much larger in the US than the car market, so truck manufacturer's based in the US met the standards.

Most car manufacturers aren't keen on reintroducing a diesel car in the US.  It's seen as a big risk.  There was big news a few weeks ago that Ford manufactures a diesel vehicle in the UK that gets 65mpg but they won't sell it in the US.  The conversion rate to dollars would make the car too expensive.  To make the car affordable, it would have to be made here in the US.  If the car failed, or even stricter emissions standards were made, Ford would loose a lot of money.
