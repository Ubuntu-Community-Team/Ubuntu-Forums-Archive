### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2010-09-15
Mark Shuttleworth wrote (emphasis mine):

> suppose you could say that no matter how generous we are to downstream users, **if upstream is only measuring code, then any generosity other than code won’t be registered.** I don’t really know what to do about that – I didn’t found Ubuntu as a vehicle for getting lots of code written, **that didn’t seem to me to be what the world needed. It needed a vehicle for getting it out there**, that cares about delivering the code we already have in a state of high quality and reliability. Most of the pieces of the desktop were in place – and code was flowing in – it just wasn’t being delivered in a way that would **take it beyond the server**, or to the general public.

That is and always has been the "main thing" for me. Taming Linux and making it available to ordinary kids like me. Linux (and Debian in particular) was *already* awesome, but not "accessible" to the ordinary, ungifted, geek-challenged desktop user. It wasn't so much *code* that was needed to "bring Debian to the ordinary user," it was the *mixing* of existing code in such a way as to bring it to the *desktop* for ordinary use, not just servers.

At the same time that some of the elitists in the Debian community whine that Ubuntu is "dumbing Debian down," they themselves developed much of the code that Canonical uses to make Debian "friendly." Ubuntu, Mepis, Knoppix, and other Debian forks have done what Debian refused to do: Go to the desktop rather than just to the server, and be "friendly" enough for ordinary desktop users.

**So perhaps it can be said that Canonical's focus is on contributing to Linux *users* rather than Linux developers.**

It's the height of hypocrisy to extol the virtues and sing the praises of FOSS, then turn around and whine bitterly when someone actually takes FOSS and runs with it.

-Robin

If you believe average users installing Ubuntu is the only way people make their computers unbootable or the only way people accidentally erase files, I think you should take a look at the computer section of Yahoo! Answers some time. It is full of daily questions from Windows and Mac users who have trouble booting up their computers, who have accidentally erased files, or reformatted their computers without backing up.

The real problem is not that you can make OS installation (and that goes for *any* OS, not just Ubuntu) easy for average users but Ubuntu doesn't. The real problem is that downloading, installing, and configuring the OS is the *primary* means by which people get a usable Ubuntu, whereas most Windows and Mac users just buy a computer with Windows or Mac OS X preinstalled and preconfigured.

Android would have had very little uptake if, instead of releasing Android-preinstalled and optimized phones, Google had said "Hey, just download this Android image and install it on some random phone you already own. If you have problems, check out this guide, or post to our forums."

We don't live in a technological meritocracy. It isn't enough for Ubuntu to just keep improving, to be easier to use, to be easier to install, or to fix all its major bugs. Doing all that will *not* result in the masses using Ubuntu. It may mean more power users migrating over, but it'll remain a niche operating system as long as it is not preinstalled and well-configured on a well-advertised and highly attractive (both in terms of physical appearance and competitive pricing) laptop computer.

Super-geek?  I didn't even have a home computer until 2001.  My first dip into Linux, after trying out a Knoppix CD, was with a cheap notebook that came with Linspire pre-installed.  I had no idea what Linux was all about, but I could use that Linspire desktop right away.

While I did't care much for Linspire the company, the distro itself was Debian-based and easy for a novice to use.  I don't recall having any problems with it crashing or anything like that, but it was awhile ago and I may have forgotten.  I know that between Knoppix and Linspire, I was convinced to try to stick with Debian-based distros.

So, yes, you were wrong when you said that Ubuntu was among the first to bring Linux to the desktop.  It wasn't even among the first easy-to-use Debian-based distros.  Do a Google search on **Linux timelines** and check out some of those timeline charts, and you can see for yourself.

That being said, Ubuntu was a great thing to happen to Linux, in my opinion.  I was a dial-up user who took full advantage of the free CDs.  Ubuntu helped increase Linux awareness.  I thought the idea of the word "Ubuntu" was beautiful.  Ubuntu has helped move things forward.

But so did other Debian-based distros like Knoppix and Mepis.  In particular, I don't think Mepis' Warren Woodford gets nearly enough credit for what he put out there.  There were other live CDs before Mepis, and there were some other easy-to-use distros, but I think (and someone can correct me if I'm wrong) that he was the first one to really put those two things together.
