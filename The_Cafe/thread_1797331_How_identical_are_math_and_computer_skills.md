### Post by beew on 2011-07-04
Theoretical computer science is a very much like mathematics,--some would argue that it is a branch of mathematics, considering the work of Alan Turing, Komogorov etc.. But what a lot of people call "computer skills" are assorted cook book results derived from trial and error, that would probably be difficult for the mathematician because there is no overarching conceptual framework to go by, only a mess of details. The same goes with programming. I think the math people in general have no problem with algorithms and problem solving (concepts) but probably find it difficult to keep track of details in syntax and idiosyncrasies of language and some more of less random conventions. I am a mathematician and this is what I find anyway, other may have different experience.

I think the difference is a bit similar to that between a theoretical physicist and an engineer.

I have a suspicion that the field of psychometrics is full of hogwash. One thing they do, for example, is match samples of two ethnically different groups by IQ, then measure, say, brain size in these groups. Then, they'd find that the average brain size is the same for these two groups provided they have selected people with the same IQ. From that, they conclude that IQ and brain size are related, which in turn is used to defend the idea that IQ reflects some seriously important innate stuff.

These particular studies were done and redone by luminaries of psychometrics and cheerfully recited by others, including popular press.

What this amounts to, statistically, is that in group j they look at brain size (B_j) distribution, conditional on IQ_j=x, and then observe that the two expectations are equal: E(B_1|IQ_1=x) - E(B_2|IQ_2=x) = 0. For jointly normal (IQ,B) with correlation Rho this means only that
Rho = S(IQ_2)*[E(B_1)-E(B_2)] / [S(B_1)*(E(IQ_1)-E(IQ_2))].

But that can be either large or small, saying nothing about the relation of IQ to brain size. For example, Rho will decrease as the standard deviation of brain size, S(B_1), increases.
