### Post by mdmarmer on 2009-12-11
I've been programming for 40 years plus.  There was an early expectation that computers would become much easier to program, similar to the early expectation that computers would quickly become very useful for language translation.  It's only recently that computers have become important in language translation.  Early on, there were classic books, still worth reading, like The Psychology of Computer Programming or The Mythical Man-Month, which purported to explain how to solve the great problems of computer programming.  Such innovations as structured programming and ERP (among others) have made computers more powerful and easier to program, but programming remains difficult, in many ways an art rather than a science or engineering discipline.

Computers will become easier to program.  There are many obstacles to overcome, and it will be a gradual process rather than an event.  Open source and linux are great advances and will serve to make computers easier to use.

I've scratched the surface here, and I don't want to write a book in this forum.

Answering your questions:

1. I don't know.  Linux and open source are very important.

2. See 1. above.

3. I think we can learn the most by studying the creative process, the history of science, following in the steps of Derek deSolla-Price, who wrote Little Science, Big Science, and was one of the first to show that 90% of the knowledge is in 10% or less of the texts, and to analyze creativity.  "Creativity is digging new holes, rather than digging old holes deeper."  

Mike
