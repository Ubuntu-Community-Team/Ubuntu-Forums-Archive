What is the model of mobo, and what are you planning on replacing it with? Usually replacing the mobo means replacing most of the other parts... 

Something running a 3ghz chip is probably running DDR RAM, and all the new boards run DDR2 or DDR3, which are not compatible. You'll most likely have to upgrade the RAM.

Is the HD a SATA or IDE? If it's IDE, most new boards come with SATA connections, and if you're lucky has an IDE, which, unless you are getting an adaptor, means you'll have to replace the HD at the same time. The same goes for the CPU, considering you'll probably be changing the socket when changing the mobo. Likewise for the connection to the video card, whether you currently have an AGP or not.

First thing I always do is find the motherboard that I want, and then I work from there. Then I know what parts *need* to be replaced at the same time, and which are still compatible.

### Post by Delever on 2008-07-24
I suggest to save some money for full upgrade at once. Reasons:

- Your motherboard probably has AGP socket for video card.
- RAM type is probably DDRAM.
- Now Intel with Core Duo processors has good edge over AMD, it eats DDR2 or DDR3 RAM nicely. And most motherboards now come with PCI Express socket for video card instead of AGP.

What I am saying you may not be able to upgrade gradually and reach same good performance as with full upgrade. And I believe Crysis is particularly hungry for those pretty frames.

If you buy now and are saving money, few things from my research:
- If question about DDR2 or DDR3 arises, go with DDR2.
- If question about RAM FSB 800MHz or 1200MHz arises, go with FSB 800.
- If question about nVidia card GeForce 9600 or GeForce 9800 arises (in the same price range), I can assure that 9600 works with linux just fine, but do not suggest anything ;)
