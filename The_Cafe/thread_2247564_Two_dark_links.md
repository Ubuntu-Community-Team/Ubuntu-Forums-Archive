### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-10-09
The Linux 'community' that counts, the one that makes the decisions, is the developer community. Developers have different interests than users.  Unlike the commercial world, FOSS developers are typically free to do as they wish without concern about displeasing users and the resulting loss of sales and income. Sometimes that community ought to keep things behind closed doors.

By conducting this dispute in public, often in a childish and vindictive manner, all the participants do Linux a disservice and cast doubt on the ability of its developer community to behave as professionals and adults. Frequently, the emphasis appears to be on asserting an individual developer's right to choose, in contrast to a desire to make the best software for users. 

The systemd dispute also shows that Linux, considered as a single entity, lacks a mechanism for making fundamental choices about fundamental alternatives. The developer community does not want choice force upon it, yet it hasn't made an effort to create an alternative to the scenario the current emotion-laden hissy fit exemplifies.

As a user, I judge software by what it does for me. That's very much a top-level assessment. I expect the plumbing to be invisible and to just work, without error, day in and day out. Init systems are plumbing. 

(Re: Torvalds:  If I worked for Torvalds and was the subject of his verbal lashings, I'd be looking for a new employer. Torvalds, for better or worse, is a public figure in many respects, and that means he's lost the freedom to expect his public utterances to be ignored and not reflect on the community he represents more than anyone.)
