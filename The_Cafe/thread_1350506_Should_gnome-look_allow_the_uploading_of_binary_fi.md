### Post by t0p on 2009-12-09
I think a lot of people are overreacting here.  [The malicious "screensaver" .deb]("http://www.omgubuntu.co.uk/2009/12/malware-found-in-screensaver-for-ubuntu.html") that sparked this debate is the *only* piece of malware I've heard of being distributed through Gnome-look.org.  Have there been others?  I don't know.  How long was this .deb up before its evil nature was discovered?  I don't know.  But I feel pretty confident that this is not a common phenomenon.

Those people claiming that this episode is reason enough to disallow the posting of .debs are making a couple of mistakes.  The first is that Gnome-look.org (or any site) has some sort of duty to protect users from their own stupidity/naivety.  Gnome-look.org has no such duty.  There are a multitude of warnings all over the internet that users should beware of installing software from untrusted sources.  And those warnings are as far as it should go.

The second mistake is assuming that binaries are somehow unique in their capacity for evil.  Source code can be equally guilty.  How many users actually examine source code before they compile it?  I suspect very few.  How many users would recognise malicious code?  Again, I suspect very few.

The people now campaigning for the removal of .debs from Gnome-look.org are being rather elitist.  The fact is, packages in .deb form are much easier for less-skilled users to install.  Shall we make life harder for the less-skilled users, just because of one extremely rare occurence?  Or should we take heart from the fact that the problem was [discovered and dealt with]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1349678") thanks to Ubuntu's excellent community?

### Post by aysiu on 2009-12-09
I would prefer people just look at the ratings, comments, download numbers, and posting dates of themes.

If a "theme" just got posted up last week from a new member, has only 10 downloads, no ratings, and no comments, that might be a good theme... or it might be malware.

If, however, a theme got posted up five months ago by a years-long theme-making veteran user, has 30,000 downloads, tons of ratings and comments, then it's highly unlikely to be malware.

Heuristics are imperfect and leave you with a lot of false positives (which is one of two main reasons antivirus software is pants). So apart from some team inspecting every single .deb or .tar.gz before allowing it to be uploaded, a social bureaucracy makes the most sense.
