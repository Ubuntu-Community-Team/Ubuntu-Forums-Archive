### Post by hrod beraht on 2008-12-08
> **Paqman said:**
> That seems like an odd philosophy. Surely the whole point of an OS is to automate as much as possible, to allow the user to spend their time interacting with the apps, rather than the OS?

[[COLOR="Blue"]Aaron Griffin[/COLOR]]("http://phraktured.net/arch-way.html") of Arch probably answered that best:

[INDENT]*Relying on complex tools to manage and build your system is going to hurt the end users. If you try to hide the complexity of the system, you'll end up with a more complex system. Layers of abstraction that serve to hide internals are never a good thing. Instead, the internals should be designed in a way such that they NEED no hiding.*[/INDENT]

And people often confuse simplicity and ease-of-use:

[INDENT]*'Simple' is defined from a technical standpoint, not a usability standpoint. It is better to be technically elegant with a higher learning curve, than to be easy to use, and technically crap.*[/INDENT]

And as far as the OS being automatic:

[INDENT]*Relying on complex tools to manage and build your system is going to hurt the end users. Maintenance and upgrading should be an active process, not a passive one.*[/INDENT]

That active versus passive is important. When the OS maintenance/upgrading is done by the user, they have an awareness of what's happening. When it's automatic, they tend to be more like many computer users, who have absolutely no idea how their computer/OS even works, and so they are the ones who most often have difficulties. Think about a struggling Windows user you may know. Why do they have difficulties? Is it because they are stupid? No, it's because the operation of they system is so hidden from them that they don't know what's going on. If the operation of the system is explained to them (or just obvious from the start), they are going to be much better off in the future.

Bob

### Post by fwojciec on 2008-12-08
> **kevdog said:**
> How does Arch handle kernel updates?  When do you install a new kernel?  Or do you have to compile from scratch (which really isn't that hard to do once you've messed it up about 50 times before getting it right).

Kernel is just another package and gets upgraded periodically like other packages.  When you install/upgrade it a script generates a boot ramdisk (using Arch specific utility called mkinitcpio) that'll be specific to your hardware.  While it supports all sorts of hardware, only the modules that are useful considering your hardware will be actually loaded.  Compared to Ubuntu the difference is that new version of the kernel replace previous versions instead of being installed in parallel.

Of course if you want to use your own custom compiled kernel you can do that as well.  There are also alternative kernels available from AUR.
