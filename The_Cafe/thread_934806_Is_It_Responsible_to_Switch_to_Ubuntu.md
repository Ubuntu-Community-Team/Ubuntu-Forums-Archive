### Post by lorenbr on 2008-10-01
I'm an American volunteer working in a small government office in Central Thailand.  Theoretically I'm tasked with doing community development, but I have pretty broad leeway on that, and something that I've been faced with ever since arriving is virulent computer infections on computers running pirated Windows XP.  The solution that I came up with was to try and convince my office coworkers to switch over to a more stable solution, i.e. Linux, i.e. Ubuntu.  I saw this as being both a) more secure and b) less illegal.  I don't take a moral stand against pirated software, but at some point it seems to me that a government office in the emerging economy of Thailand will no longer be able to thumb its nose at international intellectual property laws.  Anyways, to that end I've been working for a few weeks on trying to get Ubuntu optimally configured to meet their needs.  I've figured out how to get the language to switch like their used to, and I've got the music playback working mostly correctly.

But in all this configuration, I'm starting to doubt my central premise.  Is it responsible of me to push this switch, knowing that most of these people do not understand the basics of the windows computers they are using, not to mention the basics of Linux.  I believe that I've stumbled across enough Thai language resources that they should be able to support themselves, given enough introductory hand-holding (I'll be here another 18 months or so, so I'm optimistic on that front).  But is this the right path to be taking generally?  I just perused a poll asking whether users were actually using Ubuntu to work.  The responses were predominantly positive, but the amount of people who experienced serious enough problems has got me thinking twice.  True, no one here is going to need much from their machine: word processing, presentations, the occasional spreadsheet, playing music, burning cd's, etc.  But I worry that I am carrying over my own enthusiasm for Open Source and problem solving into a place where the solution is fundamentally faulty.  My coworkers are not going to interested in solving the little configuration and compatibility problems that I have been encountering since I started this game.  I hope to get all the kinks worked out and then hand them off a stable and functional machine.  But am I not just putting off problems until a few years down the line?  Of course I want to teach them to really use the system, but the familiarity and convenience of their Windows experience are going to put definite dampeners on their enthusiasm, especially when it comes to getting underneath the GUI and going to the shell, with all its incomprehensible commands in Roman script.

I wonder if anyone else has anything thoughts?  I'm looking for some words of wisdom here.  I am enthusiastic about this idea, and have really been enjoying the time I spent setting things up.  But I'm afraid I'm forcing it.

### Post by lorenbr on 2008-10-01
Zombiepig, I honestly appreciate your support.

Part of my concern is that I do not want to leave the users with a static product.  If I don't teach them how to upgrade and install on their own, then they'll end up breaking something when they need to replace the speakers or they want to install a webcam.  Morever, there will come a point when they will need to move on from Hardy, and I want to make sure they are capable of doing that.  If they can't solve the problems on their own, what is going to happen is they are going to hand their machine over to the local computer guy who is going to uninstall Ubuntu, install a pirated copy of Windows XP (that may even come with some viruses for free!), install NOD32 (a particularly useless piece of free antivirus software), and generally just set us back where we started, until the pattern is shocked by honest enforcement of intellectual property laws.

As for running proprietary software, the hope is to completely wean them.  But at the moment OpenOffice isn't doing for me what I need it to do.  There's more tweaking to be done.  In fact all my work may end up relatively useless if I can't convince them that they want to make the switch.

At this point I spend my days trying to set things up to a point where I think they can be convinced to "buy in," so to speak.  I just need to make sure I have them buying in to the right set of assumptions.  If the computer breaks, will they be able to fix it?  They are not going to have a computer guy across town to fix their problems.  And a simple nuke and reinstall probably won't do it either.

Morever, I am concerned about the frequency with which people express strongly negative opinions about Ubuntu. But if I get everyone to switch over to Ubuntu, and then some serious crashing or compatability problem surfaces, then I've hung them out to dry... at least a little bit.

IMO, the best way to find out if it is viable is to just jump right in in a limited way.

pick a local have that is interested in the project and has a head on his shoulders. toss him an ubuntu cd, and point him to a computer. do not help him. tell him you aren't going to help him, why you are doing this to him, etc.
