### Post by ve4cib on 2010-01-07
In the next ten years I'd *like* to see Linux move up to the 5-10% range on the consumer desktop/notebook market.  If it's at about 1-2% right now then I think that those kinds of gains are doable.

With tablet-style devices and the line between traditional computers and portable, embedded devices getting more and more blurred I don't think it's unreasonable to see Linux making fairly significant gains in the consumer market.  With Google pushing Android and ChromeOS I think we'll be seeing more and more out-of-the-box Linux-based devices emerging in the next few years.  I can imagine a world where computers are sold with both "Windows Ready" and "Chrome Ready" stickers on them.  And I'm fully expecting Android to start to move out of the phone market and into other handheld devices, like multi-purpose E-Readers and tablet-style devices.

Windows I'm expecting will continue to dominate the desktop world.  Some businesses and governments may start to move away from it, but it's been sufficiently entrenched for long enough that it is not going to go away quickly nor easily.  And arguably it shouldn't if it's adequately serving the needs of the people who use it.

In the mobile device market, unless the next version of Windows Mobile is as much of a breakthrough for Microsoft as Windows 7 was I can't see anything but losses or barely-sustained status-quo for Windows in those kinds of markets.

I'm expecting that mobile OS alternatives like Android, Symbian, and OSX (or iPhoneOS, or whatever Apple calls their mobile OS) to have varying degrees of growth, which will likely marginalize Windows Mobile even further.  (Maemo I expect to either make significant gains, or eventually be scrapped and see Nokia move over to Android.)  This is unless Microsoft does something amazing, which is entirely possible.  The ZunePhone could be an amazing piece of technology that will outclass the iPhone, the Nexus *, and whatever other super-phones appear.  I doubt it, but it's possible, I suppose.

In the server world I'm not expecting much change from how things work right now.  A combination of Windows servers for internal business applications and domain control, with Linux servers for web hosting, firewalls, content-filters, and automated backups will probably remain the status-quo for many businesses.  The corporate world is very slow to change in a lot of ways (which explains why IE6 is still as popular as it is).
