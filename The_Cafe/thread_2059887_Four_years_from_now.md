### Post by Welly Wu on 2012-09-19
So, I recently purchased a new System76 Lemur Ultra Thin (lemu4) notebook PC and I am hoping that it will last me for the next four years until April 2016. That's a long time from today and anything can happen, but I am pretty confident that it won't die or crap out on me by that time. I tend to overspend on PCs in order to get the right balance of features and performance and then I keep them for a very long period of time. I like my System76 Lemur Ultra Thin (lemu4) because it is light, portable, and well balanced. I have brought it to Starbucks, the library, home, and Barnes & Nobles Booksellers and I have not had any major problems that weren't caused by my own fault. The PC hardware is ultra rock solid reliable. Ubuntu 12.04.x 64 bit LTS is reliable too although I have encountered my fair share of problems including one kernel panic tonight and a lot of pain caused by misconfiguration of software. The really good thing about modern PC hardware and software like Ubuntu is that yeah it will become obsolete by that time, but the basic functions will remain pretty much the same over time so there is not really a compelling need for me to buy a new computer every year. Ubuntu has slowed down a bit since I bought it on June 26th, 2012, but that's due to the fact that I needed to download, purchase, and install a lot of software applications which I use regularly. My next purchase will be Microsoft Windows 8 Pro 64 bit Upgrade on October 26th, 2012. In fact, most of my cost structure and expenditures are for Windows. It's terribly expensive, but it is the most popular operating system in the world. I hate paying the Microsoft Tax, but what else can I do? I use Ubuntu most of the time. I found that it does a much better job at everyday tasks than Windows 7 64 bit. I mostly surf the web, check and write e-mail messages, connect to social media outlets, and download a ton of music, movies, e-books, magazines, etc. I don't create high definition multimedia content. I have found that Ubuntu is a capable alternative to Microsoft Windows 7 or any previous version and it is liberating. I can do whatever I want with Ubuntu with little to no restrictions on my freedom. When I need a new piece of software, I can download the free and open source version. If I need a specialized piece of software application, then I can download and install it from a third-party software vendor that makes it available for Ubuntu or I can purchase it. I plan to purchase Microsoft Office 2013 Home Premium subscription for $100 USD when it becomes available and I will share it with up to five family members and friends soon. I find that LibreOffice Writer is suitable for my needs, but my future professors are quite anal and a few will insist that I use Microsoft Word and PowerPoint. I already have Microsoft Office 2010 32 bit Professional Plus Service Pack 1.

The thing is that modern PCs manufactured from January 2010 to today are quite powerful and capable. There is little reason to buy a brand new PC unless you work at a job or in an industry that requires that you stay up to date every 2 - 3 years. Home users with PCs can get by with what they already have from 2010 quite fine now. I find that the PC industry has reached a point of maturation. There are few key innovations that drive the industry which require that you purchase a brand new PC. As it stands right now, modern PC and electronics technologies are sufficient to meet the needs of most home PC users. I don't need a Full HD 1080p resolution monitor or screen for my needs. I don't need an Intel Core i7 quad core CPU. It would be nice to have and I could have afforded to buy a System76 Gazelle Professional 2012 model, but I thought hard about it and I decided that I could forgo the extra expenses.

High definition multimedia is very expensive. I rely on the standard JPEG, DVD-Video, and Red Book Compact Disc formats along with Adobe .PDF files. Content consumption is very affordable and readily available. I don't play PC games anymore as I don't have the time or inclination to do so. I am pursuing a masters degree in literature and the arts so it's the quality of the content itself that matters and less so the medium or format.

I see Ubuntu users with much older PC hardware that are able to use the current versions of Ubuntu and Microsoft Windows just fine. One thing that I am thinking about purchasing is a 3 terabyte USB 3.0 portable hard disk drive in the future. I have a Western Digital My Passport 2 TB USB 3.0 Portable hard disk drive and I have 1.1 TB of available disk space. At the rate that I keep purchasing new music, movies, magazines, and e-books in Adobe .PDF format or .MOBI formats, I expect to fill it up gradually by the end of next year in December 2013. Unless there is a breakthrough in solid state drive technology, I just can not afford to purchase a 1 TB SSD in the near future. I would also like to purchase a Western Digital VelociRaptor 10K 2 TB Duo external hard disk drive with Super Speed USB 3.0 technology and ThunderBolt support built in. I find myself transferring hundreds of gigabytes worth of data on a weekly basis and I need to find a way to transfer that huge amount of data more quickly.

I am also thinking about purchasing a 23" Full HD 1080p resolution HDTV with HDMI so that I can connect my System76 Lemur Ultra Thin (lemu4) to the HDTV and I can use it as a second external monitor and TV to watch videos, movies, etc. I don't need a 10 finger touch monitor. The trouble is that I don't have enough physical room in my bedroom to fit it in.

If SSD prices continue to fall, then I might purchase a 2.5" SATA-III 6 GB/s MLC NAND FLASH 512 GB SSD next year and I will re-install Ubuntu and all of my software applications from scratch with 12.04.x 64 bit LTS. I am looking to pay around $250 - $300 USD for one sometime in 2013, but this is not a priority. Amazon is selling a Crucial M4 SATA-III 512 GB SSD for $369 - $400 USD right now. Who knows? SATA-IV 9 GB/s technology might become available by Christmas 2013. I might be able to snag a deal on an older SATA-III 6 GB/s SSD at that time in the future. However, this is not a priority for me at the current time.

Finally, I hope that Ubuntu continues to make improvements in energy efficiency and battery life with future LTS releases. If I can gain an extra two more hours of battery life with my current PC hardware and Ubuntu 16.04 64 bit LTS, then that would be great. As it stands right now, I can get about 4 hours of battery life using the Jupiter app. This is important for me as I will be in classrooms studying or teaching English literature to undergraduate students after I graduate with my masters degree and I land a full-time job. Classrooms don't come with enough AC outlets nearby.

Your post is very long!

One thing in it I noticed is that you are thinking about getting a monitor. 

I would highly recommend looking at IPS monitors. Some IPS monitors have better colour capability (bits per colour) than others. IPS monitors have much better colour than others. Unless you start overclocking your IPS monitor (there is a fast one at 120hz.net) your monitor will only refresh at 60 Hz. (60 frames per second.) That is fine, though you might notice the difference if you start playing first person shooter games.

A good one to get, not too expensive for what it is, is the Yamakasi Catleap. It is available on ebay. It would be shipped to you from Korea. People rave about them.
