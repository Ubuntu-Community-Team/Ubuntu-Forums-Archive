### Post by 3rdalbum on 2008-04-21
Agreed about NTFS. It's reasonable to assume that people will want to store DVD ISOs on their hard disks, and would assume a fault with the drive when vfat won't store anything past 4 gigabytes in a file.

The problem is with Seagate's "power-saving" mode. These hard disks not only spin down after 15 minutes of idle time, but they also drop off the USB bus, saving an additional 1 watt per year or so. When the disk is needed again, Windows simply reacquires it again at full USB 2 speed. All other USB-enabled operating systems actually implement the CORRECT behaviour: They think that the mounted device dropped off because of possible faults with the device or cable, and so to prevent data loss the operating system reacquires the drive at USB 1.1 "Full-speed". Slower, but more reliable. If the drive drops off again, it will be reacquired at USB 1.1 "Lo-speed", which is even slower.

For the people who are saying "Why does it matter, Linux only makes up 4% of the computer market": Firstly, reacquiring the drive at lower speeds is the CORRECT behaviour. Secondly, it's every non-Windows platform that has trouble with the Seagate drives, not just Linux. Thirdly, if Windows Vista SP2 or Windows 7 implements the same reliability function as every other operating system, that's going to break compatibility with the Seagate drive.

It's as dumb an idea as creating your website to test whether the user has Flash 9, and if they have any other version, deny them from seeing the site. What happens when Flash 10 comes out?

How to disable power management on FreeAgent drives on Linux system. 
 
1 - It is assumed in this example that the drive is attached to device /dev/sdc

 $ sudo sdparm -a /dev/sdc

3 - Force restart the drive ( if the drive was in stanby mode): 
 $ sudo sdparm --command=start /dev/sdc 
 
4 - Disable power management: 
 $ sudo sdparm --clear STANDBY -6 /dev/sdc
