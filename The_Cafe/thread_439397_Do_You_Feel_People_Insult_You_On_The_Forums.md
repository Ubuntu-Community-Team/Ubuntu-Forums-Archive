### Post by Tomosaur on 2007-05-10
> **kelvin spratt said:**
> kerry

I would have thought that not bringing attention to it would go some way to alleviating the problems. If you don't rise to the bait when you feel insulted, then eventually it will just stop bothering you.

I actually did a large piece of coursework on research into dyslexia - I find it very interesting, and actually thought I might have a very mild form of it (even now I find myself writing letters backwards, missing out words, swapping letters about etc). It doesn't bother me though, for the most part I'm pretty capable of writing fairly eloquently (rar rar!).

I did find some interesting techniques that may help dyslexic people though:
1) Use a high contrast environment when writing. It seems that blue is the best background colour to increase focus and thus alleviate some of the problems with dyslexia. Use blue paper instead of white (not great for a work environment, but fine for personal use), with a light coloured pen or pencil (not red, especially if you're male - unrelated to Dyslexia but apparently males have real trouble reading red text). This is far more easily accomplished with a computer - most word processors are capable of a high-contrast setting, so that the background is blue or black, and the text is white. You can also use a high contrast theme for your windows etc.

2) Listen to lyric-free music while you're writing / reading. There is evidence to suggest that music stimulates the parts of your brain controlling language - regardless of whether the music has lyrics or not. By getting into the habit of listening to music, you will make better associations with language and words. The reason for lyric-free music is because listening to lyrics forces most people to 'hear' the words, rather than treat them as just background noise. If you've ever spaced out when listening to music (ie, you can't really 'hear' the words, you just know that lyrics are being sung), then that is essentially what you should be aiming for. By eliminating the lyrics, there's on danger of you muddling them up with whatever it is you're trying to read / write.

As you're probably aware - none of these techniques are 'scientifically proven' to help dyslexia, but they're worth a try. Dyslexia is still barely understood at all.
