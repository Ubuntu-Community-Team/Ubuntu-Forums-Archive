Probably it does. It’s easy to think “if I had to learn all this, then dang it, everyone else should have to as well”.

The idea of “dumbing down” is a harmful one, but a useful way to think around it is to compare it with human intelligence. Who is smarter: the person who has a PhD in physics, or the person who has a PhD in physics *and* can explain that physics accurately in a way that laypeople can understand? Who is smarter: the politician who knows exactly what needs to be done, or the politician who knows exactly what needs to be done *and* can explain her plan accurately in a way that voters can understand?

In the same way, software that can deal with all the intricacies of package management, and present them in a way that 20 percent of users understand, is *not as smart* as software that can deal with all those intricacies and present them in a way that 30, or 50, or 70 percent of users can understand. So making software understandable for more people is not dumbing it down, it’s smartening it up. And with user testing, you can measure just how smart the software is in that respect.
