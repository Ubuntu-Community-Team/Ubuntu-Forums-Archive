### Post by dtfinch on 2010-05-09
I remember a time when most progress bars were fairly accurate, at least in the sense of number of tasks or volume of data. A good progress bar would use both stats, since a lot of little files usually take more time to process than a single file of equal total size. Some were bad, sticking at 99% for the last few minutes, usually an installer running some post-install script that it didn't include in its progress estimate, but it was better then nothing.

Nowadays, too many progress bars (thinking of Vista here) are entirely fake, starting out at a fast, predictable speed, even if nothing is happening, and gradually slowing down at a predetermined rate, never reaching their goal, until the task finishes at which time they jump to 100%. It's like they just have a policy of always displaying a progress bar, even though they rarely ever track progress. They're less than useless, like spam, pretending to have useful information, but producing none.

### Post by Meep3D on 2010-05-10
The reason for progress bars is because in the early days on computing if a program was doing something it would just sit there unresponsive until it finished.  Too many people thought the program had crashed and as a result reset the computer.  

Bootsplashes and other throbbers exist for largely the same reason, with progress bars used generally when the action takes a while (rather than a few seconds) as people get impatient with them, while a progress bar provides the illusion of progress (even if inaccurate) rather than just making the user wait for an indeterminate time.

---

### Post by Meep3D on 2010-05-10
Loadsplashes generally have a 'loading x' status on them to let you know they are doing something and exist so people don't launch the same app repeatedly as they think nothing is happening.

A common trick is to dimiss the loadsplash a few seconds before it's actually done to give the illusion that the program is actually loaded (when in reality it hasn't) to make things seem quicker.
