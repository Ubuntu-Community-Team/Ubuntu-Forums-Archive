1. It actually DOES have multitouch in the OS itself, it's just not supported/integrated into the OS yet. I'm not entirely sure why, maybe multi-touch patent issues? Don't quote me on that one. But anyways, there's not many apps that take advantage of the multitouch yet, but one of them is the Dolphin Web browser. It has multitouch zooming, just like Mobile Safari, which seems to be the most important part of having multitouch. Though, it's still not as fast as the iphone's multitouch. 

2. I don't think it really has a lack of Apps at all. If you take a look in the Android Market, there's plenty of apps, even the useless fart Apps. The iPhone's App Store just has a lot more because it's been out much longer. Android Apps will increase more and more within the next few years. 

However, I still believe the iPhone's overall quality of Apps are still well ahead, especially the games. But it should be long before Android games are caught up as well. Remember when the iPhone's App store just released?

3. Matter of preference, but I like the Droid's casing rather than the Nexus One's. 

4. It's only a slightly longer boot-up from the iPhone's boot-up. All within about 30-45 seconds. Coming from the Blackberry Storm, (which had about a 5min boot-up) this was a major leap forward. 

5. I can't comment too much on this. But I did hear of some bugs, such as Android locking out users when entering the wrong unlock pattern too many times, thus requiring them to sign into their Google Account, which wasn't working, even with the correct credentials. Google took forever (months) to get this fixed. There was a workaround for this involving having to press the home button rapidly after receiving an incoming call, or something like that. So maybe your statement on this is correct, but I haven't ran into any problems of the like yet. 

So my overall opinion is that I think iPhone is still a well-designed phone and still ahead of everyone in the market. It also has been out much longer than the Android which has only been out for a year and a half? iPhone still has it's boundries, and you can get the most fun out of it by Jailbreaking the phone. Android seems to have less limits, so it has much more potential, it's just still striving to work it's way there. 

Also, you don't really have much reason to root the phone, unless you want to install certain apps and themes. The Android has no problem letting you install 3rd party apps outside the market, just by enabling a setting in the phone itself. As for tethering, there's an App in the market itself, called "PDAnet" that allows you to tether, though I don't believe Linux is supported yet. I use it on my Windows Netbook. (btw, I don't think Verizon or any network supports tethering without paying their 'tethering fee's, I just find it a little surprising a tethering Apps is in the App Store)

Anyways, that's my fair opinion on both phones. I think an Android phone is your next best option after the iPhone, especially if you are on Verizon(in the US). OP, if you're stuck on ATT due to wanting to keep your number, then might as well go with the iPhone. If you don't care about changing your number and want an Android phone, get a Droid from Verizon or wait until Spring when Nexus One is available on Verizon. Have you used Android before enough to make a rational decision about it?

### Post by aysiu on 2010-01-10
If you like AT&T, I guess it's just a matter of how long you can wait. Apparently, AT&T is planning to add Android phones to its line-up in the next nine months:
[AT&T Adds Android, Palm to Its Lineup](http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704842604574642414052259406.html?mod=rss_Today's_Most_Popular)

I've used both the iPhone and an Android phone extensively, and if anyone cannot see both *pros* **and** *cons* in both, she definitely needs to spend time with both kinds of phones.

Here are some basics I would list (not comprehensive) for you to consider:

**iPhone**

*pros* [list][*]Touch sensitivity is excellent.[*]Lack of multi-tasking is actually a plus performance-wise.[*]If you have OS X or Windows, integration with iTunes is essential to a positive experience.[*]At this point, anyway, apps tend to have more polish. Yelp!, Facebook, and Skype, for example, are all more fully featured on the iPhone than for Android phones.[*]The little magnifying glass for placing the cursor to edit words when typing is nice.[*]I don't know if this is true for the Nexus One, but at least comparing the iPhone 3G to the MyTouch 3G, I can definitely say the iPhone takes pictures more quickly (less time between hitting camera button and camera actually taking pictures, less time between taking a picture and being able to take another picture afterwards).[*]The swiping back and forth between pictures is very smooth. In fact, all animation and transitions are very smooth.[/list] *cons* [list][*]If you don't have Windows or OS X or if you don't like iTunes, finding ways to sync files to the iPhone will not be easy. There are workarounds, though.[*]The Google Voice for the iPhone got rejected by Apple. In order to install non-Apple-approved apps requires jailbreaking the phone. Many folks argue jailbreaking is easy, but Apple keeps fighting the jailbreaking iPhone-hacking community. Google does not fight the rooting Android-hacking community.[/list]

**Android**

*pros* [list][*]Supports Linux, Mac, and Windows out of the box. Also supports both MP3 and Ogg out of the box.[*]Excellent support for Google services (obviously), including Calendar, GMail, Voice, and Contacts.[*]A lot of great free apps.[*]Ability to easily make ringtones out of any song.[*]If you have a phone with good hardware specs, multi-tasking is nice.[*]Ability to open windows in the background in browser.[*]Rooting is easy and enables multi-touch (which is neat but highly overrated, yet, even on the iPhone, where the implementation is much better than on Android) and Apps2SD for those who want to install a million apps.[*]Being able to have a wallpaper photo instead of just a bunch of app icons is nice.[*]You can send certain contacts straight to voicemail (say, wrong numbers), and if you have Google Voice, you can straight-out block certain people from calling.[*]When you're typing with the onscreen keyboard, you get multiple word suggestions for autocomplete, not just one.[/list] *cons* [list][*]Some little niggles in the interface in Android need to be worked out. In certain dialogues, you have to tap on the text field in order for the onscreen keyboard to pop up. Android should automatically detect if you have a hard keyboard and have it open. If not, the onscreen keyboard should always pop up automatically when a text field is obviously what you're going to be typing in.[*]I got a chance to play with the Motorola Droid, and its touchscreen interface is much more sensitive than the one on my phone (the MyTouch 3G), but it's still not as sensitive as the iPhone touchscreen.[*]Android Market interface needs a major overhaul. The ability to sort apps or filter in various ways would be great. All we have now is Featured, Top Free, Top Paid, and Just In.[*]If you don't have and don't want a Google account, some of the phone's best features will be useless to you.[/list] Those are just some things that popped into my head. That's not a comprehensive list of pros and cons.

I happen to love the iPhone. I think Apple did an excellent job with it. My two major reasons for going with an Android phone were (1) I use Linux exclusively, so I don't have iTunes, which means syncing with an iPhone would not be fun; and (2) I love Google Voice, and having the application installed makes the GV experience. You can sort of kind of use it with the iPhone, but it's not fully integrated.
