### Post by freebeer on 2009-05-16
The original question has, I think, a serious mis-understanding of economics and how it works.

The history of economics has always been about the delivery of goods and services at lower/faster/better rates.  The more people who can take advantage of the availability of these goods and services, the more productive/creative, etc. they can be.  Which in turn creates more goods and services, improves quality and longevity of life, etc.  There's a cascading effect that benefits all.

Let's take the eye glasses example.  Let's say a pair of eye glasses costs $100 retail.  The retailer needs to buy them from the manufacturer at, say, $50.  He also provides the service of fitting them out, choosing the right prescription, etc.  So he nets $50 for himself for his service.  Note that the goods and service price is bundled together and appears to be $100, but it's really $50 hardware, $50 services.  

Magically one night the glasses become free.  Since the retailer has no hardware costs, he can drop his price to $50 and still make the same money per customer.  The customer, however, can buy 2 pairs now with the same $100.  Now maybe two of his kids can learn to read, or otherwise achieve more as a result of having access to glasses that wouldn't otherwise be available to both.  Two people become more productive instead of one.

Conversely, our glass retailer is a money-grubbing scum bucket and decides to continue to charge $100.  He now makes an extra $50 per customer.  He's got an extra $50.  Does he salt it away in a tin can somewhere?  No, typically he'll use it to buy additional goods and services to improve his quality of life.  Maybe he goes to a restaurant, or hires a kid to mow his grass.  Whatever is chosen, that $50 gets recirculated into the economy.  The kid now has $50 to buy stuff, too.

Obviously this is a very simplified model, but it illustrates the multiplicative effect that lower costs has on the economy as a whole.  Economics isn't a zero-sum game.
