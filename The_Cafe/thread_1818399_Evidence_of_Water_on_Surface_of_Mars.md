Yes, you're both right that Jupiter's magnetosphere deflects the sun's charged particles and that within the magnetosphere you are safe from the solar wind. The problem is that the solar wind is the least of your worries. The charged particles are actually created by Jupiter's moon Io during volcanic eruptions. The particles are forced into a torus by the might of Jupiter's magnetic fields. The magnetic fields, in turn, are loaded with plasma from the particles.  Jupiter's magnetosphere, in effect, traps and concentrates the charged particles. You, my friends, would be like two nerdy lobsters who thought they would be safe from the cook stove's flames if they jumped into the boiling water. ;)

> **Bandit said:**

I was thinking about this after my last post. Once you've begun to shield the planet from light, you would want to start breaking down the sulfuric acid as quickly as possible. The cooling, scientists say, would take mere decades. The cooling would be hastened by breaking down the atmosphere (and there are theories for doing this). But it gets even cooler because you don't have to wait to colonize Venus. At about 50 kilometers high, the atmospheric pressure is the same as that on earth. This means we could create cloud cities until the rest of the planet cools. A [balloon of oxygen and nitrogen]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonization_of_Venus"), for example, would float the way a helium balloon floats on earth. Humans wouldn't need to wear pressure suits in the upper reaches of Venus's atmosphere, only protection from the sun and presumably the atmosphere's acidity (which at that level is not so high as to be fatal). Also, freeing the water (and converting the other compounds to water) from the atmosphere would readily lower the atmospheric pressure until it was much like earth's. (If all the water on earth were to boil into the atmosphere, our surface pressure would be like that of Venus.)

The whole problem with Mars is that it's atmosphere is vapor thin and even if one were to somehow add to it, the solar wind and the planet's weak gravity would soon give it up.
