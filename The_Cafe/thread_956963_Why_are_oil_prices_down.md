### Post by Giant Speck on 2008-10-24
> **SuperSonic4 said:**
> +1

Did anyone else notice that prices didn't fall nearly as fast or as far as the price of crude oil though :mad:

Do you mean to say gas prices didn't fall nearly as fast or as far as the price of crude oil?

[IMG]http://i279.photobucket.com/albums/kk122/SpecKtacle/chgaschart-3.gif[/IMG]

On July 4, 2008, the price of one barrel of light sweet crude oil was $145.29.  On October 24, 2008, the price is down to $64.34 per barrel.
That is a 55.7% decline.

On July 17, 2008, the average price of a gallon of regular unleaded gasoline in the United States was $4.11.  On October 24, 2008, the price is down to $2.78 per gallon.
That is a 32.6% decline.

Crude oil prices and gasoline prices don't fall together.  A decline in gasoline prices is the consequence of falling crude oil prices and therefore takes place after it is established that oil prices are falling.  The fall in gasoline prices is delayed.

That's also why gasoline prices don't spike at the same time oil prices do. If oil was to skyrocket after a day of trading, you won't see a significant rise in gasoline prices until several days later.

### Post by Erdaron on 2008-10-24
It could be several things, and decrease in demand due to a slowing economy is just one of them. With Saudi Arabia leaving OPEC, I think competition in the oil supply market will get tighter, leading to lower prices. Also, with natural gas (of which Russia, non-OPEC state, is a major supplier in the European energy market) becoming a more popular source of energy, competition of oil in the general energy market becomes tighter.

Discoveries of massive oil fields in Brazil won't affect immediate prices, since that oil is years away from the market. However, the fact that it's coming might be one reason Saudi Arabia decided to leave OPEC to "[not leave a customer without oil]("http://blogs.moneycentral.msn.com/topstocks/archive/2008/09/11/the-death-of-opec.aspx")" - cash in on the oil while it still can.

As for gas prices not falling as far or as fast as prices of crude. Price of gasoline does not depend on price of crude alone - there are production and delivery costs that stay put. Plus distribution channels might be trying to use this time to increase their profit margins which were minimal while prices of gas were high. I'm not talking about ExxonMobils of the world, but rather local gas delivery companies and station owners. I remember reading an article (maybe a year ago) that in Florida, some gas station owners stopped selling gasoline because profits were just too small.

It has been brought to my attention that the price of oil we hear on the news is for a [contract to buy at a later date.]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futures_contract")  In other words, the refiners won't see the physical oil they are buying until at least next month.

If the refiners purchased oil contracts farther out (example: the oil they receive next week they purchased in July) they will be loosing money by selling cheaper gas.

Therefore, the cause of low oil prices could be due to an event anticipated in the near future.
