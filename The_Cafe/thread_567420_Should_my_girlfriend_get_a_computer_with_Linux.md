> 1. She does not have time to learn a new OS

2. If something stops working, she can't turn it in for service, since hardly anyone will know how to fix Linux

3. There is no support for it -- you see labels with "works with Windows" all over the place, or even "works with Mac", but there's hardly ever any reference to Linux

4. If she wants to get a job, and she doesn't know how to use Vista, only Linux, they won't give it to her since "all businesses use only Windows, not Linux"

I have installed Ubuntu on my parent's laptop, my mother-in-laws computer, I bought a laptop with Ubuntu installed for both my wife and brother and one of my friends has swapped over to Ubuntu full time following my introducing it to him (as a dual boot at first).

Here is what I have found:

1. The learning curve is no steeper than it would be for Vista (including problems with application compatibility).

2. Open source software rarely stops working on its own, it is usually something that you've done to it.  Because basic users tend not to dig into things like Synaptic, this is rarely a problem.  If the hardware breaks then the warranty should be honoured anyway.

3. True, but on the up side, the majority of things like webcams and external hard drives work by simply plugging them, far easier than in ANY version of Windows.

4. Using Linux for any length of time will provide much better computer skills than using Windows, in my experience.  Linux tends not to hide the truth about what is being done, for example rather than entering an network password, it is a WPA passphase that is required to get into a wireless network.  Knowing a little bit of passing information about what your computer is doing and why is of great benefit.  Also, using Openoffice.org and then occasionally MS office (on a friends computer, etc... ) means that the basics of where applications tend to hide their functions will sink in, almost subconsciously.

As a final thought, I should point out that although all the people I have converted, use Ubuntu exclusively, most of them do have a Windows XP virtual machine installed for those "not available" applications.  In my case it is BBC iPlayer and 4oD, my mother in law needs TurboCAD, parents Photoshop, etc...  However these virtual machines don't store any files and are just used to run the required application.  On a modern laptop with Ubuntu installed the VM's often seem to work better than natively installed OS's (mainly because they only have a couple of applications installed on them).  The VM I use is VirtualBox and I have found it to be very easy and powerful to use.

The only real sticking point for you I guess, is that I do help out my various Ubuntu installs as and when they need it (for the mother-in-law and wife farely often).  The friend with Ubuntu installed however, can get along fine without me and he wasn't exactly what I would call a power user.  He just uses computers, but isn't afraid to give things a go.

Anyway, what I am really saying, is that I think it would be fine for your girl friend to get a Ubuntu laptop (and I think she will have a much better experience with it than with MS Windows), but only if she can get in hold of you, should she have an unexpected problem.  Though to be fair, most problems (wireless!!!) can be fixed by simply rebooting the computer.

Hope this helps.
