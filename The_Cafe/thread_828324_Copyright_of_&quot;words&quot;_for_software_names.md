### Post by KingTermite on 2008-06-13
I don't have a lot of "legal" mumbo jumbo to explain this, but this is something that has burned me for a while now.

How are software companies (or any company for that matter) allowed to copyright words that are already in common use? They name their software that common word and now you can't use that word without paying them a royalty fee. I mean WTF?

I (we) got burned by this about 4 years go when we created a control in Windows that would be used in our application. The control was a file navigation control. We were going to call it something that had the word "explorer" in the name. We found that we could not use that word in the name at all because Microsoft had a copyright on the word.

How the heck can you copyright a "word" that has been around since before your company ever existed? I don't mean were going to just call it "Explorer" like theirs, just use the word in the name somewhere.

And this isn't a Microsoft bash...other companies do the same thing. Try using "Photoshop" in your software's name or a thousand other names, I'm sure. See how far it gets you.

I've heard UPS has copyrighted their particular shade of brown so nobody else can use it. How is this stuff legal? Maybe, more appropriately, WHY is this stuff legal?

Maybe I should go out and copyright words like "document" and "file" and see if I can start making the big companies pay ME a royalty.

Ok, rant over. Not "bugging" me this moment, but something that I remembered today and find really annoying.

### Post by LaRoza on 2008-06-13
Trademarking words often fails. Intel learned they couldn't trademark a number, so then they made a name (Pentium)

Kodak, Google, etc are not real words and are easy to defend (google is actually a typo that worked out)

The trademarking of colours and symbols is common, but only enforced when it is (usually) logical. When a certain colour (like UPS) is an identifying mark, it would be misleading for other companies to use it.

It works for everyone, not just the big companies. The Ubuntu logo and name are trademarked, Firefox is, etc. It helps ensure the integrity of the name.
