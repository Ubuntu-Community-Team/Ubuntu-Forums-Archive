### Post by donkyhotay on 2010-06-07
Never understood the obsession with gold/silver. We practically base our economy off of it, we hoard it to manipulate prices, but ultimately except some uses in the electronics industry (which is a very recent development) it has practically no real use. Other minerals are rarer if you need something rare for economic purposes, but the value of gold seems to boil down to

1: it's shiny
2: it's valuable for exchange

and of course the only reason it's valuable for exchange is... well... because it's shiny? Seriously, I can just picture the following occuring in some prehistoric society

caveman1: Ooh! Shiny rock! <he picks it up>
caveman2: Ooh! Shiny rock! I want! If you give me rock I'll give you some food!
caveman1: Ok
caveman3: Hmmm... <caveman 2> gave something useful for worthless shiny rock. There must be some reason rock is valuable. Hey there <caveman 2> I'll give you club for shiny rock!
caveman2: Ok
Caveman4: Everyone is giving good stuff for shiny rock, I'd better get shiny rock too. I'll give best spear for shiny rock!

and this just continues on and on until you reach todays society. As has been stated before, never forget gold/silver can not be eaten, and besides being shiny and a great conductor of electricity, has no value whatsoever besides whatever arbitrary value we humans give it. If no one chooses to trade your gold/silver for something thats actually useful (like food or tools) you're left holding a rock that, no matter how shiny it is, is completely worthless. You can't even use it to make tools with like you can with other metals like bronze or iron.

### Post by alphaniner on 2010-06-07
> **donkyhotay said:**
> Never understood the obsession with gold/silver. We practically base our economy off of it...

In fact we no longer base our economy off it, which is part of (if not the root of) the reason we're in this mess.  But that's another discussion entirely.  To the matter at hand:

[INDENT]There are certain features of the original commodities that have become money historically. These features gave the original money metals an advantage over all of the other commodities that might have served as money, and in some cases historically have served as money.

There are five of these characteristics: **recognizability**, **divisibility**, **portability**, **high value in relation to weight and volume**, and **continuity of value over time**. Any physical commodity that possesses these five characteristics is a candidate to become the monetary commodity of a particular social order.

Historically, gold and silver have possessed these five characteristics to a greater degree than any other commodity. There have been rivals. Salt has been a rival. Animals have served as money in history. Even women have served as money in certain societies. (The problem with women as money is the problem of divisibility. Half a woman is not much use as a monetary unit.)

-Gary North, [[COLOR="Blue"]What is Money, Part 2[/COLOR]]("http://www.lewrockwell.com/north/north763.html")[/INDENT]

Granted, in a TEOTWAWKI situation, precious metals won't be of much use to you.  But in any situation less severe that that, few investments are more worthwhile.

### Post by fatality_uk on 2010-06-07
If you are serious, don't invest individually. You will never make a return like that. The small amount you have to invest would get eaten very quickly when you start trading in "genuine" commodities.

Look at investment clubs, speak to a dealer and/or a broker. Usually they will give you some free time (depending on your invested amount).

Learn more than you could ever want to know about which metals are in demand and which will be used in fabrication of consumer products over the next 3-5 years. They certainly wont be gold, they will be things you have never heard of, trust me!

Coins are a very shaky bet IMHO!
1. They can get stolen
2. They can be eaten by your dog
3. The can get lost
4. Unless they are MINT (I mean virtually UNUSED) and very RARE, the usually wont appreciate in any sort of meaningful way. They are nice as a collectors piece, but usually, traded coins from stores will never gain any real value.
