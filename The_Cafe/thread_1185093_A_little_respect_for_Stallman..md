Let's get this out of the way once and for all : 
The only thing that GPL enforces is that GPL'd programs remains under the GPL. 

If you write a program from scratch, you're free to use whatever license you want. 
But if  *you choose * to re-use existing code in a program you 're writing, you can only do so to the extend allowed by the license of that code. Under BSD-licenses, this may mean you have to preserve copyright notices. The GPL requires that the code remains GPL'd you an not allowed to re-distribute the code under a different license. 
If you have a problem with that, you're free to*** not*** re-use GPL'd code, but in stead write your own and license it whatever you want.

I checked out Stallman's wikipedia page and spent the day reading his [Selected Essays]("http://shop.fsf.org/product/free-software-free-society/"), and the significance of his life's work has dawned on me. Stallman selflessly quit his job to start GNU/linux, and has dedicated his life to it, and I am incredibly grateful that there are people in an individualistic society like ours willing to selflessly dedicate their time to collective efforts which help us all. Without copyleft like the GPL we would not have a free software movement, and without the movement neither we nor our grandkids would have ubuntu, wikipedia, and the millions of other things the future holds when we all work together to create something. Without organising ourselves, we can't prevent a minority from privatising, controlling what we do with it and preventing us access to it.

For clarifying this point, and reminding us of the ultimate goal - to have a completely free information infrastructure, whether it's science, law, cuisine, encyclopedias, or software - Stallman deserves respect. But lets keep it short of hero-worship, there are lots of people building the FS movement, and lots of people who have struggled throughout history for freedom from domination of all sorts.

### Post by brallan on 2009-06-17
> **Primož Papi&#269 said:**
> GPL is a good licence, but I sometimes think that BSD and MIT give more freedom - as in more freedom for developer to decide...

Actually, by the nature of software being thousands of little components each made by others, the end result is that public domain and non-copyleft licenses give LESS freedom, since the social freedom of protecting people's future access to this can be denied. Whenever we take away an individual's freedom for a social good, we make a trade-off. It's obvious that one must not allow a murderer the freedom to eat people or a lawyer the freedom to copyright laws or make them secret, because the social harm is so great - one loses the social freedoms of walking safe at night and knowing a judge's reasoning for sentencing someone to jail. There is a little individual freedom you give up when using GPL code. The trade-off is a far greater social freedom - free software movement would NOT exist without this encouragement. For more info, read [Copyright vs. Community]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/copyright-versus-community.html")
 
> **Primož Papi&#269 said:**
> the whole idea of FSF and GNU is similar to communism.
In Soviet Communism, people were prevented access to the copy machines, that's why [samizdat]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samizdat") came into being. Here's what RMS [has to say]("http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/shouldbefree.html") when folks ask him: “Why Don't You Move to Russia?”

> In the United States, any advocate of other than the most extreme form of laissez-faire selfishness has often heard this accusation. For example, it is leveled against the supporters of a national health care system, such as is found in all the other industrialized nations of the free world. It is leveled against the advocates of public support for the arts, also universal in advanced nations. The idea that citizens have any obligation to the public good is identified in America with Communism. But how similar are these ideas?

Communism as was practiced in the Soviet Union was a system of central control where all activity was regimented, supposedly for the common good, but actually for the sake of the members of the Communist party. And where copying equipment was closely guarded to prevent illegal copying.

The American system of software copyright exercises central control over distribution of a program, and guards copying equipment with automatic copying-protection schemes to prevent illegal copying.

By contrast, I am working to build a system where people are free to decide their own actions; in particular, free to help their neighbors, and free to alter and improve the tools which they use in their daily lives. A system based on voluntary cooperation and on decentralization.

Thus, if we are to judge views by their resemblance to Russian Communism, it is the software owners who are the Communists.
