A lot of that kind of feeling was reflected in the music. Jimi never hesitated to state his position on issues, and it's reflected in his music. I love his rendition of Bob Dylan's "All Along The Watchtower, " and songs like Machine Gun and his famous recording of "The Star Spangled Banner" clearly stated his ideals in a time of tremendous social upheaval. I woke up every day wondering if the next moment might be my last. Hence a lot of people lived "in the moment." At least subconsciously we were aware that any moment some body could "push the button." The open hedonism of those times is clearly understood by those of us who lived through them. It's not a mystery why some of the most talented and gifted of those times flamed out. It wasn't so much a matter of immorality as it was a sense that your next breath, and everybody else's, could be your's and their last.

When it comes to Zappa, there's no comparison. He was unique, a musical genius in his prime, and way ahead of all of us, even those of us who thought we got him. His mind operated on a different plane of existence,  translating his most creative ideas into a context that anybody else could even begin to understand. I wish I still had the Hot Rats albums I used to have, but that vinyl wore out long ago, and for some reason, I've never restored any of that stuff to my current collection. I started trying to become a bit of a jazz history buff, and sort of lost track of some of the music that sent me off in that direction.
