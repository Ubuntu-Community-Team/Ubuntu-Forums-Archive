### Post by LinuxIreland on 2010-06-04
Hi Ubuntuforumers! :-)

Just wanted to drop by and let you know that we've been growing an idea on  some social networking sites for a few days, gathered a good old set of community members and we're in the process of moving to our server to collaborate and build our learning environment (using Moodle). Here's an outline:


  **The Free Software University**


The free software university is a project focused on creating an international free software learning environment for programming, design and technology.

  All FSU project work will be released under the [AGPL]("http://www.gnu.org/licenses/agpl-3.0.html") and [Creative Commons]("http://creativecommons.org/") licenses. 

All FSU course material will be available under a [Creative Commons]("http://creativecommons.org/") license. No student will be refused enrolment to the FSU and no tuition fees will be charged.

There are no barriers to entry, simply a willingness to learn or teach. Instructors will be academically qualified - sourced form industry partners and volunteers, along with mentors with years of industry experience and numerous publications.


Being free.... doesn't make it easy ;-)


Lloyd :-)

### Post by LinuxIreland on 2010-06-04
Hi Kaldor,

There will be no requirements to begin, other than an XHTML browser.

We will support GNU/Linux, unlike most online courses - and we won't support proprietary operating systems, unlike all online courses, lol...

..this will be delivered through the browser. We're going to be using GPL's apps in the work, if required - so everything will be free (as in freedom) and we will make sure that they are easily available with no cost.

We are still in the process of formulating the course contents (although we already have instructors) but it depends if you're learning C++, BASH scripting, PHP, JavaScript, HTML/CSS or Office Applications of course. But if you were registered, you could directly contribute and tell us what you'd like. We'll be focusing the areas that have the most requirement and include business models of free software and the philosophy of free software.

But if you're using Ubuntu, I think it would be highly unlikely there will be anything you need that's not in Add/Remove (Ubuntu Software Center) - and if it's not there, it'll be in Synaptic - so... no worries on that side! :-)

We're lucky there's so much GPL out there, we should thank the FSF, lols... :-)))

Hope to see you there! Even if you want to contribute without studying or teaching, just hanging out and commenting is super welcome and useful!

Too many cooks... make loooads of food! :-)))

### Post by LinuxIreland on 2010-06-04
Hey Spoons!

> **spoons said:**
> OP, since the plan is not to charge tuition fees am I corect in saying that the tutors will be doing this pro bono? 

There are different types of 'tutor' in the plan - there are those qualified as, eg. BSc, MSc (and there may even be some PhD's work in the course materials) *winks* - which is cool.

Much like the development of Free Software, we have devided that, to accellerate innovation, we'd like to partner our idea with industry so students can work directly on 'real-world' projects. This also means that industry benefits. So, we propose 2 things:

1) To protect the freedom of the student's work, the projects will _have_ to be AGPL - licensed and 
2) The industry partners contribute by providing some 'graduate hours', giving us qualified lecturers and instructors in the courses.

The 'other types' of tutors include GNU/Linux hackers who have been in the industry since before I was born and well - they don't have a (computer science) degree as - they didn't exist back then! But they are excited about teaching and we want to let them! :-))

So, qualificatrion + experience. Who said you should only have one tutor? :-))

> **spoons said:**
> Secondly, will you be making availiable samples of courses for people who are unsure whether to enrole?

Yes, we're super new - and in the process of defining courses and making course material - but I'd like to see the ability to see the entire course (as the coursework will be AGPL licensed anyway), excluding tests and to be able to interact with current / previous students. So, in brief (which i'm quite poor at) - definitely! :-)

Hi Spoons - yes, there's a plan afoot :-)

In the first instance, we will be offering short courses (which we hope to build together in a modular way to make longer courses of study).

The focus will be to provide a high standard of study in the aim for each module to be the equivalent (academically) as a professional certification, eg. (dare I say it) "Microsoft certified Professional".

### Post by McRat on 2010-06-30
> **kingbirdy said:**
> I signed up, and I must say, this looks quite interesting. I don't suppose yo have an ETA on the courses starting? Also, may I recommend you do some courses in MS-DOS and the Ubuntu Terminal, as I am but one of many who would like to learn such things.

While DOS is now available free and MS doesn't seem to complain, it's never been Open Source other than the fact it's the most heavily reverse engineered program(s) in history.

If you can find old Peter Norton books (Norton AV started with one REALLY smart guy) you can learn as much about DOS as perhaps anyone currently working at MS does.

It's a dead OS but is still the best O/S ever for floppy disk generation.  It's death was memory and HDD size address limitations and the fact it wasn't re-entrant safely.  You couldn't call INT21h from inside another active instance reliably?  

I never learned more about DOS than I needed to know to get the job done.  But something to ponder:  Microsoft was never as good with DOS as their users were.  The "outsider" DOS gurus made it do tricks, then MS would follow.  DOS was limited to addressing 640k worth of RAM, and in order to make it address more, outsiders created DOS Extender libraries.  Phar Lap was perhaps the most common.  And multi-tasking apps in DOS were first developed by outsiders IIRC.  They wrote Terminate & Stay Resident (TSR) programs that could make the machine have more than one program active at the same time.  The first DOS viruses were TSR's, and that's how Peter Norton cut his teeth on Anti-Virus software.  It was free back then, and documented, not sure if the source was distributed, but I believe you could get it.

I often wonder what would have happened if DOS would have gotten "fixed" back then.  A modern computer running 64-bit extended DOS would be brutally fast.
