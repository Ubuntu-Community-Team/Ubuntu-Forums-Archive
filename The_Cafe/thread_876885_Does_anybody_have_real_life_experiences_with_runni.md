### Post by JLB on 2008-08-01

### Post by timcredible on 2008-08-01
if it's a really small company, you can make it a pass-through for tax purposes so that the company makes no money and pays no taxes, all profits pass-through to you, and you pay normal taxes, it's easier that way.  the biggest surprise for most people is that social security is really 12.4%, not 6.2%.  if you're a w2 employee you don't see that because you pay 6.2% and the company pays 6.2% on your behalf.  but if you're self employed, you have to pay the entire 12.4%.  add in 3.9% for medicaire/medicaid, at least 20% for federal, at least 3% for state, 1% for local, you might as well plan on paying 40% for taxes, and 20% on insurance (for the company and for you), etc. if you go the self-employed, 1065, or 1099 route.  if you want to really know the correct details, you're going to have to pay someone to lay out the info for you.

### Post by Mad_Dawg on 2008-08-01
> **jingo811 said:**
> Is there any disadvantages when donating money to charities for "tax deduction" reasons?

**Never** make a donation or buy equipment **just** to get the tax deduction! If you spend/give $1000 as a deduction, you will reduce your taxable income by $1000. However, if you are looking at a 30% tax rate, you tax bill will only be reduced by $300.  Spending $1000 to save $300 is not good business.  If you were going to make the purchase/donation because it is something that you need or believe in, then go for it.  The tax break is gravy.

The exception to this is if a small donation or purchase will put you in a lower tax bracket in order to lower the percentage that you owe.  Run the numbers first, be sure that that the expenditure is not greater than the tax savings.
