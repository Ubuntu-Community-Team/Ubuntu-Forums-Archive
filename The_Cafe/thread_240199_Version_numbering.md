### Post by prizrak on 2006-08-20
Versioning by date makes sense to me. Consider this, someone find out about Ubuntu and wants to try it. They see there is a Dapper 6.06, which is easily translated into June 2006, so they know that this particular version came out  a bit over 2 months ago. If they are Googling they migh find Breezy or even Warty. Now since the version tells them when it came out they know exactly how old it is. Now make Warty Ubuntu 1.0 and Dapper Ubuntu 4.0 (it's the 4th release right?). You got no clue when it comes out. 

I'll make it worse, there is alot of debate over what should even be called a 1.0. The first release of the Linux kernel was 0.19 (I think, was not 1.0 for sure), which suggested that it is a very raw piece of programming. One could argue that Dapper should be called 1.0 because it's the first LTS version. Others may argue that Wary was excellent (and it really was) and could be a 1.0. 

Date versioning system also helps with releases such as Edgy. Edgy is not meant to be a stable enterprise OS, it's more of a technological demo and enthusiast system. In the conventional system it would have to be called a 4.5 or something along those lines. However Ubuntu releases a new version every 6 months (conventionally understood as a major version) so there could be issues with that (Ubuntu doesn't deliver on promises and such). 

The date system is very impartial and suggests nothing about stability/readiness of the OS, it simply states when it was released. This way there is no preconcieved notion of how ready the system is. 

Hell when I saw MythTV 0.19 I thought to myself "damn I wonder if it's even usable" then I went to their site and saw that it's a serious piece of programming work that rivals just about anything I have seen on the Media Center front.
