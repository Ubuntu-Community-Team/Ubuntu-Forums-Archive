### Post by tsali on 2009-08-02
Steve is correct insofar as commercial hardware manufacturers would like to work with a standard driver model.

The LSB was a good start.

However, for those of you who want the hardware vendors to "just release the specs" and let other developers make drivers:

1. A lot of the innards of this hardware may be driven by OTHER'S proprietary code - they are not free to release the code with opening themselves up to liability.

2. The software drivers can make or break the user experience with the hardware. When drivers do not work, who do think is going to get blamed? Who's reputation suffers? The vendors must control their driver code to protect themselves.

3. In some cases, there may be legal commitment associated with the driver code. For example, a wireless card may only be FCC compliant when used with the associated vendor supplied drivers. Releasing hardware specs or driver code may result in the device being used inappropriately, in a way the the vendor can be held liable.

4. Lastly, in the hardware world, money talks. There is risk associated with supporting open source. They have to have some assurance that rewards will be sufficient to offset it.

But the argument is about more than that.  It's about the nature of language and its evolution.  Look at any language, and you'll see that the meaning of words changes as time passes.  A word may initially mean one thing; but people may start to use that word to mean something else.  More and more people use the word in its new sense; and finally the dictionaries record its new meaning.  Language changes as its users use it differently.

"Linux" originally meant the kernel.  But, as the user base has grown and become less specialized, those users have used "linux" to refer to the wider operating system.  More people now use "linux" to mean the operating system than those who adhere to its original sense.  Thus, the meaning of the word has changed: "linux" is the operating system.
 
And look! the dictionaries have changed to reflect the new meaning.  From [the google results for "define linux"]("http://www.google.co.uk/search?hl=en&safe=off&defl=en&q=define:linux&ei=x0h3St-BIdShjAfGm-GoBg&sa=X&oi=glossary_definition&ct=title"):

["a UNIX-like operating system that was designed to provide personal computer users a free or very low-cost operating system"]("http://www.primode.com/glossary.html");

["A GUI based open source operating system that has emulated the UNIX architecture"]("http://library.thinkquest.org/06aug/02257/glossery.html").

There are also definitions of the old school, that linux is the kernel.  But these appear to be the exception, not the rule.
