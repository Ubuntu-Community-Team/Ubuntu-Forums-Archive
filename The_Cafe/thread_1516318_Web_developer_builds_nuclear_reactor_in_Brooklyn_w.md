Mark Suppes is a Web developer for the fashion house Gucci, but in his off-hours, he tinkers with his homemade nuclear-fusion reactor in a Brooklyn warehouse, unknown to his neighbors. In a video interview with BBC News' Matthew Danzico, Suppes cheerfully shows the reporter how his reactor works, warning him to step back as the machine activates and emits a small amount of radiation. The reporter is heard laughing nervously in the background, telling Suppes to be careful.

Suppes' is the 38th homemade reactor built by an independent amateur scientist, according to Fusor.net. On that website, "fusioneers" discuss attempts to create a nuclear-fusion reactor that produces as much energy as it consumes, a problem that has plagued some of science's greatest minds...

### Post by Miguel on 2010-06-24
> **McRat said:**
> I remember when physicists said that superconductors could only work at very close to 0 K.  

But they are continually raising the temp that you can make one work at.

Someday, perhaps they will be able to do it at room temp.

The problem with superconductivity is that no one really understands how it works for the High-Tc materials (All those fancy YBaCuO compounds, or 1-2-3). There is a rough idea, but no more than that. On the other hand, for "classical" superconductivity (The one in Hg, Al, Nb and AFAIK all pure elements), the mechanism is [pretty well known since 1957](http://prola.aps.org/abstract/PR/v108/i5/p1175_1).

There have been some efforts to find "classical" superconductors with a high Tc based on hydrogen, but at very high pressure, for a while. This is because metallic hydrogen should become a superconductor with an impressively high temperature of 240 K (-33ºC or -30ºF). Nevertheless, I don't think anything higher than 20K has been found yet.

Finally, the trend in the last two years is researching some compound of iron and arsenic, called the iron pnictides. These are apparently a mixture of classical and high-tc superconductors. These could help us understand the mechanisms better or create a better material. Who knows?
