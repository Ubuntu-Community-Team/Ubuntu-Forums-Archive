### Post by Fludizz on 2010-08-12
I've been using Opera since version 6 and never stopped using it since ;) However I do not use the Turbo option ;) I like my pages with all images and layouts as the site designer intended :P (and don't like the data-intercepting possibilities turbo provides). I have FireFox installed in case someone needs to look something up on the internet using my laptop (it is permanently set in privacy mode for that reason). I personally am not too fond of firefox. Yeah the adblock plugin is great but that's the only + I could find. It just feels a lot slower when compared to Opera.

I did have minor issues with Opera and google, those where related to the IPv6 / IPv4 usage of the browser. Opera had a bug where it did not fall back to IPv4 on the first attempt to reach a site, forcing you to try to access it at least twice. This resulted in broken SSL connections and me being unable to access gmail and other google services when I was in a non-IPv6 network. (My home network is fully IPv6 ready and before the end of this year I'll have native IPv6 connectivity from my ISP. Otherwise I would've disabled IPv6 operating system wide as a workaround.)

Last version of Opera fixed that issue :P

Btw, Google is my main resource when starting with trouble shooting :P Although the result quality seems to have degraded a bit... it used to be easier to find a correct page. There's a lot more search-result faking going on.

### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2010-08-12
I do Google. I was playing with [cuil]("http://cuil.com/") and [cpedia]("http://www.cpedia.com/") recently, though, and they're really quite nifty services.

I think Google's recent problems spring from the same dilemma everyone faces: they cater exclusively to people who assume that because something is on the Internet they shouldn't need to pay for it at all. In doing so, they are perpetuating that belief. Like any web service, I would prefer they had a subscription option. It could go like this:

[&#10004;] I would like to pay for the resources I use myself, so please withdraw advertising and creepy behaviour from my account.

Dropping Google wouldn't change anything; it would just move the problem to another provider. It happens all over the place. That needs to change, of course, but switching to the nearest &#8220;free&#8221; solution isn't going to do it. We need something different.

To Google's credit, they have *real* non-creepy revenue via some parts of YouTube, business services, Android and other things unrelated to their search engine. That's why they are a lot less creepy than, say, Facebook, and in most cases just plain excellent. I think there's hope for them in the privacy front. It seems they *can* sustain themselves without creepy behaviour. Granted, there's still the looming problem that their income is being earned from something completely detached from their expenses (search?), but maybe they see a connection where I don't.
