### Post by Meep3D on 2010-03-06
> **JohneG said:**
> Why do people reckon Photoshop is better than GIMP? And let's exclude the fact that it's 8 bit for now.
The interface takes a disproportionatley large area of the screen - the toolbox having panels in it is just silly.  The toolboxes also disappear behind what you're working on so your canvas must be smaller than the big toolboxes.

No CMYK, making it useless for printing.

No layer groups (folders) making it a nightmare to deal with large amounts of layers and to be able to treat layers as a group - in PS I can put 5 layers in a group and call it 'Button', then just duplicate that layer and call it 'Button 2'.  In PS I would have to duplicate and track all 5 layers manually.  Add 10 buttons and that is 50 layers.  PS folders are colapsible and heirarchial too so I could just have a main folder called 'buttons', rather than 50 layers in Gimp.

No non-destructive layer effects.  Drop shadow, stroke, glow, colour overlay, gradient overlay, bevel + emboss can all be added non-destructively to a layer, adjusted in realtime and update automatically as the layer updates and does not require rasterization as in Gimp.  You can also copy and paste layer stylings, for example I have the perfect layer style of drop shadow, stroke and gradient I can copy and paste that from one element/button/window to another, where with Gimp you'd be SOL if you didn't remember exactly what you did and it would take an age repeating things on every element.

No recording macros - I can record an action in PS easily and then just 'play' it at any time to repeat.  Script-fu is hardly a replacement for this.  Script-fu is also painfully slow.  I am not sure why.

The utterly unnecessary micromangement of layer sizes is also pretty painful, for example create a new 300x300 image, create 5 layers, increase the canvas size to 400x400 (I think there is a tickbox now at least).  The layers themselves will still be 300x300, requiring you to *increase the layer size to the canvas size*.  I have utterly no idea why on earth you'd need a smaller layer size than a canvas size (it's certainly not useful for masking) and have never seen it in any other app, but it requires constant micromanagement in Gimp.

No 'smart objects' which allow you to preserve the original image through transforms (such as resized), allowing you to safely enlarge previously shrunk objects (such as logos, images) without having to re-import the original.

It's just not very good, in fact it's not even at the level of Photoshop 5 yet.  The biggest problem though is that their appears to be no hope in sight, Paint.NET is arguably better than Gimp - it's much, much easier to use, despite the fact that Gimp has been in development for 15 years.  It occupies that ugly middle ground where it has the complexity of a high end app but fundamentally lacks the abilities.

Gimp is to Photoshop what MS-DOS is to Bash.  Sure to someone who has used nothing but MS-DOS, Bash is 'pretty much the same', but for someone who uses Bash extensively MS-DOS would be like having your legs cut off.

### Post by Meep3D on 2010-03-06
The text tool is also woefully inadequate in Gimp.  In Photoshop you have precise controls over leading, kerning, faux bold + italic, drop caps, uppercase plus multiple antialias methods (a godsend for small point sizes).  In Gimp (last time I checked) you can't even apply bold or italic in the same textblock as normal text - you must put the bolded text on a whole new layer.  Doing "This is **bold** and *italic* text" in Gimp would require 5 layers.

I think my hatred of Gimp largely stems from the fact that I get told by someone I should use it 'because it's better than Photoshop' every couple of days, despite it being at least a decade behind technologically.

Free software should be promoted because it's *better*, not promoted on the basis of "it's better because it's free".  It should definitely not be promoted to people unless you understand their needs and the software in question as often all that happens is they end up thinking "this free software stuff is crap".
