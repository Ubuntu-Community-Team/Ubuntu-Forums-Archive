I know that in pre-Shuttle times, getting stuff into space was terribly expensive and wasteful.  Take a look at a Saturn V rocket.  All that metal, just to get that tiny pod on the nose cone into orbit.

NASA really played on the "reusable" angle of the Shuttle.  I remember seeing a publicity shot of *Enterprise* sitting on the back of a jumbo jet, and in my childish naivety I thought that was actually how they were going to be launched - taken high up on the back of a 747, then it'd power up and fly the rest of the way itself!  I was quite disappointed when I saw what a pre-launch Shuttle actually looked like.  Reusable, yes.  But very wasteful too.

Anyway, I don't think reusability is a big factor nowadays.  The ESA, the Chinese, and whoever else (have Japan got a space programme now?) seem to be getting on fine using low-cost rockets.  I don't think NASA will disappear any time soon; but even if NASA *did* vanish in a puff of smoke, the rest of the world would continue regardless.  I know it's hard for some people to accept, but America is *not* the centre of the universe, nor is NASA the be-all and end-all of space exploration.  I'm just hoping that someone will aspire to something beyond low earth orbit before I die.  The rover pictures are all well and good, but I really want to see footage of humans on Mars before I die.

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-07-08
> **Dustin2128 said:**
> Started to panic with the problem at 31 seconds pre-launch. Thought for a second that it was a waste to wake up so early (10) ;).

I was working at NASA when STS-1 launched in 1981. I helped troubleshot the problem that delayed the launch for 2 days. Just before launch, 4 of the 5 on-board computers take control of the launch, and all five computers have to be on-line before it is "a go" for launch. It was finally determined that the backup (5th) on-board computer had the wrong version of software installed. That caused the backup (5th) computer to not come on-line when it was scheduled to be; therefore, "no go" for launch. 

During the years that I worked at NASA, I saw things and heard about things that happened. All I can say is, you just can't make stuff like this up. :) Some of it is really hard to believe unless you have actually been there to see it. Some of the things we learns from the mistakes have benefited the medical, mechanical, electronic, software, and quality assurance fields significantly.

When the delay occurred at T-31 seconds, I was thinking, NOT AGAIN. That is close to the time that the computer check occurs if I remember correctly. Of course that was 30 years ago so things have probably changed. I was relieved when I heard that they were just checking that the boom and been fully retracted.
