That question left too many variables unaccounted for.  It did not allow for "the law of large nubmers" to be applied, and therefore could not actually be solved using probability calculations without a lot of extra data (the guy's genetics, for example).  It should have been phrased as follows:
> "A very large country only allows couples to have two children.  Assuming that any given child has an equal chance of being born male or female, what percentage of the families who are known to have two children, at least one of which is a boy, actually have two boys?
The answer would be "one-third".



But now try this slight variation (the only difference being that the second child has not been born yet):
> A large country only allows couples to have two children.  Assuming that any given child has an equal chance of being born male or female, what percentage of the families who have already had a boy and will in fact have a second child are going to end up with two boys?
I say the answer now is "one-half".

### Post by McRat on 2010-06-03
Eh.  Even by their own reasoning, they are wrong.  But Tuesday is important for totally different reason than they think.

I'm going with semantics and sequencing for 49.3% Alex.

The assumption that if you have two children, and one is a boy, so the odds are 1/3 of two boys is really bad reasoning.  There are not 4 possibilities.  One item is determined, it is not variable.  Only one variable exists.  It's a sequencing problem.  So the odds are 50/50 assuming even sex distribution.

Simple version: 

One child is born, it is a boy on Tuesday.  Odds are 100% it's a boy.
Second child is born, 50% chance of boy.  Odds are 100% * 50% = 50%

One child is born, unknown gender.  Odds are 50% it's a boy.
Second child is born, it's a boy on Tuesday.  Odds are 100% it's a boy.  100% * 50% = 50%.

OK, so it is never 1/3.  Unless you believe that a slot machine with 1% odds has better odds after you pull the handle 100 times without winning.  (hint, you don't).  Every sequenced event is it's own odds.  You pull the handle 100 times, and your next pull is still 1% odds.  Pull 101 slot machine handles concurrently and your odds are pretty dang good.  Sequence is very important.

Now, saying "One is a boy born on a Tuesday" is not saying "One and only one boy was born on Tuesday".  There is a mathematical difference between the two phrases.

"One is a boy born on a Tuesday.  The other is another boy born on a Tuesday."  Is completely valid according to the exact wording.

But OK, let's assume they have additional unprinted rules that exclude two Tuesday boys.

They are STILL wrong.  Sequence comes into play if you are saying one and only one boy is born on Tuesday.  And with sequencing comes parallel events, just like the 101 Slot Machines.  Twins are parallel events.

Twins are roughly 1:50 odds.  In any "one and only one" scenario, twins are excluded.

So not only are second Tuesday births invalid, so are all twins, regardless of birthdate.  Twin boys on Wednesday are a fail.

So the odds are reduced another 2% of 2 boys due to sequence.

Now assuming both boys COULD be born on Tuesday (this is how it's worded), it's not really 50/50 because twins born on days other than Tuesday are eliminated.

Twins are 2% of all births.  Twins born on WThFSSM are eliminated.  So 50%-(2% / 7)= 49.3%
