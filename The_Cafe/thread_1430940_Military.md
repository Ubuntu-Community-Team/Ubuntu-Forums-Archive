### Post by HH60Gunner on 2010-03-16
I am currently in the USAF, and as my screen name implies I am an aerial gunner on a combat search and rescue helicopter.  Although we're not spec ops, we do have a pretty specialized and fun job.  As for my feelings it's kind of two sided, there are things I love and things that I hate.  I started off in the AF as a communications computer systems controller, but back in 2006 when the AF cut back on 40k people my job was the most overmanned so they were not allowing people in my job to re-enlist in that job.  So I retrained into the aerial gunner career field.  My job is a blast and one of the most rewarding jobs, when I am actually doing my job.  However since the gunner career field is a bit overmanned right now for helicopters we find that our additional duty quickly becomes our main job, and the gunner part is just what we do on the side.  It's sad that I am at a desk more now as a gunner than I ever was in comm.  However when we deploy, all we do is the gunner portion and we have an awesome time doing it.  Nothing like going out into harms way to save some lives.

### Post by oldefoxx on 2010-03-16
I did enough service time to retire, but that was back in '81.  I followed that with a civilian career, from which eventually earned a small retirement.

I could go into details about either, but I don't really see the point.  Were I more interested, I could follow up on one of the many retiree-based leads that flow through my inbox, some even listing service commands I once served at.  You wonder where they find out things like that about you.

I never attended a high school or college reunion, nor do I intend to.  Same with trying to run down old military buddies or people that might have been there when I was.  Sort of reminds me of a time, when traveling on a bus in uniform, a woman asked where I was bound to.  "Back to Newport", I said.  Then she said, "Then you might know my son there", and mentioned his name.  I just looked at her.  She had absolutely no idea of how many ships and commands were situated there, and even if he were on the same ship, just being in a different work group or division would keep me from knowing him by name.  I just said, "I don't think so", and let it go at that.

Yes, we've been there.  Yes, we've done that.  And I am certainly not ashamed for having been a part of it.  But all that is behind me now.  The funny thing is though, that I find more to take pride in with recent generations of the men and women who serve now than I did at the time.  They are there now when we need them, just as we were there for others when it was our call.

i suspect you would have more opportunities to practice your trade in the Marines or Army. battles are coordinated via field grade officers chatting on IRC. no joke, i've seen it myself.

AF likes to purchase huge already-completed-items or sets of items from manufacturers and outsource their support to civilians (think of how this applies to CS and network security), whereas the ground combatants tend to be more willing to produce them in house.

we saw this a few months ago when the insurgents had broken the security of one model of UAV and where able to pick up the same image the UAV's controller saw, thus letting the insurgents know who was being watched, who was about to get blown up if they didn't quickly depart, etc... as i recall, the Air Force had to wait for the commercial company to take it's sweet sweet time fixing the vulnerability. all the CS majors in the world don't help the Air Force if they won't let them fix the problems encountered.

and since the Marines are 10x more ghetto and underfunded than the Army, that means you are 10x more likely to find yourself in a truly Computer Science position or given CS tasks as a Marine.

as a civilian employee, however... yes, your CS skills would be put to use as well in the Air Force.

### Post by spcwingo on 2010-05-31
Well, you could start out in the reserves or guard and go to college on the Montgomery GI bill + Post-911 GI bill + Pell grants + ROTC.  Then after getting your commission transfer to active duty if you want.  Also, the star program will put you through college.  All that program amount to is this:

1) Go to Basic Training
2) Get sent to college on the Reserve's dime.
3) Enjoy your free college education and time in the sandbox ;)

BTW, the star program is just for medical fields. IE: medic, x-ray tech, pharmacy, dental, etc.
