### Post by seeker5528 on 2009-11-21
> **koshatnik said:**
> Bands dont "sell out", they just make it big. So what?

As a fan it can be hard to know if an artist sold out or not.

I would define selling out as:

A: The artist doing want a record label, producer, etc... wants instead of what they want.

B: Fail to uphold some level of artistic integrity when it comes to what they think will sell verses what their heart tells them to do.

An artist can make adjustments in an attempt to make something appeal to a wider audience without going against what their heart tells them, that's a different thing than just going after the money.

If an artist has always created base on what others dictate or what they think will sell, you can't really say they sold out if they continue to do the same thing.

Also, artists are people and people change, as they change their creative inspirations change as well. Just because the music changes doesn't mean they sold out. You don't always know if artistic differences in the music are the result of changes in the way the artists think or feel about stuff or if they are kowtowing to the labels, producers, etc... or just going after the money.

In the case of producers, different artists will have different relationships with different producers. A producer could be anything from a sounding board to throw ideas off of, to an artistic partner, to taking control over what does or doesn't make it into a release.

So pairing the artists with the right producers is an important factor that could significantly impact the end result without necessarily meaning the artist sold out. It might just mean they ended up with a better or worse producer on a particular release or series of releases.

Maybe in some cases the well just dried up and they are struggling creatively.

Many of the classic composers lived in times and places where there were limits on what they were allowed to create, that didn't stop them from creating stuff they liked and were passionate about. Depending on what type of stuff an artist wants to do it can be the same with record labels, in particular if they made a name for themselves doing one type of thing and want to do something different, that's one of the drawbacks of signing with a major label.

No matter what route the artist chooses to go, reaching a level of selling millions of copies of an album is hard. When you can buy individual tracks on the internet and there is competition from artists who make their stuff available as free downloads, it's probably even harder these days.

I never really got into American Idol in the early seasons, but the last 2 or 3 seasons I've been watching it and I think it's been pretty good, the people that win aren't always the people that do the type of stuff that I prefer, and while they do try to push the contestants to be creative, it's not necessarily the most creative people that win either, but the winners on the seasons I have watched have been people I can listen to and recognize that they do a good job.

For the types of artists that you could expect to do well on Idol, Idol is a good deal, yes it does have that manufactured element, but there is lots of exposure during the show, so even if they don't win it's about as close to a guarantee that there will be a significant number of people interested in at least the first album that is released after they have been on the show. For the artist that wins, there is a relatively short term of commitments, some touring and promotional commitments with a single album record deal, probably with additional touring and promotional commitments. Once they have satisfied those initial commitments, then they are free to look at other options.

Artists that lose on Idol can find themselves in an even better situation than the winners because they have that exposure, less touring and promotional commitment, still get offers for record deals, and probably also have more freedom to fight for a deal that is more favorable to what they want to do creatively.
