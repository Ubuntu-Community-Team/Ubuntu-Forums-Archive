Are the goals you describe real objectives of free software?  I don't think it is specifically.  The purpose of the free software movement is to distribute high quality software that can be used to perform, and teach people about, state-of-the-art computing.  We have done that.  Incidentally, and more obvious is the effort of the same to perserve and protect human rights regarding information and technology.  We are doing that as well.

If you spend any time on a Microsoft forum, and a discussion of free software arises, particularly where a person mentions monopoly, the first thing that those favorable to Microsoft do is attempt to deny monopoly.  Next they take a trip into a fantasy world and describe the imaginary state of "legal" monopoly, which totally ignores the reasons for which we originally established laws against it.  This should come as no surprise, since Microsoft's leverage over their customers in the license of its program is highly restrictive, the position they gained through which to dictate in the market by locking independent vendors into "per CPU" sales contracts that completely hedged out superior technology in the eighties.

But as it stands, it is not Microsoft that free software must attempt to overcome, nor its position in the market.  The real challenge to free software is to overcome the threats to human rights by setting public policy in a way that is unfavorable to the paradigm of selling software to people under terms that force them never to share, and to surrender real control over their hardware to the developer.  While Microsoft has all the money and most of the market, those things are dependent solely on its ability to sustain a business model that is hostile to the freedom to study and the freedom to share, for which we have the greatest weapon in the world to combat, that is, the internet.  And now the freedom to share has come under fire there as well, in order to prop up dead forms of the distribution of information.

Regardless of its size, Microsoft cannot continue to support its business model if the paradigm by which information is distributed publicly no longer supports the way they have done business prior to the ubiquity of the internet.  Relieved of that support, Microsoft, like SCO, will have to design a better, less restrictive, and more humane way of doing business or face the possibility of not being able to do business at all.  In which case, if the licensing terms revert to what the status quo was *prior* to Microsoft, and their software respects the rights of the user to use, study, and modify it any way they want, Microsoft won't be on the opposite side of free software...it will become free software.

People who come here with problems in Ubuntu that an experienced user finds silly probably went through the Windows learning curve in much the same manner.

She had inadvertently deleted the link to Internet Explorer from her desktop, and wondered if I knew a quick way to fix it so she didn't have to take her computer into the shop.  She is unquestionably a person of great intelligence and intellectual prowess in all other areas.

The human brain physically creates neural links in response to stimulii.  As we continue to perform a task repeatedly, the distance between those links become shorter, hence why you can have two individuals with similar dexterity where one can play guitar and the other cannot.  As we come to understand many related tasks, we gain the ability to extrapolate unknown paths from existing paths.  The body physically doesn't need sleep, only short periods of inactivity.  But the brain demands sleep, during which time it prunes frivilous neural pathes created during that day and reinforces the useful ones.

The could (and should) demand that computing be brought full force into the educational systems of our communities, preferably *free software* computing that guarantees the right to study and modify.  At the very least we should demand that the representation of computing in schools be made on what is available to know and *not* on what is prominent in the marketplace.
