### Post by niteshifter on 2009-11-22
> **NoaHall said:**
> Ubuntu isn't the right distro for people wanting to learn new skills. It's the right distro for people who want a quick easy set-up, so they can compare it to Windows.


That's bullcrap coupled with a lack of imagination. Ubuntu is very nearly the perfect distro for folks looking to learn "new skills" in the form of learning how to use a computer vs. learning how to do the same-old same-old but with different (and inexpensive) software: It permits both approaches.

"Using a computer" is not checking email, surfing the web, syncing with your mp3 player, etc.. That is using specific purpose software.

To use a computer is to instruct it. How do task X differently than what "canned" software offers or that such software is missing. Or instruct it do task Y for which there isn't a ready made solution. In other words, learning the art & science of programming.

Lest you say that is "uber-geek" stuff, just for CompSci or IT degree types: "Educators, generals, dieticians, psychologists, and parents program. Armies, students, and some societies are programmed. An assault on large problems employs a succession of programs, most of which spring into existence en route." (From the Foreword of Structure and Intrepretation of Computer Programs, MIT Press).

Life is programmatic in nature. Homo Sapiens is a tool-using, pattern-seeking, real and abstract problem solving creature. We are all "programmers", we are all programmed.

Out of the box Ubuntu offers - and with no setup or extra installation steps - specific purpose software point-for-point with what OEM installed Windows offers: Broswer, email and IM clients, office suite and so forth. It offers more than what the retail version of Windows offers.

It also offers - fresh out of the box - a robust shell language, the GNU coreutils (awk, find, grep, sed, tr & company), Perl and Python. With just those tools - and a willingness to learn with a bit of imagination - a user can craft their own unique solutions to many a "Gee, I'd like to do X with my _______" type problem. (fill in the blank with music files, photos, emails, captured web pages, and so forth). If they want or need more, less than a half hour with Synaptic and they can have a full-featured development system for whatever language or methodology they wish to learn or use.

But there is one thing these machines can not do, even with a wonderful distro like Ubuntu: Think. Or, if you will, read with human comprehension. That's up to us humans. So, non-GUI apps should not be hidden, people need to read and think before they "buy". To hide them is tantamount to what Microsoft and Apple do: Decide what you need. Take away your freedom to choose. Tell you - encourage you - not to think.

Could the descriptions in Synaptic be improved and call out more clearly what is a CLI app or lib? Sure. But if a more immediate effect is desired, how about this: When we more experienced users help a noob we stop assuming everyone uses the CLI? When we tell 'em to install program P to solve their problem that it is a command line tool, that it won't show in the main menus and either tell them how to make that happen or give a link to someplace that explains it.
