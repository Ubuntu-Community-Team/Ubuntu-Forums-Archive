### Post by jamyskis on 2007-03-14
Actually, let me rephrase that question: why doesn't Canonical offer affordable commercial support for private users, especially freelancers (like myself) who work from home? Looking at the commercial support options, the cheapest option I can see is $250 plus taxes for a year's support, which is fine if you're running an office with four or more computers, but as soon as you look at home offices and private users, you begin to see why these markets don't exactly open themselves up to Ubuntu, and you understand why many of them choose to stick with Windows. Regardless of how useless Microsoft support is (and it is), it's still cheaper than having to pay $250 for a year's support that the average home user is perhaps going to use 2-3 times a year.

Quite frankly, Usenet is not recommended if we want to get people using Linux, and on the forums perhaps one in every four questions gets answers satisfactorily. IRC is a possibility, but many questions get passed over there too, and all these internet-based options are useless if the person can't even get their computer started. There needs to be an affordable telephone-based solution and for the average desktop user that just doesn't exist at this moment.

I'd recommend that Canonical start offering desktop support on a per-ticket basis, say $40 for five calls, which I would say would suit most average users down to the ground.

Over the past two years, they have had some success with goals one and two and I suppose they want to focus more on goal three.  As stated already, offering support for the home user is harder and is still a smaller market.

### Post by saulgoode on 2007-03-28
Support for Linux (any distro) has an inherent problem in that it requires a certain degree of familiarity with a broad range of hardware (and thusly, access to that hardware). While Microsoft Support may have no problem redirecting a customer to Lexmark, for example, to seek help in resolving a printer issue, the Linux service rep would not have that option because Lexmark does not support Linux. 

A Linux service would have to either 1) perform their own testing and documentation of Lexmark printers or 2) refuse to support Lexmark printers. "1)" would be very costly and "2)" would not satisfy a paying customer.

Lexmark may be the extreme in this regard, but the same general principle would cause difficulty for anyone considering implementation of user-oriented customer support service.
