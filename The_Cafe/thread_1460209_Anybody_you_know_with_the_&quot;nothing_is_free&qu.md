### Post by aysiu on 2010-04-22
Well, I think a better approach, instead of trying to convince your dad that there *are* things available for free is to actually tell him why things are free.

I wrote a piece a couple of years on this:
[How does open source make money?](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntucat/how-does-open-source-make-money/)

A lot of open source software is available for free to the consumer, but it still cost money to produce. Big companies like Sun, IBM, Red Hat, and Google fund open source projects because it actually benefits them. Yes, there are some open source projects that are just some kid in a basement. If your dad doesn't want to trust those, that's understandable.

You can tell him Firefox is free to download because Mozilla, the company that makes Firefox, earns hundreds of millions of dollars from Google just by having Google as the default search engine. If he feels guilty about getting it for free, he can click on some Google ads.

That Java plugin he installed for Internet Explorer... did he pay for that? That Flash plugin he installed to watch YouTube videos, did he pay Adobe for it?

Well, at a certain point, if he won't listen to reason, just leave him alone. Sometimes you just can't convince people. Happily use Ubuntu yourself and let him take five minutes to start his web browser. Sometimes you just have to pick your battles.

For what it's worth, my experience of user-friendliness is that it's very highly subjective - what one person finds easy, another may find hard depending on what they're used to. It's very likely that his idea of user-friendliness is "works exactly like Windows".

I found I had a harder time learning to use Mac OS X than Ubuntu for the most part - I found the desktop to not be very intuitive initially, despite the fact I'd used XP, Vista, KDE, Xfce and Gnome in the past. Also, I've had to compile more software from source on OS X than on Linux (excluding the time I built a Linux from Scratch system). And this is supposed to be more user friendly than Linux? Hmm...

Regarding the "nothing is free" mentality, one way to go might be to try the razor blade analogy - companies like Gillette sell razors at a loss or even give them away and make money on the blades, as mentioned in [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freebie_marketing]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freebie_marketing"). You could say the same about a product like MySQL, where they give the product away and charge for support.

Or in the case of OpenOffice, Sun bought StarOffice and open-sourced it because it actually worked out cheaper for them to buy a competing product and open-source it than to pay for a load of Microsoft Office licenses, so it was actually the more cost-effective option. Meanwhile they continued to sell StarOffice as a commercial product for people who needed the added extras it offers.

Another good one is the Django web development framework - this was something that the company who released it developed internally for their own use, and by open-sourcing it they got a better product because other people took it and adapted it for their own needs, fixed bugs, and added features, so everyone wins. Since they were a news site and not a software developer they weren't really interested in selling their product commercially - all they cared about was getting the framework they needed to get their work done.

For that matter, even Microsoft give some software away free (Visual Studio Express Edition, for example).

It might also be worth pointing out that much of the Internet is available essentially for free, or is ad-supported.

### Post by blueturtl on 2010-04-22
Tell your dad that Linux/Ubuntu is not free of cost, but free as in open. The cost is covered by commercial sponsors that collaborate and share production costs and in return get to use the system in their infrastructures. Everyone wins because the development model allows the participants to tailor the system to their specific needs and at the same time they don't have to put down all the time and money by themselves.

If he asks why they give it away to consumers for free, just tell him that an operating system is a necessary commodity that does not have any cost associated to it outside it's development. In other words, selling a computer without an operating system makes little sense, because nobody is going to be able to use their computers without one. Also duplicating the system once it has been developed costs virtually nothing, so charging extra for it only drives up prices of hardware.

Also don't force your dad, he will only be more determined never to have a go at it. I know because my dad is the same way. :) Let him try it if he wants to.
