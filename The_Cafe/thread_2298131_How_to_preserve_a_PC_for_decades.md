### Post by TheFu on 2015-10-09
Backup the data - image with the 50+ yr optical media.
Clean all the dust out.
Bubble wrap them.
Bitrot is an issue, hence the need for 50 yr storage.
Moving parts are also an issue, so the optical drive and spinning disks may not work. Have some spares of the identical model, so you can use the parts to make a single, working model.

Store everything in a humidity, climate-controlled location - 35% or less humidity and 60-70 degF location. Seasonal temperature changes can loosen connections.  

All of this is a guess.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2015-10-09
Not to mention corrosion of electronic components.  I have a couple of Texas Instrument calculators from the late 70's that gave up after some chips just corroded.  No replacement parts available.  Commodore computers from the early 80's suffer similar problems with some motherboard chips that are no longer available.  I've got a psion hand PC from the early 90's that lost its graphics display because of internal corrosion of the graphics chip.

Besides TheFu's excellent suggestions, you should boot your machines every couple of years and take notes of what works and what doesn't.  Do a web search for the stuff that doesn't work and you will quickly see what components fail.  Capacitors can be troublesome on newer equipment, but those are easily replaceable.

The archives and museums community was really very into this kind of question around 20 years ago, when some of them started making a transition from being reactive to looking ahead. ### Post by TheFu on 2015-10-10
> **Habitual said:**
> and these "old stuff" HDs can be taken out and used in newer hardware?

What kind of "old stuff"?

Actually, this is perhaps the most important part - data migration and storage. 

Look into M-disc storage which claims to have a 50+ year lifespan. Bluray for DRM media is not well supported on Linux, but for data m-disc should be fine. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-DISC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-DISC)

Of course, some data is stored in a proprietary way and requires the original program to access in an meaningful way. When I worked in huge enterprises, we were legally required to maintain data "forever", but we were not required to maintain access to that data using the old program. When a DB has 2500 tables with complicated table-to-table-to-view-to-view relationships - exactly how good is that data? Not a all. For data at home, that loophole needs to be avoided. 

Open data formats are very important.

Fortunately, these days we can often use a virtual machine to have access to old PC OSes, programs and that important data.  Inside an enterprise, that isn't always possible when the OS is tightly tied to the 20 yr old hardware with no spare parts available.

### Post by night_sky2 on 2015-10-11
There's not much you can really do, except taking the dust off them from time to time. You can of course try to 'prolong' their lifespan by replacing faulty hardware, but there will come a point where you'll need to spend quite a lot of money and that's usually when it's more viable to simply just buy something else.

I recently prolonged the life of my older Pentium 4 Desktop computer who was having some boot issues by replacing (and at the same time upgrading) the RAM from 2X512MB to 2X1GB with brand new Samsung sticks bought from China on Ebay. It cost me a reasonable 25$ shipping included and the computer will probably live a few more years as a result.

### Post by Bucky Ball on 2015-10-14
I had an IBM Aptiva going for about 15 years and have had my home-built desktop going for about 12 (P4 3Ghz with RAM upgrade to 4Gb and +SSD). That runs fine. 

I think one of the secrets is to attempt to see where things are going (it is impossible to totally 'future-proof' the hardware but you wouldn't set-up something with no USB3 ports probably, for instance), design around that and use quality parts. If you have a generic silver box power supply, that is a bad start ... for instance. :)

The Aptiva was a quality machine for the time as far as hardware and build (I used the speakers even after the machine died, for about 20+ years! and they were great quality sound). I spent a lot of time selecting the parts for my home-build. Quality power supply, 80+, rated for about 600,000 hours from memory (but that was a bit arbitrary as this model hadn't really been officialy tested beyond that but was known to keep on going and be in good condition).
