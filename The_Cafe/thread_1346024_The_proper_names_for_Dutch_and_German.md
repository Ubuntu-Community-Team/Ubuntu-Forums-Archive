### Post by jwbrase on 2009-12-05
> **t0p said:**
> And why oh why does the English language insist on "translating" place names?  Okay, I can understand Koln (sorry, I don't know how type an umlaut)  and Cologne, as they do sound similar.

Actually this is a fairly interesting story. "Cologne" is the French name of the city, which the English borrowed. The French name is a result of the sound changes that turned Latin into French being applied to the late Latin name: "Colonia," which in turn is a shortening of the full name of the city in Latin "Colonia Claudia Ara Agrippinensium", which is the name Emperor Claudius bestowed on the city in honor of his wife (who was from the city). German "Köln" came out of that at some point.

> But why change Roma to "Rome"

Going from Old to Middle English, alot of vowels got reduced to a schwa, spelled with an "e" (like in German). (Compare pronunciations like "Colaraduh," or "Missouruh" in some American dialects).

Going from Middle to Modern English, people got too lazy to even pronounce the schwa (which is where we get all of our silent e's). Also, interestingly enough, the "o" got changed to an "oo" (in pronunciation, not spelling) in the Great Vowel Shift, before being brought back to an "o" under the influence of foreign pronunciations. Apparently there is a pun in Shakespeare that revolves around the then-identical pronunciations of "room" and "Rome".

> and Napoli to "Naples", and a thousand other Italian placenames into ridiculous "translations"? Bah!!

**EDIT:** Do other languages do that? (Other than French: I know they do - ie "Douvres" for Dover, "Londres" for London.)

Languages tend to do that quite a bit. Often the word for a place in one language will be a borrowing of a mutation of a borrowing of a mutation of a borrowing of the original name of the place, while the language spoken in that particular place has changed since the original name was given, so that even the "native" name is a double or triple mutation of the original. And it doesn't just apply to places. Often the same root will appear in a language several times with several different meanings, having been borrowed back and forth between languages through several paths and in various ways in between.

I could probably find roots in English where one variation went from Proto-Indo-European->Proto-Germanic->Old English->English, another took the same route but got used in different grammatical environments or different dialects and got changed as a result, a third went PIE->PG->Frankish->Late Latin/Early French->Norman French->Middle English->Modern English, a fourth went PIE->Latin->EF->Renaissance French->Modern English, and a fifth went straight from PIE->Latin->Scholarly Latin->Modern English.

I already know that the words drag, draw, and tractor represent examples of 1, 3, and 5 for one root.
