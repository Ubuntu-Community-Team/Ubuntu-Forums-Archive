> Bibble 5 is an ambitious project to revolutionize Digital Photographic Workflow. Expanding on Bibble 4's speed and power, Bibble 5 is designed to unite unparalleled photographic editing capability and blazing speed in a sleek, modern interface.

It is, however, proprietary. But I don't mind buying it. This is almost the ONLY serious option for photographers on Linux. And it doesn't look like its was made in the 1990's.

I've taken a look at LightZone, and wasn't happy with the raw file processing; the colours were way off, and would have been more time and effort to re-right the colours. Using LightRoom was soooooooooooo addictive. That 30 day trial is dangerous. PhotoShop is too slow, and for the same basic fix for a batch of 300 photos, of which you still need to cumbersomely sort through and decide which you will use (depending on caffeine levels, tends to become a hit and miss affair), LightRoom is sooooooooo much better. I can't sing the praises of LR enough. LR is the main reason why I still have XP on my computer. As soon as Wine can handle LR, I'm a Ubuntu devotee.

Would I pay for Bibble? Only if it's good. I've tried Gimp, but for serious cataloguing, collection handling, batch editing, Gimp and PhotoShop are the wrong tools for that job. Would I pay for LR again? Only if Bibble or Rawtherapee isn't better. I'll admit, I haven't tried rawtherapee, yet, but I'll give it a go tonight or tomorrow.

The greatest concern I have is getting the colours absolutely right. To do that, you need to calibrate the monitor. On mac and XP, you can do that with a little device called a [Spyder]("http://www.northlight-images.co.uk/article_pages/spyder2pro.html"), but I don't think it's available for Linux, which would be the last hurdle for me. I notice a big difference in colour cast and tones on this same monitor when comparing Ubuntu and XP.

What's important for me? Having the right tools. I've got to be able to deal with a massive number of images regularly, and I've got to deal with them easily. LR is really, really good. I've used it for almost a year now, so I know some of it's draw backs. However, it's still tough to beat. From my understanding, LR 3 (coming soon) is much the same as LR2, but with a few new features, which would be only nice to have, not as essential as the change to LR from PhotoShop or Gimp.

### Post by winjeel on 2009-12-30
I've just had a go of Rawtherapee, and these are the things I don't like:
- small image viewing area. the use of space in the menus isn't maximising the image viewing area (chief concern)
- Can't flag or pick and reject images, and seems like you can't filter your selection (or it's not viewable, see menu problem below)
- No masking for spot processing (like being able to increase or decrease exposure in certain areas you might need to brush over)
- The tonal curve is there, but isn't as easy or user friendly as LR. It doesn't separate darks and shadows, nor lights and highlights for separate processing.
- Menu and information can run over other icons making some things in the icon areas disappear or unreadable
- IPTC data doesn't have all the boxes I need to enter key information
- It's way of zooming in for a detailed view and preview doesn't seem intuitive, but it is present, and I guess when you get used to it, it'll be fine.

Things I do like:
- It's on Linux
- You can adjust the width / spread of tonal adjustments such as highlights and shadows
- Does have IPTC data (but not all)
- Can process ARW (raw file for Sony Alpha cameras, but don't know if it can handle Minolta digital)
- Quick and easy to learn it's menu system
- Includes an RGB histogram


- Otherwise, it's much the same as LightRoom, and all other differences are not immediately observable in a two image test session (twenty minutes), and these very few things listed above, are the only key differences. Certainly, if I were on a budget, Rawtherapee is fine, but it's still not my weapon of choice.

As for Bibble, I'll check it out later.

### Post by winjeel on 2009-12-31
I'm in the middle of writing an article for a blog, and this is what I made of Bibble so far:

>    	 	 	 	 	 	  At first glance, Bibble looks like a very serious competitor to LightRoom. It has much of the same sliders as LightRoom, including Exposure, Highlights, Fill Light, Blacks, as well as Noise Ninja built in. In one mouse click you can see which parts of the image has been clipped, where the colour information has gone off of the histogram (also shown). You can select and reject images, rotate them by degrees (“Straightening” them), adjust colour, tone, and make various Presets of your own. The main issue I have with it is that the IPTC data is not comprehensive enough for my needs. With new American laws where information of the original photographer could be lost, these “orphaned images” can be used without attempts to find and compensate the photographer. Consequently, adding photographer contact details is vitally important. The cropping tool is different to that of LightRoom, and needs a little getting used to. But interesting, you can add layers to images, something that (using full name now) Adobe PhotoShop LightRoom ironically does not do. Amazingly, Bibble offers a wide variety of Lens Correction (simulation) Presets, based on a variety of manufactures and cameras, including Sony 35mm G and Leica 100mm f2.8 APO! Again, something LightRoom does not offer. After working with images you can Save As Tiff 8 and 16 bit, or jpeg. On that note, only sRGB colour space is available in Bibble 5. I prefer Adobe 1998 for the wider colour gamut (increased colour information). In short, I wish I had known about Bibble before purchasing LightRoom; it would have made the choice just that bit more difficult.
