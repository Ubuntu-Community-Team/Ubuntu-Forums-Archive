### Post by Xianath on 2010-06-23
It's ridiculously high. People just fend for themselves and don't see the big picture. Here's an example of what I think piracy is causing:

1) Every single document floating around (bank forms, tax forms, you name it) requires a Microsoft product to open reliably. There's the occasional PDF and with a lot of luck you might get OO.o to open a .doc and not mess it up, but it's hit and miss, mostly miss.

2) Kids come out of high-school with several years of Visual Studio experience behind them. Suddenly everyone who can tie their own shoes (if that) is a .NET programmer. How's that good for the industry?

3) Starter jobs come with requirements such as experience in 3DS Max, Photoshop, Autocad, Protel, OrCAD etc. Either of these costs a year's tuition or more. How is a student expected to have had access to them if not for pirating? I know a guy in Australia who worked his a** off freelancing for a year using nothing but free stuff until he could afford proper pro tools. It's not fair for people like him to face competition from such kids. How are they even supposed to learn the value of work?

4) Movies are no longer being produced for repeated watching like "The Godfather" was, because no one would bother buying the DVD. Instead, movie companies produce junk that only has one-time eye candy value and has to be seen on the big screen or not seen at all -- Transformers, Fantastic Four, you name it.

5) More and more bands rely on live performances and merchandise to make a living, and while unfair to the bands and making their lives harder, it's a treat for us fans.

6) People have no incentive to try free alternatives when the commercial ones are freely available. FLOSS is therefore left for those who actually believe in it.

Unfortunately, even with zero piracy level, 99% of the movies will still be crap, Windows would still proliferate, and FLOSS would still not be a viable alternative for the average user. It looks like the status quo works for everyone.

Overall, it's all a big mess, but sooner or later it will get sorted out. Software companies will move to a SaaS model which would hopefully be more accessible than the current outrageous prices of software (also inflated by piracy, or so some claim).

[Quote]       The Literature
Empirical research on file sharing and record sales has been limited and inconclusive, primarily, we believe, due to shortcomings with the data. Most of what we know about the effect of file sharing on sales is based on phone surveys. There are numerous industry studies which arrive at a diverse range of conclusions. For instance, Forrester Research (2002) and Jupiter Media Metrix (2002) find neutral or positive effects, while the International Federation of the Phonographic Industry (2002), Edison Media Research (2003) and Forrester Research (2004) document a sales displacement. A general difficulty with these studies is that they compare the purchases of individuals who download files with the purchases of those who do not. While downloaders may in fact buy fewer records, this could simply reflect a selection effect. File sharing is attractive to those who are time-rich but cash-poor, and these individuals would purchase fewer CDs even in the absence of P2P networks. A handful of academic studies rely on micro data to address the issue of unobserved heterogeneity among file sharers.3 Rob and Waldfogel (2004) study the survey responses of a convenience sample of U.S. college students. For hit albums which sold more than 2 million copies since 1999, they find no relationship between downloading and sales. Expanding the set of albums to include all music the students acquired in 2003, downloading five albums displaces the sale of one CD. This difference is interesting. One interpretation is that piracy does not affect hit albums but hurts smaller artists. It is also possible that file sharing had less of an effect on sales in earlier years. After instrumenting for downloads with the school the students attend &#8211; everyone at Penn has broadband access while this is not true for the other schools &#8211; the resulting estimates are too imprecise to draw any firm conclusions. Zentner (2004) employs European survey data to study the relation between file sharing and sales. Using measures of Internet sophistication and access to broadband as instruments, Zentner finds some displacement. Unfortunately, neither the Rob and Waldfogel study nor Zentner&#8217;s work allow inferences about the total impact of file sharing on record sales because neither paper studies a representative sample of file sharers. Zentner also lacks information about the total quantity of downloads or purchases of any individual. Finally, Blackburn (2004) examines the relationship between the supply of digital files and sales, using the timing of the RIAA lawsuits as an instrument. Unlike broadband access or Internet sophistication, this instrument has the advantage of not being a choice variable. Restricting the estimated effect to be identical across albums, Blackburn (2004) concludes that increases in the supply of files had no impact on sales. For instance, the sales numbers for new releases often peak in the first few weeks, while the stock of files on P2P networks continues to increase, leading to a mechanical negative relationship between the supply of shared files and sales. ### Post by gnomeuser on 2010-06-24
My position is that while currently downloading for personal non-commercial use is something for which one can at least get threated with legal action I do not believe this should be the case. How a model which rewards artists while retaining the good in sharing, namely the ample access on equal terms for all. Personally I am strongly in favor of a Magnatune style option for music artists, the movie and tv industry are a different target and probably require a different model or at least a tweaked Magnatune model.
I naturally have a Magnatune subscription and I am very happy with it and the terms, I would happily recommend it but the selection is currently limited to lesser known artists though many very good.

At least I believe especially the movie industry's current approach and general behavior is vast overreacting. Massive privacy invasion, a push for stronger laws with detrimental effects to society and freedom of speec. Claims in the millions for clear small infringement cases, Tanenbaum e.g. we can hopefully all agree represents a harmful overreaction. In their approach they are also not regarding of someone is already a good customer. E.g. I would argue that I spend more on entertainment than most people, they thus also make a good profit on me and that has never been taken into account. 

It is my impression that most people are either very limited downloaders, a movie or album every once in a while or that one special TV show that doesn't air in their country. These are likely to become customers if paying is as easy as downloading. Since downloading means going to sites which may have be big targets for malware and where one might waste time downloading a fake or corrupted file. Having easy access at pay seems very attractive for this group to me. 

There are also those who download a lot but still buy a lot. Though everyone has a limited budget for entertainment and a growing range of subject on which to spend it than previously this group tends towards having an above average budget planned and would probably keep spending even in economic downtimes. These are valuable customers though they are currently looked upon as criminals many fit for gigantic fines. Clearly it is not in the interest of the industry to sue this group as it would hurt profits. The current model does not take this into account and it is a shame.

Finally there are massive downloaders who do not spend a lot of money. This is hopefully a small group who do this for a current lack of money, such as being young or otherwise fiancially pressed currently. One could imagine students here, being pressed for money and still at an age when culture experiences are highly valued. These I believe will be shown to grow into customers when their economy allows for it. 

Children especially might have very limited budgets as well as the technical skills required to download. With no money to give back will tend towards large consumption of culture yet not giving back at all. 

Finally there is a group of has the means but also have an abnorm consumption and no intend to become a future customer. I believe this is a fairly small group and that they should be tolerated and I also think that the filesharing community will generally also be capable of controlling this somewhat. Never the less these users may represent some degree of loss to the industry and are to be considered harmful.

I am against all commercial use of downloaded material, though the definition of commercial would have to be closely defined.

I believe the harm done to free speech and privacy by continuing to enforce the laws as they stand and increasingly as they are pressed to be changed by the industry in the future outweights any real harm. 

I believe that given the option to pay reasonably and easily in a desirable fashion (no drm springs to mind) or watch commercials such as iTunes or Hulu provides p2p customers will flock to become customers. Those who do not become customers never will even if sued. Cases like that are often PR nightmares that even up costing a lot of money and taking up a lot of time. 

Allowing non-commercial downloading is in the interest of the industry, new markets will open up. E.g. with an Android powered TV it would be extremely easy to offer content in very approachable ways. These streams of revenue I believe will grow big quickly as well as allow for a lower cost in distribution, provided e.g. a P2P scheme was used ironically.

Even in these days the music industry especially is reporting growth. They are not make less money, just less money than they expected. In these considerations they have at least previously not taken into account e.g. the end of the period of rebuying old LPs as CDs, nor has the emergance of new items on the entertainment budgets. Such things as Internet site subscription, more expensive subscription fees for such things as cable TV and finally video games and DVDs which have growth into a big market.

Even then they report growth, I ask if the current response is moderated. I would suggest that the industry faces many forces which are ignored in favor of piracy and if taken into consideration would give a more accurate picture of the real loss faced.

This I believe, it is what I am let to by observation, studies and recommendations from noted experts in copyright as well as historical presidence of long term good as considering this a fair right limited to non-commercial personal use.

I may be wrong and would love to see firm good studies done (or be refered to peer reviewed ones that have been done) on specific points.
