### Post by keithpeter on 2011-08-16
That was interesting, thanks for posting.

Writing has various 'voices' and styles, each appropriate to an audience and a context. It has been suggested that SMS instant messaging, Twitter, chat and forums are discursive and are seen by participants as a form of speech. Speech, especially imagined as speaking in the street to a crowd, can lead to more personal references and aggressive language. Sentences are shorter. Complex nuances left out in favour of impact.

Other contexts suggest a more formal 'literary' style of writing, which tends to be less focussed on the persons and more on the ideas.

That article was written 30 years ago, not 200. Thirty years! And the guy felt the need to clarify what a modem was, as it was quite possible that some of his readers wouldn't be aware of the "new technology". Today, if a kid asked what a modem was, the answer would be something like: "it was a device dinosaurs used in their computers, when they ran out of smoke to do their signals". Which brings me to the secondary discussion that popped up in this thread: what you call "deformation" of the language is called "evolution" by linguists. That's how languages have been formed, and how they got to be what they are today. Sure, some people's writings are so "evolved" ( ;)) they become nearly impossible to read, but I think that's precisely the product of the speed everything is changing lately. Basically all language changes and neologisms started as misspellings, or just plain barbarisms. The difference is that, while a few centuries ago people had years, or even decades to get used to them, now we can barely catch up, before everything changes, yet again.
