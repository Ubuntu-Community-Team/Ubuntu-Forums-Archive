There is a legal loophole which allows everyone to use these restricted formats.  For MP3s, have you ever purchased a stand alone MP3 player which came with software for Windows?  Even if you don't install the software, you have a license to create MP3s.  The same holds true with DVDs and a standard DVD drive.  If you have a retail drive, you have a license to watch DVDs.  Seriously... read the EULA that comes with these hardware components and you will find no mention of specific operating systems outside of general use "Windows".  Also, if you have purchased the computer system from an OEM and it originally had Windows on it, then you are covered completely on all MS Codecs and fonts.

The unfortunate part is there are those who have purchased hardware from companies which do not license the software for the hardware.  An example of that is purchasing a laptop from System76 or similar hardware vendors.  There's no license with the DVD drives or codec software normally licensed through MS.

Let's not forget the people like me who always build their own system (with the exception of laptops) and use OEM components in all hardware purchases to keep the price down.

So how do you legally purchase codecs?  Without purchasing a legit copy of Windows itself, there is very little you can do to purchase the license.

### Post by aysiu on 2007-08-19
Ubuntu not including restricted formats has more to do with ideology than cost. From [the Ubuntu philosophy page](http://www.ubuntu.com/community/ubuntustory/philosophy): > For Ubuntu, the 'free' in 'free software' is used primarily in reference to freedom, and not to price - although we are committed to not charging for Ubuntu. The most important thing about Ubuntu is that it confers rights of software freedom on the people who install and use it. It is these freedoms that enable the Ubuntu community to grow, continue to share its collective experience and expertise to improve Ubuntu and make it suitable for use in new countries and new industries. Price and legality are definitely considerations, though.
