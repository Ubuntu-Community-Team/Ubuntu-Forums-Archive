### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2011-07-21
Until about 2 years ago, Windows XP was all I had ever known. I'm hardly a geeky power user by any means, and I never set out to *find* a new operating system. All I wanted was a way around the trouble I was having with my computer.

One of the dance students' father is a computer repair tech who became my go-to guy for all things computer related.  He bartered computer maintenance in exchange for his kids' dance lessons. He was fixing my 'puter for the fourth time in about 2 years because of these relentless issues:

**[COLOR="Purple"]"Windows rot:"[/COLOR]** That's a term used to describe the  inevitable slowdown of a Windows computer over time as stuff accumulates or whatever. Whether it's registry errors or obsolete registry entries, libraries of virus definitions and blocked malware bugs, whatever it is.

**[COLOR="Purple"]"Bloatware:"[/COLOR]** That's my term for the expensive software you have to buy - not for your own use, but just to keep the operating system happy. Anti-virus software. Anti-spyware sweeper. Optimizing software. Disk cleaner. Registry cleaner. That kinda stuff. What qualifies it as "bloatware" is that these are not applications for *my* use, but for the *computer* to use in it's own upkeep.

**[COLOR="Purple"]Zero Tolerance for Benign Neglect:[/COLOR]** **I want to run *applications*, not the operating system!** My 'puter, like any other household appliance, is a *tool*. I use mine for schoolwork, music editing, dance choreography, e-mail and web browsing (and now blogging!), and occasional gaming. But the ceaseless vigil of defragging, updating, scanning, and cleaning up accumulated cruft on the hard drive took alot of time. The price of having a workable Windows 'puter is this endless vigil of regular tasks. If people had to change their oil and rotate their tires every 30 miles instead of every 3 to 5 thousand miles, it would hardly be worth driving a car! If your central air conditioning required service once a week, you'd be like, "hell with this, I'll just use fans!" Why should a busy college student's computer require so much precious time to maintain instead of use? Rawr! All the stuff stored on the hard drive is held hostage to these incessant demands!

Some of the dance kids use Macs instead of PCs and they're awesome! A Mac doesn't "rot," it is less susceptible to malware, and can tolerate a little benign neglect. And the graphics, wow! I was jealous. I wanted a Mac! Besides, all the cool kids use Macs, right? So I looked into it.

Omygosh, have you seen the PRICE of Macs?  Screeching halt! There'd be no bartering for one of those magnificent machines. I'm stuck with my ancient hand-me-down PC and lifelong maintenance hassles.I shared my lament with my computer guy when I took my 'puter to him for the umpteenth time. He said:

    > First of all, Windows doesn't 'rot' if it'd configured properly. And second, without all that 'bloatware' as you call it, your machine would crash and burn on Day One. How important is it to you to maintain it? And thirdly, you just have to make time to protect your investment, son. You maintain your house and your car, why not such a valuable tool?


Should I say it? Well, I'm sure *thinking* it, so here goes nothing. I replied:

    "Um, well, then what happened? The folks at Dell didn't configure Windows properly when they put it on my computer? And the Geek Squad didn't configure it properly either when they re-installed the OS (the only cure for "Windows rot" is a complete re-installation of Windows)? And um... *you* didn't configure Windows properly either when *you* re-installed it? Twice?"

I shouldn't have said that. Oops.

    "Fine," he spat back in reply. "Use Linux then, you little smartass. You'll be begging for my help on the first day. **[COLOR="Purple"]You're better off with Windows, kid. You're not ready for Linux!"[/COLOR]**

That was the first I'd ever heard of anything *other* than Windows and Mac. Linux? A third alternative? Hmmm.

I had made my computer man so angry that he refused to re-install Windows for me. Omygosh, what have I done? Now what'll I do? I was desperate and angry.

So I Googled "Linux for home computers" and followed a bunch of links. One caught my eye. "*Linux for Human Beings*," it said. Hey! Not "Linux for Home Computers," but Linux for *people?* Waitaminute... a visit to the web site fascinated me, and an on-line "distro chooser" led me right back there. Still desperate and angry, I downloaded Ubuntu Linux and burned it to a CD on a borrowed computer. The minute I got home, I installed Ubuntu, giving it the entire disk and wiping away Windows. I was still fuming, and desperate enough to take that risk. I could always say, "It was *your* idea, Mister Computer Man!"

**[COLOR="Purple"]Oh. My. Gosh![/COLOR]** It was effortless, even for a silly, impulsive, autistic, technically challenged, artsy-craftsy kid like me! I was stunned! There was OpenOffice to completely replace the Microsoft Office suite. Firefox in place of Internet Explorer, Thunderbird took the place of Outlook Express, and it had Audacity to edit music files, GIMP to edit images, wow! And y'know what it cost? ZERO! It's free! And legal!Why had I never even heard of this before? And y'know what else is cool about Linux? Let me count the ways:

[LIST]
[*]Linux has no registry. So no registry errors! No accumulated registry entries to confuse the OS and slow it down.

[*]Linux needs no "bloatware" because you're not running it as "Administrator" all the time. No viruses. No spyware. Immunity from all that stuff unless you log on as "root" (Administrator) and do something completely stupid.

[*]Benign neglect is no problem in Linux. It can update automagically, or not at all, and it'll still run fine.
[/LIST]
After two months of Linux delight, I ran into my Computer Man at the dance studio and couldn't help smiling. "I took your advice, sir," I offered politely. "Best advice ever! Linux runs great and completely solved all my problems. I can even re-install it if I mess it up, and without wiping away all my schoolwork and dance stuff," I bragged. ("*Just try that with Windows,*" I thought, but didn't say it out loud.)

He didn't hesitate with his retort, but it was priceless. Know what he said??

Wait for it...

**[COLOR="Purple"][SIZE="4"]"Then stick with Linux, kid. You're not ready for Windows yet!"[/SIZE][/COLOR]**

More great advice. What a wise man! I've taken his advice, and have not needed his services for two years. And his kids have become awesome little dancers. They use Linux too, at the dance studio between classes. They just don't know it's Linux.

-Robin

### Post by rg4w on 2011-07-21
**1) How did you find Ubuntu?**
I'd heard about Linux in the mid-90s from a friend, and as a long-time Mac user my initial reaction was that the whole idea was stupid.  "How can an OS be any good if it's given away for free?"  Well, thankfully I've learned a few things since then. :)

As a multi-platform GUI app designer, I became curious about Linux, esp. after the advent of Red Hat, and tried a few distros now and then.

Three years ago I finally decided to give Linux a more serious try, and by that time Ubuntu had become the clear leader so it was the obvious choice for an app developer.

The more I use it, the more I love it.

**2) What you use your distro for?**
Mostly the stuff we all use computers for: email, web, music, etc.  I've been porting my dev tools to it and increasingly use it for both desktop and web app development.  I get a perverse pleasure writing my Mac apps on Ubuntu. :)

**3) What has using Ubuntu taught you**
Everything.  Linux is like Unix, and Unix is arguably the core of computing.  Security is a big part of what I've picked up from the Linux community, but I've also learned a lot about file systems, the internal distinctions between the kernel and the window manager, and a lot more.

But I think the most valuable thing I've learned from Linux is community.  The OS is great and all, but even greater is the community that builds and shares it.  Once you take the onerous DRM crap out of the computing experience and encourage people to share, something truly magical happens:  sharing happens freely, joyously, inspiringly.  

I attend some of my local Drupal meetings even though I don't use Drupal very often but I just love the people and the flavor of their social interactions so much.  Same for the vibe at the SoCal Linux Expo, and my local Linux user group.

I came for a safer alternative for using PCs, but I stay for the people.

**4) What is one thing you want to learn/want your system to do?**
Run reliably.  I like tinkering as much as the next guy, but I have work to do and need my system to be predictable.  Ubuntu 10.10 delivers that well, and I'm looking forward to 11.10 for more of that with Unity.
