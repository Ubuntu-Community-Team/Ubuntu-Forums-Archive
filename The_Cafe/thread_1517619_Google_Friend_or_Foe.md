### Post by headflux on 2010-06-25
I don’t think anyone can deny that Google is one of the most innovative companies in the world, that they have some very cool products, and are the blueprint for a small garage based startup coming good.

However, over the past few years I’ve increasing felt the ubiquitous nature of Google to be somewhat sinister.  I intrinsically feel uncomfortable with large successfully companies…it’s a dog eat dog world, and that makes me feel suspicious.

Ok, my personal prejudices aside, the recent scandals over Google’s ‘inadvertent’ harvesting of data by its StreetView cars from private Wi-Fi networks, followed by the company’s apparent reluctance to hand over that data to the authorities seems disturbing.

Google claim that the data harvesting (over the past 3 years) was “a mistake”…this sounds a little far-fetched.  I’m no programmer, but surely such an activity would require some pretty specific code, and a hardware setup that isn’t built purely for creating a photographic map of urbanised areas.

So is this a sign that Google is the new Evil Empire? Is it prompting anyone to switch Browser, Search Engine, or retire their Gmail account? How will it damage Google (if at all)? Does anyone care?

Thoughts?

> Corporation are not evil. To paraphrase *[The Corporation](http://www.thecorporation.com/index.cfm?page_id=314)*, corporations are not evil--they are amoral and dangerous.

Ultimately, publicly owned corporations have one primary motivation, to maximize profits for their shareholders. We, as consumers, are the ones who need to hold companies accountable by rewarding them for good behavior and punishing them for bad behavior.

If corporations find it profitable to be "good," they will be good. If they find it profitable to be "bad," they will be bad. If a CEO doesn't give the company enough profits, that CEO can be replaced with another CEO.

If you want corporations to be good, give them money when they do good and refuse them money when they do evil. A corporation is really no different from Pavlov's dogs in that respect.
