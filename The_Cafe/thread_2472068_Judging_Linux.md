### Post by DuckHook on 2022-02-16

If we Linux enthusiasts are to be completely candid with ourselves, the public has *no* perception of Linux. Among my larger circle of non-tech friends, most have never even heard of it. They don't know what is in their phones, their cars, their routers or their dishwashers, nor do they care. They think of these things as appliances. To them, real computing is done on desktops or laptops and the only OSes they've ever seen or known on computers are Windows or Apple.

The more knowledgeable will have heard of Linux, but think it's some exotic OS that belongs in the backoffice. When I show them Linux in full desktop glory, most are gobsmacked that anything so powerful and complete even exists.

The public that you are referring to are a tiny subset that have already moved into having some basic familiarity with Linux. I agree that they generally have some misguided notions about Linux, but what are we to realistically expect? Judging an OS is like judging anything else—say, a car. The first thing that springs to mind is to compare them. So, can Linux run MS Office? Can it run Adobe Illustrator? Can it run Kill the Zombies (or whatever game is currently in vogue)?

Membership in a small and exotic club, no matter how amazing such club is, means having to educate others as to what we do, what we are and what we stand for. It's unavoidable. We do it with as much patience and good cheer as we can muster (often my patience as at low ebb too), but expecting a greater level of prior knowledge from the uninitiated is unrealistic. As I said: people don't know what they don't know.

Long ago, maybe in the 70s, I read a science fiction story about a large ship carrying passengers to another solar system.  Over the years, generation after generation of passengers had lived in their cylindrical world without any understanding at all that beyond the metallic skin of their home lay a whole universe. Even fewer would stay long enough to gaze out at the sea and find themselves fascinated, but eventually return to the hold.

Some few of them might dare to venture to the railing and enjoy the spray on their faces.

And some, so very few, might jump into the water as we have done, finding that we have no need of the SS Windows to convey us to our desired destinations as we climbed on to a sleeker, faster vessel.

All the others, watching aghast, would reckon us mad and point out that we could not sleep on the ship's hammocks, eat from the ship's galley, play at cards in the ship's lounge ...
