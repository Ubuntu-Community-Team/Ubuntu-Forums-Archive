### Post by muz1 on 2006-08-09
Why Linux? Why do you use it??? I would really love to hear why people use Linux. Real stuff if you know what I mean. Your mental perceptions and such.

Below are my thoughts. Your feedback and further discussion is most welcome.


Cheers
muz

-----------

**Linux and me**

I remember jumping on this bandwagon about 5 years ago. ***Windows sucks*** was the general concensus and ***Linux rocked***. Linux. Hmmmmm. Have heard about it. Never used it. I want to be part of the cool crowd. If I say that Linux is better than windows, I will appear smart. After all... all the smart people use Linux.

About two years ago, I finally got the nerve up to install Xandros on my computer. I tinkered. Never really did anything. It seemed okay. It just seemed like something was missing. I played every now and then but basically, Windows was what I returned to every time.

I removed Xandros and a while later installed Ubuntu 5.10. This seemed alot more interesting. At the time, I was getting big time into standardisation, usability, functionality, accessibility and other technologies that were designed for multiple platforms. The more and more I looked into topics that covered Linux, things started making sense. Such as GPL, Creative Commons License and Open Source community based development.

I started to look at the industry that was pretty much running so fast that they could not see what was really needed. All they could see was other corporations and the so-called marketplace at large. Was this really what was needed? Was the corporate mentality really going to satisfy the needs and desires that were out there? Did they even know what people wanted? Were they even listening?

My opinions started to form and I started vocalising them when the discussion of Operating Systems were on the table. I found myself being isolated by most due to the Windows mentality. Most people seemed to use windows because that was what they were used to. They criticised Linux for being geeky and hard to use. They said that Linux would never go anywhere. That apache was nowhere near as good as IIS. That MySQL was crap and SQL Server was where it was at. That ASP and .NET technologies were the future.
Something inside just did not feel right. Even when I did not have a retort to their criticisms, I just could not agree with their words. 

Inside all I knew then and know now is that corporate strangle holds and Vista are not the answer. If we look at the so called *progression* of computing, I actually think we are going backwards in a cloud of misconception and false beliefs. This scenario really works against the structure of civilisation and progression. What could help is if focus was put on the problem only to emphasize that maybe we got it wrong . To create a revolution of sorts. A radical reshuffling of power to cause an objective redrafting of what is best for people in general.

**Why do I use Linux?**

Well... 

Linux to me is a chance to more objectively understand what choice is about. Linux forces me to consider other people. To write code that will work well for others not just myself. To help others instead of criticising. To step down off my pedestal and get my hands dirty. To rethink my actions and consider what benefits others.

I am still learning as we all are. The climb is sometimes steep but the view is breath-taking.

Thanks for reading :)

### Post by TeeAhr1 on 2006-08-10
I'm one of those wild-eyed bearded guys (well, I shaved last week, but before that...), I really am here for the revolution.  A hopefully-brief-but-surely-long-winded-by-the-end explanation:

When I was a kid, it was always expected of me to finish high school, then go on to a computer science-y type degree of some sort.  My eventual history major was probably as much a rebellion against that expectation as anything else.  I didn't want to be sensible, I wanted to change the world, and I didn't see how a C-Sci. degree would achieve that.

Until about two and a half years ago, when I met Free Software.  On that day, my life changed.  I probably started with Firefox, OpenOffice, etc.  Read about the $200 laptop.  Eventually bit the bullet and installed Linux at home (yeah, mostly because it was the cool thing to do, but guess what?  It is the cool thing to do! ;) )

Here's the deal: I believe in the revolution.  I do.  I think we as human beings need to find a better way to live together, I believe that corporatism is a downhill, one-way trip, I believe we're better as a human race than this.  Scarcity is so over, if we want it bad enough.

And Linux, and FLOSS in general, is bringing that closer to a reality.  Sans materials, You can't take the specs of a car and make a copy of a car.  You can't take the blueprints of a house and make a copy of the house.  But I can take a copy of the Dapper ISO and make *as many copies as I want to*, for negligible cost.  It's the end of scarcity.  It's the end of corporatism.  It *is* the revolution.  There's nowhere else for it to start.

So I'm going back to school this year.  For computer science and marketing.  Yes, mother, you did tell me so ;)

### Post by AndyCooll on 2006-08-11
For me initially it was the cost that got me interested in Linux. 
I'd been using pirated stuff for years and my conscience got the better of me. While still using Windoze I realised that there were free software alternatives out there that did everything I needed them to do. Since I didn't use 99% of M$ Office (for instance) so why did I always have to have the latest version? I wanted a "legal" computer (I'd just bought my first ever M$ OS licence) so I began looking for free alternatives of all the other software I used. In my search I came across Firefox, Gimp and oOo ...and then Linux.

Linux seemed as if it might be the answer because though I'd bought a legal licence I actually had more than one box. And so it has proved, for now I'm removed from the pressure of whether I can legally install a piece of software or not.

That was why I first used Linux ...however as time has gone on I have become hooked by the philosophy of open-source. I'm not a programmer so I guess it's because of DRM, patent issues, copyright issues etc. I don't like being told what I can and cannot do with my computer, simple as that. Nonetheless, open-source is an important issue for me.

And now, one year later I use Linux (of the Ubuntu flavour) for two reasons. Firstly I am still, if not more so, an advent of the open-source philosophy. Secondly I use it because I genuinely prefer it. I use W2K at work because I have to (but with open-source PortableApps on my USB key where possible), however I'm always glad when I can get back home on to my Linux boxes. I don't dislike Windoze or indeed any of the M$ products, I simply prefer Linux and the open-source versions. They provide all the computing facilities I need. And when I'm using Linux I never feel I'm using a lesser product, indeed IMHO it is superior. 

Finally for me personally, I've learnt far more about computers and my OS in the last year than I ever learnt while using M$ products.

:cool:
