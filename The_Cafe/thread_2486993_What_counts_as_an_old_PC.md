### Post by guiverc on 2023-05-19
I'll add some more thoughts

- the hardware in a box matters...

I have ~25 boxes I use in QA-testing (*Quality Assurance*), and some GPUs can be problematic with some *linux kernels* but not others.. eg. some 2003 & 2004 IBM Thinkpads were great with 18.04 & the GA kernel stack, but didn't like some newer HWE kernels; other Thinkpads of the same vintage with different GPUs had no issues.

I ~recently replaced an nvidia GPU in a 2005 HP box as newer kernels had graphic-glitches that were beginning to become annoying in my testing (*using too much of my time*)..  I just swapped out the graphic card (*actually put in an OLDER card*) & issue is no longer present. Those issues appeared first in Kubuntu/KDE and Ubuntu/GNOME first.. eventually (3 releases later) were showing in Lubuntu/LXQt (*second last impacted*) so software stack on the same hardware can influence things too... the only *flavor* that wasn't impacted was Xubuntu/Xfce  (*with that card; on most hardware it's often Lubuntu last impacted*).

Yesterday I had a PC have issues.. screen just became unusable ~ten minutes after it was turned on, though operation appeared to continue.. I just opened box; withdrew graphic card, went & picked another card which would *fit *(ie. same slot type) & had same external connectors, turned box on & it boots normally. I was hoping I'd replaced the AMD.*radeon* card with another AMD.*radeon* card; alas no I think I used the Nvidia card recently extracted from the prior mentioned 2005.HP, but for now it's working perfectly (~30 hours operational).  Events (*hardware needing to be replaced*) like that make me feel the box is *old...*  (*that box is another 2008 dell*)

I had a reaction to your mentioning *light* and Lubuntu.. Lubuntu is **light** if you use it straight out of the box, or use it with Qt5 apps as intended.. If you're using GTK3 apps though; you may find Xubuntu and it's use of Xfce (GTK3) actually lighter, thus consider the whole stack.

Also of note... the 2005 HP box that has now finished it's QA-test, was been upgraded many times since it was originally purchased; like all my boxes it was a *refurbished *box (*when I purchased it*), but I've upgraded CPU since I got it, have upgraded the RAM numerous times (*only recently to maximum though, it was kept lower much of the time I had it as I felt it better for the QA* *I used it for*), and it probably resembles at least a *[newer] *2007 HP probably.

### Post by exploder on 2023-05-21
I have two FX series systems, 12 year old hardware. I run Mint 21.1 Cinnamon on these old desktops. Originally, I was going to donate these computers but after some performance tweaking they seem just as snappy as the new system across the room! Pretty much most of what I did can be found here.

[https://easylinuxtipsproject.blogspot.com/p/2.html](https://easylinuxtipsproject.blogspot.com/p/2.html)

The desktops each have 16 GB of DDR3 and 1 TB WD SSD's. Memory use at start up is about 650 MB and everything runs just great! One system has an AMD RX 460 and the other has an NVidea GT 1030, just cheap older graphics cards. I credit Mint Update for the reliability of NVidea graphics. I do use the proprietary driver and the way Mint Update works saves me a lot of headaches!

All the tweaking really made a difference. A stock install ran alright but performance was very underwhelming. If you sat down and used one of these older systems, you would never guess how old it is by it's performance after all the tweaking things. 

I wanted a modern OS that looked nice on these older desktops Cinnamon is actually very light with most desktop effects turned off. I do leave effects on dialog boxes turned on, the rest is off. I am going to donate one of these systems in the near future because I have way too many computers! 

Both these desktops are in modern cases and they even have some cheap, quiet RGB fans installed. Older parts can be found often for free or very cheap. All solid caps on these systems, that is a deciding factor for me when attempting to refurbish any system. Spinning hard drives are fine for extra storage but installing an SSD makes a huge difference! I have seen SSD's new as cheap as $25.00, it's worth the investment! 

For very little money and a few hours work, you actually can have a very nice system and get years of use out of it.

### Post by him610 on 2023-07-15
Technology and PCs go in cycles. Remember when we had these processors?
4 bit - before my time. I think I once had a HP calculator that had a 4 bit processor.
8 bit - ancient. I had one of these; I used it for years; my very first computer, a Leading Edge Model D.
16 bit - old. I had one of these with an intel 486 CPU.
32 bit - not so old, one can still find software available for it (Debian and maybe others.) I still have one of these.
64 bit - modern, current, mainstream CPUs. How long have 64bit computers been available? Ten, twelve years, or longer? I have four with various ages all running the same version Xubuntu 22.04.2 LTS.

Some of the interfaces and peripherals change; some get abandoned as others replace them. Think about the advances for mass storage, the Pci bus, network adapters, BIOS to EUVE, etc.
