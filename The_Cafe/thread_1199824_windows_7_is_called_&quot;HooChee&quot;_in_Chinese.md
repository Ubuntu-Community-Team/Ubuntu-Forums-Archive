### Post by Gizenshya on 2009-06-29
You are only partially correct.

Yes, they are calling it &#25143;&#19971; in China.  However, that is **simplified** Chinese, and that *does* make a difference.  you said those characters mean "window" and "seven."  The first character you posted (&#25143;) is phonetic, and has no meaning.  It is nothing but a sound.  It has the exact same pronunciation of the character for window (&#25142;), but it does not have a meaning.  You were completely correct about the character &#19971;, though.  &#25142;&#19971; Means, literally "window 7," but "&#25143;&#19971;" means "Hoo 7" (a sound, and then the number seven, quoting your romanization).

Simplified Chinese is primarily a phonetic language, keeping only some of the actual meaningful characters (like the characters for numbers).  The Communist government forced the country to change to a simplified, phonetic version from the actual millennia-old meaningful characters (commonly called "Traditional Chinese").  China is the only Chinese-speaking country that uses the simplified writing style.  The communist Chinese government not only killed the culture and heritage of the language (within its controlled territory, anyway), but it also killed many other aspects of tradition and heritage (again, within its controlled).  It calls itself China, but it has only controlled the land for half a century, and has decimated the culture of the land.  

What people call "China" ("the People's Republic of China," aka the PRC) today is only China in name, and has nothing to do with the China that existed before the 1950's on the land occupied by communist China today.  The thousands of years of culture and heritage that existed in the land controlled by the Chinese Communist party lives on in what is now commonly called Taiwan ("The Republic of China," or ROC).  The CCP taking that name is like the US naming itself Great Britain, and claiming its heritage.  A rose by any other name, is still a rose.  The US would still be what it is today, with only a few hundred years of history, despite the name. And as such, one should keep in mind that the PRC is a very young country, at only half a century.  Taiwan is a democratic *de facto* sovereign state (just to make that clear, it is NOT controlled by the PRC!), and is a strong ally of the US in south-east Asia.  The PRC would very much LIKE to control Taiwan, however, but they do not.

mikewhatever,

I was just giving a little lesson on some common misconceptions about that subject (the language issue, and why it is an issue), that is all.  I explained the situation.  You can probably guess my feelings on the subject from that post... but I kept to the facts, so that others can have a better understanding of it.  While I would prefer the situation to be different (as would pretty much everyone, because nobody is happy with it), that is the *status quo*.  Without understanding a little history in an extra paragraph or two, there would be 5 pages of Q & A about Who, What, When, Where, Why, How, and To What Extent.  So, in an attempt to preeminently address confusion, I covered the basics of those questions.  Now, anything further than the basics would not have been relevant to this topic.

and afeasfaerw23231233, it's not *quite* that simple... both are "correct", the former in "Simplified" Chinese, and the latter in "Traditional" Chinese.  Though the "hoo" character in the Simplified Chinese version does not have a meaning, the term, taken holistically is the official Simplified Chinese name of Microsoft's Windows 7.

Much like Google is not a word.  Googol (10^100) is a word.  Lots of people think that Google has the meaning 10^100, though it does not-- it just happens to have the same pronunciation.  But, Google IS the name of a company, and is therefore correct.  If I named my Cat Google, just because it isn't a word, does not mean it is incorrect.  All of that might be inspired by the word googol (no idea if it actually is), but the word does not have the same meaning.  So, if I say the sounds "goo""gol," two things can come to mind.  Google, Inc, and googol (the number).  Depending on the context, people can understand what I'm talking about.  Simplified Chinese uses this concept to take out characters with redundant pronunciations (hence, simplified), at the cost of losing thousands of years of tradition and culture in meaningful characters.  However, people can usually understand what they are talking about, if given enough context.  So, they are taking meaning from the words, and making it a phonetic language, like ours.  We only have 26 characters (well, 36, including numbers), and we make hundreds of thousands of words.  The letter "b" means nothing on its own, but we can use it along with other characters to make character strings that do have meaning (words).  But the Numbers (0-9) DO have meaning on their own.
