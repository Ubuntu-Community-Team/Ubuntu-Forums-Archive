### Post by samjh on 2007-09-10
As long as what you are learning is Krav Maga, and not someone's personal slant in self defence labelled as Krav Maga, it's worth a look I guess.

The problem with Krav Maga is the commercialisation of it, and the decline of general quality which inevitably follows commercialised martial arts (just look at Tae Kwon Do), including combatives (Krav Maga being one example).

One thing you've got to remember when it comes to combatives is that you are able to learn combatives in almost any decent martial art.  If you train with your focus on "**martial**", rather than "art", you'll end up with those very techniques and attitudes which various spin-offs of combatives aim to teach.  All combatives derive their roots from traditional martial arts - be it Oriental, western Medieval and Renaissance, South-East Asian, etc.  The basic principles of practical combat have remained the same, for as long as the human body and mind have remained the same.

### Post by samjh on 2007-10-01
> **Hallvor said:**
> That does not make sense. Some martial arts are better suited for self defense than others. It is not all about attitude, like you claim. You cannot expect a style will large gaping holes to be as effective as a more versatile one for self defense.

It seems my post has been either misunderstood, or not fully put into context.

What I was alluding to was not that EVERY martial art is suited for self defence.  Rather, my point was that there are common themes that run through almost all martial arts, which have been distilled into what is commonly known as modern "combatives".

These themes include:[list][*]Timing[*]Range[*]Structure (the physical relation between various parts of the body)[*]Protection of vital areas of the head, neck, rib-cage, groin, etc.[*]Use of combinations in attack and defence.[*]Efficient transition between various strikes, holds, throws, and attack and defensive movements.[*]Basic open-hand and closed-fist strikes, elbow strikes, low-level kicks to the forward and flank, and knee strikes (subtle differences exist, but fundamental principles are very uniform).[*]Minimising vulnerabilities to oneself during attack.[*]Creating opportunities for simultaneous attack or counter-attack during defence.[*]Agile and deliberate movement.[*]...and more.[/list]
It's not difficult to see why combatives draw upon these common themes of traditional martial arts.

Combatives are designed to impart the most useful fighting practices onto soldiers with minimum amount of training, in the shortest time.  There is no finesse to combatives training.  Finesse and technique are replaced by raw aggression, speed, and sheer physical strength.  The differences in philosophy and technique are blurred so that they are not really relevant to combatives.

So if you spend a couple of years training in Karate, Judo, and perhaps boxing, and then distill the most useful stuff from those styles, you'll end up with something that is very similar to what is covered in military combatives training.  The only things you probably won't have are bayonet fighting, weapon retention, and the kill-or-be-killed mentality that military training gives you.

Most people don't need combatives training for self defence.  What most people actually need are self-discipline, basic striking skills and holds, and tactical aptitude.  If you are taught any of the traditional martial arts by a worthwhile instructor, you are likely to learn those things sooner or later.  If you're taught combatives, you'll learn them very quickly.  Neither will make you into Bruce Lee reincarnated, but it will provide essential survival skills for when the crap hits the fan and you've got to escape with minimal damage.
