### Post by cozmic2 on 2014-08-23
Hey!

I'm posting this topic because I am interested in participating in the open source community, but frankly, don't know where to start.

I'm pretty good at coding, mostly java and PHP. I've started my own open source projects before and put them up on GitHub. The projects work, and look pretty good (mostly web content management system stuff), but I have never been able to get anyone interested in them. I've tried a number of attempts to market them, based on advice I've read in blog posts and whatnot. There are a few websites where you can post links to your project. I've tried those, but I don't get any feedback from anyone. I've tried posting on a few relevant forums. I get a few comments, "That looks really good," etc, but no real interest. No suggestions. No one really wanting to use the software, let alone contribute any code. In about a week the topic falls off of the face of the forum and dies a slow death. I've also tried using twitter, which is what most of the blog posts suggest you do: "How to market your product to the masses using twitter," etc. etc. etc. The problem is, I have no idea where to start when I create my account and have zero followers. I tried following a few open source people, but none of them follow me back. In a few words: I am not good at marketing. I am good at coding, but it seems no one is interested in the code I write (outside of my day job, and that code really isn't mine. It's the property of the company I work for).

My question is: how can I get people to take interest in my software? Do I need to start by getting involved in the community in other ways? Even if I can't get someone to use my software, I at least want feedback. Even someone saying "This is terrible" is infinitely better than total, deafening silence.

Thanks for reading, and I hope someone here can give me some advice.

### Post by J_Me on 2014-08-24
I'm not a marketing person but here are some questions you need to ask that may help.


1- Are there any other products in the market that do the same or better job than your product.
2- If there are other products how are the people that use them getting hold of the software. e.g. closed source, a distro specific package, compiling the software themselves from source.
3- How many people use a product like yours. e.g. is it something like angry birds or is it a widget for a specific distro that hasn't been released yet.


These maybe difficult questions to find out and the results maybe negative so brace yourself.


But the most important thing is keep at it.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2014-08-24
That is the nature of open source.  Look around at existing open source projects and see if there is a way to contribute--testing development versions, submitting bugs, writing documentation, and even coding new features or plug-ins.
[I]
You can't make people come to your parade.[/I]  My father use to say that and I never understood what it meant.

Do you have a blog?  Sometimes just putting down your thoughts a few days a week on technology or open source matters will gain you a following.  Then you can put posts about a recent open source project that you are working on.  Give a presentation about your project at a local Linux User's Group (LUG) meeting.  Write a whitepaper about your project and submit it to one of several linux magazines.  Give a presentation about your project at a regional open source conference.

This is the route that Johnathan Thomas took when he unveiled his new video editor [OpenShot]("http://openshot.org/developers/").  He had a need to edit videos of his kids and the linux video editors at the time were few and clumsy.  So he sat down and wrote one.  Every year since, he has given updates on the project at the SCaLE conference through entertaining presentations (watch the videos) and hosting a technical booth on the conference floor.  He ran videos in his booth that were created in OpenShot and that would gather a crowd and open a discussion about new features and what contributions were needed to move the project forward.  

Just following Johnathan's example is one way to improve the "buzz" of your open source project.
