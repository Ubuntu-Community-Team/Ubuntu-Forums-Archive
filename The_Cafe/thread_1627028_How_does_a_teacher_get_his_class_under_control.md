### Post by czr114 on 2010-11-21
There is a certain amount of disruption which is so minimal as to be difficult to sanction individually. I remember classes with a lot of background noise, students not in their seats, clock watching, etc.

It was the worst during transition times.

When much of the class is adding to that, there is only so much a professor can do. What it comes down to is students lacking respect for the educational environment, as well as the large sums many students spend to be in those classes.

It wasn't always the fault of the students, though. In the hard sciences, students were responsible for everything, including ensuring errors didn't propagate from step one onward. That meant lots and lots of study, often to the point of autodidacticism. When the professor comes to class to talk vaguely, share stories, reminisce, hand-hold those who didn't learn last week's material, misallocate time weighting among concepts, etc., it's not hard to see why so many students weren't above creating that background distraction.

I can remember many a class where little actual learning occurred during class hours. The students who were learning everything on their own, and coming to class only to sit and be assessed, can't fully be expected to be into the lecture when the professor wants to drone on about long-dead mainframe schedule frustrations or green screen induced headaches when students are supposed to be learning network programming.

The whole educational system is in need of a revamp. So much of what can be learned can be done with students working through automated teaching systems or by TAs employed to keep the pace. There seems to be a shortage of dedicated, energized professors; let's devote them to the classes which truly benefit from group dialogue.
