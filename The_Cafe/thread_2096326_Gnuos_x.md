### Post by Aaron Christianson on 2012-12-19
Once upon a time, in a land called MIT Artificial Intelligence lab, there was a hacker named Stallman who got mad because he wanted to fix a bug in the operating system he was using (some kind of Unix), but company that distributed it wouldn't give him the code.  Boy, was he ever angry.

So, Stallman set out to create a free operating system, which he called GNU (meaning GNU is Not Unix. recursive acronym. it's a hack. GENIUS). First, he invented the idea of free software, which everyone else today calls open source or FOSS or FLOSS, and coined the idea of copyleft (ok, this really is a genius, cause it hacks the legal system). Then, he and his pals wrote lots of programs to recreate the userland of a Unix system. This was good. These programs are useful indeed. Anyway, they had some trouble coming up with a kernel for it. They also never got around to creating a window system, but their desktop environment was pretty alright.

GNU was used with various kernels; Solaris, BSD, you name it; but it's own kernel was a piece of junk, and it's never going to be released for real. I could say it was used with various Window systems, but there is only X. Hopefully not indefinitely.

However, one day, a brilliant young-ish man by the name of Steve Jobs came along. He and his peeps created a hybrid kernel based on Mach and FreeBSD, along with a very shiny window system and and some nice APIs called Cocoa, and he put it on some bad hardware (but latter he wisely switched to intel). It was epic win. Hipsters everywhere hailed it as the ultimate achievement in design, and let's be honest, it is pretty pretty and pretty functional. (OK, so I skipped the part where he was bought back by the company he started, etc., but moving right along...)

Sandwiched right in the middle of this shiny, magical, immaculately conceived operating system was much of the software from the GNU project, hiding safely inside the terminal, an application that most users would never open.

So, Steve Jobs supplied the components that the GNU project was missing (well, everything above the kernel was proprietary, but whatever). However, instead of giving credit where credit is due, he, like any great artist, stole. He called the system OS X instead of going with some sensible title like GNU/Darwin/NeXTSTEP. What a cruftmuffin.

Anyway, I'm here to ask everyone to please use the term GNU when refering to OS X in the future. Let's give credit where credit is due.
