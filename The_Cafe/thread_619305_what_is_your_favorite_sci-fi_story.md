### Post by ticopelp on 2007-11-23
> **Vansinnesvisan said:**
> I don't see why Star Wars is considered sci-fi. It is fantasy in space. I think sci-fi fringes on the ends of probable and possible science. It explores possibilities and ramifications; as well, extrapolates where science one day may be. For example, something like Star Trek would explore the technical and ethical consequences of an interstellar race with peculiar views of race and religion. While Star Wars lays aside all things technical or cerebral and focuses on adventure, mysticism, and story.   

Star Wars is just Lord of the Rings - but in space, to me. Great story, but not sci-fi.

Sci-fi in general is too broad a term to be limited like this. By this definition, a lot of famous and influential sci-fi works would be inadmissible, including many of the other books cited in this thread. Star Wars isn't hard sci-fi, certainly, but it comes out of a long-standing [Space Opera](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_opera) tradition that has shaped a lot of modern sci-fi storytelling. If you're going to write Star Wars off as "not sci-fi," you'll also have to write off Douglas Adams, E.E. Doc Smith, Robert Silverberg, Frank Herbert... uh oh. 

Star Wars isn't serious, and it isn't cerebral or technically centered, but I think science fiction is more than broad enough to embrace it.

### Post by YoungCthulhu on 2007-11-30
I agree with Rune0077, ChilliBob and Humph  with your choices of SF authors.  You all got it so right _*even without asking me first!*_  :lolflag:

Basically you can't go wrong with anything by 

[LIST]
[*]Philip K Dick
[*]Iain M Banks
[*]Greg Bear 
[*]William Gibson
[*]Theodore Sturgeon
[/LIST]

I also think that Nancy Kress is a very under-rated hard SF author but that her short stories are better than her full length novels.  She really puts in a lot of effort in researching the basic physics behind her stories before putting pen to paper.  IMHO Greg Bear, of course, is the master of background research

I think the short stories of Charles Stross are much better than his novels, although I really enjoyed and can recommend "The Atrocity Archives" and the recent "Jennifer Morgue", but then I am a huge fan of H P Lovecraft (see below) and both of these novels are really tongue-in-cheek "Cthulhu Mythos" novels.  In addition, Charles Stross is a great champion of the Linux, Open Source and Creative Commons causes. :KS  :KS.  Like NK, his short stories are magic!

My pick of the most terrifying story would be "The Father Thing" by Philip K Dick, closely followed by "The Man with the Horn" by T E D Klein.  The strangest "The Voluntary State" by Christopher Rowe, the best researched/informative "Darwin's Children" by Greg Bear.  The best end-of-the-world novel would have to be "The Forge of God", also by GB.  The funniest stories involve the antics of the AI starships in the "Culture" novels of Iain M Banks (eg: "Excession", "The Algebraist" in particular)

I read "Dune" and "Ender's Game" and enjoyed them both, but would agree with many above that the sequels were nowhere near as good. :(
