### Post by aysiu on 2009-07-25
Even though I feel that the warrant had *some* justification, I also think there should be a bit more than was provided in the case of that Boston College Linux computer:
[http://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2009/04/boston-college-prompt-commands-are-suspicious](http://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2009/04/boston-college-prompt-commands-are-suspicious)

So it's not always true that search warrants are based on proper evidence, but they often are and are not so easy to come by.

If this search (in your situation) is, in fact, baseless, then the person who instigated the search will come out looking foolish. If, however, you seem to have something to hide (through encryption, virtual file shredding, physical destruction of the drive), it will appear to the police that whatever that person accused you of is probably the truth, and the search was justifiable, which means they will just find more reason to invade your privacy further, and it means they're more likely to believe this person in the future and less likely to believe you.

Yes, in theory, you should have the right to privacy, but the way to fight for it is through the court system and proper channels, not through encrypting your data or destroying your hard drive. Again, you don't say what country you're in, but here in the US we have an organization called the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) that's dedicated to protecting individuals' rights. You may have something like that in your country as well if you're not in the US.

Fight it through the proper channels. Even though it's terrible someone is looking through your private things without just cause, it looks as if you're in a messy situation regardless of what you do. Better to show the police you have nothing to hide than to give them further cause to keep suspicion on you.
