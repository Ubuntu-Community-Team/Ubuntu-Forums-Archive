### Post by buzzingrobot on 2015-08-21
None of that surprises me. Been there, done that.

 It is difficult for large organizations -- like Munich -- to move large numbers of people -- like employees -- from one software base to another. Software changes imposed by management won't be received with any enthusiasm.  They may be actively resisted, especially if employees are looking for another grievance to throw at their employer.

Regardless of what some say, LibreOffice and other FOSS software packages that mimic Windows software *are* different. The points of difference may be few, but it only takes one to frustrate an employee who has learned to do a few specific tasks in Office by rote. From that you get unhappy employees, unhappy customers and clients, lower production, increased training costs, etc.

Additional irritation and costs arise when people in your organization send files to other organizations which are not 100% compatible with the software in use there.  In the case of Munich, suppose they send spreadsheets to Berlin every month. So, what happens when Munich's LibreOffice spreadsheets require an extra click or two in Berlin?  Berlin will complain  to Munich about its broken spreadsheets, that's what will happen.

There's nothing for these organizations to gain from switching to FOSS if they need to spend time and money just to be able continue doing exactly what they did with Windows.

Even if workarounds exist, why should anyone who already owns and uses Windows bother with that?  Why spend the time and the money to move to Linux, discover the issues, and train people on using workarounds?  It makes more sense -- financial sense - to just stay with Windows.

An individual may move to Linux and never experience any issues.  But, an organization with hundreds or thousands of employees using their computers for many, many different functions very likely will.

*Any* change to employee procedures is disruptive.  I've been professionally involved in cancelling $7-figure software purchases due to hostile reception by employees. (They were right, too.)

Licensing costs are not usually a controlling factor in software choice over the long term.   The alleged wonders of FOSS freedom and the alleged evil of Microsoft don't count for much in those environments. You have to have an answer for the questions: What can Linux do for me that Windows cannot? Why is it worth the hassle and risk of moving?
