### Post by earthpigg on 2010-09-10
Hi,

I'm not anywhere near a teacher or college professor, but tell me what you think of a course titled as such being composed of the following curriculum: 

Assume two sessions a week, 1.5-2 hours each.

First day, survey. Students list what they use and are most familiar with, and how knowledgeable they are in general.

Ground rules: You are allowed to 'cheat'. You can ask a friend for help or advice in real life, no reason this shouldn't also be allowed in a class like this. Phone calls and the like are allowed during any assignment, and so is talking to the person sitting next to you. The only real rule would be that no one can physically touch your computer except you - remote desktop and sshd will also be prohibited. 

Students will be advised that all assignments can be completed with the provided materials at zero cost, and nudged in the right direction if they are seen taking their credit card out to complete an assignment. If they prefer to go that route, then that is perfectly ok.

The class would start with each student having a computer identical to what s/he would get if they went to a store and purchased one. Students that usually use Windows would get a 'new' Apple computer. Students that usually use Macs get a 'new' Windows computer. Anyone claiming to be a 'power user' or similar gets an Ubuntu computer. Grab-bag mix of Windows/OS X versions, along with a mix of Ubuntu's current and previous LTS, along with the most current version.

The Windows computer would have freshly had it's "system restore" media used, same with the Mac. The Ubuntu computer would be similarly setup as if it had just been purchased at ZaReason or System76 or Dell.

Rest of Week 1 and Weeks 2-3: Search Engine Use. 

Week 4: System Security. Students decide if protection in the form of a firewall, antivirus, etc, is necessary. Then, if needed, locate and install. ("Do I activate this Norton 30 day trial, do I use the first google result I see, or go elsewhere?") If the student decides none of that is needed, justify it in a paragraph or two.

Week 5: Word Processing & Printing. The student selects a printer (from the 30 or so on-hand), verifies it is compatible with his/her computer, installs it, configures it, and prints a letter to a friend using the word processor of his/her choice. Extra Credit for this week: Export the document to PDF, and e-mail it as an attachment.

Week 6: Photography. Modify a few things on a digital image that's been provided. Make the image brighter, format the picture so that it's resolution is the same as the monitor's native resolution, and make it's size is under 1mb while sacrificing as little quality as possible. e-mail it to the teacher as an attachment.

Week 7: Data Backup. Students will find a folder containing 1gb of 'vital' data on their computers, and need to decide how they will back it up. Dropbox, thumb drive, etc. Justify it with a paragraph or two, detailing what was taken into consideration, and the possible weaknesses of the method selected.

Week 8: Multimedia Backup. Students are provided with a DVD containing a family movie, a music CD, or a movie on DVD (student's choice). Create a fair-use backup of this, and write a paragraph or two about the software selection and backup medium used (hard drive, cd-r, dvd-rw, etc). Write an additional paragraph explaining the difference between copyright infringement and creating a fair-use backup.

Week 9: Establish Web Presence. Students are presented with a list of common social interaction websites. Linkedin. A few $0.00 blog outfits. Facebook (yes, facebook). Maybe a $0.00 online dating site or two. Assignment is to establish a presence on one of those that the student was not previously present on. Using an assumed identity for this assignment would be OK. If the student does not wish to participate in this assignment for ethical reasons of any type, type a paragraph or two about it.

et cetera

Mid-Term: Set up a router with password protected wifi, and enable port forwarding for a specific port to a specific computer.

Final: Upgrade the System's RAM. Go 'shopping' at a simulated computer store. Select the RAM appropriate for your system, and install it. If the upgrade works, that's an A. If it doesn't work, but the student can identify why it didn't work, also an A.

### Post by Brunellus on 2010-09-10
Who do you intend to teach?  If you're teaching rank beginners, I daresay this is too ambitious. 

I agree that there may be too much time in "search engine use."  I would have devoted that much time back in the mid-1990s, when Boolean searches still were necessary, but these days most search engine algorithms are good enough to give most people what they want without too much trouble.  Anyone requiring more will either discover it on their own, or, more likely, require further specialized training.

(An aside--that just made me think of the STUNNINGLY BORING Lexis/Nexis and Westlaw training sessions I had to sit through during my first week at law school.  Fortunately, I still remembered how to do Boolean searches...)

I would focus a basic course more on basic productivity--office and communication--and drop out the photography section, or leave that to a very minor component.
