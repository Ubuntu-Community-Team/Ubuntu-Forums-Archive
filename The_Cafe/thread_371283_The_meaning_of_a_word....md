### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2007-02-26
This blog post from the fellow who brought us such gems as Tomboy and Gimmie is really great.
[http://www.beatniksoftware.com/blog/?p=55](http://www.beatniksoftware.com/blog/?p=55)

He makes a great point. So many distributions seem afraid to mention the word Linux. In doing this, they are really poking at a big problem.
The Linux project is huge. All the people building the kernel, those simple terminal applications like Dialog, the efforts of Debian, all those drivers, the GNU project... it's one single common goal, and it is amazing that it is all compatible. This is not something that should be ignored.

By slapping a single exclusive title on an end product, distributions are concealing those efforts. They are saying "We built this!", even though they really only pieced it together with the help of thousands of others, a bit of glue and some paint. It is one of my own fears in releasing free software, that somebody could pick it up and practically call it their own. People take that risk, but I don't think there is anyone here who wants their work to go uncredited.
By not upholding the Linux project as a whole, distributions are accidentally forgetting the choice and the openness that is offered and how every desktop, be it SUSE, Ubuntu, Debian, Red Hat... is built around the same principle.

Linux is a big word, and this guy speaks the truth: It has meaning and it has a presence. When people hear about Linux they probably will know you mean that free, open operating system that is supported by a huge and powerful community.
Ubuntu? On its own, it means nothing. After all, without Linux, Ubuntu would just be a dream.


Ubuntu does fairly well with the Linux idea, in that they do in fact mention Linux quite decently in the documentation (and it's hard to miss it in Grub!), but in my opinion it does not hurt to make that kind of thing evident :)

### Post by userundefine on 2007-03-01
Well, it also has to do with the fact that what we collectively term "Linux" is in fact a collection of thousands of programs with their own names that operate around what "Linux" really is, the kernel.  And then there's the issue of do we call it GNU/Linux Ubuntu, or just Ubuntu Linux, or Gentoo, or Gentoo GNU/Linux, or Gentoo Linux, ad nauseum.  A lot of distributions do not ship vanilla kernels from kernel.org anyway, adding their own modifications, so they have a right to call it what they will.

Most companies *do* use the "Linux" brand, Red Hat Linux, Novell Linux Desktop, etc.  But nevertheless it's not all about Linux, it's also about Samba, KDE, Gnome, CUPS, and so on.  "Linux" may be a strong brand name even if it's a misnomer, but every distribution touting the name of Linux in their distribution advertising would be somewhat disingenuous to the collaborative nature of open-source systems (GNU GPL and similar licenses).
