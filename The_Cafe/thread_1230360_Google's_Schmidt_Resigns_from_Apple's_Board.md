### Post by Giant Speck on 2009-08-03
[http://www.cnbc.com/id/32267370](http://www.cnbc.com/id/32267370)

> **Apple **said Monday **Google **CEO Eric Schmidt resigned from the Apple board of directors, a position he has held since August 2006. 

The resignation comes at a time when the businesses of Apple  and Google  are increasingly overlapping.

Apple began as a PC-maker in the 1970s but now has expanded its business into a wider array of digital media with its iPod, iTunes, and iPhone products. Meanwhile, Google's business is widening from its early days when its business focused on Internet search. Google is attempting to challenge Apple with its own mobile phone, among other intiatives such as Android and Chrome OS. 

Schmidt had been recusing himself from board decisions in business areas where the two companies overlapped. 

In a written statement, Apple CEO Steve Jobs commended Schmidt for the work he had done on Apple's board. 

"Eric has been an excellent Board member for Apple, investing his valuable time, talent, passion and wisdom to help make Apple successful," Jobs said. "Unfortunately, as Google enters more of Apple's core businesses, with Android and now Chrome OS, Eric's effectiveness as an Apple board member will be significantly diminished, since he will have to recuse himself from even larger portions of our meetings due to potential conflicts of interest. Therefore, we have mutually decided that now is the right time for Eric to resign his position on Apple's board."


Weirdly enough, both of their stocks are up at the moment.

### Post by MikeTheC on 2009-08-03
There's actually two levels to this...

On the one, what both have said and done is quite legitimate. If you have to keep recusing yourself and leaving meetings that you would otherwise nominally attend due to conflict of interest, there's very little point in being there in the first place. After all, you're dealing with a situation in which the appearance of collusion -- which as we all know is illegal, boys and girls -- is simply unavoidable.

On the other, Google itself would need to start worrying about where Eric Schmidt's loyalties lie. As much of a fan of Apple's products as I may be, I won't lie and say I totally agree with all of Apple's business decisions. The fact that, for instance, the Android lacks touch and multitouch capabilities I believe can be quite clearly tracked back to Eric being on Apple's BoD in the first place. I'd be willing to bet that if he hadn't been on the board, the Android would have all the same technological features as the iPhone (regardless of the UI implementation).

Besides, Apple is about to get potentially dragged into the quagmire of an FCC investigation. Eric is wise to take this opportunity to distance himself from that as much as possible.
