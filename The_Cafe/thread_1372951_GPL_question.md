Now public domain is a specific concept within copyright law. Once something is out of copyright (which takes a really really long time due to various copyright extensions enacted internationally), that works automatically becomes public domain. Now in the world without copyright, there will be no copyrighting of derivative works, **because there is no copyright at all**.

In the United States, if the holder of the trade secret can show that they took reasonable precautions to guard their trade secrets (employee NDAs, safe-keeping of documents, etc) then anyone obtaining that secret can be enjoined from using it. For example, if you obtained the recipe for Coca-cola, the company can sue you to prevent you from using the recipe. 

In fact, regarding the original question of this thread, a company wishing to keep their derivative of GPLed software internal might use a click-through agreement as one means of guarding their trade secrets (I doubt that many do this, but courts might very well consider such measures as evidence of an effort to protect company secrets).

This is not really written into the terms and conditions of the GPL, but it was addressed during the drafting process and the conclusion was to place privacy concerns (of both persons and corporations) above those of Free Software advocacy. The issue is directly addressed in the GPL FAQ under [Stolen Copies]("http://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-faq.html#StolenCopy").
