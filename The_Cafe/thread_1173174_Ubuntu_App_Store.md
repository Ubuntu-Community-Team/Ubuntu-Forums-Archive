### Post by E1000 on 2009-05-29
The problem with Linux adoption is not that the system is immature, nor is it that Linux is too hard to use (Ubuntu pretty much solved that problem).

The problem is commercial support. Many commercial applications have no viable (for the professional) open alternative. Mathematica, Adobe products, AutoCAD... the list could go on. Software companies are hesitant to support Linux because the market is fragmented and software distribution is difficult. Many also fear that once they sell one copy, a software pirate will copy it and upload it too all of us "cheapskates" in the Linux community. Also, if Ubuntu goes out of style tomorrow, do they want to take the time to repackage another port?

If Ubuntu had an equivalent to the iPhone app store, several problems would be solved at once. First, distribution would be taken care of. Second, it would create a steady revenue stream for Canonical (even if they took only 2% it could be substantial). Third, it would bring commercial credibility to the open source world; there would soon be evidence of financial success for Linux based proprietary software. Lastly, the app store could act as a platform for which any Linux distribution could work to become compliant to the standard and join in on the app store goodness. It could take the fragmented world of _consumer_ Linux, and unify it.

Closed source on Linux is not what many free software idealists strive toward, but it is a fact of life that in **some** cases, closed source works better and is more practical. If Ubuntu took this step, a world of possibilities would open up to in in the netbook market, and in the consumer market as a whole.

The problem with linspire/Lindows is that they chose to make the platform closed. This does not solve one of the largest problems; market fragmentation. If Ubuntu made an open platform, they would share the benefits with many other desktop Linux companies, but everyone would reap the greatest benefit; commercial viability.

Issues with the revenue model would be brought up if companies like Redhat or Novell were going to consider complying to the Ubuntu standard (the last thing that anybody would want is multiple competing Linux app stores). But if the business tycoons at Canonical could figure a way to play nice with the big guys, many of the little Linux distributions would fall into compliance out of necessity.


Also, this should not be seen as a threat to open source. The us-vs them mentality is the reason why Microsoft is bleeding money. Companies like Apple, Novell, Sun (RIP), Redhat, and even Canonical, realized long ago that it is possible to have the best of both worlds. Ubuntu is, and would continue to be, a platform based on free software. But an app store would bring opportunities for many business that had previously written Linux off.
