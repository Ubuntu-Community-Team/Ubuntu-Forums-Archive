### Post by Sean Moran on 2009-10-07
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric_efficiency](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric_efficiency)

The fundamental concept of volumetric efficiency refers to the relationship between what goes in and what goes out, and tends to be measured in percentages above or below 100%.  As far as i know, it mainly refers to internal-combustion engines (car engines), but the same maybe applied colloquially to the input and output of other aspects of modern life, including us human beings.

 As an example outside of motor-racing, a coal-fired power station may provide enough energy to smoke up a whole city, but costs  lot of the planet to do so, whereas as solar panel or a windmill (one for fine weather and the other for stormy weather) may create a relatively small amount of energy, maybe just sufficient to run an house or two), but the cost is payable only to the Sun and the Wind.  Other than that, it's FREE!  On a fine day with a good sea breeze, one might say that the volumetric efficiency of those alternate power sources could be 100% divided by zero ie. infinite, like an anti-matter powered car that runs just as well on an empty tank of gas. Reliability is the primary concern.  In the dark of a cold winter night with no wind, you gone unless you can fire up some scarce natural resources to fuel you along!  Car engines with lower ratings of volumetric efficiency are also generally seen as more reliable than Formula One engines, and that is why they have off-ramps to the pits at racetracks.

Human beings are also natural resources aka human resources.  Taking into account the major costs of a viable existence whether home or office, such as a roof, electricity supply, desk, chair and computer hardware, there's not a whole lot of the total that goes into software, but supposing you could get your software directly from the Sun, as a gift, the volumetric efficiency of your work output on a daily/weekly/monthly/annual basis would be improved by a significant margin, such as converting from coal to solar power, depending on the occupation those resources.

At the same time, the reliability may suffer,  such as one may have a drivers' license to operate an automatic and feel quite comfortable at 100km/h on highway, but behind the wheel of a stick-shift Ferrari at 300km/h then crashes.  This would have been known in motor racing as a 'user error' if computers were around when Fangio was famous, but luckily crashing an operation on a computer is not usually as hazardous as plummetting into the armco at the bottom of an hill at top speed.

Back to computers and open-source software, and bearing in mind that you already have an head start on the financial input with OSS, how has adapting to open-source software improved or diminished your overall volumetric efficiency over time? and if you are yet to adapt, what loss of volumetric efficiency strikes you as your greatest twinge in the hip-pocket nerve?
