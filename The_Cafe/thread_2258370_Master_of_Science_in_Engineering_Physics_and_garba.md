### Post by taipan4000 on 2014-12-27
The world will always need engineers. Without science and engineering there is no invovation. It's all a question of what type of engineers, where and when.

During the 1980s when I was a student, there was only one university in Australia that offered a course in aeronautical engineering. At the time, one of my lectures said that people who didn't want to work outside Australia should not study aeronautical engineering as there was no work for them in the country due to the absence of an aviation industry.

It also comes down to what is happening with the economy. For the past 30 years mining engineers were highly sought after in Australia. Not any more, about 10 to 15 percent are out of work - double the national average for unemployment. For geologists it's even worse.

The first thing you need to do is to decide what type of engineer you want to be. Then find out where that type of engineer is most needed and possible relocate to one of those locations. Most educated people end up being economic refugees, they move to where the work is. You've already started that by relocating from Sweden to the US.

I notice your education has been in engineering physics. In the early 1990s I worked with a person who had BSc in physics. He couldn't get a job as a physicist. He started working as a maths and science teacher in high schools. He then worked in mineral assaying laboratory, in the desert. He then studied geology part time and became a geologist. Now he's an executive of a mineral exploration company.

As John Lennon said, "life is what happens to you while you're making other plans". You may want to be a certain type of engineer, but opportunities dictate otherwise.

The other thing to consider is how do potential empoyers see you. What are their impressions of you? Maybe they don't think you would be a good fit for their company and its projects. Maybe you need to make enquires with different sorts of companies.
