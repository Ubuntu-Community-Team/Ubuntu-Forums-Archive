### Post by Simian Man on 2010-06-14
Learning to play a specific song won't really help you play that song any more than practicing a variety of music.  The hard thing is building up your strength and dexterity in order to change chords quickly enough for the song to sound good.  The person who said "1 or 2 days" is full of it; there's no way you can play that piece so quickly.  I'd really recommend getting a beginners book and going through it - or lessons if you can afford them.  If you just play that piece over and over, you'll likely get frustrated after a few weeks.

Another thing to consider is that a steel-string acoustic guitar (used in the piece you linked) is the hardest type to play because the strings are very heavy and tense.  It takes much more hand strength to finger a chord (especially a barre) than on an electric or a nylon-string guitar.  If that's the sound you like it's definitely something you can do, but keep in mind it will take a little longer.

### Post by Breambutt on 2010-06-14
Indeed'o.

I always tell aspiring new guitarists to practice on songs they know inside out and could play inside their heads. It may take some time before your fingers bend and stretch naturally so that you can nail any chord switches in a mechanical manner, but I think it's pretty easy and painless if you just start working on a song with a set amount of chords and don't have to worry about other things. Of course this doesn't constitute as well-rounded exercise, but more like a technical workout.

I'd also recommend getting a nylon string guitar before you gain more finger strength and develop hardened tips. It's also a good idea to record yourself playing every now and then and revise it maybe half a year later - even if it feels like you already got it nice and tight, it might sound pretty awful later on. I still do this with pieces I'm trying to polish up; although not by a half-a-year margin, but sleep over it and give it another spin.

### Post by steveneddy on 2010-07-02
To the OP:

Buy a good enough acoustic to learn on and that's it.

A $200 Yamaha acoustic guitar is a good starter guitar.

Get the guitar guy in the store to string it with light strings or maybe start with a "silk and steel" set of strings. It will be easier until your fingers callous up a little.

Truth be told here, starting playing will hurt your fingers. Those that really want to play will push through the pain and keep learning.

And do your best to find a good teacher that you are comfortable with. Tell him/her what you want to do and then just do it.

Have fun!
