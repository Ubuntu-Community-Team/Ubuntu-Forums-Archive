It's a different world now... different technology, different expectations, different users.

OLPC project, I think, broke the mold of requiring new, expensive and powerful computes to use with each new upgrade.

OLPC solicited manufacturers to build the machines before they decided on the design we now recognize.  Companies that spent time putting their version together went ahead with designing one for consumers.

These new devices offered portability and connectivity for a price that undercut the traditional expensive beige boxes at the expense of processing power.  So they sold the realization to users that most of what they do on the computer doesn't require expensive hardware (email, browsing, typing, etc.).  Even after Microsoft successfully killed it with convincing people they want Windows on it and proving that it's too feeble for Windows, the seed was still planted.  The form factor was more important than before and got people thinking to what they really use or need in a computer.

If this didn't break the mold people wouldn't have considered there was something different and nothing would have changed.  This pushed people to wanting to get on the Internet anywhere, anytime and cellular became a solution, as did the phone (and tablets).

So yeah, 20 years (even 10) and a lot has changed.  What the world wasn't ready for then, they are ready for now.
