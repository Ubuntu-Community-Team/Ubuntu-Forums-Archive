### Post by Laibcoms on 2008-01-10
You see, that is one of the main reason why FLOSS or "Free/Libre Open-Source Software" (take note of how it is exactly written) is highly favorable and more popular among the FLOSS Community (ie both Free Software and Open-Source Software) is because people read it as a verb, people misunderstood it as a "call to action" "Free Open Source Software".

So in the first place, you answered your own question why there is a need for "Libre", to remove the confusion and misunderstanding. To clearly show that this is NOT a call to action to "free" the "open source software".

In fact, we can argue that there should be an "and" like so: Free/Libre and Open-Source Software", which others do, which is okay, but the idea behind the "FLOSS Community" is to remove the distinction between the two schools-of-thought.

Secondly, and the most common reason, is to clearly tell the reader or the people that "Free" here means "Freedom". Libre is from the Spanish and French word which means freedom.

So "Free Open-Source Software" doesn't mean "make" open-source software free-of-charge. It tells about both the libre software AND the open-source software. Again, both schools-of-thought.

It's just that. There is no redundancy here.
If you research it, in other nations, they change "/Libre" to their own local non-ambiguous word for freedom.

Here in the Philippines, we call it "Malayang Software" (as short as that), because the word "Libre" in the Filipino language is as ambiguous as the English word "Free".

We didn't use "Free/Malaya Open-Source Software" because it is simply too long, and there is a big risk that the media and people who are not knowledgeable about the FLOSS Movement might write it as "Free/Malayang Open-Source Software" or "Free Malayang Open-Source Software".

Those two different way of writing it is are both totally different from each other and to FLOSS. Just like you, you mistake it for as a 'verb'.

You see, writing it simply as "Free Open-Source Software" causes the confusion in itself. FOSS was first used by the US Government, and that started the confusion.

Before the US Government introduced "FOSS", "FLOSS" has been in widely used. The European Union, African Union, and others use "FLOSS" when writing documents and official government statements and laws.

FLOSS removes the "verb" misinterpretation.


"Free/Libre Open-Source Software" that's the way it is.
Secondary choice: "Free/Libre and Open-Source Software".

Always remember, FLOSS is about removing the distinction between the two schools-of-thought, namely:

Free Software (ie Free/Libre Software), and;
Open-Source Software

The FLOSS Community and supporters had enough of the political differences and arguments, so FLOSS was born.

### Post by Laibcoms on 2008-01-30
> **Tiekyl said:**
> Hmm...I'm gonna post anyway.

I read all of it, but, I was left with one question. Is it possible to be open source and *not* Libre?

Yes it is possible.  I've encountered software that are open-source but not Libre.  These are the software that uses a License approved by the OSI (Open-Source Initiative) but that license is not approved by FSF (Free Software Foundation).

In other words, there are Open-Source licenses that do not give Freedom to the end-users.

On the other hand, there are Freedom Licenses or FSF approved Licenses that OSI do not honour.  Now how that translates to layman's term is beyond me, because you can NOT say that an FSF approved license is not open-source because part of the FSF ideals is for the software to be open-source.  (I'm sure I'm missing something here.)

I compiled the licenses that were approved by both FSF and OSI here: [http://snow.gameshogun.ws/3general/floss-licenses](http://snow.gameshogun.ws/3general/floss-licenses)

Do note however that it is NOT, in anyway, the bible of FLOSS licenses.  Im sure I missed a few and even made mistakes.  FSF and OSI have different names for licenses and also have the habit of categorizing different versions together (thus making it more confusing).
