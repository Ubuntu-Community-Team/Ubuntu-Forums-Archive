### Post by timzak on 2008-07-07
I just installed Xubuntu 8.4.1.  This is my first look at Xubuntu since the Feisty days.  I could be wrong, but it seems like the developers have made more of an effort to lighten this version up?  Maybe my memory is failing me and I thought Xubuntu Feisty was heavier than it actually was.

A clean install uses only 1.5 GB of hdd space!  Pretty trim.  Most of the app choices seem very smart for a lightweight distro, though I'm surprised Sylpheed or Claws are not installed by default.  I could see why FF 3.0 was included, but no lighter alternatives were offered either.

Perhaps the Gecko Media Player is too new to include, but I found it plays large movie files much more smoothly than the default Totem.  I tried a 250MB .avi file I made with my digital camera and it stuttered with 100% cpu usage with Totem, but Gecko was able to play it smoothly at around 90% cpu usage.  This might be a better lightweight movie player for future versions of Xubuntu.

Ristretto is nice...gets the job done.

I'm surprised there's no standalone audio player...music is played through Totem, I think.  Isn't Audacious pretty lightweight?

Overall, I was more impressed than I expected to be.

### Post by timzak on 2008-07-17
Just did a clean install of Gnome Ubuntu 8.4.1 on the same system I had Xubuntu and Debian XFCE on.  I'm trying to find the perfect OS for this old laptop (Dell 650 Mhz, 384 MB, 9GB).  Surprisingly, I was able to get the OS footprint down to 98.6 MB just by disabling unneeded services and auto-started apps (even Update Notifier uses 5 MB).  I also took out one of the panels, disabled the wallpaper, etc.  Nothing too complicated, yet I was able to get the RAM usage less than I could for Xubuntu.  Weird.

In summary, here's the tweaked OS memory footprint for three different distros on this laptop:
Xubuntu 8.4.1:    104 MB
Ubuntu 8.4.1:      99 MB
Debian Lenny XFCE: 56 MB

I think there are more options in Ubuntu for disabling memory-consuming items than in Xubuntu.  Perhaps that makes the difference.  For now I like Ubuntu's combination of memory usage and ease of use, so I'll stick with it.
