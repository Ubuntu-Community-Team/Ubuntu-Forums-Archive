### Post by mkendall on 2010-06-06
McRat,

What is incorrect about your assessment of the problem is that you're seeing it as, "What are the chances that a given child is a boy?" or "Given that my first child is a boy, what chances that my second child will also be a boy?" The answer to these questions is 50% (or rather 51%, unless the birth rate has changed in the last 30 years, but we'll ignore that). You appear to be seeing both the Tuesday birth as well as the other child as red herrings.

Leaving out the Tuesday birth, another way of asking the question in the original thread is, "Given all families with two children where at least one of the children, -- whether the first child, the second child, or both -- is a boy, what fraction of those families have two boys?" The answer is 1/3, because you'll have families with two sons, families with an older son and a younger daughter, and families with an older daughter and a younger son. (This is also assuming that the pool is large enough that the actual differences in numbers become statistically insignificant.)

### Post by mkendall on 2010-06-07
> **McRat said:**
> I will adopt a boy on 12/2010.  Paperwork is being finalized.

My wife is pregnant.  She has only a 1/3 chance of having a boy???


No. Your wife has a 50% (51%) chance of having a boy. Your question falls into the "What are the chances that a given child is a boy?" category, not the "One of my two children is a boy (we don't know which), what are the chances my other child is a boy?" category. 

It's like flipping a coin. Let's say I've flipped a coin 9 times and every time it came up heads. What are the chances the coin comes up heads on the 10th flip? The answer is 50% because the result of the 10th flip is independent of the first nine. And that's where your thinking seems to be taking you with the children, that each event is independent of each other - the first child will be either a boy or a girl and the second child will be either a boy or a girl so therefore there is a 50% chance that a given child will be a boy.

But it isn't stated whether the boy in the original problem was the first child is the boy mentioned or if he's the second child. Now we aren't looking at discrete events of conception. We're looking at families.

Let's take a pool of 1,000,000 families with two children. Approximately 250,000 of these families will have two daughters, 250,000 will have two sons, and 500,000 will have one son and one daughter -- 250,000 with the son being the first-born and 250,000 with a first-born daughter. If we disregard the 250,000 families with two daughters that leaves a pool of 750,000 families of which 250,000 have two sons, or 1/3.

Recap, you're looking at the wrong event of conception instead of the event of two children at least one of whom is a boy.

### Post by seanelly on 2010-06-07
mckendall, if you randomly pick one family from the list and one child from the family, the odds of selecting a boy is 0.5. Here are the odds of picking a boy laid out:

BB: 0.25*1 = 0.25
BG: 0.5*0.5 = 0.25
GG: 0.25*0 = 0

Now, although the odds of picking a boy from the BB group is from the outset is 1/3, the odds of the boy being from the BB group given that a boy is picked is BB/(BB + BG +GG) = 0.25/(.25 +0.25 +0) = 0.5.
