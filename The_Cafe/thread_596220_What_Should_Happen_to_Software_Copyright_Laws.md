### Post by juxtaposed on 2007-10-29
Copyrights... Get rid of them.
Patents... Get rid of them.
DRM... Bad, but it should still be legal.
Cracking DRM... Good, and it should be legal.

Even if I think something is bad, I don't want to make it illegal.

In short: You make something, your rights are to do whatever you want with it while it's in your possession, but once you've made a copy for someone else and sold it or given it to them, you have no special magical rights to still own the copy in their possession. Don't get mad and sue them when they've done something that you don't approve of with it, like share it with their friends.

EDIT: And don't give me the "you don't own it, you're just licensing it". "I buy something from you, I own it", not "I buy something from you, you own it".

### Post by MCrittenden on 2007-10-29
Thanks for the response guys. Trust me, I need all the input I can get for this thing.:wink:

So the consensus is that copyrights are alright but DRM only harms the innocent. Is there some other way to protect software that actually targets the pirates? I think developers are going to push hard for *some* type of protection. We're talking about billions of dollars and hundreds of thousands of jobs lost every year as a result of software pirating. To ask developers to just let it happen seems a little unrealistic. 

I'm also a little surprised that most of you guys seem to be OK with the current copyright laws. Last I checked, copyrighted software is protected for 70 years after the death of the author, or 85 years, in the case of a corporation. That means that by the time it is released into the public domain, source code will probably be both nonexistent and archaic. If the constitutional basis for copyrights is to promote advancement, this seems to be the direct opposite. I thought you guys, as pro-open-source as most Ubuntu users are, would object to this.

EDIT: Just saw aks44's post. You make a good argument for copyrights, but could laws be modified to not be so strict? For example, I think it was Lawrence Lessig (best known as the guy who founded Creative Commons) who suggested that copyrights be for five years, renewable once, and developers must submit a copy of the source code to some neutral authority in the government who will release it upon the copyright expiring. This way, developers still profit, but source code is also released while it is still usable, rather than dozens of years later. 

Again, thanks a LOT for the response.
