### Post by mimada on 2008-02-09
Look up hiragana, katakana, and kanji in wikipedia. Kanji is the chinese characters used by Japanese. Hiragana is a simplified set of characters often mixed in with Kanji. Katakana is used for foreign words adopted/adapted by the Japanese (i.e., television is terebi in Japanese and is usually written in katakana.)

Proper names are usually written in Kanji but since you made the name up you might just use Katakana. Hope this helps.

### Post by ryukent on 2008-02-11
Yazuki,

Perhaps a little explanation would help. Basically, in Japanese there are three character sets. Two of them are purely phonetic so each corresponds to a sound but does not have meaning (Hiragana and Katakana) and the third with each character having more than one sound and also possessing a meaning. It is customary for foreign (non asian) derived words to be written in Katakana. Here Yazuki would be written &#12516;&#12474;&#12461;. This is sharper and more simplistic than the other two. In Hiragana (which is normally used to phonetically write Japanese words it would be &#12420;&#12378;&#12365;. If you decide to use Kanji (the third kind) &#20843;&#38957;&#26408;, it becomes a lot more complicated. &#20843;&#38957; referees to a town. But the characters have meaning too. The first character is read differently when written on it's own. It means 8. The second means head. Finally, the &#26408; means tree. It actually looks like a tree is you look carefully. The problem is that these characters have many readings and people might not initially know how to read it in the way that you want. They would also be confused by the fact that the characters have meaning as well as sound. As it is not a Japanese name you are trying to spell, they might not immediately jump to the conclusion you mean Yazuki and that that is your name. If you use &#12516;&#12474;&#12461; it would be easier. However, you might decide you like &#20843;&#38957;&#26408; better and that 8 head tree is something you want to portray along with the name. In that case stick with it. If you want to write it using a more stylised Kanji, then download some other Japanese fonts..... there are many. Or you could ask a calligrapher to write it. Chinese or Japanese would both be fine. I hope that helps.

### Post by scottro on 2008-03-21
The Yasu of the restaurant mentioned can be easy, smooth, that sort of feeling.  (It can also mean inexpensive.)

Using katakana is clearer--katakana are often used in the same way we use italics.  Finding meanings for names can be fun--I write Scott as su as in sushi, lucky, auspicious, ko as in hokori, pride and for to use hito, person--it's pushing it, and is sort of a joke, and absolutely no one would read it correctly without explanation.  

There's probably several kanji with the pronunciation, or possible pronunciation of ya and ki.  Considering that zu could also be a "voiced" as linguists say, pronunciation of su, there's probably several for that one as well.  

(Although, voicing--using tenten for those who speak the language) only, as far as I know, goes into effect if the following syllable begins with an b, h, p, or m, so in this case, using a kanji for su would be wrong, since the next syllable is ki.)

Another nice choice for ki would be the kanji for spirit or energy.  I'd recommend going to a bookstore and browsing through a kanji guide, or finding a nice online one, and browsing through to find kanji you like for all three syllables.  For example, putting ya in at [http://www.saiga-jp.com/kanji_dictionary.html](http://www.saiga-jp.com/kanji_dictionary.html) gives me eight or nine choices, house, arrow, eight, smelt (as in refine, not the past tense of smell or fish) 

ki gives over 1,000 results.  :)  (A common syllable.)  Steam, energy, wood or tree, return, are some from the first page. 

Zu gives me around 300, including a brain, a drawing, and a shrine.  

Like Linux, there's lots of choices.
