### Post by BoneKracker on 2010-05-27
> **Ranko Kohime said:**
> When you start typing miniature dissertations as your password, especially in something like PGP or GPG, you can be fairly certain that Grandma's cookie recipe will remain a secret even if your girlfriend's laptop is stolen.

True, but with caveats.  Each _word_ is roughly the equivalent of a single character for brute-forcing purposes (regardless of language, and regardless of whether you think you have obfuscated it in "leet-speak").  So if you think are clever by having something like "@w3s0m3n3ss91" as your password (obfuscated "awesomeness91"), you aren't.  Once readily-available dictionary tables are run, you basically have a three-character password.  The time required to run such tables has decreased so rapidly, and the processing power become so widely available, that it has become unsafe to use word-based passwords (except in pass-phrases involving long strings of words).

It is best to avoid the use of words _in any form_ and ensure all your authentication mechanisms include brute-force deterrents (e.g., delays between authentication attempts; break after n failed attempts, etc.).

And since "grandma" is using her computer for credit card purchases, online banking, etc., the secret cookie recipe may not be the attraction.

> **BoneKracker said:**
> True, but with caveats.  Each _word_ is roughly the equivalent of a single character for brute-forcing purposes (regardless of language, and regardless of whether you think you have obfuscated it in "leet-speak").  So if you think are clever by having something like "@w3s0m3n3ss91" as your password (obfuscated "awesomeness91"), you aren't.  Once readily-available dictionary tables are run, you basically have a three-character password.  The time required to run such tables has decreased so rapidly, and the processing power become so widely available, that it has become unsafe to use word-based passwords (except in pass-phrases involving long strings of words).

It is best to avoid the use of words _in any form_ and ensure all your authentication mechanisms include brute-force deterrents (e.g., delays between authentication attempts; break after n failed attempts, etc.).

Now, the last time I checked, wherein a password contains mixed case elements, the checking fails if the password is spelled correctly, but the case is incorrect.  Which means if I were to type out the actual text of my password, there could still be hundreds of variations therein for a DICTIONARY attack, (the dictionary consisting of said typed password as the dictionary entry), much less a brute force attack.

Brute force, regardless of the speed at which it can go through generated passwords on any given machine, still requires several elements which may not necessarily be available.  In the case of encrypted files, it may not possess the encryption keys.  In which case it have to not only generate the key, but guess the password for it as well.  This is still well beyond the realm of any computer available at any level today, save the problems that some encryption types are noted for having.

The other issue is whether or not it can engage brute force at the speed of the machine, which can only be done when dealing with a local file.  Brute force against any network server is still largely improbable, due to the slow connections.

### Post by Ubuntiac on 2010-06-02
> **spezticle said:**
> the privacy matter with facebook is really really really simple.
don't put anything on there you don't want the world to see. plain and simple.

I wish it was that simple.

The problem most people are overlooking is the network aggregation effect. This is the effect where 100 pieces of data separate in the hands of different people are harmless, but aggregated into one place where they can be analysed can be very powerful and thus dangerous.

For example, while you may not mind the owner of each individual website you visit knowing you've visited. It's very different to have a single company knowing *every* webpage you visit, when and for how long and then comparing that to patterns of millions of other people to create a psychological profile of you designed to manipulate your behaviour in ways against your best interests.

Of course when this behaviour is carefully hidden away behind interfaces and PR that only publish the "benefits" (*cough* *cough*) of giving away each individual piece of data, the problems are not readily visible unless you happen to have experience somewhere like direct marketing firms, statistical analysis, mass psychology etc. Most average people have no such experience though and assume that posting a picture of them and their dog is just like showing someone on the street that same photo. That person though doesn't have access to the massive data stores, stats analysis and psychoanalysis resources that places like FB have. That person would also have a personal relationship to you decreasing the chances of doing something directly harmful to you. Look up Zuckerberg's IM conversation where he calls people who give him private information "dumb f**ks" to get an idea of how much reluctance Zucker would have in harming people who's data he now owns. Its not about whether anyone hacks into Facebook. There's millions of dollars of IT with incentive to prevent that.  It's not about the guy who might loot your place while you're away at a concert. A real, but relatively unlikely tabloid fear. The trouble is the pervasive, subtle yet powerful shaping of our minds and cultures in a way that has our worst interests at heart.

The trouble is that "Just say no to sharing" ignores that a lot of data is very difficult to *not* share. How many people surf exclusively through a proxy to avoid sharing their IP address? How many people's house isn't on Google Street View? How many people haven't had their name writen along side a photo of themselves somewhere online without their express permission? People once gave away all these kinds of data very easily. It used to be pretty safe. When they did, it was rarely stored for long at all and in most cases that data was all very decentralised. Technology has changed faster than habit, especially when the financial incentives are all in the direction of convincing you to give everything away... followed up with the personal information of your friends and family.

Just as the internet's network routes around non functioning nodes, so does the social network. You don't upload and tag photos of yourself? There's likely hundreds of other people who can and would - even if you don't have an account. As long as the people connected to you are part of the matrix, you end up with a de facto account with all kinds of data, even if you do nothing.  "Just say no to sharing" ignores that humans are inherintly social creatures who have always had a need to share, both about themselves and others. It ignores that even if we choose not to share about ourselves, that others will share about us whether we like it or not. This gave us communities. It gave us the internet as we know it. Open source definitely wouldn't exist without it. Unfortunately it's also the weapon now being used against us.

Removing your node (data) is almost meaningless. To avoid being profiled and manipulated requires removing enough of the nodes (other people entering data) that creates your profile whether you choose to or not.  The most likely way forward as I see it is the creation of an alternative that gives comparible (not the exact same) or better benefits and actually matches people's unconscious expectations of what's happening to their information: secure, decentralised to the point of being worth very little financially, only recorded as long as they wish and shared in ways that are clearly understood, easily connected to the people we actually care about.

Removing your node from the social network may make little difference, but the good news is that the network effect works in reverse, too. When the people around you voluntarily move their data to a more decentralised network, suddenly they're feeding in less data about you, too. The network no longer knows who you are and begins to crumble.

It's a fight we can win, but it will take more than ignoring it and hoping it goes away.
