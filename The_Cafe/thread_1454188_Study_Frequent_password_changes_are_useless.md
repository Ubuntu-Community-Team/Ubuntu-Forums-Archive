### Post by new_tolinux on 2010-04-14
Like said there:
> This article looks at the issue at one level - a stolen password.    While changing passwords often might not prevent unauthorized access in  that event, it can prevent problems in other areas.   Often times, in  larger companies, employees leave and nobody notifies the IT Department.    A password that expires in 30 days at least limits the long-term  problem of continual access in that case.   Co-worker snooping is at  least slightly mitigated by password changes.    The problem of  unauthorized access because someone selected "remember password" on a  public computer is also reduced.

So it isn't as useless as it seems when reading the article.

### Post by eriktheblu on 2010-04-14
Unfortunately, it will pretty much take a literal act of congress to change our password policy.

In my work I have half a dozen accounts. Each of them is on a different password change schedule, and all of them require a minimum 10 characters, 2 upper case, 2 lower, 2 numbers, 2 special characters. It is quite unfeasible to expect a person to remember those without writing them down.

I've gone to a method of using a password pattern, where the starting point shifts with the mandated password change. This way I can annotate the start point without leaving the entire password available.

I've heard with World of Warcraft accounts, the thieves will wait a while in order to conceal the download source of their software. This way the victim may blame an unrelated party.
