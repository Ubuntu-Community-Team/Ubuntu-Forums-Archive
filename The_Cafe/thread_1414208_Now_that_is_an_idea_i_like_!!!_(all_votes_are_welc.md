### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2010-02-23
I have an immense list of problems with this in my head. Here is a snippet:

[LIST]
[*]Providing this kind of selection at install could easily produce a belief that applications are locked in, and if one wishes to get different software he must reinstall the OS. After all, what sane product would do this otherwise?

[*]This adds an "Advanced" button. One of the reasons why GNOME (and by extension Ubuntu) is a shining beacon of hope for usability is that we don't have those. We don't ridicule our users with "advanced users only" sections, we don't arbitrarily hide certain features under headings that don't describe them just because we ran out of space in the rest of the UI.

[*]Outright encouraging the use of locked in codecs (which seems to be subtly slipped in here) is to give in to proprietary stuff; to make it happily accepted in the Ubuntu ecosystem. That is a bad, bad thing.

[*]The defaults are meant to be good. It is more productive to improve those than tell people "instead of our broken choices, you can choose any of these choices as well; each one is broken in a different fashion, so if you're really smart you can get a smooth system by switching between them." (Well, that's what it says to me. I actually consider the defaults very capable).

[*]The software still needs to be downloaded over the Internet and installed after the OS is installed. You can either do it via the install CD, which is slow and considerably less safe than doing it in the running OS; or, you can restart to your fresh new desktop and do it in Software Centre with an up to date list of all the software you could possibly install, less risk of failure, your DVD drive not wailing away, and the ability to keep working while the software is installed.
[/LIST]

Why should it be easy? It should be straightforward, as it already is, in the form of a 'try before you buy' live cd, with an installer that makes as few questions as possible. But easy gives rise to a lot of frustration later on. Isn't it better for a user, in the long run, to make a clean cut from windows, expect no similarities, and get used to different tools and software? An experienced user on the other hand, would know exactly what programs to install and remove after the installation. A specific tool would be nice, but there's the software center for that, already. So on the whole, I don't think there's really need for something like that.
