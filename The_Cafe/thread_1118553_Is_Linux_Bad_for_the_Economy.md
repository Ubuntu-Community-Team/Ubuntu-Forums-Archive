### Post by frup on 2009-04-07
How do you measure economy?


If you take six companies, lets say Intel, Google, IBM, RedHat, Canonical and Nokia.

These companies require certain software products to run and create there products. In a proprietary world each would require licences for all their workstations and servers, plus each would have to develop close to every product from scratch or pay huge prices for licences.

The GPL allows all these companies to run Free software and use it to create new products. Intel has an interest because it's hardware is pushed further and it can compete against rivals (such as Nvidia who does not offer Free software drivers)... The also get members of the community patching their mistakes.

Googles story is famous enough I probably don't have to explain it and then we take a look at android.

IBM produces solutions using Open Source. The open source nature allows customisation to any customer. The decentralised product system means that IBM is not the sole maintainer, development costs are lowered, increasing profit.

Add in redhat and canonical whose involvement is obvious.

Then we take any small company, usually to small too have any influence over a product. The open source nature of GPL software allows them to hire any programmer for as long as they need to modify products to increase their productivity. This creates a job for the programmer and efficiency for the company. The costs are far lower than modifying a proprietary product (in terms of licensing) and possibly even less than the actual cost of the software license... Surely that is good for the economy.

I probably haven't formulated this example the best it could be but I'm getting the basic outline.

Granted, this is all resting on the assumption that unemployment and welfare are *undesirable* outcomes to be avoided. ### Post by MikeTheC on 2009-04-07
The basic theory I've always understood behind the notion of "Free Software" or "Open Source Software" is that they feel software should be treated like a language, in that everyone has equal access and opportunity to learn and use a language. Imagine if every company had to invent their own language for their employees to be able to communicate with the company as a whole and other employees individually; and imagine if each company had to license that language, and so on and so on.

The analogy may sound silly, since there are no normally-used langauges out there which are copyrighted, patented or trademarked, but stop and think for a moment how much society has been able to flourish as a direct result of this fact.

### Post by Stupendoussteve on 2009-04-07
The only people who do not benefit from Linux popularity and growth are those selling commercial software, such as Windows. Other businesses are able to benefit from the support offered with commercial linux offerings, or using in-house support systems to use Linux at little or no cost. Businesses spend little or no money on the majority of their computer software (though people should always contribute some financially) and focus more on the activities that really support the economy, like creating and selling products.


I have no problem with someone selling software. I think the presence of free software (philosophically and financially) also drives the paid for services to release a better product, or be left on the wayside. If people could get free cars, many would use them, but others would still choose to pay for a premium if it was better.
