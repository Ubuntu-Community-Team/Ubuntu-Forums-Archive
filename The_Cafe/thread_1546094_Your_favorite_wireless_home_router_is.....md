### Post by simmonds78 on 2010-08-05
At home I'm using a Linksys WRT54GL with the tomato firmware ([http://www.polarcloud.com/firmware](http://www.polarcloud.com/firmware)).  

I've had tomato on this router for about 3 years now and my only reboots have been when I upgraded the firmware (out of habit not necessity) or when the power goes out.  Before tomato I was using DD-WRT and though I believe it does offer some more advanced configuration options I had constant stability issues and rebooted at least weekly. The new versions may have fixed these issues but I don't have a need to find out.  

The speed of 'G wireless' is not a problem for me, I doubt it's a problem for > 98% of users.  I don't do many huge network file transfers and when I do I don't mind waiting a minute or two.  Just a few years ago 54mbps was lightning fast.  My network demands haven't really changed since then.  Plus my internet connection is nowhere near 54mbps (thanks AT&T) so I'm only seeing the cap while transferring files on the local network, usually to/from my NAS which is mostly used for nightly backups while I'm asleep.

What I might be loosing in speed I feel is made up by reliability or hardware and flexibility of configuration.

Speaking on the reliability of this hardware+firmware combination.  At a friends business I was asked to set up a building wide wireless network for as little money as possible.  I used 7 WRT54GL routers with tomato in 'Access-Point + WDS' mode.  It worked without a hitch.  This network frequently sees 100+ users and I've only been back once to replace a ruined router after a storm.  No complaints.

I hope this helps somebody.

-simmonds78

### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2010-08-05
I have a D-Link DIR-655 and it's AWESOME. I know, it's hard to imagine, but D-Link's higher end consumer routers are actually really good products. I wish they just stopped making the hacked together cheap ones and changed their target market, because geeks give them a bad rep after using the sub-60-dollar routers&#8230;

(And yes, there's another detail here: any router that costs less than $40 is going to be useless unless you're really lucky, though third party firmwares help a little).

It has lots of features including all sorts of stuff Linksys likes to separate into more expensive products. QOS, Wifi guest zone, WLAN partition (so connected devices don't see each other), forwarding a public port to a different private port (so, port 19613 globally on the router could lead to port 21 on a local computer), emailed log updates&#8230; all sorts of cool stuff.

That's all stuff you can get with something like DD-WRT, naturally, but it's also really well built hardware. It has worked steadily for me for over two years and it hasn't faltered.

Love my router :)
