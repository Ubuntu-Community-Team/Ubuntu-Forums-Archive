### Post by DarkOx on 2007-11-17
> How are any of those BETTER than the proprietary software that you mentioned? What do they have that would compel someone to want to switch?

Sorry, I misunderstood your question at first. To answer it, I don't think anyone will switch to Linux for a single killer application. But I think they may switch for the default experience.

Amarok organizes your music collection better. It has features iTunes lacks, like the On-screen display -- if we include scripts, like the "Volume-per-song", it has even more features. It's superior.

Would anyone switch just for the music player? No.

AWN can switch to both the old flat view, and the new 3D view. According to a Mac-head friend of mine, when he upgraded to Leopard, the OS X dock could only use the 3D view. That combined with various plugins makes AWN more flexible and customizable. It's superior.

Would anyone switch just for the dock? No.

Compiz manages my workflow better than equivalent Window Managers on proprietary operating systems. Especially with the desktop cube metaphor and grouping windows in tabs, it helps me manage the information on my desktop better than I could on OS X or Windows.

Would anyone switch just for Compiz? Maybe, but probably not from OS X, with it's similar Spaces feature. 

Most proprietary productivity apps that are the focus of billion dollar companies (Photoshop and MS Office are examples) will be superior, feature-by-feature, to FOSS alternatives. They have more developers and more resources and FOSS isn't some magic process that can make good that difference. Especially when most FOSS developers, in particular for those doing desktop apps are doing this as a hobby, in their spare time.

Which brings me back to the default experience in Linux. I open a photo, and Gwenview gives me a more robust option to edit/view it than the default viewer that comes with Windows. I need to quickly edit a photo, and the GIMP is right there. Amarok is a joy to use. Compiz manages my information, and looks good doing it. Kontact/Thunderbird are about on par with Outlook 2007, but are far and away superior to Outlook Express (Windows Mail on Vista) which comes by default with Vista (as it doesn't have PIM functions like contacts or calendaring built it).

In short, the reason I use Linux, and the best argument I think it has going for it, isn't a single must-have application. It's that many of the applications are better in little ways that collectively make the experience superior -- and that this superior comes often right out of the box, at a fraction of the cost of proprietary apps.
