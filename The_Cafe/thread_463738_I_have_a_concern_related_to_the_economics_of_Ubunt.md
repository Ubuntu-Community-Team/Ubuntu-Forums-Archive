### Post by qstn9990 on 2007-06-04
It is clear that adoption and usage of Ubuntu has increased significantly in the years following its release. Distribution of the operating system at no cost undoubtedly facilitates this. After all, when faced with a pricing difference of hundreds of dollars, it is natural for many consumers to opt for a less expensive alternative. However, I would like to bring to your attention some concerns I have regarding the current state of F/OSS distribution at a large scale, with an emphasis on the possible economic outcomes of doing this.

Let us suppose that Ubuntu's popularity skyrockets over the years and acquires a significant amount of the operating system market. Needless to say, the short term benefits to the consumer would be endless; all the benefits of free software would apply, and much of the computer software available to consumers would be available free of charge. However, there may a much darker side to this issue, some of it obvious, and some of it less so.

Let us begin with the most obvious side of the issue. Persons employed in computer software development industry in the United States number approximately 1,197,600, [1] with a mean annual salary of anywhere between $70,000 and $88,000. If Ubuntu continues give away its software for free, many of these jobs could disappear. If Canonical doesn't charge for the operating system, it is clear that it will not profit from its sales, and consequentially it might not be able to hire as many software engineers and programmers. In addition, since all the of the *software* running on the operating system is also available at no charge and (in most cases) conveniently available, there is little doubt that consumers will dramatically decrease their spending on consumer software available for the operating system. What could result from this is thousands, if not hundreds of thousands of jobs lost.

I anticipate there would be replies indicating that Canonical intends to emphasize software support (rather then sales) if I did not cover this topic as well. Referring back to the first source, we see that jobs related to computer support in the United States number about 514,460, with a mean annual salary of $44,350. Clearly, then, if support became the focus of the software industry, its jobs would dwindle in number and pay less.

Of course, the GPL permits distribution for a price; this is part of the definition of free software. Considering human nature, however, it is possible to completely undermine and sabotage attempts to profit from commercial free software. Let us suppose, now, that Ubuntu begins to charge for its operating system, a feat which might alienate some of its user base (considering that many on this board cite the [nonexistent] cost of using Ubuntu on the desktop as a reason to switch from their previous operating system); but a feat that occurs nevertheless. Now, this same tendency of individuals to opt for software lower in price arises. The GPL requires the source code to be distributed as a precondition of distributing the software itself. This presents its own advantages, as many of you no doubt already know. The unfortunate fact that there is one significant *disadvantage* to doing this if the original vendors of the software intend to make a profit: that the source code can be recompiled by a third party (in that same volunteer effort so familiar to open source) with intent to make it available at no charge (this is presently the case, in fact, with Red Hat Enterprise Linux and CentOS) [2]. In a similar manner, consumers might flock to the version that is available at no charge, and the original software vendors are, again, forced to relay exclusively on support to generate profits, which would bring us back to a beginning of a ruthless and perpetual cycle.

For all of these reasons, profiting from a free (in or both senses of the term) operating system may be a difficult, if not an impossible feat to achieve. Believe me, I certainly hope I am wrong about all of this; but as of yet I have failed to become convinced that Canonical and much of the other free software distributors can successfully replace the jobs created by proprietary software corporations, as well as other businesses. As Ubuntu increases in popularity, we must not only take short term benefits to the consumer into consideration, but long term economic consequences as well.

   A young hoodlum, say, heaves a brick through the window of a baker&#8217;s shop.  The shopkeeper runs out furious, but the boy is gone.  A crowd gathers, and begins to stare with quiet satisfaction at the gaping hole in the window and the shattered glass over the bread and pies.  After a while the crowd feels the need for philosophic reflection.  And several of its members are almost certain to remind each other or the baker that, after all, the misfortune has its bright side.  It will make business for some glazier.  As they begin to think of this they elaborate upon it.  How much does a new plate glass window cost?  Two hundred and fifty dollars?  That will be quite a sun.  After all, if windows were never broken, what would happen to the glass business?  Then, of course, the thing is endless.  The glazier will have $250 more to spend with other merchants, and these in turn will have $250 more to spend with still other merchants, and so ad infinitum.  The smashed window will go on providing money and employment in ever-widening circles.  The logical conclusion from all this would be, if the crowd drew it, that the little hoodlum who threw the brick, far from being a public menace, was a public benefactor. 

   Now let us take another look.   The crowd is at least right in its first conclusion.  This little act of vandalism will in the first instance mean more business for some glazier.  The glazier will be no more unhappy to learn of the incident than an undertaker to learn of a death.  But the shopkeeper will be out $250 that he was planning to spend for a new suit.  Because he has had to replace the window, he will have to go without the suit (or some equivalent need or luxury).  Instead of having a window and $250 he now has merely a window.  Or, as he was planning to buy the suit that very afternoon, instead of having both a window and a suit he must be content with the window and no suit.  If we think of him as part of the community, the community has lost a new suit that might otherwise have come into being, and is just that much poorer. 

   The glazier&#8217;s gain of business, in short, is merely the tailor&#8217;s loss of business.  No new &#8220;employment&#8221; has been added.  The people in the crowd were thinking only of two parties to the transaction, the baker and the glazier.  They had forgotten the potential third party involved, the tailor.  They forgot him precisely because he will not now enter the scene.  They will see the new window in the next day or two.  They will never see the extra suit, precisely because it will never be made.  They see only what is immediately visible to the eye.*

### Post by smiggs on 2007-07-03
Even if the software is free, you will still have to employ people to make that software work. It might be that the team is now spread across a number of different organisations but people will still get paid to work on the software if it of value to organisations. For applications companies support and customisations are their main operations and since generally this is business to business this will continue and on the consumer side Google has demonstrated that a good product and brand lead to revenues through advertising rather than selling software. At an operating system level progress will be driven on two levels hardware and software companies will employ people to make sure that their product works well on the OS and you'll have consumers adding in the nice to have features. Small organisations will bundle these products together as Distributions and perhaps provide support, many firms already provide support for Windows in addition to the support given by Microsoft so this is hardly new.

In summary life would continue and now every hacker would have access to the source code but we would remove Microsoft from the equation, our progress would no longer stilted by their controlling influence. Free software and open source does not mean the end to making money on software, just because you can get hold of the source does not mean you know what to do with that source although of course you may choose to employ someone who does.
