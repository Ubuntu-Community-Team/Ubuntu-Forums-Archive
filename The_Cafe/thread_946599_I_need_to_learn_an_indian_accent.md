Ok, their accent is very easy to do, if you know how.

It is born entirely of their language. So to get an Indian accent, learn the sounds represented by an Indian alphabet, write English words in that alphabet, then pronounce them as they are written.

[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_English](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_English)

It is important to get their sounds right. Some of them are hard for English speakers to do without thinking about it.

### Post by Sporkman on 2008-10-13
When you do your d's and t's, touch the tip of your toungue farther back on the roof of your mouth than usual. They actually have different versions of these consonants depending on what spot the toungue connects, IIRC (originally based on sanskrit syllables..?)

### Post by LaRoza on 2008-10-13
> **Sporkman said:**
> When you do your d's and t's, touch the tip of your toungue farther back on the roof of your mouth than usual. They actually have different versions of these consonants depending on what spot the toungue connects, IIRC (originally based on sanskrit syllables..?)

Yes, that is right.

That is called a retroflex sound, as opposed to dental. They also have aspirated and un-aspirated sounds as well.

Say "night rate" and "nitrate". The middle "t" is aspirated in "night rate" and not in "nitrate". We usually don't make such distinctions, but in Indian languages, it is important. 

The sounds each have a letter in the various scripts (except Nastaliq, a Persian script, which uses a version of the choti he to mark aspiration).
