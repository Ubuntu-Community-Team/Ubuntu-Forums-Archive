### Post by old_salt on 2008-02-14
The point to IBM's release of Lotus Symphony on Linux is to substantiate their press release about how Linux has been ready for the Corporate desktop environment. To support that, IBM is offering the Symphony Suite as proof. 

Lotus is about the only product that stands a chance of dethroning MS Office which has been the single most reason preventing Linux widespread adoption. Open Office and variants just don't cut it and Word Perfect unfortunately is.... stick a fork in it. 

With an Open Document standard about to be set (March), the playing field will be even and choice of Office Suites will be driven by cost, features and support. Open Office is okay for home use but not corporate. Lotus had a market share years ago and is still being used by some today. To update the Symphony Suite to today's standards, allow community contributions for templates, content etc, and coupled with IBM's and the community's support; MS doesn't stand a chance. Let's face it, I'm not alone in stating I refuse to pay $600 every 2-3 years for a word processing app. Corporations are tired of the game as well however they are locked right now. 

IBM and the community has a real opportunity with this and I'm glad to see it happen and will contribute as best I can towards it's success.

### Post by forrestcupp on 2008-02-15
My last post was based on hearsay and not experience.  I have since tried out beta 4, and I've changed my opinion.  The word processor part of Lotus Symphony is pretty amazing. 

First of all, they were right, the UI is beautiful compared to OO.o.  But that isn't all there is to it.  It has a lot of excellent features that OpenOffice will probably never have.  The text effects are amazing.  You have the ability to select text and choose to make it outlined, shadowed, embossed, and other things.  You can also rotate your text.  I think my favorite new option is "Fontwork" in the 'Create' menu.  It is their version of MS Office's "Word Art."  That is something I have long wanted in OpenOffice.

I'll have to say that I was surprised, and impressed.  The fonts looked a little heavy which makes the spreadsheet part kind of unusable for me.  There are still some kinks that need worked out.  One problem that is unacceptable is if you copy formatted text of different font sizes from another source, everything is pasted into your document at whatever your set font size is.

They have a lot to work on, but they have a good thing going.
