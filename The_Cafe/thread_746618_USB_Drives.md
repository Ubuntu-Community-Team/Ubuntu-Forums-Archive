There's quite a bit of performance variation among USB 2.0 drives. Originally, when the drives were mostly used for transferring documents between pcs, nobody cared very much whether the drives could write at 200MB/s or 300MB/s. With the advent of high capacity digital cameras and MS Vista's "ReadyBoost" system, however, speed suddenly became important.

There's also the ancilliary issue that the flash drive standard was really only meant to write to drives up to about 2GB, so making 4GB and 8GB drives work took a bit of jiggery-pokery on the part of manufacturers. This inevitably led to compatibility problems between faster, higher capacity drives, and older USB 2.0 ports.

I'm guessing your 8GB drive is one of the High Capacity, super fast ones (the designations vary among manufacturers, of course), while your 4GB drive may be of the older, more compatible, sort.

The fact that one video plays on the 8GB drive is a bit of a mystery, although I've seen my older machines recognize a similar drive for awhile, and then suddenly not. Also, I've found that older USB ports can read certain types of files off fast drives, but not others.

It's all a bit of a black art at the moment, so I don't honestly know what you can do to fix the situation, short of doing major surgery on your 360.

### Post by Tundro Walker on 2008-04-06
The knock-off drive could get corrupted quicker.  The down-side to flash drives is eventually their storage space gets fritzed, so they can't hold as much stuff.

But, you can still see the files you can't play, so I'd say that isn't the issue.  If it was, I'd think your x-box would show them as unrecognized file types or something.  Or, maybe the initial file headers are intact enough to make the files seem good, but when the whole file tries to run, there's corrupted parts that bugger things up.

You could try re-formatting the stick.  Maybe check what format your good stick uses, and reformat your knock-off stick in the same format.  Just to be safe, try to use FAT16, maybe FAT32.  That way you can switch them between your Ubuntu comp's, too.  If you're just using them between Windows/MS stuff, maybe you can format them as NTFS ... I don't know, does x-box recognize NTFS?  Probably don't want to format them as ext2/3, because I don't think Windows recognizes that format (Linux plays nice with MIcrosoft, but not vice-versa...funny that.)

Worse comes to worse, just set your ipod or whatever mp3 player you have to "storage" mode, and use it as a portable flash/hard drive to sneaker-net your files over.  I do that with my Creative Zen at work all the time.
