### Post by SuSUntu on 2007-03-08
> **OffHand said:**
> That might be true if there was a difference between the two. However, both consumers and companies use the same Ubuntu version so it doesn't really matter.

No differentiation (or at least minimal differentiation) in products for distinct market segments doesn't nullify my assessment as you say it does. The economics of open source is very, very complex, and for anyone to suggest otherwise, as seems to be the case here, is only demonstrating loyalty for their favorite distribution as opposed to using rational thought. 

To explain by example, as a company gains ground in the market where its bread is buttered, differentiation may evolve out of necessity where little or no differentiation originally existed, first leaving the consumer (cutting-edge) product development slow or stagnate, followed by dissolution of the consumer product in favor of the commercial version. In other words, the evolution of the product may necessarily tend toward the form of the product that has potential to earn the company money. The product has cycled from no differentiation (consumer version has precedence) to no differentiation (commercial version has precedence), and contrary to what you say, this latter state of "no differentiation" will matter to the very people on this forum.  

The first step then (in the slow death) is the defection of the original users of Ubuntu who are moving (or have been moving) on to the new Linux flavor-of-the-year which caters to more cutting-edge tastes. Secondly, when (if) Ubuntu can't make a go of it commercially (e.g., it fails up against the entrenched RedHat as is the author's main assertion), Ubuntu fades into a mere shadow of its former self.

Anyway, I'm with **JAPrufrock** in regard to the importance I place on Ubuntu: Linux distributions come and go, and I'm usually gone long before they've  "died their slow death". I just can't get emotionally attached to a piece of software.
