### Post by 1clue on 2012-04-02
Ah, the ambiguous, nefarious ***they.***

Speaking from the perspective of an American computer programmer who has been doing network programming since before the Internet opened for commercial/public use:

I can't really recall any point where the American government claimed that they **weren't** monitoring the net.  Lots of talking heads said it, lots of speculation on the anonymity of the net by people who don't really know how it works.  At every point though, the only people who can truly be anonymous on the net need to Know Something.

It is certainly possible to be anonymous on the net, but not easy at all.  "They" can certainly figure out what Internet connection you're using, and probably the serial numbers on the computer you're using.

I've seen claims that China has hackers who can get through just about any firewall in moments, not so sure I believe it but it's not out of reach of possibility if "They" spend enough time and effort to figure it out.  If China can do it, I strongly suspect that the USA can too.

On top of that, there are several disadvantages for private citizens.
[LIST=1]
[*]A warrant can get legal, official access to your network without your knowledge.
[*]In the USA, any number of George Bush Jr's achievements allows that access without a warrant as long as you can somehow be shoehorned into a "POSSIBLE" terrorist classification.  Meaning of course that somebody whose name sort of sounds like yours can be classified that way.
[*]The US Government has restrictions on encryption technology that does not have a "back door" for them to easily crack it.
[/LIST]

SOHO routers (small office/home office -- the ones everyone uses on their cable modems and such) almost all have significant deviation from proper function.  Most of the bugs don't affect the users much, but if "They" do some research then "They" can probably find a pretty simple back door.

Given that the Open Source router images like DD-WRT have communities who seem to be militantly against systematic testing, the images in question almost certainly have more bugs than the commercial SOHO suites.

IMO if you don't have commercial grade security equipment and commercial grade software on it, then you have no hope of real security.  Even so, I seriously suspect that every firmware for any router made or sold in the USA has a back door put there to facilitate monitoring, and so I suspect any corporation who really wants security does their own firewalls, their own encryption and possibly runs their own physical lines in critical places.

Back in the 80s and 90s it was a lot easier to get by under the covers but that's just because nobody cared that much.  Today the core network is IPV6 which makes slipping by anonymously a lot harder.


Now regarding Facebook passwords.

I did a bit of research, and it turns out that your employer can demand just about anything they want.  The real question is, do you want to work for a company that would even ask such a thing?  I don't have Facebook, but even so I would tell them to lay their lips on my posterior before even giving them that much information.

Facebook's response to that was entertaining IMO, both in that they were threatening lawsuit against those businesses and that they (the businesses in question) opened themselves up to discrimination suits if there were information on Facebook which classified them as some sort of minority and then the company didn't hire them.

### Post by jonathonblake on 2012-04-03
> **Warpnow said:**
> Why is targeted advertising so scary?

The major issues are:
* The advertisers knows things about one that one does not (yet) know;
* The add is for something that has already been purchased;
* The add is for something that one neither intends nor anticipates purchasing;
* The add is for something that is outside the field of use of the target;

Too many complaints of the first type caused Target to redo their personalized catalogs, so that the target does not realize that Target knows "x", even when the target does not yet know "x".

The second type is, at best, annoying. At worst, it will cause customers to return the product, having been convinced that they bought the wrong product.

How tolerable the fourth type is, depends upon the quality of the add.  Geico insurance commercials tend to be funny. However, I won't be buying vehicle insurance in the future, so indunuating me with commercials for Progressive, Nationwide, National, All-state, etc is more than annoying.  Those advertizers are paying a premium rate for a non-target. A guaranteed waste of their money. As a side effect, they also build ill-will against the advertizer.
