No, CD/DVD is not dead, nor will it be anytime soon. They'll still be around, decades from now. Won't be using the exact same technology we use now, but whatever is there will support legacy media.

First, an observation. Paper was invented millenia ago. It's still around, despite the predictions of the past few decades of a paperless office / paperless society. They still print books, by the millions ;)

But the thing is this: archiving. Specifically, powerless archiving. You put something on optical media you don't need power to maintain it. There's petabytes out there stored on both optical and magnetic mediums, either aren't going away any time soon.

Ok, how about audio cassette tapes? Once hugely popular for music, they were killed by the audio CD .... which is nearly killed off by "fast internet". So audio cassette tapes are dead and buried, who uses that old crap, etc. The newer medium is so much better ....

Except audio cassette tapes aren't dead. Not the market there used to be, for sure but also not dead. Google "audio books on casssette tape" if you don't believe me.

It's not just nostalgia buffs that use them. Some people depend upon them - the ones who are blind. For them, the lowly, obsolete, crap audio cassette player does one simple thing a CD player (or internet stream) cannot do: It "remembers" where you were when the batteries / power fails or when you need to be do something else. Such a simple, seemingly trivial thing to people like us (who can see) is very valuable to others.

Which was my point about CD/DVD not going away anytime soon. They are valuable to a specific group - the professional archivist. The longevity of the media (not the cheap discs we get at our local big-box), the lower cost per unit and the lower rollover (moving data) cost makes CD/DVD the preferred choice with them.

Would they (PAs) like a denser medium, like flash memory devices? Oh heck yes. But the density is not quite there yet. Physically robust they are - you can run over 'em with a truck, wash and dry them ... just be careful walking across a carpet with one or keying a radio near one. The last I can personally attest to: I scrambled a 4GB USB stick by putting my cell phone in the same pocket.

Someday, probably in the next decade the density and power requirements will be what PAs need and they'll be engineered to be robust with respect to electrostatic and electromagnetic effects. Add some more years for the above to demonstrated in a proof-positive manner. Then a few more decades to move existing CD/DVD archives over (secure funding, labor, etc.) ....

Like I said, not going away any time soon ;)
