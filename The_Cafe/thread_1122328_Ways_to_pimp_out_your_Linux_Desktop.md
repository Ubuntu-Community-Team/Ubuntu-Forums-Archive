### Post by SomeGuyDude on 2009-04-10
Whew. Um... well here's what I did. Keep in mind I'm an Arch user, so right off the bat I got the choice of any DE/WM. That said, here's my process:

1) I think about what kinda visuals I'm aiming for. Am I aiming for a top or bottom panel/bar setup, do I want panels at all, etc. Maybe I want to use a dock like AWN this go-round.

2) After I've done that I design my conky appropriately. In the case of an AWN setup I'll make my conky more big and fat all over the place because I can put the more useful stuff right in the dock. If it's a slimmer panel like Tint2 I trim it down.

3) Once I've got my conky and panels where I want them I look for a background that has a space where I need it to be so I can easily read my conky. Landscapes, minimal designs, simple photographs work the best. Nothing overly cluttered because I don't like my conky to have an opaque background.

4) Wallpaper chosen, I look for the more normal theme elements: a GTK theme and icons. Color scheme to match the wallpaper.

5) Finally, I pick a font that sits well atop the GTK theme.

There ya go.

### Post by Lunx on 2009-04-11
Like so many new-comers to Linux , Gnome, KDE et al, I was amazed (confused?!?) by the number of possibilities offered in how I configure my desktop. Started out simply playing with themes, colours, wallpapers, panels etc before getting to grips with the mysterious features offered by Compiz. Played around with other eye-candy like screenlets etc, and tried Awn and Cairo Dock as well. Even had a taste of the more mininimalist approach with OpenBox in CrunchBang. Done a few fresh installs now, changing releases and file systems etc and now I don't worry too much about all that, trying to keep Gnome as simple yet functional as I can. I install Gnome Do, 'coz I find that with it I can delete my bottom panel and not even bother about docks, keyboard shortcuts give me all I need to get to where-ever I want to be. 

Thing I noticed quite a lot when I first tried Linux was how crap the fonts render at times, but now I've worked out how and what to install, and what sizes and settings to use, I reckon they actually look better than they did with my old OS (Jaunty is a BIG improvement too I've noticed, the subpixel rendering actually works for me now).

Fresh install I usually grab the following apps for eye candy and other more practical uses while I'm downloading the main updates
[LIST]
[*]Compizconfig Settings Manager
[*]Compiz Fusion Plugins Unsupported
[*]Fusion Icon to manage above as well as other wm's
[*]Gnome Do (can't live without this!!!)
[*]msttcorefonts (the more "normal" fonts)
[*]Assorted Gnome and other themes
[*]Conky (very simple, very functional)
[*]Gkrellm (if I can't be a*sed with conky)
[/LIST]

After installing the above, the only other thing I do is get Tahoma font installed.

Between Compiz, Conky and the Do, I have litte need for anything else other than replacing much of the artwork with my own creations (Compiz cube and background, wallpapers, splashscreens etc.)

Compiz is pretty cool if you move beyond the effects and look at how many functions are actually useful in a productive way. Same for Gnome Do, very unobtrusive , yet there as soon as I need it. Awn and the other docks also have some good features, but I figure it's a bit impractical to install every flamin' thing. 

Other thing I notice with much of this eye candy is how everything seems to aim at the glossy, glass effect. It's certainly nice when done well, but when every desktop app tries to do the same, the effects are a bit overwhelming to the end user who's actually using half a dozen of these programs, sometime minimal, subtle, and understated is far more ellegant (OpenBox on CrunchBang nails it IMHO) 

Great idea for a thread by the way, will follow with interest the thoughts of others and the set ups they choose.

### Post by cardinals_fan on 2009-04-12
This topic was made for me...

1. Install preferred distro.  Currently favoring SliTaz and NetBSD.
2. Download and compile [musca]("http://aerosuidae.net/musca.html") with custom config.h (which I have backed up and attached).
3. Install screen, zsh, and make sure that DejaVu fonts are available.
4. Copy over .zshrc, .screenrc, and .Xdefaults (backed up and attached).
5. Choose one of my 300+ desert wallpapers.
6. Edit .xinitrc to launch musca and set the wallpaper (with hsetroot).
7. Logout, log back in, and enjoy!
