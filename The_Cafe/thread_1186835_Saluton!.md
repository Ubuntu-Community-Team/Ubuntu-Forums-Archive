### Post by Grant A. on 2009-06-30
Esperanto is an interesting constructed language, but sadly, it doesn't seem as if it is truly easy for everyone to learn. It looks the easiest for Slavic and Germanic language speakers, as its pronunciations are Slavic in origin, and its vocabulary is highly Germanic, along with its syntax.

Would this be a great language to bridge the language barriers in Europe? Sure, mostly to the speakers of Slavic or Germanic languages, but the southern Europeans may have more difficulty.

On a scale of 1 to 10 in how difficult it is to for people speaking various languages to learn Esperanto (1 being incredibly easy, and 10 being excruciatingly difficult), I would say that Germanic language speakers would be a 1 (especially English speakers, since we are a bastardization of German, which got corrupted by Norman and Latin, so we really have all bases covered there, except for the hard c sound), Slavic language speakers would be a 2, Romance language speakers would be a 3 (because of the interchangeable sentence structure, and a high Latin-based vocabulary, but not so much on the pronunciations), Greek language speakers would probably be at a 5, and Altaic language speakers would probably be at 5, as well. However, for people speaking non-Indo-European languages, the difficulties would be very high, especially for people speaking languages in the far east (save for Korean, which is in the Altaic language family, but they would probably not do too well, either).

Another problem with Esperanto: The accents and diacritics are VERY difficult to find on a keyboard, and can really only be found on a U.S. Alt. Intl. keymap.

Esperanto is a much better put together language than Interlingua, which is pretty much just a Romance language itself.

I think Esperanto would be good to know for places where many different Indo-European languages are spoken (The Americas, Europe), but other than that, it's not that great.

English has a far better chance of becoming the universal language. Indeed, it is already the first global Lingua Franca. English is further propelled into a good position by the fact that two superpowers have used it one after another (America, Britain, and possibly India after the U.S.), and because of the fact that the British Empire encompassed a huge area, making the overall Anglosphere very, very large. 

The only downside to English is that it is one of the most difficult languages to learn, along with the other Germanic languages. It's not really English's verb conjugations that makes it difficult. Nay, English has very uniform conjugations for verbs. It's more of the fact that English words can sometimes have unpredictable pronunciations.

For example, I give you the word, "[Ghoti]("http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ghoti")"

This may look as if it is pronounced, "go tee", correct?

Well, actually it's pronounced, "fish".

Well you may ask, "how is this possible?". Well, it's simple.

**gh** as in lau**_gh_**
**o** as in w**_o_**men
**ti** as in ac**_ti_**on.

Not just that, but we have digraphs that can have multiple sounds, such as "th", which can sound like the th in **_th_**e (Voiced Dental Fricative), and like the th in **_th_**ick (Unvoiced Dental Fricative). Before the 13th century, we had two different letters distinguishing these, the thorn (Þþ), and eth (Ðð). These two letters were used interchangeably, but by the 13th century, eth had died out of common usage. The Thorn, however, lasted until the late 18th century. The typewriter effectively killed out the Thorn, due to the lack of it existing on it. Now you may ask, "Why is he rambling on about this?", well, it's simple: Esperanto has too many funky looking characters. If it's difficult to type Esperanto in the age of computers, then it is unlikely that it will adopted. This is why [Ido]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ido") was created, it is an offshoot of Esperanto that, among other things, replaced the special characters with digraphs. Ido and Esperanto are mutually intelligible to one another, but Ido has a better chance of thriving, because Ido can be typed on a standard English keyboard. By piggy-backing on the coattails of the Lingua Franca, it may have a chance.

Anyways, I commend you on dedicating time and effort to learning a language, but if this Esperanto thing really is going to catch on, we need special keyboards for it, as the U.S. Alt. Intl. keymap is difficult for a newbie to find on the first try.

I think that Esperanto will work great in Europe, and great as a gateway to learning the real Indo-European languages. ;)

I personally want to learn Esperanto, but I have doubts that it will actually help me later on in life.

This may sound linguistically-centric of me, but I personally prefer English as the universal language. It already has a huge amount of speakers scattered around the world (Mandarin has more, but they are mainly all in China, making it a bit pointless to learn, unless you plan on moving there.), and I also prefer it because it's the language of my ancestors (along with Scots).

Adia&#365;, miaj amikoj. ;)
