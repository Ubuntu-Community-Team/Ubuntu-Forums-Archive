### Post by Gizenshya on 2010-01-02
> **3pinner said:**
> I tried hugin and really did not have good success with it. wasn't really very intuitive, and pictures came our real distorted for some reason.
Probably because I did not overlap enough or something.

I don't mean to be rude, but you hit the nail on the head with your last sentence.  It doesn't matter how good the software is if it is not used correctly.

Overlap **at least** 60% for best results.  There are several reasons for that, but it is best.  The vast majority of people I've talked to who've tried panas at some point, failed in the beginning because of improper technique.  Most of them think either 'just touching' or only a 10% or so overlap is sufficient... but that just won't cut it to warp/merge the images correctly.  And 60% overlap, if done properly, means that you can take out any single image and still have total coverage.  That simply means that 1. there must be triple-overlap at regular intervals, and 2.  that this overlap is sufficient enough to link and merge/warp images opposite removed images.  A 60% overlap fills both requirements... though only just.  Feel free to overlap as much as your battery and memory card can take! :)  In about every 2nd or 3rd pana I've made I've had to take out an image (overexposed, unwanted animal or plane entered frame, focus errors, camera shake, etc.).  If done properly, these things can be easily taken care of by taking out an image, or from GIMP or PS editing of the images to remove the artifact using data from both images, depending on the cause.

What really sucks is that if you didn't overlap enough, you'll have to retake them.  There isn't a way to get it to work (without adding in false data with Gimp or PS).  :(  I hope you didn't take them too far away from home...

Also make sure you get enough above and below (in a horizontal pana) to make the image not look too 'linear.'  Also, it is important to remember that the highest part of your image can only be up to as high as the lowest point in the 'top' portion of any of your images.  And for the bottom, it can only be as low as the highest point in the 'bottom' portion of any of your images.  And that is only if there is no warping needed (which, for most purposes, is impossible).  So make sure you take enough pictures to include plenty of image both below (and on either side) of whatever you're taking a picture of in order to have plenty left over to crop out your desired image.

The whole idea is to get enough to take out any image without affecting the final image, AND to have enough **area** in the 'stitched' (and warped/merged) image file to crop out a pana of the desired quality, size (total pixels), and dimensions (width x height).

Also, make sure you set your camera such that there will be as little exposure and color difference between the extreme edges of your image.  Much of this can be corrected with image editing, but sometimes with permanent information loss (color depth, for example).  It is better to take this into consideration before hand.

Hugin is an amazing and powerful program, and I thought it was very intuitive.  I've never read any tutorials about it, but I made a few pana's the first time I ever opened the program in just a couple minutes with no troubles.

Feel free to ask if you have questions.  There are probably tutorials online as well (don't know for sure, though).  I strongly recommend you learn Hugin.  If you don't like the interface, try something else... but if hugin can't work with the images you made, then they just can't be worked with without adding in extra data with an image editor.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2010-01-03
It's counter-intuitive, but you should be using telephotos for your panos.  You need longer focal lengths to get a flatter field and less pincushion.

You also need to make a tripod head that rotates the camera through the lens' "apparent" center (roughly where the aperture blades are).  There are some decent howto's on the web.  Search for panorama tripod head.

Overlap, proper focal length, proper camera rotation, and proper use of the stitching software are all needed for good panoramas.  Proper exposure is helpful but you have a little more latitude to correct in software.  HDR can also extend the dynamic range.  There are lots of tutorials on the web on how to properly expose for panoramas and how to use HDR to control contrast and pull out details simultaneously in shadows and highlights.

I would post some of mine but they are huge--300 MB or more (and that's compressed!)

### Post by Gizenshya on 2010-01-03
^^^ that info is very important as well.

ust make sure that whatever focal length you choose is kept the same for all images in the pana.  The programs take in focal length and other aspects of the lens in consideration when working out the math for the image.

There are two reasons to zoom in.  1.  Less distortion.  2.  More detail! :D

The distortion is because of the mathematics of warping the images.  This is also why the pivot point (fulcrum?) is so important.  The further you are away from something, the less the last part matters, though (well, the less of an impoact it has on the resulting image's accuracy).  It is also important to keep images near you out of the frame.  Due to the mathematics, they can be sliced up or appear multiple times.  This is because the programs do all the math in 2-d, and not 3-d.   And don't go looking for programs that can do it in 3-d, because they don't exist.

And lower focal lengths also have another undesired effect.  They need to be warped more extremely in order to get the pana.  And this, combined with fewer pixels anyway, means that there will be areas with very high detail, and other 'stretched' areas with very little (relatively, of course).  But none of these areas will have anywhere near as much detail as a proper pana done with a longer focal length.

The program that you use will not affect any of this, though.  The mathematics are the same.  They are very hard to explain with text, though... but how and why these things aren't as true as understanding how to do things the proper way.  :)

There isn't really any limit for the focal length.  You can take a cheap 3mp camera with a great 10x lens and easily take a 100mp picture if you want.  It would take a lot of time and picture taking, but it can be done.  The only limits are patience and card space :)  (well, and daylight if it takes too terribly long haha)  Focus can change for consistency, just not focal length.  Exposure can change as well, optionally, depending on the look you're after.

I've attached an image of a pana I took at Meteor Crater in Arizona (USA) that is part of a series of panas I took there that day.  It is much, much smaller than the original, of course, and very compressed. (I'm on 56k, lol)  I've highlighted two areas, in the bottom-right and top-left, where I didn't take enough pictures to leave enough image for me to crop out the pana I wanted.  I later went back with photoshop and added some false data to make it blend.

Also look at the sky.  I changed exposure to take into consideration that the 'left' of the crater ws in shadow, and the right had direct sunlight.  BUT, the sky, of course, remained the same color throughout the sky (left-right anyway.  If was, as it usually is, lighter towards the horizon, and darker blue going up).  I later changed that by taking out the sky and replacing it with a pseudo-gradient I made from a portion of the sky at the far-right (since I liked that color the best).

The alternative would have been to base exposure on the sky, and either leave the left dark, or brighten it up (though color depth may have been an issue).  I chose the first option because I wanted to maintain the color depth and contrast in the crater.

The best thing to do would have been to take the image at another time of day.  Either with the sun directly above, or in line behind me.  But that wasn't an option as I was just passing through, and it was very much an impromptu picture.

The image is pretty 'linear', and by that I mean much wider than it is tall.  This problem was because I didn't take into account how much warping it would take to get the images to merge into a pana.  In other words, I didn't take enough pictures!  There isn't any way to go back and fix it, unfortunately... it's just a lesson learned.  I did, in my final version of it, increase the skyline up higher.  This helped a bit, but it does make the crater itself appear a bit 'low' in the image.  Ohh, well.  The black highlighted spot in the bottom-right is because I needed more image data in the bottom, and I decided to just photoshop in false data so that I could preserve more of the image bottom from the rest.  (if that sentence makes sense...)  If you'd like I can attach a compressed version of the merged image that I took that crop from, for reference.

### Post by RaZe42 on 2010-02-15
+1 for hugin. It works pretty well for basic panoramas.

also +1 for qtpfsgui. but please, I you have to make HDRs (or rather tone apped images), keep them classy.

My HDR workflow in qtpfsgui:

Create three different tonemaps:
Reinhard '02 (default settings, use as a "base")

Fattal (try to find the best noise reduction value to suit the picture. This one will be used to spice up the final image)

Mantiuk (default settings)

Then blend the three images as layers in GIMP to suit your taste.

If you're just looking for an increased **D**ynamic **R**ange as in H**DR**, then maybe the new Exposure Blend plugin in DigiKam would be the best option (although I haven't tried it yet)

For general postprocessing I'd really recommend DigiKam, and for retouching GIMP is your best bet (obviously)
