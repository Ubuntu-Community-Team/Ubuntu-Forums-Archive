### Post by deepwave on 2006-11-23
Pretty good article and it sums up the approach in non-CS university courses.  Well almost, at least in my case.  Then again I live in Canada and not the UK.

At the University of Toronto, the CS faculty teaches most of the courses using open source, mostly Linux, and non-MS programs.  In fact, one of my profs was worried about one of my classes, that we were under-exposed to Windows world.  The one semester we used SQL Server and VB.net instead of Postgresql and Java, did the CS students ever whine.  I personally liked the fact, that we got exposed to both Linux and Windows, and got a taste of both worlds.

In non-CS courses, there is a trend of using one particular product.  A good portion of these programs run on Mac G5s interestingly.  MS Office is still the dominate office suite.  This year however, the admins deployed OpenOffice on the Windows machines too.  In some cases, I find I have a choice of two or three different programs that do the same thing.

I think that academia should hold itself above the corporate turfwar, and show both sides of the software world.  I think the real problem, as you mentioned in the article is the over reliance on tool-teaching.  I believe this all stems from the modern view of education, on teaching students "career" skills for particular trades.  Skills are skills.  You can apply them anywhere.  I think academia should stick with what they always excelled at: teaching concepts, theory and thinking.
