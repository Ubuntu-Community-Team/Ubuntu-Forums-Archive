### Post by Nevon on 2009-06-29
Wow! It kind of scares me that pretty much everyone who's replied so far sees nothing wrong with a person being held captive against his or her will for an indefinite amount of time. Now, I know absolutely nothing about the case that was linked to in the first post, but in my opinion incarceration should only be used as long as the person presents a danger to society, and thus cannot function as a member of said society. 

Somehow some people have mistakenly begin to think that sentencing should be used as a means of punishment or a way to tornment the accused, not as a way of rehabilitating that person to the point where he or she can once again function as a normal person, without posing any significant danger (or at least any more so than the average person) to the people around him or her. Who that kind of thinking benefits, I do not know. It does not benefit society, as keeping a person contained and alive is very, very expensive (and no, the death penalty is not any cheaper). It certainly doesn't benefit the person that has been sentenced, as that person has no incentive to become rehabilitated - all he or she has to do is wait until the sentence comes to an end. It doesn't even benefit the victim, as the crime cannot be undone (except for crimes of an economical nature) and any psychological damage has already occured. If venegance is the only thing the victim seeks, then he or she is no better than the person being sentenced.
