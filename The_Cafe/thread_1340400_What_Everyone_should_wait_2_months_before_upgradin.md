### Post by MoebusNet on 2009-11-28
I think what the poster meant was "if you need a production-stable OS, wait a couple of months" or something similar.

If you use Ubuntu for income-producing or valuable data storage, or for any reason would be inconvenienced by data loss you may indeed wish to allow the major bugs in any relatively new OS to be discovered before committing to it exclusively. This is not a knock against Ubuntu, just a common-sense real-world attitude toward preserving the results of all your hard work.

This should not prevent you however, from installing & running the latest Ubuntu (or other OS) separate from your valued data. A Virtual Machine, a different computer, even a separate partition are all options to explore a new OS without fear of data loss.

Of course it goes without saying that there are only two type of users; those who back up their data, and those that lose their data.

### Post by ibuclaw on 2009-11-28
If an upgrade fails - tends to be the user's fault indirectly.

The usual suspects are systems with extraneous packages, weird/broken setups (Thanks to Add/Remove and Ubuntu Software Centre, you can now break your system's package dependencies without even realising it) and lots of third party repositories enabled that suffer the bad end of the stick.

If this is the case, it doesn't really matter how long you wait - your system will most likely break if you try to upgrade.

There are ways to prevent this from happening though if you run pre-checks before upgrade....

### Post by Ji Ruo on 2009-11-28
If you're new to GNU/Linux then waiting to install or upgrade to the latest version is very sensible, even if it's just for a couple of weeks. If you rely on it as a stable system for your work or pleasure then even more so.
