### Post by GnuSense on 2008-11-24
Actually, Red Flag is a very nicely appointed Debian KDE 3.5.x release, with a great mix of applications, one of the pleasantest live CDs I've ever used.  The 1.60 version ("Che") was based on testing, I installed it to a few systems that were being donated to Latin American countries, and, as I recall I had to make a swap partition manually and add it to the fstab, but otherwise I was very impressed.  I liked the art work, lots of eye candy (maybe superkaramba, I'm not sure), but no compiz fusion, IIRC.  Testing at that time didn't have proprietary nvidia drivers in the repository, I don't know what its status is now, but I suppose that has been corrected, since testing  (Lenny) is about to become stable some time in the next few months.  I just downloaded **_[COLOR="Blue"][1.70]("http://iso.damianfossi.com/redstar/redstar1.7-esp-final.iso")[/COLOR]_** and look forward to playing with it.

As to the distribution's politics, socialism is a democratic ideal, the notion that capital (industries, utilities, etc.) should be controlled by the workers (>95% of the population), not a tiny class of super-rich who live off the sweat of the majority.  Communism is envisaged as a distant goal when there is abundance and people are motivated to work by a sense of fellowship, where the products of labor are distributed "from each according to his ability, to each according to his need."  You can argue whether this is a feasible situation.

Marx and Engels, two of the important 19th century socialist theorists, thought that socialism would first come to the most advanced industrial societies, which had republican traditions, a relatively educated labor force, and industries which could soon churn out enough goods for everyone.  But in the event, the only successful socialist-leaning revolutions happened in very poor countries, which were often in turn beset by their neighbors (the early Soviet Union, for instance, faced a huge civil war from various political factions, the old aristocratic ruling class, bandits and 14 foreign countries including the U.S., Britain, France & Japan, about 300,000 foreign troops in all, though you wouldn't know it to read western text books). In that situation the only way those revolutionary states could survive is by organizing themselves hierarchically, like an army, so-called "democratic centralism,"  which turned out to be far more centralist than democratic.  In the USSR within 20 years most of the original idealistic socialists were imprisoned and/or executed by careerist bureaucrats, who ruled the society for the next 50 years.

Nonetheless, the 20th century experiments with socialism were not unmitigated failures, even though they didn't become the democratic workers' paradises that the earliest socialist had imagined they would.  

The Soviet Union, for instance, developed from a pre-capitalist society, just out of serfdom with overwhelming illiteracy to a rapidly modernizing industrial state within a single generation.  In WWI Russia had 1 rifle for every 9 troops, by WWII 25 years later they out produced Germany, the most industrialized state in Europe, if not the world.  Truth be known, the USSR is what crushed the Nazi empire, the most evil dictatorship in human history, a state that planned to kill all non-Aryan people in the world (more than 90% of humanity), and that made a pretty good start, killing half of the world's Jews.  America's contribution was insignificant by comparison (reflected in the casualty rates, 300,000 dead GIs vs. perhaps 20 million Soviets).  By the late fifties the USSR was launching the world's first satellite in to orbit.  When the Soviet Union imploded most of the population were plunged in to utter destitution and male life expectancy dropped from 64 years to 57 years, a situation unprecedented in human history absent a major war, famine or plague. 

Tito's Yugoslavia managed to forge a society where people who had been at each others' throats for centuries lived together in peace and harmony, with quite a bit of local democracy and the third highest economic growth rates in the world from 1945-1975, right after Japan & Israel. When socialism collapsed there those societies devolved in to a bevy of warring states ruled by local thugs, and in most cases standards of living have still not recovered from what they were in "socialist" times (Slovenia being an exception). 

Cuba, despite 50 years of embargo and attacks by the the massive empire next door has eradicated hunger & illiteracy, even though its only allies in the Eastern Bloc disappeared almost 20 years ago.  Cuba has by far the most educated and best medicated population in Latin America, and its life expectancy, infant mortality and general health care are as good or better than those of the United States, a country that spends 2.5 times the entire Cuban GNP per capita on health care alone. 

Capitalism has not been an unalloyed blessing for most of the world's population, despite what the mass media attempts to convey (hmmm, newspapers, TV & radio stations owned by large corporations, financed by advertising from other large corporations, how could the MSM not be completely objective or even ultra-liberal?)  

In Argentina, for instance, where this distribution came from, a Reagan & Thatcher supported military dictatorship "disappeared" 30,000 people with a few years in the late seventies & early eighties, far, far more than all the counter-revolutionaries that were ever executed in Cuba over 5 decades of constant attacks (there were over 600 documented assassination attempts on Fidel Castro alone).  In the meantime, after following the "free market" prescriptions of the IMF, the Chicago School and the "Washington Consensus" Argentina's economy completely collapsed by the late nineties, a situation that might seem remarkably similar to what is happening in the United States and the rest of the industrialized capitalist world today.  Argentina went from the world's 4th richest country per capita to the situation a few years ago where 58% of the population were living in poverty, defined as less than $3 per day and hundreds of thousands of people died from increases in morbidity caused by malnutrition (in a country that continued to export huge quantities of beef and grain to the industrialized world).  I can see why some Argentines might think that socialism is not so terrible.
