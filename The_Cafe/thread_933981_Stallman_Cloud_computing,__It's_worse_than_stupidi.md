### Post by geonik250 on 2008-09-30
Cloud computing is total rubbish. One way among many that this can be asserted is by how much marketing talk expert proponents of it use. They state the huge advantages it has.
"Imagine being able to edit your documents from anywhere in collaboration with so many people!" Yeah right like the world is full of people flying around the globe and editting documents together with other perpetually travelling collaborators.

One aspect of cloud computing is the so called Sofware as a Service (SaaS) model. Software stops being treated as a product and rather seen as a service to subscribe to and consequently be paid on a subscription basis.With the proprietary software business model failing, or at least not being so lucrative any more, free software having played a major part in that, big business promote this as the new way to get our money for something we already accomplish using free programs for no cost at all.

Since their IT infrastructure is moved to the cloud costs for hardware and skilled personnel operating it diminish. Actually this is a moot point. Yes you do not have to support a datacenter but now you are in need of very high quality networking. So you must upgrade your LAN and all other relevant to networking infrastructure which of course costs money (not of the monopoly kind). Oh and if you thought sys admins are hard to come by and expensive to pay good luck finding a network administartor capable of handling your heavy traffic network.

It is stupid for certain things. Where privacy is important, you should never use net-based apps. There are too many points of failure.

Stallman is right however, when he talks about the proprietary issues. ### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2008-10-02
All you must do to see why cloud computing is a waste of time is to try using the thin clients set up in any corporate environment. It's slow, weak and artificially limited. You are at the mercy of what the administrators thought of and can no longer 'just plug in a flash drive and have it magically work' because the magic of modern operating systems has been abstracted away into limited web-based technologies.

Company goes under? Burns down? Your data is gone.

People need to stop deluding themselves aboud the association between cloud computing and "free beer" (let alone free as in freedom). A company can easily *claim* to be running open source software on their servers and show you the code, but actually be running something completely different and significantly more evil. Locally run applications give us accountability.

In terms of free beer stuff like Google Docs, there is nothing (I repeat: NOTHING) stopping them from a sudden switch to being a payed service, which is entirely likely from many such sites given their fragile reliance on advertisements to make money.

People also need to stop deluding themselves about cloud computing somehow reducing processor overhead. Cloud computing solutions so far have only accomplished simplistic number crunching tasks like word processing, simple spreadsheets and basic image editing (much of which is done client-side anyway). If Linux has taught us anything, it is that those same tasks can still be done on a sub-$200 computer.

Even without this buzzwordy cloud computing craze, the place for fancy computers is multimedia stuff. Heavy video processing with big files, 3D graphics, games, flash banner ads. That stuff *will not* move to web services, and if it does it will be tremendously slow.
Heck, look at Google Docs right now, even! It has the functionality of a 'rich text' editor with online collaboration and revision tracking. It takes measurable time to get the word count for a document.

Here is something else: Any Internet connection comes with the magical ability for your home computer to face the Internet and provide a service. Geeks like setting up their own home servers for calendars and the like. HP is even touting a home server solution for non-geeks. That solution is flexible, inexpensive and fits completely within the infrastructure we have.

I think the problem comes down to web technologists completely losing touch with what the web is. They are trying to turn it into a complete platform, but that is just wrong. "The web" is HTML loaded over HTTP with a few touches; it is essentially a way that information is presented to browsers that is designed around hyperlinks, hence the name "web". The idea is that content flows from one server to the next, resulting in an organic grid of relevant data with each piece maintained by its own sovereign author. What the overengineering web lunatics are forgetting is that the web is *not* the Internet! You can pipe ANY service through the Internet.
Abiword can pipe through the Internet for collaboration features. Empathy is making that superbly easy for developers via libempathy and Tubes, so applications can seamlessly link up with a user's instant messaging contacts. XMPP (Jabber) will serve us very well with that Empathy stuff. Evolution talks with other software thanks to some standard protocols running over the Internet.

The computing platform is the operating system, sitting on the hardware. It is abstracted enough as it is for developers, and we have made it difficult enough for *the users*. Let's not screw this technology up any more, shall we?
