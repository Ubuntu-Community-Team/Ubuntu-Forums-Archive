### Post by wrycatcher on 2007-04-04
This boils down the fundamental question of the purpose of college/university:

1) Is it to prepare you for your career?
2) Is it to prepare you for life (in general)?
3) Is it both?  Should it be?

Just before I started school, I opted to go into an intensive humanities curriculum as an alternative to the traditional "core requirements".  Philosophy, literature, history, art, and even some theology.  Unlike you, I was actually interested in classic literature, and I found myself especially drawn to philosophy.  I absolutely relished those classes.  I felt more alive studying, writing, and debating those topics than in any of the technical classes. They represented an expanded view of life, not just how to write good code.  Am I a more technically skilled *software engineer* because of those humanities classes?  No way.  Am I a better *person* for those classes?  You bet!  Most of *who I am* today was **NOT** developed in my CS, math, or physics classes.  Those classes determined *what I do* (for a living), but they are not who I am.  My experience of, and outlook on, life is much richer because of all those seemingly pointless non-technical courses, and even the college experience outside of the classroom.  It's probably difficult to appreciate it right now when you seem to have a very clear, unequivocal vision of what you want to do (and NOT do) with your life, especially when you see your educational process as merely an impediment to that goal.

It's your life, you get to decide what you'll do and not do.  Listen to various stories from folks that've been there and done that.  Then perhaps you'll get a glimpse of the bigger picture.  Good luck in whatever you decide to do.  Just remember to never stop learning...
