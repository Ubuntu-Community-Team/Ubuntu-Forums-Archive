A tad bit off topic, mostly on, but why do people rag on Apple for being proprietary? They've open-source most of the underlying important parts of the system, their own open-source browser engine is the most compliant (and uber-light and well documented), and even their kernel is open source. ### Post by p_quarles on 2009-09-27
> **starcannon said:**
> +1

It is not evil to want to earn money so that bills, employees, and share holders can get paid. What *is* evil, is trying to make it so that others can not do the same. 
So far as I know, Google is not trying to stop others from making money in their market; they just require them to create their own apps, thats not asking too much is it?
Somebody not giving out free lunch is not evil. It is important to be a gracious recipient of any gift; if too much pressure is put on, one could perceive that the hand that feeds, may be withdrawn.
Well, you're right that they're not evil, and obviously they have to make money for their investors.

But Android isn't a "gift," it's an open source project. Yes, there are many situations in which open source is a *good business strategy*. A mobile OS/development platform is, Google thinks, one of those situations, because a large community of developers is much more crucial to the success of the platform than that Google gets a license fee for each OS copy distributed. The open source license was always intended to bring as much development as possible to the platform. The fact that one can fork the project like Cyanogen did is merely a side effect of that, and doesn't effect the primary business strategy much either way.

But Google's sales teams are put in a difficult position if they tell Motorola, for instance, that pre-installing Google Voice and Google Maps will be X dollars per unit, while a community modder has these available publicly for free. Yes, it's probably true that anyone using the modded firmware already has legally acquired versions of those applications, but that's not the point. The point is public, unlicensed distribution of Google's bread and butter. 

Yeah, it would be great if they can work something out, but those complaining about betrayal of open source principles really don't understand how business works. I would argue, as well, that there's a lot of misunderstanding about how open source works.
