### Post by Irihapeti on 2022-12-02
I have to watch my salt intake these days for various reasons, and I think that QIII is on the right track. I.e. substitutes can be bad for you, and we do need a certain amount of sodium. You can get horribly ill if you try to do with insufficient salt, especially if you're also drinking a lot of water. (It's called hyponatremia and can be fatal.)

However, as with many things, it's very easy to consume far more salt than you actually need. And if you want to know what you do actually need, don't listen to some random internet dude or dudette without a medical degree :), but talk to your doctor.

I think it can be a mistake to suddenly go from using too much to cutting it out altogether. Your food will inevitably taste weird. Try reducing the amount of salt, and maybe adding other seasonings instead, and do that for a week or a fortnight to give your taste buds a chance to adjust. Then try another reduction if you need to, and so on. I did that many years ago with sugar when I decided to stop putting sugar in my tea and coffee. I was quite surprised how little time it took before I couldn't stand the taste of sweet coffee or tea.

Not an answer to the original question, I know, but I hope it's of some help to someone.

> Will I get enough iodine if I use sea salt?


Fact: **Raw sea salt does contain some iodine but not nearly enough to meet every day needs**,  so to meet iodine requirements, other sources are required. While most  of the minerals naturally found in sea salt can be acquired through  other foods in the diet in more meaningful quantities, it is not the  case for iodine.
