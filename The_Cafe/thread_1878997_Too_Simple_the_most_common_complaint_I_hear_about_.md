### Post by ScionicSpectre on 2011-11-10
"It's too simple."

Most people I know who use Linux love it, and people who see Linux think it's amazing (Unity, GNOME 3, and KDE 4 all give good impressions). The only verbalized complaints I've heard in the past year have been that things are too simple.

I think that when the last complaint you hear about your interface design is that it's too simple, especially with so many competing environments, you've succeeded to a large degree. These complaints tend to only come from hardcore computer enthusiasts, not normal people.

What do you guys think? Do we fail more when we upset power users, or is it merely a sign that we've succeeded in making our software more accessible? Can a balance be found? Is simplicity necessarily linked to decreased functionality, or is this a false assumption?

### Post by Thewhistlingwind on 2011-11-10
The problem isn't users, it's developers. Development for Linux is too hard.

We should focus on making it trivially easy, so that developers have no reason NOT to provide their application for Linux. (May I add that the windows DLL's are only part of the problem and that packaging is a huge part as well.)

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-11-10
Only Unity and Gnome-shell are criticized for being "too simple", although I would use the word "dysfunctional" or "inefficient". KDE is the most customizable environment, people complain that it is "bloated", but not simple.

I do think that the "simple" interface on Unity is a good thing to attract new users. I am not so sure about Gnome-shell. What upsets me the most is that people whine instead of just switching to something more efficient (i.e. xfce or Debian/LTS Gnome 2).

As for developing applications, it is neither hard to develop apps for Linux nor is it hard to package them. Companies like Mathworks have been making commercial software for Linux for years. Their version of Matlab works on ALL distributions (regardless of kernel, xorg, DE, libraries, etc) and it has a full windows-like installer of its own (i.e. no need to package anything).
