### Post by ClientAlive on 2011-04-03
I recently learned about how laptops come to market and what these  companies are called. One article I read is here:  [http://www.mindconnection.com/library/computertips/whomakeslaptops.htm](http://www.mindconnection.com/library/computertips/whomakeslaptops.htm)  and is pretty informative.

I also recall a time (a few years back) when I was a little serious  about running Linux. Mostly, at that time, motivated by the idea that if  I went with Linux when purchasing a new PC I'd save money on the  overall cost (by not having to pay for Windows). I quickly discovered  that the major companies that sell PCs simply will not sell you a  computer without an operating system installed and that Windows is (or  was) the only o/s available. (And, of course, you have to pay hundreds  of dollars extra for an operating system that I now know is inferior).

Now here is what I've been wondering about for the last few days:

Do you remember the big fiasco with Microsoft years back where they were  in Federal court and being accused of monopolizing or something like  that? Didn't that have something to do with forcing consumers into  situations where they had to use/ buy Microsoft? Well it seems to me  that's exactly what is going on with PC vendors today.

Now I do-not know much about that event, its circumstances or even its  outcome. I repeat - I don't know much at all about that (and I don't  really have the time to invest to research such a broad and  controversial topic right now). Please forgive me if I'm way off base  here. I make no claim to being an expert on the subject. It's just that  it occurs to me there may be some link here.

The bottom line is this: If you are told that you can not purchase the  computer without an o/s and then that there is only one choice available  for an o/s - you are effectively being told that you have to buy that  o/s in order to acquire a computer at all - and I can not see how that  is legal. I've been wondering if this is being pursued in the courts;  and, if not, why?

When a person contacts a PC vendor and is told they can not purchase a  PC without an o/s, that it simply will not be sold that way, the reason  given is one of liability. What they tell you is that, without and o/s,  the company could not be certain the computer would function properly;  and, as a result, could not guarantee the product. I have an idea that  would mitigate a PC vendor's liability in the  event of selling PCs without an o/s installed (though making other  operating system choices available is certainly an option too).

One possible solution to this would be to create a stripped down, simple  o/s like program that can be quickly and easily installed by the  vendor. It would simply verify that the hardware is functioning  correctly, the chipset is working, and whatever else and that with  "proper" installation of a "proper" operating system the computer 'would'  function. They could even write it into the sales contract and have the  consumer digitally (or physically) sign - stating what it is they are  buying and that they understand and agree.

If something like that were an option for consumers we would be in a  position to choose whatever distro of Linux we would like, pay less for  the computer (or get a better computer for the price), and not have to 'waste' money by buying a component (Windows)  we don't need or want - and then removing it from the computer.

Pretty rough, unpolished thoughts - I know. But what do you guys think?

> **ClientAlive said:**
> The bag of coffee may be "included" in the price, but it's value has not been exempted from that price.

Large OEMs pay well under $100/copy for Windows, and most of them cover a good portion of that cost by bundling subsidized trial applications from Symantec, etc.  In practice that has meant a machine without Windows, and thus without the additional "shovelware," costs about the same as one with no OS at all.  In some cases they have actually cost more.

Users also want to have their purchases be supported by the OEM.  (In fact, Microsoft does little or no end-user support, certainly for consumers.  It's all provided by the hardware vendors.)  It would be extremely expensive to develop the staff and expertise required to support a computer if they have no idea what operating system you're running, especially when it's an operating system that will be used by, at most, a few percent of their customers.

It's just not economically worth it for most major system vendors to offer anything other than Windows to consumers.  Dell made a couple of forays into this market and have now largely retreated.  

They do offer support for Linux on servers.  Businesses will pay the $300 it costs to have RedHat or Novell Linux pre-installed and get a support contract in return.

You can find systems without an operating system installed in [Dell's outlet center]("http://www.dfsdirectsales.com/dell/notebooks/ctl641/cp11869/cl1").  (Select "No Operating System" in the drop-down at the top.)  These are mostly machines coming off corporate leases and, being two or three years old, aren't very competitive with new machines in terms of hardware, though.

These issues have been argued over for [years]("http://hardware.slashdot.org/story/04/01/27/0135208/Dell-Offers-FreeDOS-With-New-PCs") now.  I believe that, if large OEMs thought they could make money selling and supporting non-Windows machines, they would have done so already.
