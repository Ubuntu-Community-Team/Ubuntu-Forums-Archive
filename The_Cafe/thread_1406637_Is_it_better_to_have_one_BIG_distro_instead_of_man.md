### Post by blueshiftoverwatch on 2010-02-14
> **Starlight said:**
> That's why I'm wishing all the best for Haiku. It's supposed to be a single, unified, open source desktop operating system. With no fragmentation: one kernel, one desktop shell, one software management system, etc. I wonder when it will be released...
I highly doubt that Haiku is ever going to go anywhere. Like ReactOS, it's just a hobby project that has no hopes of ever succeeding in the real world. Linux has been out for around 20 years and it's desktop market share percentage is still in the lower to mid single digits. Even if Haiku is technically superior to Linux, technical superiority doesn't mean it'll overtake the market by a long shot. A lot of other factors come into play, like hardware and software compatibility.

It's a circular cycle of: people won't use an OS until it has good software/hardware compatibility with what they need to do and an OS won't have good software/hardware compatibility with what they need to do until more people start using it. A cycle like that is very hard to break out of and that Linux has been doing very slowly over the past 20 years. If you don't believe me, just ask yourself why x86 and it's older brother x64 are still the dominant CPU architectures even after far superior architectures like PowerPC have been released (and abandoned by the one company that supported it, Apple).

I think it's far too late at this stage in the game for a new OS to enter the desktop arena because of the need for big businesses to run legacy applications. The mobile phone market, by comparison is the complete opposite.

Plus, even if the OS did become as popular as Linux. What makes you think that fragmentation wouldn't take place just as it did on Linux? If it's open source there is nothing to stop someone from making a fork/derivative.
