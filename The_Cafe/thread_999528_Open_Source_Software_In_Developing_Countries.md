>  Open Source Software In Developing Countries

Nelson Mandela explained Ubuntu as follows;
    "A traveller through a country would stop at a village and he didn't have to ask for food or for water. Once he stops, the people give him food, entertain him. That is one aspect of Ubuntu but it will have various aspects. Ubuntu does not mean that people should not address themselves. The question therefore is: Are you going to do so in order to enable the community around you to be able to improve?"

    Ubuntu is also a distribution of the operating system, Linux.  Ubuntu is a distribution centered around the idea of community and the need for others to feel like they are not being controlled or oppressed.  Ubuntu is an Open Source project, one of many.  Open Source software is a type of software meant to empower the users - not a business or company - with control over the software.  In developing countries people have very limited choices in computers and software.  Users of software there are either rich enough to afford a Windows PC, or use pirated versions of the software.  Very few use Open Source software, even though it works, and works well. 

    Open Source software is important for developing countries because to promote world peace, and bring about a nation of educated, well-founded, and safe cultures, you need technology, and more importantly you need information.  an article posted in Linux journal states, "'Even a quick look at the use of computers in the education sector, NGOs, alternative media and civil society is enough to convince us of the potential of FLOSS', says the report, which reviews what's going on in Latin America, Africa and Asia."  FLOSS stands for Free Libre Open Source Software. They go on to say, "In Asia, of some 20+ countries looked at, 'the highest overall FLOSS related activity' seems to be taking place in countries like India, China and Taiwan, (excluding Japan, which is not object of this study) followed by South Korea, Malaysia, Singapore and Thailand."

    If Open Source software was widely used, then the people there would be able to access more information.  If they had FOSS there would be more people in the world who would better their level of education and achievement.  The absence of FOSS has impacted society by forcing people to either spend $300 on software, or pirate the software, and risk losing the ability to access information (when Microsoft pulls the plug on their machine).  This problem started back when computers first appeared as a viable technology for the public.  They used Microsoft Windows, and therefore had no other choice than to use it.  Now FOSS has a new product, and the world needs to see it.

    One way to fix this "little" problem would be to (like everything else) educate people on the subject, and teach them about the benefits of using software created by a collection of people all over the world, instead of a select few in one place.  People are creatures of habit, and therefore people must get used to a new way of doing things, and when they do, who knows... maybe they will open their minds to something else.
