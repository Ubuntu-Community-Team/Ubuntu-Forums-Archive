### Post by igknighted on 2008-03-04
Red Hat distributions with commercial support: RHEL, SLES/SLED, Mandriva

Debian distributions with commercial support: Ubuntu, Xandros, ?

Looking at that list, I would say that the Red Hat distributions are more popular in business.  Looking at most major server applications, they are built for these Red Hat distributions.  Debian itself is popular, but if you are a Debian expert you will likely be looking for a niche job with a Debian based company.  Note that this is the current situation, and could change if Ubuntu is able to make a strong push into the corporate world (and Xandros, I suppose... in many ways they make a pretty good server).

Don't count out BSD and Solaris.  Both are very popular in the server rooms and Solaris especially has a lot of corporate momentum (Sun) behind it.  BSD does as well if you count Yahoo!, assuming they don't get swallowed up my Microsoft.

### Post by scottro on 2008-03-04
Having done a job change recently, my impression is that those seeking Linux skilled people were primarily looking for RedHat.  That's in the NYC area.  

My VERY subjective impression is that there was less demand for Solaris than there used to be, about 5-6 years ago.  

Keep in mind that the IT market constantly changes. Often, more than a sysadmin for RedHat, they'll want a sysadmin who knows Apache. If you're a Ubuntu ace and know Apache (for example--it could be MySQL, Postfix, or other things) inside and out, and you do well on your interview, they'll probably figure that you can figure out what's different between Ubuntu and RedHat pretty quickly.  

My last few years have been almost entirely with FreeBSD, but I was hired for what was primarily a RedHat/CentOS job.  There were various reasons, I had some experience with postfix, which they wanted, as well as some Cisco. 

In general, my advice to people is to figure out which aspect of computers is the thing that you love doing--that is, what keeps you up till 3 AM trying to figure it out--and concentrate on getting better at it. 

Also, for better or worse, unless you're a programmer or a really really high level sysadmin, you'll almost certainly be involved in doing some Windows work, both desktop support and probably some Active Directory.  

Hrrm, I should probably add, or if you work for a huge corporation--in that case, you also might be able to avoid, or almost completely avoid, Windows work. 

Networking is also important--by networking, in this case, I mean with people.  The more people you know, the better the chance you'll get a job offer before it's posted. If I were leaving my job, and I had a friend with similar skills, obviously, I would recommend him and suggest they interview him before they post for a replacement. 

I don't know how helpful certifications are, though they might get your foot in the door.  I have a few, but actually, they never got me a job--of course, they're MS and Cisco--I got my first IT job through networking, and by the time I looked again, my interests had changed to Unix.  They did get me a couple of interviews, but I really didn't want a Cisco or MS oriented job by then.  I do know, when I was first certified and looking, there were a lot of "paper MCSE's."  My joke was that when I was hired, I was a paper MCSE, but I improved to the point where I had become a cardboard one and on a really good day, an 18 point pressboard one.  (Hrrm, funnier at the time, guess ya had to be there.)

So, I haven't given you a really specific answer I fear, but hopefully, it's of some help.  The short answer is right now, in NYC, RedHat seems more in demand than Debian or Ubuntu. On the other hand, a job I interviewed for a few years ago was switching from FreeBSD to Debian, so you never know.  

Find what you really enjoy doing in computers. I think I would base my choice more on that, because no matter what your skill, there will probably be a demand for it.  

Of course, that's hard to say too--in the US at least, more and more companies are outsourcing and more and more people are getting into IT.  However, it seems as if most people do get hired eventually.  <shrug>.
