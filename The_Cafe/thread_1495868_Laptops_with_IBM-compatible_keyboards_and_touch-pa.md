### Post by Shining Arcanine on 2010-05-28
I consider keyboards with often-used in the traditional places for QWERTY keyboards to be IBM-compatible and all others to be IBM-incompatible. Wikipedia has a SVG file showing a qwerty keyboard layout with the keys I consider to be often-used colored in blue:

[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Qwerty.svg](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Qwerty.svg)

Also, I consider touch pads that emulate the behavior of an IBM two-button mouse to be IBM-compatible and touch pads incompatible of emulating that behavior being IBM-incompatible.

I plan to buy a new laptop next year and for reasons I will not explore, I have decided to window-shop early. In the process of window-shopping, I have discovered a disturbing trend that many systems do not come with IBM-compatible keyboards and touch pads. I consider this to be a major usability problem for me and what is worse is that while manufacturers tend to use the same layouts across their product lines, it is difficult to tell whether or not a particular laptop/manufacturer uses an IBM-compatible design because they usually do not publish good pictures of the actual keyboards/touch-pads.

So far, I have been able to see the keyboards and touch pads used by a few manufacturers. The following manufacturers make laptops that are IBM-compatible accord to the definitions above:
[LIST]
[*]Dell
[*]HP
[/LIST]

The following manfacturers are not:
[LIST]
[*]Acer - Touchpads only have 1 button
[*]Apple - Fn Key is where Left Ctrl key should be and their touch pads lack buttons
[*]Asus - Touchpads only have 1 button
[*]IBM/Lenovo - Fn Key is where Left Ctrl key should be
[/LIST]

I have not looked at other manufacturers yet, although I find the task of locating pictures of keyboards and touch pads to place one manufacturer into one group or the other difficult to do, so I was wondering if people here could help me with that. Since I plan to buy a new laptop next year, having a comprehensive list would help me narrow my search tremendously, as the previous metric I used (battery life), was difficult to use because no review site has a sort by battery life option to allow me to sort the models that they have reviewed, which forces me to look at each model manually, at a great cost of my time. Narrowing the list of laptops by IBM-compatibility before considering factors such as battery life appears to be a great time-saver, because so far I have not found a manufacturer that uses both IBM-compatible layouts and IBM-incompatible layouts on multiple product lines and I do not plan to buy any laptop whose layout is IBM-incompatible.
