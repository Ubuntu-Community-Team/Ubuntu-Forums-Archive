### Post by handy on 2007-08-06
I was a self employed tech support guy, working out of a converted bedroom in my house.  Was, because I've been retired for about 2 years.

Running the business for 10 years, I learned a great deal, becoming more efficient with every job.  

A love of computers is where I got the knowledge to put me in the position to help other people get past their hardware, OS, networking, internet & peripheral problems.  

I find it difficult to understand how a computer science degree would teach a person what they are going to need to know in the field.  There is a huge amount of information that I don't know, that I would have learned had I done an IT based university degree.  I learned what I needed to solve a new problem when it presented.

It would seem to me that after finishing a computer science degree, & getting a job in tech' support, that that is when the practical education really starts.

I never worked for big corporations, a couple of solicitor firms, a couple of accountants, a couple of car dealers, with networks no bigger than 10 machines.  I had hundreds of single computer customers, home users & farmers, the ways that these machines were set up were very individual, everything was a version of a windows OS.

I ended up creating a collection of Ghost images, which saved me a huge amount of time when rebuilding a system, or creating a new one.  I used a corporate edition of XP, to get around activation, this saved customers a good deal of money due to the time saved in this process.

Interestingly, two other guys in this town that did the same thing have stopped as well.  Working on windows problems everyday for years creates a burn out situation.  It is not always an easy job, & when you have customers with the solicitor & car dealer mentality, (often highly impractical, & greedy) it can become quite stressful.  In my situation, I had no one else to call on in a tough one, you just work into the night out of business hours, & on weekends when doing big (replace network) installs.  There is surely stress in the IT tech' field, that's for sure, & it is possible to spend a great deal of time to travel a very small distance.

I charged the business customers for every minute, which helped cover all the hours that I couldn't possibly charge the private customers for, it worked out ok in end...

So I think for people who use large support companies, that when you get a tech' fresh out of university, that person is really just at the beginning of their training, they will be cheapest to employ & would be expected as the years go by to go up levels of responsibility moving further & further away from the customers.  That is the problem with any organization, the more people involved the less efficient & the less personal they become.
