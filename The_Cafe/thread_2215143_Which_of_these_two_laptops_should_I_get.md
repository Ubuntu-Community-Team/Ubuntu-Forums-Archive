### Post by mattlach on 2014-04-06
Intel CPU's are by far more capable than AMD ones, depending on what you need to do with it.   For a movie watching, web surfing, email machine, either will be just as good (though Intel will likely give you longer battery life) but from the perspective of raw computing power AMD has completely lost the competition.  It's not even close these days.

A friend of mine does a lot of development in Scala and finds his compile times are HORRID with modern AMD CPU's.    He bought a FX-8350 (apart from the exterme FX-9xxx chips, the top end AMD offering) and found that his compile times were actually 20% longer than with his 2009 Phenom II 965, and at that point Intel was already beating up AMD from a raw performance perspective.

The Bulldozer (and follow up piledriver and steamroller) architectures (which everything current AMD is based on) have been absolute disasters from a raw computing performance perspective.  At the same clock speed a single Haswell core has about an 80% performance advantage over a single AMD core, and uses much less power at the same time.

So if you plan on doing anything CPU intensive (Rendering, encoding, compiling, etc.) Intel is by far the better choice.   

AMD has simply COMPLETELY lost the CPU performance wars, and it shows in their advertising efforts too, as they try to de-emphasize CPU performance and tout the performance of the integrated GPU in their APU chips, but even there INtel has been catching up, and the latest haswell based integrated Iris Pro GPU's...   

As a former AMD fan in the late 90's and early 2000's it pains me, and I like supporting the underdog (which is why my HTPC and my server both have AMD CPU's) but when you need raw performance there simply is no reason to go AMD anymore.   They even lose in the bang for the buck department these days.

There is simply nothing equivalent about their performance at all.

### Post by mattlach on 2014-04-15
> **ubuntuman001 said:**
> 
I don't know about other people, but it seems like your aversion to AMD is a bit exaggerated.  Obviously if looking at the highest end processors from both companies the intel ones are definitely known to perform better in terms of raw power and power usage.  But out of the processors I have listed for these two mid-range laptops, can the difference really be that great? I'm not too convinced.

It really depends.   For email, web, office, etc. I wouldn't be concerned.   That AMD chip will be just fine, but the Intel will outperform it.

I just ran the numbers.  Couldn't find these specific CPU's benchmarked against eachother, but I was able to linearly interpolate from data from their same architecture stablemates.

Based on that, between the i5-4200U and the A10-5757m, the intel CPU is almost 30% faster at non-threaded loads, and it does this at a nominal TDP of only 15W, where the TDP of the AMD CPU is 35W.

So the AMD chip is taking 2.33 times more electricity to produce 78% of the performance, despite its much higher clock!

Now there are some caveats here.   The Intel chip is technically a 2 core (4 logical with hyperthreading) and the AMD has 4 real cores, but  while AMD's core design does have 4 integer cores, there are only two floating point units, so two cores have to share one.   AMD may edge the intel design out in highly threaded usage scenarios, but it will be close.  Not sure.  Very tough to predict how much the turbo clock will kick in when all cores are loaded.

That being said, most computer software run still isn't threaded enough that this becomes anything but an academic discussion.   Unless you do lots of encoding, rendering or certain scientific simulation

There is no other way to say it.   The Intel Haswells are the better design.  They use less power (provide longer battery life, and become less hot) at the same time as they are much faster per core.    Even by brute forcing it, and throwing extra cores, higher clock speeds and more than double the power usage after the AMD chip, it can't keep up, at least not in the types of applications most people use.
