### Post by p_quarles on 2008-04-10
> **popch said:**
> I am not all that concerned about describing sandwiches.
Lettuce move on?

Expressing oneself in any language is a feat which is subject to uncountable variables. For some meanings, pointing is in fact quite adequate (e.g., "I'm thirsty," or "My bladder is full"). These are meanings which have to do with relatively unmediated physical realities. 

The more abstract one gets, the more barriers there are to successful communication. This almost always leads to a specialized terminology that shares attributes of both natural languages and constructed languages. The success of communication depends upon all participants being adequately fluent in both the language and in the specialized jargon. 

To put this more simply: ease of self-expression is far more dependent upon the properties of the interlocuters than it is upon the properties of the language in which they are communicating.

I don't know why people take Whorf seriously, but that's tangential.   The idea that cultures are unable to express "something" is ridiculous.  How do you test that?  Do you present someone with something they've never before seen and ask them what it's called?  How are we able to engage in scientific endeavors like astronomy?  There is no language on Earth that evolved to express the concept of a point of infinite density that punches a hole in space-time, yet we English speakers have invented constructions that express this concept: we call it a black hole.  There's nothing special about English that allows us to do this.  The term "black hole" is only superficially descriptive, as is "space-time".  Would any other language really do any better or worse?

The focus here seems to be on content words.  Nouns like catsup, sandwich, singularity  and space-time are as easy to invent as le chien,  &#1088;&#1086;&#1076;&#1080;&#1090;&#1077;&#1083;&#1080; or los huevos.  Every language creates new words for things they haven't seen, as someone had to a very long time ago when they first saw a cat, some eggs or when they had to call their parents something.  There is no language that's limited to the vocabulary it has at any given time.  Period.  

What really matters, in terms of who can talk about what, is the grammar of a language.  Grammars differ greatly from language to language, but they all share features in common.  For instance, they are all capable of talking about something that is distant in both time and space.  They can all describe spatial relationships of objects in sight (as in, the construction of a sandwich, or the color and shape of an object).  They can all describe who is doing what to whom.  If there were any limiting factor to a language, this would be it.

I am, by the way, a linguistics major.  That doesn't automatically mean I'm right, but I just want you to know I'm not pulling criticisms of other linguists out of thin air.
