### Post by macogw on 2007-04-11
That'd be cool, but I'd get kids a bit younger than teens, starting at like age 10, I think.  Once kids hit mid-teens, if they haven't already found something really interesting about computers (or if they grew up without one due to finances or whatever, the urge to know "but how?  but why? what happens if I ....?"), they're not likely to ever get a "passion" for them because too much else of life gets in the way of it.  Kids who are a few years younger and don't have homecoming/prom/winter formal/job/who-knows-what-else hanging over them have more time to spend building an interest in computers.  Does that make any sense?  It seems like there's a window of age where you're most likely to pick up those hobbies that just stick with you for good.  

Anyway, this kind of reminds me of how my boyfriend and one of his friends want to make a hacker (with a h not a cr) camp for kids.  Crash courses in how to code, probably C, how stuff works under the hood...there'd be no script kiddies produced, just real coders, knowing how he is.  

I like both ideas because I'm all for getting kids into computers.  Too many kids these days (what am I, an old woman?) think they know all about computers because they can hit "next" 30 times to install some random game they found online that probably has 10 viruses stuffed inside it.  I mean, I don't know a lot of computer-literate-and-confident (not timid like my mom's "I don't right click" syndrome) babyboomers, but the ones I know know a LOT about computers.  They're programmers with random circuit boards that can be wired up to do something instead of typing in code with a keyboard and all sorts of neat things like that.  kids are a lot more confident with computers than babyboomers tend to be, but I can't figure out if that's good or bad.  It's good because they have a chance of finding/learning something new by clicking something new.  It's bad because the "it won't happen to me" (crash, virus, phishing..) attitude can take over when you're too confident on a computer but don't have the security know-how.  Of course, I'm not counting the other kids in my computer science class.  I'm guessing that they, like me, were of the "confident enough to click on things and if it breaks, well tough cookies, i'll figure out how to fix it" variety (as opposed to the "well i clicked a bunch of things and now it's broke.  fix it for me" variety).  When people say kids are good with computers, it's very misleading.  They're often just good with AIM/MSN and email and Googling, and well, what else is there, right :p  Parents just see their kids clicking around really fast and assume they know what they're doing
