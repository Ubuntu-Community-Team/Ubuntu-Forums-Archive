Pay as you go though so you buy the phone and if you ever want to make calls on it you put an amount on it. You can, use the inbuilt wifi to get all your apps. You probably need to put the sim card in for whats app, but that's free to use (once you get a free year or pay the $1 a year to use it)

### Post by qyot27 on 2016-11-07
Yes, any given Android phone bought as either prepaid or unlocked will be able to function as a standard computing device.  If you get a prepaid device and then don't pay the monthly service fees, they cancel the phone/text/data service*, but you already own the phone device outright and can use it the way you want as a computer.  The rub is simply that you're restricted to WLAN connections and have no mobile data (and obviously no texting service or minutes).  So inside of your house or other free Wi-Fi locations, there'd be no real problem using the apps on the phone, but if they require an Internet connection it'll cut out as soon as you're out of range.  You can also load music or video files on a microSD card - since many phones have limited internal storage - and use it as a media player regardless of the presence/quality of a Wi-Fi connection.  So despite the thread title, most of this post is going to be about the situation that'll arise when you do find the need for the phone functionality.

*However, this can be a gotcha based on if you ever decide you do want to use the phone as a phone but don't want that carrier.  You'll have to reactivate the phone on their network, be assigned a new number (since the old number got axed when the service was cancelled before), and be obligated to stay with them for X number of months before being able to switch to a provider you *do* want.  Short answer: even if you don't want to use the phone as a phone, pick a provider you'll be happy with, just in case.

If you do go with a prepaid phone, I'd avoid Sprint or its major consumer subsidiaries Boost Mobile and Virgin Mobile (in the US, these two are owned by Sprint and use CDMA; this is not necessarily true anywhere else in the world).  Sprint's unlocking and carrier switch policies whenever you do eventually decide to make the phone useful will come back and bite you hard.  Sprint's minor MVNOs (read: not actually owned by Sprint but have agreements to use Sprint's network) can actually give you good value for phones which are basically unlocked from the start without giving you hassles.  RingPlus and Ting are the two that stand out here, and they serve slightly different niches.  I considered both of them, but eventually went with Ting because if I ended up needing to buy a new phone, they had a nicer selection, and I prefer the simplicity of a pay-for-what-you-use (you know, mostly traditional postpaid) service.  RingPlus offers more standard-for-cellphones monthly plans, but for far less expensive - some of them are free - prices.


I'm speaking from experience here.  Last year, I purchased a Boost Mobile-branded LG Volt 2 shortly after I got a new job (Best Buy had it on sale for $100).  Great lower-mid-range phone at the time I bought it, but the monthly service costs were way too high, so after the month I paid for when I bought the phone and the month of free service Best Buy footed, I switched to an anemic $3 daily as-needed plan (which they no longer offer) just to keep the phone active until I could switch carriers.  When it finally came time to switch away from Boost, it took a couple of months of back and forth just to clear away the right obstacles and get everything switched over.  If I had done what the clerk at Best Buy told me to do and just stopped paying altogether, I'd still run afoul of Sprint's 12-month stipulation for switching away - the phone must be active for 12 months on your existing provider before it can switch to a different Sprint MVNO**.  There's a reason GSM phones are usually a better deal (you may only need to stay for 6 months, and swapping GSM cards is pretty much the only hurdle), but I knew jack about phone networks or any of that when I bought the phone - I was just looking at the phone specs.  Ting has the advantage of being able to use either Sprint's CDMA network or T-Mobile's GSM network, based on which existing phone you want to bring over or which one you buy from them.

**that's just a technical limitation; Sprint's particular CDMA configuration isn't shared by the other CDMA-using networks in the US, like Verizon (only Verizon? I'm not sure).  And most phones either aren't usable with both GSM or CDMA, or if they are (my Volt 2 happens to be, IIRC), the GSM part is only configured for international GSM bands, not the US domestic GSM bands.  It'd require replacing the firmware to make that work.


As far as the Ubuntu phone idea goes, there are no manufacturers currently offering them.  The existing ones have all sold out, and despite rumors back in July about the Meizu MX6 having an Ubuntu variant, there's still no information or announcement about it.  I also remember reading about the phones not being configured for US-based LTE networks, so it could end up not working right with mobile data if you activate it with a provider that lets you bring your own device.  I'd love to get one once the network kinks and availability issues are hammered out (well, aside from salivating over the definitely-not-an-Ubuntu-phone LG V20, but that's really only because of the sheer power and featureset in that thing).

### Post by 1clue on 2016-11-07
Any smart phone, no sim card. Preferably unlocked/rooted. Preferably something that was fairly common.

Lots of people posting this already, but just adding another vote.

IMO look on craigslist for a used previous-gen smart phone that somebody wants to sell. You get it really cheap, and it's probably something that the phone companies still sell.

My preference is to get something like a Galaxy Note 4, that has significant memory built-in and also supports a SDHC card. And batteries that can be swapped out.  If it's a used phone that probably means the batteries are about half cooked, and you can always use more memory.

***Edit:** I recently switched to a Note 5, but I don't recommend this. It lost both the external SDHC slot and the removable battery, two things near the top of my list of desirables. I upgraded without researching what I would gain or lose. I would undo it if I could.*
