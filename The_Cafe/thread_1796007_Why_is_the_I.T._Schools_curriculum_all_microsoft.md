### Post by Roasted on 2011-07-03
This is not always true everywhere. However, where it does exist, it disgusts me.

Recently our state underwent some massive educational funding cuts. Because that makes sense to cut educational funding and childhood health insurance, right? Anyway, several school districts responded with "what do we do now?" I have been a squeaky wheel for several years, silently suggesting from time to time "let's try this instead." It never was well received.

At one point we had the opportunity to drop in an Ubuntu LTSP thin client lab. Mostly because the 3 setups we had, all Microsoft based, were very very unstable, slow, clunky, etc. Sure enough, it flew with flying colors.

This raised the big question - can we use this more widespread? We're in the process of beginning to flirt with that idea now as we move forward, as are several other school districts in the area.

The bottom line is, **you don't need Microsoft anymore.** I'm sorry, but you just don't. Fortunately our school's curriculum is based more-so on concepts than exact instructions. You know, the basic fundamentals to learning the "idea" behind each application, and the ability to take these skills with you. The truth is, by the time these students get out, Microsoft Office will have been overhauled another 3 times. Why do you need to specifically teach "Microsoft Office 2007", etc. You don't. And if your curriculum says that, their head is up their ***.

We are beginning to move forward with this, and it's very exciting to see taking place. I believe the next few years will be very interesting for us.

### Post by Sternfan on 2011-07-06
A couple of thoughts (I don't post much, but I've found this thread interesting).

My background - desktop/server support since the mid 90s.  MCSE 2000/2003.  Taught the MSCE track since 2004 at a local tech school.  I also tinker and use linux (90% ubuntu, 10% fedora).

Tech schools exist to prepare students for jobs.  They almost invariably end up working with a SMB (small to medium sized business) - rarely do they end up with a large, fortune 500 company.

So what are the SMBs running?  In 15+ years I've only run across ONE SMB that had exactly ONE **desktop **running Redhat.  Everything else is Windows, with a small smattering of MACs.  I also did break/fix for a computer shop.  And to the best of my recollection, I never had a linux box come in for repair.  99% windows and a few MACs.  (I even tried talking the owner into selling old PCs with Linux installed, he wouldn't take me up on it).
That's the desktops.  For the servers, it is some variation of NT - NT itself, 2000, 2003, 2008 etc.  There might be a few linux boxes running Apache, Squid or Mysql, but overall it is an NT show.  A close friend of mine just got a server-support job with a medical records company - something like 50 servers mostly running on VMware - he has yet to tell me about any Linux servers.

And why do the SMBs run mostly Windows?
- Apps - tons of "windows only" apps needed for businesses.
- Active Directory - there is nothing close to this in Linux - Group Policy for controlling the business desktop etc. is a must.
- Integration with servers such as Exchange.

So, in short I believe that tech schools teach what is needed for jobs, and that the jobs are MS-based for the vast majority.  If this was the auto industry, the students would learn how to work on chevy's, fords, toyotas etc.  Because that's what most of them would be working on - not Maclarens, or Ferrari's that represent about 1% of the autos on the road.

So the trick is to get Linux into the business world, and there are a whole ton of issues on making this work.  Just going through some old links, check out these:

A 50 page thread on setting up Ubuntu as a Domain Controller:
[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=640760](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=640760) 
I actually tried to do this and could never make it work - probably had something like 50 hours of effort tinkering with this.  

And years ago I put together a post on using Ubuntu in a business: (even 3 years old I think the thread is worth the read)
[http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=408100](http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=408100)

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2011-08-01
Every time this topic comes up, what depresses me most is the view most people seem to take on education. 

<soapbox>

Vocational education post-secondary is fine; I wish society valued it as much as we value college.  

But for kids in K-12, we need to be giving them *education* not *job training*.  My high school education included (in no particular order):

 - physics
 - chemistry
 - Latin
 - calculus
 - art
 - marching band
 - music theory
 - sewing
 - "shop" (a random assortment of woodworking, metalworking, and other craft/factory type work)
 - Public speaking & debate
 - history
 - English literature

I could go on, but the point is NONE OF THESE THINGS is useful in my job field.  I for one would have been better served (from a job training standpoint) by being taught shell scripting and database administration.

But that's not the point of education.  The point is to expand our horizons, to let us experience a wide range of knowledge and skills, to teach us how to learn things we know nothing about, etc.  

Given that list of subjects above, would a little familiarity with unix-like operating systems, simple scripting languages, or HTML be such an outlandish thing to teach?  I daresay such things would have a wider application than calculus, in any case.

Learning how to do your father's job the way your father did it is dark ages thinking.  No matter what tools they choose to teach 12-18 year-olds (or choose to teach them with), I hope they're aiming a little higher than to churn out yet another generation of cubicle-filler with the same limitations as the last.

</soapbox>

tl;dr: education != job training.  Expand their little worlds a tad, it won't hurt.
