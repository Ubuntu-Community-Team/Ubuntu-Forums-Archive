### Post by Methuselah on 2009-04-05
Well, I'm going to get more on topic and say my few views on this idea of where the windows/linux situation is heading.
I don't often comment on this and ended up writing a rather lot but heck, I had the time today...lol

Anyway, I'm not holding my breath for linux to 'oust' windows.
I won't affect whether *I* use it or not but I admit it would be cool to deal a blow to MS.

I believe eventually microsoft will lose it's lopsided market dominance and it will be to the open source alternatives.
Nothing proprietary will threaten windows for the same reasons nothing proprietary ever has.
I've seen reports where even microsoft acknowledges this, ranking linux as a bigger threat than apple.

It's the same as the browsers wars: exhibit A is firefox.
Open source projects are like non-state actors in national security parlance; they just don't have the same centralised vulnerabilities corporations have.
Microsoft cannot play by its ancient textbook to combat them; they actually have to play fair and be better value.
How long was IE6 out before we saw a new release and why is IE8 the talk already?
See exhibit A.

Now, linux has made strides towards addressing its deficiencies relative to windows and while windows is trying to improve security etc, any convergence benefits linux because linux is free and windows is getting more expensive.

The events of the last year have not been insignificant.
We have seen linux coming pre-installed on quite a few computing devices from well known manufacturers and we have seen related improvements in hardware support.
Pre-loading is the way in, and it's happening to a greater extent than ever despite pressures on OEMs from Microsoft,
Especially in tough times, it just makes economic sense to build a linux market where your profit margin can be higher.

This brings us to why microsoft has made special provisions for Xp to continue to be supported on small laptops.
Windows Xp, put on death row by Microsoft itself, had to be given a stay of execution because it's the only thing they had that could compete with applications of linux on netbooks.

Vista also proved that microsoft was not longer able to sell anything.
It would no longer be accommodated unconditionally.
In some circles, it was rejected outright as unsuitable.
Sure, some home users thought latest = greatest but many big customers that actually mattered to MS did not budge.
So windows 7 will be making an earlier debut than I'm certain was initially planned.

This trend of microsoft creating what's not needed or being caught flat footed and reacting to innovations elsewhere will continue and eventually they'll find themselves outflanked.
In the open source community many entities create exactly what they need and others often find this buffet available to pick from for free.
It is hard for a single company to compete with this combination of focus and diversity.

However, where people get it wrong is declaring years of the linux desktop etc.
That has a PR appeal but there will never be a year where everyone gets up and decides they're replacing their windows installations with linux.
But as far as I know, linux usage has been trending upwards.
This will likely continue, and the growth rate will increase over time until linux years arrive in succession.

- The majority of home computer users I know don't care what operating system they use.  Many of them don't even know what an operating system is. - Many advantages of Linux lie in the enterprise field.  I'm probably going to take some flak for this one, but I just don't think that Linux offers most home users that much of an improvement over Windows. - Home users almost always buy their operating systems preinstalled on single machines.  In this case, the monetary savings from switching to Linux are really quite insignificant.  A major enterprise customer will care most of all about the support they receive, which is exactly how Linux firms make their money.

In my opinion, this all amounts to very bad news for Canonical.  They have invested a tremendous amount of time, energy, and money into making their system ideal for home users.  Red Hat has spent that time, energy, and money making their system ideal for the enterprise and building a platinum reputation as a great company to work with for tech support.
