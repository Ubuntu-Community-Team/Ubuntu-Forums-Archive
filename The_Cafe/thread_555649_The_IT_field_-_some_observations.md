### Post by FyreBrand on 2007-09-20
Those are interesting observations.  I had a similar experience at a recent internship.

It was a .NET project so I knew they would be using XP and I wasn't looking forward to that.  Anyway here is what I noticed especialy about the IT contractors management of our desktop systems.

### Post by toupeiro on 2008-02-06
Not knowing how to judge exactly what you are looking for with these IT reps, I will say that the not mixing products comment they made seem WAY in left field.  If you have to recommend that you only use select manufacturer products with that manufacturers OS without any flexibility, then perhaps its time to consider alternatives that will let your IT infrastructure adapt to the flow of your business more freely rather than be pigeon-holed and herded by a company which has high cost associations to legally run, while they simultaneously squash your own technical innovation.  If the last decade of being a sys admin in an internationally based company has taught me anything, its to maintain a stable environment, while looking ahead, listening, and positioning yourself to shift your technology and infrastructure with the direction of your business demands when it comes .. because it will definately come, regardless of how big or small your company is.  Not always the easiest task to accomplish -  I personally disagree with the poster above..One thing I can say about the IT field -- Maybe anyone can be in IT, but to be good in IT is a completely different ballgame.  I think it matters less about how much you know going into something sometimes, but how much experience you have with change, how willing you are, and how fast you can learn.  I appreciate techs who know little about the environment coming in, but only need to be shown something once, or better yet take it apon themselves to just figure it out before I have to show them anything. :)  Its one thing to do that at home, its another thing to do that with a SLA (Service Level Agreement) at 3AM and seconds of downtime start equating to potentially thousands of dollars lost.

Long story short, While IT assessments are good from time to time to see how close you are to the creamy center of industry standards and practices, don't underestimate or ultimately undermine the decisions and capacity of your techs, regardless if they are on the payroll employees, or contract employees unless they are simply not delivering what you need them to be.  Afterall, they were hired for a reason, and if you are talking with new IT reps, be careful that what "standards" they preach are still flexible enough to adapt to /your/ needs as they change over time.  Also, don't let money savings be your only driving factor for an IT staffing change, In my experience, no outsourcing company could ever truly deliver on the cost savings promises with their standards.  It usually ends up costing so much to get there, that once you are there you've spent twice the amount of money you did before ever outsourcing, and are in a situation where your runlevel support cannot be done without their presence unless you completely reinvent your infrastructure model again.
