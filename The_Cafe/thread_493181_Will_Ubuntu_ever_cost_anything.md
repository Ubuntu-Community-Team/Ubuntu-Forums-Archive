### Post by az on 2007-07-05
> **DM was on fire! said:**
> Ubuntu will most likely never cost, considering all the software is freeware.

It is *not* freeware.  It is free-libre open source.  There is a big difference.

Free-libre open source software is licences so that your freedom to obtain the software, to use the software for any purpose, to study the source code and modify it and to redistribute the changed program are protected.

You cannot take free-libre open source software and turn it into non-free software.

Freeware is different.  It is non-free to begin with (non-free, here means you do not have the above mentioned freedoms).

> **Drifter said:**
> What if Bill Gates becomes the number one rich dude in the world, he is # 2 now and then decides to let windows be free.  Which one would win out then, I don't know just wondering.

Free in what sense?  Free of cost or free as in software freedom?  If it's free of cost, it's freeware, and my freedoms are still not preserved.  If it's free as in freedom as in licenced under a GPL-compatible licence, then windows and Ubuntu both win.

### Post by Tundro Walker on 2007-07-05
> **zero244 said:**
> I have to disagree. Coming from a Windows world where much of the software is not free. I think Ubuntu or Canonical at some point will have to charge something.
Can open source last forever. Perhaps the next generation of programmers will not want to work for free.
The people who own and run Canonical are not going to live forever.

Ubuntu in its current form is free........but that could change in future releases.

From Shuttleworth himself, his goal is to provide a free/libre OS, in the hopes that everyone will use it, thus comodotizing the market with it (IE: it is no longer something that can be charged for).  Now, Shuttleworth is not an idiot.  His idea is to profit from the culture and needs the spring up around the free OS.  Thus, hardware deals (EG: Dell), support, consulting, specialized programming, training, certification, etc.

Think of it this way.  When writing was invented, books were expensive.  It was costly to hand-scribe them, costly to get the paper, bind the book, transport them (keeping them safe), etc.  Today, it's almost a negligible cost to write things...you turn on your computer and type something to the world on the internet...you snag some paper and a pencil for mere pennies and write a book to whomever wants to read it.  It is a foundation of knowledge transfer.  But, while knowledge transfer through written word is practically free these days, there is a culture that is profiting indirectly from it.  You have technical writers that do training manuals and sell them.  You have novelists that charge for their books.  Etc, etc.  Because writing may be easy, but writing is merely a means to an end, that end being the exchange of information.  Some information is worth more, but some is becoming so common it's free (EG: you can find information on BASH scripting for free online, or you can go buy a book...)

Computers are the next foundation of knowledge transfer support, by providing access to the Internet, publishing tools, etc.  Microsoft has been profiting from this for a long time, in essence making it an "elitist" club to get onto the internet (because you not only have to have a computer, you have to fork over tons of money for their OS license, MS Office, etc).   At first, it wasn't so bad, but they're corporate "profit" mentality is starting to hamper the access and use of computers as a technical enhancement to society.

Shuttleworth created Canonical so that Ubuntu could provide a free foundation for computers, bypassing any charge necessary to "fuel" your computer.  This lowers the cost to join the "internet" club to just the price of a computer and a dial-up connection.

While it sounds like a gamble, Shuttleworth realizes that software is becoming as easy to crank out as literature is.  Just as tons of folks started providing info for free when literature was easier to crank out, so are software developers.  After a while, there will be no justification to pay to use an OS or certain software, because someone will have provided a free one for all to use.  

Shuttleworth realizes this and is trying to skip over just offering an "alternative" to Windows, but something that is superior to Windows.  This removes one of the hurdles.  Plus, it's free.  This removes another major hurdle preventing folks from coming over.  Ubuntu dev's are then trying to make it easy to migrate to Ubuntu (by migrating your Windows IE bookmarks, work, etc), which removes another hurdle.  A final hurdle will be to make it easy for folks to GO BACK to Windows, since it gives them an easy out...a way to go back to their safety net.  Some folks would think that's stupid ("once you have them, you don't want to make it easy to get out"), but the opposite is true.  A lot of folks don't want to jump in the pool if it's impossible or difficult to get out.  It's too big of a commitment.  This hurdle will be difficult, since Windows is closed-source, though.  But, by helping out the "One Laptop Per Child" program, he's bypassing that hurdle by simply converting a whole generation of young computer users to Linux instead of Windows.  If you cut off the source, it's easier to snuff out the flame.

Shuttleworth's gamble is on so many folks taking up Ubuntu, that the whole computer culture will have to start recognizing that services are required for it...paid services, from tech support, to consulting, to training books and classes, to certification, to hardware deals with Dell and parts manufacturers.  The idea is not to profit directly by selling Ubuntu, but indirectly by all the culture shift it causes.

Shuttleworth is also gambling on Microsoft's continued efforts to look after other big corporations' best interests rather than its customers...the dreaded "Trusted Computing".  Shuttleworth knows folks are willing to part with a little personal info (like monitoring what programs a user uses most so that it can help focus dev resources on making those better) so they can have a good platform, but he knows folks don't want half their computer power sucked up by programs spying on them and denying them access to things they're not "worthy" of seeing/using based on someone else's (corporate-motivated) opinion.  People want software they can trust to do what THEY want it to do, not software that decides whether to trust the user or not.
