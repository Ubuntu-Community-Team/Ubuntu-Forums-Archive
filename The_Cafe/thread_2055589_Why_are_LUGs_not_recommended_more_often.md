### Post by TheFu on 2012-09-10
> **tartalo said:**
> Are you in a LUG? What do you think?

I'm a LUG organizer in my area and active in and another.  I  always try to recommend LUGs for people looking for longer term  understanding and solutions that take longer. Most people asking  questions here seem to be trying to learn just enough to get it working. They aren't looking for a lifestyle, just to solve an issue and move on.

How many people running "other OSes" join UGs?  0.000001%? Linux isn't any different.

If  someone is just looking for an answer to 1 specific issue, this forum  is a great way.  Teaching someone how to ask a good question  [http://catb.org/~esr/faqs/smart-questions.html](http://catb.org/~esr/faqs/smart-questions.html) is an important skill for  everyone too both here and in person.

Our LUG meets monthly and always has a  presentation for at least 50% of the meeting.  With 10-25 people showing  up, it is hard to answer everyone's questions completely.  People forget their issues when they show up too.  Selective memory.

We run an  email listsrv, but younger people don't seem as inclined to use that  method of communication.  The organizers are older, like me.  **We don't do facebook or twitter.**  Most of us do not have accounts and will not have accounts due to privacy concerns.  Email is our preferred method. We have some non-local email traffic from people that left our LUG, but want to remain connected.  It is that sort of group.  After every meeting, we try to grab a snack or a beer to forge more lasting friendships too.  I think that personal connection matters.  There really are some very interesting people around here.

There  are 4 or 5 LUGs in my metro area - all meet on different nights in the  month.  Two are mainly for university students and the other two are  mostly professional administrators.  The topics between them are  extremely wide, but for very different crowds.  I suppose someone could attend each if they wanted to drive 45-90 minutes to each meeting one-way.

A once-a-month meeting doesn't build friendships the way other activities do.  To get folks to show up consistently is hard.  Life gets in the way, but around here, so does traffic.

### Post by TheFu on 2012-09-11
> **tartalo said:**
> But I agree that selling to new users the LUGs as "free support" would be a mistake.

Every few months someone from a company will have an "emergency support request" on our LUG email list.  They usually want to know the "magic stuff to type" to make their server work again.  

Invariably, their "emergency" isn't sufficient for any budget for people or replacement hardware.  The machine is usually running a 6 yr old, unpatched version of Redhat-something.  After about a week, they say there is some budget for a professional who can fix it in a single visit.

Later we find out at the LUG meeting what happened from the member who lived close enough for a visit.  A 6 yr RAID controller failed months earlier and the storage was limping along degraded until another failure happened.  There are no backups and that controller isn't made anymore.  We never hear back from the original person again, not even a "thank you."

I'd guess that only 1 out of 20 first-time LUG attendees will come back.  I know that I don't always attend every meeting unless the topic sounds interesting.  Last month another LUG had someone come in and talk about integrating Linux with Active Director - the entire room was packed.  The topic was relevant to our members day jobs.  

A good topic brings in lots of people, but a topic like that is not very useful for people playing with Linux at home.  It is a different crowd.
