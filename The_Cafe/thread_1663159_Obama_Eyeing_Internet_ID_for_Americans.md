### Post by euler_fan on 2011-01-09
The only news here is the the US GOV is getting behind the idea. As more online services move to adding some form of ``id token" (Like Paypal where they will SMS you a one-time ID verification code in order to log on), it is a matter of time before something like this was going to get endorsed.

My issues with it are threefold. 

First, who do we trust to issue such an ID? In a web of trust this is relatively explicit because of the signatures on the crypto keys. With a CA-based system we have to trust someone who we may not have any reason to trust (not even by proxy) which favors large, established vendors.

Second, avoiding government involvement in establishing identity is going to be inevitable because of the court system. Once a lawsuit is filed where a material issue is the basis to believe or not believe a person's online identity because they have this token which may (but probably cannot be explicitly proven to have been) compromised there will end up being common law. Issues with large financial impacts end up being legislated then then there goes the lack of significant government involvement.

Third, the history of chip and pin fraud in Europe demonstrates, where there is a will to break a security measure there is a way. Ergo I would argue that rather than trying to prevent all on line identity fraud, we make it easier to challenge illicit behavior.

### Post by euler_fan on 2011-01-11
> **cascade9 said:**
> Last I heard, you werent required to carry an ID card in Denmark? 

No ID cards here (.au), or New Zealand, Ireland, Norway, Japan (apart from gaijins), the UK is in the process of replealing the Identity Cards Act  (will take effect on 21-01-2011). Thats just off the top of my head.....

This scheme isnt a national ID card anyway.

Having a legally recognized ID card and being required to carry it, much less produce it on demand, are very different things.

The difference between all state ID's complying with a set of national minimum standards (remember the REAL ID Act of a couple years ago?) and a single national ID card aren't that different in function so much as form.

And if some certificate linked to your legal identity becomes a legally recognized standard of identification (something like, ``This person has a valid certificate saying she is Alice as defined in statute therefore she is Alice") then it becomes a de facto ID card, albeit in electronic form.

Again, this is nothing new. Companies issue crypto keys and dongles, Paypal will send you SMS messages with one-time log on credentials, and everyone can link a crypto key with their identity via services like Verisign or CA Cert. The issue is to what extent it should be required in order to have an existence online.

As we should all be intimately familiar with from our own presence here, it isn't strictly necessary to have our online ID linked to our real ID in a binding way to still have a meaningful Internet. But as anyone who has ever looked at seller feedback to determine who to buy from on Amazon or Ebay should know, it isn't sufficient either.
