### Post by chessnerd on 2011-03-22
I fall into the group that feels that FOSS is a good idea and I love some of the software that it has produced. I also love the fact that the community of FOSS has a lot of power behind it. However, I do not advocate every piece of software being open source. I don't care how someone licenses their intellectual property.

If someone wants to keep software proprietary, that is their choice. There is some really good propriety software and I see nothing wrong with people keeping the source code to themselves. By the same token, I believe that open source software should be able to be licensed anyway the creator(s) choose, not just the GPL or other "mainstream" open-source licenses, assuming they have considered [the practical limitations of doing so]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/License_proliferation"). I am also okay with projects that only open-source part of their code.

Basically, I'm not idealistic about FOSS, but instead advocate the freedom of software developers to choose the own course that their software will take. I hold this stance for all creative works, and feel the same way about Creative Commons.

(Also, I don't like using "FLOSS" because Free/Libre Open-Source Software is clunkier and it sounds like I'm talking about dental hygiene :P)

### Post by gnumbr on 2011-03-22
I think that many people are naive about how bad proprietary software *can* be. Windows XP spies on you, as does Mac OS X. People value convenience _much_ more highly than their freedom - a fatal mistake, no matter what is said about it. This corporate creep could be the early days of a world where corporations and governments are in control of us, most of the time (if you think it is bad now, wait and see). Okay, so the odd codec and application will not end the world, but you need to reject them as soon as you find something which will suffice as a free software alternative; we need to keep it in our minds to continuously seek these free alternatives on a regular basis (as frequently as is practical).

If it is law that spying on people is illegal, then why are software companies getting away with it? That is what you need to keep at the forefront of your minds. Closed software is NEVER a good thing - would you trust a food product, of which the ingredients were hidden? Of course not. Would you give up personal and financial details to a surreptitious web site? Of course not, so why do you trust software of which you know little or nothing, apart from the BS which is fed to you to keep you pacified? My analogies may seem a little obscure, but I am sure you can make sense of where I am coming from.

There is absolutely no reason whatsoever for the source code of *any* software to be kept secret from the user. If there is nothing to hide, then hide nothing. Talk is cheap - let ME be the judge. Give me the source of your wonderful software, I'll study it and scrutinise it, and then compile it to see if it does the same as the pre-compiled version. If you want me to trust your software, then there are to be NO secrets. In an honest world, secrets are a bad thing. Show me you have nothing bad or damaging, and I shall use your products.

That is the very core of "free software" - it is freely examinable, and you should question as to WHY people refuse to show you their recipes - it just stirs suspicion. Imagine I were a stranger (most users are total strangers to the writers of the software they use) and I offered you some food, in the street, out of the blue; surely you'd be hesitant and slightly suspicious, and yet you trust mega corporations on a daily basis - monolithic, faceless money machines that have FAR less to lose through deceipt and lies to people they will never have to face, than a guy in the street who is probably as honest, and accountable (you can report him to the police, as you've seen him) as the next "average Joe".
