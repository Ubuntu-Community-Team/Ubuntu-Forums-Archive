### Post by aysiu on 2006-12-06
A few months ago, an acquaintance of mine found out I use Linux (we were playing an icebreaker that involved picking sides of a room based on the one of two items you most identified with--the first one was "Windows or Mac"). His response to hearing I used Linux was, "Oh, you must be in technology, then," to which I replied, "Not exactly." But since we were in the middle of a game, that conversation sort of petered out.

I identify on these forums primarily as an ex-English teacher mainly because I taught English for five years (I've been at my current job only two and a half years), and I got interested in desktop Linux while I was still teaching.

My current position is more technologically-oriented than other positions in my office (I work in admissions), as I oversee anything that goes into the database (data entry) and anything that goes out (reporting). It is not primarily a "technology" position, though. It does require me to be a bit more adept with the software than other users in my office, but I'm still considered by the college to be a power user. I'm not in the technology department. I'm not a programmer or a system administrator.

More importantly, I think people should recognize something amazing about Linux--at least for me. People typically think of cause and effect working this way: person is interested in technology in general ==> person then gets interested in Linux.

For me, it was the opposite direction. I got interested in Linux because of all the spyware/malware problems I was having with Windows. I just, as a lot of Windows advocates say, wanted to get my work done. I didn't want to have to tinker with the registry and rebooting and spyware cleaning and searching all over the internet for answers.

Through having fun using Linux, I have become more interested in technology. I'll tell you that's about a 180 degree spin from where I was ten or twenty years ago. In high school, I wrote letters to all my friends (even the ones who lived nearby). When we all went away to college, they were already on email. I went away to college boasting to my friends, "I'm not going to use email. I'll keep writing letters." Eventually, though, I  caved. You can't keep writing letters to people who respond to you with email. In my studies, I also refused to live up to the Asian nerd stereotype of being into computers and math. I was good at math, but I didn't like it. I was more drawn to humanities, and in university I ended up majoring in English.

When I started using Linux (initially, I was turned off by it--too much of a culture shock, but Firefox eased me into the world of open source), computer use became fun. I had more fun getting to know how to do things, finding ways to be more efficient, seeing what's under the hood. Understand--that is not part of my general personality. I never cared to discover the inner workings of the registry in Windows or understand how .dll files were related to the programs they supported. Learning DOS commands didn't appeal to me.

Something about Linux made computing fun for me. Just wanted to throw that out there. I'm sure a lot of people get into Linux because they're naturally curious people--tinkerers, "hobbyists." For me, getting into Linux made me more curious. Any others out there like me?

### Post by izalac on 2006-12-06
I guess I belong into a third group of people then.
I'm a techie, and I wrote my first program at the age of 8 in Amstrad CPC6128's Locomotive Basic. Over the time, I became CP/M, Amiga, DOS and Windows power user. I finished a math & science focused high school and computer science college. I got introduced to Linux some 10 years ago...
...but I never really started using it. It started as a directory in my DOS system running under UMSDOS and slowly getting its own partition that was nearly constantly present on my computer. But I never made a switch because my hardware was never really compatible with Linux, and I spent most on my money on graphic cards, RAM, better processors etc. I was a gamer in my free time. I was "the guy next door who's good with computers" for quite a while, but mostly focused on Windows platform which I used because it gave me what I needed at the time and because my hardware worked on Windows.

Then things changed.
Spyware, viruses, and various other forms of malware started running rampart on Windows. I got a job at a computer service... and I saw what can become of uncared-of Windows installation too much for my liking. On the other hand, I began to lose interest in gaming. Third, I replaced my old Fedora Core 2 with Dapper.

I find myself using Linux more and more every day. I'm still dual booting but that's likely to change.
I still know much more about Windows than about Linux, and I earn my living mostly thanks to my Windows knowledge. However, I found Linux, as a platform, much better logically organized. So far I found troubleshooting... clear, unlike Windows where some solutions border with black magic, and cannot actually be explained in a logical manner.

I found a lot of Linux applications easier to install and run than their Windows counterparts. Configuring IIS is a nightmare for me, but I find Apache configuration clear and much easier, for instance.

You might need to learn a bit more to use Linux, but that knowledge is scaleable. I found out that as I learn more, I can use that knowledge on other pieces of Linux software... On Windows, as you learn more, acquiring more knowledge becomes much more difficult.

That won me over.

I'd put the equasion as:
Linux = SANE technology
