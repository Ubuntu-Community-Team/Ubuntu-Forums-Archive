### Post by tehkain on 2007-06-22
Free software as a business model is very different then what we all think of when we hear 'selling' or 'product'.

I will use IBM as my first example. They find linux and the GNU toolset to be the best platform at the moment. So rather then spending large amounts of cash to write their own kernel and userspace stuff they instead decide to use these open components. Since they are using these components they share the modifications and fixes. They submit bugs, solve bugs, and commit large amounts of code. This cost money and man hours but in the end it is a fraction of what licensing or writing an OSes would cost. Also they gain the millions of man hours a year in free development from other people submitting code to open projects. 


Then we get into companies like novel. So they cannot sell this stuff but they can sell the closed solutions that go along with open source components. This is how novel does it(plus the support i will explain next). 

Then we get into redhat(and Can to a lesser extent) who sell support. Very few companies can install, modify and manage large amounts of servers so they look to a company like redhat to provide them with support. Thus all redhat has to do is manage a distro of software other people coded. Then they make money off providing a support service.


In the end what makes our model cool is that a company can do alot less preliminary work and make alot of money. Rather then writing a massive OS or app they instead only need to manage an existing one. 

In football this is kinda like - Being the coach and building a team for the future while loosing every game that year with a chance of success in the future(closed), or being brought on with a team you know well that already contains all the best players and winning the super bowl that year(open). I guess the later coach might need to get solve some problems but they are far less then if he had to build it on his own.

The idea behind 'free' software is that certain rights of the developers and users are protected.  That that software can can be modified to your personal liking without a company telling you that you aren't allowed to.

The problem with this is that it will kill the 'software as a product' model that we're used to today.  When you buy a game, that's a product.  When you buy a copy of a utility program, that's a product.  When you download the Opera web browser, that is a product.  The reason this kind of model will die is simple.. anyone is free to take and redistribute the code, if you take the software in a direction that users don't agree with someone may very well take the code and compete with you. 

So, what will replace the product model for software?  I suspect that software will become more of a gimmick, or rather, the means to sell you something else.  Ubuntu, Red Hat Enterprise Linux, SUSE Linux Enterprise Desktop, etc. These peices of software exist as a means to get you to purchase support from that company.  They're motivated to keep you purchasing their subscription services by keeping the software up to date and secure.  

However, Red Hat is a very profitable company, so don't be fooled, free software can be used to make money one way or another.  It just requires looking at the problem in a different manner.

### Post by az on 2007-06-22
Taking a step backwards, your realize that companies don't really need to own software.  

There is no value in having software sitting on your shelf.  The real value (what you pay for and what you make money from) is making your computer do stuff.  That can be managing your data, or simply running a workstation on which someone types letters and sends email.  

In the case of proprietary software, it's a two step process, where the company has to buy the software, and buy the support  (The bigger the company, the bigger the need for "production" or "mission-critical" computers which can not stop working).  When the software becomes obsolete, they have to start again.  Rinse.  Repeat.

In the FLOSS model, the software is trivial, since anyone can "stand on the shoulders of giants" and improve upon existing works, making computers do stuff is a lot simpler - you don't have to start from scratch.  The software is free, so the company only has to pay for support.  They don't own the software, everybody does.
