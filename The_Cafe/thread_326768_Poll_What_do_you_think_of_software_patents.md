### Post by neowolf on 2006-12-28
Hmm I think in the software world patents are not as neccesary as in the physical world, as in the physical world a product can be taken apart and it is easy to see what it is made of and copy it; therefore some form of protection from copying of their ideas must be afforded to inventors to make it commercially viable for them to push their product.
However, in the software world, a closed-source product cannot be taken apart, it is impossible to gain the source code from a binary product, so it is very difficult to copy. Patents protect methods and techniques to ensure they can be properly developed, but in the software world there is often only one way to do something or one way is almost indifferent from another; this means that patent infringement and abuse from the owners is very easy. For closed-source products I think that being closed-source effectively affords them all the protection they need, for the market to work properly companies have to keep developing new products in competition, software patents make the company more prone to milk cashflow out of an existing product rather than develop new ones as they know they have patented to only way to provide the service and locked competitors.
That said, I think patents protected for the use of open-source software is a good idea in the present patent situation, like the Open Inventions Network and such schemes.

### Post by Astronomer on 2006-12-28
> Patents do not protect products, they protect ideas.
Technically, you're not supposed to be able to patent an idea.  Ideas are free.  Patents are intended to apply only to processes, methods, or implementations -- expressions of an idea -- that achieve a specified goal.  And it's this specified goal that is all-important.  If the application of the same patented process can be used to achieve a different goal (a new idea), then the process is free to be used for goals other than what is specified in the patent (since ideas are free).  So, yes, it is conceivable that a spoon can be patented for the use of eating.  But then someone can get around that patent by manufacturing spoons for the intended purpose of digging in the garden, thus taking advantage of the notion that ideas are free.

Don't laugh.  This happens a lot, especially in the pharmaceutical industry.  When drugs are patented, the patents must indicate a specific goal.  For instance, if a drug company patents Drug X for the use of mitigating heart disease, then for the life of that patent, nobody can manufacture Generic X for the purpose of mitigating heard disease.  But if another company discovers that Drug X helps alleviate ingrown toenail pain, then that company is free to manufacture Generic X -- identical to patented Drug X -- solely for the purpose of alleviating ingrown toenail pain.  Since the Drug X patent didn't specify anything about ingrown toenail pain, the generic drug manufacturer is free to make and sell its generic version as long as it isn't marketed for heart disease.

What then happens is that doctors, who are aware that the drugs are identical, will prescribe the generic version -- even though it's intended for ingrown toenail pain -- for their heart patients, thereby rendering the original pharmaceutical company's patent essentially useless.

As difficult as it is for drug companies to make sensible use of patents, it's even more difficult for patents to make sense in the software industry.  Software is, essentially, an abstraction, much like mathematics.  Yet nothing in mathematics is (or should be) patentable.  (Imagine having a calculator on which you couldn't add 2 plus 2 because another calculator manufacturer owned the patent to that.)  Being able to patent software algorithms would be much like being able to patent mathematical proofs, which should never be allowed.

Additionally, software patents are overwhelmingly written so that any competing software that achieves the same goal, even if through completely different methods, is presumed to be infringing.  This would be like the Spoon patent holder suing you for using a Fork to feed yourself under the premise that *anything* used to feed yourself infringes on the Spoon patent.  In the real world, this wouldn't (normally) fly.  But in the software world, it's consistently allowed.  The result is that if someone has a patent on just one method for drawing a circle on the computer screen, then the presumption is that any other software that draws circles is infringing, even if the method used is completely different.

Another problem with patents in software is that, unlike copyright-infringement liability, patent-infringement liability extends to the user.  If you read a book that has been illegally copied, you are not liable for copyright infringement -- only the entity that illegally copied the book and supplied it to you is liable for copyright infringement.  But if you acquire a patent-infringing device or software program, you are legally guilty of patent infringement every time you use it, despite the enormous burden it would be for you to conduct a diligent patent search for every device or software product you acquire.  Is this even remotely fair?

It's a mess.  Part of the problem is that the USPTO is rather clueless when it comes to understanding and enforcing software patents.  But even if this problem were solved, I believe patents shouldn't be issued on software for the simple reason that software is much more like a book than it is like a spoon.  Software is an expression of ideas using a syntax-enforced language.  This is the kind of thing copyright is intended for, and copyright alone should be sufficient to protect software.
