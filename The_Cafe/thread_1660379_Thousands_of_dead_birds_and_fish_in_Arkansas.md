> Biologist at the US Dept. of Fish and Wildlife here. The general consensus so far is that the bird deaths are caused by poorly-timed idiots with fireworks (the bird bodies supposedly show injuries commensurate with blasts or impacts but we have yet to perform necropsies here) and that the fish kill is unrelated. Bird kills are not terribly uncommon, let's not forget. (We'll be getting some necropsies done later in the week from the Louisiana kills, I'll post what we find as I consider it a matter of public record.) Personally, I'm not sure about any overarching umbrella cause, but the fish kill in the Chesapeake was temperature shock. Overall, let's examine the link. Birds and Fish are communal animals, often migratory, and have extremely well-tuned energy partitioning mechanisms that are vulnerable to unexpected changes. After a migration, these animals are stressed, weak, and literally almost dead from starvation. Climatic variation can be the difference between life and death. With communal animals, the instinct to stay with the group overrides the need to dash off alone and hunt for food. They suck at prioritizing. They die together over a small space and in a short time. The reason we haven't found migratory bats doing this is simple-they're much better at adapting their behaviors.

In the winter, massive flocks of blackbirds of various species (red-winged being the most common by far) occupy the southern US.  Starlings quite frequently join these flocks, and in fact a number of starlings were among the dead in this incident (or was it the Louisiana one?  Probably both).  Incidentally, agricultural producers do kill large numbers of red-winged blackbirds during the winter; it's a specific exception laid out in the migratory bird protection treaty.  

When large numbers of any animal live together, large numbers can die together for a variety of reasons.  Animals are not immune to freak weather events; I'm less convinced about the mass-startling by fireworks idea, but I suppose it's possible.  This doesn't really look like poisoning or disease, though.  When large flocks of e.g. starlings get poisoned, they rain down from their roosting trees the next morning.  Disease is similarly unlikely to cause them to sprout blunt-force injuries and fall from the air.  

In any case these incidents are far from unprecedented, and while we should seek to assign a cause if possible, the best approach for now is "Don't Panic."
