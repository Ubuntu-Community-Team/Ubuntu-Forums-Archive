### Post by TheFu on 2024-02-28
> **him610 said:**
>  Since then, I got to wondering, what do others do when the internet is down.

I self-host most things, so it usually takes a few hours before I notice any issues and that the phone isn't ringing (I use VoIP for my phones).
If the outage occurs when I first hop on a computer, it might take an hour, since I usually respond to emails only in the mornings. My email server is 2 ft away. When the internet connection comes back up, emails start flowing again, but thunderbird doesn't know about the internet.

If I'm coding, it is often best for the internet NOT to be a distraction.  When I'm in "_the zone_"  8+ hrs can easily go by. It is the best high I know. Better than any drug. It is hard to explain, but I'd guess fighter pilots get a similar exhilaration when your mind is fully working to solve problems.  For many years as an enterprise architect, I lacked that great feeling that coding with full concentration required can achieve.

I also have an RSS feed reader (Nextcloud News) that pulls articles local for reading later.  That can fill another hour - all off-line.  If an article is really good and I want to have a local copy, I'll wallabag it - also self-hosted.  The Wallabag server works from all my clients, and there are interesting, but not critical, articles held inside it that I haven't fully read. Some are 10 minute reads and some are in-depth, 45+ minute reads.

When I do notice the internet is down, then I'll troubleshoot a little.  Recently, I've had some router issues, but a fresh firmware reload with ZFS as the file system appears to have completely solved the prior issues with UFS.  Router uptime:
```
# uptime
 2:09PM  up 8 days,  7:44,
```

I also have a few books loaded from the local library, so I can read or listen to those when the internet is down.

Of course, there's usually yard work or cleaning or house maintenance that need to be done. I'm excellent at procrastination. World class.

For example, I need to change a blinker bulb on the car. I'd been waiting for a nice day - heck, today is fairly comfortable outside, so I really should do it now.  

According to the maintenance manual, it requires removing the bumper, to get to the lamp and that's about 90 minutes of work. OTOH, the experts say I can wiggle my hand from the wheel-well into the same spot and change the bulb in about 5 minutes, with just a little dirt. I can wear latex gloves to mitigate the dirt. ;) Don't remember the exact technique, but I saved instructions somewhere. It will take me more time to find and re-read those instructions than it will to actually change the bulb.

Bought some new fire detectors last spring. They aren't quite the same hookups as the current ones, so I need to shut off the power to them for the re-wiring and replacement.  Probably 5-10 minutes for each location, so less than 1 hour total.  Of course, finding the correct breaker switch for each one will include lots of yelling. Had an electrician rewire some of the connections last spring. The wires had been chewed by a critter years ago, so we'd been changing batteries much more often than the hard-wired power should require.

Hopefully, everyone knows to replace your fire detectors/alarms every 10 yrs. The sensors wear out.  Would suck if your house burned down over a $20 alarm that wasn't working.
