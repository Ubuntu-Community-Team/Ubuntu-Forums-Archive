### Post by Brunellus on 2008-08-04
The flamewar will end NOW.

The Blizzard/Glider lawsuit centers on two related but separate legal issues:

**(1) Whether the Glider software infringes on Blizzard's Copyrights**.  This is a more complex question than it might appear.  Although Glider does not directly copy any Blizzard code, apparently, it requires multiple copies (instances) of WoW to be running simultaneously, which violates the terms of the WoW End-User License Agreement.  Thus Blizzard argued (successfully), the Glider developers, by making their software available, are positively encouraging the infringement of the EULA.  

**(2) Whether the Glider Developers Tortiously Interfered with Blizzard's Contracts**.  Blizzard has a contractual obligation to paying customers to deliver a service;  they argued that the presence of Glider (in contravention of Blizzard's EULA and Terms of Service) caused Blizzard to divert significant resources away from delivering their service (the game) to bot enforcement.  That's a compensible harm in American law. 

The whole affair hinges on the remedy.  If an individual has harmed a legally-protected interest that belongs to you, the law gives you the choice of either making the other person compensate you monetarily for the harm done (these are money damages) or compelling the person to stop whatever it is they are doing that keeps harming you (an injunction).  Blizzard is seeking a permanent injunction.
