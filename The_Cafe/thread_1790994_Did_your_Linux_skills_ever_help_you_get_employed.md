### Post by Dangertux on 2011-06-26
I think any type of "unique" knowledge you may possess is going to set you apart in an employers eyes. However, the relevance to the actual job description will greatly determine the weight of how far it will get you.

In my case , I need to have decent Linux skills to do my job. It didn't really help me get employment as in, get me an instant promotion, it was more like a person applying to be a truck driver will need a CDL.

As far as certifications go, more and more companies are shying away from what you have on paper versus what you can actually do. Mostly due to the fact that obtaining a "high end" cert has become relatively easy due to some companies "pushing" their employees through the certification process. Don't get me wrong, get all the certs you can, and they may help you score an interview. However, when push comes to shove, if you really want that job in the IT world, don't sit on your cert be prepared to throw down and show what you got.

Edit : Also in my personal opinion since I've come to the point in my career where my opinion actually matters on some hiring decision, I am more interested in a candidate that is eager to learn, and can really think creatively as opposed to a candidate who walks in with an "I love me book" full of certifications. Too much book learning tends to stifle creative thinking, it also tends to make people think they know best, even though that might not be the case.

### Post by rg4w on 2011-06-26
I'm an independent consultant and developer, so my own experience may not directly apply to conventional full-time jobs.

That said, I've found my Linux skills benefiting the work I do on every platform.

Linux is very much like Unix, so it's taught me a lot I can directly use on OS X, and since the "pro" versions of Windows now include a Unix-like shell it's very helpful there as well.  Even on the "Home" editions of Windows where we have only DOS, using Linux has made me more comfortable writing batch scripts and working with the command line in general.

Participating in this community has also raised my awareness about security, which has had a positive influence on my work with other OSes.

And with the ubiquity of client-server/web apps, since Linux runs so many web servers it's greatly increased my skills in that area - a very valuable commodity these days.

It's also helped my vendor relations:  as soon as I tell them I'm using Linux they understand that I probably know what I'm doing and route me to their more experienced representatives. :)

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-06-26
It hasn't helped me get a job; however, it has helped me keep one. We have had layoffs over the past few years and I have survived them. I have a broader knowledge than other people I compete with for continued employment.

It isn't the Linux desktop use that has helped; but rather, the troubleshooting skills I learned from some of the early distros. They were a lot harder to get working than what we have today. The experience has enabled me to explore other things as well; because, I know if I break something I can fix it. Learning things like how to set up DNS, LDAP, virtualization, LAMP stacks, and servers has been a significant benefit. One of the benefits of FOSS is that I can try new things without spending money on software I will never use again or software that doesn't do what I need it to do.

Edit: I should mention that the company where I work uses a lot of Linux computers. The management software for scheduling, accounting, and such do run on proprietary software on a Microsoft OS; however, the web servers, mail servers, DNS, LDAP, software development, software configuration management, and most of our products run on Linux.
