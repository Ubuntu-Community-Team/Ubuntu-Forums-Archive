### Post by Mizzou_Engineer on 2008-06-15
The definition of old to me mostly depends on the availability of suitable replacement and upgrade parts for the machine rather than age. A computer is new as long as you can add that RAM or new card you need to get your job done or fix a broken part. A computer is old when you have to hunt around for used/obscure parts for it to do your work or if you have to jerry-rig a solution to get around the lack of some needed but unavailable more-modern feature. 

The reason that I do not consider age or power as a good indicator of being outdated (that's what "old" in this context really means, anyway) as a several-year-old machine can be more powerful than one coming off the line today. Compare the power of a 1 GHz PIII or Athlon to that of the brand-spanking-new Intel Atom or VIA C7. The 8-year-old platform is at least as fast as the brand-new one. But yet people still buy Atoms and C7s for HTPCs and little servers. I guess I consider a computer outdated when it is either broken with no replacement parts/extremely expensive used replacement parts or I honestly have no use for it. I'd probably consider the components available in a 10-year-old computer to be about at the edge of what I could make use of.

### Post by Trenchbroom on 2008-06-16
What is "old" now?  I've given this thought a few times lately and it's a pretty good puzzle.  To me it all comes down to "killer apps"...

Thinking back over my experiences with computers (from C64 to present) what drove my need for new computers were games--bigger, flashier, better(?) games.  I would upgrade parts each year and the whole system within 3 years just to get my gaming fix.

Once you get off of the game treadmill your need to upgrade stretches much farther between machines.  However, something curious has happened in the 21st century--the hardware got so fast that the other "killer apps" that drove people to upgrade their hardware have been vanquished.  Anyone here remember having to upgrade their three year old computer to use a later version of a word processor, spreadsheet, or tax program?  Sounds kinda funny, but 15 years ago it was commonplace.

No one has to upgrade their computers to use the latest version of their favorite office suite, paint program, play DVDs, rip songs from CDs, or watch movies over the internet anymore.  In fact, any computer built since 2000 can likely handle these typical tasks quite well.  It's like once computing speeds got above 1 ghz the only need for faster computing was for games.

In fact the only new "killer app" I see on the horizon is HD video, be it in Blu-Ray format or streaming.  Until then I'm quite content to be off of the upgrade treadmill; happily continuing to use an older machine for all of my computing needs.
