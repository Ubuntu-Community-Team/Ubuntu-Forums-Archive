### Post by 3Miro on 2011-07-22
Things are not simple. There are many models of i7 and i5 and you should look at the individual models. In general, i7 comes with 4+4 cores, while i5 comes with 2+2 (2 real 2 fake form Hyper-Threading). In contract, Phenom X6 has 6 real cores.

Can your video editor take advantage of all cores? If so, then the Phenom would probably beat most of the i7 model (not all, some of the highest end i7 would beat X6). If your editor doesn't take advantage of the extra cores, then you need to look at cache and single-threaded performance. Phenom x6 will probably fall short of i5 (by little) and i7 will be only marginally better.

You should also look at GPU acceleration for video editing.

If we consider the cost of the machine, for the same amount of money, Phenom x6 will beat all Intel CPUs. That is, $200 Phenom x6 will beat any $200 Intel (only the low end Intel processors sell for $200). There are more threads about the subject.

For Mac: the advantage of Mac is that it comes with good software for video editing. For the very simple video editing, the OS doesn't matter. For something more "professional" Mac probably comes with the best software. However, the largest movie studios use Linux (Avatar for example was made on Linux). The issue is that the high-end editing software is very expensive and overall hard to find.

### Post by akand074 on 2011-07-23
As many people have said. There are tonnes of different models. I'd personally get an X58 chip i7. i.e the i7-9xx series or a second generation i7. But again, for desktops: 

- The i5-6xx series is a dual core hyper threaded with turbo boost
- The i5-7xx series is a native quad core (without hyperthreading) and with turbo boost.
- The i7-8xx series is a native quad core with hyper threading and with turbo boost with same chipset as i5-7xx series
- The i7-9xx series is a native quad core (or six core) with hyper threading and turbo boost on X58 chipset (uses intel QPI connection between controllers and devices)
-  2nd gen i5 is like i5-7xx but with new technologies and some higher clock speeds available (unlocked ones too)
-  2nd gen i7 is like i7-8xx but with faster connection than even most of the i7-9xx series and new technologies (unlocked ones too) 

Depends on the one you want. If you're encoding, more threads = faster (most encoding software support multi threads now) and higher clock speed = faster. Really all that matters to be honest. Rest of the technologies are better for overall performance for overall common usage. Not a specific task like encoding. Check prices of each and see which suits your needs/budget best.
