### Post by handy on 2011-07-06
> **Triblaze said:**
> I fail at keeping up to date and informed on this stuff, what is haiku and why should I care?

Wikipedia says it's some open source OS that's been in development for quite some time, what makes it special?

R1, is to be an open-source rebuild of BeOS, with some enhancements. After that they will then move into more radical development.

Haiku is built for the desktop & due to this it is incredibly efficient & speedy. BeOS had the reputation of being absolutely brilliant when working with multimedia.

The Haiku filesystem is absolutely brilliant, it has the ability to build very useful databases using Filetypes, Attributes, Index and Queries, as can be seen in this workshop:

[http://www.haiku-os.org/docs/userguide/en/workshop-filetypes+attributes.html](http://www.haiku-os.org/docs/userguide/en/workshop-filetypes+attributes.html)

I personally expect to be using a Haiku system as my multimedia machine, which will benefit from the use of the strengths of the filesystem with the straightforward GUI & the inherent speed of the Haiku system in general.

Here is a recent review of R1 Alpha 3:

[http://www.haiku-os.org/community/forum/my_review_haiku_alpha_3](http://www.haiku-os.org/community/forum/my_review_haiku_alpha_3) 

As far as Haiku becoming my main system is concerned time will tell on that one. As it develops over the next 5 years it is possible that it will give me all I need & I would leave Arch. 

I do find it hard to imagine leaving Arch for any reason though. :)

### Post by handy on 2011-07-06
> **Spr0k3t said:**
> I believe Haiku will forever remain in the unreleased stated, or released without ever being finished.  There were several other operating systems back in the 90s which touted the exact same arguments and died a slow death or devoured whole by other larger counterparts.

Haiku has some corporate sponsors & also users donate funds. This has enabled Haiku to pay for coding which keeps the project moving.

I'd be astounded if Haiku died. I think it has got past the most vulnerable stage as it can now present a usable system with (still non-encrypted) wireless support, its browser is now useful, the multimedia support is already brilliant. 

Such a system gathers more interest for Haiku & attracts more coders & users like myself who have been following the Haiku development for years, are heartened each time an official release (such as the recent alpha 3) is made available & we can see just how much it has improved since the last one.

Package management is being worked on, encrypted wireless & a driver solution are prime candidates for coding now, It is possible that a tool will be made that makes it much easier for Haiku to benefit from drivers written for Linux.

Anyway, as far as I'm concerned the future is looking pretty rosy for Haiku.
