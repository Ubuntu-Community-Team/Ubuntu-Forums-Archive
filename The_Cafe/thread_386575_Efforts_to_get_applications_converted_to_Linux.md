Yes, it all sounds like a big conspiracy theory, blah blah blah, but it's not THAT far from the truth, and although Linux does have problems for some developers - you'll just have to accept that until you put your money where your mouth is, the situation is not going to change. Donate to Linux devs so they can spend more time improving something, or stop buying propietary apps so that companies are FORCED into extending their target audience to other people than Windows users.

And there are a world of others that the computing public uses on a daily basis to run their businesses, etc. ...Companies like Intuit, Sage and so on won't consider porting their apps to Linux until they think the cost of re-writing code and marketing their Linux version will be less than their potential revenue from the sales of Linux software. We can send all the letters and E-mail messages we want to them, but if they don't think it'll make them money, they'll reply with a form letter thanking us for our interest and assuring us that they'll do so when it's economically feasible for them to do it.

In other words, it's up to these companies to decide to do it. It's not up to us to "pressure" them into it.

> **wpshooter said:**
> ... And please don't suggest to me that these businesses switch to some currently existing Linux alternative.  **IT JUST AIN'T GOING TO HAPPEN**.

These businesses already have people trained on these existing M/S compatible packages and they are NOT about to spend the funds to re-train these people on some substitute/alternative programs (that do not have a tried and true history of business performance)....Businesses are absurdly conservative about their practices. If some company with a higher stock price isn't doing it, they're not going to either. So, if they want to keep throwing away piles of money at these software vendors because they haven't bothered to look at the alternatives, or actually consider whether re-training would cost less than the contracts they have with commercial software vendors, then that's their problem.

Unfortunately, it ends up costing all of us, because they price of the software they use is passed on to their customers in the price of the products they sell us. I've seen organizations that replace their computers every two years, just because it's policy. Every time they do that, they re-buy all of their software. If there's a new version of the software out, they pay to re-train their staff. This is piles and piles of money that consumers end up footing the bill for.

The real problem is IT staff that think their job is to do whatever their company's software vendors tell them to do. They should be looking into alternatives, and telling their bosses what will work better and cost less. Most of them aren't.
