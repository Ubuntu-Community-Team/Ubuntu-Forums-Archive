### Post by Tundro Walker on 2007-07-03
> Those willing to give up essential liberties for temporary security deserve neither liberty nor security.

- Benjamin FranklinSome think we fought for independance so we could have the right to blow our hands off on the 4th of July with firecrackers.  As true as that may be, there were a few other reasons...  Here's the quickie version of why America (US) fought for independence from England.

In a nutshell, we were tired of being treated like a red-headed step-child, forced to do a lot of work with little compensation (since it was taxed away) and little say in how England ran (since they wouldn't let us represent ourselves in English government).

The timeline goes a bit like this...

1) "Unwanted" folks came over on colony ships, mostly pilgrims, so they could practice their rather austere religion (compared to other religions) in peace.

2) Colonies prosper, create goods & farm tobacco that were popular in England.  Something about some Salem Witch trials...lots of folks die being crushed by rocks and drowned as a way to prove they're not witches (England reads the newspaper, and rejoices that the witch-hunt wackos are safely contained in a colony, far away from all the other sane people in the world.)

3) England continues buying goods from a growing colony, dreams up some taxes to re-coup the cost of the goods being shipped, but denies the colony representation in government.  (Basically saying "we'll tax you as part of England, but you don't get to vote or count as part of England", to which the colonists say...)

4) "No taxation w/o representation."  Colonists dress as Indians, and throw a tea party in Boston harbor.   England is not amused.  (EDIT: Tea was one of the few goods shipped into the colonies from England that couldn't be produced locally.  England, knowing this, decided to start taxing the colonies a "tea tax" as a means to generate more revenue from the colonies.  The colonists, annoyed by the over-milkage of taxation, decided to make a statement about the "tea tax" by dumping the latest shipment of tea into the harbor.)

5) England pens some "rules" and "restrictions" that the colony must follow, basically trying to keep it in line while still not giving it representation.  (If you want an idea of what this was like, watch the movie "Pleasantville".  The "black & white" folks pen up some rules for the "colored" folks to follow in order to protect the peace.  The rules are basically written shackles, denying them essential liberties and freedoms...)

6) Folks got more ticked.  Local Boston Elks Lodge turns into a small, underground, revolutionary headquarters.

7) Declaration of Independence signed, sealed and delivered.  The names on the document basically act as a who's who of mutiny.  IE: the folks signing it know they're basically signing a very ballsy death warrant for themselves if their revolution doesn't succeed.  John Hancock signs his name in huge letters as a big f.u. to England, thus he popularizes the term "put your Hancock here" when some folks ask you to sign a document.

7) England sent over some troops to keep things in line.  Something about Benjamin Franklin and a kite and a lightning storm.  Not sure...

8) Gunfire exchanged...all hell breaks loose.  (Gunfire was exchanged when some revolutionaries were trying to access a supply depot that the English had blockaded.  They wouldn't back down, so shots got fired.  It's still debated who fired first.)

9) More and more English troops come over.  Americans thoroughly piss off English generals by using guerilla skirmish tactics instead of "fighting like true gentlemen".  English troops still insist on lining up and marching to deaths while being shot at from bushes.  To this date, a theory that the "lone gunman" Kennedy assassin was just a Revolutionary in the wrong place at the wrong time is much debated...  

One of the big thorns in the English's side was the Kentucky Long Rifle.  The typical musket lost any amount of accuracy beyond 75-100 yards.  The Kentucky Long Rifle, however, forged in the wilds to take down game at long distances, had a much longer range and better accuracy.  This made it a tide-turning weapon in many battles during the Revolutionary war.  Typical tactics had patriot troops lying in wait to ambush English soldiers en-route to a battle.  Kentucky Riflemen would snipe the officers, destroying the command structure, then other patriots concealed in the bushes & trees by the road-side would launch a volley of musket fire into the English troops.  After initial gun-fire, armed combat ensued, with bayonet, hatchet, knife, etc, and it got down-right brutal.  It should be noted that sniping an officer was seen as an outrage, and went against all codes of "gentlemanly" fighting conduct at that time, which just enforced the English's idea that the colonials were just "savages".

10) Several English victories, but English troops do a thoroughly good job of pissing off any loyalists they have in the colonies by acting like an occupying force of a foreign nation, instead of acting like troops who are here to protect the loyalist populace from terrorist revolutionaries.  Rumours of English troops killing the women and children of revolutionaries doesn't exactly win them support.

11) A certain English commander makes a very large blunder, and turns what should have been an overall English victory in the colonies to a turning point in the war.

12) Colonies boot English out after more war, suffering and scandal.  English ticked that they're denied access to a huge chunk of land, and Americans proceed to breed like rabbits, populating the land.

13) Lots of lawyers get together in a court house on a couple hot, sweaty summer days, and hammer out the United States Constitution.  First 10 articles are called the "Bill of Rights" which includes such fav's as...[LIST]
[*]Freedom of Speech, Press, Religion, Peaceable Assembly, and petitioning of government for hearing out of grievances (the colonials explicitly wrote this in, because before the Declaration of Independence was penned, an article of grievance was drafted and sent to the English government, in the hopes that they're complaints on taxation and representation would be addressed in fair manner.  To this, the English government pretty much told the colonials to "get stuffed", and that, as colonials, they were pretty much second-class citizens, and not allowed to complain.)
[*]Right to bear arms (which NRA later interprets to mean assault rifles, rocket launchers, and other things that wouldn't even be considered "sporting" if you were hunting deer.  However, governments should fear their people, not vice-versa, so most feel the common person owning an assault rifle is only fair if the governmental military can own one, too.)
[*]No unlawful search, seizure, or quartering of troops in a person's house without owner's consent (explicitly written in, since the English troops had a nasty habit of shacking up in a person's home, whether welcome or not, and the person was responsible for feeding and bedding the soldiers...or else...)
[*]Right to speedy & fair trial, shall be treated as guilty until proven innocent (oh, wait, that's "innocent until proven guilty"...my how my experience with the police has jaded me), and no cruel or unusual punishment while imprisoned (apparently jail rape doesn't fall into this category, for it seems to be an issue that prisons do little about)[/LIST]Later additions to the constitution make it a breeding ground of lawsuits and legal woes.  Popular para-phrase "of the lawyers, by the lawyers, for the lawyers" strikes a few laughs at parties, except from folks being sued at the time...

14) Later...  Louisianna purchase (which got a huge chunk of land).  Mexican American war (which brought Texas and some other land into play).  "Thanking" the Amer-Indians (who helped during the revolutionary war) off into reservations, so the rest of the Amer-Indian land could be taken.  Purchase of Alaska from Soviet Union.  Annexation of Iraq as 52nd state (oh, wait...Bush didn't want us Americans to publicize that yet...d'oh!)

Of course, during this time, the French & Spanish are trying their own colonies in Canada and Mexico...and getting much the same result as America, which is why Canada and Mexico are both called "Canada" & "Mexico" today, instead of "France 2" and "Spain 2".

EDIT: It's noteworthy to say that Spain, France and England were the super-powers of their time during 1400-1700's.  They were constantly at war, and had large empires to fund, thus creating the colonies in the "New World" to exploit resources and make a profit for their war-chest.  However, by doing a thoroughly excellent job of pissing off their money-makers (their colonies) by treating them as second-class citizens and causing revolts, they lost their money-makers, and as a result, their territories dwindled.  The moral of the story is, don't treat those under you as inferiors (be it a parent with their kids, a manager with their employees, or a country with its colonies).  It's not nice, and, it may end up costing you in the long run.

I'm sure history will repeat itself at some time.  Perhaps when we have a moon colony or a Mars colony, the colony will be treated like a red-headed step-child, and will have to revolt.

But enough about that...let's go blow some **** up with some fireworks!

Actually, if you think about it, we American's didn't really hate England (England, Great Britain, UK...take your pick).  There's a reason we named the original states "New England" and still call them by that name today.  It's because, like most folks, our fore-fathers loved their homeland.  That's why it struck them so deeply to not get a fair shake during 1700's.

Look at it this way, you've got a kid who's moved out of the house, because he didn't quite like living under a few of the rules in your house.  But, they still love you...you're their parents, afterall.  But, after moving out, you insist on still treating them like a child, and tell them that even though they're living on their own, in a different place that requires a different way of living, they have to live by your standards...they still have to be home by 10pm, they can't drink or smoke or have sex...  Plus, they have to give you half their paycheck every month (!).  

There comes a time when the kid gets tired of being treated like a kid, and says "hey, I don't live with you anymore...if you're not going to be a positive influence in my life and treat me like an adult, and instead be a mooch that doesn't recognize my contribution to the family, then I don't need you."
