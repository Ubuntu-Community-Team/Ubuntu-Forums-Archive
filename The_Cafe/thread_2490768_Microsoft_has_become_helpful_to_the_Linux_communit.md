### Post by TheFu on 2023-09-15
It is a trap.  

Don't get pulled in by just a few nice things. Nokia did.  There are thousands of little companies who were treated unfairly by MSFT, under Satya too.

Beware of github too, which is owned by MSFT.  For F/LOSS projects, it doesn't matter too much, but if your company is paying for them to hold your proprietary code, who says they aren't looking at it and will use that information for their own needs?

MSFT additions to F/LOSS are for their own good.  This is common for all companies.  They've built something that is dependent on the code and when they need it to work correctly or better, then the company programmers will help out.  That's no different than any other company.  I don't fault companies for looking out for themselves.  

When I had a corporate job writing proprietary code, I'd do things that helped our company first. Helping the clients usually aligned with that, but not always.  For example, most of our US customers begged us for a license management system to ensure they never violated any license agreements.  However, it was clear that many of our clients, mostly in Asia, were less than happy about the new license server requirement.  Guess you can't make everyone happy.  I included the user guides for the license server and all the programs that allowed full visibility into their license use.  At one company, they had bought 500 client licenses (we sold licenses for the servers and the clients in separate numbers).   When I was there doing some other work, I ran a few commands and saw they had only 5 unique clients accessing the system in their 2nd year of implementation.  I could have told them about it, but didn't. They had the manuals. They had the software.  Showing them how to use it wasn't my role and they never asked.  It was a huge multi-national company with a relatively good reputation for quality products.  At the time, nearly everyone in the 1st world would have had at least 1 of their products at home.  I had about 5 and that was before they were a customer of our software. That happens in every company, especially if they are for-profit.  Maximizing profit, while getting the client what they need, within their approved budget is the goal.  Corporations that actually make products care more about getting things done than the actual costs involved, with certain limits.  MSFT is for-profit. There's no mistaking that.  I know they withhold information and specifically design products to force other products that only they sell to be added.  It is like kudzu or bamboo. Once you have a little MSFT in your business, it keeps growing and growing until you can't remove it without a blow torch.  

Heck, even Canonical makes hooking up Ubuntu desktops to MS-AD trivial rather than having their own LDAP implementation that is trivial to hook into. I find that a bit embarrassing.

BTW, "embrace" is a trigger word when used with MSFT in the same sentence.
"Embrace, extend, and extinguish" ... where have we heard that before?  [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embrace%2C_extend%2C_and_extinguish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embrace%2C_extend%2C_and_extinguish)   [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_v._Microsoft_Corp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_v._Microsoft_Corp).

It's a trap.  We've learned this over many decades, even when they are smiling and pretending to be helpful, it is still a trap.
