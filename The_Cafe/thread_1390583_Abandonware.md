### Post by isaacj87 on 2010-01-25
Hello all,

Recently, I've decided to take trip down memory lane and look up some old games I used to play/wanted to play. Given that WINE is a few steps behind, sometimes one doesn't have the opportunity to play the latest and greatest games. So, just because a game doesn't have state-of-the-art graphics doesn't mean it isn't a great game. 

But I've started to think about something. Here I am wanting to play an old game that came out in 1998, Heart of Darkness to be exact, and I can't find it anywhere. The company that published the game, Interplay, [doesn't seem to be a publisher anymore]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplay_Entertainment#Re-emergence") and is in limbo. Furthermore, they don't sell the game anymore. (A quick trip to Interplay's website shows no online store). The developer of the game, Amazing Studio, is seemingly defunct. So, what to do? 

Now, I understand, intellectual property doesn't disappear into thin air. When a company acquires another, all IP goes with it. So, technically, not forgetting about copyright laws, Heart of Darkness still belongs to someone. Plus, I could obtain a copy of the game via Ebay or Amazon in used condition. But this game is almost 12 years old and probably doesn't make any money as used games rarely generate income to the company (am I wrong?). How am I to justify spending money on something that seemingly has no value?

What, as a consumer, should I do? According to copyright law, [Abandonware]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abandonware") is illegal. I understand and respect this. But what if the game isn't still in production? What is the forums take on Abandonware? And do you feel, like me, that in the end, the consumer is snubbed?

### Post by Chronon on 2010-01-26
> **Starlight said:**
> I don't know about the legal aspects, but it seems to me that there's nothing morally wrong with torrenting something that the owners don't sell anymore, since they aren't making any money from it anyway.

I agree, but the same thing can be said about used software that's being sold now.  If I sell a game that I legitimately bought to a friend, the copyright holders/publishers get nothing from this transaction.  

The difference seems to lie in the ethics of producing new copies of something.  Creating and distributing copies of something that someone sells cuts into their profits by shifting the supply curve.  With abandonware, whoever owns the rights to the game is not leveraging demand against supply to make a profit so it isn't apparent that you are depriving someone of livelihood.  It isn't clear that the owners won't attempt to leverage that in the future, however, so I consider it a bit of a gray area.

It does appear to me that copyrights held by companies that cease to exist lapse into the public domain and in this case it seems that one has free reign to distribute and copy the material.  I'm not sure how to verify that this is the case, however.  Also, I am not a lawyer!
