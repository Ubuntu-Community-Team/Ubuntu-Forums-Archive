### Post by JDShu on 2012-01-04
It's never too early (or too late!) to learn a skill. There are plenty of young people who have learned how to program, you are not unique there. Personally, I'm pretty envious of them :)

I understand being impatient about these things, so my advice is to think of a project to do, for example a computer game, and try to write it with what you know. If there is something you don't know, then learn it. Having a goal in mind will give you the motivation to learn the basics, or enough of the basics, to achieving that goal.

A final bit of advice: One very important skill for programmers is the ability to communicate. Learn to write good English. Capitalize letters at the beginning of sentences, and learn to use apostrophe's properly. It is very difficult to read something that doesn't conform to the current standards.

### Post by V for Vincent on 2012-01-04
First of all, good idea. There's no required age for learning to program and it's a very, very useful skill. So go ahead and learn it now that you still have a lot of spare time on your hands. Second, don't get distracted from your goal. Sooner or later, people are going to start telling you to learn C/C++/Perl/Ruby/Java/Haskell/... First, make sure you understand that it's your own ability that determines what you can build, not your toolkit. Also, start small. Focus on building simple, but solid applications at first. Don't start on dozens of projects without ever finishing one.

Once you've got the main syntax down, start browsing some python projects on github/launchpad/sourceforge/... to learn more. Finally, once you've seen a few real projects, read a few books on proper application design.

Once you've taken care of all of that, you'll be well on your way to programming whatever you want.

### Post by undecim on 2012-01-04
I started programming when I was 11.

A lot of people start learning to program young, and if you enjoy doing it, then it's a great hobby that can turn into an excellent career. It's great to see that you can teach yourself (that alone is an incredible and rewarding skill to have!), and that you're doing your best to understand every part of it.

But don't worry if you don't understand some things; You're 13, and your mind is still developing. (And it will keep doing so until you're around your mid 20's) If you're learning anything at all and you're enjoying doing it, that's all that really matters. Clearly you enjoy it enough to learn it on your own, so you're off to a great start.

I think the most important thing to have when you're learning to program is something to apply it to. When I was learning, I had a program called Visual Pinball, which would allow you to create new pinball tables with Visual BASIC code. My brother started writing Lua scripts for Hedgewars when he was 10, and has started learning pascal now at age 11. He plans to start adding code to Hedgewars itself soon.

So maybe find yourself a game you enjoy that can be scripted in Python.

If you learn to program in one language, programming in another is easy; It's just different syntax. More important than the language itself are the concepts and analytical skills that you develop.

Python is an excellent language to start with. After you've learned that really well, try learning C. A lot of things that are done automatically in Python are left as the programmers' job in C, so you get to learn a lot more (assuming you have a good teacher or learning resource).

### Post by DangerOnTheRanger on 2012-01-04
I say the earlier the better - I started with SmallBasic (the FOSS SmallBasic, not MS' SmallBasic) back when I was around 8, for example. All I can say is, hang in there - with patience, you'll get the hang of software engineering soon enough. :)

One of the best ways to make programming fun that I know of is to either find an open-source project you find interesting or exciting, and contribute to that project; or find some problem domain (that interests you, of course) for which no open-source application currently exists, and start your own open-source project which caters to that problem domain. You'll be making a big contribution to the open-source community, and you'll learn a lot about managing an actual open-source project along the way.

Starting and maintaining your own FOSS project is a pretty fun thing to do - running your own FOSS project teaches you a lot about lots of different things: The best practices for a non-trivial software project, where to host code, how to politely receive people upset at you for bugs, and so on.

### Post by effenberg0x0 on 2012-01-05
@OP

My advice to you would be: Try and learn the really hard stuff while your brain is young, you have no family to support, time is not an issue, etc. I bet a guy like you can do school stuff with an arm tied behind your back and closed eyes, right? Use your time to learn the stuff you like right now. As you grow older and jump into partying, then into a relationship, a succession of jobs, start your own family, have to pay the bills, etc, it's a lot harder to learn anything.

I learned to use a PC when I was your age. It was not like things are today. Few people had PCs at home. There was no Internet (we exchanged 5.25 disks, sometimes even via postal service and with known and unknown guys - eventually they would rob your stuff). We had to buy PC magazines to get some software, learn from the few books we could find. Then BBS, 386 and Internet started to get popular and things gradually became easier. But my generation didn't get to have all the learning resources that are commonly available now.

Don't worry about your age. You can learn anything. If you have fun with learning, don't stop and don't loose this flame. Learn the popular stuff, like Python, Perl and Ruby, but also try some C/C++ and even Pascal and ASM. As you master a programming language, other ones come easier. Hack everything you own, read Fravia+ and find +Orc. There are no limits.

It's not just about PCs and programming: Read whatever you can get your hands on, from your local literature to classics, mathematics, physics, businesses, etc. Try other languages, cultures, musical instruments, sports, etc. You don't have to be the best at each of these things. Just have some contact with as much information as you can. Put on a backpack and travel the world, etc. As weird as it seems, having a rich general culture will leverage opportunities in your life, and also make you a better IT professional if that's the path you choose. 

Soon you'll be an adult. Responsibilities change. It's a whole new game where time is worth more than money. Other adults, struggling to find time to learn anything that can differentiate them in the market (a programming language, a second language, etc) will ask themselves how the hell did you get to know so much about everything. Your professional development will be faster and you'll have access to much better business opportunities, maybe know enough to start your own business while very young. And having success in professional life generally leads to having more money and time for yourself and your family - which is what we, the adults, struggle with :)
