### Post by DoctorMO on 2007-04-23
The drive to understand to know something is a reward in and of it's self.

But to keep your mind focused you need to want the end result, when I do python training I tell students to pick a small project or I give them one; something which they can aim for and want to get working. along the way they pick up working knowledge.

Attention spans can either be a sign of overloading (doing too much) not getting enough sleep, water or too much sugar/caffeine as well as depression, lack of social contact or stress. So it's not always something that you can fix with the wave of a wand.

On the other side of the coin, it's very important to not learn to not learn.

### Post by BoneKracker on 2007-04-23
One of the things that helps me us maintaining an awareness of the hierarchical nature of computer and information systems architecture.

The little nit-noid things, seemingly innefectual, that take so much time to get so little actually done -- these are often the building blocks that are reused over and over again to build systems.

For example, sit down and write a program in assembler to add two eight-digit base-10 numbers together.  If you aren't building on similar previous experience, this may take hours.  And yet, you could just click on your calculator app or pull one out of a drawer.  But the reason the calculator app works, and the reason your calculator works, is because somebody wrote that code.

So, your python script, if it runs every time some user triggers some function on a website, can be quite powerful and quite valuable.  Much of the value of computers centers around the concept of reuse.  It's easy to forget that when we are writing code as a learning exercise.

Almost any "system" is really a system of systems.  As technology people, we grab onto some level within that hierarchy and master some set of tasks.  Some people design EEPROMs.  Some write assembler.  Some write C.  Some design computers.  Some write business apps in high-level languages.  Some are "inter-enterprise supply chain integration architects".  Each layer is part of the foundation for layers above.  And each layer build on those below.

But the work of each of these people is essentially the same.  They are building (or operating) systems.  While the enterprise integration architect is designing a $100-million system, the EEPROM engineer is designing a $10 part that will be used in 10 million machines.

I guess my point is that you have to learn to value the seemingly mundane or insignificant tasks.  Or, rather, you need to learn to _evalute_ them, so you put your energy into those that are reusable building blocks (or otherwise important) and not into those that are truly unimportant.

Part of that is recognizing that digesting, learning, and synthesizing knowledge that you will reuse later (for the rest of your career or even your life) may be the single-most important case of reuse for you.
