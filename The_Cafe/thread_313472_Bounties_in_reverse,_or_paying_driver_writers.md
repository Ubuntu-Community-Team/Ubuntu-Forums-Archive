### Post by michaeljt on 2006-12-06
Canonical currently runs a bounty system, whereby they provide a task and a fee for someone who will take on that task.  It would be nice to also have the same system in reverse: a user wants an improvement in Ubuntu which is beyond what Canonical could normally provide (e.g. a driver for a piece of hardware would be a particularly likely one).  It could work as follows.  If any Canonical employees are reading this, I would be particularly interested in their views.

1) The user submits the task and say how much they would be willing to pay, or if someone else has already put out the task, they "join" it and put in their own contribution.
2) Canonical monitors new submissions, and if a new one is similar to an old one, the submitter is given the option of "joining" the old one instead.  If they don't want to, the two tasks may still be able to be grouped, so that they can later be carried out together.
3) Once the contributions on offer have reached a certain threshhold (say Eur 1000), Canonical can look at what it would take to implement the task - getting an offer from a suitably qualified person (say a Gutenprint developer for a printer driver, a KDE developer for a KDE improvement, a kernel networking developer for a networking driver), if necessary contacting hardware companies for specs (if they are willing to publish them) and possible contributions (including hardware for testing).
4) Once the contributions on offer have reached the threshhold necessary for implementing the task, Canonical collects the money and commissions the work, possibly including backporting to the current Ubuntu release.  All source code GPL'ed, LGPL'ed or BSD'ed of course.

This could also provide an additional source of income for Canonical, which would be an additional guarantee that they can keep on improving Ubuntu, and thus in everyone's interests.  Of course, it needn't necessarily be Canonical organising this, but it needs to be someone trusted by the people offering their contributions, and with the popularity of Ubuntu, Canonical is well placed to do it effectively.  And 500 contributions of twenty euros each should be enough to pay for a driver.  If people are willing to pay for the Turboprint printer drivers just to get a single driver, they might be willing to contribute in this way too.  And this way it would end up free too.
