I admit that I quite like Windows 7. I believe it is the best Windows version so far. As much as I disliked Vista, as much I have to take off my hat and say that W7 is well done. However, I also have a laptop with Ubuntu and another desktop with Arch, and I am also happy with them. 

Would Ubuntu, Fedora, SuSE do just fine for most people? Of course. If they come pre-installed and if you didn't have to re-install every six or so months. Then you have Ubuntu with its brown theme ... if I get someone to try the live CD, which is hard enough, they usually get turned off right there. Sure, it can be changed, but first impressions matter. It's been discussed many times, but Cannonical sees no need for any changes or even offering different options at install. So much lost potential!

Linux stands in its own way. A million of distros that confuse end users, various packaging systems (no universal standard), everyone cooks their own little soup, partly differing directory structures, and so on. Windows succeeds not because it has been so fantastic, but because it actually follows its own weird standards (more or less, more so now than a decade ago). Sure, Linux offers more "freedom" (most users don't care, to them a computer is like a toaster and they don't want their toaster to be part of the resistance!) and choice, but choice is a double edged sword.

So, in short, the "enemy" here is "within". It's not Windows. Linux is much like a pile of clay shards saying, "Hey, I'm a vase!", when Windows is actually a whole, undivided flower pot of magnificence. For us geeks, the choice is great. If we don't like something, we modify it or find a distro that does what we want. For the end user, this is only confusing.

This is why I believe that Linux succeeds or fails on its own, completely independently of what and how Windows does.
