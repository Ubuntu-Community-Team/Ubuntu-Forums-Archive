### Post by saulgoode on 2007-02-16
This is not precisely about DRM but it is about "Trusted Computing". 

At a recent LUG meeting, a member was discussing a job he was assigned to update a company's servers to Windows Server 2003. One of the things that he encountered was that, after performing the update, a Vista machine which was connected to the network would no longer boot, even after subsequently being disconnected from the network.

I guess the idea is that since the Vista machine could no longer obtain a proper signature from the network server, it must presume that a "takeover" had occurred or that the machine was "stolen" from the network. The only recourse was to reinstall Vista on that machine (fortunately, not much data had accumulated on it yet).

Perhaps there were ways that the administrators could have avoided the problem; but they were working during off-hours and were not familiar with Vista's intricacies (they were updating the server, not the workstations). 

I am not completely against Trusted Computing; it does have its applications.. I am just skeptical about relying too heavily upon it (and not educating your users on its potential for disaster) -- something which Microsoft seems to be doing in a big way.
