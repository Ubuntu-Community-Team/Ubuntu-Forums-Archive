### Post by Brunellus on 2006-08-28
> **wordsmythe said:**
> Brunellus, how is that Beevor book?  I picked it up a couple years ago but never got around to reading it.
It's trenchant, incisive, and relentless.  It's very quick-paced, and sheds a lot of light into a conflict that not a lot of people want to think about.  Beevor is at pains to point out that the lines of cleavage in Spain were simply Red and White, but rather much more comoplex--centralism versus local autonomy;  ethnolinguistic rivalries;  religion, and so forth.  One gets a real sense of the chaos and the bitterness of the infighting, considering the players involved:  The Falange, the ultra-Catholic Carlists, and the pro-Alfonsine monarchists on the one side, and the Basque and Catalan nationalists, plus the communists, socialists, left democrats, and anarcho-syndicalists on the other.  

I marched through it in about a day or two, and will probably re-read it. 

It's an interesting and fascinating companion to a recent work of fiction whose events take place during the war and its aftermath:  *The Shadow of the Wind* by Carlos Ruiz Zafon--a novel set in post-War Barcelona, with extended flashbacks to the crisis of the '30s.  And also about books.

### Post by Marquis_de_Carabas on 2006-11-15
Check out the text of Cory Doctorow's talk: [Ebooks: Neither E, Nor Books]("http://www.craphound.com/ebooksneitherenorbooks.txt"). It's a great discussion on the nature of ebooks vs paper books, the pros and cons for writers and readers, the huge problems with current copyright laws etc.

I'm sure most people here have heard of him, but for any who haven't, he's a writer  (I've read his first novel and a few of his short stories, and very much enjoyed them) who worked for the Electronic Freedom Foundation for four years. He's released all three of his novels (and most of his short stories) under a Creative Commons license and they can be downloaded freely from his website. There are a few derivative works - audiobooks etc. - floating around.

Interestingly in the same talk he speaks of owning over 10,000 books. This is what people often seem to miss when they start complaining that paper smells nicer or that 'real' books are prettier: it's not an either/or situation. There are some things that ebooks are better suited for and some things that paper books are better suited for. I own a few hundred paper books and am constantly buying more, but I also have a few hundred books on my laptop. Ideally I'd like to own both versions of every book: paper books are much better for reading in the bath, ebooks are much better to quote from in essays.

(Incidentally, some favourites include Neil Gaiman, Terry Pratchett, George Orwell, Aldous Huxley, Tom Robbins, Noam Chomsky, Douglas Copeland, Robert Anton Wilson, Robert M Pirsig...)

Edit: Sorry, don't want to make this too long, but I just found this great quote which sums up a lot of Doctorow's ideology:

"You know as authors, as creators, there are a lot of terrible things that get done to us; people rip us off, they plagiarize us, they criticize us unfairly and so on. Those are all par for the course. But the one thing that I think that every author, no matter what medium she works in, has a real creeping horror of is being banned, is being censored, is having all of your books piled up and set fire to. And when you look at what the protracted term of copyright coupled with the impossibility of legally digitizing large courses of work is giving us, it is a slow-motion burning of the library."
From [an interview with RedHat magazine]("http://www.redhat.com/magazine/015jan06/features/doctorow/index.html")

### Post by blazoner on 2007-05-16
As to Fiction vs. Non-Fiction, to each his own.

I personally find electronic means better and easier to reference Non-Fiction sources like textbooks and manuals, yet I find an appeal to paper and ink that adds ambiance and familiarity to a good novel.

> I remember reading some criticism years back that, as I remember it, asserted that as taste and intelligence mature in fiction readers they demand stricter realism -- a small child enjoying the pure fantasy of fairy tales with bizarre plots, supernatural characters and anthropomorphic talking animals, and the most mature readers requiring the almost drab, internalized psychological aridity of...I dunno, Updike maybe.

To quote the highly successful author Jane Yolen:
> As Walter de la Mare, that consummate poet of childhood, once said, "Only the rarest kind of best is good enough for children."  As adult readers, we forgive even our favorite writers. This I find to be a crude effort at pigeon-holing those whose tastes do not align with yours. (I'll explain in a moment.)


Some of the most fantastical literature that has been imagined in the last 150 years is as poignant today as when it was first penned, precisely due to its fantasy elements.

Some prime examples:

[INDENT]Lewis Carroll's *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* and *Through the Looking-Glass, and What Alice Found There* including all of the original artwork by Sir John Tenniel.

L. Frank Baum's *Oz* series.
Both well conceived political diatribes.


C. S. Lewis' *The Chronicles Of Narnia*.
A wonderful primer for learning Christian principles.

J. R. R. Tolkien's The Hobbit, and The Lord of the Rings
Works that have redefined fantasy by a master artisan.
[/INDENT]
Science Fiction has it's own laundry list of influential authors, from Asimov, to Crichton, Edgar Rice Burroughs, and even *GASP* Gene Roddenberry.

And what is the link between these two "Genre's"?
The willing suspension of disbelief.

And why, you ask is this important?
Simple.

It is the basic premise that separates fiction from lies.  The ability to trust an author's creation at face value, because you have agreed at the outset that what you are reading is untrue to some extent or other.
Beyond that, it is your opportunity to immerse yourself in the author's vision.  In fact, it is the highlight of written communication, to transcend the words themselves, and see things on a level beyond the capabilities of written language.

Lies, on the other hand, are falsehoods that are being presented as truth, with the intent to deceive.

If Copernicus was unable to suspend his disbelief, or rather suspend his belief in the dogma of his day, where would we be?
OK, so that one is rhetorical.... but telling.

Fantasy fiction is a valuable tool for sharing belief systems.  I have read many books written from different religious viewpoints, including all of the major world religions, and many minor ones.  In fact, Myth and Legend are of phenomenal importance to any society, carrying truth from generation to generation, and attempting to explain the world.  Greek, Norse, English, (Arthurian legend anyone?) Chinese, Native American, American West (Pecos Bill and Paul Bunyan).
Science Fiction often does the same sort of thing including Secular Humanism as well.  It also frequently postulates on different forms of social engineering, such as Orwell's *1984*.
Speaking of Orwell, how about the "anthropomorphic talking animals" in *Animal Farm*?

Not only is Science Fiction a "theater of ideas" from a social viewpoint, but also a driving force behind modern science, in both theory and practicality.

How many scientific inventions and discoveries have come to fruition due to fiction like Star Trek?  And how many of the scientists responsible for Lunar exploration had Edgar Rice Burroughs as their muse?  Examples?  How about scanning people's bodies for illness or injury in the "Sick Bay"?  Have you had a CT scan?  Maybe an ultrasound?  Others?  A tablet computer to write on?  Everything from string-theory and a multiverse or omniverse, to black holes and worm-holes, and yes, even Sub-Space.

Sure their are those who grasp at alternate realities as a way to cope with this one, but that's the rule, not the exception.  And it is found in many different aspects of all societies.  (Africans rejecting education, including hygiene and AIDS prevention, because it is brought by the white man.  The person who is so into developing code that they cease to function effectively with others.  The person who is so into education that they judge themselves and others by the "Important" books they read, while not bothering to interface with the rest of humanity.  I could go on and on, and probably include 98% of humanity in one aspect or another.)  In fact, history currently records only ONE perfect man, and that is also the subject of much debate.

Personally, I am an optimist, even though I see people gravitate towards the lowest common denominator in life.  Expect the best out of others, and more often than not, they will live up to your expectations.  Expect the worst out of others, and more often than not, they will live down to your expectations.

Those of us who have deal with the criminal element have a responsibility to look for the best in humanity, lest we live our lives in despair, or cheapen that which is most precious.
