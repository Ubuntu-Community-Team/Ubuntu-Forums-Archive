### Post by Pogeymanz on 2009-12-26
I hate the licenses that you have to accept to use the software!

Why are proprietary licenses so ridiculous? Even Opera, which has the most tame license I have read thus far has a few points that I don't like:
> 
YOU ACKNOWLEDGE THAT THE SOFTWARE IS NOT INTENDED FOR USE IN (I) ONLINE CONTROL OF AIRCRAFT, AIR TRAFFIC, AIRCRAFT NAVIGATION OR AIRCRAFT COMMUNICATIONS; OR (II) IN THE DESIGN, CONSTRUCTION, OPERATION OR MAINTENANCE OF ANY NUCLEAR FACILITY.


That seems silly, but I guess it's acceptable because it doesn't say that you "can't" use it for that. Just that it is not "intended".

> 
Automatic Updates: The Software which you use may automatically download and install updates from time to time from Opera Software. These updates are designed to improve, enhance and further develop the Services and may take the form of bug fixes, enhanced functions, new software modules and completely new versions. You agree to receive such updates (and permit Opera Software to deliver these to you) as part of your use of the Services.

You can't just allow me to use the version I want?

> 
Opera Software ASA may at its sole option terminate this agreement and deny use of its Services to you at any time for any reason, including as a result of your violation, or alleged violation, of the terms of this Agreement. 


All agreements have something like this. I hate this umbrella crap. I know it wont ever happen if I'm a good boy to them, but I just hate that they can make something up after I agree to this document and use it against me.
> 
You shall not use the Software or any Services provided by Opera Software ASA for any illegal activities or to view, upload or transmit any illegal, harmful, unauthorized, or harassing content.

Why do I have to promise you that? Why can't you just say "I take no responsibility for what you do with my software."?

If I ever write software that I don't want to release the source for, here will be my license (in a nutshell):
> 
To use this software, you agree to the following:
1. You will not reverse engineer or in any way attempt to get the source code for this.
2. You will not re-distribute this software.
3. I am not responsible for anything you do with this software.
4. I do not guarantee anything about this software, including that it will work as intended.


Does that seem so bad?

### Post by JC Cheloven on 2009-12-26
My standpoint:

1) Software isn't only tools. It manages my information: my list of friends, my personal documments/mails, which school my daugther goes to, my bank account ... I should have the RIGHT to know what exactly my software does with this. Much in the same way as I have the right to know which ingredients are in the food I'll eat.

2) Formats are not only 'dumb' containers. To access a piece of information, I need to read/understand the format. And I have the right to access the information! The formats should be all open, and established by independent public instances (ISO for example). Companies shouldn't be alowed to use closed formats to lock customers on their products.

3) Monopolies are bad for the people: A good thing of capitalism -if any- is free competition. But this is blown away by the presence of a monopoly (or an agreed trust, which is the same in practice). Laws should be harder against monopolies for the shake of general interest.

So, proprietary software is a threat to my rights. Proprietary formats are even worse, near to an insult for me. Finally, I have no words for a proprietary software/format from a monopolistic company.

BTW: I didn't catch wheter a previous post [about this]("http://www.fsf.org/appeal/2009/lawrence-lessig/") was ironic or not, but I mostly agree in what the FSF has been the only source of hope for the freedom of computer users during two decades.

### Post by Methuselah on 2009-12-27
In the majority of cases, when I read priorietary software licenses, I do not want to click 'I agree' and thus should not use the software.
I suspect this would be the case for the majority of people so that's why they don't bother to read it.
However, not bothering to read it is anything but a good faith agreement to abide by the terms of the license.
But chances are you've already paid for it so you damn well intend to use it.
So most people pay for software and are in breach of the license from the moment they click 'I have read and agree to the terms' because they haven't read.
At that moment they have theoretically lost their right to use it though they haven't used it once yet.

It seems extreme when he says it, but that must be the kind of conundrum RMS is alluding to when he calls priorietary software 'unethical'. 
I think the proprietors generally assume most users will not read and fully comprehend the pages of legalese but include it to cover their arses.
The users that don't read the license agreement cannot in good faith agree and the few that do read it probably do not intend to abide by some of the terms but click agree anyway.
After all, you've already paid for it and if you don't 'agree' you cannot install it!

I don't 'hate' proprietary software though as their is an ecosystem of providers and users that depend on it.
However, I generally prefer free software (I would even pay for it) and I believe that FOSS is a better fit some applications.
IMO, an operating sysem is one such application since the vendor that controls your OS has defacto control over your whole machine and the vendors who provide additional software for it.
This is evidenced by the fact that Microsoft has the ability to limit your computer's functions (affecting even software thet did not make) if they deem you to be in breach of their license agreement.
