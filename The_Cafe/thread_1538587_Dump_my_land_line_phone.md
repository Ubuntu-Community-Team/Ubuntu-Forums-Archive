You do not need to have an active landline to get 911 services to your phone jack. If a home has *ever* had an active landline, you can use an inactive landline to place 911 calls out of your home. It's the same as a cell phone that has been deactivated. It can still place an emergency call.
 
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9-1-1#Inactive_telephones](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9-1-1#Inactive_telephones)
> Inactive telephones
In the U.S., [[COLOR=#0645ad]FCC[/COLOR]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission") rules require every telephone that can access the network to be able to dial 9-1-1, regardless of any reason that normal service may have been disconnected (including non-[[COLOR=#0645ad]payment[/COLOR]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Payment")) (This only applies to states with a Do Not Disconnect policy in place. Those states must provide a "soft" dial tone service, details can be found at [[COLOR=#3366bb]http://www.fcc.gov/Bureaus/Common_Carrier/Reports/FCC-State_Link/IAD/pntris99.pdf[/COLOR]]("http://www.fcc.gov/Bureaus/Common_Carrier/Reports/FCC-State_Link/IAD/pntris99.pdf")) On wired ([[COLOR=#0645ad]land line[/COLOR]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_line")) phones, this usually is accomplished by a "soft" [[COLOR=#0645ad]dial tone[/COLOR]]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dial_tone"), which sounds normal but will allow only emergency calls.
