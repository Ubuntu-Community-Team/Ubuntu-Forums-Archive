### Post by MoebusNet on 2015-05-05
Some of your options wil depend on what you consider "old". Some people consider a several-year-old laptop with a dual-core CPU, 1Gb RAM & 120 Gb HDD to be "old". Others, myself included, still use an ancient 13-year-old notebook with a single-core CPU for casual web browsing and light document work. Almost any of them that use a Pentium 4 or newer CPU can be made usable - perhaps not running Ubuntu, but there are a number of distros (Wary Puppy Linux, Lubuntu, etc.) that specifically support older CPU's (ie, non-PAE) and video cards (ie, <256Mb VRAM).

Trying to use "old" notebooks for server-type functions, you will run into issues with connectivity (bandwidth) with older interfaces, depending on your needs. For a home-type DVR, you will be limited by your video card to process your video output to a HD TV. Some people have had success in using old notebooks as media servers (photos, music, video) within the limits of the bandwidth available.

Don't be afraid to experiment; have fun!

### Post by veddox on 2015-05-06
about giving the to charity:

That is a good idea, *if they are still reasonably good*. Speaking as a teacher at a school in Africa, we sometimes get old computers donated by somebody in the West. A few are still usable, but all too often they have about 128MB RAM, broken touchpads and an Intel Pentium from 1999. I'm sure the people who send these out do so with the best intent, but they don't help, we can't do anything with them. In short: **don't give away junk**. (If they have enough power to run Windows 7 reasonably smoothly, or Ubuntu or Linux Mint, they are very much welcome. But if you can't even start AbiWord on them without crashing them, no thank you!)

I don't know how old your laptops are. If they do fit into the above category of "too old for service" and you want to donate them, your local school computer club is probably the best port of call. If nothing else, they can always take them apart and cannibalise the parts...

### Post by veddox on 2015-05-07
Thanks for the feedback, sudodus!

[B]Minimal requirements:
[/B]
At the very least, the computer should be able to run Lubuntu with a LibreOffice or Firefox (or another modern browser like Chromium) window open, more or less smoothly. AbiWord/Gnumeric don't count! That means the specs should be approximately:

Processor: Pentium 4, 2.6 GHz or corresponding AMD K8
RAM: 512 MB
Hard drive: 15GB

**Preferred specs:**

Donated computers often end up in areas where relatively low levels of education means that it is very rare to find somebody with good or even basic Linux skills. A laptop with the above minimal requirements could still do reasonable service if set up with Lubuntu, but if nobody knows how to work Lubuntu, that isn't much good. (Also note that administrating old hardware often takes a fair amount of technical knowledge.) The exposure people on the receiving end will have had with computers is most likely based on Windows. Hence, it would be better if a donated laptop can run Windows 7 (not XP, due to its EOL) reasonably well. The required specs for that are somewhat higher:

Processor: Pentium D or better
RAM: 2GB (1.5 at the absolute minimum)
Hard drive: 30GB

Basically, it all boils down to one question: *can you reasonably expect somebody to do work with the computer you want to donate?*


*N.B.: *Processors aren't my strong suite - can somebody confirm that a Pentium D is sufficient for Windows 7? And what kind of clock rate would you need?

Huh! Nope, you're dead right there, Skaperen. I was using one of those in mid-1982; THIRTY-THREE years ago.

The '64' refers to the amount of RAM they had. We're talking here, not about 64 MB.....but the grand total of (wait for it, wait for it...) 64 whole KILOBYTES of RAM.

Incidentally, the MOS Technology 6502 8-bit CPU ran at the THEN staggering speed of 1.02 MHz... Wikipedia's article is a trifle misleading, as the 6510 referred to didn't make it onto the shelves until the end of 1984. The first production run used the then-available 6502.

These things might be considered 'dinosaurs', but there's a thriving community out there who are doing things with the C64's that would have been sheerest fantasy to the original manufacturer, Commodore (or CBM...Commodore Business Machines as they were on this side of the pond). Like fitting DVD drives; hard drives; and loads more hardware. Running synthesizers, emulators, and all sorts of stuff.

Apparently, one of the criteria, however, is that you mustn't attempt to make the CPU run any faster (you certainly couldn't replace it; SOCKETS? Huh? Whaa...oh, never mind. They were soldered to the board)...
