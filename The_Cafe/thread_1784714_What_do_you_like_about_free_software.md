### Post by Copper Bezel on 2011-06-17
I like knowing who's responsible for a piece of software that I really like, rather than having to attribute it to a corporation. Sure, most of the core OS components are written with corporate sponsorship, but a lot of the little gadgets and extras come from folks just seeing a problem and deciding to solve it for everyone. 

I like knowing that my software is my software, and that I can do what I want with it. I'm not getting a service by agreeing to some particular restriction. There are no ad supported features, premium versions, trial editions, and so on. If something irritates me about how a piece of software works, I can very often find a way to reconfigure it to my needs and preferences. 

I like that, thanks to the system's graphics toolkit, I can expect applications to match *my* "branding," not smother me with the distributor's. 

By the same token, free software is generally designed to *work*, not to market itself. I mean, I like flair as much as the next guy, but a lot of commercial software aims at the experience of the first ten minutes, not the long-term usefulness, and impressing users with a baroque interface when a simple, relatively invisible one would suffice.

I like knowing that open source projects are publically audited, so that elements that might be malicious, messy, or inefficient under the hood can be identified.

Edit: and as Macskeeball said, I also appreciate that I don't have to fight with built-in restrictions on an application made to protect its license while I'm trying to use it in a perfectly legal way.

### Post by amauk on 2011-06-18
Free & Open source is more than just source code

Having the source open is, from a technical perspective, one thing
But from a social perspective, it's a whole lot more

It's the community, ease of contribution and piece of mind that come as by-products of open code that I respect

The fact that the GNU/Linux ecosystem has no single controlling entity means it cannot be bought out, beaten down or litigated into bankruptcy. It cannot die. A company (and it's proprietary products) can.

The fact that developers maintain transparent processes to software development ensures that nothing happens behind closed doors, and your average end-user has the opportunity to become involved in the development process.

The fact that different vendors compete not on closed code and proprietary protocols designed to lock you into a system, but on feature-sets, ease of use and ease of integration.

The fact that, if I don't like something, I am free (technically and legally) to change it.
