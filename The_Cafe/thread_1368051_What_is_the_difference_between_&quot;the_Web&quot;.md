### Post by JKyleOKC on 2009-12-30
Lovinglinux has what I think is the best explanation of the difference. "In the beginning" there was no Internet, and only a few folk used any sort of networking at all. Communicating between computers in different cities was a most unusual thing and required all sorts of special equipment.

With the introduction of the personal computer in 1975, a generation of geeks began building their own machines. When IBM brought out its first PC in 1981, the general public began to take notice. By that time the early adopters had come up with modems (a geekish word compressed from "MOdulator-DEModulator" which described how it works by converting bit signals to tones) and were operating local bulletin board systems. Some of those systems were communicating with each other via long-distance phone, and that was the beginning.

Meanwhile the US Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) was funding serious research aimed at making such interconnection possible on a large scale; the result was ARPANet which connected a number of large universities and military installations in the US. It wasn't open to the public, but as the word of its existence spread a clamor arose to make it so. When that happened, the Internet was born.

At first it bore little resemblance to what we see today; not until the World Wide Web was invented did it become a tool easy for anyone to use. However the Web is nowhere near being the only tool on the Net; it's just the most well-known one.

Nowadays many web sites use Flash and large graphics images that require high speed broadband connections to be usable, and I suspect that's what your friend had in mind. Text-only uses of the Internet, such as non-HTML Email, UseNet, and FTP, can be perfectly acceptable with slow connections and hardware. I belong to a newsgroup community in which several members are on slow dial-up and cannot use tools such as most antivirus programs because the daily updates would take several hours to transfer (yes, these folk use Windows and have to deal with viruses).

Hope this provides a bit more info; it's been a wonderful ride but I wouldn't want to go back to what I was using in 1965 (a Model 33 Teletype machine at 10 bytes/second)!

**THE INTERNET:**
[The internet]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet") is a network of computers that use a particular understanding of interpretation of carrier signaling (a protocol called [TCP/IP]("http://lmgtfy.com/?q=tcp%2Fip")).

What the Internet is a standard for connecting computers and actually the implementation of that standard. When you are connected to the internet you have the possibility to interact (send and receive) in this network. You have a layer of software that makes this connection. It can receive and "interpret" packets. That's the IP part, Internet Packets. 

So the internet is just a passel of computers. That's all. Hard-drives around the world, at your disposal. Pretty cool, eh?

You cannot DO anything though until you have a client that relates to a server (a program on a computer that has files to share). The client is a program on your computer that talks to the server. Think of them as on a date.  :) That's another layer of software.

Files on the internet need to be "interpreted" according to their "type" and so are differentiated by their type of resource locator that "specifies where an identified resource is available and the mechanism for retrieving it." [from [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator])  
Here's a list of some of the types of data THAT ARE NOT THE WEB.
I think these are called [URI schemes]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/URI_scheme"), often referred to as URL protocols (but that's not a pristinely correct way of referring to them.

email  mail://
files and directories  ftp://
bulletin board style groups of linear threaded posts with a hierarchical naming structure (very similar to forums but text-based)  news://
there also is fax, gopher, mailto, finger, talk, irc,...
Now I'm not quite right on all this but it's generally true. 
I'm learning MORE than I want to know right now so I'll stop here.

**THE WEB:**
The web was predated by an Apple program called Hypertext.
The web first appeared in text and was managed by a client program called lynx:// (as in LINKS, cute eh?)

What makes it [The Web]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web") is the ability to click on a link and go to that page (file|site). That is called a Hyper-Link.

As you (should and will now) know your client to look at the files on The World Wide Web is called a browser, eg lynx, Mosaic, Netscape, Firefox, Chrome, Opera and last but not least Internet Explorer.

### Post by MaxIBoy on 2010-01-13
"The 'Net" is short for "The Internet," a scale-free network of computers which are interconnected in a hierarchical system.

"The Web" is short for "The World-Wide Web," AKA "WWW." The Web is a system of hypertext, which means that the "Web pages" can have inline links to each other with no limit to the complexity of the resulting network. The Web is usually used over Internet connections.

The Internet has many other uses besides Web pages: email, FTP, IRC, and Usenet all existed decades before the Web was invented. File transfers which use P2P software protocols such as Bittorrent or Gnutella also account for a huge proportion of Internet traffic.
