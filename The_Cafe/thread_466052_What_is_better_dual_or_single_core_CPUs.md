### Post by mustang on 2007-06-06
Assuming they're of the same type (same architecture) one rule of thumb you can count on is that a dual core processor will never be twice as fast as a single core processor because there is additional overhead needed for two cores to work together.

### Post by reckless2k2 on 2007-06-06
It is more about what you are using it for. When you say "hungry", you need to really define what you mean. Given that, unless you are doing HEAVY multi-tasking, video editing (doubt it in linux), or doing serious gaming (doubt it in linux), you are not going to see much of a need for a dual. you will see slight speed differences but things will depend on the RAM size and speed difference between PC models as well as HD speeds (SATA vs. PATA). There are a lot of factors that go into it. either way, you probably have hard drives large enough to dual boot regardless and test or use. ### Post by RandomJoe on 2007-06-10
> **jgrabham said:**
> Yes, but 2gb Ram!  I think it just about makes up for it!
I don't know the motherboard, so can't say anything specific to it.  However, I can say that *NOTHING* will properly make up for a crappy motherboard!  I've lost count over the years, how many systems I've seen that had otherwise great components, but a lousy mobo.  Their performace stank the place up.  Then you see a more modest system overall - but with higher-quality basic components (like the mobo) that would smoke them.

Worse still is if you get a low-quality board that runs okay --- MOST of the time.  I had one of those too.  Actually was a pretty nice system, except for the utterly random, infrequent freezes or crashes.  I eventually moved all the parts to a new mobo and gee, it all worked just fine.

While replacing a mobo later on isn't a huge deal, it's still a lot more work than just popping in another stick of RAM later on.  Especially if you take into account the need to change / update drivers for an existing OS (particularly Windows, in my past experience).

Besides, while I do have 2GB RAM in my desktop, it's really not necessary.  I do a lot of DVD ripping / encoding for my home theater system and it doesn't even come close to touching all that memory.  I often push the encoding job to my server, which only has 768MB.  For that matter, I've had the desktop running for several days now (I generally shut it down during the week when I'm at work) and even the buffers haven't consumed all the memory.  I have 11% used by programs and 61% cache.

More isn't *always* better - just more.

As to your original question, I prefer dual-core.  You rarely have just one single process running to the exclusion of all else, with dual-core you can have one core hammering away at the big process and still find the system usable for other things.  Nice!
