### Post by guyver_dio on 2011-12-29
Yes a degree in almost anything these days doesn't mean much to an  employer (I've got a bone to pick with this but I'll save that for a  more appropriate thread). 

In my experience, software development or any programming field can be hard to get into at first because of a double edged sword thing going on. Employers generally want 3-5 years experience minimum before they'll consider you but nobody will hire you so you can get the experience. There are ways that help though, I don't know how you're colleges work but here some degrees are known as co-op degrees. That's when you do practical work with a real company while completing your studies. That counts as experience, if they don't have that you can always go to companies and ask for unpaid work experience. 

If you want to know what type of jobs are available, go see what's up on a job site at the moment. My guess is web development is more common than anything else.

Also keep your eyes peeled for internships, this is how I got into my job (i'm a web application developer). I feel this is SOO much better than doing a degree, instead of a degree where you get into a job and throw away 90% of what you learned because you never use it and eventually forget anyway, you learn your job by doing the job (funny that huh). 

My honest opinion, I did a degree, if I could go back I probably wouldn't have bothered. An engineering degree might be worth while, at least here there's a better success rate in getting employment from college in that field and other fields like medicine where a degree is basically mandatory. If it's something you're passionate about I'd learn it on the side, be proactive, approach companies and ask for unpaid work experience and wriggle your way in that way. 

Again this is just from my experience so hopefully a lot more people will have their say.

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-12-29
If you like Math, Science and Computers, then you can take a look at Scientific Computing. Basically you would be writing programs that solve complicated math problems related to science and engineering. There is good demand for people with good understanding of both Math and Programming. Some schools provide degree in Computational Science, but those are not many. If you can double-major in Math and Computer Science (that's what I did, but this is very school dependent), then you can go to graduate school with either Math or CS. Without the double-major, you can still go for Math or Computer Science and take as many classes from the other field as possible.

To get a decent job, Masters is required, Ph.D. is much better. Basically, if you want go that route, then make sure you like staying in school.

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-12-29
When I went to college for my degree in electronic engineering they were still teaching about vacuum tubes. I have worked as an engineer for 40 years. 

I started designing analog circuits. Then things started moving to digital circuits so I learned digital circuits. Then things move to microprocessors so I learned about microprocessors and assembly language. Today, I work with virtualized datacenters (think of Amazon Elastic Cloud). 

I guess what I am saying is, get a degree in an engineering field. Get your foot in the door. Then be prepared for a lifetime of continuous learning. Technology doesn't stand still just because you got a degree. A degree in engineering will tech you some fundamental skills that can last a lifetime. The specific subject of the degree may get you your first job and may influence you second job; however, after that it is your experience and accomplishments that matter.
