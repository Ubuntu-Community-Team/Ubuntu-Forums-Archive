### Post by SeijiSensei on 2011-07-05
> **prodigy_ said:**
> IBM made a grave mistake when it chose DOS over some kind of lightweight UNIX and locked PC users in a completely non-POSIX environment.

The 8086/8088 processors in the original IBM PC and its offspring could not support multitasking at all.  It wasn't until the advent of the 80386 that Unix even became an option on the Intel platform.  There were *nix operating systems for that chip and its successors.  Even Microsoft offered one - Xenix.  These were all seen as either server OS's or designed to compete in the scientific workstation market.  Considering that early PCs had one floppy drive and a single user, and that IBM never intended the PC to be a general-purpose computer, DOS was a reasonable choice.  (The PC was a skunk-works project at IBM's Entry Systems division; it was justified to upper management as a more advanced data-entry system for IBM's mainframes.)

Microsoft could have based Windows 95 on a POSIX platform, but it just bolted an interface onto DOS instead, just as it had with Windows 2. 3.0, 3.1 and 3.11.  NT was more akin to Unix, but it wasn't intended for ordinary users.

On the issue of pricing, remember that many Windows machines are subsidized by bundling commercial software trials and what-not with the machine.  Most estimates indicates these bundle bring in some $50 or more to the OEM.  That's why machines with Linux pre-installed rarely carry substantial discounts when compared with Windows equivalents, even when the cost of the OEM Windows license is included. Linux doesn't (yet) have any such subsidies because of the dearth of commercial software for the platform.

Next year will always be the "year of Linux on the desktop."
