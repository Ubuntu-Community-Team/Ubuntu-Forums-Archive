### Post by schauerlich on 2009-10-17
Language is one of those things that can't be pinned down and scripted. Most of what we consider "grammar" is really just a *preferred*, standard way of writing sentences, and has little bearing on the *functional* use of language where it is used most: when it is spoken. I would venture to guess that most of the sentences that one speaks out loud would not be considered "correct" when written down. And yet, there is no mass confusion when people talk out loud. Why is this? Because language is a system based on flexibility and creativity. Units of similar sounds (phonemes) combine to create units of meaning (morphemes), which are combined into even larger structures called sentences, with some rules (syntax). The rules of syntax for most spoken languages are actually remarkably lax when you think about it. It's similar to writing code in C: There's plenty of stuff I could write that would be syntactically valid, and the compiler would understand, but other programmers reading the code would complain about its quality *in its written form*. However, in its function form (compiled/spoken), there's little difference.

The point of the preceding block of text is this: A language cannot be regulated, because it is defined by those who speak it. And if my understanding of human interaction is correct, it's very difficult to get humans to agree on *anything*, much less something as thoroughly ingrained in our brains as language.
