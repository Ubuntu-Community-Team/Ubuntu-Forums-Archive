### Post by keithpeter on 2012-03-31
Hello terrykiwi83

Sounds like minds are opening.

mtpaint and tuxpaint spring to mind as well as the heavyweights (GIMP &c). Inkscape could be fun as well. One of the light programming environments might allow pupils to explore programming (Squeak or a traditional LOGO). Children around 10 or 12 have done audio stuff with Audacity, and why not some video?

Some questions/observations

1) Are these stand alone PCs with local printers or is there some kind of network being assumed?

2) Have you seen Edubuntu? Server and thin client model?

3) Office Suite: LibreOffice is great in a closed loop environment, is there an expectation that pupils will take files home to work on? If so, LibreOffice Portable App on USB sticks would help parents using Windows PCs at home

4) Which of the interfaces are you looking at? Unity/Gnome Shell or something that looks a bit like the PC at home such as a single panel XFCE?

### Post by patchwork on 2012-03-31
> Open Source (Free) or Closed Source (Paid)

This may be a bit misleading.  Whether the source code is available to the user or not does influence whether an application or suite is available with or without a fee.

I would expect the majority of your audience to be more concerned with cost than availability of source code, so I might rename this heading to something like "Freely available software versus commercial software".
