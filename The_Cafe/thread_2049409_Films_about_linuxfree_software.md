### Post by 1clue on 2012-08-30
I seriously doubt I would personally see an action/drama/whatever movie which was "about" Open Source software, and I've been pretty much living it for over a decade.  A documentary about FOSS and its history and main actors similar to Pirates of Silicon Valley would be interesting though.

On the other hand, I could imagine a non-documentary movie where FOSS played a significant role.  Meaning that Open Source tools are used front and center, mentioned by name and the nature of the license affected the plot.

The problem is, movie writers, producers and directors have a terrible record of making a movie about anything seriously computer related which is also technically feasible.  War Games is about all I can think of, and maybe Pirates of Silicon Valley but that's not really even about the software.

War Games is far and away my favorite computer drama.  When that movie came out, the computer the main character used really existed, and it was awesome.  Every minor detail about computers was technically feasible, except that the AI was obviously way out in left field, and I seriously doubt any military would put the control of nuclear assets entirely in control of a computer.

All that said, the idea of a good fiction is to have everything realistic except one or two things, and then build the story around that.

War Games is one of the only movies about computers I've seen where I didn't immediately snort and say something like, "Bull$#!+".

To compound the issue, a movie about Open Source would be much more demanding regarding the truth of how FOSS works.  The users tend to be much more involved or at least more knowledgeable about computers in general.  If they're going to target FOSS as a main story device then they're automatically targeting real FOSS users as a significant part of the audience, and probably the mainstream non-FOSS nerd collective as well.

IMO they would need a serious Name from FOSS as a consultant, and they would have to listen to him/her.  IMO they would have to NOT use RMS as that consultant, because he's such an extremist about FOSS that nobody near the center of the unconverted masses would take another look after that.  We would need somebody from Apache or FreeBSD or a similar group, somebody who could adequately describe how FOSS can work with commercial software smoothly.

### Post by 1clue on 2012-09-03
Actually sci-fi and fantasy are my favorite genres.

What I'm talking about has nothing to do with fantasy or any other genre.  It has everything to do with making a story that the viewer/reader can connect with viscerally.

At one end of the spectrum, you have a movie made of a bunch of random images none of which have anything to do with any other.  That is not a story, and it makes no sense.

At the other end of the spectrum, you have a complete uncut movie of a real event, from beginning to end of that event with lots of content to describe what it all means.

A good story teller tells the story in a way that the spectator can relate to.  The more the story fits in with their reality, the more believable the story is.  Real life experiences, boys relating to girls in the normal way, scrubbing floors or shoveling a pig sty might not be things you've done, but you know people do that sort of thing and you can imagine what it's like.  So when somebody starts throwing fireballs or a dragon strafes the village you can feel the shock of it.

I'm a professional computer programmer.  TRON goes into detail about how computers "work" but those details are both childishly simplistic and wrong even in the most basic elements.  Those elements are common knowledge now for people who grew up in a computing society.  The plot is childishly simplistic.  Dora the Explorer explains malware, but doesn't really understand it herself so she does a crappy job.

I spend so much time disbelieving the computing basics that the plot is irrelevant.

I really shouldn't need to describe this here.  You can google the elements of good storytelling and get a much better description than I can give here.  There are thousands of really good fantasy stories that are wonderfully written and many of which are belivable enough that they can make you think it's real.  Same with any other genre.

### Post by 1clue on 2012-09-03
@forrestcupp,

Let's wind this whole thing down in a few more posts.  We're seriously detracting from the thread I think.

Fantasy is to a departure from reality, but it still needs plausibility in most of the points in the story or it isn't a good story.

Take a look at Tolkien, you have a lot of deviation from reality, but horses are still horses, humans are still humans, and Middle Earth has ... laws?  a natural way of how things work together which is mostly the same as it is on Earth.  You have multiple races, each of which has characteristics.  You have magic, and it behaves a certain way although you don't really know how that is.  There are monsters, and frankly Earth has/had monsters too, creatures that preyed on humans and who were given imagined powers, or forces of nature that were explained in stories of gods.  It's no significant stretch to give credence to the tales and create a world.  Tolkien's world followed mythology that had existed for hundreds or thousands of years, created the "universe" and then followed those rules.

Now going in the direction of sci-fi, which is where I personally would have placed TRON, and the same thing applies:  Take Frank Herbert's DUNE series, you have a galactic culture which is so far in the future as to not really remember Earth much, but even though the technology is nearly indistinguishable and the society is dramatically different, there is still enough which is similar that the story is incredibly plausible.

Take a look at Star Wars.  Even if there are funny robots and goofy comic relief characters, even though there is The Force and space ships and all sorts of things which can't be explained by modern technology or anything else for that matter, the story is plausible because those things which are "human" are consistent with what we know, and motives span across races but are still recognizable, and technology is consistent with what we believe could happen someday.  Read:  The future stuff is fine, but the rules we know are  NOT broken.

My issue with TRON isn't the departure from reality, it's that the things which are claimed to be consistent with reality are not, and the descriptions of the things which are deliberate changes are not plausible either.  Thinking back to when TRON the original was in the theaters, the entire focus at that point was that it was computer generated, and everyone I remember talking to about the movie thought the special effects were the only thing worth seeing, the rest of it was junk.
