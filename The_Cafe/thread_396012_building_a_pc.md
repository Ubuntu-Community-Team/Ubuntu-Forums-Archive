### Post by BarfBag on 2007-03-28
Would you be willing to go over $1,000 (total, of course) for a nice monitor?  You could easily get an LCD for under $200, but the 23" screens are simply beautiful.  If you saw one in person, you'd see what I mean.

Look on [Tiger Direct]("http://www.tigerdirect.com/") and [NewEgg]("http://www.newegg.com/") before going to your local computer store.  They tend to have better prices.

I recommend you get an Intel Core Duo 2 operating @ at least 2 GHz.  1 GB of RAM (DDR2 is faster and will last you longer) should be enough.  I can't recommend a motherboard specifically (there's simply too many), but I've always had good experiences with ASUS and MSI.  For a graphics card, get an nVidia.  They have the best drivers for Linux.  If the motherboard you get has an AGP slot (don't recommend it, if it does), [this]("http://www.newegg.com/Product/Product.aspx?Item=N82E16814150210") is amazing.  That's what I have.  If you get a PCI-E motherboard (faster and will last you longer), just pick a PCI-E nVidia with 256 MB of VRAM.

Hope this helps.  Building PC's is fun.  I've built quite a few.

### Post by maniacmusician on 2007-03-29
The core 2 duo smokes Pentium D in every sense of the word. 

AMD makes good processors as well, and they are cheaper, but when considering performance, Intel's Core 2 Duos outclass anything that AMD has to offer as well. I'm not being a fanboy (I use whatever performs  better), just giving you straight facts. 

Do you think that waiting longer to buy a PC will increase your budget for it? If so, you should take into consideration that Intel's next line of processors (codenamed Penrynn, I think) will be released later this year. They'll be cutting edge, but probably a lot more expensive as well.

Regardless of any of that, you should at least wait a little while before buying, because rumors say that Intel will soon drop the prices of its Core 2 Duo processors. You can take the savings you get from that and buy a nice LCD monitor.

Generally, I fix in my mind a budget and an anticipated time to buy.  Then, I'll snag whatever's in the budget at the time I need the gear.  Occasionally, I'll hold off and wait for prices to drop on a component if I really want that functionality.

The waiting game is worth playing if you're disciplined about your use profile and what functionality you're willing to accept at the price you're willing to pay.  A lot of bad hardware buys come about because people are really chasing shadows--"I want a computer that is AWESOME!"--rather than dealing in concrete needs ("I need a computer that will accomplish these tasks.")

### Post by AlanRogers on 2007-03-29
IMHO building a PC is a great way to understand how the whole thing hangs together but it's one of those "Been there, done that" experiences. Once you've done this, it'll be more cost effective to buy a ready-made PC assembled in the far east which has the components you're looking for and then spend your time on getting your OS the way you need it to be.

Someone I know spent £2,500 on a custom machine from a company I'd never heard of, only for the hard drive to fail and he's now got a really expensive doorstop - no warranty it seems. Meantime, I've never spent more than £500 on a computer and never had such troubles but then you couldn't describe them as bleeding edge either.
