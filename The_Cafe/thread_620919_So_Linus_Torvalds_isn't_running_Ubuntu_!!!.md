### Post by Darkhack on 2007-11-24
> **derby007 said:**
> The thing I find facinating/interesting about Linus, Linux, FreeSoftware. Linus invented/started/catapulted all this 'free' OS thingy, but he is getting paid to maintain it !!! doh !
Note: this is a very generalised statement, no personal attacks pls, ie. I just find it peculiar.

I know you state it is a very generalized statement, but no matter how generalized it is, it is still wrong.  Linus did not start/invent the concept of free software.  Free software (free as in speech, not as in beer) has been around as long as computers have when scientists and universities would openly share code to avoid duplicate effort.  One of those university students, Richard M. Stallman (commonly referred to as RMS) worked at the MIT Artificial Intelligence Laboratory and noticed the commercialization of software which began in the 1980s with companies selling proprietary software.  RMS, started the Free Software Foundation (FSF) in 1985 and created the General Public License (GPL) to combat this issue and uphold the four freedoms of software...

    *  The freedom to run the program, for any purpose (freedom 0).
    * The freedom to study how the program works, and adapt it to your needs (freedom 1). Access to the source code is a precondition for this.
    * The freedom to redistribute copies so you can help your neighbor (freedom 2).
    * The freedom to improve the program, and release your improvements to the public, so that the whole community benefits (freedom 3). Access to the source code is a precondition for this.

At the time, all operating systems were proprietary and did not uphold the four freedoms.  RMS and the FSF stared the GNU project to create a free OS.  GNU (a recursive acronym) stands for Gnu is Not Unix.  By 1990 they had built nearly all of the tools they needed.  Compilers (GCC), libraries (glibc), and other generic utilities.  They did not however have a kernel and they set out to create a microkernel called HURD.  Because of their complex design and message passing system between components, development was extremely slow and the kernel was very buggy.  In 1991 (six years after RMS had started GNU) a young Finnish lad studying at the University of Helsinki named Linus B. Torvalds (LBT) created Linux to improve upon Minix which was also a Unix-like OS but was written by Andrew S. Tanenbaum (AST) primarily as a teaching tool for his OS design class (this is at a different university in Amsterdam, not Helsinki) and was thus impractical as a powerful desktop OS.  Linux then combined his kernel with GNU to create GNU/Linux (what RMS prefers to call the OS, not just Linux) and it caught on due to the HURDs snail pace of development (HURD is still in development and not really usable, even after all these years, but most everyone has given up on it).  The BSD systems also were catching on during this time but then they got into a lawsuit with AT&T which claimed that they were using proprietary UNIX code and thus it scared away developers who then went to work on GNU/Linux.

In summary, Linus was a late comer to the game.  Free software had been around since his birth (1969) and the vision for a completely free Unix-like OS was around six years before he even started working on Linux.  If HURD wasn't a mess we'd all be bragging about how cool our Ubuntu GNU systems are, or if AT&T never filed the lawsuit, we'd all be using FreeBSD.
