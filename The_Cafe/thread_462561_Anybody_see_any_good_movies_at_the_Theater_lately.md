### Post by RAV TUX on 2007-06-03
The best review I have seen on the "300" movie:

> [FONT=Arial,Helvetica][SIZE=+1]***300***[/SIZE][/FONT]
[B] If you treated '300' as a serious depiction of Greek life in the second struggle against the Persian Empire--you must either be joking or have a scandalously defective understanding of Ancient Greek history and culture.

I went to see '300' with a good friend of mine. He's a classicist by training, and we had been schoolboy Latinists together. (He can probably cobble together more Greek than I can by now, as well). We laughed through the *whole movie*.  We continued laughing, up to the point of weeping, for almost an *entire hour thereafter*.  
[/B]  
** Why?**

In that age, men were men, and buggered each other in the name of love, brotherhood, and honor. "Don't ask, don't tell" it most certainly was *not*--the whole practice was thought to lead to a tighter phalanx.  

Personally, I had my doubts about just how much homoeroticism was going to be on display in the film--the advance press had billed it pretty much as nothing but a gore-fest. But boy, was I surprised! Far from shy away from the subject, which would have made a lot of the jingos in the audience cringe, the film seemed to glory in it, even if it did so accidentally and with schoolboy innunendo. The film's Leonidas dismisses the Athenians as "boy-lovers" in the first five minutes, notwithstanding Sparta's use of man/boy sex for the purpose of forstering unit cohesion. 

Things went downhill from there. The moments of (unintentional?) comedy are too numerous to count. Particularly amusing were scenes dealing with Xerxes--yes, the unimaginably powerful *padishah* of an empire that stretched from Egypt to India. You'd have thought in this day and age they could have found a real Persian to play the role. But apparently, Hollywood has decided that Xerxes is Shaq at a fetish convention.

The (again, unintentional?) gay humor around Xerxes is alone worth the price of admission. The scene where he attempts to woo Leonidas is fantastic: with Xerxes standing above and behind, bringing his enormous hands to rest on Leonidas' shoulders. The King of King sure comes on strong, apparently. Then there's the bit with Ephialtes, where Xerxes is made to utter a line that should live forever: "Whereas the cruel Leonidas would have you stand, I only require that you *kneel*."  

Given all this--and more that I don't care to cite--the story arc with Leonidas' wife seems tacked on. She is, after all, the only hetero character in the whole movie. From a purely functionalist perspective, she exists to have her tits bounce about on camera and maintain hetero interest. She is otherwise totally superfluous to the telling, as the intrigue-and-treason component is totally false. Persian gold would *not* be circulating in Sparta. The Spartans had a foreign-exchange control regime that Kim Jong-il would be proud to use: all gold and silver was confiscated. The coin of the realm was heavy iron ingots. 

(Yes, yes, I know that the Spartan general Eurybiades would be bribed by Themistocles the Athenian later in the war, but that was away from home, in the field, and far from the prying eyes of the folks back in Sparta.)

Lastly:  the Spartans weren't fighting for *reason*. The Spartans had little use for things like logic, reason, or freedom in the sense that the film wanted to portray. It is not for nothing that the great logicians of the Hellenic world came from everywhere *but* Sparta.  The Spartans, over all, were concerned with *duty*. Their dogmatic insistence on ceremony was half of their being in Sparta. The other half was the display of strength for strength's sake. 

In all, I thought '300' was a good afternoon's diversion, a comedy with many severed heads.  It was most certainly *not* a meditation on patriotism and sacrifice, and certainly not a portrayal of the ancient world as it was or even as we use to understand it. The fact that a film like this could be made and that people will accept it as truthful speaks to a definite poverty of our cultural life. This is not tragic. It is shameful.[http://ouij.livejournal.com/212705.html](http://ouij.livejournal.com/212705.html)

Thank goodness a voice of reason exist in the universe and restores my faith in humanity.
