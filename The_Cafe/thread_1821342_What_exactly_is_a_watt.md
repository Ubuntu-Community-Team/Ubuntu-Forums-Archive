I was reading up a little on how electricity works and this is what I've got so far.

You have watts, amps, and volts and the way it's been explained to me is in terms of water usage, let me explain.

Ampere = unit of physically counting electrons. 1 ampere = 6.25 x 10 to the 18th power number of electrons.

Volt = amount of pressure pushing the ampere(s).

Watts = amperes x volts

But here's what I don't get: if volts is like water pressure (PSI - pounds per square inch of pressure) and amps is like the amount of water (gallons) then why does the electric company charge me for my electric usage in watts, which seems like a made up unit.

But I'd still have the same amount of water in the glass. The same (only in reverse) as if the water pressure was lower.

So, why does the electric company charge me in watts? You can only actually use X number of actual electrons (amperes) of electricity, just like you can only use actual gallons of water (gallons). The pressure that the water comes into my faucet has no real relation to the physical amount of water I used.

### Post by alphacrucis2 on 2011-08-08
A Watt is the SI unit of power. Namely the rate of energy transfer or conversion equal to one Joule per second. The Joule is the SI unit of energy. Namely the quantity of energy equal to the work done by a force of one Newton applied over a distance of one meter. In turn the Newton is the SI unit of force. Namely the force that applied to a one kilogram inertial mass will cause it to accellerate at a rate of one meter per second per second.

Your electric company would normally charge for energy not power. For various reasons they often bill in units of kilowatt-hours. A kilowatt-hour is equal to 3.6 million Joules. ie you run a one kilowatt heater for a time of one hour, you will be charged for one killowatt hour of energy.

### Post by ve4cib on 2011-08-09
> **NMFTM said:**
> I was reading up a little on how electricity works and this is what I've got so far.

You have watts, amps, and volts and the way it's been explained to me is in terms of water usage, let me explain.

Ampere = unit of physically counting electrons. 1 ampere = 6.25 x 10 to the 18th power number of electrons.

Wrong.  A *coulomb* (C) is 6.25 x 10^18 electrons.  An ampere is 1 C/s.

> Volt = amount of pressure pushing the ampere(s).

Yes.  A volt is defined as the voltage resulting from 1 ampere producing 1 watt of power in a conductor.  (I'll expand on this slightly later.)

A watt can be defined by completely ignoring electronics.  Let's get back to base SI units: kg, m, and s.

A newton (N) is the standard unit of force, measured by multiplying mass by acceleration: N = kg * m/s^2

A joule (J) is the energy corresponding to one newton of force exerted over a distance of 1m: J = N * m = kg * m^2/s^2

A watt (W) is the unit of power, power being defined as energy per second.  One watt is equal to one joule per second: W = J/s = N*m = kg*m^2/s^3

So a watt measures power, or the rate at which energy is applied.

If power can be transmitted by the flow of electrons, and the rate of flow is defined by an ampere, we get the following:

Power = Voltage * Current

Current is measured in C/s (or A), and we know what power is defined as (see above).  Therefore, by simply balancing the units we can see exactly what a volt is:

kg*m^2/s^3 = V * C/s
V = kg*m^2/s^2/C = J/C

So volts measure the energy (J) transmitted by each coulomb.


> So, why does the electric company charge me in watts? You can only actually use X number of actual electrons (amperes) of electricity, just like you can only use actual gallons of water (gallons). The pressure that the water comes into my faucet has no real relation to the physical amount of water I used.

This is a good question.  Not working for an electrical company I cannot answer that.  I suggest it has to do with a combination of economics and the manner in which electricity is generated and transmitted over wires to your house.

As a consumer, I'd prefer to be billed for amps used rather than watts used. The utility company may be less enthusiastic about this idea so they bill by watts used.
