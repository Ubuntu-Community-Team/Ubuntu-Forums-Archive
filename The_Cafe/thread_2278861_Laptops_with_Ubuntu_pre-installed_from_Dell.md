### Post by mattharris4 on 2015-05-19
People reading my posts here may have noticed my comments on Dell pre-installing Ubuntu on some of their computers.  Until very recently their Ubuntu computers were $1300 plus.  That has been rectified now.  They have 15" laptops with Ubuntu pre-installed for $249 and up.  The cheaper Dells with Ubuntu unfortunately come with Celeron and Pentium CPUs and they don't have any Ubuntu laptops with i3, i5 or i7 processors in the $400-$700 range like they do with Windows computers (yet, anyway) but the Pentium used here score OK on Passmark tests as the Pentium CPU in the $279 model scored a 1758 which is adequate so for things like web browsing and word processing they will probably be fine for many people (although if they had an i3 or better I would recommend spending the extra money for one as an i3 is more future-proof than a low-line Pentium).  I do not recommend the Celeron version of this laptop, that CPU scored 1003 on Passmark and you only save $30 by buying a laptop with that CPU in it.  These computers come with 4GB of RAM and a 500GB hard drive, both of which are adequate for most people today.  Here is a link:  [http://www.dell.com/us/p/inspiron-15-3551-laptop-ubuntu/pd?ref=PD_OC](http://www.dell.com/us/p/inspiron-15-3551-laptop-ubuntu/pd?ref=PD_OC) .  I have not used these computers myself but I think the Pentium version would be a good buy at $279 (or $349 with an optical drive) if someone wanted to use Ubuntu and not have to install it himself.  The version of Ubuntu used in these as of May 2015 is version 14.04 LTS SP1.

They also have 14" laptops from $189, the Pentium versions are $249 and $300.

If anyone buys one of these computers please post a review [here]("http://ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=1543006") after using it for a couple of weeks.  It may help others considering these computers to decide whether to buy one or not.s

If you live in a Western country outside of the US (Western Europe also including the UK, Jersey, Alderney and Guernsey; Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Greenland, Iceland, Japan, S. Korea, Singapore) you can likely use these in your home country with a simple plug adapter for the power block (almost all power blocks are capable of handling both 110-125VAC and 220-240VAC current and either 50 or 60 Hz).  US wireless adapters are usually also licensed for use in Western countries as well so that is not a concern, actually there are extra channels in many countries that are illegal for use in the US but all US channels work (Western wireless adapters can also legally be used by tourists in China of all places although I would not attempt to ship US technology there if I were a resident, some of it is embargoed due to China being a Communist country and I do not know how this affects consumer-level computers -- obviously if it is manufactured there as a couple of brands of computer (here's looking at you, Acer/Gateway) are and unaltered you should be able to ship it back).  

You might have to pay customs duty and VAT (the UK is notorious for this when having anything imported into the country) to import a computer but for a $300US computer VAT only adds about $75US or so to the price plus shipping from the US to your home country.  As an example shipping into the UK should be possible for less than $50US (I have shipped there in the past although not specifically a laptop), you can contact the US Postal Service to request a shipping estimate for 15 pounds to your home country or maybe even get it online at [www.usps.com]("http://www.usps.com") .  If this interests you and you live in what is considered a Western country the next steps are contacting your home country's customs service for an estimate of duty and VAT payable, the US Postal Service for an estimate to ship 15 pounds to your country, add the two to the converted price and if it is still a passable deal (as it would likely be to the UK for example) contact Dell regarding a possible purchase and shipment to you.  You can pay with a VISA or MasterCard if Dell is willing to do this for you.  

Good luck, please post back with the results but I suspect that if you live in a Western country you should not have a problem buying a computer from here in the US.

EDIT:  Don't know if it helps but there is a blogger that was able to get a Dell shipped to his home in Russia.  That is a shocker to me but looking at his Ubuntu article history he has been buying Dells since at least 2006 and having them shipped there.

### Post by steve208 on 2015-07-03
I bought the 4 GB Pentium model with Ubuntu pre-installed from Dell recently. So far it has exceeded my expectations. Not a bad machine at all, especially considering the very reasonable price (around $300 US with all taxes and shipping). I still like to support Zareason, but I had to get one of these Dell Ubuntu machines just to see if they screwed it up or what. No complaints after two weeks so far. I figured I would wipe the drives clean and install from scratch, but so far I am still running the pre-installed OS to see what Dell has done with it. I am pleasantly surprised for now.

EDIT 2015-07-26: Dell is still Dell (and Dell still prefers Windows...) A bogus update from a Dell repository has attempted to brick my machine. Kernel panic and a consistent refusal to boot at all. Tried modifying settings in the bios to see if I could get any boot at all, but nada. I was planning on wiping the drives eventually - Dell forced my hand. The laptop not having a CD/DVD drive and messing with UEFI made the task more complicated, but the machine is alive and well running Fedora 21 (my Ubuntu on a stick installer seems to be broken - the installer kept crashing.)
Before the reinstall, df listed some partitions as OS with a file system type vfat... these were in addition to the expected partitions for Ubuntu... Makes me wonder what Dell was up to.

tl;dr Spend a little extra and support Zareason or another dedicated Linux provider. Keep the money in our community, as the saying goes.
