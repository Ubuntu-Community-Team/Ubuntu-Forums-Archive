### Post by ZylGadis on 2007-01-27
[http://www.fsf.org/]("http://www.fsf.org/")

This is Free software. Open-Source software shares most of the features of Free software, but there are some important differences. In fact, OSS as a term was established about 10 years ago, while FS is about 20 years old.

I won't talk about the differences, you can find out about that yourself.

Also look for the ideology of the Free/Open/Net BSD people - that is a third fraction, even though they are a minority even inside FLOSS.

FLOSS, by the way, stands for Free/Libre Open-Source Software, and is a term aiming to encompass all of these fractions, despite the crucial differences between them.

Read about all of the above, and decide whether you are actually looking for OSS, FS, BSD, or the entire FLOSS. Nobody would disagree with the definition of OSI, but, remember, that is only the definition of OSS. There is no official definition of the entire FLOSS, afaik.

### Post by Artemis3 on 2007-01-27
I would not consider the *bsd people a "third fraction" as their license does fit within the Free Software definition. They are simply not preventing their software from becoming non free by third parties. The OSS group on the other hand, are for some reason pissed with the idea of freedom or people having ideals, and only want to show technical merits. Reading their definition leads to basically the same as Free Software, but using many more complicated words.

Compare them here:

[http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw.html](http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw.html)
[http://www.opensource.org/docs/definition.php](http://www.opensource.org/docs/definition.php)

Open Source can also be a term used by non OSI people to promote non free software, as some microsoft "shared" licences or other variations which allow access to the code but no rights to modify, compile, distribute, etc.

When you talk about "bsd like licenses" it is always Free Software as defined by the FSF, meaning they grant you the 4 liberties; the backbone of the Free Software philosophy.

FLOSS is a very confusing acronym, i suggest you avoid it like the plague.

### Post by koenn on 2007-01-27
There is no official definition. 
There is some historical context. Unix (by AT&T, I think), was sometimes sold with a "source" license - allowing the end-user to modify the source code and recompile it, to patch bugs or to add functionality. The university of Berkely, California, was one of the first to make extensive use of such a license, to the extend that they created their own " berkeley software distribution" - BSD. That must have been somewere in the 60s or 70s.  It was not entirely "open" or "free" under todays definitions, I just mention it to show this goes way back. 
It was also quit common for unix sysadmins to program their own tools, and pass them on to fellow unix sysadmins - in the form of source code so they could be compiled to eun on different systems. 

At some point, some guy (Stallman ?)  was unhappy with the way things were going in the unix world (probably something to do with pattents, or copyrights, or commercial differences between unix vendors - I don't know but the fsf website should have something on this) and set about to create a "free" alternative to unix : GNU. Free as in free speach, liberty, not free beer, gratis.
"free software" was defined by the GPL. 

Problem with GPL was the ambiguity of the term free. Most people understood it as "gratis", you get it for free. So opponents of free software would claim this was 
a/ communist
b/ a bad thing : business would not be able to make money from creating software, and thus the whole software industry, and therefore the development of (newer, innovative ...) software woluld come to a stand still". 
So Free software got bad press over a misinterpretation of the word free, and arguing the difference between free speech and free beer stood in the way of arguing that free software actually was capabale of producing quality applications. To overcome this (and probably to get some of the GNU ideology out of the way), Open Source software began being used as an alternative term for  (GNU-style) free software. Later on, Open Source Software was more or less "defined" by the Open Source Initiative.

Between all that, people and groups turning out free/open/ ... software still needed a way to indicate under what condations they released their software. GPL was quite common, but now exists in 2 or 3 versions. Some found GPL to far stretching and designed their own license - Apache has a specific license, *BSD probably has, Xfree or Xorg used to have a spicific license,  I think firefox/mozilla and some other major applications also use a custom license, and so on. 


So there is no real official defination, although most people will agree that "freeware" (software distributed in compiled form but  distributed "for free")  has nothing to do with free sofware, open source, or anything related.  

see also : [http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/categories.html](http://www.gnu.org/philosophy/categories.html) -

Actually, the word "free" in the english language is inaccurate. It does not distinguish between free of charge and freedom.  That's why the term* free-libre open source software* is the most acurate way to describe it.
