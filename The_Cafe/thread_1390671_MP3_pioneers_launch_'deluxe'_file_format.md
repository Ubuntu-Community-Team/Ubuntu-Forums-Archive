### Post by tjwoosta on 2010-01-26
> 
Here's a question though: what if you first ripped a song from an audio CD to 320kbps mp3, then you make a new audio cd with that song on it, and then rip it again, this time in .flac format.

would there be sound quality loss? 

.mp3, .ogg, .acc = lossy 

Even if you use the highest quality settings with a lossy format, the copy will not be as good quality as the original, although it may not be noticeable. Transcoding a lossy format to any other lossy format will cause even more quality loss, even if its to a higher bitrate then before. Transcoding from lossy to lossless wont cause any more quality loss, however if you were to try and transcode that back to lossy again you would lose quality.


.flac, .alac, .ape = lossless

Rip a cd to a lossless format and you get a perfect quality copy. You can transcode lossless to any other lossless format as many times as you want and not lose any quality. 

The only bad thing about lossless is that it takes up the most space, this is why lossy exists. Lossy uses compression methods to make the files smaller.



So to answer your question, yes. When you ripped the audio cd to 320kbps .mp3 there would have been minor quality loss from the original, but probably not noticeable even on good equipment because 320kbps is good even if it is lossy. Now you burn a new cd and rip it again in .flac format, this would be theoretically the same quality as the cd your ripping it from, but still not as good as the original copy.


> [quote]Frequencies above 17 kHz disappear and sometimes you get a phasing effect which can be annoying.
huh? [/quote]


Lossy formats use compression methods to make the files smaller. Certain frequencies that were present in the original will not be present in the lossy copy.
