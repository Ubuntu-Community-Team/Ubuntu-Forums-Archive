### Post by gnomeuser on 2010-03-14
I have the HTC Hero and the Nokia N900. My observations on HTC is that they release poor tested updates and since they have to port their own Sense UI every time the updates are also rare and have a tendency to be big. They also force you to upgrade using Windows and only Windows. Avoid HTC at all costs. The phone itself is slow, the Android applications generally are decent but lack integration with each other. For the price you'd normally pay while a decent phone the Hero isn't the best you can get and I would avoid it.

You should also give thought to how much typing you will be doing on the device and if you are truly comfortable using the on screen keyboard or you want a hardware keyboard. I thought I could make due with touchscreen keyboards but the keys turned out to be very small, inaccurate and slow to react on the Hero and I craved a hardware keyboard like my Nokia n810 has.

You should also ensure that the device does all standard upgrades over the air or you are likely to get tied down to specific applications to flash them, often ones that only run on specific versions of Windows. 

For Android the centralised account system with Google is really great, there still are things that don't work quite right but having a device where you don't have to configure your email, calendar and so on is really a great experience. It makes the device feel natural and like something that is pleasant to use.

I replaced my Hero with the Nokia N900, it isn't an Android phone, but the Maemo (soon to be MeeGo) OS is a full Linux, even in ways that Android is not. It uses everything I have on my Ubuntu desktop. It is also a powerful and professional device with a good hardware keyboard. The UI is intelligently designed, easy to use and feels elegant with a touch of subtle bling. You do miss the features of the centralized account system from Android and the wealth of applications for Maemo isn't that huge, however they are useful - I now have a phone with a Tomboy clone e.g. though it doesn't sync using Ubuntu One much to my disappointment as that would be really cool. The Web browser on the N900 is really well designed, it isn't that fast and since the device isn't multitouch pinch zoom is replaced with the rather less "manly" twirling. This works really well and the whole browser is so easy to use and naturally designed it is a pleasure to browse on the N900. The Media player is also really kickass, it uses Tracker to index and organize your files, acts like a regular USB mass storage device. It is quick, elegant and featureful where it matters. All it lacks is some Podcast support and Audiobook handling to be added to it's greatness.

Finally on the iPhone and Android devices Skype (an skype like applications) aren't allowed to use the data connection making them utterly worthless. As this is a major use case for me, I really value that the N900 doesn't have these kinds of restrictions. You don't even have to hack it to get root, just install a package. Tethering should be possible but I haven' tried that. Nokia maintains that it is your device which is refreshing.

I really would recommend looking at the N900 it is a super device and if you like Linux it runs it pretty much the same way as your desktop does. If you want Android the Nexus has cool hardware, updates over the air regularly and generally follows Android upstream closely. If you like me really want a hardware keyboard the Motorola Milestone is what you want (The Droid as sole in Europe with multitouch).

I have in depth reviews of both the N900 and the Hero on my blog (link in signature) and will continue to post updates there on my smartphone adventures.
