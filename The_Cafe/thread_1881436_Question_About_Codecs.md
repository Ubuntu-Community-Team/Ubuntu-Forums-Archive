Presumably, the cost of the codecs would be included in the price of the PC, tablets, phones, etc., when Ubuntu is installed by the OEM.  The price would be passed on to the enduser that way.

Right now, you can buy the licenses for the codecs in the Ubuntu Software Center if you are living in a country where they are restricted by patent laws.  They are part of the "Fluendo Complete Playback Pack."  By buying the codecs, you can watch DVDs, listen to MP3s, etc., all to your heart's content. 

The guy who makes Linux Mint chooses to disregard the laws of the United States, Japan, and other countries. That's a business decision he's decided to make.  Canonical could make the same decision (and run the risk of potential lawsuits), or they could pay for the codecs for each and every user who downloads Ubuntu (which would be very expensive for an OS they're giving away for free), or they could do what they do now.

You haven't really offered a convincing case for why they should change what they're doing.

### Post by aysiu on 2011-11-15
Ubuntu has two commitments in terms of being free--being cost-free (money) and being open source (freedom).

Read more about it here:
[http://www.ubuntu.com/project/about-ubuntu/our-philosophy](http://www.ubuntu.com/project/about-ubuntu/our-philosophy)

That said, every time I've seen Ubuntu preinstalled by a major OEM (HP or Dell, for example), it's come with proprietary codecs. I guess the OEM ponies up for the licensing fees?

Also, you can install the codecs yourself quite simply by installing the metapackage *ubuntu-restricted-extras*.
