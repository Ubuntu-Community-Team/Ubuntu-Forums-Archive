### Post by skwishybug on 2007-11-25
I run an anti-virus. Why? There may come a day when my bank's computers crash due to a virus either preventing from getting--or wiping out entirely any record of--my money that might have been caused by an e-mail that passed through my computer with a virus on it.

While it would (might?) not have been perpetuated by my computer running Linux, it may have been a bulk e-mail that hid the virus that as a responsible person, I could then alert friends and family who also got the e-mail of the virus threat.

As with the drive to eliminate malicious code from the posts on these forums, we all have a responsibility to stem and hopefully eliminate the spread of computing viruses. And like the trolls who post malicious code to "fix" other people's computers, the makers of computing viruses may never be defeated, but they will have a much harder time if everyone is watching for them rather than shutting into their little shells and telling the world to fend for themselves.

Users of Linux and FOSS talk about having free and open access to software for everyone and that the use of closed and proprietary code hurts the computing community on the whole need to remember that free and open access includes information. While we may not be at (as big a) risk of getting the viruses that are floating around, if we truly believe in free and open source computing should be willing to do our part and scan files for viruses and alert those who may also have gotten it of their existence.

When broken right down FOSS, and open access to software really means sharing information--openly sharing the information on how a program works (through allowing access to the source code). But why should we limit ourselves to sharing only information of software packages and how they work? If a friend, or colleague, or your own mother sent you an e-mail that was an obvious scam telling you how great an opportunity it was--and actually believing it, would you not reply to them alerting them to the scam? Then why stop there? Clam AV, Avast, AVG, these packages run in the background and (for most) do very little to interfere with the operation of the system (unlike Norton for example). Do you not owe it to your mother to let her know she has a virus on her computer when you get that e-mail from her?

"With great power comes great responsibility." The line from *Spider-man* rings true. Linux offers its users great power over their computer with an almost assured immunity to the perils of the web. Should we not embrace the responsibility with it to help those who, for various, reasons cannot, will not, or have not embraced Linux?
