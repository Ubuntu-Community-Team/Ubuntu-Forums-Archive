### Post by keithpeter on 2011-11-19
> **cbowman57 said:**
> It's really only non-standard to us.  

A lot of people would just look at it as if they were using a toaster or a microwave oven.

Which is why I think tablets (ok, iPads) are proving popular. You just directly swipe icons and stuff happens. The 'affordances' of the device are represented visually, in the same way that a toaster has a slot and a knob to say how burned you like your toast.

GUI based 'full computers' have a more complex set of functions and depend on metaphors. One example of the barriers to use involved in the desktop metaphor might be the idea of a window itself...

People who have very limited experience with computers (tends to be older people where I live) will need to pick up the idea of a window as being a small part of a screen with a border. So an instruction like 'move that window' won't be understood automatically - the screen just appears as one large colour field. This seems to apply especially to dialog boxes that pop up as you click on other parts of the interface, and that might be some distance away.

A popular first session has always been messing about in Paint (windows) or TuxPaint. Do a wacky picture, save the picture file, print in colour. You have a fun session which develops mouse control, gets across the idea of toolbars and windows, files and saving, and seems to release the inner child in many adults. 

Just try facilitating such a session and listening to the questions that participants ask you and you will understand what I'm on about...
