### Post by DrMega on 2008-02-25
[QUOTE=suibhne;4395968}I have been thinking a bit more and I suppose I really mean, what do the proprietary models do better than the non-proprietary? 

There must be something?![/QUOTE]

From my point of view, there are a couple of things:

1. Development environments are better in Windows. I know people criticise MS Visual Studio, and it has its faults but it is all there, I can edit code, design forms, link to the backend DB, check files into and out of source control, all from one place.

2. Device drivers (especially for less popular devices), in two years I have never been able to make my TV tuner work (I'm not blaming anyone for this).

I suspect part of the problem in my second point is that sometimes it is legally impossible for the manufacturers to release source for the drivers in case it compromises some patent. For example, in my afforementioned TV tuner, it has built in hardware mpeg and dvix encoding. As these are proprietary formats the manufacturer is no doubt concerned that releasing the source for the drivers might reveal technical details about the mpeg and divx formats (as if that info isn't already widely available).

### Post by p_quarles on 2008-02-26
> **sumguy231 said:**
> Partially true, but it's really just like any other platform: the commercial games are generally of a higher quality than free games. Which is rather odd because in most other software categories free software is able to match or exceed the quality of commercial software, but when it comes to games it seems like most are developed by people with no skills and short attention spans.
This is partly because games, unlike nearly every other category of software, are almost exclusively developed as shelfware. A great deal of GNU/Linux stuff works so well because there are huge companies (e.g., IBM, Google) behind it. They don't develop it specifically to sell, but because they need the software to work well for their business models. They make improvements, and those improvements are then made available to all other commercial and community distros that wish to make use of them. 

The business model behind game development, on the other hand, depends entirely on having a product to sell. I hope and expect that we will start seeing more native Linux games in the near future, but it's very unlikely that any of them will be open source.
