### Post by Snapdragon19 on 2010-08-18
How about this: **files** are like little packets of information that computers understand. Lots of **files** can go together to create a **program**. **Programs** allow your computer to simulate things you might have in real life but with all the advantages of being computerized. For example the program '**Microsoft Word**' simulates paper, but its advantage over real paper is the ability to edit it as much as you want. **Folders**: think of **folders** as bags to hold several files and/or programs in. They can help you organize things on your computer in the same way a real filing cabinet helps you organize all your documents. Hope this helped.

### Post by Eldera on 2010-08-18
For a book, I second the suggestion of Psionic Stranger: Windows 7 for Dummies.

I have never used Windows 7, so I'll give you some personal advice from my experience in learning XP and let you decide what still applies. One of the things that I found difficult in learning to use computers is that there are usually two or more right ways to do the same operation. 

If your Mom was taught to do housework in the same way I was taught by my Grandmother, there was only one right way to do something: One washed and then ironed. (One really can't iron and then wash.) When making a cake, one mixed the "drys" in one bowl, the "wets" in another and then combined them. The "One Right Way" mentality was developed without my even thinking about it. Your mother may be looking for the one right way to do things on the computer and it does not apply there. I found this very confusing.

For example, files are something with which one works. One creates them, modifies them, or deletes them. Programs or "apps" are the tools with which one works on the files. If one wants to work on just one file, one can start by finding that file (Documents>sub-directory>file name) and clicking on it will by default open it with the tools (program) most appropriate to use to do something with that file. 

One can also take out your tools first (Open a program) and then create a new file or open an existing one from within the program. EX: open Word and then open a file from the "File" Menu.

You can get the thing you are working on out first and then get out your tools, or you can get out your tools first and then the thing you are going to work on. Two good ways to do the same thing. 

If you don't know whether your Mom has a "One Right Way" mindset, why not sit down and discuss it with her? I actually had to re-adjust my thought patterns when I started using a computer. I started by finding one good way to do something and ignoring the rest.

It has been so many years since I used Outlook that I can't comment. For IE perhaps you could set up a lot of bookmarks of her favorite Websites as crutches until she gains some more experience. How many ways does she open IE? Help her find One way with which she is comfortable and stick with it.

I wish both of you well. I have been enjoying computers for nine years now. Don't get discouraged and don't give up.

Eldera

### Post by Eldera on 2010-08-19
You certainly have plenty of answers! May I suggest you divide them into these categories:

1: Things that will help her right now. Examples: Info on the difference between folders and programs; how to open and navigate IE; how to access, store, create and delete an email message in Outlook.

2: Things that she might be interested in later. Examples; What a hard drive is; what RAM is.

3: Things she may never need to know. Example; A detailed history of how computers were made.

Too much information at one time could be overwhelming and counter productive.

Get your Mom to ask you some questions. Perhaps you can find out what is really bothering her and work from there.

Ask your Mom whether she would prefer a book or some videos. She is the one who will be using the material. 

I have never read "Windows 7 for Dummies". I found "XP for Dummies" very helpful when I started learning how to use a computer. It was written in such a manner that one could read a chapter on what one was interested in and not read the whole book. Very useful. Since they are both by the same author, I am guessing they are both written the same way. Perhaps your public library has a copy and you could check it out before buying one.

Have a great day. Eldera
