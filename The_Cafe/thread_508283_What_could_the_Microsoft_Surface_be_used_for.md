### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2007-07-24
Don't need Microsoft's surface. In fact, you probably don't want it since it costs a lot more money than other equally usable multitouch solutions. (And yes, we know the method they use).
You can't knock it completely, though. They have found a really visually attractive implementation of this technology, and it appears fantastically responsive. Luckily, the mainstream is split between whether MS or Apple "invented" multitouch (even though they both use completely different techniques that are not in any way similar except that they take multiple touch points), so the crowd may see a bit beyond those easy misconceptions and realize that they are merely building with existing technologies...

Try [www.nuigroup.com](www.nuigroup.com) for the open community that is focused on multitouch tables. Building your own is surprisingly easy! ($200 if you own a projector or an LCD screen that you are willing to canibalize). I highly recommend the Diffused Illumination method vs. FTIR, although FTIR does have more tutorials. DI is easier, cheaper and gives better results than FTIR.
A Diffused Illumination table (like Surface) can recognize things that are not living human appendages, which makes it very useful for very cool things that connect physical and computer interaction. (For example, using real paint brushes in a paint program, or having the desktop recognize peripherals placed upon it and popping up a quick tutorial for how to use them, or tagging objects and having those tags recognized by the table.

[MPX](http://wearables.unisa.edu.au/mpx/) is a modified X server that manages multitouch, both for legacy and current applications. (Of course, the legacy apps don't get shape recognition and are designed for boring, cluttery single touch interfaces, but they are still pretty nice).
