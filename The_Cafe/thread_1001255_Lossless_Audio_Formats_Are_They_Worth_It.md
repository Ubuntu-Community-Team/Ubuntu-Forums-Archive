### Post by Sinkingships7 on 2008-12-04
> **Eisenwinter said:**
> I'd like to raise another question here.

I have an mp3 file.

If I take that file, convert to WAV or FLAC, then convert it to OGG, will it sound better than converting it straight up from mp3 to ogg?

No. In every case, you cannot improve the quality of a media file no matter what you do, or how hard you try. If you start with a lossless file, then cut it to a 320kbps mp3 file, you will lose quality. If you then cut it to a 256kbps aac file, you will lose quality. If you then convert it to a 320kbps mp3 again, *you will still lose quality*.

The point is that every conversion can only make it worse by chopping off bits and bits. It can't add the original sound back in.

EDIT: I should add that there are artificial ways to "add sound back in", but it's only for a trick on the ears; it doesn't actually improve the quality of the file.

### Post by MikeTheC on 2008-12-04
My only portable digital media players are iPods, so therefore the only formats I can play in that context would be AAC and MP3. Naturally, I choose MP3.

Without a doubt, bit rate is a major factor in sound playback quality. Now, that being said, it also depends on the quality setting of the encoder used. I have heard 128 bit files that were horribly slurry, and ones which were excellent. Naturally, if you're ripping your own stuff, then you're in complete control of that.

As for your question about differences in processing power (i.e. "battery consumption") between playing a lossless format vs. a lossy one, I would think more processing power is required for a lossy format, given it has to sit there and kind of re-encode a non-lossy playable data stream (for the benefit of pushing out to your speakers), whereas a lossless format can probably be very nearly dumped straight to the speakers. In reality, however, my guess is that the processing power differential is negligible, principally because the CPUs in these systems (along with their related firmware) is so extremely optimized for this kind of task.

### Post by pp. on 2008-12-06
> **Eisenwinter said:**
> If I burn the mp3 files as an audio CD, (cda format, I think), and then rip them into FLAC, will it still sound worse when I convert them to ogg?

Yes, it will, because the signal already has been damaged by the conversion to MP3. Nothing you can do will undo that damage. You can add to the damage, though.

> **psusi said:**
> A good lossy codec is one where you can't tell the difference between it and the original.  If you can't tell the difference, then the question is no different than asking if a tree falls in the woods, and nobody is around to hear, did it make a sound?  Well sure, technically, but who cares?

Actually, you should have written "where you can't tell the difference ***ceteris paribus***", meaning "when everything else remains unchanged".

Things that may change after the lossy compression:
[LIST]
[*]You want to play your music for someone with more accurate or better trained hearing
[*]You buy better equipment, starting from the playback device and ending with the speaker or phones
[*]You train your listening capabilities
[*]You start actually listening to your music and do so in a quiet environment.
[/LIST]

I agree that you might get away with conservatively applying a lossy compression.


> **Shwefty said:**
> Short answer, no.  The human ear can only hear a frequency of about 20 kHz.  To prevent aliasing in the signal, 40 kHz is used.  .mp3 is 44.1 kHz.  Past that it's bit rate and frankly I just can't hear that quickly.

Short answer, wrong.

Compression is not about the sampling rate but about the number of bits of storage used for a given duration of sound. Hence, 44.1 kHz is the sampling rate which may or may not suffice for reproducing the desired signals with their harmonics and phases. CD quality is at nominally 16 bits for each sample. That yields 44.1*2*16*1000 bits per second, not counting the overhead for the framing and error correction and such, of course.
