### Post by hoppipolla on 2009-10-23
This is just something that occurred to me a second ago. I realized that most of the plugins that we commonly use, and also some things about the overall desktop environments that we use, are created in such a way as to make the desktop feel more "natural".

Three great examples I can think of sitting here are wobbly windows, the cube and window dodge. Another very good example is the nature of the default Ubuntu desktop.

By contrast, many of the Windows and Mac visual features don't have this quality. Windows often feels professional and quite rigid, with very functional features with a specific purpose (with the possible exception of Aero translucency). Mac is more stylised and refined, almost posh in it's appearance.

I dunno, I think the style of our desktops reflects the nature of open source and the kinds of things everyday people tend to create to "improve" their desktop experience. Window dodge for example is wonderful and works well with wobbly windows :)

What do you think? Is there this pattern to the open source desktops and Compiz/Kwin plugins we keep seeing, or is there a different one?

Hoppi :)

### Post by Firestem4 on 2009-10-23
I happen to agree with you Hopp.

Windows and Mac want to make you do something a certain way. This way is in how they've developed and defined their desktop environments. Users become adjusted because 'that is the way it is' and theres nothing that can be done about. Open Source DE's on the other hand are created by people who want to use a desktop that is easy to use and not defined by a corporate identity. This allows change and evolution geared toward the ways humans interact with desktops.

To me the flexibility of open source desktops allows us to turn the desktop into something that is most comfortable to us. 
Open Source desktops however follow a natural evolution of what users want from it, while still pushing the edge (eg: KDE4x and Gnome 3). This type of development is more natural and its not defined by a corporation trying to maintain and push a specific feel of usability. 

Mac and windows both have their own direction they want to go with their Desktop. And it is evident because each new iteration is largely a more advanced regurgitation of the predecessor; clearly expressed in XP to Vista to 7.
