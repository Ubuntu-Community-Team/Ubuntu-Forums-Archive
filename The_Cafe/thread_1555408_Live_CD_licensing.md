No-one said it is illegal to sell ubuntu cd's (or any other operating system or software released under the GPL license).
As both me and earthpigg said, it is **perfectly legal** for you to sell the cd, but you are NOT allowed to put ANY restrictions on the use of the cd. If the original code (the linux kernel and other software) is released under the GPL licence, you are **not** allowed to sell it under **any other license**.

Putting a restriction on how many machines the buyer of the software is allowed to install on is (at least as far as I understand the GPL license) a violation of the license, so you can't do it.
But you *are* allowed to sell a **software support** contract (where the customer pays you to help him/her with the software), and I'm sure you can find a legal way of making that kind of support valid for only one machine without violating the terms of an open source license...

### Post by Dr. C on 2010-08-19
> **komitaltrade said:**
> ...

So, did somebody know the answer?

There are two very different issues here. Copyrights and Trademarks. FLOSS licensing is about copyright licensing. You have the right to distribute the software under the terms of the respective FLOSS license but you may not use a trademark for example "Ubuntu" or "RedHat" to describe your distributed software without the permission of the trademark owner. 

The respective Trademark policies for [Ubuntu]("http://www.ubuntu.com/aboutus/trademarkpolicy") and [RedHat]("http://www.redhat.com/about/companyprofile/trademark/") restrict commercial exploitation of the trademark, and also provide permissions for certain non commercial distribution. 

In addition one must keep in mind that for commercial distribution of [GPL]("http://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl.html") software section 6c does not apply and one cannot for example rely Canonical's offer to provide source code under the GPL, to meet the GPL source code requirements to distribute commercially GPL binaries from the Ubuntu distribution.
