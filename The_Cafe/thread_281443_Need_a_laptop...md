### Post by Miguel on 2006-10-21
Hi Iarwain,

IMHO, you are focusing the "problem" from the wrong point of view. Buying a notebook is totally different from buying a desktop. Instead of specifying the specs first, you have to decide what kind of laptop you want. You have to choose amongs the following:
[list]
[*] Ultraportable (11~12 inch, around 1.3 kg). Mainly for very frequent travellers. Lack power and features. Very expensive
[*] Thin-and-light (13.3 or 14.1 widescreen or 14 normal, around 2.0 kg). These usually have all the features expected, while still light. Enterprise quality laptops are expensive and light. Not-so-light laptops are cheaper.
[*] Mainstream (15.4' and around 2.9 kg). Most notebooks sold belong here. Decent size, lots of extras. They are nearly a desktop replacement, though they are not nearly as portable as an enterprise-grade thin-and-light.
[*] Desktop replacement (17 inch widescreen, around a ton). The concept for this is having a large screen and a large keyboard (some even have a numeric keyboard too), plus as much features as possible. High-end GPU's are usually found here. Same for TV cards. Where is the catch? The laptop is not really something you want to carry around, and battery life is usually abismal.
[/list]

This is it. The size you are buying (budget excluded) depends on how much you will be lugging around the beast. If you just want something you can carry ocasionally with you (e.g. when school ends), get the big and bad ones. If you attend lectures with your laptop daily, it's either thin-and-light or a light 15.4 (such as a MacBook pro), though this is more personal.

And then, you choose specs. Of course, most ultraportable come with low-voltage or ultra-low voltage processors that compromise performance, very few 13.3 laptops come with dedicated video card (the Sony SZ is one, but starts at... 1700€?).

### Post by Miguel on 2006-10-21
*Warning: this post is a work in progress. I'm putting off my head interesting laptops with a dedicated graphics card, while the links for reviews will come probably later. *

13.3 inches widescreen:
[list][*] **Sony Vaio SZ**. Light, cool, and... expensive. Might not suit the resolution, though.
[/list]
14.1 inches widescreen:[list]
[*] **ASUS W3**: Extremely sexy, thin and not too heavy (2.4 kg with everything AFAIK). The price is high, though. Some say it's a bit too hot. ASUS has another similar but cheaper, not-so-thin model.
[*] **HP nc6400**: Thin and light (2.0kg). Enterprise quality, which means good build quality, a stick and matte screen. A dedicated ATi X1300 is available as an option.
[*] **Dell Latitude D620**: An enterprise dell. Nothing superb, but should do de trick. Ultracustomizable. Some users are complanin about screen quality (link [here](http://forums.us.dell.com/supportforums/board/message?board.id=latit_video&message.id=23548&view=by_date_ascending&page=1)).
 The worse built **Inspiron 640m** doesn't have a dedicated GPU.
[/list]

In 15.4 inches widescreen some options are: [list]
[*]**HP Pavilion dv6000t**. This month's most popular notebook in notebookreview.com
[*] The (probably much) more expensive mobile workstation **hp nw8440** and the business **hp nc8430**
[*] [b]Thinkpad T60[b]: The first widescreen thinkpads, by Lenovo. Very professional, very sturdy, expensive and not as light as expected. There is also a 14 inch version.
[*] **MacBook Pro** Ultrathin, very light and metallic. You don't get Windows with this one ;).
[*] An Acer Travelmate whose model number I don't remember.
[/list]

Some non-widescreen models are the ASUS V5 (and its more expensive Lamborghini cousin) and most Thinkpads (can't go wrong with these).

Here you have a starting point. You will see that most, if not all, of these notebooks come from the business and enterprise model lines. This is because these models usually come with a better build and keyboard, as it is the main input. I would imagine a programmer values a good keyboard. I'm not saying consumer notebooks will have bad keyboards, but it is definitely a common trend in certain manufacturers (Dell, HP come to mind).
