### Post by neorou on 2007-06-12
So my work colleagues and I have this ongoing discussion. The topic: "why don't users just RTFM?".

Our institute use Macintosh and Windows computers exclusively. I introduced ubuntu linux into our system and have gotten mixed responses. We need the ubuntu boxes to run massive biological data sets and there are no solutions out there that run on either the Mac or on Windows. The ones available are very expensive and does not allow any extensive reconfiguration. 
The higher IT admins reluctantly allowed us to use our ubuntu boxes.

Somebody told me the other day, linux is only good for people who know how to RTFM. Well, there are lots of Mac and Windows users who should RTFM and they don't. They call when they run into problems. I wish I would tell them to "RTFM!", but I can't.  These people pay my salary.:p

Of all the distros out there, I find that Ubuntu provides the best set of documentation in general.  I haven't yet found a good set of docs for beginners. Most users end up not wanting to look for the manual as they often have to when trying out something new. When they do find documentation (like the one available on the main ubuntu.com site), they get overwhelmed after a while and stop their reading.

The most common complaint that people have is overcoming that great hurdle that is 'getting started'. Once people get past that stage, things become easier for them and they can find documentation and solutions on their own.  I feel like the saddest thing about all of this is the documentation is available, and people are not wanting to put some effort into getting to it.

On the one hand, I feel like I don't need to worry about finding a job, but on the other hand, I wish that the same hand that feeds me would grab a  book and turn a page...
Thoughts? Comments?

### Post by Hendrixski on 2007-06-12
Rule #1 in software development: YOUR USER IS NOT YOU

that means that if you have a habit, like for example reading a manual, that does not mean the user will want to do that.  If the user has a choice (and they do in a capitalist society) then they will chose the option that best fits their needs.  if their needs are to not have to read manuals, that's what they'll chose.

So if RTFM were to be a law in Linux, then all of the people who just want to crank out spreadsheets and documents and don't give a flying sh*t won't bother with Linux.

If it were to be a universal law... then I think it would have more offenders than the speed limit.

Besides... reading manuals is so last century... people prefer videos ... point them to screencasts.ubuntu.com

[LIST]

We are bombarded with information everyday, and reading is the slower of the two forms.  You have to process and then visualize, instead of directly visualizing.  To add to the overhead of reading, you have freakin' lawyers, scientists, teachers, training manual writers, etc fluffing their writing with way more words than needed to get their point across (legal documents...ugh!...nuff said). And that, in a nutshell, is why a training class to learn (some programming language) costs $500-$1000, but only takes a day, while the book costs $50, but takes a week to learn.

### Post by Hobbsee on 2007-06-12
My personal view, and please note that it doesnt represent the ubuntu developer community, or any other group, is this:

- I've done a fair bit of technical support on IRC
- Many of those questions asked can be answered with a factoid, with a link to the documentation
- If i give you a link to a howto, via a factoid or whatever, then I expect you to read it.  Why should I copy&paste what should be right there in front of you, had you actually opened the page and read it?
- If an error says "dpkg failed, please run this command", then i expect you to look at the error, and run said command.  it's typed *right there*.  Not file a bug on it. 
- If you've read said manpage, or documentation, or error message, and have a question based on that, then that's absolutely fine - they can be unclear, particularly on a new topic.
- the main reason that the ubotu (irc bot) database, and associated documentation was written, was so that people could refer to it, and stop asking the same things over and over.
- And basic linux knowledge (maybe, that a bootloader exists, about root and sudo stuff, even an awareness that things will be different), is always helpful, and probably should be a prerequisite for installing linux - do you jump on a mac, and expect everything to work like windows?

Off the soap box for me too...
