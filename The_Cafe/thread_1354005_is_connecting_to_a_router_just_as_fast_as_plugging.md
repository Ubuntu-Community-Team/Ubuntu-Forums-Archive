### Post by issih on 2009-12-13
Unless you have a seriously pokey internet connection and a bad wireless signal, then the bottleneck will be the isp connection rather than anything else.

A relatively standard throughput (according to wikipedia) on a 54g connection is in the region of 22mbit/s. So assuming your broadband pipe is smaller than that, then it shouldn't make a lot of difference.

Obviously there are proviso's concerning other people using the wireless network throughput for local area traffic and hogging the bandwidth, and obviously any local operations will be vastly quicker via cat5, but generally, for web usage, provided the wireless is behaving itself, it should make next to no difference.

Hope that helps

To the OP,  Connecting to a router should require no more configuration than connecting directly to a cable modem.

### Post by issih on 2009-12-13
There is no difference as far as the networking is concerned between different types of web activity...its all just packets of information.

Differences in download speeds are mostly due to the geographical location and internet connection of the servers.

Differences in your ping rate to a single server are usually related to the way your isp provides your broadband locally, the number of users logged on, contention ratios etc.

Its almost certainly not your local networking that causes it. There will probably be a tiny overhead associated with the NAT and so forth, but the difference to the overall rate will be tiny.

All in all unless you are in a university halls with access to a T1 line or similar, just forget about it :)

### Post by Icehuck on 2009-12-13
When dealing with your local network, directly plugging into the lan will always be faster then wireless.
