### Post by disturbed1 on 2008-03-29
I don't believe many in this thread has a clue what the Canonical support services are for, nor do some have any experience with outside support services.

Canonical will not write a driver for you just because you paid $250. The support is only support for applications in the main repo, not universe, nor multiverse. $250 is for **ONE** desktop for one year. It only covers hardware support for hardware that is supported by the kernel. You'll be lucky to even get nVidia binary support since this is a nVidia product with a driver provided and written by nVidia, not Ubuntu. The server support is for **ONE** server, not any of the connected machines, if you want supoort for those connected machines you need to pay $250 per year per desktop that you want support for.

It's basically there for installation and setup support only. Not much else. They'll get you up and running, and help you retrieve your data from the recovery console in the event of a crash, so they can help you install again. If you have a simple question about editing .bashrc files you'll be refered to the wiki, forums, or mailing list.

Some of you will read that with a gaping jaw. That's because it isn't meant for you ;). Imagine attempting to deploy 5 domain controllers, and WebDAV, with NFS, NTP, NNTP, POP, IMAP, servers, that need NFS, SAMBA, and even Apple Talk support, along with Fax on demand, and sql and JBoss databases on fibre networks connecting to 250 local machines and 100 WAN clients. And that's a tiny network install not including local and abroad web servers, nor CRM software. Paying for a little help would be worth it :D

The support is not for a home user. The forum, wiki, and mailing list is.

Microsoft has home support. You're allowed 3 free calls, or 90days after the first call, depends on which version of the OS. After that they charge. If you call up and say hey my Creative Labs Xfi isn't working, you've just used a call, and you'll be forwarded to Creative Labs. Microsoft will only support Microsoft written software and drivers, no 3rd party stuff.

With Microsoft you can call or go just about anywhere and get some type of support on a pay per instance case. Not so with Linux. You are all your own. Or you can rely on the opinion of 250 other people that installed Linux and know even less than you.

Depending on who your VAR is, and how much support you need, in some cases it **IS** cheaper to deploy Windows 2003 servers with XP clients. A Microsoft certified IT staff is cheaper to employ and to find. Certified and properly trained Linux admins are harder to come by, and demand upto 3x the salary.
