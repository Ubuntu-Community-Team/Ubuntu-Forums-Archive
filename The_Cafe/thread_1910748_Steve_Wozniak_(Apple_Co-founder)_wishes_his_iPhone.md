### Post by HappinessNow on 2012-01-17
> ...Wozniak says he also wishes it (iPhone) would do "all the things my Android does, I really do."

Ouch. That hurts, coming from the co-founder of Apple, who is much beloved by those in the Apple kingdom, as well as those outside of it. Wozniak says his main phone is the iPhone and that he loves "the beauty of it" as well as its simplicity, compared to Android. But, he says, Android phones are not that much harder to deal with, and "if you're willing to do the work to understand it a little bit, well I hate to say it, but there&#8217;s more available in some ways."

...Wozniak thinks that voice commands do better on Android than the iPhone, and he told Lyons that Siri on the iPhone 4S is not as good as when it was a stand-alone app for the previous iPhone:...

Heresy? No, it's Wozniak's engineering analysis, detached and unbiased. He says the iPhone is a great choice for many people, including those "who are just scared of computers altogether and don&#8217;t want to use them...."

Nothing shocking or surprising here, just conventional wisdom at it's best.

### Post by gutterslob on 2012-01-21
Imho, for those a bit less computer-centric, Android phones *are* a tad harder to use. My lil' sister is pretty intelligent by most standards, but when she came to visit a couple of weeks ago she couldn't get around using a Galaxy Nexus. She said it felt too overwhelming compared to the first time she used an iPhone.

Personally, I think Android has come a long way in terms of usability, but I think the majority of people who use an Android device (particularly Honeycomb or ICS ones) and come away disappointed are those that expect it to mimic iOS, which it clearly is not. The operating systems might do the same things, but from a cerebral perspective, the two go about their business in different manners.

There's still a long way to go for Android in other areas though. ICS, in my opinion, is their first OS that doesn't feel like a beta product and is on par with iOS in terms of speed and fluidity, but their marketplace is still a turd-filled mess. Many "serious" devs/companies (meaning those that make products for use in professional fields/environments) are still afraid of developing for Android. Part of the reason might be due to the device fragmentation out there; their applications reputation rests on its ability to provide a consistent experience between devices, it just becomes so much harder on Android with every Tosh, Dell and Sammy having a gazillion different offerings. How Google addresses this remains to be seen, but at the moment it seems they don't really care all that much as long as people are seeing their ads.

But then again, what do I know? I got a free Galaxy Nexus from my carrier but still insist on using my N9. I'm not the type that believes in the whole "app-ecosystems enrich our lives" drivel.... oh, and to be honest, I don't really like all the icon and widget overload you get with modern phones either (which is why I probably prefer Meego-Harmattan cos it's the most minimal looking/feeling of em, even if it still uses icons in the applications screen). Horses for courses, I know... just haven't found a horse for my type or course yet. :P

If you told me to choose between Android or iOS at this point in time, I'd probably take Android. The main thing that keeps me away from iDevices is their over-insistance on iTunes. I know that you no longer need iTunes installed on your PC for handset updates or device activation since iOS5, but you still need it to transfer media (or use some Mono infested app as an alternative on Linux). If I can't transfer media files via a usb connection with simple drag+drop actions or (ideally)shell commands, then it's not for me. Other than that, I think iDevices are pretty decent for most people.
