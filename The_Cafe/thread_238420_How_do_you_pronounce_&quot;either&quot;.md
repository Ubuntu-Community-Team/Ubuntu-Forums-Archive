### Post by majesticturkey on 2006-08-17
Either is the same as "the." It depends on the word before it. If the word ends in a consonant sound, you say "aye-ther", but if it's a vowel sound, it's "ee-ther." Of course, that's how it's *supposed* to be, and like all things linguistic, has changed. I usually say "aye-ther."

As for the accent/dialect thing, an accent deals specifically with pronunciation. Dialect deals with other things. In English, the standard Midwest American dialect would say "Me and George are going to the store." Standard English dialect is "George and I are going to the store."

### Post by lex25288 on 2010-08-11
The rule would be the e sound in Standard American  (i.e. hence all over America), the i sound in the UK and Ireland.
People used to follow this rule right up until recently, nowadays it doesn't really matter where you are from.

> **fuscia said:**
> seriously, if there's no lack in clarity, who tf cares how one pronownces or speels a werd?

No offence seriously, but I beg to differ. Words are supposed to be pronounced one way or another not only because of their spelling but also because of their etymology. So I am sorry but I'd rather you spelt werd as word, speels as spells and pronownces as pronounces.
As for the difference between "accents" and "dialects": pronouncing a word in a certain way consistent with a certain region is referred to as an accent (mostly in the US) whereas using a different word rather than the one that would be the norm is referred to as dialect.

E.g.
bath pronounced with a broad a (which is the norm in Southern England) instead of the American pronunciation is considered accent.
Saying beleft instead of believed (used pretty much throughout Kent UK), where believed is clearly the norm is an example of dialect.
