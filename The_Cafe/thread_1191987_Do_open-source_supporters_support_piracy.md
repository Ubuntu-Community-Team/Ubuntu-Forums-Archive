### Post by KOld Iron on 2009-06-19
Actually there is no relation at all, unfortunately terms are being used inaccurately (again):

- Open Source does NOT imply, that it is free of charge (it often is, but it is quite alright to charge for open source software). The term Open Source describes on one side a method of developing software and on the other side certain licence models which governs the (re-)distribution of such software. Therefore, from a legal point of view, you can also pirate open source software.

- Free (of charge) software is not necessarily open source software. There are tons of free programs available, which are NOT open source.

- Pirating means obtaining and using software under illegal circumstances and/or using software in violence of the attached licence for this software. This often means using closed software with cracked keys to avoid paying for it, but it could also easily mean using open source as a base for a commercial product which is then sold as closed source (also happens quite a bit).

So the question, whether open source advocates also support software piracy is a bit like asking whether people advocating freedom of speech are supporting demagogy and hate campaigns. Maybe some do, incidentally, but there is no causal connection.

### Post by Sublime Porte on 2009-06-20
I think there's still a lot of debate that needs to occur regarding the nature of intellectual property. Intellectual property is not real property, it is 'pseudo-property', it's akin to claiming the entire atmosphere is your factory for bottling nitrogen, and anyone who breathes is stealing your nitrogen. If it actually were property, then there'd be no need to create artificial laws to try and enforce it's ownership. As they say possession is 9/10ths of the law, and intellectual property doesn't recognise possession as meaning anything.

The thing that I find most disturbing is EULA's that try to dictate what you can do with intellectual property you've supposedly purchased. If someone sold you a car, and tried to tell you which roads you could drive it on, or what modifications you were allowed to make to it, or who you were allowed to permit behind the wheel or even in the passenger seats, you'd tell them to 'shove it', or you'd buy it, but completely disregard those stipulations, declaring them null and void.

Intellecual property just isn't a natural form of ownership, it's artificially maintained, and in the long term is unenforceable. The only solution I can see for those who want to profit from their ideas, is to move from actual products to services, and many have already done this, tying in subscription services to their products..

As for the argument about it being over-priced, this is just nonsensical. If a car were ridiculously over-priced would you then steal it? The price of something, does not in any way justify stealing it (if you actually believe intellectual property is real property).

**Disclaimer:** My post is not about whether it's right/wrong to break the law in your respective country, you should not. it is about the nature of property and what constitutes valid property, and how those who wish to make money from their intellectual endeavours could better go about it.
