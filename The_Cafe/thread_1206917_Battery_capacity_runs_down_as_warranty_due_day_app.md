### Post by tom66 on 2009-07-07
You can crack open your old lithium ion battery and replace the cells in it for half or less than the manufacturer's sale price of the battery, especially if you buy the cells on eBay. 

But be careful when soldering them in (or just be careful in general) because they are very volatile and can be known to burst or explode if mistreated. You'll want to find the same voltage and number of cells, but you can go up in capacity if you feel the need...

But really, lithium ion batteries suck. They get hot when charging, they're very well known to be explosive, and they don't tolerate over-rating. over-charging, or being frozen. If only capacitors in million-farad ratings were practical (whilst we do have supercapacitors they're only in the thousand-farad range at reasonable voltage) then we could screw lithium batteries. Capacitors have very long lifetimes, in the 200,000 hour range if treated appropriately.

This is true. However Lithium batteries still degrade over time depending on how you use them. The number one most common way people degrade laptop-battery life is by using their laptop constantly while the power-supply is plugged in.

The best way to keep your battery life is to constantly cycle the voltage level (IE: run the battery down and then charge it up). having it at peak-charge all the time while in use degrades it significantly.

### Post by Gizenshya on 2009-07-07
the thing about batteries is each different type has different characteristics.

The main ones with Lithium-Ion batteries are: 1.  If the charge gets too low, the battery changes chemically, and becomes explosive if charged after the change.  I've heard unconfirmed reports that they slowly change over time anyway, just exponentially faster below certain levels.  ALL Li-ion batteries have min-circuitry now that stops the batteries from charging if they reach a certain level of power (less than X percent or voltage) for safety.

2.  The more charge a Li-ion battery contains at any given time, the faster it looses overall total charge capacity.

I read one research paper that showed that charging to 80%, and no more, was optimal.  At 100% charge, batteries lose x amount per time, and if overcharged to 120%, it is about 3x per time.  And at 80%, it is about .2x per time.  So they'll last approx 5x longer if only charged to 80%.

Of course, the longer it stays fully charged, the faster it loses capacity.  Also.. I read that report about 2 years ago.  So the numbers might not be right... but 80% was optimal, though it may have been to a different ratio (and the same goes for overcharge).

What I think...

Companies are forced to produce batteries with longer and longer life for laptops.  Since the technology hasn't changed much in Li-ion batteries in many years, if what you are saying about the batteries not lasting long is true, I think the most probable explanation is that they are setting the software in the computer (or the internal battery circuitry) to charge the cells more than they did in the past.  S, the same battery of a new laptop might initially last 20% longer... but that's just because it has the same technology as before, but overcharged.  And this makes the life of the battery go down significantly.

I think that is a reasonable explanation for the situation, and not quite as 'evil' as one may initially think.  Business as usual perhaps ;)

Note:  I do not know (remember?) whether the battery capacity decay was linear or exponential for overcharging.  I vaguely remember it being linear, but I'm not very confident in that.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2009-07-07
With a Mac G4 powerbook, I use two batteries and rotate them every 50 cycles.  I use an applet called Coconut that keeps track of the capacity and cycle count.  I keep the spare battery at ~40% charge during storage.  A decent laptop battery should last about 300 cycles or 3 years whichever comes first.  If you don't use it much, it will still degrade over 3 years so that is why cycling is recommended.  It's a race between cycles and aging.  

I get about 3 hours of real use for each discharge.  Apple claimed 4.5 hours which is total nonsense.  Both batteries started above 4400 mAh, the rated capacity.

I got 280 and 275 cycles on my two batteries before capacity dropped to 75% of rated capacity.  Apple recalled both batteries because of explosive Sony cells.  I'm at 5.5 years and now on a second set of Apple/Sony batteries.  So if this pair lasts 4-5 years then I believe that's the maximum you can expect with a pair of batteries rotated every 50 full cycles.

Capacity drop was bilinear--steady until ~75% then it seemed to drop at a faster rate after that.

It's not a conspiracy.  Mac batteries are $129 US each.  They seem to deliver their rated life (300 cycles and still maintain 80% capacity) under specific conditions.  In this case I use the 2-battery, rotation method and I get near rated life.

If you use the laptop on battery every day, you will easily get 300 cycles in one year.  Of course, heat, super deep cycles, and general abuse will reduce capacity quicker.

There are a lot of battery manufacturers.  Consumers can't peak inside to see what the batteries are made of.  I would imagine there are a lot of crappy batteries in the field.

For all the griping about overpriced Apple products, at least the batteries (and powerbook) have delivered in this case.
