### Post by Kopachris on 2009-03-26
Found this post on a [blog]("http://www.zolved.com/blog/view_entry/29056/Why_we_capitalize_the_I_in_the_Internet_"):
> 
Most arguments I've seen for lower case "i" when using "the Internet" point out that we don't capitalize newspapers, television, radio and blah blah blah. There are many radios, but when we say "I saw it on the Internet" we are not referring to one of many computer internets we could possibly get on. Just the one.

And to bolster our case, we present you with this:

Exhibit A - The Tech Encyclopedia definition for "internet";
(1) (Lower case "i"nternet) A large network made up of a number of smaller networks.

(2) (Upper case "I"nternet) The largest network in the world. It is made up of more than 350 million computers in more than 100 countries covering commercial, academic and government endeavors.

Exhibit B - Some BBC guy's commentary from when Wired declared it won't capitalize "i" in the Internet;
"The fact is that the Internet is the name of a specific collection of networks, while internet is a generic term for two or more connected networks. The two are as distinct as planet Earth and the earth around your begonias, and they should be distinguished in print."


- The Zolved Team
My reply:
> 
Newspaper, radio, and television can refer either to the medium or the object with which it's read. For newspaper, the company that produces the actual papers with news in them could be referred to as "the newspaper". For radio, it can refer to the station, or the object used to turn the transmitted (and later captured) light waves into sound. Television can refer either to the company that provides the moving pictures coupled with sound that your object also called a television decodes and displays. Similarly, internet can refer to the actual global network, or your little part. You don't call your computer an internet because your computer does a lot more than just read the internet (even netbooks). Many people refer to their internet browser as "the internet". Going off of all the other similar network nouns, this could actually be correct. Think about it: the newspaper is a network of writers and editor producing something that is distributed to a lot of people. The radio is a network of people with transmitters (albeit on separate frequencies (usually)) producing something that is distributed to a lot of people. The television is a group of channels producing lots of stuff that is distributed to a lot of people. The internet is an even larger group of people producing even more stuff that is distributed to almost as many people. "Internet" is not a proper noun, and should not be capitalized unless at the beginning of a sentence or in German.

Your thoughts?

### Post by MaxIBoy on 2009-03-26
Right now, there is only one internet. Thus, it is a proper noun and should usually be capitalized (Internet.)


Whenever someone is talking about "another Internet," he should usually be using the term "another WAN" (WAN = Wide Area Network.) The Internet is a big collection of WANs owned by different ISPs, all connected to a few big hubs; these hubs then form the highest-level WAN. Very few WANs have survived without eventually becoming part of the Internet, but there are some (notably FidoNet.) Even FidoNet has gateways to the main Internet, but it still uses a different set of protocols.

### Post by dmizer on 2009-03-26
Unlike some other rules in English grammar, capitalization rules are fairly simple and are rarely (if ever) given exception. If unsure, it's usually best to rely on the dictionary, which in this case indicates that "internet" should be capitalized when referring to "The Internet". The Internet is a proper noun and refers to a specific network.

Keep in mind, that some other nouns have both common and proper versions. For example, "daisy" can refer to either a type of flower (common noun) or a person's name. Obviously, when used as a person's name, "Daisy" should be capitalized.

So, since "the Internet" is the proper name for a specific network, it should be capitalized.
