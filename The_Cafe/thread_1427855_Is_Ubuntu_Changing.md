### Post by Madspyman on 2010-03-12
They're trying to commercialize on the same things that made OS X so successful, app store, music store, but then hold to Microsoft formula of offering their OS on other manufactures hardware, Dell, Asus etc. 

It's cheap for manufactures to offer Ubuntu on their PC's/netbooks, and FOSS development means less overhead so Canonical makes a profit selling apps, music, and through Yahoo searches, all Ubuntu branded pre-existing services offered through partners they agree to share profit with. 

It's almost a win win for everyone, except Linux doesn't have support from a whole lot of major mainstream developers, so consumers shy away from it, meaning Ubuntu gets put on less hardware.

It's a smart business model that unfortunately relies on Linux gaining market-share.

It's a catch 22 Linux won't gain a consumer market until it has support from major developers, which it won't get until it has a consumer market.

Ubuntu is free though, so why shouldn't Canonical, try to make money off an existing user base by offering to sell us music (which we buy anyway) and proprietary software (that may have features FOSS doesn't). 

If existing users start purchasing things from Ubuntu's software store, more major developers may want to get in on it, thus giving hardware manufactures a reason to sell computers with Ubuntu on them, peaking consumer interested and raising both Linux and Canonicals market-share.
