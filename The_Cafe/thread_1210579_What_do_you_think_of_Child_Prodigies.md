### Post by ksennin on 2009-07-12
Einstein being bad at math is a popular myth.

Einstein found his math classes frustrating because the German school system at the time was heavily into rigid memorisation rote methodologies that he disliked even as a kid, prefering to learn things from a more reasoned, analytical approach.  He was not a model student precisely because of his mindset of questioning whatever was presented to him as dogmatic. He was also a physicist, not a mathematician, which means that math was his tool, but not his primary focus.  He later in his life had to learn tensor calculus from a friend to better develop his theories.

As to the original thread inquiry, I was considered by some a quasi-prodigy during my early youth, being somewhat gifted at math, linguistics, and other areas, but that simply meant my abilities at those fields developed early, and their development did not follow the same pace as I "grew up".  So people caught up with me and I was no longer that special.  My speaking up on quantum physics at 40 is better than when I was 10, but once I impressed people, and now I just bore them to tears.

I was also gifted at drawing, being capable of drawing my own comic books since age 5 or so, but my parents always said I would starve as an artist, energetically repeating this whenever I drew anything.  Thus, I never received any formal training and stopped practicing it regularly.  Living in a CentralAmerica country where most people feel that reading books is a waste of time was also not a very stimulating environment.

As to what contributions one makes, I am a structural engineer and have designed many buildings people find useful.  None have fallen down.  Yet.
