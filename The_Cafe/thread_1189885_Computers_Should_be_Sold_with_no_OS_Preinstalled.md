Hi!

### Post by koshatnik on 2009-06-17
Its 2009. Why the hell are we still using optical media?

The solution is relatively simple. All PC units and laptops come with a boot chip that gives a menu of choices of OS's. The end user then decides what they want to use, loaded off a flash drive or off the chip itself. After a certain period, say 30 days, the boot chip asks the user to commit to a choice (so that paid for OS's actually get paid for), and once commited the boot chip gets by-passed on future boots.

The OS of choice is then installed on the harddrive from flash memory. You pay your license if appropriate, badda bing sorted.

The only problem would be from competing linux distro's all wanting to be on the chip. But then, I guess you could just make if free versions only, with the option to upgrade to a full paid up distro should you want to.

The main thing is that users would be making a choice.

Preinstallation of the Microsoft OS on the otherwise open spec PC is the same.  The user will not go and try an alternative OS because it came with Windows.  He/She will use what's already on there.  This is exactly what MS intend - and exactly what those of you in favour of preinstallation are supporting.

I repeat myself: pre-install OSes leave computer users with the false impression that they don't need to make any decisions about their computer.  This is my big concern: too many people believe that they don't need to learn anything to surf the internet safely, which puts *everybody* at risk.  Making computers w/o a pre-installed OS the standard will go a long ways towards correcting this problem.  Installing an OS on your own isn't hard, especially for a new computer with no pre-existing data that's at risk for deletion.  The only reason most people are afraid to learn this is because the process is hidden from them, so they assume it must be over their heads.  And vendors will have no problem burning a blank cd with the free Linux distro of the user's choice at no extra cost, because it's good salesmanship (and it costs next to nothing to do).  Of course, they'll prefer to direct customers to the Windows OS rack instead so they can sell us something else, but A) after the initial complaints, people will learn that there are alternatives, and B) (more importantly) after the installation process, people will realize not only that they *can* learn about their computer, they probably should have a long time ago.  Even if Linux doesn't gain any market share from the process, the average computer user will become more educated, and the internet will be safer as a result.  That is my main goal.
