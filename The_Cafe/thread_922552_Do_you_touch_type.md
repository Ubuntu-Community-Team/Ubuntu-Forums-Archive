### Post by northern lights on 2008-09-17
I'd also like to highly recommend touch typing.

My family owned a computer since the late eighties, so I typed on keyboards before elementary school (not saying I knew what I typed...) but I was never taught touch typing in school. I also thought, I'm faster than with a pen so why bother?

### Post by ClyN3il on 2010-08-21
Ktouch was one of my favorite programs when I was using OpenSUSE--I haven't yet found a completely free program for Windows that is as good. Now that I'm using Ubuntu/Gnome, I'm about to give Tux Type a try.

Learning to touch time is undoubtedly worth the time investment if typing is something that you do on more than a rare occasion. And for the same reason, so is switching to Dvorak. It really is easier, faster, more comfortable, and puts less strain on your hands (less risk of carpal tunnel syndrome). Especially if you're learning to touch type for the first time--I'd imagine it wouldn't be much harder than learning to touch type QWERTY, maybe easier, since you're relearning everything anyway, there will be less old bad habits getting in the way. I'd never want to go back (especially since my muscle memory has fully forgotten QWERTY, and now I can only type on a QWERTY layout by hunt-and-peck). Anyway, if you do any kind of writing more than rarely, you owe it to yourself to learn to touch type with Dvorak. And as long as you're going to the trouble of learning to touch type, I can't image why you would choose to do it in QWERTY, when Dvorak allows you to type faster with less strain, and any machine you'll be typing on that's not in a museum will allow you to use one just as easily as the other.

### Post by Windows Nerd on 2010-09-15
> **ClyN3il said:**
> Ktouch was one of my favorite programs when I was using OpenSUSE--I haven't yet found a completely free program for Windows that is as good. Now that I'm using Ubuntu/Gnome, I'm about to give Tux Type a try.

Learning to touch time is undoubtedly worth the time investment if typing is something that you do on more than a rare occasion. And for the same reason, so is switching to Dvorak. It really is easier, faster, more comfortable, and puts less strain on your hands (less risk of carpal tunnel syndrome). Especially if you're learning to touch type for the first time--I'd imagine it wouldn't be much harder than learning to touch type QWERTY, maybe easier, since you're relearning everything anyway, there will be less old bad habits getting in the way. And you won't be tempted to look at the keys, since the letters on the keys don't match what they do anyway!

I could sort of touch type QWERTY, never by actually learning, but just by doing it so much over the course of my life, that eventually I didn't have to look at the keyboard anymore. Then I decided to learn Dvorak, and it was really difficult and frustrating for about a month or maybe two, but eventually I got used to it, and now I can type over 50 wpm--when I'm out of practice! I'd never want to go back (especially since my muscle memory has fully forgotten QWERTY, and now I can only type on a QWERTY layout by hunt-and-peck). It takes all of about 15-20 seconds to switch to Dvorak on major Linux distros, Windows, or (I hear) Mac, and then you can easily switch back and forth any time. Plus it's great fun to see how confused people get when they try to type and it's set to Dvorak!

Anyway, if you do any kind of writing more than rarely, you owe it to yourself to learn to touch type with Dvorak. And as long as you're going to the trouble of learning to touch type, I can't image why you would choose to do it in QWERTY, when Dvorak allows you to type faster with less strain, and any machine you'll be typing on that's not in a museum will allow you to use one just as easily as the other.

Then there are those people who use Dvorak with blank keys. Then they _really_ get confused when people who use QWERTY try thier keyboard.
