> &#8220;It&#8217;s difficult for small developers to invest in the smartphone segment of Nokia when nobody knows its future,&#8221; said Suhr, creator of Twitter application &#8220;Gravity,&#8221; which was showcased by Nokia when it opened its Ovi applications store last year. &#8220;The new shiny things aren&#8217;t available and there&#8217;s only the old-fashioned stuff, where it takes a lot of work to make the software look good.&#8221; 

Nokia, which doesn&#8217;t disclose its catalog size, says it has 1.7 million downloads a day of apps including QuickOffice, Skype Internet calling service, Shazam music identifier, Spotify music, Snake games and Lonely Planet travel guides. The company&#8217;s secrecy about the number of apps is &#8220;probably because it&#8217;s still rather small,&#8221; said Gartner&#8217;s Jones.

Its offerings lag behind Apple&#8217;s App Store, which has more than 225,000 apps. [Android has more than 70,000]("http://www.androlib.com/appstats.aspx"), according to Androlib.com, which tracks the platform&#8217;s apps.


Shrinking Share

The market share of Symbian, Nokia&#8217;s main smartphone operating system, fell to 44.3 percent in the first quarter from 48.8 percent a year ago, according to Gartner. Although mostly on Nokia phones, Symbian is also used by Samsung Electronics Co. and Sony Ericsson. iPhone&#8217;s share rose to 15.4 percent from 10.5 percent, while Android soared to 9.6 percent from 1.6 percent.


Switching to Android

Many developers don&#8217;t want to wait, and say they can&#8217;t take the risk of developing for a yet-to-be-perfected platform. Even long-time Nokia software authors are looking elsewhere.

Android-based smartphones threaten to top the iPhone in 2013 in market share, according to Framingham, Massachusetts- based IDC. Shipments of Android devices may reach 68 million that year, making it the second-most popular operating system after Symbian, according to IDC...
