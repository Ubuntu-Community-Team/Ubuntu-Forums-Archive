10)  Opening up just documents from trusted sources after a virus scan guarantees safety.

MYTH - Unless you disable autoexecution of macros or disable active content features of data formats that support them, you can get a virus.

I had A/V software when I received an Excel spreadsheet from Hewlett Packard Engineering circa 1995?

In the spreadsheet was an execute-on-open virus.  This was before that feature could be turned off in Excel easily, or before A/V companies even scanned spreadsheets.

It replicated itself on over 7,000 documents before I caught on.

It's the #1 method of getting a true virus (self-replicating program).  As more data formats are being basterdized (sic)this problem will get a lot worse before it gets better.

Is it that freaking hard to separate your true DATA from your Macros/Executables???  No, it is not.  The idiot who came up with this should be shot, but he doesn't run Microsoft anymore.

### Post by doas777 on 2010-06-24
the "myth" I wish they had covered is the misuse of the word "virus" to describe all kinds of malware. very few viruses are around any more, for any platform. they have been largely replaced by trojans, and the replication is web-based, rather than using a flaw in a specific peice of host software to replicate to peers (a virus) or system vulnerabilities to do the same (worms). the means for dealing with viruses/worms is entirely different than that for trojans, adware/spyware, rootkits, or plain old fashioned social engineering (of which trojans play a part). *for viruses and worms, you harden the system. for the rest you harden the user.  *

I still hold that ubuntu is largely immune to viruses specifically, but worms, trojans, rootkits, and foolhardy users are definitely an issue no matter what platform you are on.

I'm particularly amused by Myth 9: **Viruses Can  Infect Humans, or Alien Spacecrafts**

kinda puts the whole thing in perspective. nevertheless, not a bad list  for folks that are new to malware topics. Thanks Tristam. And thanks for the nod the other day. I had despaired that I would never make y'alls list.

yes and no. i've noticed that the users with the most malware problems, usually have multiple malwares, and often they fight eachother. these same users are also often woefully negligent when it comes to installing crapware, whether intentionally or not, and that causes much of the slowdown. most of the time, when i see a box with every fricken toolbar ever, a full system tray, and 29 fricken IM clients on it, I know that it has a serious malware problem as well. 

the last serious infection I fought, involved 4 rootkits, numerous trojans, a worm, a handful of viruses (which weren't doing anything btw, because they targeted flaws in specific game softwares that were not installed ), and 3 peices of scareware (which started as trojans). 

scareware is software that screams it head off, trying to trick the user into stuff like purchasing fake antivirus software to clean uninfected files. another varient, blackmailware/ransomware will do things like encrypt your files and extort money to get the password. some will steal personal data, and then extort payment to destroy it or keep it quite. As Dr Strangelove said, a doomsday machine loses any purpose if you don't tell anyone that it's there. I love that movie...

now Rootkits, are the stealthiest of softwares. they are designed to hide themselves from the os and system software (like antivirus). they are usually used to assure that an attacker can keep root access to a rooted box, so reliability and undetectability are essential to their functioning. however to work correctly, a rootkit must be able to alter the system kernel in ram to do things like patch functions so that queries about the kit or the resources it consumes return innocuous results. in '05 sony/bmg released a rootkit that would hide any file starting with $sys$ from the windows filesystem api. because they operate at such a low level, rootkits are some of the scariest of malwares, but they also don't play nicely with others. if another rootkits comes along and patches the same function the mine did, then my kit might become vissible, or just fail to function. 

worms in particular are very territorial, since they too exploit the system apis and kernel image to perform their nastiness and to propagate. most sophisticated worms install mutex's to cripple other common worms and rootkits, as well as to make sure that the host only gets one instance of that particular worm (another key diff between worms and viruses). Most worms after win32/Blaster download and install the MS patch that blocks blaster if it is not already installed. my guess is common worms nowadays patch conficker as well. 

so, the moral of my story, is that this guy had a combinition of malware that screams and whispers at the same time, working across purposes for multiple masters. when you get that kind of conflict in the deepest recesses of your system, you have some bizarre problems and a rather slugish system. your're right, a virus is designed to be quite, but the user is often too foolhardy to allow it that privilege.
