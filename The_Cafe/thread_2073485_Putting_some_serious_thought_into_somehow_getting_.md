### Post by TeamRocket1233c on 2012-10-19
Hiya! I've been putting some serious thought into somehow getting the K-12 public schools over here to adopt FOSS, specifically in the form of Debian for the network servers, and Edubuntu for the workstations, considering what the potential benefits of using Linux would be over sticking to MS and Windows.

The biggest benefit would be that once the growing pains were to have passed, they'd be looking at a four or five-figure savings over MS, as Debian and Edubuntu, and their respective software, are free. Also, as opposed to waiting on the phone for support, they'd get free, community-based support pretty much instantaneously though user forums or real-time IRC chats, which would really help should a problem arise. In addition, they'd be able to relax a bit when it comes to viruses and malware due to not having to worry about it, plus they would have more uptime than downtime.

Also, as for gradebook and school administration software, and test generators, those are all available for Linux, and really, Thunderbird, and Firefox, Opera, or Chromium, would really make better e-Mail software and a better browser than Outlook and IE, and as for productivity and creativity software, OpenOffice, as well as GIMP, MyPaint, and Inkscape would fill the gap pretty nicely.

However anything that sounds great in theory could always be a bad idea in practice. Also, both the kids, as well as the teachers and principals and stuff, would most likely have a hard time getting used to the Unity desktop.

### Post by lykwydchykyn on 2012-10-20
> **TeamRocket1233c said:**
> Well, what about seeing if they can't at least pilot Debian and Edubuntu in a couple of the grade schools and middle schools and seeing if they like it better than Windows, and then letting the board decide whether or not to switch over?

I can't speak for every school district or gov't, but based on almost a decade in local government IT, here's what I think:

- Don't operate on the level of operating systems or even applications.  Operate on the level of solutions.  Look for problems or needs that need a solution, and see if you can engineer a solution using free software.

- It helps if you have people like teachers or school administrators who want to see something happen, but can't get the funding to do it commercially.

- Don't try to replace something that is currently working well enough with something untried, untested, unknown.  That's gonna be a non-starter for everyone involved.  Look instead for places where current systems fall short (in functionality or quantity) or don't exist.

- Don't bank on the whole cost savings/no anti-virus/open-source aspect getting much traction with anyone.  You'd think it would, but really it just doesn't.

- Unless there's a channel for paid support, or unless IT employees are totally behind it, you can forget about major deployments.

- Respect the fact that people who work in school IT and other schools positions may not be familiar with the systems, and may not want the liability of dealing with them.  Remember, they're the guys who have to answer the phone in the middle of the night when something goes wrong, so be accommodating of their preferences and biases.

- More than anything else, you need contact with people in the school system.  You need to know where the needs are, and you need to get them excited about your solution.  Anonymously chucking a debian box at them isn't going to go anywhere.

- Finally, if you do pitch some kind of solution to them, make sure you go over it with a fine-toothed comb and get it child-friendly.  FOSSers have their own little culture and sometimes warped senses of humor.  For example, I'm sure whoever wrote XFCE's network connections manager thought it was hilarious to name it "Gigolo", but if it shows up on a kindergarten workstation, that's the end of that.   And while we all understand that "beastie" is the BSD mascot, an image of a penguin and a gnu playing music with "the devil" is going to raise a few eyebrows in Oklahoma, I'll bet (I'll leave it to you to find out which major FOSS educational project features this).

### Post by hsweet on 2012-10-20
I and a fellow teacher got  a Ubuntu/Edubuntu thin client lab into my distirct which has been running for 7 years now.  We just did it.  I set up a discard computer to be the server and another piece of old junk as a thin client, showed the working sytem to my principal who found $1500 somewhere for our first server. 

 We did the wiring ourselves, aruged a bit with the network admin who was nervous about a rouge DHCP server (he came around after he saw it not trashing his network),  ran as a dual windows/linux labs for a few years and eventually ditched windows entirely as well as upgraded some key bits of network hardware.

It's not been possible yet to grow beyond the one lab, but we're still here (actually I've since retired but the lab has not)  Saved the district buckets of money too.

I think the moral of the story is that it is easier to get forgiveness than permission, have something to show, play up the $ savings, they are real.
