### Post by phrostbyte on 2007-10-12
Copyright is in crisis. Photocopying, sound- and video-recording, computers, and the net have all made it increasingly difficult for the owners of copyright to enforce their rights. Levies or legal penalties only patch the holes in an already leaky system. The flaw lies not in the technology or in our society, but in the very notion of copyright. 

Intellectual "property" does not behave like material property. If I give you a physical object I may no longer have use or control of that thing, and may ask for something in return -- some payment or barter. But when I give you an idea, I lose nothing. I can still use that idea as I wish. I need ask nothing in return. 

The laws of exchange of matter being so very different from the laws of exchange of information, any attempt to trade ideas with material goods was destined for trouble sooner or later.

Not only do people hold on to ideas for material gain, they also hang on to them for psychological gain. The ego likes to be identified as the source of a particular insight or concept. But what right has the ego to attach itself to something that was never its in the first place? 

We say "an idea came to me". I did not make it happen. What I do is shape the ideas "that come" into forms -- usually words and images -- that satisfy me, and hopefully communicate something to others. If I am to be paid for my work (which I am not averse to), I should be paid for my time and energy, not some dubious concept of intellectual property.

Thoughts are free. They should remain free, and be given freely. 

And, following the universal law, the more we give the more we shall receive.

What is wrong with copyright?

**Copyright encourages anti-social behavior.**Humans are by nature social beings, we like to share the things we learned and experienced with others. Copyright makes this behavior illegal and encourages even with close friends to a policy of nondisclosure. 

**Copyright is censorship.** Copyright makes it illegal to encourage other peoples ideas and work into your own. Since it actually restricts the ability for a person to express him or herself, it against the notion of freedom of speech.

**Copyright makes innovation difficult.** This article was written in less the five minutes because it builds on someone else's work. If every time we have to start something from scratch. If that work was copyrighted and restricted, this would be a much longer and difficult work to produce.

**Copyright doesn't work.** Despite the laws of copyright, copyright infringement is practiced by at least 50 million people in the United States according to the RIAA.

Introduction written by Peter Russell was used without permission.

We should figure out ways to remove the need for copyright while still ensuring authorship can be profitable (since that is "in principal" the purpose of copyright).

### Post by saulgoode on 2007-10-13
I do not consider copyrights to be objectionable. Society does benefit from encouraging authors, artists, and even software programmers to share their creations and granting them a limited-time monopoly on marketing their creation is an effective way to subsidize their endeavors.

The only problem I have with copyrights is their duration. It is not necessary to grant *lifetime* monopoly rights to an original creator; a much shorter duration would still encourage them to create and provide better benefit to society. 

In my opinion, copyrights should only last about 25-30 years. Artists should be able to find a market for their product in such a time frame and, with such a "head start", still hold an advantage in an open, competitive market even after their copyright has expired. (For example, if George Lucas's Star Wars copyrights no longer applied, he would still have quite an advantage on other distributors despite no longer holding the monopoly.)

Copyrights are a government subsidy of creativity and I feel they provide a better incentive to create than efforts such as direct patronage (where investment is made "before the fact" with no guarantee of a beneficial outcome). Like any government subsidy, there needs to be a balance between the costs and the benefits; as well as an assessment of whether or not there is actual need of a subsidy. 

Currently, copyrights are costing society more than the benefit they provide and there needs to be some restructuring; however, I do not agree that copyrights should be eradicated altogether.

I used to think this as well, but I don't believe it would solve some of the most fundamental problems with copyright, namely, the fact that ignored by most of the population and the fact that enforcement of copyright requires intervention and meddling by big business and government into people's personal lives.

If you notice on BitTorrent networks most of the stuff being shared as at most a few years old. Having a copyright that lasts 25 years does not have much effect on the legality of file sharing at it's current state.

Also a copyright that lasts even one year has no difference in the way it must be enforced. It still has all the totalitarian consequences of perfect enforcement.

That's why I think the very notion of copyright, or "limited monopoly", should be dropped as a legal concept, and a better system should be designed, one that respects the rights of people to share ideas and experiences with one another, that respects the ability to build on top of that shared experience, and at the same time allows authorship to be rewarded.
