### Post by Ironi on 2005-05-18
[QUOTE=Ironi]My main nitpick with your backstory is that it's too conservative; a lot of the technology that you have arriving after 2020 is likely to arrive much sooner than that.[/QUOTE]
The glaring problem with your backstory is that a "secure" PC that isn't completely under the user's control doesn't arrive until ~2020 with the rise of NanoTek. In reality, though, such a thing is much more likely to arrive, as a whole, by 2010 if not sooner. Microsoft and others are already working on the bits and pieces. Links:
[list]
[*][Microsoft's NGSCB](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next-Generation_Secure_Computing_Base) (according to [this article](http://www.crn.com/sections/breakingnews/dailyarchives.jhtml?articleId=18841713), it's been shelved. I can't find any others elsewhere to confirm that, though.)
[*][Intel's LaGrande](http://www.intel.com/technology/security/) [(Google)](http://www.google.com/search?q=intel%20lagrande)
[*][Phoenix's Core Managed Environment](http://www.google.com/search?q=phoenix+%22core+managed+environment%22)
[*][The Trusted Computing Group](http://www.google.com/search?q=%22trusted%20computing%20group%22)
[/list]

The general idea here is that companies are trying to push "secure" features that will also have a convenient side-effect of making [Digital Rights Management](http://www.google.com/search?q=%22digital%20rights%20management%22) that utilizes the hardware significantly more difficult to circumvent. Note also that "circumvention" of a "protection device" is already illegal under 1998's Digital Millennium Copyright Act. [(Google)](http://www.google.com/search?q=dmca+circumvention+%22protection+device%22) [(EFF)](http://www.eff.org/IP/DMCA/)
