Linux, and Ubuntu in particular, are 90% of the way there. The biggest obsticles are:
[LIST=1]
[*]Popular software recognition: Photoshop and Quickbooks, in particular
[*]Current mass market licensed formats: MP3, Flash, etc
[*]Newest hardware compatablity
[*]Proprietary hardware issues
[/LIST]

The hardware issues are the biggest problem, and I understand why Shuttleworth is pursuing that route. It may not be a popular idea with open source purists, but if Ubuntu is going to get widespread acceptance, it will need to do this. Other Ubuntu variations can stay 'true' to the open source ideal. The flexibility of Linux!

I've had very few issues with older hardware that I couldn't fix. In fact, I think Linux is a prime example that we don't NEED to keep buying new hardware, just because the OS (Windows), and programs don't work on it. Thousands of used units out there, still capable of working just fine, for someone, especially someone who'd never be able to afford a new computer. Those units are wasting in landfills, doing nothing for anyone, other than polluting the land.

I've taken it on myself to do a part to slow that down. I take in old hardware, Ubuntuize it, update it if I can, and give it away. So far, have giving away six units. Very please families, and students. K.Mandla has been an inspiration for this. Some great examples of equipment rehab.

Xubuntu, Ubuntu w/fluxbox/Openbox, or IceWM. All good alternatives for very low end equipment. Even DSL. 

I'm glad Ubuntu has some visionary people at it's helm.

### Post by dmizer on 2006-11-21
shall we compare apples to apples?

how many network adapters work "out of the box" in windows?  and by "out of the box" i mean ... when you open the box for the wireless adapter, you discover that the install disk is a coaster.  furthermore, when you visit the manufacturer's website, you find that they don't offer downloads of their driver software to you because you didn't subscribe to their support service when you purchased the product.  now go! ... *clicks stopwatch timer*   make it work.

for linux, look here: [https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/WirelessCardsSupported](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/WifiDocs/WirelessCardsSupported) for the ever expanding list of cards you can unpack from the box, plug in, and connect to the internet.  no hassle, no waiting for the driver to install, no reboot necessary.  THAT is hardware support. 

windows can't do that because it relies on the manufactures of hardware to provide the necessary drivers for your add ons.  if manufactures stopped providing drivers for their hardware, windows would be completely hosed. THEN the sound you would hear would be the windows dam breaking.

linux has hardware support, windows does not.  it only is perceived as having hardware support because drivers are provided for you.

and yes, we do have a more than ample supply of topics such as these.
