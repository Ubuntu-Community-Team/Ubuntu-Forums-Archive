### Post by perce on 2007-10-02
I started in 96 on the University computers. There were neither Gnome nor KDE at the time. We logged in in the terminal, and started an almost forgotten windows manager called fvwm from the command line. It was mostly a blue screen, but you could open xetrms on it and use the command line again. It sounds pretty scary now, but it was multitasking, and windows wasn't really yet, so it looked as science fiction to us. Moreover we could open windows on some other people's screen just to annoy them - it was so fun! this lasted until our Linux guru showed us they the other could delete our files if we opened a window in his screen. We were playing a MUD called Albion, a sort of text-only role playing game. I first used Perce on that MUD for the first time. The real fun was more chatting with people all over the world  than killing the monsters. They were all the same anyway...  And we used talk to talk with people from other University departments - nowadays talk is almost forgotten, but it is what people used in the good old Unix days before MSN messenger. Two friends of mine even got married with talk. 
And there was no spam in emails... also none of my friends had emails at that time, so email was pretty much useless for me anyway.

### Post by Caffeine_Junky on 2007-10-02
:lolflag: ..just start at the beginning "failed to start the X server" and work your way in from there.

When you are new to linux there is not much that **dose not** make you "scratch your head" :shock:

Actually, before you even attempt to install the OS you need to get a grasp on:

a) setting your BIOS to boot from your CD drive (if you have one)

b) a basic understanding of partitions/partitioning (if you are going to dual-boot ..which allot of people will do in the beginning)  

..then once you are in to the desktop comes the navigation of an environment that you may have never seen before..

But, really, these things I speak of apply to any new OS you try.

learning how to install stuff is very important to know right from the start!, if you come from a "doubleclick the exe" OS you need to know about Synaptic package Manager straight away or you will be lost.

I will say tho,  Ubuntu has come along way since I first tried it with the release of 5.04.  Now you can execute a .deb from within nautilus and you will be greeted with an installer. very nice.
