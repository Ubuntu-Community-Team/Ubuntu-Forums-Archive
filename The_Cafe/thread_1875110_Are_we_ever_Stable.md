### Post by Paqman on 2011-11-04
Depends what you mean by "stable"? Stability can different things if you ask different people. Do you mean "stable" as in lack of bugs and high reliability, or "stable" as in not likely to be upgraded?

If it's the latter, then LTSs are inherently more stable. They're released, then relatively few changes are made for several years. This is what big companies want. However, this doesn't mean they have less bugs. Only really important or security-critical bugs are likely to be patched on an LTS release, so it's quite possible for the packages in an LTS to have bugs that the newer packages in a six-monthly release don't, because they've been patched in the newer versions.

### Post by 3Miro on 2011-11-04
LTS releases take less risks with new software (a GTK2 -> GTK3 jum would have never happened in an LTS release). Then the LTS is around longer and more bugs get fixed overtime. Right now, 10.04 would have fewer bugs and it would work more smoothly than say 11.04 or 11.10.

On the other hand, newer versions come with newer drivers and those are not always backported. I have Intel Sandy Bridge graphics on my laptop and 10.04 probably wouldn't even boot.

This stability is relative. A year after the LTS release, it will work better for most people, but there are plenty of exceptions.
