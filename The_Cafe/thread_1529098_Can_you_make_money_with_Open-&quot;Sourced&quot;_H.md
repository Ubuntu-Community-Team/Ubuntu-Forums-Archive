### Post by earthpigg on 2010-07-11
A business that I would appreciate an entrepeneur starting up:

Sell individual laptop parts that meet a certain set of specifications and allow customers to assemble them themselves, with the confidence that they can upgrade the latop later withOUT being tied to a single OEM such as Dell or Acer.

We have the ATX standard for desktops. We have Mini-ATX or whatever it is called for baby desktops.

Let's create some laptop standards.

LS designates "laptop specification", followed by a number. In the US, this number will be "maximum display size, in inches". For the rest, it will be "maximum display size, in cm".

LSUS designates the measure is inches. LSEU designates it is in cm.

LSUS-15 would correspond to LSEU-38.

Create a basic set of standard sizes for:
LSUS-8
LSUS-13
LSUS-15
LSUS-18

Just as you can put a MicroATX motherboard into an ATX case, you can put LSUS-8 components in a LSUS-15 laptop - but not LSUS-15 stuff into LSUS-8.

Any laptop keyboard bearing the LSUS-15 logo will physically fit & connect to a laptop case bearing the LSUS-15 logo.

Where possible, have the specification correspond to what is already used as a *de facto* standard - no reason to force the creation of a new RAM or hard drive formfactor, for example.

Create the Open Laptop Standards by working with Intel, Asus, nVidia, Western Digital, etc. Create a logo. Trademark it. Promote the logo. Sell certification services that correspond with a right to display the logo.

Anyone can make hardware in the given formfactor, and anyone is free to reproduce and modify the established standards all they want... but only folks that play ball with you can display the "LSUS-XX" logo. (Anyone can spin a Linux Distro, but only folks with approval from the [Linux Mark Institute]("http://www.linuxmark.org/") can actually call it "Linux" in the United States.)

People and business can then purchase laptop components and assemble their own laptops. If the LSUS/LSEU logo is present and has the right number, they can be absolutely confident that the parts will assemble into a functional laptop without circut boards sticking out at crazy angles.

If I see a pre-assembled laptop with the LSUS-15 logo on it, I can purchase it with the confidence that if something ever breaks... No one company will have a monopoly on being able to repair that laptop and be the sole provider of replacement parts at whatever price they feel like setting.

Alternatively, I can build my own netbook to the exact specifications I desire by going on tigerdirect.com and filtering search results to "Show only LSUS-8". I will have the confidence that any hard drive, ram, netbook case, netbook keyboard, and display I come across bearing the "LSUS-8" logo will assemble and create a functional netbook.

You wont be the next Steve Jobs with your standards organization, but you may be able to earn a very comfortable living.

I'd be a customer.

Understanding how open hardware works can be pretty hard, and examples are few.

The best example I can think of, is Arduino [http://www.arduino.cc/]("http://www.arduino.cc/").
They have had huge success with it, and yes it has been copied by numerous other companies. Some have made changes to make it cheaper, others have made other kinds of changes, and direct copies do also exist, and yes they all make money from it.
Still the original Arduino sells much better than would have been possible if it had not been open source.
The open source nature makes this project much more powerful than would have been possible otherwise. if you want to make something yourself, chances are that you can start your project almost half finished, and at a low price.

I'm not quite sure what you mean by keeping the hardware free, producing hardware cost money, I don't see a way to change that.
