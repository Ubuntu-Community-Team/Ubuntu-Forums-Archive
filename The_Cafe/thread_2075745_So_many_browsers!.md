### Post by Statia on 2012-10-24
[SIZE=1]A recent thread about Microsoft's browser choice screen (or lack thereof...) made me curious to the choices being offered.
The usual suspects would be Firefox, Chrome and Opera. I decided to dig into it and see which browsers are being offered and where they originate from.
Turns out no less than eleven alternative browsers are being offered!

[/SIZE]   
[LIST=1]
[*][SIZE=1] Opera, by Opera Software. Norway (not a EU member!)[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Maxthon, by Maxthon Asia. China[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Firefox, by Mozilla. USA[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Chrome, by Google. USA[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Internet Explorer, by Microsoft, USA[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Dragon, by Comodo. USA[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Avant, by Avant Force. China[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1]K-Meleon. Their websites are not working, but it is GPL'ed and not developed by a company.[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Lunascape, by Lunascape Inc. Japan[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] SRWare Iron, by SRWare. Germany[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] Rockmelt, by Rockmelt. USA[/SIZE]
[*][SIZE=1] SlimBrowser, by Flashpeak. USA[/SIZE]
[/LIST]
  [SIZE=1]
So, of the eleven alternatives for IE, only one is made in an EU member state.

So how can anyone state that the browser choice screen is a ploy by the EU to give European companies unfair advantage?[/SIZE]      [SIZE=1] 




(And for the argument that only American companies get fined by the EU: bollocks.
European companies get fined all the time, it's just not newsworthy for most American media, so it does not get media coverage in the US.
Don't get me wrong, I am not a EU fanboy (au contraire), but this is one of the few things they did get right.)[/SIZE]
