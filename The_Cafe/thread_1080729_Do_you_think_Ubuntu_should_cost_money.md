### Post by AusIV4 on 2009-02-25
Like most of the others here, I'm going to have to say definitely not.

The freedom is one of the bigger things that sets it apart from Windows. Suppose Ubuntu cost $20. What does that $20 get you? Does it get you the CD and let you install it on all of your own computers or does it let you install it on just one computer? Once you've installed on one PC, can you just use the Live CD on another PC? Are you still authorized to make persistent USB flash drives that would let you run Ubuntu on any computer it was plugged into? If so, does that flashdrive constitute a use of the license?

If it costs money and requires a software license, it loses a lot of its value.

Aside from that, the way most of Ubuntu's packages are licensed upstream, I suspect that a free version would quickly be forked back out, much like RHEL and CentOS. Ubuntu would become the version for those who need enterprise support, but most of its current users would move on.

Don't get me wrong, I've given money to Ubuntu (through the store) and contributed time and code to several packages upstream (I also have a project I hope to release in the next few weeks. I'm strongly in favor of users giving back, but don't mess it up with charging per license. That would be the surest way to kill Ubuntu's popularity.

### Post by airjaw on 2009-02-26
The decision to start charging for Ubuntu is a philosophical choice and only one that Mark Shuttlesworth and Canonical can make. I doubt they will go down that path because one of the core principles of Ubuntu is "Free software".

Now, thats not to say that there aren't benefits to charging for your software. If Ubuntu relied on its sales to keep them afloat, then you can be sure that they'd have fixed all the issues that have plagued their releases (and other linux distros). This is because when you are selling a product, your first priority is the customer's satisfaction. That means no stupid sound, video, wireless card issues out of the box like Ubuntu had, has, and will have in the future.

That is just the nature of free software - there is no incentive to fix things immediately. Developers have other jobs and lives and are doing it out of their free time. There is a ready-made excuse for them: they're not getting paid, so why do they need to bust their *** to fix any bugs? They get to it when they get to it, if ever. There are no such excuses in the business world, either you get it done or you go out of business.

You get what you pay for and Ubuntu is no different. Granted they do a pretty darn good job but that is just the nature of free software. I foresee no end to the endless issues of video, audio, and wireless incompatibilities for at least the next 10 years. There are too many vendors and too many disparate groups and technologies to bring together. Without some kind of umbrella linux organization that sets standards for everyone to follow, confusion and competing distros/standards will remain.

That again is the nature of the beast of free will. Sometimes its better to force one standard on everyone rather than let many different standards into play. Think IE vs. FF, HD vs BlueRay, XP vs Mac, etc. Competition is good but only up to a certain point. Can you think of what mess the web would be in if there was no W3C to enforce HTML and CSS standards?
