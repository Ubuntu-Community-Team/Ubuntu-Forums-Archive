### Post by Brandel Valico on 2010-11-03
Ahh Cloud Computing 

How this concept wasn't created and patented by Apple or Microsoft to the point they are letting others do it amazes me.

Just a quick readof the wiki for it makes me shudder.

> Details are abstracted from the users, who no longer have need for expertise in, or control over, the technology infrastructure "in the cloud" that supports them.

One of the many reasons I switched to Linux based Os's is to have "Control" of my technology not them. 

> In general, cloud computing customers do not own the physical infrastructure, instead avoiding capital expenditure by renting usage from a third-party provider. They consume resources as a service and pay only for resources that they use. 

Ahh so it is very similar to the current OS's avaliable. Where you don't own anything physical they allow you to use it for a fee. Your licensed to use it. But at anytime they can come and take that right away. Again part of the reason I switched to Linux based Os's. 

> The underlying concept of cloud computing dates back to the 1960s, when John McCarthy opined that "computation may someday be organized as a public utility". Almost all the modern-day characteristics of cloud computing (elastic provision, provided as a utility, online, illusion of infinite supply), the comparison to the electricity industry and the use of public, private, government and community forms was thoroughly explored in Douglas Parkhill's, 1966 book, "The Challenge of the Computer Utility".

If I was a conspiracy nut I would wonder how long after this concept become mainstream they start taxing and deciding what I can access. Divide it into long distance and local fee's.

> A cloud client consists of computer hardware and/or computer software that relies on cloud computing for application delivery, or that is specifically designed for delivery of cloud services and that, in either case, is essentially useless without it.

Now lets think about this. If it does become a Utility type system. If you don't pay and your computer is basically just a client box with no actual applications or any other useful things on it. When they cancel your service. Your computer is useless. Why are people supporting this idea again? 

> The Cloud model has been criticized by privacy advocates for the greater ease in which the companies hosting the Cloud services control, and thus, can monitor at will, lawfully or unlawfully, the communication and data stored between the user and the host company. Instances such as the secret NSA program, working with AT&T, and Verizon, which recorded over 10 million phone calls between American citizens, causes uncertainty among privacy advocates, and the greater powers it gives to telecommunication companies to monitor user activity.

That speaks for itself

> Richard Stallman said that cloud computing was simply a trap aimed at forcing more people to buy into locked, proprietary systems that would cost them more and more over time. "It's stupidity. It's worse than stupidity: it's a marketing hype campaign", he told The Guardian. "Somebody is saying this is inevitable – and whenever you hear somebody saying that, it's very likely to be a set of businesses campaigning to make it true." 

Now while I may not agree with everything the guy says. Some of it makes sense. This is one of those things that does to me at least.

-----------------------------------------------------------------

So what is my favorite O/Os you ask? That would be none of them.
