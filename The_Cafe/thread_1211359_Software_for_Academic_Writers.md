### Post by Maupertus on 2009-07-12
Hi everyone,

I wanted open a topic about this in the Cafe for ages, I don't know why I waited so long.

I'm writing my Master thesis on European Environmental Law, and writing some essays and articles on the side (on basicly the same topic) 

When on Uni, I've been using MS OneNote a lot, which works pretty well. When I want to write at home, I use Evernote, which is great in collecting content.

But ofcourse, it just feels annoying to use Windows, while I've been using Ubuntu for ages (and have been 'converting' fellow students for just as long ;) ). The fact that OneNote is a MS program and Evernote propriatary without a *Nix client, doesn't sit well with me as well.

Now, this should be the place where a nice little collection of information can occur wich could help (Academic) writers. 

Hence the question: What is great software to use when writing academicly?

I'm really looking for opensource products that run on Ubuntu, but if you have a real gem, please mention it in a small sideline.

For now, I've started using [Tomboy]("http://projects.gnome.org/tomboy/") a bit for my drafts, the Wiki words are nice to link certain snippets of information I've collected. However you need to 'translate' it into an other format to insert correct quotations and sources. But its total lack of editing options is nice as it isn't distracting. As I use it in combination with [Dropbox]("http://www.getdropbox.com/"), syncing is nice as well.

[Zim]("http://zim-wiki.org/") is a nice information manager to keep track of your research. How ever, again, you can't use it for the final product.

[Freemind]("http://freemind.sourceforge.net/wiki/index.php/Main_Page") is a mind-mapping program that's pretty versatile, you can use it to start with your subject and let all the branches cover your different points of view or (in my case) see the different historical developments. This is fun and handy for yourself, but I find it difficult to translate the 'map' into tekst. It's also pretty great for creating writing plans.

I'll add links later this evening. For now I'm hunting for a good reference manager.

Hope this can make some people happy. :)

Edit: I can not stress the usefullness of Dropbox, either for syncing things like Tomboy. But the version control and collaboration possibilities make it great for article reviewing while using your own software of choice (ie. OpenOffice vs. Word)

### Post by Lifelock on 2009-07-13
Well, speaking as a computer scientist, I use Latex/Bibtex for every article I write. The ease of breaking down an article in multiple files and handling of references with this combination is invaluable, especially when working with multiple authors. And the mathematics support in Latex is very helpful when I have to write down equations properly. Images are created using Inkscape and included in eps/pdf format.

Biggest downside I can think of when compared to MS Office is the lack of the ability to track changes in a document. This can be very helpful when multiple people are reviewing documents and proposing improvements. Mostly we get around this by setting up a cvs- or svn-server, combined with annotating pdfs, but I can definitely see the benefit of the track changes feature.

As mentioned earlier, depending on where you submit your article (journal or conference), you are bound by layout restrictions. Nowadays in my field, support is pretty good though. Most major journals and conferences require the use of standardised layouts (ACM, IEEE, etc.), all of which are available in both word and latex templates.

This is even the case when you are writing bookchapters or small books, such as monographs. When large books with complex layout are targeted, the publisher typically takes care the layout. All the authors of the individual contributions need to do is provide their piece of text in an initial layout (again based on existing word or latex templates).
