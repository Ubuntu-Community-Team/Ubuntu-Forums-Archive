### Post by angryfirelord on 2008-05-21
> **az said:**
> Books become obsolete really quickly.  With laptops, you get the whole library and much much more.

That's the point.

I like the new design.
Maybe theoretically an e-book would be beneficial, but there are too many problems for it to be effective. First would be the cost. Negroponte claims this device will cost only $75, yet the OLPC 1.0 ended up doubling in price. I suspect this device will reach at least $125, if not more.

Second is the durability. If I rip a page in a book, a little scotch tape (and some glue) is needed to fix it. If I nick the screen, well that's going to cost a bit to fix it and it impedes the reading experience. If the battery wears out, what's that going to cost? This device is certainly more rugged than a regular laptop, but I still would doubt its survival as a electronic reader in a much more rugged world. With a book, I can drop it off a two-story house and still be able to read the pages. 

Third is licensing and this is a big issue for Negroponte. Unlike the Internet, where all you need is a broadband connection, books usually have authors that like to get paid for their work. So where does the cost reside? Is it whoever pays for the local server? What about file-sharing, would that induce any legal issues? How would one determine if a book is "sold"? This is where DRM can be lulled in, thus crippling the usability of the device.

Fourth is screen resolution. With good paper and a good printer, the print you usually see is of a very high resolution. On this device, in order to make it cheap, the resolution is going to have to be low. Computer screens tend to put eye strain on individuals who aren't used to them.

Now to answer your point, yes there is some content that tends to go obsolete. But what about novels or plays? What about elementary content such as math, language, history, and even science to an extent? This info will become obsolete, but at a much slower pace than say, ASP.NET development.

It's a neat idea, but I don't see it as being practical.

For one, the Windows version does not support the popular "group meeting" features of the OLPC.

Actually there are many off the shelf ebook readers for Windows XP that are crippled with DRM, and that are free as in beer. Starting with Microsoft's own [http://www.microsoft.com/reader/default.mspx]("http://www.microsoft.com/reader/default.mspx"). So the cost to implement DRM in Windows XP is essentially zero.  You do not need anymore hardware support for DRM in Windows XP other than what is provided by Windows XP itself. What the DRM application needs to know is what the hardware is, in order to "lock" the content to the hardware and must trust the Operating System, and its vendor, ***but not the user*** to provide the correct hardware information, which is why DRM can be made to work with Microsoft Windows but it is next to impossible to make it work with GNU / Linux.  Try finding a ***DRM crippled*** eBook reader for GNU / Linux. 

As to why DRM support was not announced by the OLPC, they already have enough bad press around supporting Windows XP to add more.

There are many advantages of ebooks over paper books, provided the ebooks are not crippled with DRM. The one downside of electronic information is that is has to be regularly copied in order to preserve it. DRM prevents this so it ensures that the information is lost in a few years. One can read the content of a 400 year old book or a 3000 year old stone tablet but try listening to MSN music protected with Microsoft DRM 10  or 20 years from now. DRM is literally creating a technological dark age right under our very eyes and by supporting Microsoft Windows and by extension DRM crippled ebook readers the OLPC is making this problem worse.
