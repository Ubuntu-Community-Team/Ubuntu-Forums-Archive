Regarding security, you're making the classic fallacious argument for "security by obscurity." Countless security experts have destroyed this argument and demonstrated that open-source security solutions are, in virtually all cases, far more robust than proprietary solutions.

The fact that, as you say, people can "search through the code and find flaws" works both ways: while hackers can look for weaknesses, so too can those interested in improving the software. It also means that exploited vulnerabilities get fixed far more quickly than waiting around for a company to release a patch.

Also, with closed-source software, a hacker may find a flaw through "illegally" reverse engineering the code, but legitimate users would be unable to do the same thing without violating the terms of their user licenses. This puts evildoers at a distinct advantage.

No security architect worth his/her salt would ever consider closed-source security software to be necessarily better than open-source. The very obscurity that closed-source software relies on for much of it's security is an illusion. Determined intruders will always find a way around.

### Post by DarkOx on 2008-04-01
Moving away from DRM...

Open source isn't very good for niche shrink-wrap software. That is to say, software that you purchase copies of similar to other products. I don't see how, say, Photoshop could be open-sourced and yet Adobe could still afford to pay people to develop it, since there's no real service to sell the same way there is for Red Hat. 

There's the GIMP of course, and the consensus seems to be that the GIMP's good enough for most people (heck, it works for me). But for that subset of users that really needs all the features of a proprietary app, open source probably fills that need either too slowly or not at all, since there's no profit to fuel development.

(In the interest of keeping this from becoming a GIMP vs. Photoshop thread, you could also compare Excel vs. Calc or bugzilla vs. launchpad or something. For most things, Calc is fine, but when I needed to use solver, only the proprietary app filled that need.)
