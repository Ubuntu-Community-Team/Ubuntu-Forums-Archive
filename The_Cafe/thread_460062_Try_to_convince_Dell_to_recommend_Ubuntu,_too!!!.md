### Post by BarfBag on 2007-05-31
Ubuntu isn't ready for that kind of publicity.  Imagine this - the most computer illiterate person you know decides to buy a Dell.  On the front page of the site, Dell recommends Ubuntu and points him to the section where he can order an Ubuntu PC.  Seeing that it's a couple hundred bucks cheaper, he orders it.  He plugs it in and expects it to be Windows (even though he ordered Ubuntu specifically), and since it doesn't look like Windows - it sucks.  DVD's don't work, MP3's don't work, he can't figure out how to install iTunes and AIM, etc.  We already know how easy it is to install codecs and that Pidgin and Rythmbox are out-of-the-box replacements for the applications mentioned, but a computer illiterate person isn't going to.  Imagine all the backfire and bad reviews that would cause.  I know a lot of people like this, so it's very possible.

Here's what needs to happen first.  Dell has to print a small book on how to use the basics of the system, and ship it with each PC.  Then, we have to work on making Ubuntu even more out-of-the-box (as in - you're not screwed if you don't have an internet connection).  Once at least these things happen, I don't see what you said working out.

### Post by aysiu on 2007-05-31
Here's the real problem.

Although some people call The Geek Squad for help or call Dell's phone support for help, a huge chunk (possibly a majority, but I have any hard data on this) of users get Windows support from friends, family, co-workers, and acquaintances.

At this point in Ubuntu's development (in terms of both the software quality and the market penetration), it's probably best to try to win over early adopters (Windows power users--the people others call on for tech support) to get into Ubuntu. Even though that's the toughest crowd to switch, it'll make later migrations from "everyday folk" easier in the end, as the early adopters can recommend a Ubuntu Dell for purchase *and* support the purchase with tips, tricks, and troubleshooting.
