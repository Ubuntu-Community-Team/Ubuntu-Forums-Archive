### Post by JKyleOKC on 2011-11-16
Most laptops have two batteries and an external power adapter that's not a battery at all but might look like one. The power adapter is the thing on the cord that gets warm. Mine has a cord on each end, one of which connects to the laptop and the other connects to the power socket on the wall.

Of the actual batteries, one is inside the case and the other usually looks like part of the case but can be removed. The one inside the case looks like a coin, usually about an inch in diameter. That furnishes standby power to the internal clock and the configuration memory but does NOT power the rest of the system; if it goes bad you might find the clock needs to be reset every time you power up, or might even find that the machine no longer knows it has a hard disk in it. However the one that causes problems at start-up time is the much bigger one that's outside the case but made to look like just a part of it. On my laptop, for instance, it's on the bottom at the back, and is almost a foot long, three or four inches wide, and a half inch thick. This is the one that can cause problems when it goes completely dead. I simply removed it, use it as a paperweight now, and run the laptop from the power adapter. If I ever need to go portable with it again, I'll buy a new battery, but they cost about $150 for my machine and last only two or three years...

As for the "rust" you found, that sounds like it might be on the clamp for the little coin cell inside the case. It might be corrosion, from a leaky cell. That could do permanent damage to the hardware! These little coil cells cost less than $1 each so when in doubt about one, just replace it. However when you do replace it, you may lose some critical configuration information, so unless you know how to keep from losing that, take the unit to a good repair shop to do the job! If you've been taking it out, though, and the machine still knows about its disks, you might be able to replace it without harm...

### Post by Old_Grey_Wolf on 2011-11-16
> **bedpotato said:**
> 
...Should I buy a new battery? If so, which is the battery at fault? The one inside the laptop, or the one that's attatched to the wire? Is that even a battery? That little box thing that gets warm. (You can see I really don't know much about hardware at all)!

This is similar to what JKyleOKC wrote above; but, worded differently.

Some people have already addressed the CMOS battery; which, is the small button (coin sized) battery on the motherboard; however, I don't think that is the battery you are asking about.

 The black box attached to the wire is not a battery. It is an adapter (transformer) that converts the Alternating Current (AC) from the wall outlet to Direct Current (DC). From the websites you linked to, it appears that you may be in the UK; therefore, the black box is converting 230 volt AC to something like 10 to 12 volts DC.

Once the adapter converts the current to something that your computer can use there is a recharge circuit inside the computer that determines if the battery inserted in the laptop needs to be charged or if the battery is fully charged. If it needs to be charged, it will divert some current to recharge the battery.

In my experience, if the adapter fails, the computer doesn't boot regardless of having the computer's internal battery inserted or not. That is just my experience.

The symptoms you describe could be a faulty battery or a faulty recharge circuit. I would tend to go with a faulty battery; because, you say it does not occur every time. If it doesn't happen every time, then I think the recharge circuit is working; however, the battery is not holding a charge. This is just my opinion. Without having the measurement tools available you really have to guess. The measurement tools can be more expensive than making a guess, then finding the guess was wrong.

The cost of replacing a battery varies widely. You would need to compare the cost of replacing the battery versus replacing the laptop. There is then the uncertainty that the battery is actually the problem and not the recharge circuit. 

It is really an analysis of your confidence in you diagnosis of the problem and how much you are willing to spend if you are wrong.

You'll only need a recovery disk if/when Windows messes itself.  It sounds like your shop is pretty good, ask them about creating restore media if your new machine doesn't come with restore CDs/DVDs.  Many new laptops sold in the U.S. come with a hidden restore partition on the hard disk.  A certain key combination will launch a process that will restore the machine to factory software state.  That's fine until the disk fails and takes the restore partition with it.  Or the restore process won't start for whatever reason.  If you have some sort of external restore media you can install a new hard drive,  employ the restore media and you're back in business.  Linux, because install media is not restricted has no such concerns.
