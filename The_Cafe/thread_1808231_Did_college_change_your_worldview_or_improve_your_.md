Critical thinking includes a complex combination of skills.  Among the main characteristics are the following:

Rationality

We are thinking critically when we

Self-awareness

Honesty

We are thinking critically when we recognize emotional impulses, selfish motives, nefarious purposes, or other modes of self-deception.
Open-mindedness

We are thinking critically when we
evaluate all reasonable inferences
consider a variety of possible viewpoints or perspectives,
remain open to alternative interpretations
accept a new explanation, model, or paradigm because it explains the evidence better, is simpler, or has fewer inconsistencies or covers more data
accept new priorities in response to a reevaluation of the evidence or reassessment of our real interests, and
do not reject unpopular views out of hand.
Discipline

We are thinking critically when we
are precise, meticulous, comprehensive, and exhaustive
resist manipulation and irrational appeals, and
avoid snap judgments.
Judgment

We are thinking critically when we
recognize the relevance and/or merit of alternative assumptions and perspectives
recognize the extent and weight of evidence

I'm glad you qualified your last statement with "In my opinion..." because my experience runs contrary to yours.  I find that we live in a time where we have developed the tools of inquiry for resolving factual questions (i.e. questions about what is the case) to the nth degree.  Meanwhile, our handle on normative questions (i.e. questions about what ought to be the case--political and moral questions) is probably no better than it was in Plato's time 2400 years ago.  

My first two years in college were spent on physics and mathematics because I believed that these disciplines would allow me to work on the most difficult questions confronting our time. I converted to philosophy my junior year as I came to the realization that the more proximate and intractable challenges facing humanity are political and moral questions--and that our technological culture had all but forgotten how to ask these questions clearly let alone answer them.

I'm not going to disagree with you that there are plenty of over-educated idiots in academia that would rather indoctrinate than educate.  But there are some really fantastic people too.  For me, the difference is that the ideologue is a narcissist who only wants to see students that are the intellectual image of himself or herself.  The true educator, on the other hand, wants to enable the student to educate himself or herself even if that means that, at the end of the day, the student will challenge the educator's own beliefs. 

All of that said, I think it is a shame that one can complete college or university without at least having one's fundamental beliefs challenged.  The contemporary college or university is largely a shadow of its predecessor:  a giant tech school where the emphasis is not upon developing an individual capable, in turn, of critically developing the culture to whom they are beholden but upon conforming that individual to a particular task within a culture relatively flattened by the unchallenged demands of production, consumption and economic efficiency more generally.

> **krapp said:**

I'd like to hear your theory of who gave these ideological professors a job. Yes, I know ideologues give jobs to ideologues, but how did academia come to be filled with perverse professional leftist propagandists?

The idea that academia is saturated with leftist ideologues is largely a myth that has been created in order to silence criticism.  Some of the criticism is worth discussing while other views are so much hot air, but that's the nature of dialogue and we should encourage rather than discourage inquiry.  At any rate, I've found that most academics are apolitical or centrists.  The fact is that the vocal, radical minority--both right and left--makes a better story for an industry that is more interested in selling scandal than representing reality.

Full disclosure, I am an academic that tends to lean to the left.  While the old adage that says communism looks good in theory but is terrible in practice is true, I think the same can be said about unrestrained liberal capitalism (hell, John Locke himself said as much back in 1689).  As far as I'm concerned, many of the post-colonial European political-economic models strike a more favorable balance than do the extremes of Soviet-style socialism or neo-liberalism.  Just crunch the numbers and, if that's not enough, take stock of what environmental science is telling us about the ecological consequences of industrial production.

The point is not to discuss politics which is forbidden, but to simply make the point that I don't carry my particular views into the classroom.  In an introductory class I will teach the historical debate, so to speak, focusing on the historical foundations of liberalism and socialism.  Similarly, if religion is the topic, I will teach the historical origins of Christianity and its critics in turn.  The focus is on interpreting clarifying and understanding the implications of each view.  

Students often become frustrated and ask, "Ok, but which one do you think is right?"  I just shrug and say that my job isn't to teach you what to think but to show you how to frame a question in the first place.  "Who cares what I think?  In a democratic society, your thoughts on the matter count as much as mine do and there's a lot more to read so you better get started," is my answer to that question because the task of the educator is not to to teach you what to think but to enable you to engage in inquiry.

Every time I step into the classroom I remember the jackass professors who would try to ram a particular view down my throat.  Even if I agreed with them I chaffed under their instruction precisely because I felt like I was being manipulated or at least sheltered from alternative positions.  On the other hand, I remember the handful of great educators that encouraged me to challenge myself by confronting precisely the issue or position that I was prejudiced against and, in so doing, helped to develop my own ability to engage in inquiry and self-education.
