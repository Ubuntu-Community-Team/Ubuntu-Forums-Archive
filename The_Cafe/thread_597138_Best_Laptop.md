### Post by igknighted on 2007-10-30
> **cyclefiend2000 said:**
> this may not be too popular to say on a linux forum.... macbook.

i own an old ibook right now, and will definitely be saving my money for a sleek shiny macbook once it is dead.

I used to be a fan of apple hardware and defend them against the "overpriced" tag... but recently the macbook line has just gotten so pricey compared to equally spec'd laptops from other brands.  The macbook pro's are very price-competitive, but who buys $2,000 computers?  Not many people that I know.

@ the poster who just ripped HP (and god knows I'm no fan of theirs), HP is by no means to blame for your troubles.  They make so many models of laptops (as all companies do), and many are 100% linux compatible, while others are almost impossible (again, this is true of almost all laptop companies).  Just do some research about the specific model before you buy to determine if that specific model works OK.

There really isn't any major OEM that offers significantly greater chance of getting linux compatibility, unless you buy a laptop designed for linux (specific models from Dell, System76, Lenovo, etc.).  Some tips I would recommend:

Avoid the x200M from ATI.  This card has several variants and you never really know which one you will get.  One common one is an absolute nightmare to configure on linux.  IIRC the 1100 or 1150 series of ATI cards is the same chip with a different name, so be wary there too.

Be careful with the intel GMA X3100 graphics cards too (GMA 950 is fine).  The X3100 has a bug that wont allow compiz and video playback at the same time without a hack.  Since the intel drivers are OSS this should get fixed in the not-too-distant future, so not a reason to avoid the card per se, but be aware of the issue.

Broadcom chips work pretty well in Gutsy.  Atheros wifi works well using madwifi (included in Ubuntu by defaul).  Intel 3945 and 2200 wifi works.  RA2500 chips work well.  Be careful with any draft-N technology.  This might not work.

When you have it narrowed down to a few models, check a few forums to see how they work on linux.  The hard bits to check via specs alone (ie, not user reviews) are sound and power management (battery life, suspend/resume, etc.).  If these get good marks from users, you've probably got a keeper.
