It sounds like a typical Norwalk-type of virus transferred via fecal matter (most likely from an infected galley worker who didn't wash their hands after a trip to the head). A ship's closed water and waste systems can sometimes create or in the very least aid in a fast transfer. Desal (desalination) systems have also been suspect since the virus can be introduced from a recent open water dumping.

As for safety, since it's very illegal to pump (and easy to catch) any grey water waste in a harbour and the passangers are confined to the ship you are very safe anywhere off the ship. If something weird happens and people start getting really sick they can easily order the ship out of British waters. Most cruise ships have been designed to be converted for hospital duty while underway. With a month's work they can be completely converted to hospital or troop transport use. A smart idea the Brits came up with after WWI. Paid of big time in WWII and is still a requirement of all cruise ships today. 

When there's a shipwide infection like this the best thing for everyone (on and off the ship) is to keep all the passengers right onboard until matters are resolved. Add in that your tap water on land is well processed your chances of getting it are very, very slim. Besides, these viruses rarely kill resonably health people.
