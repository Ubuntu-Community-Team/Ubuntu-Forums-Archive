### Post by samjh on 2010-02-04
[SIZE="4"]Updated: iiNet wins appeal.  See post 29.[/SIZE]

--------------------------------------------------

A very significant court decision was handed down today by the Federal Court of Australia.  It is a closely-observed case both in Australia and overseas, as it is probably the first legal ruling in a higher court about the obligations of an internet service provider to protect copyright owners against infringement by its customers.  The complainants in this case are several large movie and television studios and producers, represented by Roadshow Films Pty Ltd., and the respondent is Australia's third-largest ISP, iiNet.

Link to full decision: [http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/sinodisp/au/cases/cth/FCA/2010/24.html](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/sinodisp/au/cases/cth/FCA/2010/24.html)

Here's my short brief of the case, based on the judgement's summary only (ie. this is not authoritative, and should not be construed as legal advice):

------------------------------------

34 movie and television studios sued an Australian internet service provider (ISP), iiNet, for allegedly authorising the infringement of copyright by its customers.  The studios, represented by Roadshow Films Pty Ltd. and aided by the Australian Federation Against Copyright Theft, alleged that iiNet authorised the copyright infringements because although iiNet was aware that copyright infringements were taking place, it failed to:
[list][*]Warn its customers that they are infringing copyright.
[*]Disconnect customers who infringed on copyright.
[*]Or take other "reasonable" steps.[/list]

The case hinged on three issues:
[list][*]Whether copyright infringements took place.
[*]Whether iiNet was aware of the copyright infringements.
[*]Whether iiNet authorised the infringement of copyright by failing to take the reasonable steps described above.[/list]

The judge found these answers to those issues:
[list][*]Copyright infringements did take place.
[*]iiNet was aware that copyright infringements took place (but disputed the number of infringements).
[*]Failure by iiNet to take warn, disconnect, or take other reasonable steps to halt the copyright infringements, **did not** constitute authorisation of the copyright infringements.[/list]

Furthermore, the judgement notes:
[list][*]That iiNet (and by extension, other ISPs) provide legitimate communications facilities not designed or intended to infringe on copyright.
[*]It was the use of the BitTorrent protocol which enabled the copyright infringements.
[*]BitTorrent can also be used for legitimate (ie. lawful) purposes.
[*]And very interestingly: *"The law recognises no positive obligation on any person to protect the copyright of another. The law only recognises a prohibition on the doing of copyright acts without the licence of the copyright owner or exclusive licensee, or the authorisation of those acts."*[/list]

Although this is a very big victory for ISPs, it should be noted that the Federal Court ruling can be appealed to the Federal Court of Appeal, and if that appeal fails, may then proceed to the High Court of Australia.

The judgement **should not** be seen as a blanket exemption for ISPs against copyright claims, nor should it be understood to justify any kind of copyright infringement activity or authorisation of such activities.

Interestingly, iiNet and other ISPs do have policies in place to report infringements of copyright to police.  However, Roadshow and its co-complainants have alleged that in iiNet's case, those policies are not as strong as what they want.

Another interesting fall-out is that in October 2009, iiNet signed to a licensing agreement with Village Roadshow to distribute its content.

IMHO, online streaming is something that movie and television studios should be taking advantage of.  Streaming allows them to control when and how content is distributed online, without intruding too deeply into its customer's rights (as current DRM schemes do).  Furthermore, streaming allows content to be distributed more cheaply than physical production and sale of DVDs or Blueray disks.  Online streaming should be a win-win for both studios, consumers, and ISPs.
