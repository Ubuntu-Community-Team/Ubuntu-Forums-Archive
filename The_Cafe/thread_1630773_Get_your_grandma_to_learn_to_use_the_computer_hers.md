### Post by czr114 on 2010-11-25
> **KiwiNZ said:**
> If staff were having those issues and my support teams were receiving calls such as the type you are describing then I regard it as IT that has failed and I would be asking my IT managers some hard questions and seeking to raise the standards. Thankfully in the units I have lead it has not been an issue as I regard user eduction as a primary performance goal.

It was a definite management failure, but the helpdesk had a perverse incentive to perpetuate it. Easy tickets made for great stats in volume, resolution %, and turnaround time.

Management left clueless users to barrage the helpdesk with repetitive questions as there was no real training whatsoever, even though much of the userbase was being exposed to computers for the first time in their careers.

For example, when a vendor rolled out a web portal to manage workflow and document submission, the users were given a short memo, a brief demo, then turned loose.

In the opening phase, one frequent problem arose when users completed a document in MSO, but then were dumbfounded as to what came next. Helpdesk cleared so many situations by reminding the user to open the portal and access the pre-loaded bookmark.

I came away from that situation with a renewed understanding of the importance of computer literacy education. Whether office personnel or seniors, novice users can't be expected to be thrown headfirst into an unfamiliar environment and thrive in it. There are simply too many conceptual skills which must be learned prior to effective use of even highly intuitive interfaces.

For seniors, a combination of simple interfaces like this with computer classes at public institutions like libraries or senior centers offers the best hope of bringing that demographic into the digital age.

While the helpdesk situation wasn't ideal for one on one training, a family relationship is, which is why all of us should be open and helpful to elderly family members who might feel intimidated by early computer education.
