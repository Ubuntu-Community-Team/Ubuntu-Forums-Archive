While it's true that Betamax was superior to VHS, and both were around at the same time, VHS didn't win out because it was already established. It won because it was an open standard that manufacturers could use without the hefty licensing fees that Sony demanded for Betamax. The upshot was that VHS players were much more affordable, and due to this large user-base, accessories such as blank tapes were also much more affordable, and films were much more readily available in VHS format. ### Post by gnomeuser on 2010-05-26
> **lightstream said:**
> While it's true that Betamax was superior to VHS, and both were around at the same time, VHS didn't win out because it was already established. HTML 5 promises an open alternative to Flash, and that's why it's creating a stir - ultimately because control-freak Apple hate to give up control of their platform to anyone else, including for that matter their users.

However, I hope the market is wise enough (fat chance) to avoid using the non-free H264 video codec, and instead choose Google's VP8.

I really do hate when people mix up the struggle for open formats with the ongoing rivalry for being the defacto web platform.

We need open codecs regardless of who wins out. Some platforms like Silverlight and Flash attempt to at least define a baseline by having requirements for certain codecs to be supported. HTML5 did not go this route. Regardless throwing ones weight behind HTML5 because one supports open codecs is a mistake.

It is a common struggle, WebM may be a strong contender for solving the codec issue. All the platforms will benefit and several have even announced and implemented support (HTML5 + WebM e.g. is in nightly Firefox and Chromium builds). I read yesterday that someone is porting core components of WebM to Silverlight (since it for security reasons only allows codecs implemented in managed languages).

They are separate issues. One does not imply victory in the other.

Support WebM but be realistic about it's patent status, assume there there will at least be attempts to go after it. There was with Microsoft's VC-1 codec as well despite their intentions to have it be a royalty free codec when they released it. 

As for which web standard should be the defacto standard, I would encourage looking at the technical capabilties of the different options. Personally when I do that, I see that HTML5 is hyped on the video / audio tags but other parts of the standard are very poorly developed. Silverlight on the other hand seems very technically solid to me and much more capable and productive.
