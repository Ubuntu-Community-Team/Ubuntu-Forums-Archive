### Post by Mighty_Joe on 2009-08-25
I'm a software engineer.  
Prepare for change. My job didn't exist when I graduated a mere 11 years ago (I write web apps now).  I had 5 different employers (including a 2-year stint of "self employment" after the 2001 crash) in 9 years.  I wrote code for 7 years before someone used anything I'd written.
The main project I'm on now is in maintenance mode, so there's not a lot of deadlines or long nights.  I collaborate with a small team to come up with new features, go on bug hunts, put out fires when the users start them.  I invent other projects to supplement my main responsibility so I can keep current with developments in the software world.  Don't stand still!
I've had some jobs, mostly contracting, that had 20-hour days and absolutely toxic co-workers.  Lucky for me they were short-lived.  One thing about this job, it's very similar no matter who you're working for, so you get a lot of flexibility when you can move around.  
Employment opportunities early on will depend on you getting experience.  Look for a program that can set you up with a job while you go to school (we called it a "co-op", short for "cooperative education") and hold out for a full-time job that will involve real coding. No advantage to having part-time experience babysitting the computer lab.  There's a big difference between writing code for classes and writing professionally.  The more professional experience you have, the less your prospective employer will have to invest in you to bring you up to speed.
Good luck!

### Post by Johnsie on 2009-08-26
I was working as a temporary data entry clerk. When our programmer left I happened to be the only person at the company who knew PHP so I was offered the job. (I did software engineering at university and that helped). 

What to expect? Depends where you end up working. Generally in most office environments you may be one of the few people who know anything about computers...  Here's what you can expect if this is the situation:

1. You will be asked to write software to do things... but the people asking you to do it will have no idea what they really want. They will either mumble when they try to talk technical or hit you with a load of garble that makes no sense. You will have to decipher the garble and convert it into logical thought before you even start. Having a notepad with you to write the garble down is helpful because you can sit down later and work out what the crap they were talking about.

2. Being a software engineer does not just involve writing or planning software. You may get asked to do other things, like fixing computers, setting up printers or software packages. You may even get asked to make lots of phone calls. To the average office person you will be the computer wizard who can do anything with computers.

3. People will expect things there and then. The average joe thinks you could write a complex program just like that and will ask you how long it's going to take. Expect them to be disapointed when you say it might take a few days. Always give yourself much longer than you think it will take to do the job. That way if you run into any problems you will have time to [play with.

4. People will expect you to know everything about computers. Don't be afraid to ask other professionals for help. If you're really desperate rent-a-coder.com can be a useful resource.

5. Any design theories you get spoon fed at school or uni can go out the window. Alot of time you simply don't have time to go through a long design phase. Alot of time I barely even have time to do proper testing.

6. You may have to work with old/bad equipment and may not have access to the software. Not all companies are willing to spend money on software or equipment. You fight with the army you have, not the army you want.

7. Maintaining other peoples code sucks.... Open source people go on about how open source programs are good... but when you actually have to go in and maintain/support other peoples code it isn't always that fun. When you write your own stuff you know where things are and you know your coding style. The other guy might think differently and it might be hard for you to work what is going on. Commenting your own code as much as possible is very helpful, especially when you have to go back to something 3 or 4 months later and have no idea what's going on.

8. Tell people to save to the network rather than their desktops or my documents. Normally the network file servers are backed up nightly. Most office grunts don't backup their stuff but will run to you when they lose their work.

Of course this can vary from company to company. Some companies that are more IT literate will be more understanding of your situation.

### Post by Wistful on 2009-08-26
You have two options ahead! [COLOR="Red"]ComputerScience[/COLOR] or [COLOR="Red"]ComputerEngineering[/COLOR]. Or maybe there is a third option, self-learning.

Unfortunately, I had to learn the difference the hard way. Last year after I graduated from High School and finally finished the exam, called, **le bac** ( [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baccalaur%C3%A9at](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baccalaur%C3%A9at)), with a fairly high degree, I went confident to a CE University for 2 semesters and studied enough to get a scholarship. But things weren't so bright because I studied some disciplines related to Electrical Engineering, like Physics, Electrotechnique, Methods Numerique, Logical Hardware Structures and Mathematics embedded with theory linked to these fields, which are the basics for CE, and I thought myself why the heck I need to study this things if I want to learn Software Engineering, and build a career in application development. 

Prior to being at this CE faculty I had no idea what the difference was between CS and CE, and I went and asked some of my friends from higher years which studied at this faculty, what is all about. After this I followed closely what disciplines I was going to study in the next 3 years and I decided to resign from the CE faculty because my plans do not fit in their perspective.

Now, after I decided that CE is not for me, the reasons for this can be found in the link posted in a previous post ( [http://www.eng.buffalo.edu/compscie_vs_compeng.php](http://www.eng.buffalo.edu/compscie_vs_compeng.php) ), and other articles,which you can find on the web, I went and submitted myself to a new University, this time a Computer Science faculty and got admitted. And hopefully, from the disciplines presented on their website I think this time I got what I want.

So, my advice is to be very careful what you choose, [COLOR="Red"]CE or CS[/COLOR] because this two fields which by some people are used interchangeable in a misleading way can differ in their perspective of teaching Software Engineering/Programming. Take time and study the differences, and then decide. Hope my experience will help in some way or another.

Cheers! ;)
