### Post by Chronon on 2009-07-23
Classifying certain words as base or crude is a classic tactic by a conquering culture to promote assimilation of their own culture.  For instance, in England, French terms became the terms of culture and government and English words for the same thing were considered crude.  This is the reason that you tend to call foods by French-based names rather than English ones (e.g. pork and beef).  

It's a neat trick.  Get the conquered people to accept that their own culture is crude and primitive and that your own culture is sophisticated.  Soon they will thank you for saving them from the crudeness of the old ways.  At least that's how it works in theory.

I find it interesting that there are no real "curse" words, as such, in Japan (as I understand it).  The worst curse word that I am aware of in Japanese is chikusho, which basically means beast or non-human.  I wonder whether this is due to relative lack of a history of occupation.

### Post by gnomeuser on 2009-07-23
> **swoll1980 said:**
> The guy I know with tourettes yells "pipe!" and "couch!" he then says a curse in a normal tone, because he was mad at himself for slipping.

Okay, let's do the quick and incomplete Tourette's guide. Basically you have three main groups: Vocal tics, Muscular tics and mental effects.

Vocal tic is the one that is the most famous, though the swearing is only between 10-30% of sufferers who have (depends on the survey you read). This relates to all semi-involuntary uttering. Such as the example above or me I like to yell homo at my parents, I also seem to like the danish word for goblin/elf (which ever helps santa where you live) which is "nisser". This is called semi voluntary because you are in fact aware that it will happen, you can to a degree delay the yelling if things are optimal (i.e. no stress or external interferance). This is how most TS patients get through life, we yell in private, we get through things like job interviews through concentration. It is hard to keep under control at all times though and like your friend above it is embarrassing when one slips. Most sufferers have vocal tics without the element of swearing. It is not currently well understood why this is the case.

Muscular tics would be any muscular moments, throwing of the head, twitching of the arms or legs and so on. Fairly easy to notice. Muscular tics by means of what is the cause cannot happen while you sleep, this is one way to rule out TS for such symptoms 

The mental aspects of TS involve things like OCD, manic thoughts, control issues, ADHD, ADD, depression and so on.

TS affects boys with 3 times greater likelihood than girls, for the reason girls with TS are also often diagnosed later as it a lesser risk and thus consideration.

To be declared a TS patient you only need to fall into one group (there are other requirements but let's keep this simple). Some people have just one aspect, others 2 or 3 to varying degrees. Often though the depression part is an add-on diagnosis as a result of the TS' effect on your life rather than the TS itself. This is typically because onset of symptoms happens in boys between 10-12, and kids can be cruel. TS kids especially those whose parents aren't TS sufferers themselves can have an extremely hard time growing up as being picked on constantly and being a constant outsider.

So the swearing itself, is just a subset of a subset of TS. it happens in very rare cases and even there it can often be helped with medication, therapy and concentration (as well as certain lifestyle alterations, regular exercise e.g. has been shown to improve life quality for patients as well as cognative therapy). For every extreme cases of the muscular tics there is the option to have electrical probes inserted into the brain, this is the same option offered to certain Parkinsons patients. This is dangerous and to my knowledge has only been attempted once, however the result is astounding.

Now me, I am affected to a degree of all three groups of TS, I have the component of swearing. It does greatly affect my life, so much so that I am unable to work or function to the same level as a normal person in my every day life. I have been in medical treatment for some 9 years now, and nothing has had an effect. I am though not a candidate for surgery and would not take it if I was offered at the current stage as I consider it to dangerous. Typically TS decreases it's presence in the patients life after puberty, however in my case it has accelerated and I was only diagnosed at 16 which is far later than normal. I have the unfortunate delight of being very sensitive to medication, so when I have effects the sideeffects are often worse than the improvement I get with other symptoms. E.g. once I got medication that made me so restless I couldn't sleep.. for days, I couldn't sit down to eat (and let me tell you eating soup when you are compelled to having the bowl in the your hand and wandering around the dinner table, without sleep for 2-3 days.. an interesting but not recommendable experience).

Luckily the illness is controllable, I measure out what I think I can do in a day, if I exceed that limit I pay the price with a day spend mostly in bed without control of my arms and legs or other interesting effects. I function day to day with help and by not stretching my budget of time. I also have to prepare my surroundings the best I can, when I meet new people I have to explain that if I swear at them it isn't because I'm angry or anything. 
My friends and family know the rough guidelines and try to help me out where I need. When people see me, I generally don't appear sick, just lazy, I keep things in check that way and hope one day some medication will work. Allow me to have a family, take care the house and children, perhaps even work and go dancing (to many people and loud noises tends to cause really bad periods for me but I try every once in a while to please my fiancée who loves dancing).

For most people though, TS is a personal quirk, a little twitching arm every once in a while or a compulsion to say a certain word. Most people don't need medication or other treatment.

I hope that helps.

One idea behind swear words is that they are intended to shock people.  However, as time goes on, different things shock different generations of people.  At least in US culture, go back a couple centuries to a time when the population was much more religious, and you'll see that religious oaths were what shocked people.  You can look at literature and see expressions like "What the devil," etc., that would provide no reaction in most of the population today.

These days, those words which are considered to be the worst curse words are those that are related to certain physiological functions.  The change happened when the population became much more secular.  Some of the older curse words stick around (such as the words that "heck" or "darn" stand for) but give considerably less shock to those who hear them.

You can even see the current "bad" words losing their shock value these days.  They keep making their way into TV shows and movies one by one (look at TV series from ten years ago and you won't hear certain choice words).  All this exposure means that people are becoming less and less shocked by them, and the younger generations are hardly affected by hearing them.  Instead, the words gaining more and more shock value are those negative words given to people of certain identities, such as race, ethnicity, LGBT identities, etc.  The years may change the way people do things, but the underlying intent to shock people is timeless.

As a side, the studies claiming swearing provides pain relief are interesting, to say the least :popcorn:
