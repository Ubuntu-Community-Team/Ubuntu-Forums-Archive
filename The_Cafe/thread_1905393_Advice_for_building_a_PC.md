### Post by Black_Sector on 2012-01-06
Some of the new Thinkpads and Thinkpad Edge series have docks that you can use to plug your computer in. Laptops are not as easy to fix or upgrade, but if you get a really kickass laptop and a doc its easier than fiddling with cords and it wont burn out your power supply. I got a Thinkpad for $1400 from TigerDirect and it has an i7 and 2gb video memory from Nvidia and up to 9hours of battery life using the intergrated intel graphics isntead of the Nvidia Quadro.

HOWEVER, if you were to get a desktop, I would say you could save some money and go AMD. You dont necessarily need the power saving and scaling features of the i7, and a Quad is more than anyone needs these days. You can certainly get a hex, and you wont feel much bottleneck. They are great for the money.

Also, you will almost never need 16gb of ram unless you are running live sessions and doing video editing while booting an OS from ram. I have RARELY gone over 4gb, with torrents and 40 tabs open, doing video encoding WHILE I was playing 3d intensive gaming.....Still didnt break 4.5gb, though it went past the limits of x32 without PAE kernels. 16 is just stupid.

If you go AM3, you will need to make sure it supports SLI if you want to go Nvidia.....ATI is the better value in Windows, but the drivers suck in Linux again. Once the ATI drivers are fixed though, I think ATI is a better value IF it works. However, Nvidia has a vested interest in Linux and Android, and AMD/ATI does not......I would prefer to support the underdog, but they dont seem to care about their fellow underdog in the Linux community.


I like having a better sound card, but thats because I use my desktop for an entertainment center.

Asus makes great motherboards, though I never liked their keyboards.....always felt cheap and springy.

I like getting the 'bare bones' with the mobo already installed and all the little wires in the right place, then I just plus everything else in myself.

### Post by cbanakis on 2012-01-06
Personally, I will NEVER buy a desktop.  (Always build my own)
Laptops, and all-in-one systems are another story.

I have always stuck to my pattern of buying all my parts 1 level below top of the line.
(top of the line has a way higher markup, because its top of the line)
And in most cases, you can go down 1-3 levels without ever noticing a difference in performance.

Depending on your exact needs, If your planning on getting/building a desktop AND a laptop, I would suggest getting a cheap netbook (>$300)

If you get a good/expensive laptop, no matter what, it will suck compared to your desktop, so whenever you need performance, you'll be running for your desk.
(in which case, whats the point of spending top dollar for your laptop)

So why not save the money, and get a practical netbook, so you can check your email on the couch.

Some benefits to building your own desktop...

You dont have to pay for anything you dont want (including windows)

A lot of the hardware will never really be outdated.
(Case, Power Supply, Case Fans, Optical drives, Hard Drives, etc)
These are all things you usually only need to buy once, then come upgrade time, all you really need is a new motherboard and processor.

It is usually cheaper to build your own.
There are plenty of cheap desktops out there, but I assure you, if you look into them beyond the front seat specs, you will find that all sorts of corners were cut, in order to make it as cheap as it is.
(Not always, but usually)

Some recommendations...
[www.Newegg.com](www.Newegg.com) for buying
Asus Motherboard
(And a cheap no name company "Rosewill" makes allot of cheap parts that are shockingly well designed)
I always saw Rosewill stuff on newegg, and chuckled a bit thinking "how crappy must that be?"
When I finally ordered a Rosewill part, I was so surprised at how nice it was.
