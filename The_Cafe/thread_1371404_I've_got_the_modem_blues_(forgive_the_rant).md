                TELEPHONE LINE NOISE -- SOLUTIONS

BACKGROUND:
   The Telephone Set is simple in appearance and operation yet it
   performs a surprising number of functions.  Here is a list of
   the most important ones:

   1.  It requests the use of the telephone system when the handset
       is lifted by pulling loop current of 18MA minimum.
   2.  It indicates that the system is ready for use by receiving a
       tone, called the dial tone.
   3.  It sends the number of the telephone to be called to the
       central system.  The number is initiated by the caller by
       pressing number keys or rotating a dial.
   4.  It indicates the state of a call in progress by receiving
       tones indicating the status (ringing, busy, etc.)
   5.  It indicates an incoming call to the called telephone by
       ringing bells, audible tones, or lights.
   6.  It changes speech of a calling party to electrical signals
       for transmission to a distant party through the system.  It
       also changes electrical signals received from a distant party
       to speech for the called party.
   7.  It automatically adjusts for changes in the power supplied
       to it.
   8.  It signals the central system that a call is finished when a
       caller "hangs-up" by breaking the 18MA minimum loop current.
 
LOCAL LOOP:
   Each subscriber telephone is connected to a central office that
   contains switching equipment, signaling equipment, and a power
   supply that furnishes direct current to operate the telephone.
   Each phone is connected to the central office through a local loop
   of two wires called a wire pair.  Normal house wiring will use
   the four conductor STATION WIRE which has the following colors.
   RED, GREEN, YELLOW, and BLACK.  Typically just the RED and GREEN
   are used for a single line phone.  Businesses or newer installations
   may have a three pair cable.  The primary wire pair usually is the
   BLUE with WHITE tracer and the WHITE with BLUE tracer.  The other
   wire pairs may or may not be used depending on the application.
   For the typical station wire, one of the wires is called "T" (for
   TIP - GREEN) and the other is called "R" (for RING - RED) which
   refers to the tip and ring parts of the plug used in manual
   switchboards.  When the connection is established the two telephones
   communicate over transformer coupled loops using the current supplied
   by the central office power supply.

   NOTE: If the phone is wired with TIP and RING wires reversed,
         the phone can only receive calls.  When you try to dial
         out the first number hit will break back to the dial tone.
         Just reverse the RED and GREEN wires (at the phone) to get
         it working properly.

INITIATING CALLS:
   When the handset of the telephone is resting in its cradle, the
   weight of the handset holds the switchhook buttons down and the
   switches are open.  This condition is known as "ON-HOOK".  The
   circuit between the central office and the telephone handset
   is open, but the ringer circuit is always connected to the
   central office. (90 VDC ringing)

   CAUTION: If you are testing the phone while it rings you may
            get shocked by the 90VDC. Be careful, not sorry.

   When the handset is removed from its cradle, the spring-loaded
   buttons come up and the switchhook closes.  This completes the
   circuit to the central office and current flows in the circuit.
   This condition is known as "OFF-HOOK".  The "OFF-HOOK" signal
   tells the central office that someone wants to make a call.
   The central office returns a dial tone to the called phone to
   let the caller know that the central office is ready to accept
   a telephone number.

LOCAL LOOP LENGTH:
   In order to discuss the changes to the circuits in the telephone
   set, we must consider the portion of the system that we cannot
   change, but which the electronic telephone set must interface.
   This is, of course, the local loop from the central office.
   It is important that the electronic telephone use the loop
   current from the telephone company to provide power for the
   electronic circuits.

   The resistance of the telephone set is typically 400 Ohms in the
   United States.  The line resistance of the wire pair varies
   with the length of the line from the central office.  As the
   total resistance in the local loop increases, the current
   decreases.  Therefore, for the same speech input, variations
   in the local loop current become smaller as the line length
   increases.

LOOP CURRENT:
   Typical voltage measurements from TIP to RING with "OFF-HOOK"
   conditions should be -6.8 VDC with "ON-HOOK" being -48VDC.
   By inserting a DC MILLIAMPMETER in series with the TIP wire,
   current measurements can be taken for "OFF-HOOK" conditions.
   Typical "OFF-HOOK" current should be 18-40 Milliamps

   ABSOLUTE MINIMUM  18MA
            MAXIMUM  40MA
            NORMAL   18-40MA      "MODEMS GET PICKY ABOVE 30MA"
            OPTIMUM  24-26MA      "MODEMS LOVE IT"

RURAL or REMOTE LOCATIONS:
   The loop current may be increased in rural or remote locations
   to provide better voice service for rural customers.  This can
   cause severe problems for MODEM users.

TROUBLESHOOTING:
   There are several things the MODEM user can check while waiting
   for "THE PHONE COMPANY" to respond.  Any bets on what they tell
   you?  "SIR, YOUR LINE IS VOICE QUALITY AND CHECKS GOOD".  HA!
   (Then why can't I use my MODEM at 2400 Baud?)  Basically it's
   because they don't care, and want to sell you a BALANCED DATA
   LINE along with being TECHNICALLY INCOMPETENT or I wouldn't
   be writing this document.
   Run the following tests on your equipment:

   1.  Try your MODEM at a friends house.  (IT'S GOOD .... I KNOW)
   2.  Unplug all phones in your house and try the MODEM.
       Portable phones can cause problems, so don't forget them.
   3.  Dial 1   --  Then listen at the line. It should be "CLEAN".
   4.  Test the "ON-HOOK" and "OFF-HOOK" voltage.
       Place a Voltmeters BLACK test lead on the RED RING wire and
       place the RED test lead on the GREEN TIP wire at the
       MODEM JACK.  "ON-HOOK" should be -48 VDC with "OFF-HOOK"
       being -6.8 VDC. (Mine were -41.0VDC and -9.0VDC)
   5.  Test the "OFF-HOOK" current.
       Insert MILLIAMPMETER (Voltmeter set for Milliamps) in series
       with RED or GREEN wire and measure the loop current.
       Check the values against the above chart.  MODEMS like
       the 24-26MA range.  If readings are Negative reverse the
       voltmeter test leads.  (Add 100 Ohms or more to get 26MA.)
   6.  Listen for noisy lines as you talk local and long distance.
       (Phone company may have water in their lines, or a bad wire
       pair feeding your house, or a bad CARBON PROTECTOR.)
   7.  Keep other phone abusers in your household off all phones
       while you are on the MODEM.  Just picking up a handset will
       cause lots of garbled data on the screen and maybe kick you
       offline.  DON'T OVERLOOK THIS ONE.
   8.  Try a FAX machine as they are more susceptible to noise and
       loop problems.  If the FAX works then your MODEM should.
       (Your FAX may be dropping to a lower baud rate if it has
       FALL BACK.  You may think it is at 9600, when it is really
       working at 4800 or 2400.)
   9.  Lightning can wipe out your equipment if it is always plugged
       into the wall jack.  Just unplug your MODEM when it is not in
       use.

NOISE:
   There are three types of noise:
   1.  Impulse Noise.  These are low-frequency voltage spikes caused by
       older telephone company equipment.  It sounds like a pop or
       a "crackle".
   2.  Background Noise.  Present in all phone circuits and at low levels
       is harmless.  Has the sounds you hear when you have your radio or
       TV tuned between stations.
   3.  Interference.  Caused by defective telephone company equipment,
       telephone wires between your house and the telephone company,
       wiring within your house, etc.
   4.  Faulty PROTECTORS.  The Phone company has either CARBON or GAS
       PROTECTORS installed inside their termination box that hangs on
       your house.  The older BLACK boxes have CARBON PROTECTORS while
       the newer GREY PLASTIC boxes have the GAS PROTECTORS.  These
       devices shunt large spikes, such as lightning strikes, to earth
       ground.  The older CARBON PROTECTORS can be a source of noise if
       they have sustained a "HIT" from lightning.  The newer GAS
       PROTECTORS will shunt voltages above about 350 to earth ground.
       The GAS PROTECTORS can take several "HITS" before eventually
       being destroyed.  The Phone company takes care of these, but you
       can remove them long enough to find out if they are the source of
       the noise.  (They are installed to protect the wire pair, not your
       equipment.)  The PROTECTORS will have a HEX head of about 3/8 inch.

   Have you ever turned the volume control on a radio or TV and heard a
   scratching sound?  Well your brain tries to separate it from the
   actual sound and it isn't a big deal.  In computers that scratching
   sound is known as 'line noise' and the brains in computers aren't smart
   enough to separate noise from real data so you get weird characters.

   Line noise can be induced at a number of places.  A loose modem cable
   at your end.  Bad or improper telephone wiring.  (It's easy to check
   each termination in each wall jack in your house.)  A FAX or answering
   machine sharing the same telephone line.  Cheap telephones
   (the $9.95 type). Problems at the telephone company.  A defective
   modem at the BBS or at your end.

   If line noise is the problem then build and use the enclosed low
   pass filter.

CALL WAITING:
   Do you have Call Waiting on your phone.  Usually, an incoming call will
   disconnect you from the BBS, but it could cause line noise.  Does the
   noise come at regular intervals, say the time of a ringing phone?
   Most phone systems will allow you to disable call waiting.  Just
   pre-fix a *70, before the phone number.  For example the following
   command will dial out with call waiting disabled.
   
     AT DT *70, 555 1212

   This will only disable the call waiting for one outgoing call.  You
   must do it each time you dial out.

PAD IT OUT:
   If the current is greater than about 28MA then you may have
   to "PAD OUT" the MODEM'S wall jack to get the proper current.
   The absolute best way to do this is to insert a decade box
   and a DC MILLIAMPMETER in series with the RED or GREEN wire
   and adjust the decade box for 24-26MA.  After you have the
   MODEM working fine just remove the decade  box, and insert
   the proper resistance value in the MODEM'S wall jack.  Be sure
   to use a wire wound resistor of at least one watt rating.
   These are very inexpensive and are quite small.

MODEM WIRING:
   Most modems have a jack that says "LINE" and another that says "PHONE".
   The one that says "PHONE" should be wired to all the phones in your
   house.  In other words the first thing that your phone line should
   connect to is your modem and then when your modem is "OFF-HOOK",
   all the phones will be disconnected by it.  All your phones should
   be DEAD in the house when your modem is "OFF-HOOK".  I don't really
   recommend changing all the wiring in your house to support this, but
   it is reasonable.  Especially if it fixes your problem.
