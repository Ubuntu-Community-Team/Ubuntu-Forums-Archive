Here you go. 
look for the "position" variable, to move the window. You paste this into your .conkyrc, but first find Radio Space fonts and move it to your .fonts folder. 

```
##############################################
#Settings
##############################################
background yes
use_xft yes
use_spacer yes
xftfont Radio Space:size=11
xftalpha 3
update_interval 1.0
total_run_times 0
own_window yes
own_window_transparent yes
#own_window_type override
own_window_hints undecorated,below,sticky,skip_taskbar,skip_pager
double_buffer yes
minimum_size 400 5
maximum_width 1366
draw_shades no
draw_outline no
draw_borders no
draw_graph_borders no
default_color d8d8d8
default_shade_color 000000
default_outline_color d9d7d6
alignment top_middle

gap_x 12
gap_y 24
no_buffers yes
uppercase no
cpu_avg_samples 2
override_utf8_locale no
##############################################
#  Output
##############################################
TEXT
Host: elkoraco@crunchbang  |  Kernel: $kernel  |  RAM: $mem/$memmax  |  Disk usage: ${fs_used /}/${fs_size /}  |  CPU usage: ${cpu cpu0}%
```

thanks!

It may surprise you, but this is Ubuntu 11.04 with Unity. In ccsm, there is a setting for panel transparency.  I set it to 0.  Then I had to get rid of the nasty shadow under the panel.  I'm on another computer right now, so I can't check, but the file for the panel shadow is in one of the subfolders of /usr/share/unity.  It's called "shadow.png" or something obvious. I copied the original, named it shadow.png.old, and opened the other one in a image editor and erased it so everything was trasnparent.  The shadow was gone at next login.

The conky isn't sooo complex, but it is actually two stacked on top of eachother so I could get the staggered look (I hear there is another way to do this now, but I haven't tried it yet). I made them just from modifying the default conkyrc and reading the man page, but I also looked at other people's conkyrc files to get ideas.

The quote at the bottom is actually a third conky that gets updated regularly from a feed.

The real trick is the background.  I wanted to make my panel and conky look like one peice, so I set everything to transparent, made a flat-color background, and took a screenshot.  I opened the screenshot in gimp and made a new layer with transparency at about 75, and just put black in all of the paces that I wanted shaded (this took a while, to get the shape exactly as I wanted it).  I saved that as .xcf.

Then, whenever I want to change the background, I open that file, drag in the picture I want for the background, scale if it needs it, and then I put the layer with the image below the transparent conky-shaped background.  I export that to JPG or PNG, and set it as my background.  This takes about one two minutes extra, but I think the results are worth it.

I'm working on something similar right now for my netbook, which has an Arch/Openbox/AWN setup (netbooks are supposed to be slow, but I've got this one loading openbox from boot in about 15 seconds :D).  It will not look less good than what I have on my main computer.

### Post by el_koraco on 2011-08-31
To configure scrotwm with nm, in the transitional phase, do the following. 

```
sudo vim /usr/share/xsessions/scrotwm.desktop
```

Paste this in

```
[Desktop Entry]
Comment=This session runs the scrotwm window manager
Comment[it]=Questa sessione avvia il gestore di finestre scrotwm
Encoding=UTF-8
Type=XSession
Exec=/usr/local//bin/scrotwm.sh
TryExec=scrotwm
Name=Scrotwm
```

now, ```
sudo touch /usr/local/bin/scrotwm.sh && sudo vim /usr/local/bin/scrotwm.sh
```

I have this, you can pimp it up

```
 nitrogen --restore &
nm-applet &
scrotwm
```

chmod it, and you're good to go (take note of how I repsect you editor of choice, as much as I do hate it).
