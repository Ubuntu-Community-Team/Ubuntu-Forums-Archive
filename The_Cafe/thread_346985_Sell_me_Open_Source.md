When I see a big download button with no actual explanation about what I am about to download, I am willing to go through quite some inconveniences, just to find an alternative download, where the information is not hidden from me. My reaction to "Click the big button. It is cool." is: Excuse me, but how do *you* know what is cool for *me*?

Marketing is not about telling you what is good for you; it is about telling you that whatever this person/company/other entity is selling right now is good for you. That it is the only possible thing that can be good for you, so, yes, you can never be happy without it, you do not deserve to live without it, so give me your money/time/resources right now. 

Of course, it is all lies.

Do you like being lied to?

The capitalist economy gives something like a divine status to lies, with actual information being very much hidden--because, of course, you cannot efficiently lie when people know the facts. You cannot *sell* when people know the facts, if your thing is not actually great and they do not actually need it. Well, I want the facts, and I want them *right now*. Giving the facts, sharing information, *contributing* to information is what FLOSS is all about (in addition to writing good code). What amazes me is the existence of people who *do not want* the facts because they seem too complicated. The facts are there in your life, whether you want them or not, and if you do not choose what facts (programs, dvds, cars, whatever) you adopt for yourself, you may end up with some quite ugly facts indeed. Windows, anyone? I just got an advertisement from Amazon regarding the "new, wonderful, perfect" Windows Vista. Yes, they tell me I will like it. Yes, they do not give any useful facts. (Oh, and they do not even mention the word "DRM.") Should I believe them? I do not think so.

I can agree with you that FLOSS projects should give non-technical people information considered trivial by others. It is good to say "you can make calls from your pc to your phone," if the person has no idea what this is about. It is good to show screenshots on your website. What I disagree with is hiding the rest of the information (Yes, I do want to know that Skype's protocol is closed and incompatible with anything else, while Ekiga is SIP-compatible and you can use it to call your friends who use some other SIP program). And I don't want a flashing/moving/singing website that gives me bling to hide the lack of information. A screenshot on the front page, with links to other screenshots or videos is enough. :)

If I tell you this, it will mean that I accept that you should be dumb, and that everyone else should be dumb--that if for some strange reason they do not feel dumb, they should remedy the situation. This is "popular culture," and what happens in popular culture is that dumbness is advertised everywhere and dumb people thrive, while smart people are ostracized, and those that fall in-between sooner or later become *dumb*. 

Well, I will not tell you whether you will like the program (any program, I am not an Ekiga developer) or not. One day (hopefully with the help of FLOSS) the world will change, and you will have to make your own choices. Some advance training cannot hurt. :)

### Post by Erik Trybom on 2007-01-27
I think some kind of mix is often the best. Yes, we do want a lot of screenshots. We do want fancy videos showing the action. We do want to know why this software is the best one available, and where and how we can download it.

On the other hand, we want to know other stuff too. Is it licensed under the GPL? Is it free of charge, does it play ogg vorbis, is it made for KDE or Gnome, what are the dependencies, what hardware is needed, etc? All these facts should be just as easy to find as the screenshots. Ubuntu and Mozilla are excellent examples of good-balanced websites. 

I think most open source projects would benefit from a little thinking along these lines. It's not that hard and it attracts users. And I don't believe programmers in general don't care if people use their software or not. They're proud of what they've done and they do want people to use it, or they wouldn't have made it public in the first place.

### Post by aysiu on 2007-01-29
I go toward a happy medium when it comes to this whole "know everything"/"know nothing" debate.

I think it's okay not to know all the intricate details of how something works, but you should know enough about products you use in order to be safe and for the product not to break frequently.

For example, with Aspirin, you should know that it does make your headache go away after a while, but taking ten Aspirin every five minutes may land you in critical condition at the hospital.

With a car, you don't need to know how to change your oil or what a piston is, but you should know the way to drive so that your breaks don't wear out too fast. You should know to fill up your gas (or petrol) before it runs out. You should know when to take your car in for regular maintenance, even if you're not the one doing the maintenance.

Likewise with a computer--you don't need to know what RAM is or ROM is. You don't have to understand how the kernel interacts with the programs on top of it. You should, however, know enough to not have your computer trounced by spyware every couple of weeks. You should know not to do a forced shut down every time you turn off the computer or to regularly unplug USB devices without unmounting/ejecting/safely removing them first.

There is **basic** knowledge every person should have about products she uses. While it's unrealistic to expect people to know everything about everything they use, they should know at least *something* about what they do use.
