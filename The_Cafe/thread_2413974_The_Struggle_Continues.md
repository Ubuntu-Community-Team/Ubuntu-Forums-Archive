### Post by Shibblet on 2019-03-04
I have been a Linux user since Ubuntu 8.04.  I distinctly remember everyone talking about the much anticipated Intrepid Ibex (8.10) when I was first working with Ubuntu.  It all started when I purchased a Netbook (MSI Wind), and it had limited resources.  It came with a copy of Windows XP, and seemed to run just fine, but many people told me to give Linux a try.

This made a huge difference in the performance of my little netbook.  All of a sudden, I could see apps loading faster, boot up and shut-down times became a lot quicker.  I didn't seem to run out of memory all of the time, and most of the programs that I used were either available on Ubuntu, or had an alternative that could get the job done as well.

This action, of course, let me to install Ubuntu on my Desktop.  And this was a MAJOR increase in performance.  However, what I started to realize, was that I could not run some of the main programs that I utilize my desktop for.  Namely Adobe InDesign, Photoshop, and Illustrator, along with many of the games I was playing at that time. But, I really loved Ubuntu, so I stuck with it in a dual boot situation in order to keep Windows around for other productivity.

This was 10 years ago.  I have kept up with Ubuntu over the years... even switched to Kubuntu after Ubuntu went to the Unity Desktop... but I have always had one ingering problem.

Ubuntu, and/or Linux in general has progressively gotten much better over these 10 years, in comparison to Windows which has only progressed very slightly, and added some problematic things along the way.  From XP came Vista, and it was apparently a nightmare to most people.  And from Vista came 7, which everyone seemed to like.  After 7, was a major problem again with 8, and 8.1.  Finally, we have landed at Windows 10, which is better, but still has many problems.  The foremost of these problems (to me) is all of the background tasks that continuously communicate with Microsoft over the internet.  Sucking up your bandwidth, and rubbing your privacy in your face.

This is where Ubuntu and Linux in general really shines through.  They have progressively bettered themselves over the years, adapting to new hardware, and not spying on everything you do.  Unfortunately the Microsoft Machine keeps pushing forward as well.  So, over the past 10 years, I have done everything I can to "switch" to Linux, but to no avail.  The best I can do at this point, is dual-boot.

What it comes down to, is that I still need Windows Applications, and I still want good performance.  WINE is an AMAZING piece of software, but it does not work on the programs that I need it to work on, (Adobe CS).  I like the alternatives like GIMP, Scribus, and Inkscape…  But they aren't the same as Adobe software, and re-learning these programs from the ground up takes more time than I have to give.  I have been an "Adobe" user since the days of "Aldus Pagemaker" Circa (1995), and I can get a LOT of work done, very quickly in Adobe... not so much in the others.  

And Steams fork Proton has brought AMAZING compatibility with Windows games.  Unfortunately, I get better performance with these games in Windows.  Even games that have native ports, tend to run better in Windows.  Whether it's a driver issue, optimization off the code, or what-have-you, it just is.  And it's hard to sit in front of a PC and play a game that is getting 30-45fps, when you know it's capable of 60fps... just by booting into Windows.

Now, I know these two things are the biggest issue when it comes to Linux... Games and Adobe.  But they are the issues.  Also, VM's tend to help these issues.  Whereas I can run Adobe in a VM, games... not so much.  Performance issues again.

And I understand that Linux is not Windows, and it doesn't intend to be.  Compatibility with other OS's is (supposed to be) a secondary concept.  That being said, the Linux community is reaching out on it's own, attempting to find new ways to bring more software to the platform.  Specifically, the software and games we all want to use.  But it is unfortunately not up to par.

So, the question remains... What can be done to resolve this issue?  Steam and Proton are doing what they can to bring more Games to the Linux platform, along with WINE.  VM's allow for running of Windows programs... but then you're running Windows.  Is the only option, to make the choice NOT to use Windows software?  Games, I could live with making that choice... but Adobe, I could not, as I do need that software for work.
