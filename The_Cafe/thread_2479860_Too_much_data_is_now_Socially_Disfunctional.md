### Post by Shibblet on 2022-10-10
I quit Facebook about a month and a half ago.  It all started when FB began blocking my personal messages to friends and family when we were having discussions between ourselves (in Messenger, not on FB) about current events (strangely enough... nothing political).  I challenged their "block," stating that it was all private chat.  They persisted, I told them where to stick it, and now it's a full on deleted account.

Secondly... A couple of days ago, Google slammed the Stadia door in my face.  Then on top of that, told me that I could no longer use my YouTube Premium / Music service at the same price I had been paying for a family plan.  Long story short, they required me to purchase YouTube Premium / Music for all 6 accounts that were previously under my family plan at $14.99/month, to $9.99 per account instead.  So, quadruple my previous purchase price.  The claim is that all 6 people have to live under the same roof, and they don't.  Only 2 of the people on my family plan live under my roof, so I am not arguing the policy.  Sounds like I may be moving to Amazon for a music plan.

I have noticed a steep decline of these types of services over the past year.  And the reasons people use/quit these "platforms" are various. Regardless, they all seem to be "tightening up" their services.
Netflix, Hulu, Amazon, etc.  They don't allow multiple logins, and are starting to require 2-step authentication to alleviate this problem.
Zoom dropped it's usage for free accounts to 40 minutes back in June (July?), and now uses 2 step authentication.
YouTube is going to put 1440p to 4K behind a pay-wall.  This makes sense to me, as 4k video is literally 4x the data stream required than 1080p.

Most of these changes look as if the cost of Bandwidth and Data Usage are going up.  
I am concerned that these major internet companies have created dependencies for many people, and have now started tightening their grip.

Are we seeing the first steps in a "Data Resource Crisis?"  Can there even be such a thing?

### Post by qyot27 on 2022-10-10
> **Shibblet said:**
> This makes sense to me, as 4k video is literally 4x the data stream required than 1080p.
Minor quibble, but bitrate scaling isn't linear, especially as resolutions get bigger and bigger.  4K doesn't require 4x the bitrate of 1080p for the same relative level of quality; IIRC, the usual quote there is that it's actually more like 2.5x.  And (futurewise) 8K is not going to need 4x the bitrate of 4K.

Not to mention that advances in compression technology mean that the bitrate really doesn't scale linearly from one resolution (commonly in, say, H.264) to a much bigger one (which may be in VP9 or, increasingly, AV1).

That fact actually makes the potential of Google doing that to YouTube's 4K content *worse*, not better.

I just find it curious that people are okay with paying hidden charges that cost them far more than the up front charges, but will motivate themselves to take action once the up front charges increase.

### Post by TheFu on 2022-10-19
Expecting free speech to be protected on private property is like  .... er ... well, not a good idea.  When someone comes inside your home, do you allow them unlimited freedom of speech on any topic at all?  I don't.  If they want to talk about something I find offensive, they can do it from a public location or their own home.

Facebook is private property.  There are no "rights", just policy.

Maybe it isn't obvious, but services that are free and outside our control can disappear at any time.  Sometimes the reasons are withing the control of the service provider, but sometimes they are not.  Regardless, if you want something to be available, then you should probably self-host it. Then it is under your control.

Remember all the complaints when Google Reader was going to be shut down?  Lots of people were unhappy. Google couldn't figure out how to fit people scanning RSS feeds for interesting titles into their profit engine, so they cancelled the service.  But anyone who really cared could have used a different RSS feed reader or done like I did and put a "News" addon to my existing Nextcloud instance.  [https://apps.nextcloud.com/apps/news](https://apps.nextcloud.com/apps/news)
This pulls the RSS feeds to the nextcloud server and provides the same interface to any clients. As soon as 1 client marks a specific feed title as read, all the others have it marked as read too.  Basically, use any client you like, but never see a feed more than once. 

Remember read-it-later?  Mozilla either bought them or cloned the features.  I didn't like mozilla tracking what I'm reading and saving, so I stood up a Wallabag server.  [https://github.com/wallabag](https://github.com/wallabag)  There is a web server for desktops with an addon to "bag it" for reading later. Also, there is an Android client which can download all or selected saved webpages to be read while offline.  It exports in PDF, epub or just to the wallabag android app, your choice.  Always have plenty of reading material of whatever size you like, since there is an estimated time-to-read for each article.  Getting your oil changed?  That's 15 minutes, so you want an article that is 15 minutes or less to read. Easy to filter.  I had jury duty last spring - that's a full day of sitting and waiting with 2 minutes of listening at a time.  It could turn in to a full week, but since I know who is guilty just by looking at them, it was half a day.   /s   ... made it though about 50% of my saved articles to be read later ... all while offline.  When my tablet reconnected to the home LAN, everything read and deleted was removed from the wallabag server, never to be worried about again.  I do archive some articles that will never be removed. Seems lots of so called "news" websites delete their old stories after just a few months, so keeping their links alone is next to useless. The full article needs to be captured. Wallabag does this.

All these tools support per-user tagging, so a tagging system can be used to organize and find the stories later, along with full text search and date.

As for music, I've played with Amazon Music free, but nothing more.  It seems to be made of from uploaded music by other people, so isn't the full song or album. Very frustrating.  I'd rather not bother.  For video streaming, there is so much content available for free that just waiting 3 yrs will usually make the newest stuff available for free, legally, somewhere.  Wait long enough and everything eventually shows up, including that 1-season TV show that was not too successful, but you remember fondly.  Remember "Caprica" - the Battlestar Galactica prequel?  It is streaming for free now on the Roku channel [https://www.justwatch.com/us/tv-show/caprica/season-1](https://www.justwatch.com/us/tv-show/caprica/season-1) .  Eventually, everything shows up.

Use online services for what they are - someone else's computer trying to engage with your for THEIR profits.
