### Post by davahmet on 2007-09-09
> **nowshining said:**
> well a friend to friend - free - for him it's really a tough one but you could then charge little so I think a small charge would be fine and not a whole lot, due to this closeness - there is a diff. between a business partner and a friend or family member.. :)

No, not really.  Your argument seems to be along the line that you should never charge a friend for your services.  In essence, don't do business with friends.  I couldn't disagree more.

Nearly all of the business I do is with friends.  For that matter, I should say that I only do business with people I know, or rather, I get to know them and establish a friendly relationship as a necessary part of doing business.  Building a good relationship is a critical part of doing business if you want to achieve success.

If your perspective comes from the idea that doing business with someone means taking advantage of them, of not giving them real value in exchange for their money, then you are in the wrong business.  By this I mean that you should never have to shield your friends (or prospective friends) from your business offerings.  If you do so to sleep at night, then you are in the wrong business.

To the OP, my advice is to set a fair rate for your services, and to be completely up front with what you will do, how much it will cost, and most importantly, what you will not do.  Now, for a fair rate, I have in the past provided an estimate of how long the project will take, set an hourly rate based on similar work, and then add a 20% addition to the time requirement with the understanding that there will probably be mid-project changes.  I allow the client 2 changes and then it must be re-scoped as a new project.  About that extra 20%...the client should know it is a projected overage to allow flexibility on their part.  I also let them know that although they paid for the full amount up front, I will rebate them the prorated amount should the project come in early.  This gives them serious incentive to minimize their changes after they commit by handing you the check along with their specs.
