### Post by earthpigg on 2009-10-16
This is great.

I'm going to e-mail this to my dad - youngest brother will be going to high school next year, and my understanding is that the school provides the children with laptops.

Bypassing archaic security is the first step to ***understanding*** security.

It's not intentional, but the school giving these kids crippled systems that need to be 'helped' to achieve full functionality is probably doing a lot to prepare these kids - things coming with artificial limitations forcing consumers to either pay more money or find a workaround, unfortunately, has a very real possibility of being considered 'normal' in the future (see: iAnything. Windows Home Basic. et cetera.).

If one of my brother's peers has not done so by the time my brother enters High School, I will be walking him through this process, and teaching him how to propagate bootable USB drives.... 

in fact, an idea rolling around in my head right now is a [Masonux]("http://sites.google.com/site/masonux/") release with a desktop icon pointing to a script that (***after a very clear warning that requires two confirmations***) automatically locates and flashes any and all inserted USB media and puts Masonux on them (again, with that same script on the desktop by default of course).

*evil laugh*

the simple script would be [GPL]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNU_GPL"), of course, and be easily reworked to work with any distribution. [dd]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dd_%28unix%29") and [.img]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/.img") are *Linuxversal*, if you will excuse my pun.


If BIOS passwords start being implimented, I wouldn't be averse to maintaining a website that links to directions for how to flash the BIOS on commonly school-used laptops/netbooks.



Either let the kids ***use*** the computers, or ***don't***. Don't go in half cocked with crippled systems that will only teach the kids how to become mindless drones typing away in Microsoft Word... Remember the 19th century, where many schools primarily taught children the bare minimum needed to be a factory worker until death?

Teaching children to use proprietary operating systems is, after all, only a few levels away from being as bad as teaching them to smoke. Not ***nearly*** as bad, of course, but a similar shade of immoral.

(note that i said ***operating systems***, not software in general. an operating system is something that one way or another you ***will*** be reliant upon while using a computer. Unless your name is Linus or you are similarly gifted/inclined/knowledgeable, of course :D )

im in my late 20s for the record, but stuff brings out the teenager in me... the teenager that was banned from touching school computers :lolflag:

i do like to think i'm past bypassing security simply for the sake of software vandalism, though. 

will probably not have a graphical install-to-hard drive app shipped with "Masonux: Free Education Edition". naturally, i also wont do anything to ***prevent*** folks from installing [ubiquity]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubiquity_%28software%29"), either.

My Logic: in the process of learning what ubiquity is and installing the ubiquity package to a persistent thumb drive system, a point-and-click-happy teenager will hopefully realize/learn that using it on a school-issued laptop may get them in trouble. the lack of "install" icon on the desktop by *default* will prevent *accidental* hard drive installs.

instead, the "install" icon on the desktop would - after a very clear warning and user confirmation - detect and flash any USB drive plugged in and propagate itself.

desktop would also likely have an icon linking to a website encouraging students to learn more about WTF this "Linux" thing is all about, an icon that explains how to install ubuntu-restricted-extras, and one that points to information about how to install games (and other software) and lists some popular ones.

essentially - once one student at a school used dd to put the .img on a single thumb drive, it would propagate itself from there, turn the computers into video game consoles, and encourage learning.
