### Post by Paqman on 2009-10-13
Indeed, the problem is partly one of marketing. The trouble is, until Linux can be proven to be a good seller no-one in their right mind is going to spend the kind of money that it would take to break a new OS into the desktop market worldwide. Historically experiments with commercial desktop Linux have mostly bombed hard, so it's not surprising there's not much enthusiasm to push it. Just lookm how timid Dell's recent dabblings have been. They were clearly trying to limit their risk by only using minimal resources to test the market. Trouble was, the lack of fanfare meant it sank without a trace.

Google might be the one player that could change the game though, as they've got enough balls and a good enough reputation (not to mention vast amounts of cash) to just push something to market no matter what anyone else thinks. It'll be interesting to see. If Linux has a better reputation commercially in a few years time it could create an opening for distros like Ubuntu to take a slice of the pie. 

It's a catch 22 though. No flashy ad campaigns until it's already a proven seller, and no sales without advertising.

### Post by Algus on 2009-10-13
Well Linux faces a couple more issues as well

**- Stores stock software designed for Windows machines.**

Sure, we're all well aware of how easy it is to get new programs on Ubuntu (Add/Remove Programs button how I love you) but most folks "know" that when you want a new program for your computer you go to the store and buy it.  So they look at Linux and "Uh oh, none of my current programs are compatible and I can't buy new programs either!" 

So pretty much there needs to be a huge education campaign out there to show folks that 

1. It's ok to change programs; your new program is likely compatible

2. Software is ~easy~ to acquire with Linux 

Of course we have the alternate dilemma of Linux software being mostly digital distribution which probably begs the question, "Why would Bricks and Mortar stores support Linux if it meant lower software sales?" Though I think that if Linux had a higher adopted userbase, there'd be more commercial software available for it.  

**- Linux is not unified**

I think most of us would consider the lack of unity a strength amongst the Linux community; but that's because we're already believers.  With so many choices, consumers don't know where to go. 

I'm a believer in Ubuntu because I think Ubuntu has tried very hard to bring folks together and make Linux accessible for newcomers (I know that it's how I got my start in Linux).  However, it still has plenty of haters.  

Linux is not like other operating systems.  In fact, describing "Linux" as an OS is wrong anyway.  Linux is a shared idea that has given birth to many different kinds of operating systems.  It's really a different way of thinking about computers then Windows and Mac based machines.  People who aren't initiated into Linux don't understand this and it causes a great deal of confusion.  When they do go to try Linux, they're overwhelmed by choices.  I've been using Linux for a couple years now and even I still feel a bit overwhelmed at times.  Am I really happy with Ubuntu? Is there something else out there I might like more? 

Whenever I talk to folks who don't know much about Linux systems, I usually talk up either Ubuntu or Mint (Mint looks so much like Windows that I'm convinced it's an even better newbie distro than Ubuntu).  

The only way to get more people to scope out Linux systems is through an education campaign that can dispel myths about Linux and answer questions/concerns that people have.  Unfortunately, with a lack of unity from the Linux community (and just a little bit of infighting) that is getting pretty hard.  

Who's going to lead the charge though? Canonical? Novell? If the Linux community wants to see itself expand and become a true competitor to Windows, there's some questions that we have to ask ourselves.
