### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2008-12-01
Something has been happening a little bit in here. Someone is interested in Ubuntu, and in comes a parade of copy-and-paste posters. (Seriously, I think I've seen the exact post a few hundred times now). Paraphrasing, from one perspective:
"I see you are still using Windows Vista and have not explicitly stated that you don't like it. I suggest that you forget about all these issues you want to see resolved and continue using Windows Vista because it worked for you; a positive Ubuntu experience may only be fueled by dislike for the alternatives."
Thankfully, the very forward-thinking thread poster has a thicker skin than that and ignored the posts. However, I for one see very clearly the detrimental and unhelpful effect these posts can have.

Now, don't get me wrong, I completely understand that they are well-meaning! What is actually intended is that people should not feel pressured into anything. That they should use what they are comfortable with.

However, people - especially geeks - like to tinker. One does not necessarily need to be switching; just looking at something that is superbly cool, wondering what more there is to it.
Sometimes, even normal people are just curious about the other options.

I, for one, love both Fedora and Ubuntu. I use GNOME but have a growing interest in KDE ever since they pushed out version 4. I likely won't ever use KDE in its current state of licensing / ownership, but I hope it keeps innovating and contributing to FreeDesktop.org. I use Windows to play games, because I like computer games and see no reason to waste the Earth's precious resources on game consoles when my computer makes a perfectly good one (with an operating system tuned to that field).
People are allowed to be interested in more than one platform. People *should* be interested in more than one thing.

I sense an argument: 'There was no harm done; we just proposed an alternative alternative.'
But therein lies the problem. With the cautioning being incessant, people unknowingly say the following:

[LIST]
[*]"You (thread poster) should stop while you are ahead because we have, ourselves, decided that you are not suited to this."
[*]"Our knee-jerk reaction to any interest in Ubuntu is 'stay away'. Yes, even we users think people should avoid it. We like it less than unsatisfied *Windows Vista* users."
[*]"Your questions, (thread poster), are irrelevent. So irrelevent that we are going to post this form response to silence them." 'Obviously, the questions have no answers; Ubuntu sucks that much'.
[*]"You, Dylan, should not bother contributing time and effort to help (thread poster) because we think he should stay with Windows Vista". Believe it or not, the 50/50 ratio of "don't bother" posts actually did have me doubting the value of my contributions. I was moments away from just plain not helping because I felt that my contributions would be silenced / ignored by a fleeing thread poster. In a lot of cases, they could have been. I would have been sad if that happened, because I wrote a big wall of text.
[/LIST]

I'm not saying "don't do it". If someone is really obviously not going to enjoy Ubuntu, by all means point that out to him. But shaming Ubuntu as a weakness in general is not going to help anyone. You're going to make the person who's interested feel unwelcome and you're going to make the people here feel uncertain on their home turf.

If someone is posting here, in Ubuntu Forums, wondering about Ubuntu (or free software), that person is obviously interested and probably has an open mind. Tell the truth as you (probably) know it. At the very least, consider why you are here. Why do *you* consider this a good operating system to use?
For me: Ubuntu is a great platform for people interested in software development, for education, for casual use (64 bit Flash excluded...), for conspiracy theorists, tinkerers, artists who want to make a point. People interested in web development will be delighted to have a proper LAMP server running on demand aboard their development machine. People with bad eyesight will like changing the fonts on their desktop without losing clarity. People with mobility issues may not need Ubuntu, but will appreciate some free software we can offer them such as Dasher or Workrave.

Free software in general is not the exact goal here, but welcoming someone's first post in the Ubuntu forums with "don't bother" is not going to leave a good taste in their mouths for *any* of this stuff.

Try to welcome people, and don't spread doubts. That is Microsoft's job, not ours. It's often good to provide real, honest answers and leaving the final decision to the asker, but it is not so good to hound him into taking a particular path while providing none of the information he asked for.

Bottom line: Have faith :)
