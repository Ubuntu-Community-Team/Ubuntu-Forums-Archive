### Post by Erik Trybom on 2008-01-08
Read this:

"The Commission therefore seeks to establish a framework for DRM transparency concerning, amongst others, the interoperability of different DRMs, and ensuring that consumers are properly informed of any usage restrictions placed on downloaded content, as well as of the interoperability of related online services."

How is this a bad thing? I don't like DRM either, but as it is now DRM is used as a means to control the market. You buy an Ipod, and you can't listen to the digital music you bought from another store. You buy a copy-protected CD and can't play it on your computer (well, theoretically). You buy a Zune and the only DRM-encumbered files it will play are from the Zune store. The proposal is that the EU makes an effort to standardize DRM so that the customers get what they paid for. 

I do agree however, that the best thing probably would be to let the problem solve itself. The major record companies are beginning to let go of DRM and if they do, the customers will be much better off.

### Post by ZylGadis on 2008-01-11
> **kshane said:**
>  It boils down to** jerks that steal** and the rest of us suffer for it.

Copyright infringement is not stealing. Please stop perpetuating that common myth - it is a simplification that people who do not know better use, and the corporations are happy to exploit it.

It is impossible to steal information, because I can give it to you, and it still remains with me. In fact, I would argue that it is impossible to possess information for the same reason, which invalidates the term "intellectual property" (another common myth that people use without thinking).

To address your issue, it does not boil down to people who infringe on other people's copyrights. It boils down to broken business models and, above all, greed. I believe I am fully entitled to try something before I spend money on it, if the price is above a sensible limit. Luckily, I am not fond of the dumbed-down mass culture, so I am not in danger of being sued by MPAA, RIAA, and other AAAs. But if I were, I would have had no trouble at all infringing on their copyrights, so that I can make an informed choice whether to reward them for their efforts by spending money on their product. Laws exist to cement the social contract, not to favor the greedy; if morality is at odds with legality, to hell with the latter.

Read "Civil Disobedience" by Thoreau for a deep treatise on the matter. It is still valid today, as it always will be.
