### Post by raronson on 2009-07-23
Consider Two Examples From Recent History:

A rural preacher from New York named William Miller claimed that Jesus Christ would return, and that all believers would be taken to heaven on October 23rd, 1844.  This spawned a national movement, and thousands of people sold their belongings and relocated to eagerly await the day.

October 23rd, 1844 came and went like any other day, which quickly caused Miller to reset the date for six months later--this time using the Karaite Jewish calendar (instead of the Rabbinic).  That date too came and went, and came to be called "The Great Disappointment."

Similarly, some of the more radical adherents of the Y2K doomsday scenario claimed that on January 1st, 2000, all computers world-wide would come grinding to a halt.  I won't elaborate on this, since it's hopefully still fresh in our memories, though as we all know, Y2K came and went with minimal consequences.

Why should 2012 be any different?  In times like these I'm reminded of my favorite Sigmund Freud quote:

"Shall I believe every absurdity?  Then why this one in particular?"
