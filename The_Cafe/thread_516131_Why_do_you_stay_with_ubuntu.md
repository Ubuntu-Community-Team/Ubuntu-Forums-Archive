### Post by Alterax on 2007-08-03
For me, Ubuntu was a well-designed Linux distribution that made it easy for me to work with.  It's what my clients get; using what they use makes it easier to diagnose their problems.

As for problems that have crept up, they have actually been very few and far between.  When they do come up, it's really easy to jump to a conclusion and say that the distribution is beyond repair, that it is flawed and so on and so forth.  When you come to this conclusion, you look for another distro, use it and are pleased until the inevitable problem comes up.  Then you do the same thing and ditch it for another distro; the grass is always greener on the other side.

What I realized the last time I switched distros (actually to Ubuntu from Mandriva) is that jumping back and forth fits the definition of insanity:  doing the same thing over and over but expecting different results.  Every switch just puts you back at square one and you end up learning nothing from the experience.  It may be "easier" at first for a distro to do something for you, but that doesn't help your problem-solving skills.

This is not to say that I think a distribution should be a royal pain to work with.  Ubuntu has not been; the developers have done a great job with putting this distro together.  I chose it because it was easy to work with at first, but getting all the bells and whistles you want (or need) does take work, regardless of any distro or operating system.

My recommendation is to choose what's easy at first, and as you stick with it, you start gaining experience and getting more accomplished with it.  But if you just toss it when you get a problem, rest assured that you will spend the majority of your time swapping operating systems.

Just my two cents

--Alterax
