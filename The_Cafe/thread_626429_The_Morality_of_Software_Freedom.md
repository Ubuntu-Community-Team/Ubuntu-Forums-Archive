### Post by defrex on 2007-11-29
I have a question, and I was hoping some of you would give me your opinions.

I'm a pretty common Linux user. I run Gutsy, using proprietary drivers and the like. I always prefer to use free software when I can. It's only a preference, however; I look at software freedom as a nice thing to have, but not necessary. I prefer it like I prefer vanilla ice cream or girls with short hair.

Many people in the free software community, I've noticed, don't look at it that way. They see software freedom as a moral imperative. That is, there is a moral issue concerning the use of non-free software; it's wrong, rather then just being less desirable. A good example of the difference is cheating on ones spouse. It may be desirable, but it is morally wrong.

My question then: what is the reasoning behind considering software freedom to be a moral issue, rather then simply an issue of preference? I realize this begs the question a little, but try and bear with me anyway. It's enough to tell me why *you* believe it to be a moral issue, if you do.

### Post by Tomosaur on 2007-11-29
I think it's a moral issue - software is just a synonym for 'tool'. We are free to alter our cars (provided the result is safe for other road users, pedestrians, and passengers). We're free to modify our hammers, to change the blade on a saw, to buy different drill bits for our drill. We're not restricted to using a particular brand of pencil when we use a particular brand of paper.

Why should software be any different? If I don't like the way some piece of software does something, why do I have to buy a different software package entirely, when just changing that one little thing would be much simpler?

On top of this, surely I have the right to know what is going on inside my computer? Proprietary software tries to hide this from me.

What about education? If I want to learn how some technology works - then proprietary software is one of the few places where I'm unable to do that. Sure, with patent laws and all that stuff - I may not be able to use my new-found knowledge legally, but there is no legal stipulation that I cannot take a look 'under the hood' and see what's going on in any piece of technology - **except in closed-source, proprietary software**.

Next, we have all of the problems associated with proprietary technology - **especially** proprietary file formats. Proprietary file formats serve to lock people into one company's software suite. Just look at Microsoft Office. Up until very recently, you had absolutely **zero choice** in what software you used to read MS Office files. If you didn't buy into Microsoft, you didn't get to read many documents. If your office uses MS Word, then chances are you will do so at home. The worst part is that people don't even realise that they have a choice. They use MS Word at work - so they use MS Word at home. They're not aware that there may be alternatives out there which suit their needs better. The upshot of this is that MS 'tricks' people into buying their software, registering with them, using their proprietary, closed formats, and thereby getting themselves into even worse vendor lock in. It's like a virus. Once your company has most of its work done in proprietary formats, it is incredibly difficult to break out. What if you need to migrate the work to new systems which offer some new capability, but can't run MS Word (for example, Linux or Mac)? Up until the last few years, this was virtually impossible. You had to miss out on the new features that the new system offered, or you had to keep all of the old systems in place too (so that you could still use the files locked away in the proprietary format). Alternatively, you could sit down and waste your time opening every single .doc file, copy the contents into a text file, move them to the new system, then paste them into the new office suite, whatever that may be. It's massively inconvenient, it wastes money, and it wastes time. This means many people are discouraged from even attempting it. This means the business is held back. It can't do what it needs to do. It can't innovate, or step ahead of the competition. All of that business' shareholders and customers suffer because of this.

The solution is to use open formats. With these, anybody is free to implement that format in new software, on any system / architecture they please. If all of your work documents are in .odt format, you can move them around systems easily, you can open them in any software package which supports them. You can interchange them between different formats very easily, you can get your work done faster without being held back by the restrictions of one software package (if the software doesn't do something, just open the file in a different package which DOES do what you need).

Back to the actual software itself - if the company who makes your software goes out of business, what happens? The software is frozen in time. Everything else progresses past it, new technology comes along and your software can't take advantage of it. You might have just paid £3000 for some bespoke software - a fairly big risk for a small company - then you're left scrambling to pick up the pieces after the development company goes bust without releasing the source code, and all of your competitors race past you while your own business struggles to keep up. If you used open-source software, you'd be able to implement new features yourself - and there's no danger of the software becoming frozen in time (unless it becomes absolutely obsolete, of course). If the lead developer moves on to a new project, someone else can just step in and take over. If the development heads off in a direction you don't like, and which doesn't suit your needs, then you're free to fork the software and form a new development team around it. There's no animosity, because both teams can do whatever it is they're doing, then the software can be merged again at a later date (just look at Beryl and Compiz - re-merged to form Compiz-Fusion) with the best of both worlds.

The only thing holding open-source back, really, is that the proprietary model has set up all of these barriers and restrictions, has allowed vendor lock-in to spread to obscene levels, and has used media clout to spread FUD to keep people weary of open-source. Free software is misunderstood by the mass market - thinking it to be unsafe (people think that the ability to view the source code means hackers are more likely to get into your machine - when really the opposite is true), illegal (free software MUST infringe on patents, and most of it MUST be stolen code, obviously!), and poor quality (only corporations are organised!). All of these fears are propagated by proprietary companies who use their inherent advantage at 'getting there first and taking the best seat' to, basically, lie to everyone else. Virtually all vulnerabilities, dodgy dealings and whatnot, are a result of proprietary software - the ability to hide the code means people cannot check the quality of that code, and decide for themselves whether it is unsafe. Surely you've heard the old question - 'What would you do if you could be invisible for the day'. Most people choose something illegal or morally questionable at the very least. Other people choose something downright sick. The same principle applies here - proprietary software is 'invisibility', more or less. Proprietary developers can get away with all kinds, and nobody but the most vigilant users, critics, and quality assurance foundations are any the wiser. As soon as the alarm is raised, it is struck back down by the developer's media clout. They send out a nice, friendly PR person to calm the situation and assure people it's 'in their best interests', and most casual users just accept it - again, because they don't know there's a better way.

So yeah, it's a moral issue absolutely. It is, however, a moral choice. I'm not going to forcibly convert anyone to use my morals, but I would hope that, sooner or later, the quality and advantages of free software will speak for themselves.

> **defrex said:**
> It's true that there is an option for the people being infringed upon not to be so, but as has been said: by deception or ignorance they don't realize they are being done wrong. No. Read my last post again. The fact that my landlord is currently leasing my apartment to me does not deprive me of the right to own my own house. Are there problems with the way in which landlords treat their tenants? Absolutely. That doesn't make it "highly immoral" for people to rent-out property. 

In the same manner: A) There are problems with the ways in which proprietary software companies treat their customers; B) When people purchase licenses to proprietary software, they have fewer rights over that software than they would if they owned it outright (just like real estate); C) Many people are willing to use proprietary software because it is in some respect more convenient for them. This does *not* lead to the conclusion that proprietary software is inherently immoral; it only leads to the conclusion that any attempt to squash free software out of existence is wrong.

It's true that there is an option for the people being infringed upon not to be so, but as has been said: by deception or ignorance they don't realize they are being done wrong. 

Our society decides what is right or wrong.  Sometimes, it can take generations before a society takes a stand on a particular issue.  In a sense, it's wrong to arbitrarily decide what a society should consider right and what they should think is wrong.  They should decide for themselves.

In this sense, the Free Software Foundation has created a definition of software freedom.  It doesn't say society should do one thing or another, simply that certain criteria are needed for something to be considered free software.

> **defrex said:**
> 
The owner of the software (I'm referring to whoever made it) is nevertheless relieving people of their rights. Is this not highly immoral?

Actually, it's whomever is forcing them to use the software that is doing that.  For example, if my government only provides a certain document to me in a non-free format, they are forcing me to pick between accessing the document or protecting my rights to use free software (example, if the EULA prevents me from enjoying my privacy).  It's not the publisher of the software that is putting me in that position.
