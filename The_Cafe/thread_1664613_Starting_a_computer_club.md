I go by the notion that 'If you build it they will come'.  When I tried to rekindle our local LUG about 5 years ago I built a website and message forum with TONS of content plus the group's goals.  I then talked to local places to host meetings and even had some meeting locations picked out.  

It took a month or two, but after putting the word out plus having the site move up the ranks in the search engines we eventually had a decent group going.  Also we had a local college host meetings for us, so that helped even more.

Unfortunately though as most groups in our area seem to go, after about 2-3 years the group tapered off, and last summer it pretty much folded.  The college pulled out due to a lack of interest, most key members have moved off or found other interests, and though the site is still up it gets a hand full of hits a month.  

The bottom line is create the online community, even if it's just you to start with.  Build the site as if there are 10 people maintaining it, because that will draw people in and create interest.  Also setup some meeting places so when you do get some community interest you guys have a place to meet -- even if it's someone's garage.

I think user groups have slacked off in the last 5-10 years, and it's sad because there's still SO much room for that local and personal comradery you just can't get from online groups where people never meet in person.

Good luck and post some updates as the group moves forward ...

### Post by Ahava591 on 2011-01-11
Online forums and sites devoted to support, (e.g., wiki-types, blogs, etc.) have removed a lot of the need and desire to meet in person; in many cases this is a good thing because you're not explaining the same basic tools to newcomers every meeting. 

-I think it's been noted in a previous reply that dealing with general computer questions every single time takes away the fun of it for most people that would otherwise show up to learn and practice specific and more advanced activities.


My local public library has free "classes," (sometimes four or five or more of them every day,) that are part of their "computer mentor," gig. They're mostly for either the very young or the very old who have in some cases never even pressed the power button. Unfortunately, they don't offer anything more than that. 

If your library or local schools have something similar, I would suggest getting in touch with the group(s) and asking if you could put some one-page fliers on a table by the entrance, have pamphlets ready at the check-out desk, etc. that ask people interested in learning more advanced skills to contact you.
I think the best bet in this case is to create an email address that is used only for people seeing these fliers to contact you with their thoughts.
This will allow you to get an idea of what people are in your area, who they are, what they know and what they want to know, that sort of thing, before you actually start a group.


Remember that anybody who sees this should have a healthy fear of being scammed, so don't be disappointed when you don't get a lot of questions for a while.
-Maybe talk to the library about having them take a more active role, such as allowing you to put on the fliers that they are willing to provide a meeting room for several hours a week, maybe even having them allow people to email them directly about their desire for such a club.


I think a good idea is for the founding members to create presentations demonstrating things that you find interesting, that you have written, hacked, whatever, for the public to watch. This is a little more concrete, (and neat,) than just saying, "There's a computer club, we do a lot of stuff, come see us sometime and we'll screw around for an hour at the public library."


Good luck, and let us know how it progresses. :)

### Post by cprofitt on 2011-01-11
to the OP:

I think you need to define a few things... and then money, unless you are very wealthy, may be an issue.


[LIST]
[*]Do you want to have a meeting place 'owned' or 'rented' by the club or meet at people's houses or public places?
[LIST]
[*]Do you want to host servers on site?
[*]Do you want to provide Internet access to people working at the space?
[*]Do you want to hold training classes (either byoc or computer provided)?
[*]Do you want to allow members to enter the 'location' at any time with access to the area?
[/LIST]
[/LIST]
There are places called Hacker Spaces which sounds like what you are interested in.

[http://hackerspaces.org/wiki/](http://hackerspaces.org/wiki/)

You might want to check out what they advise.
