### Post by XubuRoxMySox on 2010-03-16
> **PC_load_letter said:**
> Why is that [good news that Crunchbang is switching from an Ubuntu base to Debian], care to elaborate? I love CrunchBang, and I'm too spoiled by Ubuntu repos I guess, losing that is a cause to worry, unless I'm missing something here.

Omns mentioned one of several issues with using Ubuntu as a base. I too have found a lot of needless dependencies being dragged in with applications. Another issue is what Canonical does with their updates. These experimental changes in the kernel that suddenly b0rk a working system and force a re-install and use of the older kernel. There are a lot of testimonials - even here in these forums - about how some update instantly disabled wireless, or sound, or graphics, or worse.

Another issue - and maybe this is just me - is that Ubuntu insists upon Beta software by default (PulseAudio, Grub2, etc). Beta, to me, means "unproven, unstable, not for novice users." Ubuntu is specifically aimed at Linux novices and touted as "beginner friendly." And so it is, as long as the Beta software works on their hardware. When it doesn't, it can give even experienced Linux users headaches. Why impose experimental stuff on newbies? They're not laboratory rats for goodnessakes.

A *lot* of Ubuntu-based distros and spin-offs have switched or are switching to a Debian base (Mepis, for example, did so years ago and it's rock-stable, gorgeous, and surprisingly fast for a KDE distro) because of these issues, and because it's getting harder and harder for developers to use Ubuntu as a base because of the changes they keep making.

I'm not bashing Ubuntu at all. The huge repos are reason enough by themselves to use Ubuntu if it works for you. But Debian's repos offer no less than Ubuntu's, and it's getting easier and easier to install Debian and use (just wait 'til the new Debian installer comes out when Squeeze moves to Debian Stable!) than ever before. Ubuntu made Debian "accessible" to "ordinary desktop users." That was wonderful! But *now* Debian is accessible to ordinary users without the randomness, the risk (unless you *want* to assume some risk and *choose* it willingly), and without being at the mercy of experimental updates that could suddenly disable a working system.

Sorry if I ranted...

Robin
