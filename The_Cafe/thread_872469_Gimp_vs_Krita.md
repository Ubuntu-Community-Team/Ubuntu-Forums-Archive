### Post by saulgoode on 2008-07-28
The relationship of GIMP to Krita is exhibitive of the very best that Free Software has to offer. It is not a competition and the developers of each of the projects (as well as many other Free Software graphics projects such as Inkscape, Scribus, Blender, etc) collaborate and provide encouragement and assistance for each others' projects. 

In addition to online collaboration on mailing lists and in chat rooms, there has been over the last few years an annual meeting held (the Libre Graphics Meeting) where contributors to Free Software graphics projects have gotten together to share problems and goals, and make plans for how to resolve those problems and achieve those goals.

This collaboration has led to great progress having been made in a relatively short period of time in the field of system-wide (and cross-platform) color management which, while not yet perfected, is already proving beneficial to the entire Free Software community. There have also been strides made in SVG graphics and OpenGL; as well as initial discussions being held on standardizing graphics formats with greater functionality than the current crop of PNG, JPG, etc. 

This collaboration is working toward the goal of allowing users to have available the proper tools for their tasks; allowing them to freely choose those tools and switch seamlessly between them. 

While I do not follow the Krita project closely, I suspect that as time goes on and their software improves, it will become more useful to artists wishing to paint with "natural" interfaces (watercolor, airbrushing, pastel, etc); whereas GIMP will continue being more of a general purpose tool for photo enhancement and web graphics. There are distinct differences between how these various activities get implemented in software; and when one takes away the market pressure of having to outsell the "competition", there is no reason not to have separate tools optimized for specific tasks.
