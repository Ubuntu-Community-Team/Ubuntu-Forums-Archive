### Post by braden7 on 2011-04-30
Why do so many people (like this columnist) seem to believe in the so-called "wisdom" of diluting the usefulness of the Linux desktop just to get some market share?

Who cares how many mainstream users switch? We have enough computer people using and developing the software ecosystem to meet our needs. Adding in "average" users by turning the thing into an appliance looking consumer device does us no good, does not improve the software, and will not increase quality.

I hate seeing all these columns with the "If Linux is to succeed....." attitude.

I have some news for them: Linux has already succeeded. Millions of us are using a fast, stable, secure, and productive operating system. We have removed ourselves from the world of commercial software. We have bypassed all the dumbing down which inevitably happens when software (or anything else, really) has to be developed for the least competent 10% of the mass-market audience.

I welcome everybody to our niche, but a niche it should stay. Not having to hand-hold the computer illiterate or dazzle those who like the shiny is what appeals to this niche userbase. Developers could change the mission to be a direct competitor for the Windows/OSX userbase, but in doing so, they have to abandon what made desktop Linux a welcome refuge from those two choices.

### Post by el_koraco on 2011-04-30
> **braden7 said:**
> 
I hate seeing all these columns with the "If Linux is to succeed....." attitude.


Stimmt. i'm also irked by the "it's too fragmented to do any difference in the desktop arena" argument. It's not too fragmented for anything. You have three major company players in the field - Red Hat, Novell and Canonical. There are also volunteer projects like Debian, Slackware, Gentoo and Arch. Basically, all the rest of the distros are just making (some more, some less welcome) cosmetic changes to the above mentioned projects and they are largely maintained by small groups of individuals whose absence in the development cycle would not be that significant. 

The reason for open source not having a strong presence in commercial markets is due to the fact that the model is just not very good for producing large scale commercial closed source products.

### Post by el_koraco on 2011-04-30
There are a few industries where Linux has had a strong foothold for a number of years, like the movie technology or appliances. I wasn't talking about not releasing software products for Linux, but using open source to produce commercial products, say like medical database software, accounting applications, basically anything having to do with vertical markets. The reason is quite clear - it's more lucrative to produce and sell software than to be hired on a one-off programming or maintenance basis.  

There are exceptions. e.g. rPath has had pretty solid success producing custom made distributions based on its own open source package management system.
