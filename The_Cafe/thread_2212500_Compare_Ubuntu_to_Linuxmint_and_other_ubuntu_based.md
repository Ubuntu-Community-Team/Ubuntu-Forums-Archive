### Post by buzzingrobot on 2014-03-21
All distributions are primarily packaging efforts for software written by people not affiliated with that distribution.  The vast majority of Linux applications differ, across distributions, only in version number and aesthetics such as theming.

A relative few distributions do foster development of applications and other tools.  Ubuntu and Fedora, for example, are strong in this regard.

Mint replaces Unity with a variety of interfaces, all of which are available for Ubuntu.  Mint develops its own Cinnamon interface.  All other interfaces used by Mint are developed elsewhere. 

Mint provides its own themes and a handful of useful tools. Otherwise, its application pool is exactly the same as Ubuntu's because the packages are Ubuntu packages from Ubuntu servers.

Mint uses a more cautious updating scheme that involves ranking packages in 5 tiers according to perceived risk.

Mint's products are typically very well done but can't be said to deliver something innovative.

### Post by mattlach on 2014-03-24
> **dbass81 said:**
> Ubuntu is just a customized distribution of Debian, with a custom graphical shell called Unity, that runs on top of GNOME.  Linux Mint is based off Ubuntu, but replaced Unity with other graphical shells, f.e. MATE and KDE.

My .02 cents.

That's a little bit of an oversimplification, but mostly accurate I guess.   Ubuntu has done a lot of work on getting hardware compatibility to work well on install, which is huge.  That is why many distributions (like Mint) are based on Ubuntu as an intermediary, rather than going all the way back to Debian.

I used to love Ubuntu (and still do as a headless server install) but I just did not like the Unity interface at all.   It drove me away in the end.   

These days I am on the latest linux Mint on my Desktop (and 12.04 LTS Server edition on my server), primarily because of the Cinnamon desktop environment, which is based on Gnome3, but made to look and behave like Gnome2, which IMHO, is the pinnacle of Linux desktop design.

Another alternative would have been to install the lighter Xubuntu, and then install Cinnamon on top of it (later removing XFCE, if I felt the need) but Ubuntu's Cinnamon packages are of comparatively old versions.  It was just easier this way, so I didn't have to mess around with other sources in order to try to get the latest Cinnamon.   With Mint, the latest Cinnamon just works out of the box.

The only downside with Mint thus far is the lack of distribution upgrades.   With ubuntu you can easily just upgrade to the next release once it goes stable.  This is possible in Mint too, but they *highly* recommend against it.

Next time there is a distribution upgrade, I may just go the Xubuntu release, and try to dig up the correct sources for the latest Cinnamon, so I don't have to worry about distribution upgrades going forward.

### Post by Trapper on 2014-03-28
The only way to get a true comparison is to try each one you are wishing to compare. Otherwise, what you will get are opinions based on **others** preferences, needs, biases and experiences. My bias is towards Linux Mint. That is because I feel Mint offers the best usable **desktops** with the proven MATE fork of the traditional Gnome 2 and Cinnamon. I do not consider Unity to be a valid desktop and Gnome 3 is about as reasonably usable as Windows 95. Those are just my opinions. Also, Linux Mint developers have now become quite involved in the further development of MATE along with their Cinnamon development. I very much prefer Ubuntu in the world of servers, especially my Postfix based mail server setups. As it always is with Linux ... different strokes for different folks. The way to find out what's best for you is through experimentation and experience. Many years ago I had tunneled thinking that Fedora was the greatest thing there is. Once I ventured further out into the world of Linux I started to discover their are a multitude of other very viable options to everything and the more you explore and dig into things, the more open-minded you become and the more you learn.
