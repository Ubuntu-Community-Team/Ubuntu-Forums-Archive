### Post by hellomeow on 2007-07-02
There is an opinion I've held ever since I first became a user of Ubuntu, and despite all the hatred the community possesses against this idea, I simply have no choice but unleash it for all to read. I could be the only Ubuntu user who feels this, but I feel that everyone must know there one among all of you that feels this. *inhales* Without further delay, here it is:

**Ubuntu should be _*proprietary*_.**

*Yes that's right, I said it!* No more open source. No more, *please*. We must take the source, hide it, shackle it, restrain it, keep it locked, locked for no one to see. Forbid redistribution, forbid reverse engineering, forbid duplication, modification, and keep all other rights available to a copyright holder *reserved*, reserved until the end of time. Rewrite the code and terminate any traces of legacy code licensed under any free-software license that ever existed. Make the code *our* property. Make an OS that people aspire to obtain.

But most importantly, Ubuntu should not be free. No, it should be expensive, very expensive, with quality triumphing over price! We must apply liberal professionalization to Ubuntu, changing its name, logo, artwork, and changing it to a beautiful, glassy, glossy OS with fonts of pristine crispness and quality. We must package the operating system in a most modern, professional looking case, and line the interior of the package with only the finest silk available.

We must make profit, money, wealth!! And subsequently, instead of making software that benefits no one, we shall make software that benefits everyone, developers in the form of money, and users in the form of increased quality. Put all your biases aside and enjoy your new found purpose in life, because the only thing better then developing software is getting *paid* to do it!

What would you rather do? Develop software for free and starve? Or integrate into the lucrative software industry, with many developers being paid over US$100,000 per year?

Of what concern is the so called 'freeness' of software, when you can improve your quality of life developing **non-free** software? Which is better? Developing so-called 'free' software, or being able to afford sending your children to high quality schools in a high quality suburban California neighborhood, and knowing they will have good chances of attending a prestigious university afterward, and be able to major in the computer sciences and engineering, and then subsequently begin the prestige cycle once again? And now, if you will, imagine this being possible only by developing software! Instead of being cramped inside your apartment slaving hours per day developing software of only acceptable quality, why not develop high quality software that people will pay for, software that is *okay* for people to complain about?

Instead of needing to open the source (which others will simply compile anyway, thus destroying the market for your product) why not begin dressing in your freshly-pressed shirt and suit pants and drive down beautiful suburban streets to your luxurious office, where a luxurious and comfortable development chair awaits you! *Who cares*, in fact, what you develop, as long as you can be assured that you will have a generously large retirement account?

Humans naturally have a tendency toward self-interest! Let us free ourselves by shackling our code, selling it, and making millions, no, **billions** of dollars doing this! What is more important, your code, or your life? What is more important, providing people in poverty with software, or being able to donate to charity, which will help poor in far more meaningful ways?

It is time to embrace economics and human nature. End self-restraint!

Now that's all said and done please point your flamethrowers in my general direction. tyia

### Post by Tsen on 2007-07-02
Alright, I feel it is necessary to reiterate and emphasize some of the points already made:

1. Ubuntu CANNOT legally be closed-source.  In order for this to happen, all of the code would have to be re-written to exclude it from the GPL.  Further, whether the final product could even be branded as "Ubuntu" is questionable--which means that, ultimately, you might as well just start with any linux distro out there.  

2. Who is your market?  Businesses will not opt for an expensive, glittery newcomer when they have tried-and-true Linux options already available.  Linux junkies won't go for anything closed-source, especially if it has an attached cost.  Fanboys of Windows and OS X won't be interested in an expensive conversion.  Hardcore computer enthusiasts won't use it, because they'd understand it was just a rebranded version of something free that is still readily available.  The average user doesn't even know there's a choice most of the time.  They view Macs as something alien entirely, not just as an alternate OS--they tend to view computers with a sense of mystery and whimsy.  Even if you raised their awareness of an alternate OS, you still have the problem of converting them.  They'll be used to Windows, and they'll judge a new OS, like it or not, right or wrong, as how good of a Windows imitation it is--minus the bugs, of course.  They don't want a new way of doing things, they don't want to learn new applications and new menus.  They just want the programs they're used to.
So who, exactly, would you sell this to?

3. Even IF you closed Ubuntu's source and rewrote the code, inevitably thousands of jaded Ubuntu fans will pick up the final free version and continue work on it.  For free.  Every time you develop something significant and differentiating, they will design their own version and implement it for free.  They won't be tied down by licensing laws, proprietary fees, or DRM in any form.  Meanwhile, you'll have to be hard at work preventing piracy, implementing enough new features to keep you one step ahead of the wolves, and developing and maintaining existing software to keep it modern, bug-free and secure, all while trying to turn a profit.  Keep in mind the hundreds of coders you'd need to maintain and the already tough market you'd be trying to enter.

It's a lose-lose situation.  Finally, may I remind you that *I* most certainly would never use Ubuntu again if it was closed source, and that's not a rare sentiment by any means.  Ubuntu, and Linux as a whole, is based on a market that values open and free software, and by close-sourcing and implementing a price for it, you're alienating the only shred of a market you have.
