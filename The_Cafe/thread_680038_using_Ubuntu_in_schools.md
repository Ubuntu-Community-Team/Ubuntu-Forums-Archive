### Post by lucia_engel on 2008-01-27
Unless your school is planning to upgrade/replace the computers, it would be hard to make a case for switching to Ubuntu. Here's an analogy:

Your school purchased a volume license for photoshop, which is valid for say 5 years. You come in and suggest the school should change to GIMP, because it's free and therefore cost less than photoshop. Why would they switch over to GIMP when they already have something usable?

The situation where you have a better chancee would be the photoshop license is going to expire in a few months and they're planning to renew.

### Post by fatality_uk on 2008-01-28
Who says they have to switch to anything other than Photoshop? I run a number of Windows ONLY applications seamlessly from my Linux desktops. And **NOT** through WINE. Look here. [SeamlessRDP]("http://www.cendio.com/seamlessrdp/")

By using thin client'esq setup, then the programs that are under license can still be used. In addition, small changes to the curriculum mean that additions such as FOSS can be added with zero cost.

These applications do not run in a usual terminal services client way, but a single windows window :)

Most schools by enough user licenses to cover each student seat, so this approach easily works.

The way to approach this is to look at setting up a migration project. My Linux For Schools project will provide templates for this kind of thing by the middle of the year with a lot of supplimentary documentation as well.

The idea is that instead of replacing old machines, as they near the end of their lifecycle and license, you can then install Linux, use tools like SeamlessRDP and then extend that life of that hardware for at least 2-3 years. thus giving a TCO seaving for that individual piece of equipment.
