> ChangeLog: HP clarifies warranty under Linux

Friday March 30, 2007 (07:04 PM GMT)

By: Joe Barr

We received the following today by email from Hewlett Packard, which clarifies their warranty policy for customers running Linux on HP hardware, at least in the specific case we reported on recently.

Per the HP spokesperson:

    HP is committed to supporting its customers and backs its PCs with a solid hardware warranty regardless of the operating system.

    If a customer alters a product and a problem develops as a result of that change, then the problem (which is not a result of a defect in workmanship) would not be covered under the warranty.     We are also taking steps to ensure support agents are clear on this policy so this issue does not recur.

Ok I am on the OpenBSD misc list and I have got this message from their founder about Intel's Core 2 processors:> **Theo]Various developers are busy implimenting workarounds for serious bugs
in Intel's Core 2 cpu.

These processors are buggy as hell, and some of these bugs don't just
cause development/debugging problems, but will *ASSUREDLY* be
exploitable from userland code.

As is typical, BIOS vendors will be very late providing workarounds /
fixes for these processors bugs.  Some bugs are unfixable and cannot
be worked around.  Intel only provides detailed fixes to BIOS vendors
and large operating system groups.  Open Source operating systems are
largely left in the cold.

Full (current) errata from Intel:

 [http://download.intel.com/design/processor/specupdt/31327914.pdf](http://download.intel.com/design/processor/specupdt/31327914.pdf)

 - We bet there are many more errata not yet announced -- every month
   this file gets larger.
 - Intel understates the impact of these erraata very significantly.
   Almost all operating systems will run into these bugs.
 - Basically the MMU simply does not operate as specified/implimented
   in previous generations of x86 hardware.  It is not just buggy, but
   Intel has gone further and defined "new ways to handle page tables"
   (see page 58).
 - Some of these bugs are along the lines of "buffer overflow" said:**
> http://www.geek.com/images/geeknews/2006Jan/core_duo_errata__2006_01_21__full.gif[/URL]

Note that some errata like AI65, AI79, AI43, AI39, AI90, AI99 scare
the hell out of us.  Some of these are things that cannot be fixed in
running code, and some are things that every operating system will do
until about mid-2008, because that is how the MMU has always been
managed on all generations of Intel/AMD/whoeverelse hardware.  Now
Intel is telling people to manage the MMU's TLB flushes in a new and
different way.  Yet even if we do so, some of the errata listed are
unaffected by doing so.

As I said before, hiding in this list are 20-30 bugs that cannot be
worked around by operating systems, and will be potentially
exploitable.  I would bet a lot of money that at least 2-3 of them
are.

For instance, AI90 is exploitable on some operating systems (but not
OpenBSD running default binaries).

At this time, I cannot recommend purchase of any machines based on the
Intel Core 2 until these issues are dealt with (which I suspect will
take more than a year).  Intel must be come more transparent.

(While here, I would like to say that AMD is becoming less helpful day
by day towards open source operating systems too, perhaps because
their serious errata lists are growing rapidly too).I think this is a very serious issue. CPU is the 'core' of any computer but I find it really sad that the top 2 consumer CPU vendors are give less Opensource support.

### Post by Sunnz on 2008-04-22
The problem with binary-only drivers is precisely that, when it doesn't work, it just doesn't work, and there is not a damn thing you can do about, since well, it _is_ closed source.*

A concrete example is that binary-only graphics card drivers does not work on PPC computers, when the computer perfectly well to run such hardware if driver exists... Linux runs on a large variety of computers, x86, AMD64, and PPC, are just the tip of the iceberg of CPU architectures that Linux supports.

Another problem is that device drivers are usually kernel modules that interacts directly with the heart of the OS. Linux itself can be as robust as it is written to be, but one single bug in the pool of closed drivers could pose a security/reliability risk to the rest of the system; since that as soon as your kernel is being rooted, it is as good as gone... 

The bottom line is, you can't fix it, you don't know how bad it is doing under the hood, it is all closed source... you don't have a choice but to blindly trust it and run it.

It is not that developers for Linux need an open source drivers from hardware vendors... all they needed is the interface specification to the device... that's pretty much just how to write code that makes use of the hardware, there are no need for any actual details on the hardware design, nor any patented/trade secrets. Taiwanese vendors has already made their API for their wireless cards openly available without the need for NDAs, and they had no problems competing with American counter parts. In fact I have not seen any better WiFi cards coming out of American companies despite them not having opened documentation for their APIs. Meanwhile, Taiwanese vendors are developing graphics cards have their APIs fully documented, sure, they would be underpowered compared to latest and greatest from nVidia and ATi, as they are just starting developing such hardware now, but it would certainly fit most Compiz Fusion needs, and possibly your average 3D games.

After all, you got to understand that hardware vendors are not OS developers (and they know it well), it is much better leave the job of writing drivers to the actual OS developers who knows their way around the OS, and have hardware vendors to focus on documenting how to code and *use* their hardware.

** You can reverse engineer a binary driver, like nouveau for nVidia graphics card, but this lacks of part from hardware vendor and is a tedious process that takes too much man power and too much time, it is just not the optimal solution. **
