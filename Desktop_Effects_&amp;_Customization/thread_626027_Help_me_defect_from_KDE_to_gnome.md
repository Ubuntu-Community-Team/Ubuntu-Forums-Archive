### Post by Sammi on 2007-11-28
I got in to Ubuntu/Linux with Breezy Badger. Used Kubuntu at first, because it felt more familiar than Gnome at that moment. Also Gnome seemed kind of "amputated". There just seemed to be a general lack of featured when compared to KDE and the Windows desktop environment.

But then I decided to try Gnome out for a minimum on two weeks, when Dapper Drake came out, out of sheer open mindedness, to give Gnome a just as fair chance as I gave KDE. At first I felt constrained and confused in Gnome, but I quickly got used to it, and when I tried KDE after the two weeks had past, it felt like KDE was bloated and got in my way with unnecessary options and questions, and I stuck with the simplicity of Gnome. I just felt more productive, because I got to work in stead or playing with shiny bells.
Then there came quite a lot of feature enhancements with both Edgy, Feisty, and now Gutsy Gibbon, and they've all added in the places were Gnome may have been weak, but never added bloat.

I do have to confess though, that there are some KDE applications that are better than any Gnome/gtk equivalent, most notably Amarok and K3B, but they run well in Gnome, so why run KDE just for them?

### Post by Melcar on 2007-11-28
Ah, the age old question... gnome or kde?  I ended up running KDE primarily because of its transparency engine.  Sure, by running Compiz you can achieve even better eye candy on any DE, but I hate XGL and AIGLX doesn't work right with my ATI cards.  I like the menu system better in KDE too.
Anyway, if you really want to jump over to the other side to party with the gnomes, do it mostly for the apps.  I myself must admit that I end up using a majority of gnome apps. simply because I like their "no nonsense" approach to functionality.  Sure, KDE equivalents are most of the time "better", but many of them are overloaded with extra features that I just don't use.  Also, Adept blows in comparison to Synaptic, so that may be enough to sway you to the other side (unless you like using other software managers like SMART).  Just keep in mind that in Gnome you may end up fiddling most of the time with gconf and/or with the terminal a lot, compared to KDE.
