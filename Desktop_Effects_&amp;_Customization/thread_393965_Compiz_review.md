### Post by kvonb on 2007-03-26
Well I took the plunge and installed Feisty on my desktop, I was bored plus I was having problems with Beryl crashing on me and the "black windows" problem was starting to get on my nerves.

  So after the second install, (I made a boo-boo by running sudo chmod -R user:user on my home directory by mistake!), I was ready to try Compiz.

  I have been using Beryl since version 1.2 something and have had fabulous results in both stability and useability, but for some reason these last few updates (1.9x and 2.x) I've been having a lot of problems with windows opening with blacked out windows and even entire graphic system lockups so I was very keen to give Compiz a go.

  Well, after having to read 2 web-pages on how to actually enable the supposed "one click" desktop effects, then a further page on how to actually enable the "cube", I've got Compiz to a useable state.

  Not fully useable, but working non the less.

  I must say that compared to Beryl, Compiz looks extremely amateurish and unfinished, which is surprising because the Compiz team started this GL desktop stuff.

  What annoys me the most is the complete lack of control that is provided.  I mean nothing is intuitive at all, I had to search Google for instructions on how to actually rotate the cube!

  All settings are changed through the archaic "Windows throwback" gconf-editor.  You might have come across this while trying to get panel background to scale, or making the desktop icons (in)visible.  It mimmicks the good old Windows system registry way of doing things, search through pages of incomprehensible gobblydygook to find an unpronounceable string value to change, hmm!

  Ok, I eventually found out that you can install a nice GUI frontend for this through synaptic (gnome-compiz-manager), but was surprised at the complete lack of anything useful in either of them.  Apparently Compiz doesn't allow you to have both a top AND bottom "cubecap" image, only a top one, the bottom has to stay an awful system colour and cannot be changed.

  You can select and enable a "skydome" image which can be seen as the background when the cube is rotated (press <ctrl><alt><mouse1>), this however seems a little pointless as there is no option to zoom or scale the cube on rotation, so you don't get to see much of it.

  It also seems that you cannot choose which keys activate cube rotation, well I did eventually find that option in gconf-editor, but very little other than the default option seems to work.  I wanted to use just the middle mouse button, bit it refused to use that, I guess it's not considered "cool" to use only one hand to perform a task!

  So what do you get with Compiz I hear you ask?  Well, you get nice but seemingly unmodifiable drop shadows, wobbly "rubber" windows that for some unknown reason seem to like to stick to the edges of the screen, making you have to pull the window halfway across the desktop to unstick it, and with a bit of fiddling and lots of foul language, you can get a rotating cube.

  I had all the extra plug-ins installed like snow and stuff, but couldn't seem to get them to work, the keys that were listed as being the activators did nothing.

  One thing I did like (at first) is the gnome theme integration, Compiz uses your regular desktop theme, no messing about with installing yet another theme!  However, it fell out of my good book when I found that I couldn't change the order of the close/minimise/maximise buttons!  I like everything to be on the left of the screen and have been enjoying this configuration, but alas the Compiz big-brother says that I'm not allowed to do any of that "Commie" style behaviour, stick to the default sonny and no-one will get hurt!

  I'm bewildered as to why Compiz would be included in Feisty and Beryl rejected!  It is very "old-school" design and implementation, conservative, and ultimately when you compare it to Beryl, Compiz looks extremely amateurish and unfinished.

  I really wish these two teams would put their heads together and put their combined efforts and skills into one amazing project, it could be top notch!
