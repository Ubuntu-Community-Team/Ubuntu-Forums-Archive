### Post by tgalati4 on 2015-03-01
Watch some videos on Youtube for how to replace screen hinges.  They can be tricky.  I've replaced a few Dell hinges--they tend to break easily.  You will need a voltmeter to test for connectivity between both ends of the cable.  The wires are thin and not easy to inspect.  If you can isolate both ends of the cable, then you don't need to pull the hinge apart, just test it in place.  If the inverter has gone bad (for a flourescent backlight), that can be changed separately from the screen.  I presume you are not getting any image on the screen by shining a flashlight through it.

### Post by aidangcole on 2015-03-07
Hi,

I have seen this problem myself. and I doubt that the problem is to do with your screen. I have a Galego and a Clevo W740SU (basically the same chassis, with similar but slightly different internals). In my case, the Clevo seems more vulnerable than the Galego. (the problem has happend on both at various times, but more often on the Clevo).
So far, I have not taken the trouble to actually open-up the cases with a view to seeing what is going on in there, as by sheer quwirk of accident, the screen has come back to life on both of them. This is I think because, for the most-part I do not tend to use them as portable machines, but use them on a table-top surface in a static position.
It seems to me that the chassis are somewhat flimsy, and when the laptop is picked-up from the right hand side, (ie the side where the USB ports are), something inside gets dislodged or disturbed and the screen will not display anything - even a squeeze on that side of the laptop can induce the screen to go blank. The fix for me, is simply to get a hold of the laptop from the right and give it a gentle squeeze - you will feel it flex a little in your hands - hopefully, your display will spring back into life.

Having just re-read my words, I guess that this comment could be taken to be that I am dissing the Galego/WS740SU, which is not my intention - I actually love both machines and would recommend them to anyone who wants a good performing laptop - Nothing is perfect in this world I guess, and one of these fine days, I might just work out how to open them up and take a look at the internal workings with a view to seeing if anything obvious can be done to fix my occasional problems.

### Post by aidangcole on 2015-03-16
By chance, earlier today I bumped into a couple of chaps who repair hardware. They are familiar with the Clevo based machines. Their opinion was that on machines (such as the Galego) that have a permanent hard-wired battery (ie not a battery which can be simply removed 'on the fly'), that there are small 'safety' switches located at points on the chassis that are designed to stop the machine from being started and disable the power supply from the battery when it is being worked on or is in a state of tear-down or build-up. Their opinion was that on some lighter-weight machines it is quite possible to inadvertantly disturb those switches without realising it..................
