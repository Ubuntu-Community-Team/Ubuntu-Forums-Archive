Long story short, I bought a Gazelle laptop and it was definitely worth it. I only wish I had swung for the hybrid hard drive...

### Post by Dragonbite on 2011-08-31
Is the extra cost worth hardware compatibility is one aspect it comes down to.

I have a laptop which uses Intel 855GM graphics.  Since 10.04 its support has been regressed.  Since then some patches have come out but never to 100%.  I cannot run Unity 3D or Gnome 3 on it because of this video issue and there are no prognosis of it being fixed in the future ever.  More or less, the bug is "closed" but that doesn't mean you can get 3D or desktop effects.

After going through this, the idea of spending a little extra for the piece of mind that "it just works" sounds nice.  I priced a [[COLOR="Blue"]System 76 Pangolin Performance[/COLOR]]("http://www.system76.com/product_info.php?cPath=28&products_id=118") with an as-equivalent Dell and the System 76 came under by close to $150 while the biggest difference is video (Nvidia vs Intel HD 3000).

Another plus, though, is the System76 is working with Ubuntu to make sure it works.  Look at the dedicated forum here.

So one part to think is "what if system xyz isn't fully compatible"?  Are you prepared to spend time trying to make it work?  Chances are good it will work, and if it is in a store you can try a LiveCD to see if it will too.  With System76, though, it shouldn't be an issue.

### Post by gdgilda on 2011-09-06
Hello,

I've owned a panp7 for almost 2 months now and have found it overall to be a very good experience.  I agree with the observations that system76 support is good and that overall the system and ubuntu seem well-matched.  I did quite a bit of research before buying this system and concluded that if I wanted to buy a linux-based laptop, going with a vendor with a good reputation for supporting linux/ubuntu was the way to go.  I could've bought another quality name brand and did the install of linux myself, but there was always the question of what to do if not all capabilities were supported.  Configuring linux for the plethora of laptop configurations can be tricky, and I didn't want to spend many hours finding the right distro, drivers, etc.

Ubuntu does offer a listing of laptops that are "certified", but read carefully the notes and the system configuration.  Finding just the right configuration can be a bit of a challenge in itself:
[http://www.ubuntu.com/certification/](http://www.ubuntu.com/certification/)

One more comment:  Your experience will largely depend on your expectations going in.  I have been running linux for several years and know what to expect.  Not everything works as smoothly as in windows.  Support for certain peripherals can be sketchy.  Ubuntu 11.04 has some quirkiness in its interface that not everyone likes (I don't mind it).  But for me the benefits of:

1) Not having to worry (as much) about the latest security exposure,
2) Having access to lots of free and good quality software that meets my needs,
3) A vendor with a support team that understands the system they are providing

outweigh the negatives.

Glenn
