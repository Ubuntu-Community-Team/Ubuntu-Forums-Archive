### Post by jbelmonte on 2013-05-21
Take a look at [http://www.notebookcheck.net/Intel-HD-Graphics-4000-Benchmarked.73567.0.html]("http://www.notebookcheck.net/Intel-HD-Graphics-4000-Benchmarked.73567.0.html"). (This is the same link 3Miro posted). Systems with HD 4000 can outperform systems with GeForce 610M or Radeon HD 7450M discreet cards. The performance of the HD 4000 is dependent on several other factors. Since the GPU has no dedicated VRAM, it must operate in accordance with the main memory and its RAM clock. The HD 4000 GPU has a larger cache that is shared with the CPU L3 cache, but how much cache it has will be dependent on the Ivy Bridge CPU. So the HD 4000 performance will vary depending on system specs. Get more and faster RAM and a more powerful processors (System76 System), and the graphics performance of the HD 4000 can be quite good. Get a system with a minimum amount of slow, cheap RAM and a slow processor (non-System76 System) and the HD 4000 graphics performance will be reduced. These effects will be noticeable for gaming, but not really for general computing, surfing, GIMP or watching videos. You may not want a HD 4000 on a true gaming laptop, but I am not a gamer so what do I know.

This won't change until we see more apps, since of course any OS is ultimately just an app launcher; apps define the scope of activities we can do with a computer.

Canonical has been making big strides with the resources at developer.ubuntu.com and elsewhere, but it's going to be some time before we see enough apps to give OEMs the confidence they need, knowing that if they invest millions in putting Ubuntu-powered machines into the channel enough customers will actually like them to make the venture profitable.

In the meantime, companies like System 76 have done a very good job of stepping in to fill the gap.

Wanna see more System 76 models?  Write apps for Ubuntu, or encourage developers who deploy to other platforms to consider porting to Ubuntu.  Ultimately, that's the key for creating a market large enough to drive the sort of expensive innovation you're looking for.
