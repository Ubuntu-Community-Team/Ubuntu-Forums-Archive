### Post by jml on 2009-06-24
I own both a MacBook and a Starling.  The Starling's construction, in my opinion is every bit as good as my MacBook.  Battery life is very good. Screen is excellent, and for a netbook, the performance is surprisingly good.  Whether it will "drive you crazy" or not depends on several factors.

First, can you live with a 10 inch screen for extended periods of time?  I find that for extended use, a larger screen is easier on my middle aged eyes.  So this is something you may want to consider as you mentioned that you will be using it to write your manuscript.  Yes, you can add an external monitor, but before I would go that route, I would consider one of System 76's larger laptops.

Second is speed.  Compared to the other netbooks I have used/owned the Starling has very acceptable speed.  However, it is definitely slower than my MacBook.  That is not a criticism, merely an observation. So writing, web surfing will be more than adequately handled by the Starling.  Managing photos should also be no problem.  But photo editing with an application like the GIMP may tax your patience.  I have never used Songbird but if it is a very CPU intensive program you may find that you spend a lot of time waiting for it to rip your music files and organize the database.  Possibly Tom (a System 76 employee,) will weigh in on these issues.

If you do decide to buy one, I would recommend getting a USB optical drive if you do not already have one as it really extends the functionality of the Starling.  On balance, I am very satisfied with my Starling.  I think it delivers great value for the price and is the best netbook I have owned.  Hope that this helps.

Joe

### Post by hfoster on 2009-07-07
I've been a Mac user since mid 1980s. We currently have in the house an iMac, a Mac mini, a MacBook, and an on-its-last-legs-but-refuses-to-die PowerBook. I bought a Starling just last week for my high school son to use in notetaking next year. I got it now to give me a chance to get it set up and to learn Linux, and to give him a chance to get used to the netbook. I just didn't want to drop $1000 for a MacBook for him, and the Starling was at a nice price point. I also wanted to buy a real Linux computer, not one that I would have to replace Windows with Linux myself. Here are my observations.

Pre-sale and post-sale support from System76 have been excellent. You would not get this level of support from Apple unless you visited one of their stores!

The fit and finish of the Starling is not equivalent to Apple products. The System76 label on the cover was installed just a little off and is likely to get picked off at some point. One of the keys has an Ubuntu sticker that will probably come off as well. The battery design effectively raises the back of the netbook about an inch, tilting the keyboard a few degrees. My son actually likes this, but it means that the computer will not fit in many netbook sleeves with the battery attached. I've also noticed that the battery tends to separate just a bit from the computer over time so I've been careful to keep them pushed together.

The keyboard is noticably smaller than standard size. Some of the punctuation keys are about half size. I have average size hands and am getting used to the keyboard but can type much faster on a full size keyboard. 

The screen is glossy and beautiful but is small. It has a 16.9 aspect ratio and a fixed resolution. You will only be able to see a portion of a document at a time. It does not appear that Ubuntu netbook remix (the os on the machine) supports having multiple windows open at the same time (someone correct me if otherwise) and in any case the windows would be very small if you could.
 
If you have an Apple mouse (single button, Mighty mouse or wireless Mighty mouse) you will find that it works perfectly with the Starling, just plug it in.

Ubuntu is NOT MacOS! I was able to do some tasks like setting up users, connecting to the wireless network, connecting to the wireless printer, and installing some software packages using the tools provided, but there have been several times so far that I have had to go to the command line to get something working. I am a software engineer by trade and programmed under BSD Unix for many years, so I am comfortable with using the command line. I personally think that an Ubuntu user should have some Unix/Linux knowledge, or else should be capable of following instructions exactly as given. If you don't know what you are doing and/or are careless, you can break your system in an instant from the command line.

I also bought the external DVD drive with the computer. A separate thread details my struggle to get it working. In the end I had to install a different movie player than the one that came on the computer (using the command line). The drive does work well though, although you will find that watching movies on the small screen is not all that exciting.

My advice would be to go to a local store like Staples or BestBuy and look at the 10" netbooks there. That will give you a good idea of whether the keyboard and display will be acceptable. Frankly, if I were in the typing business like you, I would want a larger screen and a standard keyboard.

### Post by biologic on 2009-07-07
Having used a recent generation Macbook for about a year and recently purchasing a Starling, I can agree with all of the points above regarding build quality, etc.

The only thing I would like to emphasize is the size of the default inputs (keyboard and trackpad). This was the number one concern I had about a netbook, and it's proven to be the only issue I have with it.

The keys are simply too close together for me to be able to type effectively and quickly on it. The trackpad is very small and has a scroll-area on the right hand side that isn't marked. I find it difficult to get the pointer where I want it, and difficult to do things like put in periods or slashes where I want. I keep hitting the right shift key instead of the special keys I want. And I have relatively small and dexterous fingers compared with many people.

I'm still going to give it some time, and it still serves the purpose I wanted it for. Just be aware of its limitations.
