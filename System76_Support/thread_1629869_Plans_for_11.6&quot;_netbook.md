### Post by jml on 2010-11-27
The 10.1 inch maximum was a standard created by Microsoft for netbook manufacturers to follow if they wanted to install Windows XP on their netbooks and not use Windows Vista.  Microsoft mandated that Windows Vista be installed if the screen was larger than 10.1 inches.  This has become a moot point now that most manufacturers are now installing Window 7 Starter edition.

Personally, I think that the original concept of the netbook:  small, inexpensive, running Linux, rugged, with long battery life was lost once they started putting standard hard drives into them, growing the screen past 9 inches, and installing Windows XP.   Just my opinion.

Joe

### Post by gobbles414 on 2010-12-02
I'm afraid that I must respectfully disagree with you on both counts, isantop.

There is indeed a HUGE difference between 10.1" and 11.6". Go someplace like Best Buy, Verizon Wireless or Walmart, where they have the two sizes side-by-side, and the difference is clear. Or, go someplace where college students gather and look at the posture of those using 10.1" versus the posture of those using 11.6". Consider Sprint, which recently began selling two Dell 3G/4G netbooks. Sprint is giving the 10.1" version to customers for free with contract, whereas they are charging an extra US$150 for the 11.6" model. Granted, the specs inside Sprint's 11.6" are much more impressive than their 10.1" model, but I'm sure that at least some of the additional price is related to the "premium" which many customers place on the increased screen real estate. Then there's the issue of netbook bags; one designed to hold a 10.1" will almost certainly NOT hold a 11.6".

You say that Lemur is "nearly as portable as the Starling". I feel that statement depends upon one's point of view. For example, to a frequent flyer, the 2"+ difference in width and the nearly 1.5" difference in depth could be the difference between fitting it in a carry-on or not, fitting it on a tray table or not, etc. Lastly, say what you want about Apple, but they tend to be rather good at assessing what's going to sell and what isn't. Apparently, Apple has determined that there is a substantial enough difference between 11.6" and 13.3" to justify using both sizes in their MacBook Air lineup.

Of course, System 76 is in the business of selling computers for a profit, and those running the company must decide for themselves whether or not there's enough potential profit in 11.6" netbooks to justify adding them to the System 76 lineup. I obviously don't have access to System 76's sales data or market research, but based on my own feelings plus the feelings expressed by others in this thread, I suspect that 11.6" systems would sell quite nicely indeed for System 76.
