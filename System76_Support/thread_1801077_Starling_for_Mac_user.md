### Post by BBQdave on 2011-07-10
Hi,

I have used Macs from os 9 to os X (10.4).  I found the open source software I like to use (like Scribus, GNU Image Manipulation Program) worked better in Ubuntu than the ported versions on Mac.

I have an old eMac that I use as a file and print server (Ubuntu communicates well with a Mac).  And I am running Ubuntu 10.04.2 LTS (Long Term Support) on an old dell laptop.  I like the stability of a LTS version of Ubuntu and I keep my favorite software updated (to current release) through PPA's.  You can go directly to your favorite App's web site for instruction on how to install their software through PPA.

When the old dell I am using dies, I am going with System76.  You can read through the forums to get an idea of the good company they are.

The only challenge I am still working on, is iTunes.  For me personally, I have not found a good replacement for that App.  Their is an App through Fluendo, Moovida PRO.  This looks promising, as I can continue to encode music as mp3's (to match my existing music library in iTunes); and hopefully, eventually move all my computing (and music library) to Ubuntu (on System76 hardware).

If you do try Ubuntu 10.04.2 LTS, this is the old style of Ubuntu.  The future is Unity in Ubuntu (11.04) and the next LTS from Ubuntu will not be out until next spring.  LTS have a total of 3 years of support, where as the other releases (11.04) is supported for 18 months.

Hope this was helpful.  There are plenty of great applications in the GNU/Linux world, and Ubuntu is the best (and most stable) linux desktop I have ever used.

### Post by macaholic on 2011-07-10
As someone who grew up using Macs, I can say that there are several things you need to know. 

For one, there's a lot of "DIY" when it comes to fixing problems. While System76 is very helpful and responsive with their support, most problems you'll probably have better luck fixing by googling them and playing around yourself. At first you might not be very good at it, but Linux/Ubuntu troubleshooting is something you'll get better at the longer you use the OS.

In essence, Linux is fundamentally harder to use than OSX, but I have always found it more rewarding in the end.

### Post by jml on 2011-07-10
I have used both Macs and Linux boxes for over ten years.  I enjoy using both of them.  I have to agree with macaholic.  Ubuntu is not as easy to use as OSX, but with a little effort, I think a person will be able to get Ubuntu to do what you need it to do.

Major areas of difference:  Multimedia.  Out of the box, Ubuntu will not play encrypted commercial DVD's.  You will have two options, you can buy non-open source software to play the movies, or you can add the Mediabuntu repositories and "roll your own solution."

Second issue, iTunes:  There is no really good replacement for this in the open source community.  Again, a person needs to jump through hoops to play the AAC files that are the default format for iTunes.  I save my music in MP3 format ( for the music I rip) so I have a chance to use it on my Linux box.  The purchased tracts are still problematic.  I use a MacBook Pro to manage my music collection and play it on a classic iPod.

Now you are talking about getting a Starling.  Now if this is not going to be your primary computer, the iTunes issue is a moot point.  I find that the new desktop shipping with the current version of Ubuntu, (and included with the Starling,) is very Mac OSX like.  So from my perspective, it's not a major issue.  I think that you would be quite at home with it.  Hope this helps.
