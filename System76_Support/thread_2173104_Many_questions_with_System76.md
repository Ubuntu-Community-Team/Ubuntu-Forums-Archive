### Post by rorschachwalter on 2013-09-08
> **DRatJr said:**
> 1. Are these laptops up/downgradeable?

Depends on the model and which kinds of upgrades you're talking about. Look at the options listed on the System76 website to get an idea of what's possible for each model.

>  2. Could I take one with 13.04 preinstalled and wipe it to install 12.1 or whatever?

You can do whatever you want with wiping and changing versions (even changing OSes), but System76 only officially supports the current Ubuntu release. This also means they *do not support the LTS release*. So you can install an older version of Ubuntu if you want, but System76 won't bother making it work with your device. The new machines -- the Galago and the new Gazelle, for instance -- have Haswell, which isn't supported in anything before 13.04, so an earlier version of Ubuntu is unlikely to work for you.

> 3. General inquiry: why is the 14.1" laptop model so expensive? I really want a Ubuntu laptop but 1k is a lot to drop on one. I already have my powerhouse laptop (desktop replacement) and was wanting a mid range spec one. Is the price tag anywhere near worth it?

Clevo machines are always expensive for what they are, and System76 machines are always expensive for what they are. With the Galago, the new processor itself is quite expensive (I believe it's around $440). The IPS display adds *some* to the cost, although not nearly as much as manufacturers would lead you to believe. The rest of the components are pretty lousy -- lousy speakers, cheap chassis, worst webcam you'll ever use, unusable keyboard, low specs besides the processor...but keep in mind that, again, these machines are *always* too expensive for what they are. Clevo makes a nice profit off these, and System76 has to make a profit after buying them from Clevo.

The Galago gets only ~2 hours of battery life, runs extremely hot, and has various issues with wireless and suspend.

If you're looking for something mid-range, there are tons of other options out there with better build and lower price, and a bunch more were just announced at IFA. For the price, pick up one of the new ASUS or Lenovo machines. ASUS, for instance, is often said to have the best non-Apple clickpads and drivers, and offers 1080p IPS displays (and higher!) on their new machines, shipping with a good quality body and Iris graphics.

There's no such thing as an "Ubuntu laptop", and I've found that even my generic Dell runs Ubuntu better than my "Ubuntu laptop" from System76.

### Post by isantop on 2013-09-11
Our policies regarding support for LTS haven't changed. A system will run any version of Ubuntu that's newer than the computer without many issues. We won't make a specific effort to make them run... because we already did when that version came out.

We can't support prior versions of Ubuntu on the latest hardware, due to incompatibilities. Doing so would require rolling a custom version of the LTS anyway, or delaying all hardware releases to correspond with LTS releases (and skipping entire generations of the latest hardware developments).

I've said this beofre, but I should point out that LTS releases aren't significantly more or less stable than normal releases. They're intended for businesses with large numbers of machines, and who can't update all of them every six months, and thus update them every two years instead, which is much more manageable.

### Post by gaussian on 2013-09-21
Just wanted to add a little long-time customer perspective on this System-76 debate. I have been Linux exclusive pretty much since 2004 and I have owned two System-76 computers: a Darter (Daru2, bought 2007) and a Lemur (Lemu4, bough 2012). While I just ordered a small Asus (1015E Ubuntu model) from Newegg, the next time I am buying a computer I will still give System 76 a serious look provided they have something in their lineup that fits my needs. This time I was looking for a super cheap (turned out to be $229) and a super small computer (10-inch screen), so System 76 was not an option.

It is true that System 76 models don't always live to their "Designed for Linux" hype and some models turn out to be less successful than other either for incompatibility or other reasons.  Daru2 lost its ability to suspend after Ubuntu kernel updates, causing a lot of unhappiness among customers. For me it was not a big deal, but many were really unhappy. System 76 was never able to figure out a solution, except staying with a non-current version of Ubuntu. Regardless, I would probably still be using that Daru2 if it had not been stolen (hardly System 76's fault). I loved that computer, it was probably the best computing device I have ever owned.

My Lemur has been pretty much perfect. I compromised when I bought it, I wanted to buy something smaller and now after using it for about a year I still wish it was smaller. But that is also hardly System 76's fault.

But on a big positive side: if you buy a System 76 computer you get two things. First, you get a company that supports your Linux installation and that in my experience has a very good customer service. I know there are naysayers about the latter point, but my experience has been 100% positive. Second, you get a community of a people, some of whom are very helpful and knowledgeable, using the same computer for Linux and a centralized way to reach those same people (=this forum). Try that with a random computer that you bought from a non-Linux retailer. And even the big company Linux-specific models have had their issues, I remember the Dell Ubuntu-specific netbook being quite a mess in the beginning (this was 2010). And at least with Dell and Asus you get the other extreme, instead of having to update you are forced to stay with the LTS. E.g. the Asus I just bought (and haven't received yet) has 12.04 pre-installed and has a known wireless incompatibility with 13.04. There is a solution involving compiling your own driver, so it is not a big deal for me, but for many customers it would be. So System 76 is not perfect, they don't always have answer for every technical issue, but in my book there are enough positives to consider System 76 purchase among other options every time I am looking for a new computer.
