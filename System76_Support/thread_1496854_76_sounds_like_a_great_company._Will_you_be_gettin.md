I just checked out the site, and it looks like you have some nice budget models that I dont remember last time I checked. Thats very cool. If I ever buy a new computer I will probably go to 76 for it because I would like to avoid buying computers with windows installed...I mean, I install linux anyway, but if I want to live up to my ideals of supporting the alternative then I need to stop supporting the status quo.

I really like the philosophy of Ubuntu. I like that it is providing free software to the world and making it a viable option for the common people (not JUST geeks). I respect that, perhaps more so than some others who are 'just doing their own thing' or appealing to a nitch crowd (not that they are 'wrong' for doing so). I like that Ubuntu is finding ways to make money while still making it absolutely free for the consumer to use. I believe that a lot of the world, especially poorer countries, are greatly benefiting from this and I would like to support and promote it.



Any chance that you will be getting any netbooks with touch screens that double as fold over tablets? That is really what I need for my next purchase. I need something that I can use as an ebook reader so I can use digital textbooks for cheaper and less space (Being able to see the screen outdoors is a must), but also have some functionality as a laptop and allow me to take notes in class with something like a wabcom or MSI touchpad or maybe add a multi-touch pad into the device itself....has anyone done that yet?

Using cloud programs also sounds cool, perhaps using you own personal desktop as a serve....not like a server server, but a PC that hosts programs remotely, but where you can also tap into the schools computer system or some other cloud service. 


If you could develop cloud software for students that could be hosted on university campuses for educational purposes, that would be a good place to go with this and its not something that has really been developed sufficiently by anyone yet....its a way to become the cutting edge rather than playing catch up in an artificially uneven playing field. Touch screen tablets and smartphone technology is really where this is going though....you want at least some of this stuff to be accessible to smartphones and you want to tap into the market for readers. A laptop or even a smaller netbook is just not ergonomically friendly if you are using the device as a textbook or multi-touch note taking device. Ability to sync with smartphones using touch programs is also a huge plus.


Finally, will you be shipping out your hardware with any other distros? Kubuntu, Xubuntu, ect?

What about third party distros that are downstream from Ubuntu? As much as I like Ubuntu, I have to give props to Linux Mint for taking those extra steps to make it easier for complete noobs. Being able to play commercial DVDs and listen to their MP3s out of the box without first learning how to hack their system is a HUGE point for them when it comes to new users...I still Ubuntu, but I am giving Mint to my family members. You might want to take some cues from them if you plan on marketing to the GP. The restricted driver codec doesnt really go far enough.....though if you cant include certain software for fiscal obligatory reasons, something like a restricted driver promt after you turn it on is just fine.


You do have the Edubook, but how about a multimedia laptop for musicians? I dont think any of your competition has done anything like that yet. With some further tweaking and maybe some partnership with some electronic drum kit/pad companies for software compatibility, you could have one amazing product that places like guitar center would be selling and not just Frys.

You could probably also find a market for some ultra budget machines....a dinky little pentium-3 laptop at 966-mhrz, 512 ram, would sell like crazy if it started as $99 without Wifi or optical drive....people have externals and USB inputs from older machines. 




Im just rambling a bit hoping to stir some discussion...Im not a geek or developer so I may be mistaken about a few things, though I do consider myself the creative type. I make my living on internet sales.
