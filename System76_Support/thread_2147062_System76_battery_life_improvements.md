### Post by Leslie Dorner on 2013-05-20
This is aimed directly at System76 and also other System76 customers worldwide that speak English. I was wondering if it is possible that battery life can be improved if I continue to upgrade to the latest stable Ubuntu 64 bit releases every April and October of each year? I am specifically interested in reading the replies of other System76 customers that own older notebook PC models to see if this has been the case for them or not.

I have a current generation Lemur (lemu4) and I noticed that my battery life now exceeds 4 hours and 15 minutes using Ubuntu 13.04 64 bit with the stock Linux kernel 3.8.0-21-generic version. I also noticed that battery life is further improved when I chose to upgrade to the Linux kernel 3.9.2 64 bit main line version since it supports the more modern Intel P-State power mode found in the modern Intel 3rd Generation "Ivy Bridge" CPUs and later. The older Linux kernel "ondemand" governor was scrapped and this means that CPU frequency handling and P-State power modes are handled much more efficiently thus preserving battery life.

I'm using Ubuntu 13.04 64 bit and I've logged into the LXDE desktop environment and I can almost swear that I have roughly 4 hours and 25 minutes of battery life. This is due in no small part to the light weight and efficient nature of LXDE combined with the modern Linux kernel 3.8.0-21 generic. If I boot using Linux kernel 3.9.2 64 bit and I replace my lithium ion battery with a freshly charged one, then I can almost swear that I get 4 hours and 45 minutes of battery life or longer.

What have been your findings thus far?

I am postulating that Ubuntu 14.04 64 bit will usher in even more performance and battery life efficiencies along with greater stability and long term service and support. I can see the distinct pattern leading up to 14.04 right now with this current 13.04 release and the leaked information regarding the upcoming 13.10 64 bit release in October this year. If Linus Torvalds and the Ubuntu Linux kernel team keep delivering new features and improvements in power efficiency, then I can see a much faster and more battery efficient Ubuntu release in October 2013 followed by a particularly outstanding stable 14.04 release in April 2014.

I don't see these types of innovations and improvements occuring with Microsoft Windows 8 PCs. In fact, I'd have to buy a new notebook PC with a high capacity lithium polymer battery to get longer battery life. I see that Apple MacBook Pros and Airs continue to hold their line at the 7 - 8 hour battery life mark. Linux has some catching up to do to become more competitive, but I am seeing those incremental improvements with each successive Ubuntu release or two.
