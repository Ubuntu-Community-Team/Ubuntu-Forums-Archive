System76 Tablet

I read the Carl Richell interview [here]("http://serial-coder.co.uk/blog/2011/02/an-interview-with-system76-cofounder-carl-richell/") and was thinking I could make a plea for the tablet specs that I would call my "Dream Tablet".  Besides making me jealous that Carl is 31 and CEO of System76, he mentioned he liked to read the forums for what users were thinking ):P.  I figured this would be a great thread to tell him exactly what we, as a community, want to see developed.  I've compiled a list from specs I've read and heard that people want.  I would even be willing to buy one where the hardware is put together but software isn't complete (due to ubuntu on ARM not "ready for prime time" or something).  Put a disclaimer on the web site about it, or hide it and send a link to people asking (or in my case begging :)).  The hacker community loves taking things apart so if vanilla android could run on it, all the better for sales toward that demographic (that tablet doesn't exist and probably never will unless you make it).  So the overall idea I've heard is people are looking for something in the $500 range.  I think based on other tablets this is possible with the below minimum specs:

1024 x 600 (7"), 1280 x 800 resolution (10")
.75 inches or thinner
1 pound or lighter
1 GHz+ dual core (ARM based for battery life and android hackers)
512 MB+ RAM
16GB+ solid state storage (NO HARD DRIVE!)
Storage expansion slot (MicroSD)
1+ USB port
HDMI (standard, mini or micro) out
WiFi - b/g/n
Bluetooth
GPS
Expected tablet sensors (accelerometer, magnetometer, etc.)
Audio in/out (3.5mm)
3MP+ camera (flips to face front or back like notion ink adam, if that would save cost over a second camera)
8+ hours usable battery life (preferably removable)
MicroUSB charger

Options:
Keyboard dock/stand via non-proprietary port (like a bottom USB port)
Available internal slot for mobile broadband?

-------------------------------------------------------

So what do others think?  What is a required spec for you?  Preference toward 7" or 10"?  What would you add that would make you want it more, or take away to keep the cost low?

### Post by MarkID on 2011-03-10

If there's no New York Times app for S76/Ubuntu, then I can't use it to read today's paper, and the tablet has dimiished use for me. I see three ways to deal with this.  One, Ubuntu/Cannonical can encourage development of 100,000s of apps, as Apple and Google (for Android) have done.  Two, develop "universal" apps using standards that will work in most situations (a newspaper reader, a universal remote.)  Three, an iPad emulator.  All of these approaches have drawbacks.

### Post by satsujinka on 2011-03-11
@MarkID
I'm not certain I agree that tablets are inherently different from PCs.
As far as I'm concerned tablets only have two areas in which they may be better: portable media consumption and handwriting/drawing (combined because they require the same things in a touchscreen.)

Ideally, tablets should have an option to drop into "sunlight mode" so that reading on the tablet can be done comfortably in sunlight, however, the technologies that allow this tend to be low resolution, low frame rate, and occasionally limited color spectrum. This means that a tablet good for reading will not do video or games very well. Many people are probably happy with lcd, amoled, or ips displays for reading, however, I still think that none of those are ideal for the task (though they excel everywhere else.)

Two other possible use cases I can see for tablets is in the case of students and artists. Writing is typically more effective than typing as a study mechanism so having a portable device that can hold a students textbooks and their notes (that are handwritten) would be a huge advantage. Similarly, artists may use a tablet as a sketchbook. Allowing them to create their art anywhere and skip the step of digitizing their work (which a normal sketchbook would require.)

My own analysis of the use of tablets would suggest that resistive screens would be better (as they tend to have superior abilities at fine grain control for writing and drawing.) So I don't get why so many people and companies prefer capacitive displays.

### Post by isantop on 2011-03-11
Capacitive displays are good for multitouch. It is possible to do multitouch resistive touch screens, but they're generally less robust and more expensive than a single touch equivalent. Capacitive displays are lower maintenance too, as they don't require any calibration. They also don't react to non-capacitive surfaces, like cloth of a pocket or backpack, which is good for phones in a pocket. They may also be less susceptible to damage, as they typically now utilize a sturdy glass digitizer.

They are less accurate when it comes to super fine control, but then again, for finger interaction, a resistive screen is often far less accurate. For a writing tablet, a resistive screen is best. But given everything most modern consumers want a touchscreen device for, you'll want the extra bit of flexibility that the capacitive displays provide.

Aside from that, I think "modern" consumers are mislead in their wish for a capacitive display. A quality resistive touch screen will always trump a capacitive one. So "wanting" a capacitive display is more akin to wanting a feature phone over a smart phone (which may be a valid wish, but as far as general usefulness goes...)
