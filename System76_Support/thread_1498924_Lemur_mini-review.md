### Post by bigcat42 on 2010-06-01
I received my System76 Lemur in the mail a few days ago, and I wanted to post some of my reactions. 

Overall hardware - It looks damn pretty. Like Macbook pretty. And the light weight & thin body are far more impressive in person than the numbers would suggest. Frankly, I’ve been looking for excuses to carry it around and show it off to people.

Keyboard – It has a different style keyboard than I’m used to, but I’m quickly taking a liking to it. The keys are kind of wide and flat, and in the same arrangement as other laptop keyboards that I’ve used in the past. The “Ubuntu” key (aka the Windows key, Super key) was a bit of a let down, it looks a bit like someone pasted an Ubuntu sticker on top of the key instead of matching the rest of the keys.

Trackpad – Pretty standard, feels good. The left & right buttons are sort of built into the trackpad itself, rather than being separate buttons. Still deciding how I feel about that, but it does make the trackpad section look pretty slick.

Battery – Haven’t had the time to put the battery through its paces, but System76’s estimation of 3+ hours seems about right.

Processing power – Speed is more than enough for my purposes, mostly web browsing, watching internet videos, and playing music. It sometimes feels sluggish while loading multiple web pages, but that may just as likely be my internet connection.

Heat/noise/comfort – It generates more heat than I expected, perhaps it would have run cooler with the SSD upgrade instead of my HD. Then again, the light weight and form factor make me want to rest it on my lap far more than previous laptops, so perhaps my expectations are skewed. Machine runs fairly quiet.

OS/software – (Machine basically includes a standard Ubuntu install, plus the Sys76 drivers. I understand this is largely out of Sys76’s control, but it’s worth noting as part of the experience.) Lucid Lynx is pretty slick, and I like pretty much all the updates. I am having the same issue with Flash that I’ve seen other people report, where some flash content doesn’t register mouse clicks. Also I can’t seem to get Gwibber to work; it won’t display anything from my Twitter account and I can’t even add my Facebook account.


Overall, I’m very impressed with my Lemur, and I would heartily recommend it to others.

### Post by bigcat42 on 2010-06-13
After a few days of using my Lemur, there's something I want to add - this is a 14" widescreen, in other words wide and narrow. I generally like the widescreen form factor, but it's a bit problematic with Ubuntu's default layout.

Open Firefox and you have
-Ubuntu's top application/indication panel
-FF's title bar
-FF's File-edit-view-etc bar
-FF Navigation Toolbar
-FF Bookmarks Toolbar (though I hide this one immediately)
-FF Tabs
-FF bottom status bar
-Ubuntu's bottom panel (currently set to auto-hide)

Combine all that with the Lemur's already narrow vertical space, and you're missing a surprising percentage of useful screen space. I'm still toying around with how to best address this issue. The upcoming Unity netbook interface actually addresses the issue of vertical screen space pretty well. Also, this is only really noticeable while using Firefox, so maybe I could just use FF fullscreen view more often.

I am open to suggestions about how to better lay out my desktop in order to maintain more vertical screen space.

### Post by rg4w on 2010-06-14

This is a problem with most laptops these days, emphasizing movie playback (16x9) over reading and writing (vertical space).

On my Mac I minimized this problem by moving the Dock to the right edge - a virtually unused space which frees up move vertical space.

This is unfortunately problematic with Ubuntu because of a cosmetic anomaly in panels in which the background pattern repeats vertically in a way that isn't nearly as attractive as the rest of the OS.

But if you can get past that cosmetic issue, moving your bottom panel to the right can be helpful for browsing, writing, etc.

I've also experimented with doing away with the second panel altogether, moving its elements into the ample unused space in the top panel.  While it's better in some respects it can get a bit cramped, but in some ways I like it (although it doesn't "feel" right to have the Trash near the top).
