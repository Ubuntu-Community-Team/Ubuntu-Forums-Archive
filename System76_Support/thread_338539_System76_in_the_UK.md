### Post by TheVeech on 2007-01-31
I'm reluctant to part cash with a UK company.  This is still Rip-Off Britain in many areas.

However, the idea that I shouldn't complain unless I set up a company offering the deals I'm looking for is about as ridiculous as it gets.  What...you don't like the fact that you face being ripped off?  Sorry, boy, you haven't got a foot to stand on.  You want competitively priced goods?  Start your own company or shut up like the good little insignificant consumer that you are.  We are the businessmen.  We took the risks.  So cough up all that lolly, like a good little subject, respect your superiors, BUY BRITISH, and God Save the Queen!

As consumers we have the power to take our business elsewhere if we don't like what's on offer.  That's how it's supposed to work.  And as democrats, we're entitled to voice our opinions regardless of our occupation.  If consumers are restricted in their choices - i.e. in a land-locked, possibly price-fixing scenario - they should be as vocal about parochial hardware restrictions as people here are about Free Software.  We certainly shouldn't accept the idea that we should not act like rational consumers, but throw away our rights if we're not 'entrepreneurial' enough.  Capitalism is about consumers as much as businesses, and that's the playing field we're on.  We're as likely, if not more likely, to effect change through our behaviour as consumers than if we start businesses.

But if this behaviour is restricted, change is less likely.  If I want a PC, I should be able to get one as easily in the US as in the UK, without the hassle that goes with it.  In other words, if I find a better deal with another company, I shouldn't face prohibitive penalties in exercising that choice.  UK companies (and multinationals trading in the UK) should face that competition so that they buck their ideas up - they certainly aren't going to do it voluntarily.  They only try it on with over-charging UK consumers because there's not much to make them do otherwise.

The obvious and 'user-friendly' options aren't there.  I don't want to have to go the Customs and Excise website to do sums on whether it's worth my while to buy a PC in the market of my choice.  Most people have actually got a life and haven't got the time to check with various agencies as if the machine were an immigrating relative.  As for national loyalties, well, UK consumers have been getting screwed forever and a day by many UK companies, so there's no logical reason to be loyal to them by giving them more money than the goods are worth in a global context.  Buying a PC, or any other high-priced item, in a more competitive market isn't as easy as it should be.

Maybe System76 could make it easier for people to order from the US when they're based in the UK.  Unfortunately, when I need a PC, I haven't got time to wait an indefinite period for someone at some company to think about it, however linux-friendly they're said to be.  I can just as easily configure a PC that ships with another OS and do my own install.  So I don't particularly need System76 if I've got to wait for that option.  They offer PCs with Linux on them?  Big wow.  How tough is it these days to install Ubuntu?  I certainly wouldn't hold off a purchase on the basis of vague suggestions of maybe this, maybe that, when I can grab a bargain off ebay and have it configured in no time at all.

Besides, what difference would it really make if System76 started trading in the UK?  There's another Linux-friendly company that offers UK consumers PCs with Linux installed.  The premium you have to pay isn't worth it (compare their prices with competitors offering similarly spec'd machines and you'll find a much better deal with the latter).  I'd be highly surprised if System76 or any other company trading in the UK would act any differently to how companies trading here tend to behave.  In other words, don't hold your breath for comparable prices for comparable machines.

Recall the arguments in the 80s and 90s about how UK firms had no choice but to charge over-the-odds for music CDs?  IIRC they had to charge consumers disproportionate amounts for CDs because they had to pay truckers extra for driving trucks and eating bacon sarnies, or something.  Times and technologies have changed.  Has the music industry collapsed as was widely predicted by the music industry?  Nope.  How much can you get a CD for today?  Certainly much less than the price that was said to be essential for survival.  Everyone knows that we get ripped off in the UK in many areas.

If someone rips me off, it's irrelevant where they come from or how much they play on the lie that it's me as a consumer who solely determines their employees' fates.  (I love how businesses can go through a deathbed conversion and get all Keynesian when their interests are at stake.)  Rip me off, and I just don't do business with you again.  Easy.  That's how it works.  If the problem lies in a country's market, I'd like everyone to have the easy option of using another one.  That's the way it should work and, if it did, prices in this country would drop quicker than you could get a Keynesian press release out.

Until then, enjoy a bit of linux research and happy ebaying!
