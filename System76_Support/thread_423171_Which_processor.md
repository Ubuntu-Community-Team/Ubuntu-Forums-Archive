### Post by akniss on 2007-04-27
I can't seem to find the site where I read this, but some time ago I was trying to figure out which was better between the Celeron M and Pentium M.  The main difference between those processors is the number of frequencies that they are able to scale to.  The Celeron M is either at maximum clock speed, or minumum clock speed; whereas the Pentium M has a range of frequencies it can run at. (My 2 GHz Pentium M can run at 600MHz, 800MHz, 1.2GHz, 1.4GHz, 1.6GHz, 1.8GHz, or 2.0GHz, for example.)  The Pentium M is better able to scale itself to only as much power as needed for the current task, and therefore will increase battery life compared to the Celeron M which must scale to maximum clock speed as soon as the processor is needed.

I would assume that the C2D chips are similar in that they can scale to only what is needed, and should thus increase your battery life compared to the Celeron M.  Battery saving should be most noticeable under light to moderate usage, when the C2D will be scaled to a low clock speed, whereas the Celeron M would be running at maximum.  Someone please correct me if I'm wrong.

### Post by maniacmusician on 2007-04-29
> **AusIV4 said:**
> Lithium ion batteries should be stored at a charge of 40% when stored for extended periods.
Here's a link for information on lithium based batteries: [http://www.batteryuniversity.com/parttwo-34.htm](http://www.batteryuniversity.com/parttwo-34.htm)

There's no significant difference between those processors in the area of power usage. Of course, the faster processor you get, the higher the power consumption will be, but thanks to something called **frequency scaling**, the difference is negligible. What this does is allow your processor to run at various speeds, from low to high, so that it only uses as much power as it needs. 

So, if you don't utilize the power of your processor a lot, it will stay at a low frequency and use less power. But when you need to use more power, it will be there, ready for you to use. Frequency scaling is completely automatic and adjusts for you while you're working.
