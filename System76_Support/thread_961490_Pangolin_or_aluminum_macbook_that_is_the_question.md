### Post by thomasaaron on 2008-10-28
Unfortunately, I know nothing about macbooks. I've never even picked one up.

The Pangolin, though, is a beautiful, solid machine with absolutely top-notch, state of the art hardware. There's nothing cheap about it.

You might want to compare the Pangolin and the macbook you like and see which one will give you the best bang for your buck from a hardware and software perspective.

If you buy a Mac, will you be installing Ubuntu on it, or will you keep the Mac OS?  If you are going to install Ubuntu, how difficult will it be to get all of the hardware working - and keep it working as Ubuntu progresses? With the Pangolin, we are constantly testing our hardware to make sure it works with the latest versions of Ubuntu.

If you're going to keep the Mac OS, you might want to figure out the longer term cost of ownership too. Everything for a mac is expensive and proprietary. It is nearly impossible to get changes pushed upstream. And technical support is expensive. We're still providing technical support to our first customers for free. (The extended technical support primarily pertains to hardware.)

### Post by OldDirtyTurtle on 2008-10-29
I can't tell you what to do but I can tell you why System76 just got $2,600 of my grant budget for a new Serval Performance beast.

1)  They're the little guy with an obviously great product.  Compared to other resellers (Dell) I can get more machine for my money without padding the pockets of a huge corporation (with pretty crappy customer service in my experience).
2)  They answer questions and offer support FAST.  Look at the average response time on this board for even the most trivial questions.  It is mind-numbingly fast.  
3)  They answer emails FAST even for people simply considering a purchase.  I had a pretty good email conversation going with them when I was trying to decide what to buy.  And they did not push their product - they simply answered questions.
4)  They push improvements upstream to Ubuntu.  If you believe in open-source freedom, then why not support it through a base system from a company that contributes to open source software improvements.

Don't get me wrong - I love Macs.  They're sleek, sexy, and they work.  But I believe in open source more these days.  Enough so that I've jumped ship in the middle of my dissertation on almost all software options at home and have made the full switch at work with the delivery of my new laptop next week.  I don't mean to proselytize...  I simply believe in this stuff.

You gotta do whatever is right for you, though.  :)
