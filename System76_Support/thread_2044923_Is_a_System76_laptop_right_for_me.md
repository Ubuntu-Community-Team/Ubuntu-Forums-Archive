A general rule is that  business requirements drive software requirements which drives hardware requirements. 

One requirement is note taking. One of the weaker parts of System 76 is battery life, about 2 hours and change,  which has been attributed to the current Ubuntu kernel. So if you are going to be near a plug, this is not a concern. But if you are going to spend 4 and 5 hours unplugged at a time, battery life will be a concern.

If you are programming in Visual Studio, you will almost certainly want to build a Windows 7 machine, whether on physical hardware or on a VM guest which can have a Windows based machine or a Linux based machine as the actual host. If you are moving to java or one of common scripting languages,  or even if you can use the GNU C++ compiler on an Eclipse with the C++ plugin, you can use Ununtu quite easily.

Many Engineering software packages are Windows based and you may be able to run them on Wine but may need to run them on Windp7ows machine or VM guest. Also, many of these packages require significant screen real estate. So, you may want to research whether you can use the 720P - 1368X766 display on the Pangolin, or need the 1080P 1920x1080 display on the Gazelle.

You don't mention what gaming you will be doing, but bear in mind that System 76 is using the onboard Intel 4000 chipset. This may alright for the gaming you had in mind, or not. 


Depending on how much time you need to spend in a Windows environment due to the above requiremwents,  you may find System 76 may or may not be the best choice.

My reasons for buying a System 76 laptop:
1.  I had a strong desire purchase a  laptop with with the latest chipsets and CPU's, preinstalled with a mainstream version of Linux, in this case Ubuntu,  System76 met this requirement. And  I use Oracle's Virtual Machine to maintain a Windows 8 VM guest, in additon to using Wine for a Windows app I need.
2. I wanted to be able to get support from a staff committed to supporting Ubuntu on the hardware platform they sold. I spoke with System76 associates and conversed with them via email and have confidence on gaining support when I need it.
3. I wanted an excellent display 1080P on a 15 inch from factor. System 76 met this requirement with the 1920x1080 matte in the case of the Gazelle. 
4. I wanted at least 16GB Ram along with an Icore7 Ivy Bridge chip set to extend the useful life of my purchase. System 76 met this requirement in the case of the Gazelle. 

All I can say is to make your own requirements list, talk with the Systems 76 staff to see how their equipment can meet your needs and then make your decision.

WINE (though it is not an emulator) does not work on all software and may require some fiddling for other software.

But VMs just work: you can run windows in a VM (say, virtualbox) and 100% of your software in it will work 100% unless it needs hardware acceleration for the graphics (basically, games) or other specific hardware stuff. The only problem is that a VM is a bit slower, so running software that is already pretty slow on a normal Windows machine will be yet another bit slower.

Visual Studio is good, but IMO it is not worth sticking to windows just for that, nowadays the options for developing under linux are pretty mature (eclipse, netbeans,..), and if you really need it you can run it in a VM or just reboot into a windows partition.

### Post by RedRat on 2012-08-29
Be Aware!
Running Windows programs under Wine usually works--'usually' is the keyword here. You might be able to get some programs to run under Wine and some you may well have to futz about with to get it up and running. I got accustomed to running a graphics-picture manipulation program called ThumbsPlus on Windows and found that I could install it on old Ubuntu 8.04; however, I had a deuce of time getting it on 12.04-64. It took a couple of days of fiddling around and downloading some DLLs from MS. I cannot get it installed on my Mint 13 installation on another machine (a Dell Inspiron). However, I could get my another program I use for searching Newsgroups, NewsRover, to install on the Mint 13 machine, but it will not run properly on the Ubuntu 12.04!

I give these examples to demonstrate that not all Windows programs run or install under Wine. You may well have to find Linux equivalents of some programs that you are interested in using. While the Linux community tried to provide programs that are used in Windows, many of these programs either are limited in their capabilities or only do a partial job. Be aware of that. 

Since you are thinking of notetaking in classes, battery life is going to be important. In many classrooms, you just do not have access to a wall plug. I would suggest a larger battery because, depending on performance and other factors, you may well need a battery life of several hours.

Let me first say something relevant to the OPs post. I agree with Wine (and Crossover, last time I tried it about four years ago) being imperfect solutions for running Windows programs. Virtualbox (and other competing products) work perfectly as far my experience goes for anything except games.

Now to what I really wanted to say. I have taught post-secondary (i.e. college) for more than a decade now. I hate to shout, but I will. YOU DON'T NEED A LAPTOP FOR NOTETAKING. Barring a very bad case of carpotunnel or something similar, a pen and paper is the superior technology. First, it does not distract you (no Facebook in your pen). This is not that important advantage since you probably will have your distractive cell phone with you. The bigger advantage is that with pen and paper you can make notes that are in a completely free format easily. Even with a tablet you cannot (unless you are much better than me) make diagrams, write math etc with the same easeness as with pen and paper. With a laptop you are positively scr*wed when the first math notation/diagram comes. Obviously you can do math/diagrams with computers, but it is quite slow. I teach economics and typically none of my students have their computers out in the open during class. If you want to go high tech, you can look into products like Livescribe. I have not used Livescribe or similar products myself, but at least they could work in principle.

None of this is to say you don't need a laptop for your studies. You do. Or at least it will make your life much more convenient. Just keep it in your dorm room or your backpack during lectures.
