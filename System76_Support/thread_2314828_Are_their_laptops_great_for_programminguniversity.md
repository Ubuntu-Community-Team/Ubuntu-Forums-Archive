### Post by donald-a-pellegrino on 2016-06-19
A Linux laptop is great for programming and would be a good fit for most University Computer Science departments. However, System76 appears to focus on support for Ubuntu. My perception is that most Linux clusters and shared computing environments make use of CentOS. In addition, [many XSEDE systems use CentOS]("https://portal.xsede.org/knowledge-base/-/kb/document/agat#xsede"). Shared systems, clusters, and XSEDE resources are generally the most capable target platforms for academic and research computing. It is convenient to have a personal laptop that is similar to those target platforms. A laptop with Ubuntu will be more similar than one with Microsoft Windows. However, it will be less similar than one with an RPM-based distribution such as Fedora, Red Hat, or CentOS.

### Post by roby.gamboa on 2016-09-20
The two main questions to answer are:
[LIST]
[*]What type/extent of programming are you doing?
[*]What's your carrying capacity?
[/LIST]

To answer the first question, I'm a solutions architect and tech lead for a major corporation. I have an Oryx Pro, and I have to say it FLIES. It has already replaced my server-grade desktop. I use it for programming (Java, Prolog, bash scripts, etc.) and have several VM clusters configured to try out several solution designs prior to recommending them for use at work. I skimped a little on drive space (128GB SSD OS drive and 1TB conventional HDD for data), but went with 64GB of RAM instead, which gives me plenty of headroom for a full J2EE VM stack (load balancers, LDAP server, load-balanced web and app servers, and clustered DB servers). The VMs are all set up in VirtualBox and are running CentOS, rather than the Ubuntu host OS, and I haven't seen any issues with this configuration in the last several months that I've had the laptop.

With regard to the second question, the thing is a brick, weighing in at over 7 pounds. Lugging it around on a regular basis would definitely qualify as a work-out. Like I said, I'm using mine as a desktop replacement, so this isn't as much of an issue, but it's definitely a factor for consideration. If I were to consider using one in a campus setting, I think I would go with dual SSDs, rather than the hybrid SSD/conventional drive configuration that I'm running. SSDs are definitely a lot more forgiving of shock/vibration than conventional drives are. If weight is a limiting factor, consider going with a lighter model, but keep in mind that your basically trading weight for capability. There's not much that System76 can do about that.

I've found that the support team at System76 has been extremely responsive and knowledgeable. For me, this is a definite plus. I know my way around system configuration and troubleshooting/repairing both hardware and software problems, so when I resort to contacting a support organization, it's usually because a demon has infested my system and I need back-up. Compared to other support organizations that I've worked with, theirs is excellent, which sometimes counts more than the purchase price of the laptop.

Hope this helps, and best of luck finding a model that suits your needs!
