### Post by TheFu on 2014-12-04
There are many, many guides. Unfortunately, I haven't looked at any of those step-by-step guides in 15-ish yrs.

However, gaining a good general understanding of Linux architecture, design, expectations and commands will be extremely helpful.  Everything is a file (almost).

Grab a free copy of this [http://linuxcommand.org/tlcl.php](http://linuxcommand.org/tlcl.php) (no hassle download) and read the first 100 pgs, then skim the rest of the book to know what is possible for later, but not the details. You don't need details today.  The book is also available in every bookstore if you want a physical copy.  The beginning goes a long way to explaining "why" Linux works the way it does.  The Linux/UNIX philosophy is very different from other OSes. Understanding that difference will save days, weeks, months of frustration when it doesn't work like those other popular OSes.

There are also certification books and tools from Linux.org and LPIC ... if you want a more formal setup.  I've found the LPIC training to be extremely out-of-date - they cover things that I've never seen in the real world - like things that were old in 1993.  OTOH, 5% out of date information isn't terrible in the computing world. I do not have any certs and think of LPIC certs as the shortest way to get a government job, but not a way to get any commercial-world job (that takes RHEL certs in my part of the world).

Lastly, there is no better way to learn any OS than to use it daily, always, for everything. Total immersion. Don't use Windows or OSX or Amiga or C64 or OS/2 anymore.  Use Linux, use the CLI, shell, to do everything you can. Struggle to get things working. That struggle is critical.

Linus wrote a short book _Just for Fun_ ... it explains why/how Linux was created. It is a quick read (took me less than a day). Many public libraries have copies. This is part of learning the philosophy of Linux.  A few short articles about this philosophy difference:
* [Linux Thought Shift Needed]("http://blog.jdpfu.com/2012/02/15/beginning-linux-thought-shift-needed")
* [What's wrong with new Linux users]("http://blog.jdpfu.com/2009/12/09/whats-wrong-with-new-linux-users")

And of course, the link from Lars is excellent too.

Never forget to as "is this a good idea?" - just because something CAN work, doesn't mean it is a good idea from a maintainability or security perspective. There are many solutions that are NOT secure to use and haven't been in years. Only you can ask yourself, "is this a good idea?"
