### Post by Glut on 2005-10-14
You may wish to do a search on many topics in this forum of this nature.
Ubuntu hasn't been around very long, and even though it's based on Debian, this does not make it the same as Debian. Known stability and security is very important to administrators. Also, Ubuntu lacks the commercial support of the very big server flavours. 
Case in point, we use Debian as our base server, and we have problems enough getting support for that. Tech support can knock you on the head as soon as they know that you're not running a supported os. 
Also, you may need to have known hardware support. For instance, Sun guarentees that Suse and RHEL will work with their servers. While you may well have absolutely no issues with Debian, or Ubuntu in particular - you might have to make a purchase without being 100% sure. If you're making large purchases (I'm talking more than your annual income in a single hit as a base) then it can become a financially risky decision.
The final issue is a double edged sword. Ubuntu has 6 month releases, with ATM 18 months support (soon to grow to 5 years) If you've got a 6 month release schedule, it could be very easy for the team to skimp on reliability in favour of the new and interesting technology. If you're building a server, you may not care about the new stuff. The better side of this is that you have a well defined and supported base, unlike (say) MS, where you never know when the next server release will come and you cannot plan very far ahead for when your support will end.

IMHO Ubuntu's biggest problem in becoming a well known server distro is its rep as a end user install. This reputation is not undeserved, the Ubuntu team spends a great deal of tmie and effort on this side of Ubuntu, with excellent results. The flip side is that this overshadows any effort that they make in creating a "reliable, dependable and many other words that end in 'ible'" environment.
