Hosting a domain is different from serving the dns for it. It's fairly easy to setup dns services for a group of domains. You need a minimum of two servers to do it right (that is, have real redundancy) but two vps could be used. Most good domain sellers will have 4-5 servers and offer you this for "free" when you register. 

I think my last renewals cost me about $5.95 each - that's pretty cheap considering many corporate users are still going through high end sellers and paying $30 and up.

### Post by windependence on 2009-03-26
You are very very confused. You are already at the best place to do what you want to do. I own 19 domains through GoDaddy and I don't host ANY of them there. THEY are the registrar, and you jjust by the domain. Then, you set up a server (preferably with a static IP) and you go into your DNS control panel at GoDaddy and point your domain at your server using the 'A' record. You do NOT have to host the domain with your registrar (GoDaddy). I host all my domains myself.

### Post by windependence on 2009-03-26
Going to ICANN to register your domain would be like going to the IRS building in Washington to pay your taxes. At any rate they don't register domain names, they administer the top level domains .com .net, and .org. 

Your registrar is perfectly fine. Bob Parsons isn't going out of business tomorrow. He is the owner of GoDaddy, and he wrote the software in the 80s that is TurboTax today. He's an ex marine and still privately owns the company. It is not a public corporation. 

I live only a few miles from their data center. You can't imagine the size of their center. They have thousands of servers. Their DNS is on the main backbone of the internet. If you think you will never depend on another company for the internet, think again. You rely on them each and ever day, because the internet is not owned by anyone. I think you need to read up a bit on how the internet works and specifically how DNS works. All the DNS servers around the world are all linked together. They form a huge network that turns your mydoamin.com into an IP address since computers speak in terms of numbers. When you tell your registrar to enter a DNS entry in their DNS servers, their servers contact other servers on the net and tell them mydomain.com is 123.234.213.134 or whatever the IP is of your server. Those higher level DNS servers tell others and so on and that is how your domain "propogates", or spreads around the world. This process can take minutes, or in some cases a few hours depending on traffic on the internet. Until this happens, when people try to go to mydomain.com they will get the infamous "Page Cannot Be Displayed" error because computers on the internet do not know who mydomain.com is.

You can see from the above that if you run an authoritative DNS server exposed to the net, you could really get some people PO'ed if you put incorrect entries out on the net. The thing is, you don't have to. If you just want to run a small DNS server for your home network to learn or make it easy to ID computers on your network, fine. But no one has "control" of anything if you don't run a public DNS server. None of the major fortune 500 companies I have worked for had an authoritative DNS server in the organization. They rely on external DNS servers from companies that do DNS, or the DNS servers of their registrar. Those servers are up 24/7/365. Yours may not be.

-Tim
