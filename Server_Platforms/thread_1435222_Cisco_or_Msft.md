### Post by inphektion on 2010-03-21
since you said "network systems" the choice is easy. Cisco.
In the enterprise no matter how much one may hate msft their Active Directory, Exchange, OCS, type stuff is actually good, manageable, etc.
But connecting those servers and services together with linux servers running whatever other needs you have, the top choice is cisco gear.  Others might respond with other preferences but the market is cisco.  Switches, router, firewalls, even their push to unified computing with the nexus product will be great for large enterprises. ### Post by smc18 on 2010-03-21
It wouldn't hurt to learn both to give yourself a diverse skill set in the job market.

If you had to choose one or the other, I would probably would stick with Cisco, however you can't be afraid of the command line (I doubt you are afraid because your on a Linux forum).

The way I see things is. people say they want to become a System/Network admin with no real interest in the work.  They only hear IT jobs are where the money is at, so they start learning their Microsoft products.  Well the same is going for Cisco.  There is a lot of hype for students to become Network Engineers so they go learn how to configure Cisco equipment, some not having any interest in the topic.

I'm not trying to attack you, but you have to realize this marketing trend and make yourself better than all the other people who are there just to get a job and have no real compassion for the work.


Good luck and have fun with the choice that interest you!!

### Post by netztier on 2010-03-23
> **powel212 said:**
> 
Cisco or Microsoft network systems.


When I attended the Cisco Networking Academy back in 2000 (which would've lead up to be CCNA ready, took the test later in '05), 80% of all stuff we learnt and spent our time on was networking knowledge; Layer model, ethernet, serial lines, bridging and switching, IP addressing and subnetting, routing and routing protocols, the lot. The remaining 20% were: "...and this is how you configure and verifiy all those things on a Cisco device".

In the network I maintained back then, we had just started to ship Cisco devices to replace IBM labeled OEM gear (3com switches, Proteon routers, with loads of TokenRing stuff...). With all the conceptual knowledge I had picked up during the *Cisco* course, I was able to finally bring  stability and robustness to the *IBM* based network that had been cobbled together in a pretty rough fashion.

Hence: go Cisco. You're bound to learn how to net work (pun intended), which will come in handy not only on a Cisco based network.
