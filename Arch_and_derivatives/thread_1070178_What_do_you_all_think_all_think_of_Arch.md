### Post by Sorivenul on 2009-02-15
I appreciate the Arch philosophy, relatively comprehensive documentation and support, and design principles. The support for Arch, evident from the number of declared Arch users on these Forums, is tremendous and a testament to the quality of the distribution. The "hype" about Arch is well deserved, and reminds me somewhat of the frenzy surrounding Gentoo not so long ago, except that the excitement over Arch is continuing to grow while the excitement over Gentoo seemed to plateau about 2 years ago. 

For what it's worth, in 2006, Arch was the 22nd most popular distribution around DistroWatch, 19th in 2007, 17th in 2008, and as of today Arch is 15th (taking the past 6 months into account) or 13th (taking only the past 3 months into account). While these statistics are not a complete representation of "popularity" or overall usage, they are fairly telling.

I'm a BSD user primarily, and Arch is a bit too current for me, even though I understand that updating is not always necessary. Given the choice between Arch and Ubuntu, I personally would choose Arch. Given the choice between *BSD and Arch, I would choose *BSD. I like Arch, and used it for some time. Do is still use it? No.

Just my two cents.

The majority of problems that I've had with Arch is not so much the OS itself but the applications that are updated and the bugs that sometimes accompany it.

> It doesn't even come close to how elegant of solution etc-update is.

No searching for config change files.
No fumbling with vimdiff or any other diff viewers
Gives you complete control on how you want to deal with config file changes. Either manual or automatic change acceptance

[http://learn.clemsonlinux.org/wiki/Gentoo:etc-update](http://learn.clemsonlinux.org/wiki/Gentoo:etc-update)

I know it's small, but, the lack of a "config file" merge tool was one of my biggest annoyances I had with Arch.

I'm just really surprised that Arch hasn't implemented something similar. 
etc-update is great however dispatch-conf is even better ;)

My understanding, is that the Arch philosophy is to only modify any code when it is absolutely necessary.  Which I think is very rare.  As far as security is concerned, it is expected that any problems found with any code by anyone using that code, (no matter what distro) are passed upstream for the maintainers of the package to remedy.

This seems to work.
I am a **strong** opponent of distro-specific packagin.  However, my issue is that Arch doesn't provide specific security advisories or have a dedicated security team.  Security isn't their goal, which is totally OK.  But I want something a bit more ironclad.
