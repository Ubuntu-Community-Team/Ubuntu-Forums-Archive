### Post by sgx on 2010-06-26
Start with a room that has temperature and light controls, and seating that won't deter movement.
Before spending money, use qjackctl for connections, Timemachine for recording, Rakarrack for multi-fx, Hydrogen for percussion patterns and sample triggering, and Zynaddsubfx for core sounds. Make a song with 5 to 8 sounds, and a good drumtrack. Zyn is multitimbral for layering several sounds. Make it good. Use Audacity sound editor to import Timemachine saved files, zoom in on them, and edit out the bad parts, boost quiet parts, minimize the loud spots, add fx etc.

Count on 12-24 hours  hours getting familiar with the basics of these 6 powerful apps. Your creativity and playing skill will not be hindered by them. Most musicians create useless mud when using more than 8 parts in a song. Simple melodic and harmonic elements used without conflicting each others sonic range, can be very effective and rewarding.

You should make a system for naming songs, and the various takes used to
build the final version, in both .wav and mp3 for sharing. I use the names of sounds  and fx in early titles so I can set up the same way each time if needed. Make backups. 

Cheers

### Post by sgx on 2010-06-26
View a song as a series of modules that can be seemlessly cut-n-pasted.
As you record a melody over a rythym part, try to end runs and riffs at points where the background parts and ambience will be easy to join to
other parts, mismatched effects in one song make it more difficult to edit. Rythm tracks and lead-lines that overlap the last beat of a measure can also be an issue. Give yourself some quiet at the end of a crucial measure, and joining modules is very easy. It can be short, but it must exist.

Guard your eyes, if they feel tired, they are. Take a break and practice, use smokey polaraoid sunglasses, low contrast monitor settings, avoid florescent lighting, stay hydrated. Grumpy warn-out musicians are at a
disadvantage

Cheers

Mud? Man, I can mix *concrete* with 8 trax! :guitar: But it's all about separation and space, innit? Or 'deliberate dark ambience' (aka Mud), eg: [Portishead]("http://www.discogs.com/artist/Portishead"), [Morcheeba]("http://www.morcheeba.co.uk/") etc.

@ **feardotcom**

"Money not an object?" and "Best possible sound?" 

Hmm, then depending on your musical taste, a Bosendorf Grand+ 3 Neuman mics going through an analog mixer* or the very best genuine analog synths you can get hold of ([Hammond Novachord]("http://www.synthmuseum.com/hammond/hamnovachord01.html"), Oberheim 1980-87 era, SE Omega, Waldorf, Roland 100 etc) plus a [FFADO-supported firewire]("http://www.ffado.org/?q=devicesupport/list") card to get it all digitised *at the very last moment* for drumloops, funky FX and subsequent cyber-use... ... ...IMO.

* An analog mixer doesn't have to cost the earth - very creditable results can be achieved with a Soundcraft Folio Notepad or an older model Allen & Heath (GL2 range?).

Probably this advice won't be popular around here but if you want to achieve a sound that goes more to the heart rather than the head, I still don't think you can better the *true analogue route*, preferably ending up, via 1/2 inch tape, on vinyl when it's good enough...

[www.recordplayer.com](www.recordplayer.com) - for that penultimate 99.7% towards aural nirvana...

As for the DAW stuff, I'm finding a combination of JACK, Hydrogen and the truly ergonomic QTractor (mainly for MIDI and samples) plenty for my needs. I've yet to A-B compare Focusrite firewire against a Revox B, but it's coming 'real soon now' ;-)

Oh yeah, one more point - doesn't matter which route you take (unless you keep it all inside a computer), you will be festooned with miles of cable - bank on it and never compromise on quality - best approach is learn to make your own from top quality components & cable, IMO
