### Post by sgx on 2010-04-17
There are plenty of dubious practices going on in linux and behind
the camp in the shadows. Don be deceived into thinking fallen human nature is magically vanquished, based on the kernel of the operating system you booted. Commercial software developers have to protect their products/productivity from piracy, and have to be as good or better than skilled competitors, just to provide for their families, and to be able to give to those devastated by sickness or tradgedy. Freeware coders, be it Mac, Windows, Linux, Amiga, iphone,  etc etc are in a different postion, though still needing to protect their ´day-job´ for the above mentioned reasons, and many of us benefit greatly from their generosity.
 
As a studio professional, I would have at least 5 computers, a Mac, a win7 pc, a dual-boot linux/XP pc, and two linux PCs, one rpm based, and one debian based, and one of the linux boxes set up to compile the latest sourcecode of crucial apps. This will cover the needs of most potential clients, and keep you armed with the best tools for any particular job.

I laugh at Mac whiners complaining there is too little software for them, and windows users trampled by virus, then trapped between a BSOD and a dodgy driver update. You may never need Pro-tools, Logic, Amplitube, Omnisphere or Komplete, but if a client does, then be ready and able! :)

### Post by hxcobd on 2010-04-24
If you're someone who uses a lot of samples in your work, and doesn't need a ton of synthesis and bleeding-edge effects units, linux works just fine for audio work.

Ardour is a good example. If you're recording bands or artists who are on a budget and can't afford tons of time-correction or pitch-correction, an Ardour setup might be just fine to get them recordings that are "good enough."

And if you're actually composing music, if you're using a lot of samples, there are numerous linux programs out there that can take full advantage of this capability. Renoise is a personal favorite of this variety. Hydrogen is another great one.

However, if you're like me and only settle for absolute top-of-the-line synths and effects, linux is a rather depressing beast.

I use a LOT of Omnisphere in my work, as well as virtually every other overpriced synth or effects unit you can find. For things like these, I don't trust a WINE-d DAW like Reaper to try to carry their heft.

Then again, I just don't trust WINE in general. If you're dead-set on using a huge variety of audio programs in linux, WINE is going to be the only good way to do so, generally through either Reaper or the Windows release of EnergyXT.

Myself, I'll continue to be satisfied with Windows for audio work until plugin development on linux really takes off.
