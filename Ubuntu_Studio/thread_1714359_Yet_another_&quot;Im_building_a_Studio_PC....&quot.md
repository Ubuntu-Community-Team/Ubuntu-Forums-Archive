### Post by jjex22 on 2011-11-15
I know what you mean, I've got my dream pc all planned out, only slight downside is it's currently pricing out at 2,216 quid using used ram!

Well obviously you want to start with the motherboard - if you're looking at a studio machine, then something sli/crossfire supporting is a must, also ddr3 ram is essential nowadays - not only is it the new standard, it's also so much cheaper - I only mention this as there are a few "bargains" still bobing around with ddr2 ram, and they will really cost you in the long run if you want large ram, if you can, It's also worth baring in mind that intel today released socket R - 2011 to replace 1366, so presumably 1366 boards will start falling in price after christmas, and lets be honest that socket can already go up to a 6 core i7 over 3.4 ghz, so you're not going to run into "out of date issues" any time soon.

Having built two pc's the lessons I've learned are

1) the PSU is actually the most important part - get this wrong and it can go on a killing spree

2)you have 2 options - spend serious serious money getting absolute top of the line so it'll still rock in 5 years, or buy last year's top stuff and begin with a pc one year out of date - building a pc is a lot cheaper - but they know we want the latest gear and the launch prices of components  can be double what they are in a year.

3) plan, plan, plan - there's nothing more depressing than buying a case you've coveted for 6 months as soon as your hall is full of pretty boxes to discover you have to cut a massive hole in it for the cooling system, and you actually could do with another couple of bays!

4) Motherboards - Asus, fantastic customer support for me, I'll be honest it was only the Asus board that went wrong, the gigabyte outlasted everything as far as Ii know, but assume there are always mistakes and Asus replaced the dead mobo I received free and then sent me a bios flash and tool because the replacement had the wrong bios version on it - and quick too, couldn't be more pleased withit. 

5) Finally always go by recommendations, and don't buy parts that haven't been reviewed yet - let others take the plunge - I prefer [newegg]("http://www.newegg.com/") for component reviews.

Most of all try and have fun, it can be stressful as hell at times, but you really will have a lot of pride in your new baby when it's done!

### Post by w1ngnut on 2011-12-11
Hi,

I build/repair PC's for a living and I would recommend that you go down the AMD route.
More bang for the money and they generally run cooler than Intel Chips, so no loud cooling fan noise.

As  for PSU's, work out what wattage you need to run your system, allowing  about 15% for leeway, then add 50% to it. Most home builders forget that  when you plug in USB devices they may require power from the PSU. The  extra 50% should allow you to add USB devices without overtaxing the  PSU.

When selecting a case remember that cooling is paramount.  Try to get a case with more hard drive bays than you actually need. That  will allow you to leave an air gap between them to aid cooling. Avoid  gamer cases with lots of flashing lights on them. Waste of power.

Motherboards are a matter of personal choice, however Asus and Gigabyte tend to be OK for the money.  

Corsair  memory is good, but tends to be expensive, Kingston tends to be cheaper  and has a lifetime warranty. As to how much, 32bit OS's can address  3GB, 32bit PAE OS's can address 64GB but any process is limited to 3,  64bit can address >1 TB (depending on the motherboard bios).

Finally,  hard drives. Get hard drives with the fastest average seek time you can  find. Disk I/O tends to be the slowest part of any computer. The faster  a drive can read/write data, the faster your system will be. Remember  that performance will tend to drop off as the drive gets full, so buy  the biggest drives you can afford.

If you need any further help, PM me your e-mail address.

Cheers

Trevor

### Post by w1ngnut on 2011-12-12
Hi Sylos,

The 50% headroom rule is only a guideline, not a hard and fast rule.

Any brand of power supply will do the trick, there is no need to go out and buy a really expensive make like Antec; you are only going to be running it at about 2/3rds of it's rated power output. 

The important thing is having sufficient overhead so that you are very unlikely to get close to it's maximum rated output. One benefit from this is that you can upgrade components in your computer at a later date without having to worry too much about power.

You must remember that running a power supply at, or near it's rated output is what kills them. 

If you can't afford to buy a 600W PSU, then buy the biggest you can afford.

Hope this helps

Trevor
