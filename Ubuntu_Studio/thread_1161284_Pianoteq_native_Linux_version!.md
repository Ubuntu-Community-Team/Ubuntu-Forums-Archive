### Post by ethanay on 2009-05-16
[http://www.pianoteq.com/pianoteq_changes](http://www.pianoteq.com/pianoteq_changes)

This is an amazing real-time piano modeling software.  No, it is neither free (beer) or open source.  But the software is solid, innovative, usable, and the developers have a wonderful attitude and offer great support.

Up to this point, I have used it as a 32-bit Windows application via Wine, WineASIO and dssi-vst.

I am extremely excited to see that there is a native Linux version -- another victory for Linux audio users!  I strongly encourage you to download the trial and give it a test drive if you haven't already.  It is available in 32 and 64 bit versions:
[http://www.pianoteq.com/try](http://www.pianoteq.com/try)

Ok, I am ending my little commercial.  Disclaimer:  I am not affiliated with nor do I stand to gain anything from this message.  I'm just really happy they have offered a native Linux version (thank you Thorgal!) and want to support that effort by promoting it in the Linux community.

here is a link to the Pianoteq forum discussion:
[http://www.forum-pianoteq.com/viewtopic.php?id=629](http://www.forum-pianoteq.com/viewtopic.php?id=629)

### Post by sgx on 2010-06-02
This is quite the excellent collection of keys. The vibraphone and Rhodes
are a delight, these would fit in great with zynaddsubfx, and running
two hydrogens, each with 32 vibraphone sounds for a 64 note range to
send out the beats is a jewel waiting for polish.

The Grands are very rich and responsive, and have a powerful sound that
jumps out when velocity is turned off, which means even a $99 Yamaha or
Casio keyboard can be put to good use, and with a powerful home stereo, make the neighbors wonder how they missed spotting the piano movers :)

A midi controller with deep sensitivity will be put to good use by
classical pianists, and skilled cover artists needing expression. The demo is very generous, for those needing testing time before slapping the plastic.
