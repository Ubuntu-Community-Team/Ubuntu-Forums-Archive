### Post by Stochastic on 2009-09-06
**Sonar**: Ardour (maybe also audacity, rezound, rosegarden, or jokosher - just to name a few)
**Amplitube**: Any of the LADSPA plugins will replace this.  If you want something really similar throw them into JackRack.  There's also guitarix, jesusonic, stompboxes, GTKgep, Lack of Invention, and gnuitar.
**Zbrush**: Gimp or Blender
**3dsMAX**: Blender.
**Corel Draw **: Gimp, Inkscape, or Openoffice Draw
**Adobe Premier pro**: Blender or Cinelerra, maybe also Kino, Pitivi, or Kdenlive
**Adobe Flash**: Haxe. (this is one area that is very difficult for open source software, due to Adobe's licensing on the flash format)  You can always run an older version of flash in Wine.

If he's anything like me, he won't settle for anything less than pro-quality software, especially for audio stuff.

There is no great comparable alternative to Sonar. Ardour is great for audio, but without any MIDI whatsoever, he probably won't have that much use for it, realistically. QTractor is a possibility, but again, nowhere near the sheer range of options that Sonar has.

### Post by sgx on 2010-05-04
Its important that we not make the tools of daily life into a religion. Every OS and the apps made for it, has something that is 'best', people
who reside in camps, and maintain software shrines, will blindly miss
out on something. Using the best tools within our reach ('reach' loosely defined as our time, money, ability, and desire) frees us to be
productive, dealing as best we can with the limitations of our tools
and skills. I poke fun at linux, mac, and wintel, all are deserving
at times. A video pro might be best off with a Mac, but find good use
of linux for a wide and time saving array of apps that can be updated in synaptic in minutes. An audio pro having used a favorite daw for years, has little incentive to leave their knowlege behind, just because they have options, but may find an update to something, or a failure for a needed update to occur, leaves them with a niche need that linux apps and capabilities can easily meet. People short on money, but loaded with desire and ability, may thrive just using and creating within linux, only to find at some point, that something just can't be done that they want or need to have done, and can pay for an affordable, workable alternative.
Some people choose only linux or mac or pc, its their desire and joy to break new ground within those confines, and that focus drives
innovation, which in turn invites or demands competition, from which everyone benefits.

### Post by kayosiii on 2010-05-05
> **Camuflage said:**
> Hello i have a friend that asked me if i could found some equivalents of his multimedia software, that he uses, that's the only way he would dump Windows in favour of linux. Since i'm not very aware of this type of software, i'm looking for some help with those who have experience in audio and video production.

Sonar

Ardour is the closest you will find and I suggest learning it as well as how the jack audio system works and allows you to use several apps in concert. This allows some degree of pick and mix as far as audio apps go.
There are some short commings at the moment but most of these are being actively worked on. You might also look at reaper or something similar

> Amplitub
There are a number of solutions for Amp simulations under linux. The most commonly used are the cab ladspa plugins. Keep in mind this  is sort of a pick n mix situation rather than a one stop shop. A good amp simulation will be built out of several plugins and these will need to be in the right order. Beyond that windows VST plugins can be hosted by some Linux apps though they may do not always act as intended.

> Zbrush It looks that very soon ZBrush will be the only major sculpting app not supported on Linux. MudBox is being ported, 3DCoat is well worth looking into and Blender is reasonably good at sculpting these days(if you are feeling short of cash)

> 3dsMAX
3ds max is one of the few major 3d platforms not supported on Linux. Maya, Houdini, SoftImage all support Linux. Newtek looks like it will with it's next gen product. Some of the smaller players like realsoft3d do linux versions and finally there is Blender and Wings3d if you are looking for free software.

> Corel Draw 
Inkscape is probably the best comparison here. Inkscape has a very similar editing style. Corel Draw has a lot of functionality that Inkscape does not. However Inkscape does cover everything I would concider essential for vector editing.

> Adobe Premier pro
I can't honestly say that you will find a NLE up the same level of polish as Premier pro. But I would perhaps look at Cinelerra, Kdenlive or once again Blender.

> Adobe Flash 
I can't really reccomend anything here and I don't see anything coming. There are some tools built that cover the animation side of Flash. But as an environment for developing for a specific web platform you really only have one choice. I do see tools arising to achieve similar things to what flash does but using HTML5 technologies rather than proprietary browser plugins. 

> Of course he also plays some games like crysis and all the ones that are on the top.

Thank you.
You might have noticed that a lot of pc games now say Games for Windows and they say this for good reason. 

If your friend is looking for a drop in replacement for his existing work flow with the feel good factor of running Linux then I would suggest that he/she would end up being disappointed. 

None of the replacements I suggested completely ape the ones that your friend is using. Even switching between apps on Windows that do the same thing takes an amount of time and effort.

If on the other hand your friend is looking to do creative work in a self employed sense, has some degree of technical patience and doesn't have the capital to afford licences for products you mention (they add up to quite a bit) and is good at learning software. Then I can say that perhaps picking up Ubuntu Studio as a second operating system would be a worthwhile thing to do.
