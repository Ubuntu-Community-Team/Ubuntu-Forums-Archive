### Post by sgx on 2010-04-24

What is linux's answer to dxi/vsti?
There is no answer, because there is no question. Use what you like.
Use what you can afford. Use what works, and works best. Don't use the others. To quote myself in another post:

I use vsts in Reaper, with the wineasio driver, in the same sessions with hydrogen, zynaddsubfx, phasex, rakarrack etc. Almost all vst plugins that are not sold with dongles, ilok, pace, or other high-end copy protection, work without issues. A few synthedit designed instruments,
require a specific path, or occasionally turn jackd into a zombie with some weird preset or arcane feature implementation, very smoothe sailing overall. For example, I copied all the relevant Native Instruments folders, around 5 locations in XP, to their linux counterparts, started
Reaper, and all the plugins loaded just like they were on the other
ntfs partition. Other vst hosts that I have tested, that work (your mileage may vary) are Cantabile12lite and full, MU Lab, and the Windows version of EnergyXT2.

I would rate Zynaddsubfx and Phasex as world-class synthesizer instruments. The equal in sonic potential would cost around $150, if you knew what was out there, there are a few, not many. The Hydrogen Drum/sample playback machine is the best percussion pattern designer I have seen
and any standard .wav can be used to make 32 instrument kits, not just drums!

For sample playback instruments, there are high quality collections that routinely have a 'group buy', of great value. The Sampletank 2.5.4 engine is now available with a 1 gig soundset, and 4 sound collections to choose from out of 10, for only $50 from Sonic Reality. I have an older version that works fine in Reaper/linux.

For fx, Rakarrack is great, I tested it compared with Amplitube 3, Guitar Rig 3, Shred, Freeamp  etc and an inexperienced user would likely want,
but not NEED the expensive commercial products, and be able to dazzle listeners from a simple and effective linux setup. 

More and more musicians are incorporating linux into their workflow and studio/stage. Installation is easier now, and google/youtube and forums
supply good answers.

The main caveat, is to purchase hardware that is known to work, so you can play music, instead of IT Detective.

Cheers :)
