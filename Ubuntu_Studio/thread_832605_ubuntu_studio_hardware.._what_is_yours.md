### Post by Stochastic on 2008-06-18
The only sound hardware you need to worry about Ubuntu compatibility with is your soundcard.  A mixer will only require Ubuntu support if it is a firewire mixer (and thus performing the action of a soundcard as well as a mixer).

For vocals you should get a nice large-diaphragm condenser mic and a soundcard with good preamps (see [Freebob Firewire Driver]("http://freebob.sourceforge.net/index.php/List_of_Supported_Devices") or [ALSA driver]("http://www.alsa-project.org/main/index.php/Matrix:Main") for a list of working cards).

Also if you're going to be doing Hip-hop in the studio (as your tags suggest) then try to find a card with MIDI support.

### Post by audiomick on 2008-06-21
Hallo.
First, my qualifications. I've been working as a professional sound engineer in Australia and then Germany for something over 20 years.

You say you are thinking of building a patch bay for your sound card. If the card has got 3.5 mm jack connectors, then (in my opinion) it's not a real one. Buying what you need for a patch bay that one can take seriously is astonishingly expensive, to which you have to add the time involved in building it.

I'm not really up on what's on the market, BUT there are enough units available which already have "proper" connectors on them. There are also lots of good reasons for using balanced in and outputs, and no reasons not to. Particularly if there are things like computer power supplies lying around. A 3.5 mm stereo connector is generally unbalanced, and putting the signal through an XLR connector doesn't change this.

To cut a long story short, my advice would be not to mess around. Do a bit of research and get an interface that suits your needs. You'll be a lot happpier in the long run.
Michael

I appreciate what you are saying, but a lot of us are playing around, as the OP said, with HOME studios in spare rooms, and often with limited budget.  

The purpose of the external box, is not so much to be a real patch bay, but to provide connections to other connectors, like XLR, 6.5 TS etc for budget devices that use 3.5mm TRS type connectors.  Not only matching the connector type, but relieving the physical strain on the soundcard socket that you get using adapter plugs and cables.  Particularly for a small portable setup like mine, which is running on a laptop. Plugs sticking out of the side are very prone to damage.

In addition to the inbuilt soundcard in the laptop, I have usb audio in/out via an effects pedal that has 6.5mm TS in/outs, and I am thinking of getting another USB audio 'soundcard' for general purpose use.  Many USB audio 'soundcards' still use 3.5mm however, or, if you're lucky, RCAs. Remember, it's all on a budget.

I also have a small mixer that has some balanced inputs and phantom power, for my LD condenser microphone (not a high end one, but still good sound). The mixer has RCA out for delivering the mix to computer.

The amazing thing is that, despite the many cut corners, like unbalanced inputs, with great software like Ubuntu Studio, you can produce some very good sounding recordings, and do lot's of great stuff, all in our spare rooms !  It's all good fun :)

### Post by Sakrimo on 2008-06-23
First of all, anything than records in with a 3.5mm jack is not going to give quality results, and theres no excuse for doing it, not even on a budget.

Get some serious cables, rewire and solder. One of the massive problems i had when i first started recording was wrong cables. Good equipment (150 Watt Line 6 Guitar Amp Head, Ibanez RG Prestige 2570 which is a very nice guitar), all let down by using the wrong cables.


Instrument cables are for instruments, stereo cables for stereo connections, mono for mono. Don't follow this and you WILL lose sound quality. Now it's a small budget but it does't cost anywhere near as much as you'll have spent on the rest of your gear.

I.e, £200 electro-acoustic guitar, recording through laptop built in microphone or a 3.5mm cable with a 1/4 jack adapter. Not too much to ask really?


And onto the origional question.

I plug in through a USB Mixer interface, it's your call on this one, but most even have multi-track recording features that are coming out now, much more value for money IMO than a sound card. But i can't stress this enough, ensure it is USB 2.0, you will get NOWHERE with 100ms latency.
