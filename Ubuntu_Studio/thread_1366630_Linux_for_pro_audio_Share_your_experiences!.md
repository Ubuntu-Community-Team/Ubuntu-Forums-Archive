### Post by trulan on 2009-12-31
Well, I'm not a professional but an aspiring amateur with (hopefully) more talent than money.  I'm also a tinkerer, and my system would probably be healthier if I did less tweaking and more working.  ;)  (What is a professional anyway?  I can easily sell enough CD's to cover the costs of a project, but I certainly can't make a living on music alone.)

I mostly am interested in recording acoustic music, played by myself or our band.  So the #1 priority for me is a clean, natural sound every time. So I went from four track tapes, to multitracking under windows 98, eventually on to a Presonus Firepod and windows XP.  With this setup, it looked good on the surface, but my projects always degenerated into me trying to hand-remove the inevitable pops and clicks in my recordings.  I was never able to eliminate what was causing them.

Enter, Ubuntu Studio.  Not only was I free of pops and clicks, I was able to use my firepod with my laptop, which was entirely impossible under windows (bad drivers for its firewire chip?  It works under Win7 but it's too late now - byebye windows...)

I use Ardour for my DAW, Patchage to route the signals, and add reverb and such to my headphone signal with either Jack Rack or the Calf Plugins.  For the occasional midi needs, I use Qsynth and Qtractor.  The ability to have full control of the signal routing, even inside Ardour, has allowed me to either wow or completely confuse everyone I have showed it to.  I even routed part of my signal back out of my firepod and processed it with an external device at one point, fed it back in to Ardour and mixed the whole mess down.  I'm coming to really appreciate the Linux model, many small programs working together instead of one big program trying to do it all alone.

To sum it up, after having worked with the flexibility of jackd, nothing else would cut it anymore.

I know very little of synths and VST's.  A friend was showing me what could be done with what I already had installed, and I was very impressed.  But I would be using those things as toys, definitely.  If I want to make real music I'll have to get my guitar. ;)

my experience is that supported hardware is the first need, and working software the second. For linux audio, I believe Reaper in a wine/wineasio

For workflow, a 6-desktop KDE 3.5.10 is the best on any platform to keep
apps uncluttered and accessible.

zynaddsubfx multi-timbral softsynth, rakarrack multi-fx, and Hydrogen

jackd and qjackctl and wineasio are not options.

ubuntu would not be in the first few linux versions I tried for audio, but it is ubiquitous, and what we learn here usually applies in any linux variant.

@audio          -       rtprio 99

If you still can't get it, search for help on this issue.  This is unfortunately a very common problem.

### Post by thorgal on 2010-01-07
be aware that processing at very low latency is more CPU consuming (many more buffers to process in shorter times). Just look at the jack "DSP load" at latency ~ 1ms and latency ~ 20ms. 

Run at low latency only if you truly need it (realtime processing, mainly). For editing audio, mastering, etc, a higher latency is a much better environment.
