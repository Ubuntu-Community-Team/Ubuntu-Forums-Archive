### Post by bodhi.zazen on 2007-03-26
There has been an undercurrent of what I will term Quality Control in Absolute Beginner Talk.

I have seen the issue expressed in several ways, but would summarize is as follows:

> What to do when conflicting or worse misinformation is posted in the Forums ?

My take is that if we see this situation arise we should take it as an opportunity to educate. First, the information should be reviewed, and then some type of resolution should be suggested. If you personally are involved in such a situation, ask or help ~ are there any other team members on line ? Avoid starting any kind of flame war. If you see others, team members or not, involved in such a situation offer assistance.

For example, sometimes there is more then one way to skin a cat. In this case it should be pointed out that either solution will work.

Sometimes, however, poor or even bad advice is given. In that case I think we should educate the posters ~ Why is the advice poor, what is the correct solution, etc.

If information seems more malignant in nature, the post should be reported to the mods. For example, there are certain commands which will do serious damage to a system.

IMO this is an optimal solution in the spirit of the Ubuntu Forums.

Any thoughts from the rest of the team (or others) on this issue?

### Post by bodhi.zazen on 2007-03-29
I have given this issue a lot of thought. None of the folks who raised the question have commented so I would like to re-iterate my view of the situation.

The issue I would like to address is in the treatment of members of the Ubuntu community who may make mistakes in posting advice.

Ubuntu and in particular the Ubuntu forums is open to new users. We are a community of users helping other users, and not paid technical support.

See here for policies : [***Beginner Talk Rules***](http://www.ubuntuforums.org/showthread.php?t=65842)

In particular, > [LIST]
[*]**There are no stupid questions** (II:1). Everyone was new to Linux at some point.
[*]**Be polite, respectful **(I:1) **and considerate** (II:6).
[*]**Try to communicate as clearly as possible** (I:10).
[*]**Give basic instructions**, in step-by-step form when necessary (II:14). **Try to avoid jargon** (II:7).
[*]**Answers like "STFU", "RTFM" or "Google it" [COLOR="Red"]ARE NOT ACCEPTABLE UNDER ANY CIRCUMSTANCES[/COLOR]** (II:8 ). Those answers will incur warnings, infractions or bans if a staff member feels it is appropriate. This is a cardinal rule of our community.
[/LIST]

IMO this set of rules is why I am active with Ubuntu.

IMO this courtesy not only includes posters who start threads, but to all members of our community.

Thus, IMO, we should extend the same respect to members of our community who make mistakes, whether that be a typo or mistaken advice/technical information.

It should seem self evident, but I will hold Beginner Team members to this code of conduct.

If you see errors, please educate and correct those errors. If you see malignant posts, report them to the mods.

FYI Malignant postings are moved (by the mods) to "jail" and are no longer viewable.

I also like this set of rules I came across in the UDSF :

[*]in laymans terms - Make me feel stupid first, before you make a user
feel that way. At least when I feel stupid I can do something about it,
the beginning or desktop user cant most times.[/list]

~ Quote [color=red]KingBahamut[/color]

Obviously the UDSF rules are outside of the Ubuntu forums, I think they express the spirit of Ubuntu and may serve as guidelines for our team as well (meaning team members will not be held to them, but may find them useful).

So go ahead, make me feel stupid, it is not that hard and I don't mind. But don't pick on Ubuntu community members.
