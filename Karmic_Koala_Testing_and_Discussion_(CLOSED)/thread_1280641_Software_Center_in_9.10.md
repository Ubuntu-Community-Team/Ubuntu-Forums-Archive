### Post by billdotson on 2009-10-03
I have absolutely no objections to a software centre. Being able to install a mass amount of free and open source software directly from synaptic is great (although sometimes I wish there were more repositories available to turn on my default), but with a software centre you would buy codecs to play wmv, wma, DVD (w/o AND w/ CSS), and many others. [Believe it or not, there are a lot of people that want to stay squeaky clean. Remember what the Microsoft and Novell (and now Xandros as well) deal was about: end-users being worried about getting sued by MS for "patent infringements".] 

Also, think about how useful it would be to basically have a database of the most popular and useful commercial applications for linux right there for people to get *if they need/want it*. Remember that not everyone is comfortable with something being free, they feel as if they "get what they pay for." Having the ability to buy a program you need from within the OS without even having to search the internet would simplify things for lots of people and it would *also* be advertising for the commercial software developers. Let's do a hand-count here, how many of you knew that there was a Linux version of parallels out there? What about Cyberlink PowerDVD player? Sure, many people may prefer free software, but everyone that wishes for native Linux games, photoshop or high quality 3d modelling programs like 3dsMax, etc. should be supporting developers that try to help out the Linux platform. If you never have anyone buy anything for Linux how likely is it that it will gain any significant market share? How likely is it that it will ever has driver support for even some of the latest hardware? I would say the answer to that is close to nil. 

Frankly, I think the refusal to have any commercial software on a linux install is a very narrow-minded view. Yes, open source should be promoted but if you think Adobe is going to open source photoshop you are just being delusional :). I can't say for sure, but maybe one reason applications just aren't made for Linux (commercial) is because they feel nobody would buy them. Now, I would definitely have an issue having a proprietary bit of code needed to watching HDCP Blu-ray content in system files; however, if it were contained to the blu-ray player program I guess it would be a trade-off for those that wanted that functionality.

With a software centre you take out all the internet searching, checking to see how reputable a company is and all that mess and just know that since Canonical linked it it is good. Then all you have to do is click "buy". Well, only if you couldn't find anything open source to do the same thing :) .

Even though I probably won't use it (unless I am fiddling with it for a bit), I hope Chrome OS will push the Linux kernel into at least a 5% market share. Even though it is supposed to be a sort of "WebOS" developers and normal users might start to take notice.
