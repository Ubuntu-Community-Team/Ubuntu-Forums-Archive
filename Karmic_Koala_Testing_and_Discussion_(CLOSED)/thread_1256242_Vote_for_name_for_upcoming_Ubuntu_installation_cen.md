### Post by aysiu on 2009-09-02
After looking over the options for a couple of minutes, I decided to go with *Ubuntu App Center*. I'm not thrilled with the name, but here's why I picked it...

**Ubuntu App Bazaar**
Unfortunately, a lot of people are uneducated and don't know what a bazaar is. They may too easily associate this with its homonym, *bizarre*.

**Ubuntu App Store**
Although I think this integrated package management system will allow you to purchase stuff eventually, one of the main benefits of moving from Windows to Ubuntu is the huge availability of trustworthy and easily installable cost-free applications. The word *store* brings up too much of a connotation of having to pay for something.

**Ubuntu App Market**
See *Ubuntu App Store*.

[B]Ubuntu Software Center
Ubuntu Software Bazaar
Ubuntu Software Store
Ubuntu Software Market[/B]
My main beef here is with the word *software*. It's a bit boring-sounding. It is also almost too descriptive. And many self-professed computer illiterates ("Oh, I'm so bad with computers! I know only how to check my email") don't even know what software is, actually.

Sad to say, but because of Apple's iTunes App Store and Google's Android Market, the term *app* has come to be the popular name for something installed through a kind of package management system (items installed through the Android Market are still called *apps*).

So I think *Ubuntu App Center* makes the most sense, given current sociological trends in computing.

I may not have the details *entirely* correct, but it was something like this: If we ended up using a suggested name that was distinctive enough to use as a trademark, and Canonical wanted to trademark it to protect the name from misuse, Canonical could find themselves unable to do that because they weren’t the ones who came up with the name to begin with.

In theory we could have gotten around this by running a competition, where one of the conditions of entry was “By entering you hereby give up in perpetuity any claim over the name you have suggested, and your firstborn son too”, etc etc. But by then, we really didn’t have time to run a competition like that.

So, it was a combination of a brainstorming session of the Canonical Design and Marketing teams, weeding out names that were unsuitable for various reasons, and informal user testing of a shortlist of names to see how well people understood them.

To reiterate, it’s not how we would have done it if we had had much more time, but we didn’t. Sorry.
