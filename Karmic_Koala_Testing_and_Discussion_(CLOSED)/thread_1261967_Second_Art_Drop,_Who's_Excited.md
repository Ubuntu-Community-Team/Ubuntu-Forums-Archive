Points against the "Why not use $MYFAVORITETHEME?" argument:

[list][*]Like it or not, Ubuntu, as well as being a community effort, is also Canonical's flagship "thing" (avoiding the word "product"), the main "thing" that's associated with the word Canonical, and that puts enormous inertia on its default appearance as brand image. The orange-brown face of Ubuntu, like it or not, has become a widely recognizable image - it's what stands for "Linux" on prominent websites, it's what you see as "Ubuntu" (could have been "Ibinti" or "Zbzntz" for the uninitiated; the image is what defines the brand) on "IT" magazines and tech shows. Swapping it with an arbitrary community theme for one release, especially once you've hired a team of professionals among whose goals is redesigning it, doesn't make good PR sense. This is regardless of how many high quality themes there are out there.


[*] Good design has no room for arbitrariness. It has to be aligned with organizational, user and customer goals, and there are plenty of widely established guidelines and procedures defining how that has to happen. The reasons that make your favourite theme your favourite, while they're certainly legitimate ones, since they're entirely subjective, do not have anything to do with the large scale planning and reasoning that is going to define an appearance and behavioural characteristics for a piece of software that will be used by no less than millions of people.

Using an arbitrary "good" theme is akin to buying an arbitrary "good" pre-made page design from a template vendor instead of hiring designers: it can work if your needs are modest, but your website will not be unique, and will not be thought out from the ground up with your and your users' requirements.


[*] Canonical's intentions for refreshing Ubuntu's default "look and feel" are not limited to a theme redesign, but extend to what I've gleaned from my correspondence with the Design and User Experience Team at UDS is a holistic interaction redesign, with the principles of [goal-directed design]("http://media.wiley.com/product_data/excerpt/13/07645264/0764526413.pdf") (look it up; keywords: Cooper, About Face). Slapping on a random new theme, regardless of how "good" it may be, not only does not do enough in that direction, but will undermine any holistic effort, since in being arbitrary, it's disconnected from that effort by definition.
[/list]
