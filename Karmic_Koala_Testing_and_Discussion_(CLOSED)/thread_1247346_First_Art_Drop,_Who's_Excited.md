### Post by psyke83 on 2009-08-29
Folks,

I've been following this thread, and it seems that many people have different definitions of what constitutes a "theme". Let's put aside these vague ideas and talk about something more concrete.

In the case of a GNOME-based system such as Ubuntu, here is a break down of what constitutes a theme: 

[LIST]
[*]GTK theme (buttons/widgets)
[*]Metacity decoration (window border)
[*]Icon theme
[*]Pointers
[*]Default Wallpaper
[*]GDM (login screen)
[*]Miscellaneous: usplash/xsplash, notifications, anything else
[/LIST]

With that in mind, let me offer some thoughts on the GTK aspect of the theme.

The GTK theme is limited by the widgets available within the GTK framework, and the way these widgets can be styled is handled by GTK engines. Today, the most common (and modern) engines are [Clearlooks]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ubuntu_Clearlooks.png"), [Nodoka]("https://fedorahosted.org/nodoka/wiki/Screenshots") and [Murrine]("http://www.cimitan.com/murrine/themes/gallery") (Ubuntulooks and Aurora are unmaintained, and other engines are not stable enough to be used by a distribution such as Ubuntu). There is also the Pixmap engine which allows you to paint widgets using bitmap images, but has disadvantages that would prevent heavy use in a "production" theme (heavy memory/CPU use, lack of flexibility with regards to user-defining colors).

For those that complain that Ubuntu's theme cannot compare to Vista and Windows 7, I agree in principle, but I disagree with the accuracy of that statement. In reality, it is the GTK toolkit and GTK engines that cannot compare to Vista or Windows 7. Whilst Aero began as a blatant rip-off of compiz and OS X's compositing, it most certainly takes [better advantage]("http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CYnSGTUU4P0") of compositing (see the frosted glass effect of the Start menu and select parts of applications, for example).

Take a look at The Widget Factory in the attached screenshot. The buttons and widgets you see in this application constitute (for all intents and purposes) all the standard widgets that a GTK application will use. The problem is that most ([though not all]("http://www.cimitan.com/blog/2007/12/12/gtk-rgba-transparent-widgets-with-the-murrine-engine/")) GTK applications aren't designed with transparency in mind, and custom GTK widgets tend to look quite strange (for example, Banshee's listview headers are custom widgets, and look odd due to their rounded corners which differ from all other applications). On the other hand, Gnome-Panel uses the standard button widget to represent running applications, which may seem boring when compared to Vista/7's customized taskbar widgets (in the case of 7, are vastly different in look and concept).

To cut a long story short, comparing Ubuntu to Vista/7 is apples to oranges. What you folks really mean to say is that the GTK interface can't compare to the interface of Vista/Windows 7. Keep in mind that Ubuntu is not the direct driver of development in GNOME and GTK, so it's not within our power to significantly change certain aspects of the system (without stepping on toes and forking code).

/end rant
