### Post by psyke83 on 2009-05-27
I'm definitely against this idea. With exception to the kernel and some core components, Linux's updating mechanism is very resilient and a reboot is almost never required.

Usually the most you will need to do is restart an application (such as Firefox) or to log out and back into your session (if any GNOME or X components are updated).

Installing updates on shutdown will circumvent this distinct advantage over Windows, and will likely frustrate users who expect a computer to **shut down** when they hit the shutdown button. I see nothing but disadvantages from this proposal.

### Post by psyke83 on 2009-05-28
> **davideotape said:**
> Question: Would you rather have lots of broken packages, or a 30s longer shutdown. I don't however know what happens when you try to shutdown whilst sudo apt-get upgrade is working. Anyone know?

Data-loss during activity with the package management system can be fatal. While a broken package is easily fixed (i.e. by simply removing it and reinstalling), a corrupted dpkg database will necessitate a complete reinstallation. The latter is quite possible if a system freeze or sudden power loss occurs, and a fsck won't always fix it. 

The risk of this happening is elevated if this proposal is implemented, since I can imagine some users holding down the power button to force a shutdown - perhaps they already turned off their monitor and assumed that the system crashed without turning the monitor back on to verify.

Corruption of the dpkg database occurred to me a few times on my older laptop (because the r128 video driver was often causing system freezes).
