Evening all

As a long term Kubuntu user, I'm trying Ubuntu Kamaric Alpha 3 as an alternative. I just enjoy the bleeding cutting edge! And yes, all is backed up twice! (Just love DropBox).

First impressions of the Gnome environment is that I like it, once I'd got rid of the hideous default colours. I suspect it's faster than KDE but probably not by a lot. One of the things I do like is the ability to mount network shares directly in Nautilus. Why oh why can't this be done in Dolphin?

Ubuntu also correctly identified my Nokia mobile and the networking wizard took me smoothly through to an internet connection. In KDE I have to use wvdial to achive a connection.

I do have some issues, and in fairness I expect them in a pre-release. VLC won't play .avi or .mpg files. They do play in Movie Player. It just opens and closes. I am getting to like Cairo Dock but if I try to run it with "Normal" effects enabled, after a couple of minutes I get the top left hand corner of the screen duplicated in a "window" at the bottom of the screen and it won't go away! If I run the dock without compositing enabled, all is well apart from the ugly black background that surrounds the dock. I also get the odd random freezes and lock ups, but what the hey!

All the issues I've had have been bug reported BTW.

All in all I'm impressed and consider the next release to be a worthy successor.

### Post by seeker5528 on 2009-08-03
> **Crunchy the Headcrab said:**
> Here's my dillema:  Kde does seem more advanced and future-loking but more apps seem to be going gnome.  I want to be where the native programs are.

Your choice of desktop should not dictate your choice of apps. Seek out the apps that work the best for what you want to do, don't worry so much about the toolkit they use. If a QT/KDE app and GTK/Gnome app are roughly equal, then I can see then I can see the toolkit being more of a factor in the decision. 

The main thrust of Gnome 3 was to say 'OK, this commitment to backward compatibility has worked well for us, but it's getting hard to move forward without getting rid of some dead weight'. This is a good thing and in line with the 'evolution not revolution' track the Gnome guys have been on.

This Gnome Shell thing on the other hand is a little out of left field. Seems like change for the sake of change, with minimal if any advancement in usability.

If you look at what the KDE guys did with KDE 4 it was more about creating a base platform to build on for the future, and less about drastic UI shifts. It was a huge change, but you still have a panel that is very similar to the old panel, with plasmoids that perform the functions of the old panel applets and a tray area that applications can use for some running component or notification. You still have the classic menu as an option and things are still logically grouped in the cotrol center, which looks like it will get a classic option as well. 

To me, what you can and can't do in the desktop area is the most drastic change, I never liked having a bunch of icons on my desktop, so I don't miss anything there. Having plasmoids that can run as part of your desktop is cool, but I have a tendency not to run stuff just because it's cool, and have not figured out how to do anything I find all that useful if I can't either organize some plasmoids along one edge and choose not to have them covered when I maximize an app or mouse to an edge or hot corner to have the plasmoids show above the apps or return to the background.   

Later, Seeker
