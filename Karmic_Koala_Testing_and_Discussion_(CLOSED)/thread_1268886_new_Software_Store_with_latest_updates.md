### Post by mpt on 2009-09-19
> **zekopeko said:**
> Will we get the shiny pathbar?

I don&#8217;t know. Michael Vogt was [working on it as recently as three days ago]("https://code.launchpad.net/~mvo/software-store/pathbar-integration") (and [Matthew McGowan appears to be working on it right now]("https://code.launchpad.net/~mmcg069/software-store/pathbar")), but when I asked Michael about it on Friday, he said it wasn&#8217;t working at all yet. :P And there are [many other bugs to fix]("https://bugs.launchpad.net/ubuntu/+source/software-store").

> **cicoandcico said:**
> i'd just like to point out an usability issue in the current version: **the icons in the main view (Departments) should have a mouseover effect**, otherwise the user is not able to understand that they are clickable.

I have seen no evidence that this is true. There are plenty of icon-only buttons on the Web that have exactly the same mouseover effect as the department icons, *i.e.* changing only the cursor. If we do discover that they need a greater affordance, my preference would be one that doesn&#8217;t involve mouseover effects, because mouseover effects are distracting in general.

> **Bou said:**
> Actually, the only thing I did was take the widgets from a screenshot of the store and paste them to a gnome-app-install screenshot, which yes, seem to have some extra padding. But some things seems to be different too, e.g. the store seems to have a frame around the app list which looks terrible.

Yes, I [reported that frame problem]("http://launchpad.net/bugs/426741") a couple of weeks ago. Apparently it&#8217;s a bug in WebKit-GTK.

> *Anyway, don't you agree that it looks cleaner in the mockup?*

Maybe it&#8217;s just a personal mental block of mine, but I have never understood precisely what someone means when they refer to an interface as &#8220;clean&#8221; or &#8220;cleaner&#8221;. As far as I can tell, it typically means some combination of fewer interface elements, more alignment or symmetry, or a greater proportion of space devoted to information rather than interface. But your mockup doesn&#8217;t seem to improve on any of those. It has the same number of interface elements. It has no increase in symmetry. And it reduces the proportion of space devoted to information. It comes closest to increasing alignment, in that the edges of the left and main panes now line up with the edges of the elements inside the location bar. ### Post by mpt on 2009-09-25
> **ghindo said:**
> Interesting.  Not that I necessarily disagree with that, but could you explain why that design decision was made?  It seems like most application close out with Ctrl + Q - why won't Software Store?

> **Kazade said:**
> EDIT: In fact, it's part of the GNOME HIG guidelines (see here under "Closing Operations" [http://developer.gnome.org/projects/gup/hig/1.0/menus.html#the-file-menu](http://developer.gnome.org/projects/gup/hig/1.0/menus.html#the-file-menu))

This is an ongoing problem in Gnome, which its Interface Guidelines have not solved: there are several different ways of closing a window that has menus. Some have &#8220;Quit&#8221; but not &#8220;Close&#8221;, some have &#8220;Close&#8221; but not &#8220;Quit&#8221;, some have both, and some have neither.

My approach is that people should not have to care whether an application is running or not, and therefore they should not be exposed to the idea of &#8220;Quit&#8221;. For example, I may have a video playing, Facebook open, background music playing, and Gmail open. That Facebook and Gmail happen to be operated by the same program &#8212; a Web browser &#8212; is irrelevant to my activities, and therefore it is inappropriate for the browser to have a &#8220;Quit&#8221; command that closes two unrelated tasks. And that the video is being played by the same program that is playing the background music &#8212; Totem &#8212; is irrelevant to my activities, and therefore it is inappropriate for Totem to have a &#8220;Quit&#8221; command that closes two unrelated tasks.

I intend to revise the Gnome interface guidelines to reflect this. But a necessary first step is demonstrating that an application can live happily without a &#8220;Quit&#8221; item. Epiphany is doing this already, and the Store will too.
