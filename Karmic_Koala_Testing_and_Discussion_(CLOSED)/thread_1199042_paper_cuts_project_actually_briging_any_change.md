### Post by 23meg on 2009-06-28
It seems the mere existence of an effort like this has created some positive buzz and pushed people who wouldn't otherwise be interested in working on bugs like this into working on them. That's one gain.

An important aspect of this project is that it's the first coordinated project where the newly formed Design and User Experience team at Canonical is working together. How that's relevant is: if they end up, say, fixing only 50 bugs out of 100, but having gained some valuable insights on how to get things done in coordination and along with the community, that's still a big gain. 

It has also illustrated how a strictly results-oriented approach with a limited time frame and work volume can work if people actually get organized around such a common goal. There's nothing stopping anyone from starting their own "Death by 30 Other Paper Cuts" or "Let's see what we can do to fix our top 20 pet bugs" project, and if some people get inspired by this effort to get going on such projects, that's going to be another gain.

The actual outcome of this project is likely to be greater than the sum of its parts; greater than the total effect of 100 minor bugs being fixed.

The whole thing sorta reminds me of the GNOME love bugs, these are also simple problems which could serve as nice entry points for eager developers to cut their teeth on. Abide papercuts have better PR and it's focused more cross desktop on the experience as a whole than specific applications.

I think the Papercuts idea would be useful as a Google Summer of Code type project. Each cycle the users and developers identify 100 small bugs or little improvements to target. The reward for identifying a cut could be a t-shirt* or a small donation to a charity in your name, the same for providing the fix.

It would be a nice way of getting a certain segment of users to contribute and if we pipe the improvements to upstream. It would also increase the chances of gaining new people to submit those vital small fixes.
