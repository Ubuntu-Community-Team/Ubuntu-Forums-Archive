### Post by IYY on 2006-10-27
> What do other students find useful for taking notes, collaborating in groups, keeping compatibility with systems affected by bug #1

If the computer is connected to the internet, I always found that using web apps to write notes is much better when working in groups. For example, Google Docs (the editor formally known as Writely) is a cool little tool for this purpose. Don't forget that Open Office does open Word documents.

However, if you are only sending an output to your peers as opposed to a document they might want to edit, or are just submitting a report or assignment, I always suggest to learn LaTeX. It's a bit tricker to learn than standard word processing, but in the end it's easier to create professional looking documents without the need to worry about formatting. This is something that always impresses professors.

Here is an example book I started writing in LaTeX: [www.cs.yorku.ca/~cs251010/aboutLinux.tex](www.cs.yorku.ca/~cs251010/aboutLinux.tex)

### Post by seijuro on 2006-10-27
First off I just want to say the head of your school's CS department should be tied to a tree and burned alive, VB is a terrible place to break students into programming because it strongly promotes bad programming habits that cause problems when moving on to other languages. I am working on a BS in Comp Sci through Univ. Pittsburgh and even CS101 starts with basic little hello world programming using C++ on a linux shell. That being said for me the easiest way to take notes was the old fashioned way with a pen and a notebook. However I typed out all of my homework especially sample programs. I believe aMSN has a whiteboard now for collaboration but don't quote me on that as far as collaboration goes it really depends on what the needs of the group are do you need to look each other in the eye or would text and looking at the same file via a shell suffice. As for power I would say tweak your power config so your monitor and disk drives shut off when an acceptable for you amount of idle time has elapsed. Also suspend to memory and hibernate are good batt savers if you can get suspend to memory to work with lid close you can just shut your laptop any time you're not going to be using it for more than a few minutes. It used to work out of the box on my dell with breezy but I had to tweak it for dapper.

Anyways, welcome to the ubuntu community and I hope you enjoy your stay.
