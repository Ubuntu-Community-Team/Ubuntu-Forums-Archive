### Post by bsussman on 2006-10-21
6.06 is a special release - LTS meaning long term support.  This means that certain bug fixes and enhancements will be done for longer than usual.    

6.10 is not an LTS release.  And it presents some new things that, while very good, might take longer than 6.06 to stabilize.  

This means that staying on 6.06 is a practical decision.  But sooner or later you will find a feature that doesn't work and won't be backported in 6.06 and does work in 6.10 - then you will be faced with 'the decision'.  

Linux is a very dynamic OS/App codebase - it is changing all the time.  Learning to live with this is very important to your happiness with it as an acceptable computing platform.  Without doubt (IMO) it is totally worth it.  

The LTS concept is something that seems to set ubuntu apart - it should allow conservative folks (corporations, others with a total stability need) more justification to implement this superior set of systems.

It sounds like you are still in the commercial software mentality of having the need to upgrade every time a new version of some software comes out.  This has been a marketing gimmick for many years in the commercial side.

It doesn't have to be so.  If your hardware and software are meeting your needs and your system does everything that you want from it, then why upgrade?

6.06 is going to be around for many years, from what I understand, due to its long term support (LTS).

On the other hand, unlike commercial software, you can always try out the upgrade for free, see if it does things better for you, and if not, then you can revert back without any loss, save a little time.  The LiveCD makes that kind of experiment risk and cost free.
