### Post by H3retic on 2007-11-13
When engaging in P2P, 3rd parties like the MPAA (motion picture association of America) and anti-p2p organisations track the IP adresses of those transferring files.  The risks are the same from windows or linux, because the downloads take place on the same network.

You can however, block malicious "peeping toms" by installing peerguardian (Windows) or Moblock (Linux).

### Post by kornation on 2007-11-13
Both P2P and Torrents needs people to connect to each other to work, doing this reveals your IP address to the person you are connecting to.

What the company's do is try to download a file from P2P, and check to see what the people they are downloading from have 'available' to be downloaded by other users (and log people who try to download from them).

With torrents, they can just log the IP's of people who connects to them.

The difference with Torrents and P2P in this respect is they can see what else you have with P2P and sue for EVERYTHING you have available for download, but with torrents they can only sue for the one file (the swarm they joined) pending a writ to get access to your pc, and this is harder to get a writ for when its only one file.

but with torrents - the person who creates the torrents and drop the torrent file onto a file server tends to leave there ip address behind on the server.

The torrent file itself IS NOT ILLEGAL, what it connects to is illegal, unfortunatly American law has ignored this point and sued/taken down servers holding the torrent hash files.

Coming to around 9 years ago, during the Napster years, i received a letter to my front door from Sony (i think) for downloading a Radiohead album, saying they were going to sue me and everyone that downloaded this album, it was one of the first attempts by a record company to attack P2P people and they made the mistake of trying to sue everyone who downloaded the album at the same time (you try suing 10,000 people at once and sorting through 10,000 lawyers.). Luckily they didn't follow up, but as we had a DJ license for music (allowing us to source our music from anywhere) at the time we would of just counter-sued for waste of time.

What record company's don't realise is torrents and P2P use opens peoples eyes up to more music, lets then try before they buy, A recent study in Canada showed that P2P users bought MORE albums per capita then non P2P users, and each successful lawsuit that gets into the news opens more peoples eyes to what P2P and Torrents can do - and inversly ends up with MORE users downloading and uploading.

Its also the fact that the RIAA hold a monopoly over music usage and WILL SUE YOU for having a radio/tv/internet station of pure OPEN SOURCE music that shows how stupid the current copyright law is.

Its never good to sue your own customers.
