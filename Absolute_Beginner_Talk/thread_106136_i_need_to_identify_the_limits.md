### Post by matthew on 2005-12-19
I'm extremely partial to anything by O'Reilly press, but there is some other good stuff out there as well. My personal faves in recommended reading order.

Running Linux (O'Reilly)
Linux Cookbook (O'Reilly)
Linux in a Nutshell (O'Reilly)
Learning the Bash Shell (O'Reilly)
Linux Power Tools (Sybex)
Unix Power Tools (O'Reilly)

I've read 7-8 more, but these are the ones I found most enlightening...I think Unix Power Tools is probably the best of the whole lot, but it is better to have a bit of foundation before trying to tackle it...plus, I still don't understand all of it. :)

EDIT: I read 23meg's comments and I would have to agree...these recommendations will serve to give a good foundation, not define boundaries within which you are stuck.  Really, I think the *nix OSes are by far the most versatile and amazing of any--limited only by the hardware available and the imagination of the user/programmer.

### Post by Lin-X on 2005-12-20
When I first started Linux, my documentation was three books that were way beyond me: Peter Norton's Guide to Linux, Running Linux, and The Desktop Linux Guide. It was like reading Chinese. Online the situation was even worse! Tons of information, not sorted out or kept up-to-date, no way of knowing if it was "authentic" or not. Y_E_E_E_K! In my frustration, I nearly threw away some of the best books because I couldn't understand them.
     Here is some very well meant advice: (1) choose just one thing about Linux, one thing you want to do --- say, file management --- and then read everything about it.
Try to actually do as much of what you read as posssible. When you think you understand it fairly well, go on to another thing. (2) I know this sounds rediculous, but keep reading things about Linux, even things you don't really understand; one day when you're sitting at your computer, a big chunk of stuff you've been reading that didn't make sense will come clear. You will suddenly understand (and feel great
about it too!)
     As for limits in Linux, I doubt there are any. You can do anything with this os because of the open license, therefor, you can redesign and recompile this into anything you want it to be. So have fun here in Oz; you're not it Kansas anymore.

### Post by matthew on 2005-12-20
[quote=mdsmedia]I have old copies of Running Linux, Linux Unleashed and Linux Complete. Does the fact that they are old (ie 3-5 years) make them irrelevant? 

I have them on my main bookcase in my lounge so EVERYONE will see them and ask about them. But I haven't taken them off the shelf since I installed Ubuntu (after maybe 3 years away from OSS)[/quote]Most of the info in these books is going to be current, especially the command line usage guides. Things that are likely to have changed are things like xserver details (video interface...there is a big switch underway from the older Xfree86 to the newer Xorg, Ubuntu has already made the change, many others are still in process) and newer hardware needs...wireless internet, etc.

Look through them, know that some things may be out of date, but the more general stuff is likely to still apply quite well...I've found 15 year old Unix books that have been helpful.
