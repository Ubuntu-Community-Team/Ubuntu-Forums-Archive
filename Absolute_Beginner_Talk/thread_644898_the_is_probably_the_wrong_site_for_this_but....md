### Post by jfinkels on 2007-12-19
A desktop may be cheaper than a laptop. As for hardware, the basic stuff you need for a computer to work:

Motherboard (the circuit board that connects all the different parts)
Processor (Intel or AMD)
RAM (Memory) (make sure it's compatible with the motherboard)
Hard disk drive (SATA is faster than PATA (aka ATA / IDE), make sure your motherboard supports it)
DVD/CD ROM/RW drive
Video card (if not onboard [i.e. built-in to the motherboard]) (ATI or nVidia...for Linux go with nVidia)
Network card (wired or wireless or both, if not built-in to the motherboard) (be sure to search around for info on which wireless cards are well-supported!)

And that's basically it. Go to [http://www.newegg.com](http://www.newegg.com) for researching components and prices. Here's a good site with lots of info: [http://computer.howstuffworks.com/build-a-computer.htm](http://computer.howstuffworks.com/build-a-computer.htm)

### Post by nickoli_02 on 2007-12-19
AMD has had some troubles lately. They've always been the underdog against Intel and Intel's new dual core processors are really hurting them. Apparently AMD's answer to the core duo's has some process/design flaw, and the workaround is really killing the performance. Anyway, long story short, go for an intel  processor. 

If you want to be able to play any games on the new computer then you'll need a decent video card. Nvidia does have better open-source support, but I have a laptop with an ATI card and I've had no troubles getting it set up. Just follow the how-to's. Also, ATI is being forced by AMD (who now owns ATI) to support the open source community more. 

The difference between a 32-bit processor and 64-bit processor is the length of the internal registers. If a processor's integer registers are 32 bits wide, it can reference a maximum of 2^32 address locations, which is a limit of 4GB of RAM. In comparison, a 64-bit processor can reference a maximum of 2^64 address locations, or up to 17,179,869,184 gigabytes of RAM. Of course, at this point in time there are other physical restrictions that make it impossible to install this much RAM :( In terms of actual speed between the two processors, you won't notice a difference unless you're using an OS that's specifically designed to take advantage of the 64-bit architecture.

### Post by Presto123 on 2007-12-20
Yeah...As much as I love my AMD's, from what I'm hearing on this site, I would stick with Intel. You can basically find Intel processors for cheaper and they use the DDR2, which, surprisingly, is less expensive than the DDR.

This AMD package I got, though, is a freak system having DDR2. So, I paid more in the long run, LOL.

Some pointers: 

Make sure that the Socket number matches the Motherboard you buy. I suggest going with any of the packaged Motherboard/CPU combos they have. 

Be sure that the combo contains a CPU fan. (Mine did, fortunately)

Check that the motherboard has enough places to connect what you want. I didn't pay attention here on mine and was short 1 connection. (FYI: the motherboard may seem to have two IDE, but we must remember to check to make sure that second one isn't a plug for your floppy drive.)
With my hardware, I had IDE connections for everything: Hard Drives & CD/DVD Burner.

If you are planning on using a Video card you already have, make sure its sockets are the same as the motherboard you want all the way down to the speeds.

Check to make sure that you have the right RAM (DDR or DDR2) type and see if it can hold as much as you want or will want in the future (Mine is limited to 2 gb, but that is fine for now). They will be listed on the Specifications of the motherboard. As DDR2 is cheap right now, I would suggest going with 2 gigs of it, just so you can have a good computer.

Building a computer isn't as hard as it seems, really. Just read the manual if you get one. I completely built my own for the first time at about the same time I got Linux. JUST WATCH YOUR PLUGS.

After I followed MOST of my own advice here, I rebuilt a really good and up-to-date computer (IMO) for under $200.
