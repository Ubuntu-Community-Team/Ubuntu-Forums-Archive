### Post by Pragmatist on 2006-03-20
> Sorry Ubuntu, **you've failed**...but in all the various distros the installation seems too complicated even for a **reasonably techie person **such as myself (I run several websites and do *basic* design and support).

As a techie I'm sure you've regularly experienced being wrong.  It is one of the most fundamental learning experiences with computers.  The odds are enormous that if something is going wrong it is your fault not the computer's. 

 I have taken technology courses in vocational schools, colleges, and ivy league graduate schools.  The most telling feature of how good somebody is at computers is not how many details they know--the breadth of their knowledge.  It is how much they know about solving problems--the depth of their knowledge.  They don't teach programming languages in graduate school.  They teach you how to make programming languages.  General knowledge.  Core knowledge that can be applied to learning specific details, languages, OSes, etc...

This is why you can know more than Linus Travolds or Bill Gates about some specific computer detail.  What they have that us lesser mortals do not, is a very profound knowledge of how computers work and **problem solving **in general

The main issue that is missed here is simple:  There are **beginners** in here every day raving about how easy it was to install Ubuntu.  However, it is perhaps *because* they are beginners that they found it easy.  The same way that a child can learn languages better than adults.  They are less set in their ways and have less bad habits to undo.  A **techie** such as yourself might have a harder time *because* of what details they know.  A typical example of this is when people who are used to Windows come here complaining that Linux doesn't work exactly like Windows.  Of course it doesn't, it wasn't meant to.  It is a *different *operating system.

> Nothing I could do would move it on and I got **annoyed**. It took several hours of trying before I gave up.

If you've ever coded HTML and/or simple scripts, you'd know how "annoying" it can be to find that you left out a / or a > or a ' or " or put in an extra space or left one out or were off by 1 in a loop, etc...etc...  It is these experiences that humble us into realizing that it is pointless to attach blame for computer problems.  The most productive thing you can do is take a break and then think things through slowly and methodically.

> I think the thing holding back mass acceptance of Linux is the ease with which it can't be installed.

Maybe.  But what does that have to do with your experience.  We don't know from your experience that it IS hard or it IS easy.  We just know how you feel about it from a very limited experience.

The theme here is simple.  In psychology it is known as "cognitive dissonance".  A simple example is somebody who smokes.  He has two facts that create dissonance for him.  Fact 1 = smoking kills  Fact 2 = I smoke.  We don't like dissonance so we try to resolve it.  Such as, "well, I can get killed crossing the street"  Nevermind the obvious lack of logic (i.e. you have to cross streets, you don't have to smoke), it still resolves the dissonance.  In your case:

Fact: I couldn't get something technological to work. 
Fact: I'm a technological guy.

Resolution: Something must be wrong with the technology.

This is obviously more palatable than "something is wrong with the technological guy".  However, the best way of looking at it is, "The techological guy **made a mistake**"  No tragedy.  Par for the course in computers.

My advice is that you will excel and improve the most over time, especially if you work in computers, if you put aside ego and work at solving the problem at hand.

In fact, average users (I work with a lot of them) don't know much of anything. They can turn on the computer (if it's plugged in) and can use IE and MS Word (and that's it as most don't know Excel or anything else). Many have trouble burning a CD. For the most part, the people who try Linux and who are on this forum are not average users.

The first Windows PC I owned was a Toshiba laptop that came with 3.1 and a free update to 95. When I got my update CD it took me several attempts to get it installed. I learned a lot about windows and PCs in general because of that (I was a mac person prior) and I&#8217;ve continued to expand my knowledge as my interests change and grow. It is undergoing rapid development, however, and could very shortly start to rival windows.
