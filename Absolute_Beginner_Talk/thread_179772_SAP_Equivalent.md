### Post by adamkane on 2006-05-20
I don't use SAP either. I've used Infor TakeStock, but it's similar.

If a company has any problems whatever keeping track of orders or inventory or billing, then SAP or something similar is an absolute requirement. SAP keeps inventory, orders, billing, and even shipping records all in the same central database. That's what's important. 

Want a report to know what's making money and what isn't? No problem. It's all right there. SAP is actually better for smaller businesses, because you don't have branch offices with their own branch databases scattered everywhere.

If you ever want to do something new or different, you just find a half-competent database programmer (in concert with your half-competent systems manager) to integrate a new module into the main database. Everything is cool beans.

Nowadays, the savings that SAP would provide is usually what's needed to keep a mid-size company from going out of business, and also greatly improves the ability of a startup to out-class the competition assuming they have some experience with the whole mess.

It won't be long before Brazil/China/India-based business are using open-sourced SAP equivalents. I don' t mind this new development, but this is causing great heartburn in the corporate boardrooms of USA/Europe/Japan.

If someone was to start a new business, they should use osCommerce with all its shipping/payment modules for their ecommerce solution, SugarCRM for customer management, the osCommerce ck-erp plugin for double-entry accounting, and of course OpenOffice and Ubuntu.
