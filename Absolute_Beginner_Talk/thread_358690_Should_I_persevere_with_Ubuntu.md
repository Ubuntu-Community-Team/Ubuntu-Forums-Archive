### Post by katiad on 2007-02-11
Hi! 

It sounds to me as if the bulk of your internet problems are likely stemming from not configuring your modem/router properly. If you have an ADSL connection, which I believe you claimed to have, you must put in several IP addresses (your IP address, probably, a gateway IP address, a couple DNS Server addresses...) into the network configuration settings in order to connect to the internet. This is something that also must be done in Windows - it is not Ubuntu specific. If you know what these numbers are, and have done this, forgive me. If not, you're going to need to look up the numbers from Windows in your network connections settings and write them down, and then put them into Ubuntu. I can't help you with specifics here - currently not at my Linux box and don't want to give you wrong advice. Someone else should be able to do more specific troubleshooting.

You seem to be frustrated with your lack of knowledge on what is going on, but you must realize that this is NOT Ubuntu's fault. When you try to connect to a website through firefox, and it simply says that things have timed out or could not get a response... that is ALL that your browser knows. It is not a magical detect-the-exact-root-of-the-problem utility. No operating system - Windows or Ubuntu - has a feature that just automatically does all the troubleshooting for you. No web browser can determine whether the website you're trying to connect is having problems, whether your ISP is having problems, or whether your computer is having the problem. It can't determine if you've left a cable unplugged, or just input some numbers wrong. It just knows that it cannot reach its destination. This is not Ubuntu-specific. The exact same thing will happen in Windows. 

Why? Because computers do not have /brains/. They are not built to be self-diagnosing, self-fixing machines. When something is wrong, it can only tell you what it knows, and it's up to you to figure it out - you who can determine if something is unplugged. There are far, FAR too many different computer configurations, with different hardware, software, etc. to be able to tell you exactly what the problem is. Things that would be a serious problem for one user is exactly how another user wants it. It can't determine what /your/ problem is - especially when there could be multiple causes for the problem.

For example - I don't have an internal modem. But someone who uses dial-up internet needs one, and would find that to be a very serious problem. Should the system complain every time it boots up that there is no internal modem, just in case? Or perhaps just when a website can't be accessed due to a time-out (which, by the way, happens because no response is recieved from the server - and can happen for a multitude of reasons - server down, ISP down, DNS problems, router misconfiguration, unplugged cable, no modem...) No. Of course not. That would be annoying to anyone who doesn't have/need one. It can only tell you the basic gist of what is happening/not happening, and it's up to you to figure out what the real problem is.

Even as just a user who really doesn't want to know what's going on under the bonnet, you can't expect any operating system to do all the work for you. Troubleshooting problems takes research. Sometimes lots of it. And of course, asking questions. This is true for ANY operating system. You may be more comfortable in Windows, and only you can make the decision of whether taking a new route (Ubuntu) is something you're willing to do. 

When using Windows, there is often a lot of support desks you can call to get help whenever something goes wrong, and when you sign up for DSL service, usually they will do the installation and setup for you. In Ubuntu, you will have to do this yourself, and more, you'll probably have to look up how. We have a great forum here, with LOADS of extremely knowledgable and helpful people, a ton of great how-tos, and the operating system is top-notch.

But really, it's not for everyone. And only you can decide if putting in some extra time is worth it to you. I hope you'll still give it a chance! Once it's configured (which isn't as scary as it sounds most of the time) and you've learned the basics, there really isn't much more maintenence to do to keep it running without a hitch!

### Post by Tomosaur on 2007-02-11
DNS servers are the first 'port of call' when you try to get to a website. If your DNS list is missing or incomplete, then you will have severe difficulties accessing any websites at all. Your best bet for now is to follow the instructions here:
[How to use the OpenDNS servers with Ubuntu](http://www.opendns.com/start/ubuntu.php). This should (hopefully) sort out your networking problems. Unix systems are designed with networking in mind, and although Linux is not unix, it does follow the same principles. If you don't have access to the net with Linux, your life is going to be pretty difficult.

Hopefully with your net working, you can diagnose and fix any other problems you may be having. You do need to understand though, that it is quite difficult for a computer to 'fix itself' when something goes wrong. It's true that a computer can tell **when** something is wrong, and try a different procedure if the first fails, but the sheer amount of things which could be causing problems just makes it impossible for the computer to fix something itself.

Hope you don't throw in the towel just yet :)

### Post by undertakingyou on 2007-02-11
On closer look my post is 20 minutes late.  Sorry . . . 


I hope you don't give up on ubuntu.  And more particularly I hope that you don't give up on linux as a great alternative to windows.  Most DSL modems act as a DHCP where they supply the IP address and DNS addresses to your computer.  What a lot of these modems do is send themselves as the primary DNS record and ubuntu does not react well to that.  If you go into your SYSTEM=>ADMINISTRATION=>NETWORKING.  Leave the wired connection checked and go to properties.  You may see that a box is checked that says "Assigned by DHCP".  Uncheck that and put in the information yourself.  Use your laptop running XP to give you the information by going into control panel, and to network connections.  Double click on the icon and got to properties.  It should give you an IP address, subnet mask, and default gateway.  Do not use the same IP address as your laptop!  (i.e. if your laptop IP is 192.168.0.2 use 192.168.0.5 on the ubuntu machine.)  After the information is put in hit OK on the ubuntu machine and go to the DNS tab.  You should remove the entry there (normally there is only one) and put in either the DNS addresses of your isp or use opendns and put in the following addresses: 208.67.222.222 and 208.67.220.220.  And then hit ok to get out of the networking dialog box.  Try your internet now.  It may work, or I am way off :)

I hope this helps.  I had a lot of network problems when I first started with ubuntu.  But now I just love it.  And doing this won't hurt your laptop at all. 

Like I said I hope this helps. :KS
