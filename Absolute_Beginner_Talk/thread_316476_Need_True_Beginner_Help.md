### Post by ncappel1 on 2006-12-10
If you can't figure out what to do, it may be that all the information you're looking at is not really pertininent to what you want to do. It's hard for me, as a beginner to retain any info by reading posts and websites explaining how to shrink partitions, or solve server issues, for example, I don't have to deal with any of that, so I can't follow the thread of the argument. My advice to you is to find something you want to do, it could be, "I need to rip and burn a cd." then find some literature from any source and see what you can follow. Then when you're done, do it again following directions from another source, it will undoubidly be different, maybe even very different, and that's good, learning different approaches to how to do things.

In the very basics of basics, Ubuntu is not too different than OSx and windows (which I assume everyone knows how to operate at least a little). a lot of the operations you'll perform day to day, and especially as a beginner are point and click. (with of course some copy and paste into the command line)
;)

enjoy, there is no definitive beginners handbook, maybe you could write down and document the things you do, the things which are and aren't helpful as you go on for others who are in the same position as you!

### Post by OldDogNewTricks on 2006-12-12
I've read several books on beginning Linux, but the best of them all, in my opinion, is "Ubuntu Linux" by Keir Thomas.  He helps you, step-by-step, get a successful install, complete with extensive troubleshooting helps if you need them.  Then he progresses gradually through getting your system up and running, optimized, and finally utilized to do real work.

I've read it through twice, and find I go back to it frequently.  Some editor added a subtitle: "from Novice to Professional."  Ha!  This is a book for novices.  You will not be a professional after you read it, but you will have a good working knowledge of how to install and successfully use Ubuntu Linux.

I rate this book a 10 out of 10.  I like Ubuntu Linux for non-Geeks too, but Ubuntu Linux has a slight edge over it, in my opinion.

I enjoy searching the WEB, but there is nothing quite like a well laid out book to bring all the pieces together in a concise, logical manner. This book did it for me.
