### Post by psyne on 2007-02-01
As someone who has been in the IT industry for a couple of years and has several certifications under his belt I will try to give my perspective. 

Certifications are really only useful for two things first for companies to develop a skills catalog and market dependence on their software. Second for perspective job seekers to prove credibility to claims of skill or knowledge. The certification process will teach you the standard, or suggested way to do things but you won't come out knowing everything. 

If you are comfortable at your company and are not seeking to leave anytime soon I would suggest continuing on the self taught track. Then when you decide to make a career transition I would suggest taking a skills inventory and look at the market and see what niche you want to fill. If you want to be a Linux admin then at that time go out and get an industry certification Linux+ is equivalent to an A+ cert or you can get a specific linux flavor cert like Redhat or get a general LPI linux professsional certifcation at that time so that it is fresh and relevant to the current job market. 

There is nothing worse then getting a resume from an MCSE to find out he or she is a NT4 MCSE, or they are a 2001 A+ tech. Technology changes and the certifications grow stale. The knowledge you gain from the self taught track will be invaluable to you in your future growth and also your pursuits of certifications. If you have the funds for training then try to get long term training tools books, cbts, and on ground courses. I would advise you that if you are trying to learn then stay away from boot-camps, they are great for certification purpose but for longer term knowledge learning resources will develop a foundation and pay off in the long run.
