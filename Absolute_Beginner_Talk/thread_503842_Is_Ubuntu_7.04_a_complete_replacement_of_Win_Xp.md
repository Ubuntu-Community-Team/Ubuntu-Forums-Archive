### Post by odinb on 2007-07-18
I did just that, replaced Win XP on my main laptop with Kubuntu. Kubuntu runs the KDE-desktop, which is in my opinion more recognizable than GNOME (used by Ubuntu) for old Windows users.

For your questions:
1: Openoffice comes on the DVD-install of Kubuntu, or you can install with Adept Packet manager.
2: Done with Konqueror, or with an install of Firefox. It is even possible to install IE if needed. DSL should not be an issue, especially not if your Router or DSL-modem does the PPPoE login.
3: No problem.
4: All apps you want to install is managed by Adept packet manager. Search it and click install. Names of apps can be found here in the forums, or here: [http://www.linuxappfinder.com/](http://www.linuxappfinder.com/) [http://www.linux.org/apps/](http://www.linux.org/apps/)
Adept is very powerful, it can even update your OS and all your Apps in a matter of minutes with 5 clicks or so. Try that in Windows!!

There are only 2 things that I see would cause problems in kubuntu (or any Linux distribution that I know of).
1: Outlook (Evolution on Linux), some features like voting in emails does not work (they do not work under OS X in the MS- Office-package either, only on Windows with MS Office).
2: Directx 10, but this is for gamers only, and a project is actually looking into this.

I would actually say that Kubuntu is about as easy to learn as Vista, with the benefit that you still feel you are in control with Kubuntu. In vista I felt totally robbed of control and privilegies!

Initial setup-issues with Video-drivers and WLAN-drivers are unfortunately still common in Linux-distributions, but are in most cases fixed by searching these forums and following the guides.

Good luck, and welcome over from the "dark side"!

Hi!

Good questions to be asking.

Can you completely remove XP?  Yes, but I wouldn't recommend it just yet.  As another suggested, go the dual-boot route first.  This way you have the best of both until you're satisfied that you only need one.  And it may very well be that you'll want both.  Your call.

Yes, you can run from the Live CD.  Assuming that you don't have any funky hardware that the Live CD can't deal with you should be good to go.  And the Live CD will answer your last question about your modem... you should be able to immediately connect to the internet with the Live CD.  (no configuring required - or at least in my experience).

So just pop the Live CD in and reboot.  Nothing will be installed on your system until you tell it to install.  It's a very safe test.  Do note that running from the Live CD is much slower than from an actual install.  I wouldn't want you to think it's performance is indicative of what you'll experience after a full install. :)

Should you decide to take the plunge and install in a dual-boot configuration, read up on the process first.  It's pretty easy, but you should be familiar with the subject before you forge ahead.  And back up your critical Windows data!!! Dual-booting will probably require you to re-partition your hard drive.  It's generally a safe operation, but you want to have backups just in case.  It's also recommended to defrag your Windows partitions (several times) prior to resizing them.

Good Luck!  Post questions as the need arises.

### Post by bigboy_pdb on 2007-07-18
I recommend what everyone else said about trying the Live CD first and then setting up a dual boot system. From what you're saying it sounds as though you might be a software developer. If this is the case and you're developing software for Windows, I'd recommend keeping your system a dual boot system to test and compile your code on.

1>Word Processing including presentation.
Open Office works quite well and it can read word documents. However, when I went to open my resumes and cover letters (that were word documents) which had been typed to the end of the page, I found that when they were opened in Open Office, some of the text had overflowed onto the next page. If your documents don't appear exactly as you need them to look (and it is important that they are unaltered) it might be a good idea to look into using Crossover Linux (which allows you to run Microsoft Office in Linux, but I'm pretty certain that you'll need a copy of Microsoft Office to do this). You can find Crossover Linux at:
[http://www.codeweavers.com/products/](http://www.codeweavers.com/products/)

EDIT: You could also try using WINE if you don't want to pay for Crossover Linux.

Another problem might be that you'll need to send your documents to other people who don't have Open Office (meaning you cannot send them the odt files). For this problem you could print the documents to pdf files and then send those. You could also save the documents as word documents and send them (but as I had mentioned before they may not be perfect copies depending on what you're doing).

**NOTE**: I think the problems with the documents being different is due to Open Office using different fonts. Perhaps someone else can verify this and give an explanation as to how a person can get around this problem.

2>Internet Browsing(with an ADSL modem)
You shouldn't have problems with this. Wireless networking cards can be a problem in Ubuntu depending on the chip manufacturer so if you have a wireless networking card or intend on getting one, you might want to find out more information about how to get certain chips to work or about what chip sets work better in Ubuntu.

3>Running a few language comilers,especially C.
For compiling C and C++ programs you should go to "System -> Administration -> Synaptic Package Manager". Then do a search for "build-essential" right click on the package called "build-essential" and select "Mark for Installation" and then click the "Apply" button. You'll now be able to use "gcc" to compile C programs and "g++" to compile C++ programs. You may need to install other packages (such as developer packages) depending on what you're programming. Also, if you're programming in C# for .NET applications you can install "mono" and "mono-mcs". For java you can look up "sdk" and the packages that you'd want to install would be "j2sdk1.4" and "j2re1.4".

4>And other basic utilities like photo managing media playing.
For creating/editing images you can use Inkscape to create vector graphics and Gimp to edit rastor images. For playing media files such as movies and music, you'll most likely need to install some plugins. To do this go to "Applications -> Add/Remove..." when the "Add/Remove Applications" window appears select "Sound & Video" and install the GStreamer plugins.

### Post by coolen on 2007-07-20
File roller, the default archive manager on Ubuntu, handles them just fine for me, I think you need the extras package from the repos, but after that, it should be sweet.
As for the exes, they're intended for Windows users, to give them a taste of free software. You will have all of those applications installed when you first boot up Ubuntu anyway.
.deb files are more like a very strictly constructed archive, containing the executable files, and any other files needed. They, like all non-executable files, need an program to read them.
The Ubuntu equivalent of .exe files is files marked with the executable permission and hopefully containing something the system can execute :). Of course, this is a Linux construct, not something the Ubuntu people came up with (nor did they come up with .debs: that was the work of the fine people of the Debian project, upon which Ubuntu is based).
In Linux, file extensions mean nothing. Some applications, like Gimp, will use them periferally. I tried naming a PNG file with the .mp3 extension and it complained, so I named it .png and then renamed it. It was still recognised as an image, opened and displayed perfectly :)

### Post by bigboy_pdb on 2007-07-22
chatterjee,
I know that someone mentioned that 7zip can be used as a compression utility over winRAR, but I remember having some problems with some utilities for decompressing RAR files (although that was with Ubuntu 6.10). If you have any problems with RAR files, there is a version of winRAR that RARLAB released for Linux. You can download it from their site, but I would recommend getting it from "Add/Remove Applications" instead. To do this go to "Applications" -> "Add/Remove...". In the search field type "rar" and the first item on the list will be RARLAB's winRAR. You can click the box beside it and then click "Apply" to install it.

If you don't have any RAR files that need to be decompressed then you can just use "gzip". You can "gzip" files using the "tar" command from the command line (type "man tar" to learn about it when you are in a terminal). If you need to compress some files or folders just select them in the same manner that you would with Windows (clicking while holding SHIFT or CTRL), right click one of the selected files, and select "Create Archive...". The default archive type is "tar.gz" which is a tar file (a set of files and folders that has been encoded within one single file) that has been compressed using a gzip (compression) algorithm. There are other types of compression formats in the list if you prefer another one.

### Post by bigboy_pdb on 2007-07-24
yanqui, you mentioned Ubuntu freezing up on you. There is a way to get out of it. I'm pretty certain that it won't always work, but I haven't been in a situation where it hasn't worked for me yet.

**NOTE**: To get back to your GUI session (tty7) use CTRL-ALT-F7 (I've written this in case someone attempts to go to the tty1 terminal before reading the whole post).

Press CTRL-ALT-F1 to go to a command line terminal (tty1) and log in. Type 'ps -e' for a list of processes with their IDs (use 'ps -e | more' to see the list one page at a time if necessary). Find the ID of the process that is causing the system to hang and then type 'kill ID' (where ID is the ID of the process). Type 'ps -e' again to check that the process has been killed. If the process is still in the list, use 'kill -9 ID' to ensure that the process is killed. Type CTRL-ALT-F7 to go back to the GUI session (tty7) and check if the system is once again functional. If tty7 is still hanging then you may need to return to tty1 and try killing some other processes. If tty7 is no longer hanging then go back to tty1 to log out using 'exit'.

I haven't actually taken much notice of the Beginner Team yet, but despite my number of posts consider myself very much a beginner. I've had Edgy and now Feisty installed on my desktop since pretty much April Fool's Day - hmmm, no significance **there** that I can see :-\" - but I can't help but feel that my install is not squeaky clean. You know, like when you did Windows, and configured stuff and replaced bits and didn't get rid of all of the drivers from the bits you removed but the OS just ran after all? Yeah, it's a bit that way on my system now.

Oh well.
So, I'm hoping to get my system totally cleaned up and configured properly.

Initially, when I first loaded Edgy, I was amazed and impressed with this OS. My first encounter with Linux was with RedHat 5. This is a much more polished product than that was, obviously. However, whilst I am impressed with it - I have an apple to compare the apple to - I suspect that XP users will not have the same reaction... for these reasons:
1. CLI - there is a fair bit of dealing with the configuration files, *not* using wizards - I like that, but...
2. adding hardware - (generally) much more work, not just clicking setup.exe
3. software in general - for instance, you're a small business and your accountant asks you to use a program called "My Cool Accounting Program" since it puts all the financial stuff where she can find it. You can hope that it runs in WINE, but...

So now, I'm careful about recommending Linux - it's not for everyone: not yet, anyway. At least, not the distros I've encountered. Too many of the people I see are Ikea/RadioShack/ClivePeters/HarveyNorman users who don't want to expend a lot of brain-power getting something to work. They have no understanding or appreciation of delayed gratification: they want it to just work *now* and with the minimum of effort. The idea of getting on a bulletin-board or forum like this one to find solutions to the problems would not be a reasonable approach to the ones I interviewed.

That said, there *is* a subgroup for whom Ubuntu Linux *is* ideal. This group generally wouldn't add or upgrade hardware, wouldn't need to run specialized software and aren't particularly adventurous on the PC. They want things to work... straight-away, and the same way now and in 10...20 years. They don't want to spend a lot of money on upgrades they don't even want, or install patches and security fixes. They want to feel safe online - that their computer is running an operating system that - by design - is secure against intrusion and viruses and spyware and all that.
For them, Ubuntu is ideal. I have set up a system or two already, and intend to set up more, once I get my head around all the configuration tasks one needs to do to avoid the above-mentioned dodgy OS setup.

Linux is different from XP - one is not really a competitor to the other. They each are ideal for user groups with related needs. Given the number of people who've expressed their dissatisfaction with WindowsXP issues that are addressed in Ubuntu, there is no question in my mind that there is a largely untapped potential user base for this brilliant operating system.

yanqui, there is another way of killing processes that is similar to the "Windows Task Manager". You can use "System Monitor" (System->Administration->System Monitor) to check and kill processes as well. I mentioned the other method because if GNOME is freezing up then "System Monitor" may not be much help or it might not kill the process as quickly as you need it killed if you're in a hurry.

### Post by bigboy_pdb on 2007-07-29
I installed the Microsoft fonts (as Matakoo suggested) and all the documents (that I had checked) which had been improperly formatted were now as I had initially created them in Word.

Even though I have a copy of Microsoft Office, I got the fonts here:
[http://gnome-look.org/content/show.php/MS+True+Type+core+fonts?content=19259](http://gnome-look.org/content/show.php/MS+True+Type+core+fonts?content=19259)

I extracted the fonts to a folder and I used the method for installing fonts under the "Manually" section on the following page:
[https://help.ubuntu.com/community/FontInstallHowto](https://help.ubuntu.com/community/FontInstallHowto)

**NOTE:** I did not copy the EULA file to the folder that I had created for my fonts under "/usr/share/fonts/truetype" because I wasn't certain about how that would affect the fonts once I reinstalled them.

There is a fonts virtual directory that you're supposed to be able to use to install fonts, but I couldn't use it when attempting to do so (with the use of root privileges).

**EDIT:**
This is an indicator that Open Office can be used as a replacement for Microsoft Office for a number of Word documents.

For anyone who is worried that their Word documents won't be formatted properly in Open Office (since not every Word document can be viewed in Open Office) or who is worried that their Word documents might contain objects that Open Office won't recognize, I recommend installing it first in Windows to see how it reads your documents.

From what I've seen with most regular documents that have mainly text, tables, bullets, and a number of other standard elements found in a formatted text document, Open Office seems to be able to handle these Word documents well.
