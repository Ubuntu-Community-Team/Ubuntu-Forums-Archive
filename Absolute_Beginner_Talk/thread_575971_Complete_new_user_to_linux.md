### Post by mivo on 2007-10-15
Well, when it comes to running Windows programs on Linux, too high expectations may lead to disappointment. There are Windows games like WoW that run extremely well in Wine, even to the point of flawlessness, but the are exceptions. Most Windows games, especially newer ones, will not work well, or at all. There are games that come with native Linux versions, like Unreal Tournament 2008 (the box will include both Windows and Linux versions), Neverwinter Nights and X3, so there are alternatives.

Personally, I've been a gamer for nearly twenty-five years, even wrote professionally for a bunch of printed German gaming magazines in the 90s, and I still ditched Windows entirely and use only Linux now -- but I mostly play on consoles these days. That is, in my opinion, where the video gaming market is headed to anyway (sales and revenue of PC games back that view, and fewer studios every year develop PC games, MMORPGs being the big exception).

In the end, I followed the "best tool for the job" principle. Consoles are the best tool for my gaming, and a Linux PC is the best tool for working. For me anyway, and it really is an individual decision that cannot be generalized; everyone has different needs. Linux has improved my productivity and reduced the amount  of little annoyances and limitations. I also could not really agree with the whole DRM stuff that is being forced on us, and one way to remain loyal to oneself is to *say* "no" when everything in yourself screams "no".

But still, if you are a hardcore PC gamer (unless it is WoW and some other games that work well), it will pay off to keep a Windows partition around. Linux is worth learning and getting familiar with, even if you may decide that it is not yet for you. Knowing and learning about it will provide you with a choice, and choice is a Good Thing. :)

For nearly all other applications, besides games, you will find that there are sufficient, equally as good or even better Linux alternatives. And they are all free. Truly free.

### Post by erginemr on 2007-10-15
For a hands-on quick start guide, you may search "linux for newbies" through Google.

Apart from that, I would suggest reading the following two sites, whence I also benefited a lot from:

1. UNIX Tutorial for Beginners:
[http://www.ee.surrey.ac.uk/Teaching/Unix/](http://www.ee.surrey.ac.uk/Teaching/Unix/)

2. Tux Files:
[http://www.tuxfiles.org/](http://www.tuxfiles.org/)

Although both sites start teaching from ground zero, the first site has a more academic approach, while the second is pretty downright hilarious. The articles are really easy to follow (i.e. not for geeks) and are full with very funny remarks, and I enjoyed every bit of it while reading. 

I would suggest you not to be afraid from the console (terminal) commands and start learning the widely used commands right away. They are much more powerful than MS-DOS commands, and are also very fun to learn. 

Console commands are incredibly powerful in Linux, indeed. Although graphical environments have evolved a lot over the last years, I still believe the one who masters the console also governs the whole Linux system.
