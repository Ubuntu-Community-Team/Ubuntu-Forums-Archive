### Post by xtacocorex on 2006-05-24
I'm going to play Devil's Advocate here...

I will say that if you become good at the command line, you can do pretty much anything you want with a Linux system. It also allows you to understand what is going on with the system, it's easier to "see" the internals per say. But at the same time, if you have to use a Windows computer at work, you may start writing batch files that mimic bash commands, like I have. I will say, from my experience, that the command line in Windows is not easy to use or as intuitive as Linux. 

That being said, it's not always the fastest way to do things, but you can automate lots of stuff with scripts.

I personally enjoy having a GUI. I can customize it and show people what Linux looks like because most non-users think it's just a command line. The hard part in getting Linux distributed more is to get people over that initial hump that all Linux is is a CLI.

### Post by mostwanted on 2006-05-24
I think Gnome (Ubuntu packaged version) is very user-friendly, much more so than Windows.

The Places menu comes to mind - that thing is very handy for accessing any internal, removable or network drive/folder you have access to. In Windows you need to open up Explorer for that and stuff like FTP shares don't even show up as a devices.

I also much prefer the Gnome philosophy of verbs on the dialog buttons instead of the traditional "Yes", "No" and "Cancel". Another part of the Gnome HIG that I like are the settings dialogs in Gnome apps; they remove the "Apply", "Ok", "Cancel" steps altogether and apply settings right away - much more user-friendly if you ask me. This also part of why KDE feels so alien to me - the KDE philosophy is very much to keep these - in my opinion - redundant, unintuitive steps.

### Post by IYY on 2006-05-24
In a sense, Windows has a big advantage when it comes to the desktop: it's all made by one company, and is distrubuted as one piece.
