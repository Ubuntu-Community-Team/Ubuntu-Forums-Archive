### Post by Rinnan on 2007-11-10
First, I want to thank you all for your good advice, which I stupidly did not follow, and paid the price for not doing so.

At first, I planned to buy the computer from Dell, the Inspiron 1420N.  This plan cooled a bit after I learned that the Ubuntu model cost $100 more.  This was a problem, since money is so tight, and I couldn't really come up with a good defense why a big computer company would charge more for exchanging one feature (Windows) for another one (Ubuntu) which cost them less.  This would like like offering a 100GB harddrive for $100 more than the model with the 200GB harddrive!  While I understand that support might cost more (they've got to hire slightly more expensive Ubuntu support people), support is something you pay for separately (and dearly!) at Dell anyway, and the vast majority of Linux users would be likely to need *less* support on average than their Windows users.  That alone might not have killed the deal, but the next problem did:  Dell was offering nine months interest free on Inspirons with their expensive service pack (nearly $300!), but not on any Ubuntu model.  In effect, the Ubuntu model cost nearly $300 more.  That was too steep a difference to pay, especially when I knew that the only real cost Dell was taking on was one of risk, which computer companies must do anyway to survive.

Another problem was shipping -- I live in Hawai`i and shipping takes a bit longer here.  They were estimating 2 weeks, but it could have easily been more.  And I've been without any computer for a while.  

The last store my wife and I bought at was CompUSA, and while they don't have a good reputation, we didn't have any problem with the last purchase we made from them.  So, back we went.  First, I got permission from the salesperson to be able to return the machine if Linux didn't work well.  This turned out to be vital.

The first machine we bought was a HP Pavillion.  Full of features and small and cute, it looked wonderful.  My wife fell in love with it and so I decided to give it to her and just use her machine, which worked perfectly with Linux under Feisty and had a one-line-fix problem with its sound for Gutsy, but no other problems.  She also needs to use Linux, but much more importantly, she needed to use XP.

So, Ubuntu 7.10 gets booted on it, to test its Linux-ability and to do the "ghost"ing work.  The ghosting failed horribly.   Even after editing grub / boot.ini / and using reloc (a tool to convince Windows to run after it's been moved from one place to another, Windows XP just half-booted and died.  Even in "safe mode".  Meanwhile, Ubuntu was showing off it's own, brand new "safe mode" during this whole process since basically nothing worked out of the box (only "safe mode" graphics, no wireless, no sound, no suspend, no hibernate.  Bluetooth, of all things, worked perfectly).  After putting in seven hours or so I called it a loss and returned to CompUSA.  That's where the story begins.

Grade D-:  CompUSA return policy and CompUSA in general.  I had been misinformed by the salesman (honestly, I believe) about the return policy.  I was told it could be returned in 14 days if it didn't work with Linux (or any other software I wanted to run on it.  In fact, they (at first) tried to refuse to return it the next day for anything less than a hardware failure.

Grade B:  The Salesman.  He misinformed me about the return policy, but immediately admitted being wrong and defended my right to return it to his boss.  I got it returned and a new machine (for store credit only).  Every other computer store on Oahu will return a machine in 14 days and refund your credit card, for any problems at all (including "changed my mind").

I had made a mistake in my hurry to get this process done before I got to work today.  I had deleted the Linux partition (and the XP partition) before I removed GRUB.  So although the machine had the partitions in the original arrangement, it couldn't boot.  All I needed was a System Rescue disk, from Windows NT/2000/XP/Vista.  I refused to pay the $69 dollar OS restore fee so someone could run FDISK /MBR.  So they said, fine, you do it, leading me back to the lab.  Suddenly, No one had a Windows System Rescue disk of any variety.  That's right folks, the biggest Windows Box store on Oahu, according to their own techs, has not one copy of any variety of a Windows System Rescue disk.  Not one, anywhere.  They were willing to look like idiots rather than honestly allow me to restore the computer.  Best Buy and Circuit City would not have insisted on this kind of restoration at all.  Admittedly, my own hurried mistake started it, but after working all night over a machine I was assured would work just fine with XP at least (I find out the next day that n one had actually tried it, nor could they explain why a safe-mode boot wouldn't bring up any graphics other than the logo), I was tired and mistakes were inevitable.  At the end of it, I felt like I was working for them rather than their customer. 

Another fight with my wife insured that we would in fact, get a full refund (not store credit), but the $69 is gone.  We onlly got the refund if we agreed to buy another laptop from CompUSA though (this is so that we could get the price difference between the more expensive first laptop and the cheaper second laptop without being stuck with a $200 or so store credit in a store we had no interest in shopping in).  Every little thing that would have been automatic with any other store, we had to fight over.

Grade A:  Safeware insurance.  They agreed to cover the dropped laptop, over the phone, while I stood their at CompUSA.  We still lost some money, but it could have been much worse.

The new machine, an Acer Aspire 5630, works in the ways I need it to right out of the box (sound, accelerated graphics including compiz, wireless, hibernate and suspend).  What a pain though.
