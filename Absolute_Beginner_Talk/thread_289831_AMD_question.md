### Post by igknighted on 2006-10-31
"clock speed" is no longer the only measurement of how good a processor is.  The Athlon X2 has two cores, and I believe hyper-threading (or transport as I guess AMD calls it) as well.  Each core acts as its own processor, increasing the system speed, and hyperthreading (as far as I understand it, I could easily be wrong) does a similar thing virtually.  The reason clock speeds aren't higher mainly has to do with power.  To run your system at 5.0 ghz would increase the power draw to a level that most users don't want to deal with (cooling and the electric bill are big issues here).  On top of that, add in 64 bit processing and the new X2 is leaps and bounds above your old athlon.

### Post by Henry Rayker on 2006-10-31
Back when AMD started using the model number instead of the clockspeed for their parts, it was for a reason. Sure, clock-rate was slower on the AMD parts, but Intel had a much lower IPC (instructions per cycle). This means that, in order to do the same amount of work, their clock rate had to be higher.

Imagine you have 2 cars and you're moving. One can move twice as fast as the other, but holds half the amount of cargo. If you use one or the other, you can get done in the same amount of time, but the faster car, you'll risk getting tickets and the like (analogous to the power cost of a higher clockrate).

These days, upping the clockrate doesn't have the impact on performance that it used to. An improved architecture is far more effective. The dual core parts are usually clocked down from a single core sibling; this is due to some synchronization issues.

However, congrats on the new system. I hope she treats you right.
