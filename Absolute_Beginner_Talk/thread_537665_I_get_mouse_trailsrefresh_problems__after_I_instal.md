### Post by tyggna1 on 2007-08-29
Sounds like you're encountering an error in direct rendering.

Time to appreciate what your graphics card does:

Your processor and ram is perfectly capable of making your screen show what it should--the only problem is that the compute power it takes to do that is mixed in with all your other processor.  Video cards decided to bear that burden for them in order to have a smoother computer experience.  Indeed, most graphics cards have adequate components to be a stand-alone (minus a device to input things on)

Direct rendering is just that.  When you installed the new ATI drivers, it messed up the way that it does direct rendering--so your card doesn't quite process right--giving you visible errors on your screen.

To fix it, type in:
```
lspci
```

in the command prompt, and look for *VGA compatible controller.  That's your video card.  Post your exact card name here (it'll be right after the colon)--see if anyone else knows how to fix it, and also try searching the forums.

It also could help to post your xorg.conf

### Post by tyggna1 on 2007-09-03
okay, here's the best tip you're ever going to get about tweaking and fixing video cards.  You already know the secret of the xorg.conf file--now to empower you to fix it to your heart's content.

be sure to have a back up of a working configuration.   We're gonna need to use the Command Line Interface for this, but fear not, for it is a powerful asset and tool.

in the terminal window, type in
```
sudo cp /etc/X11/xorg.conf /etc/X11/xorg.conf.backup
```

to copy your file to a backup, that way, if things ever go screwy (and you'll want to write this down on paper), you can press ctrl-alt-F1 to get to the command line and type:

O```
sudo cp /etc/X11/xorg.conf.backup /etc/X11/xorg.conf 
```
to fix it.

There, now to get your card working right!

You can lookup anything that the driver is capable of doing by typing in:

```
man driverName
```

in your case

```
man radeon
```

This will give you a list of cards that your driver was designed to work with, and below that, it'll say "configuration details" and give a list of things that say
```
option "somthing" "variableType" 
```
This is what you can write into your xorg.conf file to use different aspects of your card.  Let's break it down:

the "option" works as a flag to show your linux kernel what it needs to look for
the "something" is a name that the programmer put in there to describe the function
the "variableType" will say either boolean (meaning "true", "false", or some other form of yes and no) , integer (all whole numbers), string (or words that you can type in), or something else that is specific to the option you selected.

in the man radeon file, it gives a brief description of what each option will do with your driver.

For direct rendering you're going to want to add these options to your xorg.conf file under device:

```

Option "EnablePageFlip" "on" #increases preformance of 3D applications
Option "RenderAccel" "on"  #forces your video card to render all images (default is enable)
Option "AccelMethod" "EXA" 
#tells your computer how to render the image, EXA is faster, but it's less stable than the alternative XXA
Option	"AGPFastWrite" "on"
 #Faster than default (off), only use this if your card is AGP, not PCI or PCI-e
Option	"SWcursor" "off" #this is just for speed
Option	"DDCMode" "on" #this forces your card to use what your monitor is capable of

```

That should get you much better performance on 3D applications.  If it does not, I at the very least gave you the tools you need to learn how to better tweak it.  Give them all a try, check the forum for your specific card to see what's worked best, and *always* back up your xorg.conf file before changing anything, and after changing something that works.

Hope this is helpful.
