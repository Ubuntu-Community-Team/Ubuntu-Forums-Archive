Check with local colleges/universities to see what their prerequisites are for a computer science or IT degree. Often degree requirements are on line, and these have changed a lot as technology has changed.

Once upon a time, to get into some CS programs, especially night school, you had to have experience or taken a course in a block structured language, (PL/I or Pascal), assembly language, and data structures. It's all changed since then, as I said. 

I suggest that you pick something you like about computers and focus on that. If you want to program for a living, the programming part is only part of what you have to know IMHO.

Having a linux system is good for all of this, because you get good tools as part of the effort of maintaining and learning about the linux system. If you had to buy these on a Windows system, you'd go broke.

### Post by lespaul_rentals on 2008-04-02
You can help new users fix their problems in Absolute Beginner Talk, as well as offer encouragement.  You can join a Forum Team and dedicate a few hours each week to helping others in the forums.  If you are good at graphic design, you can make themes, icons, wallpapers, and the like to promote Ubuntu.  If you know little or no programming, you probably couldn't join the developers, but you can definitely help them iron out the programming by using an alpha or beta version of Ubuntu and reporting all bugs you encounter.  If you have a job, you can donate money to Canonical and buy official Ubuntu merchandise to support Ubuntu financially.

And, of course, you can always take your clothes off and pose for the GDM splash screen (just kidding, just kidding ;)).

So, yes, there's a lot you can do to help Ubuntu.
