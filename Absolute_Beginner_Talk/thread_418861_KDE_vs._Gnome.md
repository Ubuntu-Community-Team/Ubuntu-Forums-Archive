Yes, they're just windowing environments.  You can easily run apps from one int eh other, as long as you have the proper libraries installed (Synaptic or apt-get will take care of that for you).  For example, I'm on Gnome, but use K3B to burn CDs.

### Post by tgalati4 on 2007-04-24
It's like running shoes.  Do you want lower-priced functionality, or higher-priced flash?  You need to try on a pair and see how you like them.  All three (KDE, Gnome, XFCE) have their place.  It depends on your hardware.  Older hardware (including graphics cards) will dictate XFCE because Gnome and KDE will crawl (Pentium 1, 266MHz for instance).  On a PII, 300 MHz, with at least 256 MB and decent graphics card, then Gnome is usable, but not snappy.  PIII, 1GHz, with 512 MB can run KDE well.

It's not written in stone.  I have DSL servers running on P1's, 166MHz, but they are headless (no keyboard, monitor, or mouse), yet they run remote desktops fine using Fluxbox.  I tried Breezy Badger KDE on a PIII/500MHz machine, but it was poky and some of the nifty KDE applications would crash randomly.  Went to Dapper and Gnome and it's been reliable.  I miss some of those nifty KDE apps, but I can load them and run them as well in the Gnome environment.

I am now testing Freespire 1.0.13 and it is built on an older KDE 3.3.2, but it seems more stable than the older Breezy Kubuntu, so although it's an older KDE environment, the Linspire developers have focused on stability.  

When you find a distro that works for you, stick with it for a while.  The power of Linux is the variety and the flexibility of all distributions out there.  And they all talk to each other.

Good luck.
