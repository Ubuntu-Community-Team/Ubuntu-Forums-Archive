### Post by arctic on 2006-03-23
Being somewhat of a Linux veteran (using Linux since 2001), I'd like to point some things out:

1. We help you, because we have also been "noobs" once and needed the same amount of help, so most of us know how frustrated someone can be if thing don't work right. It is some kind of obligation we feel. And the most satisfying reward we can get from newcomers is "Problem solved, Thanks a lot". Yes, we like to hear compliments sometimes. But if you guys do things well, we will equally say "Well done!" to you.

2. Many experienced Linux users offer their help not only in one but several forums. We have only limited time and help the maximum possible in that time. Thus, some answers will be very short, reduced to to the max, to the essential things. If answers are kept short, it is not because of some disrespect towards newcomers, but because there is more work than we can handle. (I made e.g. some combined 8.000 posts in the different forums where I help, others posted way over 12.000 messages).

3. When we troubleshoot, we do not sit in front of your box. We have to rely on the information that you can give us. We try to point you to the right direction, but if someone has e.g. real problems posting the output of a certain file, then we cannot really help that person, as we can only guess what his problem could be. A newcomer must be willing to get his hands "dirty". Otherwise he will always feel uncomfortable with his Linux distro.

4. Everyone of us has only limited knowledge. We, the experienced users also continue to learn new things every day. We are far from perfect. Thus you and us will encounter problems, where we simply don't know what might be causing the mess that you ran into. So if someone does not reply to a thread, it is not because we don't care, but because we don't know a solution.

5. Some veterans are annoyed by answering the same questions over and over again. That is basically their very own problem (They should take a timeout imho). Some of those vets still reply, but rather harshly. Don't flame them. They are simply burned out. If you are being asked for the 400th time, how to burn an iso image, you would probably also say. "Damn, can't you use the search-tool?". That behaviour is not what it should be but it is human and usually moderators and admins deal with this, so no need for starting flame-wars and getting personal. ;)

6. A lot of the information we give is already available online. If you use google e..g for "cannot login ubuntu", then you will find tons of informative links. Use them to your advantage. Reading some of those posts is sometimes way faster and easier than waiting for a reply. If you cannot find some good online help, then post on the forums and say that you already searched the web and did not find anything that could help you. That will improve your image in a Linux community a lot! :)

7. We are also human beings with feelings and opinions. Respect each other and everything will be fine. :D

8. If your problem got solved, don't disappear, but come back and try to help others. If you make mistakes, no one will chop your head off. Simply help to the best of your abilities. You will automatically learn new stuff, maybe become a geek ... and: you will someday recognize your forum as some kind of a warm and friendly family that awaits your return. ;)


And now... happy posting. :)
