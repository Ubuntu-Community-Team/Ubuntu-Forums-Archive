### Post by irish_flu on 2007-03-16
The Difference between "Ubuntu", "Kubuntu" and "Xubuntu" is which desktop comes installed by default.

Ubuntu uses "Gnome", which is considered by many to be mature and easy-to-use.

Kubuntu uses KDE, considered by others to be prettier layed out better.

Xubuntu is very "clean", and is considered by many to be better for "older" computers with less resources (less RAM, slower processor).  It's "lighter" than the other desktops.

Please note, almost everything I've just said is pretty much just opinion; any number of people have perfectly valid personal reasons for choosing any of these over the other two, and none of them are "bad".

One more thing; it's very easy (and you can get help here) to switch back and forth between these desktops, no matter which "version" you download.  In fact, you can have all three and pretty much switch between them as you please.

You'll probably get the most help here for "Ubuntu", with Gnome.  That said, downloading the others won't leave you dead in the water or anything.  I'd probably start with Gnome, but that might just be because I happen to personally like it better.

As far as backing up your Windows computer is concerned, what you're going to be most concerned here is Documents and Media.

Your documents (Office files and such) will probably be contained within your "My Documents" folder.  You "Media files" (pictures, songs, movies, funny videos, etc) may or may not be contained there as well.  You're not going to be able to use the same programs and such, so there's really no need to back them up.

What are you using (currently) for web-browsing and email?  You should be able to "export" all that stuff and back it up also (and later import things like your email, your addressbook, and your favorites/bookmarks into Ubuntu).  As far as that stuff goes, it's best to look for instructions from whoever made your applications (Mozilla if you use Firefox, or Microsoft, etc).


EDIT:  Oooohhhhhhh  I just realized that it looks like you're dual-booting with Windows.  Well, if you want all your documents and media (and if you have the hard drive space) then I'd still follow my advice above.  As far as backing up Windows before you resize your hard drive partitions, the advice is the same AS LONG AS YOU HAVE YOUR WINDOWS CD and and can get CDs or downloads for ALL INSTALLED PROGRAMS.  Everything will probably be fine, and you won't need to use that backup, but you never know.  :)
