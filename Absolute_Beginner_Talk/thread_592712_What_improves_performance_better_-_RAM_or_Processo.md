### Post by Can+~ on 2007-10-26
Well, 512mb is pretty much in the edge of what are today "minimum requirements", although Ubuntu will run even with 256.

Increasing RAM rarely has a noticeable effect. Gamers are the first to notice the change, since there is more space for "rendering" calculations, reducing the amount of loads and unloads (I don't know the technical terms for this, but you get the point). For normal desktop users, you notice the change when running several applications at the same time, or a big one like 3dsmax or photoshop.

You may not notice anything, but there will be times that some extra RAM will save you from clogging your PC.

### Post by ericesque on 2007-10-26
> **rjs82vette said:**
> 
a faster processor will always make you system faster..........

A bit of a misleading blanket statement.  If your processor isn't the bottle neck, upgrading it is not going to make much-- if any difference.  Your processor can only process the data as fast as the data can get to it.  

Besides, with modern processors, during general usage you will see little difference between a 1.6 GHZ processor and a 3.0 GHZ processor.  You may notice if you're doing CPU intensive tasks like zipping a large file, decrypting a dvd, or gaming-- but again you'll only see a difference if your other hardware is up to the task of feeding the processor with the data it needs to process.

I say if you want to hang on to the machine for a while, upgrade from 512MB of RAM.  Any money you might dump on a processor, put it in savings toward a new machine instead.

### Post by Therion on 2007-10-26
This is one of those "It All Depends" questions, since which upgrade (RAM vs. CPU) will give you the biggest bang for your buck depends on a few things.  Mainly, what you do with your PC, how much RAM you have now, your current CPU and how long you intend to keep this particular box running before doing significant upgrades or building/buying a new system.  

Just by virtue of the fact that you're sitting on 512MB, I would say that adding an additional 1GB stick of RAM should (should!) show you some significant improvements in your system's overall performance.  Just how much is impossible to say without knowing more about the system's configuration.

I build a lot of Windows PC for clients, and based on "average" desktop use, I suggest no less than 1GB of RAM for WinXP and no less than 2GB for gaming machines, frequently more.  Of course that's a Windows box and we're discussing Linux atm.  Still, I can't see how having 1-2GB of RAM could do anything BUT help, even in a Linux environment.

### Post by ShadowMKII on 2007-10-27
There is not a big difference. There is something called an "architecture" in a processor that determines how a CPU processes information. Depending on how well or "streamlined" it is made will help to determine how fast a CPU will process information. A good example for you to look up is the difference between an AMD Athlon64 and an AMD Sempron (with 64-bit compatibility.) You will find that even though the Sempron will have a higher clock rating than an Athlon64, the Athlon will have a higher information processing rating. (That number is indicated in a xxxx+ fashion. Ex.: 4000+, like my signature says about my CPU.) I am not saying this because I am an AMD fanboy either, that is just how all things CPU related work. If I were you, I would get the additional RAM, and then wait a while before purchasing a new computer. You would be better off purchasing a new computer than a processor because by the time you need a new processor, you will probably have to upgrade the speed of your RAM, which can only be done by buying new RAM *basically,* get a new CPU, a new video card and numerous other things. Good decision on your part! ^-^
