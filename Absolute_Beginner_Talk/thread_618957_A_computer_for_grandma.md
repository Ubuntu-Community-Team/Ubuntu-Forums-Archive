### Post by AusIV4 on 2007-11-21
My stepmother's mother has recently moved to town, and my stepmother has decided she needs a computer. Being the family guru, my dad has asked me to figure out what setup would be best for her.

As far as I know, she has never owned a computer before, or if she has she's not used it much. She's taking a computer course in the near future, which I assume will be teaching her about Windows and MS Office.

I'm trying to figure out what is the most practical setup for her. I assume she'll just be sending e-mails and doing some word processing, so it doesn't need to be very advanced, it just needs to be simple and accessible for a novice computer user.

So here's the rundown of considerations:

1) Windows: 
**Pros**:
Huge market share, so it will be easy for her to get support at the average store.
The class she's taking will more than likely be in Windows.
My dad and stepmother will be more familiar when she has problems.
Can run on the hardware the family has lying around the house.

**Cons**:
It's Microsoft ;)
It has a wide variety of viruses.
It's expensive
IMHO, the start menu is very poorly organized (it's difficult to find a program you need when your menus are sorted by vendor).

2) Mac OSX:
**Pros**:
The genius bar would be available to help her through rough spots.
Her class might brush over OSX.
The interface is pretty easy to learn
Viruses aren't much of a concern
She'd probably get a Mac Mini, which would fit well with her space considerations.

**Cons**:
She'd have to buy new hardware
It's less mainstream than Windows, which means fewer people would be able to help with it.

3) Ubuntu
**Pros**:
Free
Runs on hardware we have lying around the house.
Intuitive menu interface (organized by category rather than vendor)
If she has problems, I can help via ssh
Security isn't likely to be a problem

**Cons**:
No chance her class is going to cover it.
I'd be her first call when she needs help.
Some audio/video codecs can't be legally obtained.

I'm strongly leaning towards Ubuntu, but I'm not sure how much having a class in Windows would throw her off when her own computer isn't the same. 

Is there anyone who has been in a similar situation who can offer advice?

Thanks,
-Austin

### Post by linuxlizard on 2007-11-21
I vote for ubuntu.

My mom is in her mid 70s and didn't use a computer until she was in her mid 60s. I've had her on ubuntu for the past few years. 

I had to set it up for her, install all the codecs (chain e-mails from family and friends often include flash animations and the like), selected the printer and camera to make sure they would work with the computer. 

Then I disabled the update notifier so that wouldn't bother her all the time and there would be zero chance of an update crashing the computer. 

Stuck big icons up for firefox and open office writer, as well as a documents and photos folder on the desktop.

That's pretty much it- it's zero maintenence. Except the printer- be warned, if something goes wrong on the printer like a paper jam or print spool problem, you're going to get the call even though it's not the OS per say.

Windows or ubuntu either way- you are going to get the tech support call anyway, simply because you're the kind soul who set up the computer for her. Even if you were just the guy who offered advice about what to buy and went over and plugged it in for her, you will get the tech support call from her. People are just like that. With ubuntu, at least you can also remote desktop in and fix stuff. 

With windows, you will get calls more often- when my mom had that on her computer all the constant update notifications for windows, antivirus software, printer driver, etc, etc, etc sent her into panics every time and she called for reassurance, usually with vague descriptions of what was happening wondering if something was wrong. Also when antivirus needed to be purchased again and stuff like that. With ubuntu just disable the update notification and you will never get those kinds of calls. 

Also, my mom really didn't notice much difference when I made the switch- and she had been using windows for several years. Windows had crapped out on her hard drive and she had lost the install disk or the manufacturer hadn't supplied it, so I switched her to linux. She may have noticed it looked a little different, and I had to show her a few things in open office writer, but it was hardly anything and to this day she can't tell you the difference between windows and ubuntu and the internet and firefox even though she uses the machine every day. She just knows she clicks on the icon to check her e-mail (in firefox) and go to her favorite web sites.

As for her class- set her up with abiword instead of open office writer- abiword has a menu system like microsoft word, open office writer has stuff in wierd places for people used to word. It's also faster to load up. Other stuff just find out what she's learning about in her class and then give her the linux equivelent. Classes breeze through stuff anyway- it will give her a feel for what can be done, but she probably won't notice much difference figuring out how to do it.
