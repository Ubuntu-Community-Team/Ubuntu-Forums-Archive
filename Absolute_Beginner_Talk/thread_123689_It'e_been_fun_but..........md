### Post by aysiu on 2006-02-01
I think you've hit on a few points. I'd say Mac's *nix-like base is a lot slicker on the outside for a few reasons:

1. They design one operating system to suit one architecture on only a handful of different models (iMac, iBook, Powerbook, Mini...).

2. They, as you've noted, have a lot of money and resources to pour into making the interface slick.

3. They makes *looks* their top priority.

Ubuntu to a certain extent and Linux and a lot of non-commercial open source applications generally lean towards almost the exact opposite:

1. They're designed to work on multiple platforms in multiple operating systems for any number of models.

2. They don't always have a lot of money backing them. Some applications (even some very good ones) are almost entirely volunteer efforts.

3. Although Linux users appreciate some good eye candy, developing all sorts of themes and icon sets, slickness of interface tends not to be a top priority.

And Linux distributions, for all their functionality, intuitiveness, or whatever, do not have the slickness Mac OS X has. Look at Spotlight v. Beagle/Kat or Expose v. Kompose or Widgets v. gDesklets. The functionality's there, but it just doesn't flow as smoothly. If you no idea what I'm talking about, use OS X Tiger for about ten minutes.

The real issue seems to be one of priorities. Slickness isn't a top priority, especially since developers are creating applications that have to work on different architectures, different operating systems, different desktop environments... "integration" just can't be a top priority when some people are running Gnome and others KDE... and others XFCE, etc.
