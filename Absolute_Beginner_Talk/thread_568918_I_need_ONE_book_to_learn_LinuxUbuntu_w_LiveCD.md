> If I can get just ONE book to get a grasp of Linux and Ubuntu, what should it be?  I want to walk off the gangway with a good grasp of Linux/Ubuntu using only my computer, the Live CD, and a great reference.

If you just want *one* book, I'm not sure how you could do better than [The Official Ubuntu Book, Second Edition](http://www.amazon.com/Official-Ubuntu-Book-2nd/dp/0132354136/) by Benjamin Mako Hill, et al. I believe the whole idea of that book is to be The One Ubuntu Book for people who just want one. I've only scanned through it, though.

I really appreciated [Beginning Ubuntu Linux](http://www.amazon.com/Beginning-Ubuntu-Second-Novice-Professional/dp/1590598202/) by Keir Thomas. It helped me get up and running with Ubuntu in no time.

I'm convinced of the advantages of books that are not distro-specific, though, now that I have a few months of Ubuntu under my belt. I read [PC Magazine Linux Solutions](http://www.amazon.com/PC-Magazine-Linux-Solutions-Magazine-Joe-Merlino/dp/0471777692/) by Joe Merlino and learned more than I thought I would, and now I'm loving every page of [A Practical Guide to Linux(R) Commands, Editors, and Shell Programming](http://www.amazon.com/Practical-Guide-Commands-Editors-Programming/dp/0131478230/) by Mark G. Sobell.

Enjoy your cruise. :)
