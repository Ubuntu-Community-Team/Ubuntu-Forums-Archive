### Post by hscottyh on 2007-01-24
Once you get your dual monitors working.....
You could always install Windows in a Vmware virtual machine. It's really cool, when you have two monitors. You can keep windows up on one of the monitors and have the best of both worlds. It doesn't work well for windows games, but does for everything else.

I would suggest Vmware Player over Vmware Server using it this way. To get a blank image to install windows into use this very handy link. [http://www.easyvmx.com/](http://www.easyvmx.com/)

Vmware Player doesn't give you Vmware Tools for the guest OS, but some searching will show you how to get that from Vmware Workstation (the pay version) and install it your guest OS. Not essential, but worth the effort. The tools makes the mouse smoother and also makes switching from the guest os and the host os easier.

### Post by jml on 2007-01-24
Welcome to Ubuntu!

As for your first question.  Watch your screen very carefully as soon as you start your computer.  On my system, I get a text message to hit a particular key (In my system its escape,) to enter the Grub boot up menu.  On my system, I only have two or three seconds before it defaults to Ubuntu.  If you press the key soon enough, you should see the option to boot into Windows.  You may find as you get more experience with Ubuntu, you will boot into Windows less and less.

As for books, despite the Availability of on line documentation,, I do prefer to have paper based references in my Library.  These are the books I have read and my impressions.

The Official Ubuntu Book is published by Prentice Hall and is a good resource for new users.  The one complaint I have is that it does not cover restricted formats such as playing encrypted DVD's, encoding and playing MP3 files and playing WMV files.   This information is available on line, but not in the book.

Ubuntu Linux Bible published by Wiley is my favorite.  Its got good introductory information, as well as information for the experienced user.  It also gives good advice in how to install all those codecs and libraries that are not installed by default.

Ubuntu Unleashed published by SAMS is OK but like the "Official" guide is rather unhelpful when it comes to restricted multimedia formats.

Ubuntu for Non-Geeks (published by No Starch Press,) is a small book that covers Ubuntu well  and also discusses restricted formats well enough to play pretty much anything you would want to play.

Finally, Ubuntu Hacks published by O'Reilly is a good "cook book" style of reference, but not ideal for new users.  It does discuss restricted formats well.
