### Post by fluffington on 2006-06-07
First off, let me repeat what others have already said: While there are tools that run on Linux that can do the same things, you're not going to find software quite as polished as Adobe's stuff, and you're probably better off running [Crossover Office](http://www.codeweavers.com/) or [VMware](http://www.vmware.com/) and keeping the apps you already have (or simply using Windows or OS X---Linux is great, but it's not always the best tool for the job).

Anyway, on with the list:

Photoshop, Fireworks: [The GIMP](http://www.gimp.org/), [Gimpshop](http://plasticbugs.com/?page_id=294) (probably your best bet), [Krita](http://www.koffice.org/krita/)

Illustrator, Freehand: [Inkscape](http://www.inkscape.org/), [Karbon14](http://www.koffice.org/karbon/), [Xara Xtreme](http://www.xaraxtreme.org/)

Dreamweaver: [Bluefish](http://bluefish.openoffice.nl/), [Quanta Plus](http://quanta.kdewebdev.org/), [Screem](http://www.screem.org/), [Nvu](http://www.nvu.com/) (Note that I only ever used Dreamweaver for the syntax highlighting and easy access to my servers, which can be done with a fairly sizeable chunk of text editors)

Flash: [Flash For Linux](http://f4l.sourceforge.net/), [Moho](http://www.lostmarble.com/)

Note that while I know most of the Adobe Creative Suite and Macromedia Suite fairly well, I don't have much experience (none at all in a few cases) with about two thirds of the programs I listed, and I don't know anything about your workflow, so I have no idea if any of these will even come close to working for you.

### Post by fluffington on 2006-06-07
I suppose I should post what I use, since the list I just posted was software equivalents (as far as such a thing is even possible).

I do logo design and print advertizing (and a little bit of web design) professionally, and do about 80% of my graphics work in Illustrator CS2. The other 20% is divided between Photoshop CS2 and Fireworks MX2004 about equally.

But, since I'm out of that job in less than a week (I'd rather be coding), and I don't have personal licenses for any of that and don't much care for Windows or Mac OS anyway, I've been trying out Linux alternatives for a month or so now.

For graphics, I use The GIMP and Inkscape. The GIMP works fine as a Photoshop replacement for me (things are frequently in different places, but it's mostly all there), but I miss Illustrator. I'll probably switch to Xara Xtreme for vector stuff when it hits 1.0; it has about the same capabilities as Illustrator (better in some places, even) and the renderer is by far the fastest I've ever seen.

For code, I use Gedit; MDI with syntax highlighting is really all I need. Of course it's not *exactly* what I want, but I'm working on a couple plugins to fix that (mostly, I want a file selector and terminal like in Kate, but don't want to install all the extra KDE stuff to get it). I have VMware set up with Windows XP for testing layouts in IE, but I'll be moving to Wine as soon as I figure out how to get it working on 64-bit.

### Post by zugu on 2006-06-07
Some Linux zealots suggested me NVU as a Dreamweaver replacement, but eventually I was shocked at the difference between the two. NVU is buggy as hell. It doesn't even by far resemble Dreamweaver. So keep in mind that NVU cannot be a replacement for Dreamweaver. The developers claim that it can be used as a replacement, but if you're in need to do something professional or you think you're too addicted to Dreamweaver, just don't use NVU.

Hopefully, there will be a day when NVU will achieve many features that Dreamweaver has now.

As for the Photoshop/GIMP dillemma: Back when I used XP only, I used Photoshop ocasionally. However, the transition to GIMP was and still is a pain for me. At first, I spent 2 hours trying to merge 2 images, and just couldn't do it by myself! And the windowed layout is a hell. Apart from the omnipresent "alt-tabbing" (sic) between GIMPS windows, it just clutters your application bar to the extent only IE 6 could. The developers should know that the "one-window - many tabs" thing made so many people migrate to other, non IE browsers.

Yeah, yeah, I know there is also gimpshop, but that's another issue.

The GIMP developers defended their layout saying that GIMP does not strive to become a Photoshop clone, but a full-featured application with its own identity. However, look at XMMS: does the Winamp resemblence make it less popular? People search for easy transitions from a platform to another. I just think that the GIMP developers are just stiff and overprotective of their product, to the extent of driving people away from it. This is the type of thinking that eventually kills FOSS.
