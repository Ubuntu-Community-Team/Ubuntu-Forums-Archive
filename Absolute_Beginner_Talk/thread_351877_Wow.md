### Post by timlewis242 on 2007-02-02
This is going to be a long one but I never do this so everyone will have to suffer through it. Besides, it might convince someone to make the step to Linux. I am not new to Linux, in fact the first time I tried to install it, I put it on an IBM Think Pad with a 166 mhz processor, if that gives you any clue. Since then, I have 'played' with it off and on over the years, always impressed by the advances of the distribution and I have even made a couple of attempts to use it in a serious manner. But each time after spending days trying to get my pc configured I would run out of time and patience and move back to Windows.
   This passed week end, I put together a desktop pc from spare parts, for my daughter who has 'graduated' to her own pc. I didn't want to give her an OS that she could install things on and change settings so I intalled the new Mandriva 2007 disrtro of LInux. It did about what I would expect it to and I was content. My wife gave me her laptop, a cheap Gateway MX3230 that she had bought for school and for fun I tried using the Mandriva cd on it. The result was a mess. The graphics support was a mess, the sound didn't work and the wireless card (built in)? Forget it!
   I was sitting on a plane today on my way to Detroit and I found the Ubuntu CD in my bag. I always carry it because I often don't carry a laptop when I travel and I use it when I'm forced to utilize a public computer, such as a hotel business center. This eliminates the threat of key loggers that might be on the machine. I through it in the Gateway out of boredom and could not believe it. I knew as soon as I got to the desktop, things are different. The graphics were perfect with no input from me at all, the sound worked and incredibly the internal wireless card was not only detected, but up and running!!! Everything on this laptop functions as it should, all the way down to the proprietary little volume control dial on the front. I was floored.
  I now sit here on the same laptop and write this post, wireless, on my couch, thanks to the hard work of the people at Ubuntu. I don't know what you guys are doing, but you are YEARS ahead of the other distros and they need to stop and begin again after they figure out what you have already done! It's through your hard work that Linux will succeed and I am now a die hard, loyal fan.
   If anyone is looking for an inexpensive (400.00) workhorse that can compete with any windows machine, buy the Gateway MX3230 and put Ubuntu on it. Though I have to assume it would be as easy on most other lap tops as well.
   Great Job!!!!!!!!!!

### Post by Ryba on 2007-02-02
I started off with Redhat 4, used them all the way up to 7.2. Never liked all the rpm hell that went along with them.

Tried gentoo but hated that I had to constantly recompile my entire computer for all upgrades (yes exaggerated but that is what it felt like at times). 

Mandrake felt like a crappy redhat rip off.

Suse 10 had some nice cool stuff but it wasn't very good about letting me do what I wanted to without doing tons of work at the time.

Fedora Core 4/5 was good but their repositories were empty compared to debian/ubuntu and was *extremely* slow at the time. I hear they have fixed that now but I might look at 7 later but no motivation right now.

I used debian (in unstable mode) in between each of the above switches. Kept returning back to debian because for all its flaws, it just worked. It had everything in their repositories and didn't require me to recompile a thousand things. I could just upgrade and still work.

I heard a lot of buzz about ubuntu over the web, tried it out, was wowed by it. After my 6 month soak test of it I decided it was actually good. I convinced most of my entire department to switch from redhat/suse/debian over to ubuntu and they have all been happy ever since as well. Main reason is cause our work computers are laptops and ubuntu was by far the best laptop distro there was. From their it evolved into making it onto all of our desktops at homes and well ...

I don't think anything really can compete with ubuntu for home desktop use if they keep up at the speed of work and quality of targeted work as they have been.
