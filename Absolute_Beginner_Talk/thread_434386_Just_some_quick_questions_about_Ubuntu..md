The nice thing about downloading and trying out a Linux distribution is that you can find out if it supports what you need *before* you actually install it.  Just run it on the live CD for a bit.  Sure, it will be slow.. but you aught to be able to get a sense of whether the hardware is working.

Once you are reasonably sure things are mostly working, you could install it as a dual-boot, so you would still have a safety net.

That's how we switched.  One day my wfie said she'd had enough of Microsoft, and was going to explore Linux.  She set up a dual-boot, and she made a checklist of all the things she used the machine for.  She went down that list point by point, and when Ubuntu was doing everything on her list she scraped the MS garbage off her disk and never looked back.  One day she asked me if I'd like to try it.  I said sure, but I don't want to dual boot. Just let it have the whole disk.  The sorry excuse for an OS that I was running at the time was due for it's annual reinstall anyway.  She got everything working perfectly, and I've started to actually have fun with PCs again for the first time since Windows came out.

Just do it.  :-)

### Post by RTrev on 2007-05-06
Just a p.s.  Most people seem to find that running the 32-bit versions works fine for them, and they don't have the added problems of finding 64-bit drviers for their hardware, and of finding ways to get their browsers to use Flash, Java, and so on.  I personally went with 64-bit, and I don't particularly care if some site that uses Flash for everything doesn't display.. I just go elsewhere.  That's a personal call, so if that matters to you I think you'll find that there is very little performance difference between the 32 and 64 bit systems.  I'd probably suggest starting out with the 32-bit, and if that works out and you find yourself wanting to experiment then you can always go with 64-bit later on.  The Flash and Java problems and things of that sort should be ironed out pretty soon, as most new machines will be 64-bit.

If you really want 64-bit now, there are lots of people who have been able to get things working that way, and who would be happy to help out.  But when you are just starting out, why borrow trouble?  :-)

Hope this helps!

### Post by teddybairs1 on 2007-05-06
i have the same card, the Dell 1390. I'm running an Inspiron B130. It's also known as a Broadcom 4318. Fiesty fawn has the drivers for it, but it needs the firmware package once installed onto the hard disk (licencing issues). I'll attach the firmware package to this post for you.

I highly recommend the dual boot at first. I still dual boot after a little over a year for back up purposes and games. Where Vista is concerned, it may not take kindly to being resized or shifted around, and I know for a fact Microsoft will throw a holy cow if you attempt the CD key on a reinstallation of the OS already present on your machine. The Anti-Piracy controls on Vista are legendary for their inability to stop good hackers, and superiority in busting legitimate users. Your best option is to repartition your hard disk and dual boot with fiesty if you intend to install.

In general, I've found Ubuntu fully supports the Dell hardware, with a little help on the wifi side. The only real loss you'll have is the dial-up modem, assuming yours came with one.
