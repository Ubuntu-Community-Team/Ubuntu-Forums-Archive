The difference on the outset is a visual one. With KDE (Kubuntu) you get something similar to windows in that you have a 'start' button where you can find all your applications on a bar that is at the bottom of your screen. That's really the only comparison that can be made with windows though as everything else such as the way you change settings and other system type operations are different than windows.

Gnome looks a bit more like the default Mac install. But again, all the behind-the-scenes operations are completely different than what you would find on a Mac so the comparison isn't one-to-one on that front either. 

In my opinion, Gnome is simpler and more straightforward. Being new to Linux and Ubuntu specifically I would stick with Gnome if I were you. Besides the simplicity aspect, Canonical (the company behind Ubuntu) started off focusing more on Gnome as the chosen desktop environment (DE) and it seems from my experience that most of the tools to work with your system are better integrated and work more smoothly in Gnome than they do in KDE. KDE is also more of a 'power user' DE that gives you so much control that a new user could easily be bogged down by it's complexity. 

You need to take baby steps with Linux since you are new to it. There is a lot of configuration and customization that can be done in both of the leading DE's but that probably shouldn't be your focus yet. You need to work on understanding the Linux 'way' first. Using Synaptic as the package manager, working from the command line when the need arises, figuring out the file structure of a Linux system and understanding the role of things such as your Home directory and why it's important. 

You've taken the first step in asking your question here in the forums. The Ubuntu community is made of people who 99.9% of the time are very willing to help and are actually interested in making sure that your Linux experience is rewarding and as uncomplicated as possible.

### Post by dannyboy79 on 2008-01-03
well, you insert the cd into your cdrom. if it automounts itself there should be an icon of it on the desktop, just double click it and it should open it. you should be in nautilus and viewing the contents of the cd. if it doesn't automount, then you can mount it with this command

sudo mount /dev/cdrom /mnt/

or

sudo mount /dev/dvd /mnt/

it's basically which ever device your cdrom is recognized as. from there, you could copy the .exe out of the /mnt/ folder by using a command like this

cp /mnt/program.exe /home/username/

that will copy a file called program.exe from the mnt folder to your users home directory. (obviously put your username instead of actually putting username)
