that assumes that the goal actually exists (if your goal is "a distro with forums without even one bad post", that's probably a "no". if your goal is "a linux distro with the best combination of usability and forum quality (and some other stuff)", which i will assume it is, then it is an optimization problem, solving which has certain costs.)

now, assume a population of N linux distros, with various attributes. you evaluate these distros sequentially, one at a time, and give them a customized score based on your preferences. let's say your scoring scale is 0-100, with 100 being perfectly ideal. let us say that you get U points of utility for every 1 point increase in the distro score. let us also say that it costs you E units of effort to evaluate and score a single distro. (measuring effort and utility in the same units). also, for simplicity, assume that you know the probability distribution of the linux distros on your ranking scale. 

As you move along in your evaluations, here is how a "rational" evaluation of whether to proceed with further evaluations or to stop may happen. 

The current distro you are on (say, distro i out of N) gets ranked, and gets some score on your scale of 0-100. Having ranked i distros, the maximum score you have found is S_i (so if you don't find anything better, you'll go to that one). you then look at your known probability distribution for the remaining N-i distros that still have not been evaluated, and calculate the following:

sum(Pr(X_{i+1} = j) * (j-S_i) * U; j=S_i+1 to 100)

(where X_{i+1} is the random variable representing the next distro you are thinking of testing, Pr(X_{i+1} = j) is the probability that the next distro will have a particular score j (greater than S_i), the sum goes from S_i + 1 to 100, each term multiplied by change in score * utility gain per point change). This is in discrete terms (assuming no fractional scores), if it's easier to think in continuous terms, this is like an integral over that section of the distribution, weighted by your expected change in utility.

In all, this gives you the expected increase in your utility from testing the next distro. You will then compare this with the effort you need to expend to test another distro, which is E units. If your expected effort is greater than the expected gain in utility, then it doesn't make sense to keep testing distros.

in more intuitive terms, if your current S_i is, say, 95, then chances are you won't find a better distro very fast, so you might as well stop, since you will expend more effort than you will possibly get in utility. on the other hand, if your current S_i is say, 5, then you can stand to gain a large utility increase with the next evaluation, so it makes sense to do another one. (assuming a distribution with a mean far away from both 95 and 5, somewhere in the middle, basically).

so, the final point to be made is, even if an OS fails your requirements for ideal (ie, doesn't get 100 score), it still may make sense to stop searching if it's "good enough", because the expected search costs outweigh the expected utility gain.

i hope this also demonstrates the need to know the details of your preference set (point costs and benefits), and the weighting you assign to a bad post (as it will affect your score for the distro). not even to mention the need to know your subjective probability distribution assessment for the linux distros, and your current S_i relative to your scoring scale.
