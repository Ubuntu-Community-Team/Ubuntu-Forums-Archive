### Post by Jason_25 on 2006-03-24

Before you do anything, feel the heatsink.  It should burn you at those temps.  If not, the motherboard could be reporting the temps wrong.  If it is hot:

1.  Zip-tie and manage the wires in your case. 
2.  Reapply thermal paste.  Use an extremely thin coating.
3.  Fans and airflow.  You need air pulling in and pushing out.  120mm fans are best.

### Post by Bartender on 2006-03-25
dvarsam -
Dude, you need to get some thermal paste on that PC of yours.  It's supposed to be there.  Thermal paste performs a valuable function, filling in the microscopic scratches & voids between the two metal surfaces.  Without it, only the high points of your CPU and heatsink are making contact, severely degrading heat transfer.  A little tube of Arctic Silver Ceramique or some other good brand will last you a long time.  MaximumPC did a test a few months back, and found that even the cheesy generic thermal paste at Radio Shack worked almost as well as the elite brands.  You only want a little bit of the stuff on your CPU - maybe a little thicker than a coat of latex paint.
  
Anyway, I've applied new and cleaned off old dried out paste several times recently.  Just cleaned off a 5 year old PIII a coupla days ago.  A little dab of good-quality alcohol (try to avoid that 50% water stuff) on a rag and a few seconds of rubbing was all it took to make the CPU look like new.
Chances are you could then turn off about half of those noisy fans.

Check out SPCR.  SPCR's focus is on silent computing, but in the forums you'll find lots of folks who can help you out with cooling questions.  If you have a dual-boot PC, you might want to check out Speedfan, a neat little Windows-compatible program that rounds up all of your thermal, fan speed, and voltage info.  Speedfan won a spot in MaximumPC's Softie Awards last month.

I'd be freakin' if my CPU was running 60 degrees C at idle.  

There are some things to try out, like getting the PC close to the ground where the air should be cooler (but then you'll pick up more dust).  One interesting thing to try is get a small room fan or hair dryer that'll run with the heat off, open up the side of your case and pour the fresh air to it.  See if you can get that temp to drop off.  A small hair dryer type fan can be aimed at certain parts like the North Bridge or just the CPU.  That may be helpful for identifying temps in Speedfan.  Speedfan identified a "Temp 3" in my PC that I still haven't pinned down for sure....

Also, check your temps in BIOS (if it has those functions) and see if it's agreeing with the idle temps you're seeing within Ubuntu or Windows.  If the temp in BIOS is already sky high when the PC has just been turned on that means 1) your diodes are inaccurate or 2) the CPU is transferring heat very poorly and is heating up almost immediately.  If I go right to BIOS the CPU is showing about 21 degrees C and creeps up from there over the next 5 or 10 minutes.

Another thing to look into (apparently easier with AMD than Pentium) is undervolting.  Not the same as underclocking.  SPCR has some good info on this.  It looks like you can drop your VCore (the voltage applied to the CPU) by a tenth or more and lose lots of heat with just a small hit to performance.  This is done in BIOS - some boards support this & some don't.

Another thing you can do to slow down the CPU speed is typing slower.  HAH  Just kidding  :rolleyes:
