### Post by riven0 on 2007-01-06
Out of all of them Xubuntu is going to be the most user friendly. *However*, depending on how old your machine is you may not be happy with the performance. I think it requires, at least, 128mbs of RAM. 

Next up would be Puppy Linux, at least in my opinion. However, it uses the old 2.4 kernel, so it may not detect all your hardware. I'll leave it to someone else to give you the lowdown on DSL. 

But the best thing to do would be to burn the LiveCD's of each one of these distro and test them out yourself. 


BTW, don't forget about Fluxbuntu. From what I know that's even lighter than Xubuntu.

EDIT: Oh yeah, if you have dial-up, your definitely going to want to test the LiveCD, because it can be tricky to set up with Linux.

### Post by jem7v on 2007-01-06
HUGE warning about dial-up modems:

Most dial up modems are not real hardware modems. They are a nasty little thing called "winmodems," which means that they're a meager piece of hardware that lets you plug a phoneline into your computer and Windows does most of the actual work with software provided by the winmodem's manufacturer.  There's no set standard on how they should work and almost all the modem companies are really fussy about not releasing the source for their hardware, so VERY FEW winmodems will work with any semblance of ease in almost any distro of Linux.  So it might be miserable getting a dial-up modem to work in Linux, unless it's a real hardware modem (as opposed to a Winmodem).

(Why did modem companies start doing this? Because it's significantly cheaper to manucacture a card that doesn't do much than a card that actually does its job.  Where I live, new winmodems cost $9 and hardware modems cost $54.)



As for choosing a distro, I've tried DSL and Xubuntu, and Xubuntu was definitely the easier one to use (although DSL ran much faster).  In the end I installed Xubuntu and then installed the Fluxbox desktop manager so that I had the power and ease of installing programs inherent with Ubuntu, but the speed of a Fluxbox desktop. (It's still heavier than DSL, but I think it's easier to use.) And you can always log into a standard Xubuntu session if you need to change a configuration setting you can't find the controls for in Fluxbox.

As for Puppy Linux, I haven't tried it myself, but I've heard it takes a lot of tinkering to get working too.
