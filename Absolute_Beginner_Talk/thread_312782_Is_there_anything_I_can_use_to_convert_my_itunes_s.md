However, if you've **bought** those songs from iTunes music store, they'll be encrypted, and you won't be able to convert them like that.

It's possible that there are programs to break that encryption (I do remember a couple, I think "PlayFair" for example), but
a) I've never needed to, and
b) It may well be illegal for you to do it.  (This depends on where you are).  Since you appear to be in the US, the DCMA has something to say about it: **no, you can't you evil pirate**.  Sure, you may think you've bought the songs, but you'd be wrong.  You've bought a license to play those songs:
1) using iTunes (on up to 3 computers, I think)
2) on your iPod
3) Burnt to some number (I forget) of Audio CDs.

The way around it would be to burn all those tracks to audio CDs, then import those audio CDs.  Stupid, pointless, and degrades quality, but legal. :(

I got bit by the iTunes DRM when my iPod crapped out.  The solution I ended up using was TuneBite.  IT isn't freeware ($15 USD), but it was well worth it to recover my iTUnes files as mp3s, complete with tags.  Basically, it is a modern equivalent of high-speed dubbing.  There is some loss of quality.. but again...   you were screwed the moment you bought iTunes music, you just didn't know it!  :)

You can google TuneBite and find it.  My only complain is that they sent me a lot of "update" advertisements until I unsubscribed from their mailng list.  Of course, this solution would require windows to run TuneBite..   but once you had everything converted, you'd be fine to use Ubuntu 100%.  And that's what we're all really after, right?  :)

### Post by AusIV4 on 2006-12-21
> **gn2 said:**

Does that make it an intrinsically bad argument? The iPod is one of the most versatile music players on the market. It can play normal mp3s or m4as, videos, display pictures, if you want to install alternative firmware, you can get ogg support as well. You can also find a wide variety of sound recorders, FM broadcasters, speaker docks (including some built in to car radios). It does *play* DRMed music, but that's not a restriction since you don't have to have DRM to play your music. I have yet to see a portable music player that offers as much as the iPod.

Windows, on the other hand, has inherent flaws and restrictions. I don't use Windows because I don't like paying for the operating system, then paying again and sacrificing system resources to keep it secure. I don't like having license agreements that tell me what I can and can't do with my computer. I like Linux because I can learn how it works on a source code level, and I can make changes and customize it to suit my needs.

Saying that using an iPod is like using Windows because both have wider support is a horrible argument. Would you quit using Linux if it suddenly supported everything?
