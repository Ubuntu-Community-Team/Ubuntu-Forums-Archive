### Post by LorenzoD on 2005-10-25
KDE and Gnome are both desktop environments. They give you panels, windows, graphical file managers and much more. The two projects have slightly different philosophies, but both are excellent environments. 

The best thing to do is to go to [http://www.kde.org](http://www.kde.org) and [http://www.gnome.org](http://www.gnome.org) and find out more. Then install the two and see which one suits you the best. Or better yet, use both!

### Post by dbott67 on 2005-10-25
As stoeptegel said, these sorts of discussions can become quite heated --- it is the equivalent of which is better: Coke or Pepsi, Ford or Chevy, Windows or Linux (or Mac), Gnome or KDE?

They are both graphical desktop environments with strengths and weaknesses.  I have been trying various distros of Linux since the late 90's but was always somewhat lost in KDE --- I found that it just wasn't intuitive ***to me*** (a long time Windows user).  I could find things, but they weren't always where I expected them to be (from a network administrator's P.O.V --- network settings and the like).  Perhaps this has nothing to do with KDE in particular, but rather the developers of the distribution.

Anyhow, back in March, I was looking for a distro for my wife's old laptop and I decided I wanted to try a distro with a different DE and I happened to stumble upon Ubuntu (and consequently Gnome).  I LOVE UBUNTU!!!  I LOVE GNOME!!!  Again, it may be the Ubuntu developers, rather than Gnome itself, but everything is where I'd expect it to be --- it's very intuitive to me.

I have tried a number of other distros since, including Xandros, Knoppix, slackware, FC4, as well as a number of other desktop environments (Gnome, KDE, XFCE) and Window Managers (blackbox/fluxbox, ICEwm, enlightenment and a couple of others that escape me at the moment), but I must say that Ubuntu/Gnome is by far my favourite.

My most recent experience with KDE was quite positive --- it appears as though many of the administrative tools have been consolidated into one area, making it easy to find & configure the computer.

Take Emerzen's advice and try both.  You can install Ubuntu and then download virtually all the other DE's and WM's from Synaptic to get a feel for them.

### Post by pbw on 2005-10-26
kde is much more easily customizable in my experience.. afaik, you cant tell metacity (gnome window manager) to remember workspace of an app, location, geometry etc etc. Which is very important for any window manager, least to myself.

To me kde has all the perks of any DE/wm except for tabbed windows such as pekwm, flux and ion. But gnome does not have this feature either. (pekwm's by far my favourite in case you're wondering)

That's not to say gnome isnt great for alot of users, because it is. It's just that gnome and kde have different goals and target user bases.. kde = highly customizable, whereas gnome = simplicity and ease of use.
