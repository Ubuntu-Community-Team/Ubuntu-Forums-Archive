Well, honestly we're talking about two different things in this post.  A USB "drive" would imply storage medium (like a hard drive, or a USB flash drive), which a USB-mounted Wi-Fi card is not.

Anything electronic can, of course, burn out.  Maybe something gets too hot, maybe some solder or weld in it isn't quite as strong as it should be.

Now, as far as those little USB drives are concerned, yes, they'll burn out eventually.  You're also correct that the more you use them the faster it'll happen.

The reason is that those little flash drives have an expected lifespan, usually given as the number of "writes" you can expect to perform before it dies.  For those little USB "Flash" drives, this number is far lower than it is for something like a hard drive (whether it's an external, USB hard drive or an internal IDE/SATA/SCSI hard drive).

So, to say it another way, everything electronic will eventually die, and the more power put though it, the faster it dies.

All storage drives eventually die, but "flash" drives die quicker than platter (magnetic) hard drives because they have a far smaller lifespan of "writes".  The other side of this coin is that you're probably not loading the OS that you use every day off of that USB flash drive, so it gets far fewer writes in a given day than a regular hard drive (that you're loading an OS and such) would.

I don't know what you mean about something being designed for a laptop, though-  USB is USB, and the more you use something (generally) the faster it dies.  Perhaps that means I've missed your point completely.  If so, sorry. :)
