### Post by 3rdalbum on 2007-01-20
First thing:

The multiple workspaces feature was NOT copied off the Mac OS. It's been around on Linux and Unix for over a decade, whereas Apple only implemented it last year.

SLED (SUSE Linux Enterprise Desktop) can still be used for free, you just won't get support or access to security updates. If you want security updates, you could use OpenSUSE (which is usually just called SUSE). Or Ubuntu, with the Ubuntu System Panel installed to make it behave like SLED's Applications menu.

Corel Linux is now Xandros.

Firefox is built-in to most Linux distributions. There is Rar support available on Linux. iTunes isn't a very good program - Amarok is much better. PowerDVD is alright but there are many alternatives for Linux. I've never used DVDshrink, but k9copy does the same thing and works great on Linux.

Some of the commercial programs you can get running on Wine, which is a compatibility layer for Windows programs, but you could also install Windows into a virtual machine. Virtualbox ([www.virtualbox.org](www.virtualbox.org)) is a fast, free virtualisation solution for Linux that lets you run any operating system in a window.

Unlike Windows, Linux is fully themable. There are a couple of themes included with Ubuntu, but there are thousands of user-contributed themes available at [www.gnome-look.org](www.gnome-look.org) for you to download and use for free. If I were you, I'd be worrying less about what a distro's default looks like, and more about getting some live CDs and trying out the system.

### Post by billingsworld on 2007-01-21
Well.  I am not a Mac guy but I can speak to the whole "which distro is right for me because I just went through that dilema."

As a new linux person, the right distro will be the one that installs recognizing the most of your gear.  In my case, ubuntu recognized everything on my system (it even gave me two floppy drives when I only have one! - sweet! lol), and I could start printing right away.

Linspire was a close 2nd.  I found it to be the most Mac like (even though it is tailored for windows), mostly because everything is closed down - the distro tries to give you everything you could possibly need in one shot.  Problem is, the distro isn't current with the standard apps so, it would have required updating software and I didn't want to have to take a crash course in that.

I tried Open SUSE 10.2, it has, in my opinion, the best installer for a techie that is loading up Linux for the first time.  Lots of information, everything is explained in detail.  Problem is, I coudn't get my printers working off the bat and the video resolution was not very good off the get go.

Then, there is the whole concept of support.  Ubuntu has the most active support forum of any of the "free distro's"  The community is very active, so active that some people trip over others in their posting - it's common to see two different people post an answer at the same time....  So, in short, here is why I choose 6.10 Ubuntu

- 6.10 - upon install, the software was pretty current
- I could get up and working right away
- I wasn't forced to learn advanced linux stuff right away to get the system working, I can take my time and go at my own pace.

As far as your applications are concerned.  Well, that's a problem.  The basic app's in linux are very good.  Don't kid yourself, OpenOffice is not as intuitive as Office 2003.  But, if you don't need the Office help system and you don't have a lot of documents with specialized macros, than OpenOffice will do whatever you need.

Gimp is very powerful and it could easily take over your Adobe image tools.  I don't know photoshop or gimp very well, so, I can't really comment. I do know what GIMP can do and it looks on paper like it can do anything Photoshop can do.

If you have used an emulator in the past on a Mac, you know how horrible they can be.  Using VMware to virtualize a windows desktop is not emulating (as far as I know) and that might be your best bet.  I plan to install VMWare at a later time although I have heard Edgy has had some difficulties with VMWare (blackberry devices) and Dapper + VMWare works better without the need to tinker.  I have gone cold turkey from Windows XP and do not have dual boot.

Before you take the plunge, I recommend installing the Windows versions of GIMP and OpenOffice and use them for a month or two before converting.  This helped immensly for me as Linux can be a little overwhelming at times.

I read a comment from another posting somewhere that, I think, sums up linux well:

Linux is not "Free Windows," it's different and all those years you spent learning Windows will make Linux a little frustrating and a little daunting at times - be patient and try to learn the new way.

### Post by Ennead on 2007-01-22
Listen to Billingsworld.

Here's a summary of the path I've trod.   You may find it helpful.  Try several Live CD distros.  Seven was my magic number.  Don't be afraid to try more, if necessary; it's a great adventure!  Pay close attention to the ones that work the best on your hardware right out of the box.  

Since that can only take you so far, you'll have to install a few distros next.  To ease my concerns about trashing my good computer, I reloaded an older computer (they're pretty easy to find these days) with about 15 distros, some of which worked great, others of which didn't work at all.  That's when I discovered the wonderful people at the Ubuntu Forums.  That went a LONG way toward making Ubuntu my distro of choice.  

Then, try a dual boot wih the distro of your choice.  At this point, I had the field narrowed down to (4) candidates: Ubuntu, Mepis, Linspire and SuSe.  Share your data between Windows and Linux.  That way, you can work on copies of exactly the same files to see what Linux can do.  Also, using a Windows version of Open Office, or other open source software, is also an excellent intermediate step.

At this point you can stay with dual boot forever.  That's what I had intended to do.  But the last sector on my HD went bad, and for some reason Windows couldn't tolerate that, but Ubuntu could.  So now my computer is exclusively Ubuntu, and it's working just fine.  

Well, I hope this has been helpful.  But, if I may emphasize one more point...

Listen to Billingsworld.

### Post by stengah on 2007-01-28
The major problem from my pov is that you rely on adobe/macromedia suites as a graphics production platform. None of those have been adapted to the Linux platform which is a darn shame!

Don't dispair! You basically have 3 options:

1. GO FOR NATIVE ALTERNATIVES

Basically, you get some promising alternatives on linux, however some stuff  is tough to work around. 

Photoshop: Pixel or Gimp 
Illustrator: Xara Xtreme or Inkscape
Dreamweaver: Bluefish or NVU

I think a lot of these alternatives are really great. Some even superior. However I realize you need others as well as right now I cannot recommend any alternatives for e.g. Flash or Acrobat Pro - they simply do not exist. I know work is being done on some Flash projects however they're in their infancy, and as far as Adobe Pro goes I don't know of any alternatives at all! 

2. MAKE MACROMEDIA/ADOBE APPS WORK BY USING WINE

Search these forums for more info on wine. There's an excellent guide as well!

Actually I've did manage to get Flash 8 working through wine basically by copying the necessary dirs from a windows install into wine, but haven't used it enough to estimate how stable it is. Photoshop 7 should be quite stable, and I suppose CS2 may work as well. I plan to post a howto if it turns out stable enough. 

In my experiencee the biggest disadvantage by wine in general is lack of stability. I do think you can get thing up an running in a pretty decent state, particularly older versions. Nevertheless, I am sort of hesitant as to whether wine is suitable for providing a production environment for windows apps. I know I do not rely on it - at least not as of right now. Other may have more experience than me on this part...

Furthermore, my experience is that this solution is going to require quite some patience and time in terms of making it work. At least 'till you get a hang of it!

3. USE VMWARE TO EMULATE WINDOWS AND RUN WINDOWS APPS

This is in my experience the best solution by far. 

Basically you can run vmware server which is a free product that allows you to install other operating system on your linux distro. Search for vmware on my blog in my signature below - it'll give you a howto and a link back to an updated howto in these forums.  In real life terms this means that once installed vmware will be located in the menu system (look under 'Programs' ->'System') and from here you can basically install and run any 'system within your system' which e.g. allows you to eg.g run windows xp with Adobe CS2 along with the macromedia stuff you need. I have used and tested this solution for those apps for about a year now, and if everything is properly installed it runs absolutely flawlessly. You can setup Samba to share files between your VMWare windows enviroment and your Linux environment and enjoy the best of both worlds! 

Otherwise you need to dualboot... !

I won't lie to you... if you're a newbie getting vmware server running may require a little bit of stubbornness and some time on your hands. Nonetheless, it's managable by the excellent help you'll find in these forums.

Overall a beginner distros I'd go for Ubuntu/Kubuntu, SUSE or Fedora, but other than that it's a matter of taste and preferences. You need to experience for yourselves. Anyway, that's what so great and exiting about open source imo... the freedom to choose. Now you need to decide... do I like choices so much?! ;-)

Anyway, hope this helps out!
