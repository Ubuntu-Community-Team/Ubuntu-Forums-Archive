### Post by tgalati4 on 2007-03-13
You have several things to consider:

Make a dedicated swap partition (1-5 GB).  This is helpful if you want to run another live distribution off the CD in the future (to test it out).  If you use the automatic partitioning, you sometimes get a virtual /swap partition that future Live CD's can't see.

Get yourself a cheap USB notebook drive enclosure with a decent-sized notebook drive.  Every 3 months, mirror the notebook drive to the USB drive so that you have a bootable Linux drive in the event of failure of the internal drive.

This keeps all of your customization, backs up your user-generated data, and allows you to either boot is externally as a backup to fix things, or you can pop it in when your internal drive fries.

Older laptop drives tend to fail when being worked hard with a professional operating system such a Linux.  In otherwords, you could only have a few tasks running simulateously under Windows before it would lock up.  Under Linux you can have hundreds of things going simultaneously (like the user who opens 150 browser tabs--seriously).

Older drives can't really take this abuse.  Some people use notebooks to record music.  Again, notebook drives aren't really good at continuous writes.  The heads heat up and warp--trashing the drive.

So Linux opens up a lot of capability that was locked behind Windows.  Just realize that your hardware now becomes the limiting factor--good battery power, decent hard disk, reliable main and video RAM.

You will soon discover what is not up to snuff.

Welcome to the community.  Good luck.

Go to the Dell website and take the Linux survey.  Dell may be including Linux on new laptops soon.
