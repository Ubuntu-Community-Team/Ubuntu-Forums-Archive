### Post by MikeMLP on 2006-08-14
I'm a bit confused.  You say:

> I am going to upgrade instead of filling the money pit with more cash.

Yet, if your motherboard is dead, you'll need to get a new motherboard, and if your CPU is dead you'll need a new CPU.  The problem with this is that your motherboard and CPU are several years old, and your other parts will probably work best with a motherboard / CPU that is of about the same age.  Your best option, if upgrading your existing parts is what you have in mind, is to purchase used equipment that just about match the parts that you lost.

If, however, you wish to buy a completely new computer
> I would like any advice about about what type of computer to purchase
You could build one yourself, if you fell comfortable doing so by selecting parts that should work well with linux (other forum members should be able to tell you how to do this, as I havn't done this myself)  I hear that IBM (Now Lenovo) is offering SUSE Linux pre-loaded on (at least some) of its Laptops and Desktops

[http://www.desktoplinux.com/news/NS7778908329.html]("http://www.desktoplinux.com/news/NS7778908329.html")

so, IBM/Lenovo is probably about the safest bet with respect to buying something from an OEM.

Good luck!

Edit: It would definitely be much better to go new (even with a cheap machine) if you can afford it.

### Post by aeiah on 2006-08-14
try and track down a wireless card that has ubuntu (debian) linux drivers for it (preferably drivers that are already included in ubuntu). perhaps do a forum search to see which wireless cards work out of the box with ubuntu. if you want wireless that is.

with graphics cards, nvidia is a bit more compatible, and although you say you wont be using it for video editing or gaming, you may still want a standalone card, and in which case nvidia is probably the way to go.

everything else should be fine no matter what standard components you choose.
