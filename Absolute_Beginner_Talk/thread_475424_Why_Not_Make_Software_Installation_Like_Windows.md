### Post by carusoswi on 2007-06-16
If one went looking for shortcomings in Ubuntu, being dependent upon the internet might be one of them.  Ubuntu without internet (or without a decently quick connection) is like pancakes without syrup, spring without fall, and the rest of the song.

I use my computer (a small form factor desktop) away from home five days a week.  When I first discovered Ubuntu, I'd be happy as a clam at home on weekends with my FIOS (fiber optic) internet connection - very fast, very dependable.  Then, I'd have to leave the connection behind and hope to be able to work with my computer and Ubuntu in whatever state it was in when I left the house, because I didn't have an internet connection during the week.

That situation grew old very quickly.  I now carry a wireless adapter that allows me access when I am away.  At first, the wireless adapter only worked with Windows (argh!!), but, for me, upgrading to 7.04 cured that problem, and, now, I am once again, happy as a clam.

I am certain the OP was answering his/her question as he/she posed it . . . if Ubuntu were like Windows, it would be Windows.

My gut reaction is to flame the OP for such a remark, but, generally, we don't behave that way around here.  All of us were probably in your shoes at one time, bme.  Hopefully, you have installed Ubuntu as a dual boot so that you can still get back to Windows during the transition.  Eventually, you will get the hang of Ubuntu - and, perhaps you can do something about that internet connection.  If you are running a laptop, I would think that could be fairly simple.  Install a wireless adapter if you don't already have one and go to some location where wireless connections are available (many for free).

That's when you will begin to really appreciate Ubuntu and the Linux setup.  With a good internet connection, the solutions to most problems are but a click or two away.  Often, you can sign on to this forum, post the specifics of your program, and someone more knowledgeable will post back a solution complete with terminal commands that you can cut and paste and be on your way.

I have come to appreciate Ubuntu and Linux more as I use it more.  If it were capable of nothing else, Ubuntu is a more pleasing OS from which to browse the internet than any WinOS I have ever used.  You can browse for hours without ever being interrupted by popup, free from spy ware, slow downs, etc - no nagging reminders popping up and interrupting you to check or allow a check for updates, antivirus aps spinning your discs without warning you first, and no worries that some criminal is trying to pry into your system.

Hang in there.  We all know it can be very frustrating at first.  Try to remember that you probably have a decade or more of Windows indoctrination in your head.  Think back (if your experience dates back that far) to the days when Windows came on a handful of floppies that you had to install one by one - sometimes it worked, other times it crashed.

I don't dislike Windows - in a less malicious world, XP would be a fine system.  It's stable (for me) and quite capable, and, like you, I am very familiar with the way it works.  Unfortunately, it costs a lot of money, its use is very restricted, you pay for almost every application (dearly - I have a lot invested in Windows software), and, whenever the need for more revenue beckons, Microsoft will wage a war of obsolescence - as they have with Vista (they did it with XP, too).  

I have software packages LICENSES for which that I paid $600 - $1000 that have run fine on W2k and XP.   They will not even install on Vista, so my choices are to purchase new licenses (when, if updates to those expensive applications are ever released), stay with my old OS (and ignore the warnings from the computer mfr that my new system's warning is voided if I install other than the OS for which the system was explicitly designed), or simply stop using those expensive programs.

Ubuntu and Linux have a long way to go in order to totally supplant the Windows (and, to a lesser degree, MAC) OS for most users.  But, MAC has always been expensive and software for it even more limited and expensive, and MS may have just gone to the well one time too many with its dictatorial "upgrade" practices.

Vista is no improvement over XP, runs more slowly, is more personally intrusive, and forces the user to abandon many aps that simply won't run in the new OS.

I didn't mean to ramble on so (actually was primed to flame you - but, these forums tend to temper that temptation.

Hang in there.  Keep using your Windows OS as your "get work done" OS until you become more familiar with Ubuntu - for me that day is still some ways off.  All my office work is done in Windows - many of my key business applications will not run in Linux (YET!!).

It may be a while, but, I am confident that Open Source, at least in terms of operating systems, will one day overtake the proprietary systems upon which we presently rely.

Caruso
