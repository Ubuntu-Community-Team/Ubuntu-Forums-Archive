### Post by Mustard on 2006-03-26
It's basically two 'schools' of thought on the desktop environment.  In a nutshell, KDE is about 'configurability', with all the configurability options up front and visible, whereas Gnome is about usability, where much of the configuration is hidden away and the basic usable functions are visible.

If you like having the ability to configure everything on your system then KDE is the way to go.  If you like to just use your interface and not concern yourself too much with configureability then Gnome is good for that.

The configureability of KDE is not something to be worried about, as you are not forced to configure it, but if you do you will be presented with a lot of options.

If you open up a gnome menu, you will see just the basic stuff you need to do something.  If you open up a KDE menu you will be presented with similar options and a plethora of other options you could use as well.

KDE is more windows like in its setup too, with a large button on the bottom left that opens up a large menu of options and sub-menus.  Gnome has the options at the top left in three seperate menus.

KDE also has some applications that are unique to it, like Konquerer (an integrated browser, file browsing application), and a quite detailed music library application, Amarok.  I don't know of any equivalent to Konquerer in gnome.  Some gnome apps are being developed that are similar to Amarok, but not quite the same (yet).

I don't find KDE as intuitive as gnome.  Half the fun of KDE I think we be actually exploring the menus and options trying to find all the things you can do.  For myself I am happy to have the most functional options present and easy to find, which is what gnome does best.
