### Post by aysiu on 2005-06-12
Some of the differences? Hm. Well, first of all, just about anything you want to do, you can do. Now, those things may not be done *easily*, but you can do them. By "things you want to do," I'm talking about changing the splash screen, the icons, the programs, user access to folders, what your keyboard shortcuts are... just about any preference you can think of.

The caveat, of course, is that some of the things you may want to do, especially things involving proprietary software (commerical DVD decoding, MP3 encoding, etc.), do not come standard out-of-the-box for Ubuntu (or for many other free Linux distributions). You have to consult the [Ubuntu Guide](http://www.ubuntuguide.org) for all of that. The instructions aren't difficult. In fact, they are usually quite the display of electronic hand-holding. They can be a bit intimidating for people not used to the command-line, though. All I can say about that is just type whatever they tell you to type!

Another thing that's different is the community. I personally love Mepis--it's my favorite distribution software-wise--but I also have a Ubuntu partition because I love the Ubuntu community. This morning, I was a bit frustrated with not being able to edit the Gnome applications menu. Within ten minutes, I had all the answers I needed. That's a response time! Try getting that kind of response when you have a problem with a Microsoft product...

There are a lot of little quirks every now and then. The problem is really that most people's computers are tailored for Windows. That's right--it's not that Windows is tailored to the computer; it's vice versa. Linux distributions like Ubuntu, on the other hand, have to be jacks of all trades, being able to conform to the specifications of all these computers designed specifically for Windows. That why, when you read these forums or other Linux forums, almost always the questions are "Why won't my computer boot?" "Such-and-such doesn't recognize my wireless card. How do I fix it?" "What do I have to do to fix my screen resolution?"

Unless someone configures Linux for you (and once it's configured, it usually runs quite smoothly), you have to be willing to get down-and-dirty. I've spent many, many hours getting Ubuntu just right for me and my computer. I know all about editing /etc/fstab and /etc/apt/sources.list and /boot/grub/menu.lst and /etc/X11/xorg.conf. Once you become familiar with the structure of Linux and its command line, it becomes super-easy.

It's the same kind of work I had to do to fix my Windows XP when it got infested with spyware last summer. Suddenly I became an expert in deleting registry keys, cleaning out .dlls, and disabling system restore. Before that, I didn't even know what the registry was. People get to know computers when they have to. And you're going to have to do a little legwork for Ubuntu. You'll have lots of help, though, and if you approach it with the right attitude, you might actually learn something.

The live CD will not hurt your hard drive. It operates off the CD itself and your computer's RAM. Go with it. Try it out. In fact, you may even want to try several distros. I recommend Mepis as well.
