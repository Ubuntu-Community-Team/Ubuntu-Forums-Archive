### Post by harisund on 2006-08-25
Hello everyone!

I have been hanging out in these forums for quite a while now, and enjoy just reading the threads irrespective of whether or not I am able to contribute myself. 

However, I have also observed a lot of stuff, and thought I would come up with a bunch of new rules that, if followed by new posters, would help everyone. 

1. First and foremost, stop considering yourself n00b, newbie or whatever. You are not going to get any extra consideration if you are one. Everybody in the forums are extremely helpful and I have very hardly seen flamewars. You say something wrong, someone is going to politely correct you. You say something stupid, someone is going to tell you why that is wrong. You say something hopelessly wrong, someone is going to inform you the right way to do it. 
Remember, we were all n00bs at one point of time. Personally, I mainly try and help out in the servers, networking and such sections, since I only access my Ubuntu remotely. I am still a n00b when it comes to something lke installing mp3 support. 

2. Please read [this writeup](http://www.catb.org/~esr/faqs/smart-questions.html) on how to ask questions the smart way. It goes a long way for the person helping you if he/she knows more about your problem. Describe as much as you can. Paste log entries. On the IRC channel if you paste an entry they will ban you, but here rest assured every line you write will be read. 

As a matter of fact, when you go to great detail in explaining the problem, it is very likely you will hit the answer yourself. 

And yes, nobody here will tell you "go google it." 

3. Something quite important, give an interesting subject title that describes the problem. Going back, 'n00b alert', 'please help', 'now what?' 'Ubuntu is not working'... of course, it doesn't mean nobody will look at your post, but a better title will increase the chances of your question being answered faster and efficiently. 

4. SEARCH !!! SEARCH !!!
Trust me, you will be really amazed at how much depth of information these forums have. 

Personally, I have found the documentation to be a bit monotonous. Most of the Ubuntu documentation is essentially "do this. Copy this. paste that. do this. type this. open this" and bam! you are done. That is a poor howto. However if you look at the forums, the lively discussions are far better. 

Have problems with the famous root account? Don't bother googling it on google. Google it on the forums ;). The forums staff are trying as hard as they can to make searching easier. There is now a big search form right on top. 


5. Reply back. 

I can not tell you how annoying it is to hit a thread where the poster has *exactly* the same problem you are having, and then the next post is "never mind. I solved it. Thanks for your help though." If you did something elaborate, perhaps you deserve a HOWTO under your name. If you did a small hack, just mentioning it will be awesome.

I have seen so many posts myself where the last entry was made months back, and then suddenly somebody comments saying "thanks for that. I dropped here searching for so-and-so, and your tip was just what I needed." 

6. Share the love. 

Everyday, I see atleast a couple of posts made by new users (0 previous posts.) And then these users are never heard of. 

Of course, I don't meant to say that every user should contribute, but trust me the feeling is amazing. If you really are a Ubuntu convert as you claim, or have been using Ubuntu for a long time, the feeling is really great when you see someone with a question you yourself had months ago when you first installed Ubuntu. And when you post the solution to that, and the original poster posts back with the thanks, you have just enriched your Ubuntu experience. 


Actually, you can ignore everything I just mentioned. The thing is, I am just way too deeply in love with Ubuntu. I use Gentoo and Fedora, and you can see posts made by me in both the forums and me hanging out on both IRC channels. But personally, after Ubuntu has come along I have grown more attached to the computer and I find the forums/IRC are the best way to contribute (short of coding/debugging, which I will eventually)... personally I don't like writing documentation/manuals/books etc, since there is no human touch. It's more like giving instructions to a robot. In a forum, it's as if you are actually talking to someone. 

The feeling of knowing that someone out there in the world, completely a stranger to you, is ready to help you with an operating system, completely free to you, is just awesome, don't you think?
