### Post by az on 2005-10-21
Forgive me if I hammer away at this...



There are two ways to look at softrware.


One way is to say that a programmer works for hours to write code, and that work is her property, or the property of the company for whom she wrote the code.

The other is to say that software is a collection of ideas and algoritms that nobody can claim as their own, since anybody is capable of writing them or something very similar.  By nature of the fact that all software is just an extension of existing ideas (or existing software), it is not fair to say that another programmer who is faced with the same problem is bound to come up with a very different solution.

Add to that the impracticality of binary-only software which is much like buying a car with the hood welded shut to prevent you from being able to change your own oil.  You end up with software developer who stingly beleive in free (as in libre) software.

The licence under which most of the applications Ubuntu are under is called the GPL, which obliges the author to

allow the applicationto be distributed
oblige that the source code for the application be available
allow others to read and change that source code (improve the software)
oblige those who distribute modifided versions of the software under the same terms.

Which means that the GPL obliges the software to remain free.

And free means freedom, but it ususally is distributed at no cost, as well.  Paying for software is not really that popular.  If it is GPL and you charge for it, there is nothing to prevent someone from getting the source code, compiling it and distributing that for free.

Open-source developers make their modey by writing code, but not by selling the code.  Both proprietary and free-open-source developers can get paid for their work.  At the end of the day, though, the free-open source developer still has more ownership of their code than the one working for a proprietary company.

Another big way to make money from open source is to provide support.  Again, you are not paid for the applications, but you are paid to keep everytyhing running.
