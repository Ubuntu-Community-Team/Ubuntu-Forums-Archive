### Post by jtibau on 2006-05-11
the deal about a lossy compression format as ogg-vorbis, is that uses much less space to store the sound (music most likely)...
As said you loose part of the information of the original sample, but it is supposed to be stuff human ears can't hear anyway, like ultra frequencies and such.... mp3 is the most famous lossy compression format for music. ogg-vorbis is supposed to be better as it is a newer codec, I think it is OSS too.
FLAC is a lossles codec, which means that you keep everything and when you uncompress the file it is exactly what your microphones recorded. However files are larger.

### Post by Kvark on 2006-05-11
Use ogg or mp3 if you are just going to listen to the music. Converting to lossy once won't be noticeable. But never use a lossy file as the source for a new file. Use the old king mp3 (mp3 is patented) if you want to play your music on any digital audio player. Use the new challenger ogg (ogg is open source) if you want slightly better quality and are prepared to limit your choices to only players with ogg support.

Use flac (flac is open source) if you are going to burn the music to CDs, encode it to other formats or edit it to make remixes or something. Always use lossless for these things to keep the quality. If you use lossy here then it gets noticeable when you lose quality over again each time you encode a new version of the file.

### Post by stairwayoflight on 2006-08-06
hi,

i am not a sound engineer, but i thought some of the dribble here was worth a comment ;-)

if i am not mistaken, cd audio records at ~44khz. that means it the sound is sampled 44 thousand times per second. for most purposes, this is considered sufficient.

i am not saying the human ear can hear higher, (in terms of frequency) but there is also the idea of wave definition. to get a clear sound, you don't just need to represent the frequency of a wave but its shape. think of those old coloring books where you "connect the dots." if the dots map around a curved surface (eg. a teddy bear's head) the series of straight lines eventually look like a curve if they are close enough together.

well lets pretend you draw circles with dots, each dot 1cm apart. the larger the circle, the more it will look like a circle and not just some n-sided polygon. as it gets smaller, the need for the dots to be closer together becomes obvious. eventually you end up with a triangle, smaller than that and you get only one dot. the dots have lost the ability to define the shape.

when you look at sound waves, higher frequencies are like the smaller circles. to start to hear the shape of a wave, you probably need at least 5 times the frequency of that wave. (think.. 5 dots is approaching enough definition to see a circle)

higher quality audio such as that afforded through formats like sony's super audio cd and the dvd audio disc don't just offer more music, more tracks (eg. 7.1 surround) but they are capable of delivering a higher sample rate. a philips red book cd will never accurately resemble the depth of tone from a concert violin performance. it simply isn't possible.

human hearing extends approximately to 20khz. for optimum quality, audio should be sampled at least 20khz * 5 = 100khz. therefore the audio cd standard at a mere 44khz does not deliver when it comes to the top of the human hearing range.

for general purposes, lossless may be ok. like if you have a crappy stereo. or you listen to really distorted electronic stuff anyway (i love it). but if representing the original sound counts, lossless is the way to go.

all that to say, cd's are an old technology and consumers shouldn't be paying anything for that crap anymore anyways. they literally are a dime a dozen. unfortunately we're stuck with them for a while. so in my opinion, *USE LOSSLESS*. you've lost so much by the time the cd dropped into your hands, why would you lose any more?

that goes double for all you paying $.99 for a "high quality" .mp3/.aac tune!!!

Hi Stairway,

Don't know whether anyone is still reading this thread, but I'm compelled to jump in here.  Actually, 44.1 kHz *is* sufficient to represent frequencies up to the observed limit of human hearing (~20 kHz), because it only takes two sample points per period to represent a wave at a particular frequency.  What you call the "shape" of the wave is actually the result of the superposition of higher frequencies, and we can't hear those.  In other words, 5 points per cycle is overkill -- all that extra information is being poured into inaudible frequencies.  See [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyquist-Shannon](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyquist-Shannon) for more information.

I had this discussion years ago on an audio engineering list, and somebody suggested that even though we can't "hear" those ultrasonic frequencies, we may register them subconsciously as imparting a richness to the sound.  That may actually be true, since the 20 kHz limit is based on a "loudness contour," which basically charts how loud a sound needs to be at a given frequency in order for humans to hear it (see [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_Threshold_of_Hearing)](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_Threshold_of_Hearing)).  I've never seen the actual data, but it should be theoretically possible to amplify an ultrasonic signal until we CAN hear it.  (Then again, at such high amplitudes, the question would arise whether we were actually hearing sound at that frequency, or some difference tone produced by ambient reflections or our inner ear!)

The main point is that 44.1 kHz is enough, and CDs are pretty great!  \\:D/  I suspect that most of the difference in perceived quality between CDs and the newer hi-def audio formats is due to higher bit depth rather than wider frequency response, but that's another story.
