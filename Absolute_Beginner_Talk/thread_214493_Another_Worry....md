### Post by Miguel on 2006-07-12
Just to make a point, there ARE firewalls in Linux. It's known iptables. It's a bit difficult to configure from the command line, but there exist graphical utilities that help us in doing simple tasks with it.

There are also antivirus software... though they scan for windows viri. The thing is, you might get hacked (difficult if you leave no ports open and run on a restricted privilege account, wich is default in ubuntu) but you won't get a virus. At least not today or tomorrow. 

About spyware and adware, just imagine how less that threat would be if windows users didn't browse the net in administrator mode. Well, that happens in linux (and Mac OS X and the BSD's). There is a reason why Vista is pushing towards limited privilege accounts.

Anyway, why don't you download a live CD and give it a try? Just remember that things *do* work differently, so try to keep an open mind. You don't lose much (a couple of hours maybe?) by burning the Dapper desktop CD in a CD-RW and giving it a try.

> 
Complacency is the easiest target


That's why security updates aren't hold until first tuesday next month in linux (or BSD). If they are ready, the patches are released. However, you are right in that we shouldn't believe we are invulnerable.

Imagine if you went to the service department at the Ford dealership.  Would you say, "I'm worried about buying a Ford.  Look at all these cars with problems."  Of course not, because you expect to see problems in a service area.  Same principle here.

> **Simon Berry said:**

Let's again take ourselves out of the present.  You've lived your entire life in the deepest jungle.  You move to Portland, Oregon in the US.  You're totally shocked at the lack of concern that the locals show toward tiger attacks and poisonous snakes in the trees.  On closer inspection, you find that they don't worry about it, because in their part of the world, those things simply aren't a threat.

There are certainly threats, but running firewall or a NAT enabled router, keeping the patches updated, and not installing software from shaky sources will nicely protect the desktop user.  Servers that accept outside connections must pay more attention, of course.
