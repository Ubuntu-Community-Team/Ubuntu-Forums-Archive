### Post by annsachd on 2006-02-11
I ran Gentoo for quite awhile and my complaint would be the compiling. If you can spare the time it takes great. But if you need your computer to get work done, then it gets kind of frustrating.

Also, since Gentoo is a bit more bleeding edge you can have your system come to a grinding halt if they don't get things just right with all their updates. It hit me a few times. Once it took about a week for them to figure out what had gone wrong.

I've only been using Ubuntu about two weeks and I got it running just about perfectly within one week. Now I'm doing the work that I typically do on my Mac at home on my PC running Ubuntu. It's a fine distro with really helpful people in the forums too.

My guess is that you'll be getting Ubuntu to at least try it. BTW I sent away for my free disks and they got here in Los Angeles in about two weeks.

### Post by UnrulyGrrl99 on 2006-02-12
I am a "former" Gentoo user myself.  I got fed up with all the time wasted compiling things mainly.  I decided to try out Ubuntu by getting a VPS from RimuHosting.  They just started offering Ubuntu as one of their distros (sorry no Gentoo though).

I liked how a complete system was up and running with php, apache, mysql, etc
and it took up little space (you only get 4gb with the cheapest plan @ Rimu).  I have another VPS with them that is Redhat but got totally fed up with that.

Since I have converted my laptop at home and my desktop at work to Ubuntu over the last week, I VERY much appreciate precompiled packages.  No more coming to a screeching halt installing something if a piece didnt want to compile for whatever reason.  No more tapping out your CPU or waiting and waiting for the programs to be compiled and installed.

Also, Ubuntu gets you started with Gnome, but you don't have to use it.
Bonus there is that you have GUIs for lots of stuff you wouldnt have with
a base Gentoo system.  Wireless settings for example.  I had no idea where
those settings were, in some flatfile somewhere, but I just called up network-admin and was up and running.  Ubuntu didn't have any problems supporting  my integrated wireless 802.11g.    Ubuntu asked me a few questions during the initial setup, and every piece of hardware in my Dell Latitude D600 was supported and configured when I got the login screen for the first time. No muss no fuss

It's all a matter of personal preference when it comes down to it.  
I don't know how well supported the AMD64 platform is within Ubuntu, so it may not work out for you.  I *highly* recommend installing Ubuntu on a different partition and keep your Gentoo until you have worked with Ubuntu for a few weeks.
