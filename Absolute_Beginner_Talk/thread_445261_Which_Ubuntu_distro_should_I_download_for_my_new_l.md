### Post by H.E. Pennypacker on 2007-05-15
I think he meant whether he should go with Ubuntu, Kubuntu, or Xubuntu.  

If it is a brand new computer, you probably don't want Xubuntu, unless you like its simplicity.  Xubuntu is usually used by people with older computers.

The only real difference between Ubuntu and Kubuntu is the desktop environment.  If you are brand new to Linux, this means nothing to you, and it probably won't make sense.  Anyhow, check out various screenshots, and see which one appeals to you more.  For me, I like the fact that Ubuntu is clean and organized (Gnome).  Kubuntu uses KDE, which I find to be heavily bloated, but many others swear by it.

This problem can be easily solved by looking at screenshots.

I do recommend, however, as a newbie, you use Ubuntu.  More people use Ubuntu than Kubuntu, and if you need any help, it is better to speak the language of the majority (Ubuntu, not Kubuntu).

### Post by H.E. Pennypacker on 2007-05-16
> **Irishpolyglot said:**
> 
 But what's this about flash incompatibilities?

Just as a general rule, as a Linux user, you should expect a delay in support from companies like Adobe (which has acquired Macromedia Flash).  Currently, our version of Flash is on the same page as Windows, but it took some time to get there.

Anyhow, for now, you won't have to worry about this.

>  Should I get the 
"Standard personal computer (x86 architecture, PentiumTM, CeleronTM, AthlonTM, SempronTM)

 
 64bit AMD and Intel computers
 
 or the Sun UltraSPARC based" one?[/QUOTE]

In your case, you should be picking "Standard personal computer." 

> 
Are you saying my computer can handle the 64bit one, but I'm better getting the standard to avoid any incompatibility issues? 

Regardless of which operating system you use, 64bit computers are less supported than 32bit computers, because of they aren't as popular.  But in your case, you don't have an option, unless you buy a new processor.

> 
Oh, and while I have your attention, should I partition my drive right now while the computer is new? 

Ideally, you would do exactly that.  It is better to partition now, because there isn't that much on the computer that is worth protecting just in case something goes wrong.  Partitioning a hard drive will go fine most of the time, but as with any serious changes, backups are always recommended.  Fortunately, even  though I have never backed up my hard drive before partitioning, I haven't lost anything.  I have been lucky.  Don't try to do the same.  Anyhow, there shouldn't be anything important on your hard drive at this time anyway, so don't worry about a loss.

> 
Is there any thread about partitioning I should read since I really have no idea how to do it? Obviously I don't care about losing data, as long as Windows still works after if I reinstall it.

Well, Windows IS data.  If something went wrong, your Windows partition could be messed up or gone.  This usually happens with people who either don't know what they are doing, or don't pay enough attention during the partitioning part of installation.

There are various guides.  You should visit the following two pages for more information:

[http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/partitioning](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/partitioning)

[http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/installing](http://www.psychocats.net/ubuntu/installing)

Go through other parts of that website too.  It's a useful website.  

> P.S. :-P I've used Vista for a couple of months on other computers and it's such a disaster that I'm completely converted to Ubuntu without knowing much about it lol :-P (it's impressive if you consider my religious following of Windows since 3.1...

I have a few words of advice.  Before you decide to try out Ubuntu, look around and see what this community is like.  Do forum searches relating to your laptop (Inspiron 9400) and see what problems others may have had.  Do your homework and make sure everything, or nearly everything, is compatible with Linux.  Do searches on other hardware too (printers, cameras, etc.).  By doing your homework first, you know what to expect.  If you don't, Ubuntu could become a nightmare for you.

Check out the support forums.  You'll notice that much of the help given uses the command line interface (the terminal).  If that scares you, you're going to have a hard time using Linux, because the CLI is the language that most people use.  Help is available using only GUI, but you would have to specifically request such help.

Know what to expect from Ubuntu so that you will not have too high of expectations (which can happen with any operating system).
