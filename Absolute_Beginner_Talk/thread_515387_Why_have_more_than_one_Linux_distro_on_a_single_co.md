### Post by Genecks on 2007-08-02
Because of packages, compiling packages, and building stuff.
It's as simple as that.

I think people are going toward Gentoo when they start getting more skillful. However, Debian-based is good for a beginning.

When you learn more about building packages in Linux, and you try building those various tar.gz / wtfevers, then you'll notice it's not so easy to do it in one distro, while in that other distro, it was a piece of cake.

Knowing what I know now, I would not go back to Redhat or Fedora. Screw it. I wouldn't see the point of doing it. However, some businesses support Fedora, Suse, and those other distros. Thus, it makes them an attractive choice when you're having device driver problems in Linux. Regardless, a person can typically get past those boundaries, however, in other versions of Linux.

Because business support one distro over another, that distro is a good choice if you've got driver issues.
Otherwise, compiling stuff becomes a problem.
And finding pre-compiled packages with installers is another.

You'll learn soon enough, though.

It's a mixed bag, a tug of war.

But to say the least, use what works. If you need multiple distros to do the job, do it.

Speed is another issue. Using ROX-file manager tends to speed things up. Puppy Linux is rocking the boat when it comes to minimal performance needs, enhanced speed, and a recent kernel. You can't pull that off with too many versions of LInux. Sadly, it doesn't do too good of a job of compiling for noobs, because it needs what the community calls "devx."

It's not too hard to install, though. However, because of the minimalist views of the designers, puppy doesn't include all the libraries you need for compiling; showing my main point.

A lot of issues, though.
