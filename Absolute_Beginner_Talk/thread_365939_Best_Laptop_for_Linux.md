### Post by jml on 2007-02-20
If you want everything to work out of the box, then I agree that System 76 is one of the best choices you can make.  They preinstall Ubuntu and makes sure everything works.  I just purchased a Darter from them and I have been very impressed with the quality of the product and the quality of their customer service, both before and after my purchase.  They also maintain a sub-forum on this site.

If you want the experience of installing and configuring Ubuntu on a laptop yourself then the two laptops I have had the best experience with were the IBM ThinkPad series of laptops, (I've used the R30, and R40e.)  Another one I would recommend would be HP, but not their consumer laptops, but the laptops they offer for small business'.  Those can be ordered with FreeDOS instead of Windows, so you don't pay the Windows tax if you don't need to.  If you do order it with Windows, the computer is not loaded with a bunch of useless applications you have to discard.  My only sugestion if you order a laptop is to make sure the wireless card is supported in Linux.  Avoid Broadcom Cards at all cost.  They can be a real pain to set up in Linux.  I've included a couple of URL's for you to look at.  I have purchased laptops from both of these companies and have had a good experience with both of them.  

Also, if you plan to add a wireless card later, be sure the laptop you get has a PCMCIA card slot.  Many new laptops come with a faster PCI-Express Card slot (my new Darterdoes,) which is not compatible with the older cards.  And there are virtually no Linux compatible cards for this new format yet.

[http://www.hp.com/sbso/busproducts_notebooks.html](http://www.hp.com/sbso/busproducts_notebooks.html)

[http://www.usanotebook.com/ourmodels_page.php](http://www.usanotebook.com/ourmodels_page.php)

Good luck shopping.

### Post by OldDogNewTricks on 2007-02-20
IBM Thinkpads and their successors, Lenovo Thinkpads have been designed fro the get-go to work with Linux, and they seem to provide the easiest way -- in my experience -- to avoid hardware issues.  They're also very fine computers.  But they are expensive.  I have purchased two used ones over EBay, and am very happy with them.  The prices were right too.  

I particularly like the Thinkpads that have an ultrabay, so I can insert a second hard drive, do my backups, and then remove the hard drive and store it offsite.  One can do this same thing with a USB 2.0 external hard drive, but the older Thinkpads didn't have USB 2.0, so the ultrabay was the way to go.  I still like the ultrabay, because I can change my BIOS settings and boot from the ultrabay drive.  I know that modern BIOS' will allow booting from USB 2.0 drives, but again, the older laptops did not support this.
