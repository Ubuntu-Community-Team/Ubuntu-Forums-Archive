### Post by az on 2006-03-24
I like to pimp advocacy, myself.

There are mild forms of advocacy which are just to be a ressource person for your friends who are interested in installing Ubuntu.  Go out of your way to help them.

You can get involved in Software Freedom Day and join your local linux user's group.

If you feel more strongly about software freedom, you can be more vocal when you shop for hardware and make it known that you want to buy linux-friendly hardware.  If you find a site, business or goverment office that prevents you from using free-libre software, either because their site is not standards-compliant or because they require you run some proprietary software just to be able to benefit from their services, let them know instead ot rolling your eyes and ignoring it.

Since I do not own a windows computer, I was told to go to an internet cafe and use a windows-based computer there to fill out my paternal-leave forms (available only online) from the government of Canada (to take time off work when my daughters were born).  I complained.  I pointed out how insecure that was.  They apologised.  They said the system was brand new.

There are lots of other examples of linux advocacy.
