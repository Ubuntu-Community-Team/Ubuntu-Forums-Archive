### Post by tom56 on 2006-12-16
Any computer from a known brand (incuding Dell, HP and Apple) should be fine. The trouble comes if a certain piece of harware used in the computer is not supported, though this is less likely to be a problem then it used to be. It's impossible to say that a certain computer (or computers from a certain manufacturer) will or won't work without having tested it. The best thing to do is to choose a computer then try looking on Google for other people's experiences of using Linux with it.

Laptops tend to be equally well supported as PCs nowadays. Though be warned that battery life may be worse than with Windows. Remember to check the compatibility of the wireless card that comes with it as this tends to be the problem people have with laptops.

As for memory, go for 1 gig if you can afford it. 512 will be fine but memory tends to be the thing that most affects the computer's performance. If I were you I'd go with 1 gig to be better future-proofed. Another thing I've found made a difference with my computer was using a 7200rpm hard drive rather than the more common 5400rpm.

### Post by igknighted on 2006-12-16
If you are going with a laptop, I would recommend an Intel chip.  It's hard for me to say this because I love AMD, but Intel's integrated graphics are actually quite good.  My friend's computer blows mine away in graphics performance, and she just uses intel's integrated graphics... my ATI x800 that I paid $150 for got smoked.  I think it is all in the drivers, because Intel releases great linux drivers.  AMD chips come with ATI mobile vid cards usually, and these almost always lead to trouble.

EDIT: Look at System76 for a laptop, it will come pre-installed with Ubuntu so you don't have to mess with windows at all (or pay for it)
