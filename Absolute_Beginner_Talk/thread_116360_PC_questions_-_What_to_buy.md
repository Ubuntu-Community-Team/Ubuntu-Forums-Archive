### Post by Onos on 2006-01-12
I am looking to buy a computer or more likely, buy the parts and build one over time - provided my existing system doesn't call it quits after 6 years of humming along slowly and steadily.

First and foremost... it will need to be Ubuntu / Linux compatible, i.e. not fluffed with hardware that is dependent on proprietary drivers or Windows-only specifications.

**My "dilemma" :**

Buying a nice computer outright would be stressful on my budget, unless I go for one of those cheapie $350-450 USD Wal-Mart/E-Machines specials... but I hear mixed reviews about them:

[LIST]
[*]2.2 ~ 3.0 GHz Sempron or Celerons mostly, maybe some Intel P4 or AMD64 on the higher end ($700+) 
[*]Integrated Audio (not that big of a deal if it can be picked up by Ubuntu)
[*]Integrated "Shared Memory" Video, typically an nVidia GeForce 6100+ GPU grafted onto the main board (sounds kind of shabby? breakage could be a problem, plus it might not be easily detected by Ubuntu? )
[*]Integrated NIC (probably a bad thing if it breaks - would need to replace the mobo)
[*]HDD with 120 ~ 200 GB storage
[*]Optical Drive, typically CD-R/RW/DVD-ROM
[*]256 MB DDR (most common) 512 MB (becoming more common) 1 GB (rare and not likely to be found in this price range)
[*]Often bundled with a printer (not needed) speakers (would be nice) keyboard & mouses (needed)
[*]Bundle of useless M$ software
[/LIST]

**The upshot is,** by the time I get around to commiting to a purchase, I would be able to pay cash for it, and Dapper Drake should be released and ready to load over a nice, clean partitioning. Plus, Dapper might be able to work with or around some of the hardware issues that earlier releases may have.

**The drawback is:** These machines are preinstalled with Windows, everything configured and imaged for that OS direct from the factory somewhere in the far corner of the world. This may probably mean compatibility issues between Ubuntu and some of the hardware/integrated chips.

I think for now, this would be my "hobby" machine - my surrogate work Desktop has enough oomph left to surf the web, check mail, and VPN to my workstation (at work!) when I feel like telecommuting.

But, I want to build a moderately fast, if not very fast machine with XFCE or FluxBox and Ubuntu (again, the hobbyist side of me: I tried mucking about with Xubuntu on the wife's machine to her chagrin, and wound up doing a basic install of Gnome + Ubuntu to get her working again after a few user-induced screw-ups).

The other (and presumably better) alternative is to build from scratch - involving choices in form factor (such as a Shuttle-sized box, or standard mini-tower) and finding a compatible motherboard, GPU/Graphics card, CPU, etc.

The trouble is, the costs for doing this range from $1500 to somewhere in the high 2000's ... something that I cannot afford to buy all in one shot and assemble; therefore, I would have to take a modular approach - buying the stuff that could later be assembled into a working, but slightly dated system in the future.

Off the top of my head, these are the pieces I would need.

[LIST]
[*]Case
[*]Fan(s)
[*]Power Unit (350W or more?)
[*]HDD (or two)
[*]Optical Drive
[*]Memory Card Reader
[*]Graphics Card
[*]Sound Card (can be integrated)
[*]NIC card - 10/100 Mbit
[*]**[COLOR="Red"]![/COLOR]** Motherboard (standard ATX format = more choices?)
[*]**[COLOR="Red"]![/COLOR]** CPU - hoping for 3.0 GHz on the average
[*]**[COLOR="Red"]![/COLOR]** RAM - hoping to support 1GB, with at least 3 slots to work with
[/LIST]
Total Cost of components: $900-1500

Also need:
[LIST]
[*]Flat Screen Monitor, 17-inch+ ($200-400)
[*]Keyboard
[*]Optical Mouse
[*]Speakers (can be cheapies)
[/LIST]


Does anyone have any suggestions as to which parts I should buy, **and in what order**, so as not to be faced with any backward compatibility issues down the road?

### Post by Michael Steinberg on 2006-01-12
I've installed Ubuntu on a Wintergreen barebones from TigerDirect which cost me about $200, a 2.8 gig Celeron D that came without HD as a $239 special at a local computer store--you can get an 80 gig HD for $30--and on a sub-$500 refurb Dell Inspiron laptop direct from Dell. All of them worked perfectly, leaving aside the Ndiswrapper stuff needed for the WiFi on the laptop and the WinModem, which I replaced with a used $10 PCMCIA card modem. You can get a new PC with Linspire from Fry's for $199, though it needs a few megs more memory--cheap if you shop around--and doesn't burn CDs. ([http://shop4.outpost.com/product/4568777)](http://shop4.outpost.com/product/4568777)). Check [http://dealnews.com](http://dealnews.com), too, for ongoing bargains. 

No monitor? If you don't need an LCD screen right off you can find 15 and 17 inch monitors for a song these days, since every business in North America seems to be getting rid of them. I found two like-new Dell 15-inch monitors left out for garbage down the street from my house. If you're picky and resourceful you don't have to spend very much for a decently fast computer. Sure, you won't be able to burn DVDs and won't have accelerated graphics (though you can add that stuff later on) but you'll be doing 95 percent of what you want to do. Maybe more.

Despite what others have said, I was not prompted to create a password or set up additional user accounts. However, using the included Linspire CD, I re-installed the O/S and when I booted into Linspire, I was prompted to create a root password and it was recommended that I create additional user accounts. It appears as if something was misconfigured when Linspired pre-installed. I also experienced sound problems with Linspire, however sound worked flawlessly under Ubuntu Linux.

Linspire was also unable to properly establish an Internet connection when I set it up to use my Linksys router. The status bar icon indicated that the connection was active, however I was unable to connect to the CNR service or perform general web surfing. I disconnected my router and connect my cable modem directly to the computer and configured Linspire to use DHCP to configure the connection. DHCP configuration was successful and I was on-line in a few minutes. Why Linspire was unable to access the Internet through my router, I don't know, but what I do know is that other distributions do not have this problem.

Overall, I wasn't impressed with Linspire. Sound was not working, I couldn't access the Internet through my router, and on a LCD monitor, it just looked horrible. With that in mind, I loaded up Darik's Boot and Nuke and proceeded to scrub my hard drive of this scourge.

From a hardware standpoint, I was impressed with the quality of the components used in the Koobox. Everything performed flawlessly and as expected "out-of-the-box", however the 6W speaker system included with the Koobox arrived damaged and the LCD monitor has several dead pixels, actually it has about fourteen dead pixels. According to the Mirus/Koobox terms of service, they are not responsible for items damaged during transit. The speakers aren't that big of a loss, but the LCD's dead pixels frustrated me. But, to be honest, when I originally purchased the system, I ordered the 17" CRT monitor, not the LCD monitor, so really, I can't complain. The optical drive is a Lite-On dual-layer DVD+/-RW drive and the hard drive is a Seagate 160 GB IDE drive, although the system does support SATA drives. The motherboard also has three additional PCI (PCI 2.2, not PCI-E) slots and an 8x/4x APG slot. There are two USB ports up-front, three in the back as well as a single FireWire port. 

My only other gripes with the Koobox are that I cannot disable the on-board video and it is very loud. You can select the amount of system memory to use for the on-board video, from 8 MB to 64 MB. As I plan on upgrading to a true video card, the fact that I cannot disable the on-board video and that a portion of my system memory is shared with a component that I won't be using, I'm a little aggravated. Upon turning the system on, the fans get so loud that the computer sounds more like an airplane than a PC. However, once the operating system loads, things begin to quiet down.

I would give the Koobox an overall grade of a B+, simply because the on-board video cannot be disabled and upon initial boot-up, it is very loud. As for Linspire, I think the fact that I used DBAN to remove it speaks for itself. In the future, if Mirus/Koobox would allow one to configure a custom system in addition to selecting from pre-built solutions, I believe they have a good chance of making it in the Desktop Linux market.

### Post by beeldings on 2006-04-29
After a week with the Koobox, I thought I should do the Ubuntu community a service and post an update regarding my Koobox. The fact of the matter is that Koobox uses old technology. As an example they use the socket 754 AMD processor not the newer socket 939 or 940 CPUs. If you want to upgrade to a graphics card, you're limited to an AGP card only as the Koobox does not feature a PCI-E slot, and even then you're limited to 1.5V AGP cards.

The on-board video is garbage. The Koobox uses the Via Unichrome IGP with the K8M800 chipset. A simple search on these forums reveals the problems other Ubuntu users have with this particular chipset. Even if one installs an AGP card, the on-board video cannot be disabled but you can set a limit on the amount of system memory it uses (8 MB being the lowest setting). However, if you wish to use a sound card as opposed to the on-board audio solution, you can easily disable the on-board sound in the BIOS.

The Performance Koobox supports a maximum of 2 GB PC3200 DDR RAM, and the unit ships with 512 MB. It cost me about $150 to upgrade to a 2 GB Kingston memory kit. 

If you wish to upgrade the BIOS you need a floppy drive but the Koobox does not ship with a floppy drive. I purchased a silver floppy drive from Newegg for about $8.00. Also, if you click on the "Compare" link on the Koobox web site, it lists a 6-in-1 card reader as available on all systems. However, none of the available Koobox builds actually come with the card reader. It wasn't a big deal for me as I don't need a card reader but this might be a turn off for new users.

The LCD monitor shipped with at least 20 "stuck on" pixels. Technical support has yet to respond to my inquiry.

Enclosed in the Koobox packaging is a Linspire package consisting of the Linspire 5-0 installation CD, the Linspire Extras CD, a user's guide and a registration card. In order to activate the one-year CNR service which is included with the Koobox, one must register their Linspire installation using the registration code printed on the registration card. However, I was unable to do so as I received a blank registration card. This didn't bother me too much as I removed Linspire within minutes of unpacking and setting up the computer, but it would have been nice to test the CNR feature and the included DVD software.

All in all, I believe that the Koobox is a rip off. For the money you would spend on a Performance Koobox and an LCD monitor, you're better off building your own system or purchasing a cheap Dell and installing Linux on it. I wish I would have done some research on the Koobox before dropping $700 on system that uses old technology. I retract my previous comments on the Koobox and would grade it as a C. I considered returning the system to Mirus (manufacturer of Koobox) but according to their terms: shipping charges are not refundable and I am responsible for charges related to shipping the system back to Mirus. I decided to bite the bullet and ride this one out. From now on, I am going to build my own systems.
