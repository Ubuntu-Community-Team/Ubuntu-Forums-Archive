### Post by DrMega on 2007-06-18
Ubuntu is a "distro" of Linux. All the stuff that's included is, as you say, available on other Linux distros. I suppose Ubuntu is a largely preconfigured OS compromising Linux, a few custom bits and pieces specific to Ubuntu (like its very nice installer for example) and a load of tools, apps etc that a bunch of people have tested and confirmed to work very well.

There is also the repositories, a collection of freely downloadable software that has been tested and confirmed to work, and be safe and secure.

In my experience, having tried a load of other distros and finding them to be a nightmare to install and configure, Ubuntu just installed with no effort and once done, all the tools most people would need were just there. This that weren't are in the extensive repository and can be added (or removed) as simply as ticking a check box and clicking Apply.

### Post by Circus-Killer on 2007-06-18
okay, there have been numourous replies, but im gonna try break it down a little bit without getting to technical (cos then i'de get confused).

okay, behind every modern OS there is a kernel, even windows has one. the kernel of an OS is the real meat of the system. without the kernel there is no OS. the kernel is what talks to your hardware, what controls your harddrive, etc. etc.

from there, other software is layered on top. think of linux as an onion, ogers have layers, linux as layers. a good example of the layers would be you GUI that you see in linux. at the base of the GUI is the x windows server, and then on top of that would be your desktop environment, such as gnome or kde. now the choice between kde and gnome is only viable because of this layering.

a good way to look at all of this is if you think of linux as a car. the kernel is the engine. without the engine, the car will not run. however, just an engine without the rest of the car wont get you far as well. you need the car and the engine to work together. all the system apps are like the other parts of the car, working with the engine to achieve a single goal. when you interact with your car, you dont directly interact with the engine, you interact through peddles, gears and the likes. so to with linux, you dont work directly with the kernel, you work with applications that talk back down to the kernel.

now as for different distributions. they all have more or less the same thing, its mostly the packaging and one or two modifications that make it different from the rest. when you buy a Volkswagen, its going to come with break pedal, accelerate pedal, clutch pedal, gears, windscreens etc. etc. If you buy a Ford or a Fiat, you would get the same thing. the difference is in the customization and packaging.

i hope that my analogies werent too confusing, and im sure i might be slightly off on some things i have said, but i'm hoping that whatever i have said will make some sense to the new users.

to you our new ubuntu users, i welcome thee. 
welcome to liberation!
