### Post by carusoswi on 2007-05-19
The problem isn't Ubuntu.  It's me.  I ran 6.10 without too many problems and, perhaps, with lowered expectations.  At that time, I did not own a wireless adapter, and, my professional situation dictates that I have a solid wired internet connection on weekends, but, my weekday situation requires either wireless access or no access.

I resigned myself to no weekday access in U6.10, but, purchased and successfully installed a Belkin N1 adapter in 7.40.  The first installation was flawless.  I had my choice of WPA security or no security, whatever.

Then, out of nowhere, the adapter disappeared from my system.  After much fiddling around, it seemed simpler to simply trash the Ubuntu installation and re-install to get the wireless back.

Back it came, but not so flawlessly this time.  It seems that, no matter what I do, it will not work with any WPA secured router.  To make it work, I took down the security on my home router.

During the week, when I really need that wireless adapter for internet access, I made the (apparent) mistake of installing via Crossover XP Office.  I cannot say for certain, but suspect that IE5 which is integral to installation of that package corrupted my NDISWrapper, so, I had to live without Ubuntu Internet access for the week.

Now, I'm back home, and have scrapped my Ubuntu 7.04 installation once again as that seems to be far simpler than trying to figure out how to fix the broken NDISWrapper configuration.

I'm back up and running, ready to re-install all my aps (if I can remember them all).  This time, I installed Crossover and XPOffice before NDISwrapper and my wireless driver.  Everything is coexisting at the moment, although the wireless adapter is still not installed with 100% functionality.  The driver installation window reports that the driver is installed, but no hardware present.  This is not right, I know . . . and my wireless will not work with WPA secured routers until I figure out why the hardware is not being recognized in that window.

IWCONFIG reports my hardware present and properly associated.

. . . but I am coming to regard Ubuntu as a sort of one night stand sort of operating system - not an installation that you grow too attached to, because, in an instant, something may become broken in a way that requires you to reinstall, and, in the process, say goodbye to whatever setup you currently enjoy.

The good news is that, re installation (especially when you become familiar with it) is pretty safe and very simple, and, instead of having to dig out all the OEM discs, you just log on to the internet and hit the update button and 90% of what you had is back in a fresh, new installation.

I embarked upon the Ubuntu trail out of pure curiosity, but have definitely become hooked.

I have read that it is not quite prime time for MultiMedia, and, to be certain, there is a need for a mature, feature packed version of audio and video editing software (on XP, I use Steinberg's Wavelab and Sony's Vegas - two of only three or four Win Aps that are truly worth paying for).  The latest versions of Photoshop don't work on Ubuntu (yet), but PS7 does.  There are some Linux native photo editing aps that look very interesting - had one that was truly unique, but lost it do to need to reinstall, and will have to find it again - it was based on zones, etc, as espoused by Ansel Adams, and I lost the ap before fully exploring its possiblities).

My business runs on PeachTree accounting software.  I am told it can be run on Linux, but, I have had no success in that regard either via Ubuntu or Crossover.

But, as soon as I can figure out these few "crossover" issues, I will scrap Windows for good.

I got a bit OT, but was about to comment on the representation that I have seen that Ubuntu (or Linux) is not prime time for multimedia.  As stated above, there is a lack of mature editing software for Linux, but, in terms of running multimedia presentations, I would have to give the nod to Ubuntu (Linux) over Windows.

I recently used Vegas (in XP) to edit a three-cam shoot of a classical concert.  As usual, Vegas performed without a hitch to edit, then render my output to DV format avi.

When I tried to check the results by playing the avi file in Windows Movie Player (or whatever that ap is called), I thought, perhaps, something was wrong with the file as the output was noticeably and unacceptably jerky.

Just for fun, I booted into Ubuntu and played it from there . . . it was smooth as silk, transitions were perfect, no jerkiness at all.

I am guessing that is because Ubuntu is less encumbered by stuff that needs to be running in the background when you are in the XP environment.  I can only imagine the problems that must confront users of Vista.

Anyhow, I apologize for the long post, but felt like getting this off my chest on a rainy Saturday morning.

Caruso
