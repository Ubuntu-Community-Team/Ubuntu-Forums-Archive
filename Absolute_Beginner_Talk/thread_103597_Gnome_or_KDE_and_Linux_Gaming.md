### Post by Estariel on 2005-12-14
The following is a personal opinion, and I encourage you to try both yourself to find out which one suits you better.

GNOME is built with simplicity in mind. Many options that could be included have removed or are not displayed in order to not confuse the user. This leads to a very polished, uncluttered look.
In my opinion this is in most cases a pro for GNOME, while in some few cases the lack of options and lack of configurability (is that a word?) is highly annoying.
The greatest con for GNOME is (again in my opinion) the fact that it is not very integrated (compared to KDE). It lacks KDE i/o slaves that make life so much easier.
Also two applications that I view as "killer"-apps are K3B and amaroK, which are both QT applications and therefore look better in KDE (because KDE uses QT as opposed to GTK on GNOME)

KDE on the other hand is really cluttered if compared to GNOME. There are options everywhere, buttons that lead to even more options and this can be pretty confusing in the beginning.
This has its bright side though. Once you know your way around, KDE is way more configurable than GNOME.
Also, KDE is very well integrated (which is for me the selling point, that's why I use KDE).
On the other hand side, another "killer"-app, the GIMP, is written with the gtk-toolkit (which is what GNOME uses) and thus looks ugly when used with KDE. There is a workaround for this though, since the gtk-qt engine gives all gtk applications a QT look & feel, and thus makes GIMP look like a QT application.

You see that there are pros and cons for both, and there are times when I like GNOME better...
You will have to find out for yourself.

### Post by AndyCooll on 2005-12-14
Estariel pretty much sums things up. It really is a case of what desktop environment suits you best.

Many say that KDE will be more familiar and easier to get used to if you're coming from a Windoze environment. You could always try Kubuntu, i.e. Ubuntu with the KDE theme.
I personally didn't find that to be the case (and I've tried to like KDE!), though that might be because I tried Fedora Core first and that distro leans heavily towards Gnome. So, maybe it's the desktop you try first that has a big influence... who knows!?

It's that "simplicity" about Gnome that Estariel mentions that appeals to me. It's also the reason why I like Ubuntu, even down to the fact that by default you initially start off with just one app for each function, e.g. Office software.
