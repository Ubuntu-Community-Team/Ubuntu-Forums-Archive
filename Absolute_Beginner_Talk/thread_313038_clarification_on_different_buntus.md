### Post by Shatrat on 2006-12-05
The only difference between those three is the default window manager and desktop environment.
You can have gnome, kde, and xfce all on one system if you really want to.
There is no important difference between them since it's pretty trivial to install a different desktop manager if you dont like your first choice.

I wouldn't worry about requirements too much, except to do a little googling to see if certain bits of hardware are supported.  Old dial up modems, certain graphics chipsets, and some wifi adapters are notorious for their manufacturers refusing to put out a good linux driver or document their product so that others can.

Check here for your video, audio, network, peripherals, and so forth.
[http://www.linuxquestions.org/hcl/index.php](http://www.linuxquestions.org/hcl/index.php)

### Post by IYY on 2006-12-05
All the *buntus are equal in power, in the sense that they have access to the same repositories of software. However, the graphical user interfaces differ, not only in looks but also in design (where everything is, how things are done.) 

> 
does one system handle certain applications or functions better than the others?


In a sense, yes. Ubuntu tends to be better with GTK apps (Open Office, Firefox, Banshee, Gimp, Abi Word) whereas Kubuntu is better with KDE apps (KOffice, Amarok, Audacity, Lyx). When I say ``better" I don't actually mean performance differences or crashing, but just the fact that the applications look and feel native to your environment. KDE applications in Ubuntu will still have a KDE theme, whereas a GTK application in Kubuntu will have a Gnome theme. These themes may not look good together. 

One way of going about choosing your distribution is by downloading both LiveCDs and seeing which one you consider to be more comfortable to work with .

Another way is to just look at the screenshots of shots.osdir.com. Chances are you'll like one look better than the other.

A third solution is to decide which one you would like best based on description:

**KDE** (Kubuntu) is focused on giving you the freedom to change every single detail in the look and feel of your user interface. You can adjust it to work in very different ways. It also has crisper, sharper graphics with more vivid colours. 

**Gnome** (Ubuntu) is focused on having good looking, comfortable defaults and limit your choices somewhat. This is good in the sense that you have to worry less about making things work comfortably because the choices were already done by professionals who understand interfaces. On the other hand, you may feel that it limits your flexibility, and you may not like certain design decisions and be unable to change them. Gnome tends to have smoother graphics and warmer colours.

I would not advise using **XFCE** (Xubuntu) on a machine with more than 256MB of RAM if you are a beginner. Many experts prefer the lighter environments simply because they no longer need the graphical tools. For example, I use an even more minimal environment called **IceWM**. 

A fourth solution is to simply install Ubuntu. You will get more a bit more support on these forums because it's more popular, and also will be running the distribution ``the way the developers intended''.
