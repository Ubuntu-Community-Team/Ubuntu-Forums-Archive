### Post by Wolki on 2005-09-08
[QUOTE=j.hill]The thing I don't get is that most computers are mass-produced, and even homebrew computers are made of mass-produced components.  Can there really be so much variation between systems?[/QUOTE]

Yes. There are lots of components, and nearly endless different combinations. Just think of of many different graphics cards there are, then multiply that with the number of sound cards.

Even Windows, which has lots and lots more driver support, sometimes coughes on particular combinations. That's one of the reasons Macs tend to work so well, because they have less different combinations and are easier to test. But that's not the way of the standard IBM/Intel architecture.

### Post by az on 2005-09-08
Also, all open source applications can work well together at the source level.  The problem is that you do not want to have to compile everything yourself.  That is why you use binary (precompiled) packages.

The problem is, if something was compiled for another linux distribution, it probably will not work with your distribution.  You have to compile it yourself or find someone who already has and who packaged it for you.

That is why there are so moany packages in Universe.
