### Post by arbulus on 2007-05-11
I would say it depends on your setup.  Some distros work better than others on particular systems, and some are better suited for whatever your needs happen to be.  For example, I tried Fedora Core 5 and 6 and couldn't use either one of them.  For my setup, they were very difficult to use.  I've also used Debian, but I'm not too much a fan of their philosohpy on "completely free" components.  I like Firefox and Thunderbird, and that's what I'd like to use.  I know that Iceweasel and Icedove are essentially the same, but it still bothers me.  I think that really is just splitting hairs.

On the other hand, I've never had a problem with Ubuntu.  It has worked wonderfully for me and can do pretty much everything I need it to do.  Installing it on a software RAID 0 wasn't an easy task though.  Other distros handle that better.

There are other things that differ between distros, which may or may not sway your opinion of usablity. For example, the package management is different between Ubuntu, Fedora, Gentoo.  Fedora uses Yum as opposed to apt-get, and is painfully slow and clunky.  Gentoo is source based, so if you're into compiling alot of software, then that would be a good one for you.  I think Ubuntu's package management is the best that I have encountered so far. 

So really I think it just depends on your individual preferences and needs.

### Post by carlosqueso on 2007-05-11
Solaris is a type of UNIX, similar to linux, but not the same.  From what I gather, it's primarily targeted at servers, but if you meet the system requirements, it might be interesting to look at.  I took a look at it, but decided that didn't need to be my next system to test.

As for why any distro is better or not, I don't think any is better or worse, but each has it's own advantages.  I'll run down the ones I've tried.

Ubuntu:  Pros:
*good hardware support
*great forum community
*reasonably good ease of use
*great XFCE support
Cons:
*no proprietary codecs by default
*requires command line to do some things
*some customization difficult.

Gentoo: Pros:
*VERY customizable--any version of the software you want available
*extremely easy to edit/create ebuilds (their packages)
*learn alot about linux
Cons:
*feels like it's not totally integrated
*you must compile everything, takes a lot of cpu power
*difficult to install

SUSE (although to be fair, I didn't spend much time on this): Pros:
*VERY easy to configure through GUI
*PRETTY
Cons:
*restrictive in how to customise
*I'm not a fan of their default configuration

BLAG: Pros:
*Anarchist software, funny :-)
*all media codecs available
*uses the more common rpms for software, but takes advantage of the seriously better apt package system
Cons:
*Small community, difficult to get help
*small repos, hard to find some programs

Knoppix: Pros:
*always meant to be a live CD, runs on almost anything
*LOTS of programs by default, I don't know how they do it.
Cons:
*sometimes the native German creeps in.
*KDE by default (sorry, i'm not a fan)
*if you have slow hardware, the CD crawls....
*designed as a live CD, so not really meant to be installed.

There you have my experiences with the various distros.  I don't intend to start a flamewar, I'm not saying any distro is better or worse than any other.  If you are interested in changing, and you have a free partition, I reccommend trying random distros.  It's a good way to learn, even if you do stay with Ubuntu.   I've tried a few, and taken things I liked from many
