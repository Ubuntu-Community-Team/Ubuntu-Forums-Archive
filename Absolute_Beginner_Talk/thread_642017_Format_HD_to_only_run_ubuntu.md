### Post by Paqman on 2007-12-16
Nope, nothing special required. During installation just select "use entire disk" in the partitioning bit and it will completely overwrite your Windows install.

Your driver disks won't work for Ubuntu, unfortunately. They're Windows drivers. The manufacturers don't ship Linux support disks, but Ubuntu does a pretty good job of "just working" anyway. You may possibly need to install drives for graphics cards after installation, but Ubuntu will download and install them automatically for you.

### Post by wankel on 2007-12-16
Hi Fenixfox,

Kajillin is right that you probably will not need your driver disks, because only few manufacturers ship with Ubuntu/Linux drivers. If they do, the most recent driver is on the Ubuntu disk or on the manufacturers website .So indeed, the only thing you need is the Ubuntu CD.

At first, the CD will run Ubuntu as the so called "Live CD", which does not touch your hard disk in any way. Once it started (should take less than five minutes on not too old hardware), you will have a nice desktop environment to play around in.

There will be an option there (on the desktop or the programs menu, it has been a while for me since using the live CD function), to install Ubuntu to your hard disk.

That part would take less than half an hour, I would guess. Somewhere during those steps, you are asked how you like to devide (partition) your hard disk. At that point, you can tell Ubuntu to use the whole hard disk.

By the way, if you just ordered your CD's, depending on the place where you live, it could take a while to receive them (for me it took over a month both times).

Of course, you could get the CD downloaded from the net as well, and burn it yourself.

Good luck!

### Post by wankel on 2007-12-16
Your hardware does not sound "out of the ordinary". That is generally good news: obscure hardware tends not to be supported as much as stuff used by the masses. 

If you encounter problems anyway, it is more useful to know the name of the chips used, than the name on the box: it could be that in some versions of your Foxconn motherboard used an extra chip for SATA support in older revisions for example, and integrated in the south bridge chip in later revisions. In that case, it could be that there one version got another behaviour than the other version.

First of all, I would not start worrying before actually having my hands on a Ubuntu CD. Heck, even then I would not start worrying! I would cheer, stuff the CD player with it and boot my pc :-)

(By the way, you know how to tell your pc to boot from CD, instead of HDD?)
