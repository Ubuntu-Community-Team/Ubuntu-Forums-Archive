### Post by TeaSwigger on 2007-11-05
First off, welcome. I hope you'll enjoy the wonderful new world open source and linux can offer. It's what you make it as they say.

Your PC is well supported. There are any numbers of things you'll have to learn just like you did when you first started off with windows. Just like then, this would be a sort of... osmosis. You start, pick up what you can and learn as you go. Accumulate. I don't mean any discouragement, quite the contrary: you did it then, you got this far, and you can do it now. 

Bookmark or jot down these sites:
[http://users.bigpond.net.au/hermanzone/](http://users.bigpond.net.au/hermanzone/) (for boot stuff)
[http://ubuntuguide.org/](http://ubuntuguide.org/)
and as noted above [http://www.psychocats.net](http://www.psychocats.net)
Tons of potentially useful info can be found at these sites, and of course post here. We're just fellow users so be patient and persistent, and provide what info you can if it's to get help on a problem.

Everyone started with no knowledge at all, so don't worry you don't know this or that. You'll figure your way with plain old persistence, thought, research, intuition etc. I'm using it happily now and I've never read a how-to book on linux or taken classes. Ubuntu has done an incredible thing in making a sophisticated linux operating system easier than could have been expected just a short time ago. Seriously, in most cases you can use the guided installation and bingo, everything will "just work." No hunting for driver discs or dowloads here there and everywhere, or browser this and antivirus that. So in some ways it's actually easier than installing windows from scratch. Then you can spend your time making things just the way you like. It's an exciting time to come into it.

Hm no Vista CD? Sheez it would've cost them what, $1? Well if I recall rightly you can request one (free, since you already bought it) from HP if you want one. You don't absolutely need it; there are many games, some scanners, and a few programs you can't run well or at all, but most you actually can run in Ubuntu, thanks to a program called WINE, or another method called virtualization (for this second method you'd need a windows CD). I'd suggest that there's a chance you may move from windows world (tm) for good and not miss anything. If you proceed methodically and take care to follow instructions etc you really shouldn't have any problems with setting up a "dual boot."

About recovering your stuff. First it's always a good idea of course to save anything important or unique to a CD or what have you in regular intervals. Just a good idea whatever the system. But say something was messed up and the system won't boot; if the hard drive hasn't been reformatted / erased, you could for example put in the ubuntu live CD. You'd boot to that and it can read from the hard drive, so you can actually copy your important files from the hard drive to another CD if you have a second drive, or USB card or whatever. There are other methods of doing the same thing.

Sorry for rattling on so, I don't know where you're at in knowledge. Hope it helps. Guess I'll close by saying, "dive in and enjoy yourself." :)

The best thing about the Windows key (and any other key for the matter) is that it can be mapped to do ANYTHING for you. If you use Amarok (a music player like iTunes but better), you can use global shortcuts such as Winkey + B and Winkey + C to switch tracks without even opening Amarok from the system tray.

And yes, Ctrl+S works to save documents, and I think Alt+F4 works in most applications.
