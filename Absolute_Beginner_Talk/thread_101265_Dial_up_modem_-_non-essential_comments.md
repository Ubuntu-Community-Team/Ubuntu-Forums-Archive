### Post by dmanderzen on 2005-12-09
First,  thank you.  Those who contribute to the common perception that the Linux community is hostile to beginners are complete idiots, I don't care how smart they are, and I applaud this attempt to reach those who might be intimidated.
    Second, I'm by no means a beginner. I switched to Linux in about 2001 because the concept was so appealing.  It's served me well as my daily OS, being forced to use Windows 98 since I've moved has given me the willies. I've worked in a technical field most of my career. For me a computer is a tool, not a toy.  I don't consider time tinkering at my keyboard as productive. not averse to the command-line but I don't have to use it often, and it's far from second nature. Just cause it's not  GUI doesn't mean it's hard, if there's decent documentation.
   Third, for anyone but a true propellor-head geek, Linux documentation and support is pitiful.  This quality is shared by every other technical segment of society, but I refuse to believe that it's inevitable. Gentoo's documentation is a shining example of what's possible.  
    I can share my recent experience with dialup as a completely typical example of what's wrong. When I first installed Linux 5 years ago I only had dialup, and it was understandable, given the youth of Linux at the time, that getting a modem to work was a complicated process.  It's nuts that I'm currently going through a nearly identical, highly time-intensive process when this is one of the most common problems that Linux newcomers face.  
   I no longer have an ISA slot so my old hardware modem wouldn't work.  I'm aware that some winmodems now work in Linux.  I hit Google and found the usual menagerie of stuff, found scanModem, found the modem compatability database, having been through the process before it probably only  took an hour to get this much sorted out.  I went to the Ubuntu site, entered "modem" in the search field, found a very promising-sounding tutorial listed, hit the link, and was told that the document didn't exist yet.  I did find a Spanish-language page that looked useful, and I could have gotten my system hooked to the internet by just downloading some stuff from the internet (???).
   Anyway, my point is that there should be some clear step-by-step documentation for the basics: new printer, digital camera setup, modems(!!)
   And the problem with most documentation is that it's written by people who understand their subject so well that they're incapable of communicating with people who don't.
   I have some experience in this, I used to write setup guides for the equipment where I work.  It's not rocket science, but like all writing it requires an editor.  For beginner-level technical documentation the best editor is a beginner.
   If there's potential for getting this kind of project going I'd like to offer whatever help I have time for, and I think there's huge potential here to offer even complete beginners a chance to contribute.

### Post by Brunellus on 2005-12-09
...this is what the wiki system is for.  

As to the chicken-and-egg problem:  Yes, to get internet access you must have internet access...but that means, in effect, finding someone with 'net access who can download the necessary files for you, and then using "sneakernet" to get it to your computer.  

My usual response to newbies having hardware problems is this:  take the money you saved by not buying proprietary software, and buy equipment that is known to work with Linux, and taht will solve your problem.  This is the "real cost" of using Linux...and it saves newbies in particular a great deal of heartache and irritation.
