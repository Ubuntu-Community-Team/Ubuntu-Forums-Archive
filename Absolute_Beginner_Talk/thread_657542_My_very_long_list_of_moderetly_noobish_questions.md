### Post by HereInOz on 2008-01-03
To answer the first one:

The problem with lithium ion batteries is that the two things they hate most is 1) Heat, and 2) Being fully charged.  The best way to prolong the life of a lithium ion battery in storage is to keep it in the fridge in a slightly less than half charged condition.

Therefore, if you are using your laptop as a desktop, running on power all the time, take the battery out and put it in the refrigerator until you need it.  If you are running your laptop on battery on a regular basis, then accept the fact that you are going to get about a 2 year battery life.

Lithium ion batteries are no where near as vulnerable to "memory effect" as the old nickel cadmium batteries, but it is still recommended by the manufacturers that you allow the battery to discharge to the point of computer shutdown at least once a month.  A complete discharge, to dead flat, however, is not recommended.

To answer the second one:

The Epson Stylus C range has good support in Ubuntu.  They are relatively cheap and the ink cartridges are also not that exorbitant.  I can't say that they are cheap, as cheap is not a term I would use to describe the price of any ink jet cartridge, but they are less diabolical as far as cost goes than some other brands. I believe most HP units are OK as well.

I will leave the rest to those more qualified than I to answer them.

3. #what does compile mean?

Compiling is the process of taking human readable program code and turning it in to machine readable instructions. Ubuntu is a binary distribution which means for 99% of all the software you will ever need are centrally compiled on big beefy machines by the project maintainers. This is advantageous because 1 their compile clusters are way faster and more powerful than your machine, and most other people's, cuts down on duplicate effort (only one compile for most people instead of one per machine), and make installing things easier, installing from deb packages is far easier and more automate then installing from source.

> 
4. What is a rootkit and is it anything to lose sleep over?

A rootkit is a type of system takeover that pro actively attempts to hide it's presence and hinder it's removal. Yes rootkits are something to loose sleep over if you ever get one, good news is that under normal circumstances you'll never run in to one. You have to either install vulnerable server software or install something you shouldn't to get one.
