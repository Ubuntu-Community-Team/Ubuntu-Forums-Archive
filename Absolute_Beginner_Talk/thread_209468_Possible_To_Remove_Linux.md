### Post by HereInOz on 2006-07-05
I tend to agree with rbalfour.  If you want to have a look at Linux, but only have one machine, consider, as he says, a live CD, or invest in one of those removable drive bays, and a second hard disk drive.

That way, you can put the Windows drive in, or the Linux drive, and boot accordingly.  Unless you have a laptop, in which case this scenario is not possible.

### Post by Average_Joe on 2006-07-05
MOSFET_MOSFET --

I've seen you in the forums asking questions for a few days -- that's a wise move no matter what your final decision.

I was in the same situation as you a week ago.  I really wanted to try Linux but absolutely needed to have my Windows XP functionality on my production machine.

A week later -- I love Linux so much -- that I am putting it on all of my machines.

My learning history was this:
First, just to play and learn, I made a dual boot machine for Vista and Ubuntu, according to the instructions here ... [http://www.commonmancomputing.com](http://www.commonmancomputing.com)
That worked out very well, and I could see that I liked Linux so much I would want it on all my machines.

So then came the more risky step for me, to try and dual boot my production machine with Windows XP.  The first thing I did was backup my data, 13GB worth, and **wow** I am happy I did that!  Because my first attempt to install Linux failed on my production machine, and I never could boot back into Windows NOR into Linux.  I didn't feel badly, though -- I knew I had my data, and I could always reformat the entire hard disk start over again.

The second time on my production machine, I just let the LiveCD installer do the 'dummy' option and everything worked out great.  There was a minor technical glitch but the guy at commonmancomputing helped me get through that.  I now am able to dual boot into XP and Ubuntu.  In fact I find I am using Linux much more often than XP, in particular for the routine tasks of web browsing, all emailing, and CD burning.
