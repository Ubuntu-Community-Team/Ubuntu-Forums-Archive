### Post by Kvark on 2005-06-26
How about turning the question around. Will commercial software as we know it today last?

The technological development is way ahead of our economical and legal structures. The whole concept of 'intellectual property' is about to collapse. Because it depends on restricting spread of new technology to make development profitable. It is becoming easier and easier to spread new ideas and technology.

Corporations in rich countries develop medicines so they own the right to make them. Poor countries that need the medicine to fight epidemics produce their own cheaply pirated medicine instead because they can't afford to pay the patent holders' prices. 

Cloth factories in poor countries often produce copies of famous clothing brands and sell them much ceaper then the originals. Watches, electronics, toys and many other products are also copied in this manner.

Most internet users download pirated software, movies and music for no cost at all instead of the high prices the developers demand. New DVD players with hard disks and everything makes it easier then ever to record TV series with high quality. MP3 players, cellphones and such are replacing the old portable CD players.

The idea of 'intellectual property' isn't going to last very long. So we best hope the alternatives are profitable. Or at least that someone comes up with new alternatives that are. Specially since much of the tecnological development, specially big steps forward like new medicines, alternative energy sources and space travel costs millions or even billions.

### Post by cwaldbieser on 2005-06-26
The Ubuntu distro itself is free like many other distros, and to some extent based off of the efforts of volunteerism (like this forum, for example).  I believe the costs associated with actually producing the distro are funded by Cannonical Ltd. which is owned/operated by Mark Shuttleworth ([http://www.markshuttleworth.com/](http://www.markshuttleworth.com/)) who is an independently wealthy philanthropist.

Basically, Ubuntu can last as long as the members want it to last.  That is one of the main benefits of the software being free (as in freedom).  People can feel free to try and help each other without having to be afraid that they are going to face legal and technical reprisals.

### Post by az on 2005-06-26
"How does Ubuntu make an income while everything is for free"

Well, the model for open source business is that the software is not the product, but the service is.  The software is the creation of the many developers who release it under a free licence.  This means that it is both free of charge as well as free to be worked-on by others,  

The latter is a strong point in favor of security.

Now, nobody is going to be able to use all the software without some sort of support.
The fact that it is free does not mean that it is easy to put together and use the different pieces.

This is the microsoft companies argument that open source software is not free.

I think that it depends on the case, but open source is usually cheaper to run than proprietary software.

 The following is an excerpt from the interview with 
Mark Shuttleworth on Slashdot (see full interview at 
[http://interviews.slashdot.org/article.pl?sid=05/04/04/1859255&tid=90](http://interviews.slashdot.org/article.pl?sid=05/04/04/1859255&tid=90))

____________________________________
Everything free -- what's the business plan?
by HoserHead (599)

How does Canonical plan on making money? Ubuntu seems to be completely and utterly free, in both senses of the word. In my mind at least, the 'services will pay for development' business plan for Free Software went out of style when the dot-com bubble burst. How will your company be different?

MS: You're right that the "services pay for development" model is unlikely to work very well for single applications. An entire distribution, though, is slightly different, because the number of users is potentially much, much greater than the number of users for, say, a web server or database app.

Canonical provides support for Ubuntu, but more importantly we provide support for companies that provide support for Ubuntu. The idea is to create an ecosystem of people who collaborate on the free software. You can see the beginnings of that ecosystem on this page of Ubuntu service providers, and I hope it will continue grow as fast as it has since Warty hit the streets.

Part of being sustainable is keeping the costs down, so we focus resources on development and support, not marketing or office waste. The guys will tell you I'm a cheap bastard when it comes to the frills (Canonical One doesn't *actually* belong to Canonical :-).

I'd very much like to make the distro project sustainable, because I've never had the privilege to work with such talented guys who work as hard as this team, and they deserve to be rewarded and to know that people appreciate the value they add every day. If it doesn't work utthat way, though, I'm honoured to consider it a gift back to the open source world, which played such a critical role in helping me build Thawte. So I hope it's commerce, though it may turn out to be philanthropy. Either way, it's still cheaper than going back to space, or hooking up with fast planes/boats/women, which I supposed would be Plan B.
