### Post by coolen on 2007-06-26
The big one, for me, is the ease of installing/removing/updating software, and, of course, the massive amounts of software available to me.

Windows update can get you all of your updates for, well, Windows, but all of the third-party applications require constant checking (or individual update checks provided by the application).

Also, the way software dependancies are handled in Linux is very nice. In Windows, each application (mostly) handles all of the tasks it needs to to get the job done. Common tasks, other than those provided by the system, are duplicated with each program, increasing the size of the system. My Windows and Ubuntu partitions are each 50 GB...my Windows partition has 8 left, whilst my Ubuntu partition has almost 40. On the whole, Ubuntu has more applications installed...

It's just more fun for me, too. One of the most exciting things about using Linux is learning, particularly in the beginning. Now, I'm no expert...but I know enough about my system to undo any damage I may cause. What would I do in Windows if my GUI failed? Reformat, and start over.

The speed of development is greater. Ubuntu is currently one of the easiest to use of Linux distributions, and it's come a long way in its relatively short life. With developers who listen to their user, are dedicated to making the system everything it can be, care about free software, and have a good corporate backing, improvements are plentiful and positive. If Windows is superior in some way to Ubuntu, or easier to use, or just nicer in some regard, rest assured that someone is probably hammering away at their keyboard, thinking the same thing...

Finally, I'm one of those people who just prefers it. From the polished GTK look, right down to the way my directory tree starts with a '/', I find the system very appealing.
