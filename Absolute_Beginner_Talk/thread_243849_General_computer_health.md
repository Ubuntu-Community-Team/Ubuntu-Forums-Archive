### Post by ciscosurfer on 2006-08-25
The best analogy I can come up with is a car:  your ignition and engine will eventually fail or become slow (or sluggish) the more often you start 'er up.  The same hold true with computers as well.  I wouldn't worry too much about your rebooting.  Unless you start to notice some major slowdowns as a direct result of rebooting so often, then you'll be fine.  But keep this in mind: As with any kind of technology, the more you use it, the faster it "wears and tears."

EDIT: I like jrjr's explanation as well.

### Post by Iowan on 2006-08-25
I've heard the same thing.
(1) Dunno if I'm convinced or not. I doubt it helps.
(2) Heat cycling. When a machine is on, the components warm up, when it's shut down they cool off.  Thermal expansion/contraction makes components brittle and can even make cards crawl out of their sockets.  Some schools-of-thought are that a computer should **never** be powered down.  Not to mention that the hard drive seems to thrash more wildly at power-up (reboot) more than "normal" operation.
