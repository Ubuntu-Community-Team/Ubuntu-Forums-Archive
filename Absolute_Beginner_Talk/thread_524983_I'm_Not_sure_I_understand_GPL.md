### Post by cobrn1 on 2007-08-13
OSS makes sense inthat if your competitor want's similar functionality thay would want to use your code, but if they add features to make their product look better, you are able to take the code and use it too. Benefits developers/companies because they can get extra functionality with less work, and they are never going to be completely left behind by the competition (take for example, how AMD is by Intel at the mo - hardware is a bad example, but it's the best I can think of right at the mo..) Consumers benefit because all the options are up to date, and ther's lots of choice.

The only time it's not so beneficial is if you need special functionality and get a dev to make it for you. You don't want the competition to use it for free when you paid for it. But maybe the advantages above outweigh that negative and you'll go opensource anyway.

Also, even if the competition can implement your code, it'll need tweaking and you'll have a lead on them - you can use that the profit, and use the intervening time to nick their code and add that.

Basically, everybody get's everything and everybody wins (in theory).

My last example: it's a bit like playing videogames with friends... you'll come up with a great tactic and kill them (assuming it's a shooting game) lots. They'll then nick your tactic, but you'll have picked up on theirs and improve tactics. If they start to beat you you'll nick their improved version of your tactics - see where I'm going - you both get better, and neither of you is left trailling for long - this can be very important in business, again, look at how AMD is trailling at the moment - that must be hurting them bigtime.

### Post by coolen on 2007-08-13
It's my belief that some companies (Novell, Red Hat, Canonical) do actually care about FOSS. They're out to make money, just like any other company, but they do it in different ways. You can pay for support, for example, which makes total sense.

The difference is this: these companies innovate to gain market share. Microsoft innovates to keep market share.
So, these companies come up with new and better features, refine and hone their products, trying to make a truly great system that's free and open source, in the hopes that people will use it. Microsoft introduces new features to catch up with their competition, to remove any "real" incentive for people to use those system. Of Linux, OS X, and Windows, Windows was the last to get desktop widgets, a decent desktop search, a compositing window manager (talking built-in, of course. We don't get desklets from Canonical, but that's part of the Linux way).

While Apple has done a fantastic job creating a nice, polished system on their own, they've taken the same basic philosophy of innovation. For these companies who develop FOSS, this may just be their preferred method (since code can be shared and improvements spread evenly), or they may really believe in it.
In any case, Linux distros are, for the most part, not in competition. We're looking at getting Linux a decent market, not Ubuntu, openSUSE or Linspire (although I'm sure each would prefer to be in the lead).
