### Post by PeterJS on 2008-01-02
Let's talk history. In the beginning (1960's) there was Unix, and there were a bunch of flavors and complicated history, in the 70's UC Berkley released the Berkley Software Distribution which was a Unix variant, given that BSD was released under an open license it fractured over the last 30 years in to a whole family of BSDs: Open, Dragonfly, Net, Midnight, etc. These days BSD is a name of an overarching class of Unix descendants generally licensed under the BSD licensed.

In 1984 Stallman and the FSF started writing a free unix clone. The FSF clone had two big components the Hurd micro kernel and the GNU userland. The GNU userland was widely successfully and forms the basis of most linux systems these days. Where as Hurd was a unmitigated failure, and still good fodder for humor these days, rumor has it DNF will be released before the Hurd.

In 1991 Linus started working on the linux kernel that was dependent on the proprietary Minix userland. The linux kernel continued to grow and catch on with people.

This made for a interesting combination, Linus had a free kernel, and the FSF had a free userland. So if you stuck the two together you could have an entire free operating system, and that's exactly what was done. So the more technically correct name for Linux is GNU/Linux, the only people that care are the FSF and some hard core debian folk, it's a touchy subject and best not talked about. Most people are lazy and use the shorter Linux name, which is what's caught on with people.
