### Post by x64Jimbo on 2007-03-12
There is no definitive Linux tutorial that takes you from A to Z in a simple fashion, because every Linux distro is different. I can tell you that many of the distros have very complete wikis that are specific to their own. Ubuntu has one [here]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com/") and one [here.]("http://ubuntuguide.org/wiki/Ubuntu_Edgy")
Both of those sites are great. Unfortunately, Linux and computers in general are really hard to write linear tutorials for because there are so many ways to do things, and they change (mostly for the better) very frequently. This is why you had trouble with Linux for Dummies (I had one of these as well and found its commands and configurations completely outdated).
The best approach is to learn through others. When people give you a series of commands, ask them what those commands are doing, and try to understand it. It really shouldn't take 6 months to get streaming audio working. Can I be of any assistance?

### Post by x64Jimbo on 2007-03-12
There really is no easy route for Linux or any other computer system. There's the dumb way and the smart way, but no "easy" way. The smart way is to learn by application, and to learn from your mistakes. The dumb way is to just copy and paste every command that someone gives you in a forum without knowing what they do. And when it breaks something because the person helping you didn't have all the facts, the dumb way is to reinstall from scratch. It fixes the problem, yes. But it does not fix you. You have to BE the system upgrade that happens when something goes wrong. Unlike your car, you don't have a mechanic to fix things when they break. You have to know how to fix things like borked xorg.conf files and misconfigured network interfaces. You have to know how to use fsck. And these are skills that you will never acquire unless you take the smart road and stick to it when you think your system is broken. It will cost you more time in the short run, but later down the road when you break something, you'll be able to tweak that conf file or reload that kernel module, and it will only take you 30 seconds versus 45 minutes for a reinstall.
