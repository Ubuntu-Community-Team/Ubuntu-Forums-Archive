Here are the rules I was recommended to follow:

1) Check the repos for the software.. many times it's there.
2) If it isn't in the repos, look for a .deb package **that is specifically made for Ubuntu**. Using Debian .deb's is not a good idea and can lead to problems.
3) If it isn't in the repos, and there is no Ubuntu.deb, compile it yourself.

Anything outside those rules will eventually lead to problems. Several veteran Ubuntu users have recommended that to me.

### Post by Marsolin on 2007-01-16
I run a website called [Linux App Finder]("http://linuxappfinder.com/") that would help your search.  The Video Editing category can be found [here]("http://linuxappfinder.com/multimedia/videoeditors").  You can also find categories for the other you mentioned.

If you want to stick to apps that are in the Ubuntu repos only you can adjust the filter options on the website for that.

I personally use [Avidemux]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/avidemux") for video editing, but I've been meaning to try out [KDEnlive]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/kdenlive") as well.

For photo editing I use [digiKam]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/digikam").  It's a photo manager.  Some other similar apps are [F-Spot]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/f-spot") and [Picasa]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/picasa").

If you need a more general image editor then I'd try the [Gimp]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/gimp") or [Krita]("http://linuxappfinder.com/package/krita").

For drawing there are a wide range of options listed [here]("http://linuxappfinder.com/graphics/drawing").

Educational software is a pretty broad and you didn't specify any details, but you can find all of them that I'm currently tracking [here]("http://linuxappfinder.com/education").

I hope this helps.

### Post by Delkster on 2007-01-16
> **nerves said:**
> Is there something I should look for when looking for software? I.E. Debian GNU

Looking around and searching in **Applications > Add/Remove** is generally a start. If you can find something there that would seem to satisfy your needs, the installation is generally very easy. On the other hand, searching may not always be very easy because the application descriptions may not use exactly the same words that you're thinking of even if they would actually fit your needs.

If you try just googling around, try adding "ubuntu" in your search terms. Otherwise you'd generally end up finding just Windows software. Even if you find something interesting with google or something else, and even locate the website of the software project, it's generally a good idea to check if the program is available in Ubuntu in Add/Remove Applications or, if that fails, in the Synaptic Package Manager. Installing from the Ubuntu repositories using those tools is generally easier than installing random packages from the web, and often they also integrate better with the rest of the system.
