### Post by anaconda on 2006-11-24
There are 2 good (small) linux distributions that would run well in your laptop. (OK. actually there are many more, but these are the more popular..)
They are 
Puppy linux
and
DamnSmallLinux

of those Puppy linux is easier to use (more like windows). They even have instruction VIDEOS in their pages, and ofcourse lots of manuals etc..
However I like DSL more, because I have used DSL and actually learned a LOT about linux, because you have to do some things manually from the command-line.. DSL is also better in identifying and setting up hardware automatically..
If your laptop cant boot from CD (old ones cant) then you can make the DSL boot floppy, which boots from floppy-drive, and continues loading DSL from CD.

Taking your laptop to a shop to get help is a BAD idea. First of all they would charge you more than the laptop is worth, and their advice would propably be to buy a new laptop (from them preferably).. 

The MD5 file was just for verifying that the download/burning to CD was 100% succesfull, you dont have to do anything with it and  you dont need to save it anymore.

But it is still possible to install DSL to your laptop. There was a method called poor-mans-install, in which you just copy the DSL-image to your windows drive to a folder called /KNOPPIX, and then set lilo or grub boot loader so that it boots the DSL image or your existing windows98. So that you would have both os:s at the same time.

The easiest way would propably be to take the hard-drive from your laptop, install it to some other machine, which can boot from CD and install DSL to your hard-disk in that machine, then move the hd back to your laptop.

### Post by scbonney on 2007-02-25
I just installed an old Debian distro on a Thinkpad 380ED with 80 MB RAM and a 2 gig hard drive. [ I'm crowing tonight.] 

On many of the older laptops it was not possible to boot from the CD drive. I helped someone with a toshiba similar to yours and finally found a floppy drive at a computer supplier. I paid $25 bucks for it.   The floppy drive has a cute little connector that looks like a mutant USB and fits in the side of the Toshiba. Actnet Advanced Technology Corp in San Francisco is where I got it.

Another problem I ran into with this Toshiba is that the batteries did not charge until I got the the toshiba Utility from the toshiba site.  I have read about people copying cd files to a partition and installing from them.. Haven't done it though.  Some of the early laptops had special nonstandard engineering. 

The cd drives on these older laptops may not read quicly enough to read some isos... I have burned some at low speed and hope they will read, haven't tried yet.  The info I have says use CD -R  not CD -RW and burn at a slow speed.  My CD drive is 8X on the thinkpad,  

Prob recycling these old machines would be best but if they work they can be great backups and learning tools.  If your's is in good a condition as the one I worked with It may be running for a while yet.

I'm a noob, just put Ubuntu on my desktop last month and I've been playing around with this stuff to get comfortable with command line.

u can find lots of info doing google searches.  Try laptop name + linux and see what turns up.
