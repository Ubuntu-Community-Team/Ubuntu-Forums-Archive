### Post by BryArms on 2008-02-11
I have been told that ram frequency is dropped on a stick to match the motherboard or the speed of the slowest stick available; whichever is lower.  And a stick of ram could exceed its rated value on a motherboard that is faster than the stick's rating, but don't count on it.

### Post by Rocket2DMn on 2008-02-11
Those numbers are the clock speeds and need to be compatible with the clock speed of the Front Side Bus on your motherboard.  That is what connects your processor to your RAM, so your processor, mobo, and RAM all have to have a common understanding in order to communicate with eachother.  I think all the RAM used at any given time needs to be running at the same speed.

### Post by Krydahl on 2008-02-11
Go to [http://www.crucial.com/](http://www.crucial.com/)

Use their memory upgrade tool entering the model of mobo. If the board can take it, mixing different speeds of RAM should work. An overclocker will worry about slowdowns because the two sticks of RAM don't quite sync, but I doubt you'll see any difference without running detailed benchmarks.

As far as I know, you don't have to have the RAM and the bus speed matching - as long as the mobo can cope with the type of RAM you put in. You might not be using the RAM to its full capability, but it ought to work.

The other advantage of buying from a dealer like Crucial is they tend to swap their RAM with no quibbles if you've bought something they've recommended for a machine that doesn't work properly.
