[http://www.linuxcommand.org/](http://www.linuxcommand.org/) is very helpful.

To go back to the difference between Windows and Linux, one of the most fundamental differences that no one has mentioned yet is the philosophy behind them. 

Windows is proprietory. You can't see the code, you have no idea what it might be doing that it's not telling you, and you (or, more likely, various security experts) can't audit the code to see if it's safe. Linux, on the other hand, is open source. Anyone can look at the code, check it, make improvements and so on.

Windows also has a totally differing license scheme. You pay for a license to use it, and it has many restrictions. You're tied to one machine, you can't make changes, you can't give a copy to your friends, you can't tinker with it to see how it works and so on. Basically, a Windows license takes away a lot of the freedoms you may want to have while using a computer. Linux, on the other hand, encourages you to give it to your friends, make copies, make changes, play around with the code; it's totally open, encouraging people to build software for it and encouraging interoperability.

Edit: You asked about performance and technical differences. The proprietory / open source aspect plays into this as well. A company like Microsoft has no interest in making a high-performing product. What they care about, reasonably enough, is making a profit. They care about performance only to the extent that it will encourage you to buy their product. If they have a near-monopoly, performance is not important to them. The same holds for interoperability. They make their money by making you upgrade, and one way to do this is to deliberately cripple various aspects of their software. That's why, for example, compatibility between various versions of Word is broken, and why (when IE was totally dominant) they deliberately broke open internet standards, making sure that everyone making websites had to comply. And once they had, IE wasn't updated for years, until Firefox finally forced to them to start competing and improving performance again.
