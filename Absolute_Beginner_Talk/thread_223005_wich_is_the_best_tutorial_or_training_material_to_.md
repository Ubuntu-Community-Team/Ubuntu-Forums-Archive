### Post by ubuntuuser on 2006-07-25
Read books and play with your system, then read some more because when you read the first time you overlooked most of the really important parts. You can learn with ubuntu as well as with gentoo or any other distro, it doesn't make any difference with what linux flavor you start. Generally, you should gain some experience with deb-based distros as well as with rpm-based ones, and then try out some source-based distros. In the beginning I found [this]("http://www-128.ibm.com/developerworks/linux") particularly helpful. Good luck.

### Post by brentoboy on 2006-07-25
go to distrowatch.com

download iso files for all 100 of the top distro's

install them each in turn over and over, playing around as you go.

be curious

format whenever you decide you want to try something else.

leave slackware on your PC for a week, until you freak out, and then come back to ubuntu.

make a dedicated decision to not format ubutnu again, no matter how bad it gets.

work with ubntu until you can watch the new clips on cnn.com

share files and printers via samba with your other PCs (the ones you have windows on becuase you still like to play 3d games)

build a website (to be viewed from within your lan)

turn your computer into a router, and put it between the rest of your network and the internet.

start filtering the websites your family and friends are looking at, just for fun.

after that, you will have a pretty good idea of how linux works.

then, you can start recommending to your boss to "take the plunge"

--
short answer: play around with it for a while.  have specific goals in mind about what you want it to do, and then keep working until you become comfortable doing it.

### Post by mcvos on 2006-07-26
Don't underestimate this. Going from newbie straight to expert isn't easy. You need to be dedicated, disciplined, inquisitive and perfectionist. Make sure you don't just get things working, make sure you understand *why* they work and why other things don't work. Make sure you understand the advantages and disadvantages of different approaches. I've been using linux on and off for over 10 years, and I'm still basically a newbie because I just don't care enough about operating systems. I'm a programmer, not an administrator.
