### Post by msak007 on 2007-03-12
Don't worry you won't get bored, there's tons to learn and you can never really learn everything (or so it seems, I learn something new every day). Read books or other online documentation, subscribe to RSS feeds of various Linux related sites (tuxmachines.org is a pretty good one that usually links to tutorials or how-tos), try to help out in the forums if you're able to, do some bug triaging with projects you're interested in (everybody needs as much help as possible), try Feisty and fill out bug reports, etc. There's just so much to do if you're not content just being a user, the list is endless it all depends on what you want. If you feel adventurous I could also suggest you try other distros that force you to work with configuration files, e.g Gentoo, Slackware, Arch, etc., just to see what it's like to do everything manually. As long as you don't switch on us, that is :).

### Post by gnoel on 2007-03-19
I would like to riff on the comment of rusty4r, "come to this forum and teach the rest of us."

What I see in Linux communities in my dabbling over the last few years are two counter-currents.  On the one hand, many Linux users enjoy the do-it-yourself ethic of building what you want from the ground up.  On the other hand, there is a growing realization that if Linux is to grow and cut in to MS hegemony, both fun and function must be easier to implement.

Riding bicycles should not be restricted to those who build them from parts.  Neither should using Linux.  I think the Ubuntu community fundamentally understands that there needs to be a compromise on both ends.

The pure Linux enthusiast has to be gracious enough to allow others to enjoy the benefits of Linux without putting in the long hours and committing more of their precious life force to the project than they are willing to give.  And more casual Linux users have to realize that nothing worthwhile comes about free and easy.

To this end, I'd like to propose to MCSE_Crossover, or somebody else who has recently made the jump to proficiency, to get together to write a "best practices" documentation to help beginners configure their systems without spending hours snorkeling the internet to receive conflicting advice.  Two good areas to start are:

1) Multimedia support
2) Printing support

Thanks!
