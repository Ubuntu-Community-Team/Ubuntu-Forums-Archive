It's kind of difficult to answer your question without going on about cost and such, because it really is an integral part of why they're not available on Linux.

Dreamweaver - Industry Standard? I know very few people who actually use Dreamweaver, but whatever. There are plenty of web design environments already available for Linux. It really depends what you need to do. Most are satisfactory - what exactly are you after? All you need to develop a web-page are a text editor, an image development package, and some other software depending on what your site is all about. For wysiwyg editors, take a look at nVu - it's very popular here. You may also want to take a look at Amaya - which, although is apparantly more difficult to get to grips with - always produces standards compliant pages (unless you break the compliance deliberately, obviously), as it is endorsed by the W3C.

Microsoft Office 2007. Bit of a brick wall there. Microsoft Office is unlikely to ever make an appearance on Linux - but most people get on well with OpenOffice or Abiword (but Abiword is only a word processor, not a full Office suite). There are a number of commercial Office suites available, but I've only used OpenOffice myself, as it does everything I need to much better than MS Office (although I've yet to use 2007).

Photoshop - This may yet make an appearance on Linux, if Linux' market share grows more. It's already on Mac, so there's no reason, aside from profit, why it shouldn't be ported to Linux. There's no argument that it is one of the leading packages available. However, The GIMP is actually a very advanced photoshop alternative - its real problem is its interface, which most people struggle with at first. The few who stick with it seriously are almost evangelical about it - once you know how it works, Photoshop quality images are easily possible. There are a number of GIMP specific galleries available, you should check them out. If the interface is still bothering you, take a look at [GIMPShop](http://www.gimpshop.com/), which is a modified version of GIMP to look and feel more like Photoshop. I don't really know of any other comparable packages. You could take a look at [Inkscape](http://www.inkscape.org/) - but that's more akin to Adobe Illustrator, in that it produces vector art.

Quick Books etc - Take a look at GNUCash. I'm not too familiar with this kind of software sorry.

Britannica / Encarta - again, don't know much about this kind of software.

### Post by jvc26 on 2007-02-20
Well to get the MS one out of the way first: Microsoft Office 2007: No it will never be available for Linux - its designed for windows and has no reason to be ported by Microsoft to a 'rival' OS. Instead you can use OO - its different from Microsoft Office, but it does pretty much everything that you need it to do.

Photoshop - You're right here - GIMP is no competitor, but it is being developed and so will over time improve. You can run PS 7 and 8 (I believe) in wine, but with varying degrees of success. Again I doubt that Adobe will release the source, and that the market share of Linux is not high enough to warrant porting it for official release.

Simulation tools for science. - Yes these exist - as an example the Engineering department at my university do one hell of a lot of their simulations on specially coded apps (linux apps, written by the guys in the department) for their simulations. For the average Joe, these are unnecessary, but I think for those who use such simulations, especially in the University environment they are available should you want/need them.

Educational software like the impressive array available to Windows and or Mac&#8217;s for both children and adults. - Here these are predominantly produced as proprietary applications. For a Desktop environment which holds such a small market share it is not in the interests of the companies who produce these apps to put the time into porting them across. Unfortunately its a common held truth, that to port apps for little financial gain for many companies is seen as a waste of time.

Dreamweaver - There are very good alternatives to this (presuming that you're writing the code rather than using dreamweaver's point and click design thing) To make good clean websites it often makes sense to do the coding rather than to let a computer do it all and come out with convoluted code makes the site load more slowly and looks messy. I'm not sure about it being the industry standard as most of the people I know round here use other software to do web design.

Finally I get to the last point, which is the one which I really was interested in:
> 
Will there ever be software available to Linux users that is quite excellent on Windows/Mac.

This is interesting as it has 3 vital presumptions.
1/ That current software available for Linux users is less good than Windows/Mac equivalents
2/ That windows/mac software is somehow inherently better than Linux
3/ That this windows/mac software should therefore be ported to Linux.

I shall go through my opinions on each in turn.

1/ I think this point is awfully flawed. Yes, some are definitely not as good and you have highlighted some shortcomings (especially with Photoshop which is the only application I would ever be tempted to use Windows for) However, you neglect all the very good software available for Linux - for instance, GIMP though not perfect is functional, OO works well and does everything you need it for, applications such as Inkscape and Xara act as excellent illustrators (and Xara, when it is finished in testing and comes out with a solid product) really will give Inkscape a run for its money. Firefox - a perfect example of Open source software being better than the windows alternative IE. Thunderbird and Evolution - both excellent pieces of software.

2/ Just because a piece of software is the most popular/most used doesnt make it the best. I would say that just because the apps that are used on windows are used on windows doesnt make them the best. This is inconsequent of whether windows itself is good/bad, it is merely just because it is the most widely used OS and so things used on windows tend to be seen as best - familiarity isnt the best thing - IE and Firefox give you a perfect example of that.

3/ Some applications would be excellent if they were ported to Linux - Photoshop being the crux of the matter. I fear however due to a lack of financial incentive, and a definite wish of Adobe not to go and give out the code for Photoshop, this is very unlikely that it will be made available, at least not in the short term. There are many alternatives out there, some which I would definitely choose over the windows more popular choices - Nvu/Bluefish over Dreamweaver for one example.

So to conclude this, I would say yes some of those apps mentioned are really good, but they are not the be-all and end-all of software, there are many alternatives, usable, faster and more stable in the case of some of them. Its unlikely many of them will get transferred to Linux, but in the meantime why not take a look at what else is out there - or even more start looking into coding and maybe help with the apps which are available - that could really make a difference. The more people willing to cooperate to make OSS better the better OSS will get, and then you won't need to carry on in the loop of proprietary software.
