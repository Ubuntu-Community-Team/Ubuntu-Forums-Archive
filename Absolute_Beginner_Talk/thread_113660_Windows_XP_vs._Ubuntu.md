### Post by Hitchhiker427 on 2006-01-06
well, I'll start off by saying that I've recently started to use Ubuntu.  I did not replace Windows XP with Ubuntu, but I do dual-boot with XP as my primary.  Seeing as I just made the switch, and I kinda have a decent understanding of Ubuntu now, I'll try to answer.

The transition may or may not be smooth.  It really depends on what you use your computer for.  I'll say that the transition would be smooth for the average computer user who only uses the computer for basic purposes.  For instance, if you only need a computer to surf the internet, IM, listen to music, watch movies, etc. then any operating system (linux, windows, OSX) would be just fine for you.  However, sometimes we get attached to specific programs that are neccessary for our computing experience.  I am an engineering student at college.  Some of the software I MUST use for my course work is unavailable in linux, and the "alternatives" are not nearly as good as the real thing.  So, what you use a computer for will be the biggest determining factor as for a smooth transition.  

As for most simple, I'm not sure.  I tried out some live CDs... Ubuntu just seemed more "polished" and ready to be used in a desktop computer than any of the others I tried.  This is not to discredit the other distros, but just simply to say that Ubuntu is a good choice, but whether it is the best choice is up to personal preferences.

As for the .doc files and such, there exists Open Office that can handle those files.  But, be warned, despite what any linux diehard tells you, open office is not as good as MS Office.  While, yes, you will be able to read the text from your office documents, it might not be exactly the same as it was in MS Office.  For example, every .doc file with diagrams (physics, circuits, etc.) that I've tried screws up the diagrams.  Simple power-points lost their custom formatting, and looked ugly in OO while they looked fine in MS Office.  Also, this may not concern you, but the lack of VBA in OO is a big deal for me.. especially in Excel.  So, yes, your office files will be read-able, but don't expect the free alternative to Office to be as good as the real deal.  As for PDFs, so far they work fine for me.

DivX and Xvid work fine here, as do Java and Shockwave.

As for sharing Windows files and reading NTFS partitions go, this is also pretty easy.  Just remember that you'll be able to read and copy from and NTFS partition, but not write or copy to.

Also, as far as hardware (eg. soundcards, networks), it just depends.  My integrated sound works fine, but my integrated network does not (this will apparently be fixed in Dapper).

I wouldn't recommend jumping into Linux unless you're ready for all of that.  Personally, I love having my Windows partition, because it's great to just have an OS that works right out of the box to boot to if Linux is acting screwy.  But, by all means, that's up to you.  I hope I helped.
