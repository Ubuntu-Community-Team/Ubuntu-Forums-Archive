### Post by FreewareFan on 2008-03-30
Here's the deal with heat control in your computer..  IF the side is on, then the only way to remove the heat that is generated in your computer, is by the use of exhaust fans.  

Now, IF your computer side is left off, there is no use for the fans, regarding exhausting the generated heat, because the entire side of your tower is off, letting the generated heat exit the box naturally.  You will find that everything will be much cooler with the side left off, I assure you!

However, as has been said previously, there is a certain risk involved with leaving the side off.  Small animals, children, bouncing balls, etc...   But, if you are in a situation where you can leave it off, it is the optimal solution..

### Post by FreewareFan on 2008-03-30
I defy anyone here to post the value of temperatures of their cpu, gpu, and hd temps with, and without, the side of their tower on. 

I assure you, that in 100% of all reported temps, the temps WILL be lower with the side off the tower.  Period.  

As I said in my original comment, fans and designed airflow are most certainly very important, WITH the side on.   With the side off, those factors no longer come into play. Ask yourself why the need for the exhaust fans in the first place.  The reason is because the side of the box is on, and the generated heat has no place to escape.  So, airflow and cooling techniques were developed.  BUT, with the side off, all those factors fly out the window, because the natural escape of heat takes care of all those things.  Of the two situations, the best way of the heat to be removed from the system, is by the natural escape of generated heat.  Just look at it logically...   You have a couple of forced air exit points with the side on, and you have the entire area of the side of the exposed case with the side off, for heat to escape  

Just take the time to see for yourself....   It doesn't take much effort to remove the side from your tower.  See for yourself what the temps are in each scenario, and you'll find what I say to be true.

No, that's not the truth. It might apply on badly designed cases, and setups where messy cables or something else restricts the airflow, but on a good case you do get lower temperatures with case side closed. Also it's important to notice that while your temperature monitors will only show you temps from CPU, GPU and possibly some other parts of the machine, there are many other parts of the system that also generate heat, and the airflow generated in a closed case plays important role in cooling these parts.

On my desktop machine opening the side makes temperatures rise by about 4 degrees, and the power feed circuitry on the motherboard gets considerably hotter than when the case is closed.

On the other machine, with a cheap case, the CPU and GPU temps will go down if the side door is opened but t the same time hard disks get hot as there is no more proper airflow going around them to cool them down. Of course it would be possible to add more fans right next to the drives to cool them, but simply closing the case side works just as well, with no extra noise..

(I'm not saying that on _every_machine keeping the side closed would result in cooler setup, juts that if the case is well designed and there are no extra restrictions for airflow the setup will run cooler with case side closed.)

Actually what I'd suggest is trying what you suggested and opening the case side, but if if the temperatures decrease with case opened instead of leaving the case open then removing all restrictions to airflow (for example replacing ribboned PATA cables with rounded ones and using tie wraps to wrap all cables together) and closing the case again. If that doesn't help I recommend getting a better case. Leaving the side open will result in noisy machine that gathers a lot of dust.
