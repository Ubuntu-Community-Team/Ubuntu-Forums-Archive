### Post by cogsprocket on 2006-08-03
I'm running Ubuntu on a Sony Vaio VGN-FS840/W.  It runs with very few problems.

If you can deal with these issues:

*Lack of Hotkey functionality *

You'll have problems with this migrating to anything but the installed OS.  For example, this notebook came with WinXP Home installed.  Doing a lot of work with servers that require me to join a domain, do web development and such WinXP Pro quickly became neccessary.  Sad days, hotkey support was lost once I ran an upgrade.

*Quirky Sound Controls*

This is really not so bad once you configure your sound a bit.  This may actually be pretty common across Ubuntu installs and, really, it's not such a nuisance (excepting that you want your notebook to be quite for the most part.)  Essentially what *should* be the master volume control only controls sound over the desktop.  Other applications require their own adjustments.  Also your logon and logoff sounds tend to be as loud as they want regardless.  Again, not really a big problem.

*Networking Is Touchy*

Both network adapters function flawlessly but, you have to babysit them a bit.  In my experience the wireless and ethernet adapters tend to fight each other but it's pretty simple to quell them both.


*Magic Gate Does Not Function*

Unless you've got a device that uses this and you transfer a lot of data to and from it this is not really an issue.  It was a selling point for me because it made transferring data to and from my PSP easy, but, after the novelty wore off (and with the installation of Ubuntu) I've really not missed it.



What you *do* get from the VGN-FS840/W:

Wireless that works -  I've had no problem getting my wireless to work with this laptop.  It makes for a great mobile device.

A thin, lightweight notebook - This has been, by far, the best notebook I've ever owned and Ubuntu runs like a dream on it.  Despite the few quirks the two are a match!  This machine has a bit of power to it as well (understand I've added a gig of RAM for VM test environments so take the power bit with a grain of salt.)

For a good development machine.  A great basic Ubuntu notebook (don't expect gaming miracles from this machine) it'll do a lot of things people want out of a notebook with very little effort.  I recommend it without question.

The specs on my machine are as follows:

Intel Pentium M 1.7Ghz 750
Intel GMA 900 Onboard video (128MB shared memory)
512MB DDR2 533MHz RAM
100GB Hard Drive
CD-RW/DVD-RW (I forget the speed)
3 USB2/1 Firewire port

It may be difficult to find this sytem for a low price (I got it on a great deal and the DoD reimbursed a portion of the cost) but it has really exceeded my expectations as far as the list of things that **must** work go.  There are a few quirks, but I'm holding out hope that they're minor and fixes can be found or future support will be available.

Just my two cents, but I don't think you can go wrong with it.
