### Post by Patrick K on 2007-05-23
I think one of the problems with open source, and I don't blame anyone, is developers work on what they are interested in (I'd do the same). However, if (s)he loses interest, the project languishes. Other times the project gets far enough to be released and the developer moves on. With no central authority to direct development, projects reach a certain state and stay there until some other person gets interested in it.  It will likely stay that way as long as developers are doing Linux software in their free time. With a paid team and a commercial model, there is an incentive to produce a more polished and user friendly product (not always done even with commercial SW). Sometimes commercial competition has some advantages. I suspect this is why Linux for the desktop will always be a hobbyist OS. 

The server side is somewhat different. With support being sold there are paid engineers that can work uninterrupted to correct problems. Paying $500-1000 dollars a year for support is nothing to large companies and it's much cheaper than buying seat licenses and support from MS.

I actually like tinkering with the OS. But when I need to do something productive I need the OS get out of the way. More than once I have been in a very creative mood. I've recorded tracks of, what I think, are very good music, only to be stymied but some issue with the OS. Creativity goes out the window to be replaced with fussing with the OS and waiting for nonexistent answers in the Forums.. While Windows works well, the lack of being able to tinker is a drawback for me. Linux is for tinkerers, Windows is for productivity. It will likely remain that way for a long time.
