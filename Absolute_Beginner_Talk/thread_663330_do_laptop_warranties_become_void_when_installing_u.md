### Post by ugm6hr on 2008-01-10
Hardware manufacturers will not "support" non-installed or non-recommended OS.

Obviously, Linux / Ubuntu does not have any effect on your hardware.  But if there is a problem, you will either have to wipe the HD or reinstall the original OS before sending the hardware back.

Some manufacturers are more sensible than others with this.  Obviously, if your battery or your LCD screen backlight etc dies, this has nothing to do with the OS, and should not require you to do anything on the HD to fix it.  However, in order to *test* the fix (or prior to fixing), they need to operate your hardware with an OS - hence they insist on the OS being one that they are familiar with.

I can tell you there are lots of posts online that Acer insist on Windows being on the machine (or, if in Asia, their version of LInux) if you return hardware to them.  As you mention - others may not.

My advice - this is not a particularly good reason to select a manufacturer.  Although I am sure that lots of Linux users will disagree.  I would select hardware that actually works with Linux, and worry about getting it fixed under warranty *if it breaks*.  You should be backing up your HD regularly anyway, so having to wipe your HD to get it repaired is not the end of the world.

### Post by scorp123 on 2008-01-10
> **Andrew Golightly said:**
>  HP's warranties stay valid when Ubuntu does get installed.  Having worked for HP in the past seven years I am qualified to say this: Linux is regarded as being a "strategic OS" internally at HP. Most if not all of HP's hardware can be somehow made to work with Linux. This is especially true for most of their printers. As far as laptops go: If you want to play it safe stay away from any laptops that have Broadcom chips in them (especially Broadcom WLAN chipsets). My personal recommendation would be to go for the laptops that say "Intel" all over the place. Intel CPU, Intel WiFi, Intel LAN chip, Intel graphics ... those should pretty much work 100% "out of the box" with any decent Linux distro (all the HP laptops I have owned so far sure did!).

Also: If you can afford it, go for HP's business laptops, e.g. the NC* or NX* or Ex-Compaq "Evo" product lines. My experience so far was that those are probably easier to get working with Linux (again: Stay away from anything that has Broadcom chips inside!!). The home user "Pavilion" product lines may have built-in webcams or fancy sound cards that may not properly work (e.g. I can't get the webcam on my dv2108ea working no matter what I do or try ... but all the rest works OK).

Also, should you go for a HP laptop: Some recent models ship ***without*** OS installer CD's. Instead those laptops have a special "System Repair Partition". Should you hose your system you're supposed to hit a button (usually **F11**) during system boot and the system is supposed to repair itself ....  **Nice theory. Except it doesn't always work.**

Especially if you repartition your harddrive(s) it could be that this repair tool gets totally confused and will not be able to do anything at all, potentially leaving you with a dead system that doesn't boot and won't do anything.

**So to avoid this: Read the HP product documentation!!!**  Somewhere on those leaflets you get there should be a hotline number or a web URL where you can order your **"System Repair" DVD's for free** (or some very small fee).

So first thing you do when you unpack your laptop and boot the pre-installed Windows for the first time: Check if you received any "System Repair" CD's or DVD's ... if they're missing: **Go to that URL or pick a phone and call that hotline and *insist* that this "System Repair" DVD be shipped to you!!**  The wise guy on the other end of the phone will try to talk you out of it, he will say BS like "But sir, you don't need those DVD's, you have the 'system repair' partition on your harddisk .... "  **As I said: Be polite but stubborn and insist that they ship those CD's or DVD's to you.**

With the help of those "System Repair" DVD's you can partition or hose your harddisks any way you want .... those DVD's will restore the system back to the factory settings as if nothing had happened. Also: You can later on upgrade your harddisks to a bigger size and still put the laptop back to the factory settings. Nice to have feature!

I am aware that you haven't decided anything yet ... But should you decide to go for a HP laptop, then the points above may hopefully help you to get around some gotchas.

Hope this helped. :)

That's probably true.  But they need to return the hardware to you in a functioning state (i.e. so you can use it).  Also - when you ring their "support" line, they need to be clear that your problem isn't a software problem.  As I said - this doesn't actually make sense, but the manufacturers think it makes their life easier (hence your laptop is cheaper).

> Fair enough... any recommendations on a set of good laptop manufacturers?

No such thing.  Asus is supposed to be good at using Linux-supported hardware, but I don't think that is true any more.  IBM also used to be good wih Linux (and offered their corporate customers Linux pre-installed, I think), but I think that Lenovo have not followed this policy.  Apple also used to be good (by accident), but the current model is not.

I hope you get the message - things change from model-to-model, and sometimes even within model derivatives.  For example, my Acer 5051AWXMi is a 5050 derivative (with different HD, processor, RAM); however, Acer swapped from Broadcom to Atheros wifi mid-production of 5050, so some work well, some don't.

My suggestion - go to a store with a LiveCD and ask to try it out.  That will give you a good indication.  Things to try - wifi, card-reader, webcam (& dial-up modem if important to you), since these are generally the things that cause issues.  Obviously if it doesn't boot at all, it may be a bit tricky to use Ubuntu (alhtough not impossible).  You can then just buy it, or look for the same model elsewhere (immoral, I know).
