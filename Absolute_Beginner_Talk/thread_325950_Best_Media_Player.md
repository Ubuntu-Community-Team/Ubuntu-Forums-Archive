### Post by pseudonym on 2007-01-01
> **OttifantSir said:**
> The Linux-philosophy, as far as I have been able to comprehend, is to have an application for every task, and to do that task great. Not many tasks for one application, and the application doing a less than state-of-the-art job performing these tasks.
I think that's pretty much on the button.

You have to remember, it's not just 'video' and 'audio' that's in play here. There are other user-interface features that distinguish one player from another... 

For instance, I use Gxine to watch digital TV, but VLC or Xfmedia to watch other videos. Why? Because the Gxine interface is well designed for DTV, where the other 2 either don't support it very well (xfmedia) or have the function poorly iplemented (vlc). For other video, VLC will play whatever Gxine doesn't, and loads faster.

And with an audio player, the feature I want is the ability to scroll quickly through my music, without having to bother setting up extra playlists or play around with albums/artists/genres and whatnot (though TBH these days I only have about 10GB of mp3s/oggs, so it's quite manageable without that stuff). And Xfmedia performs this task admirably. So does XMMS, which I use from time to time for an aesthetic change. Things like Amarok and Rhythmbox hold little interest for me.

So I imagine it would be pretty hard to come up with an 'all-in-one' media player that a) loads quickly; b) is equally well suited for all modern media, both support-wise and user-interface-wise; and c) doesn't drown you in a sea of over-configurability. And I guess that's why we have a proliferation of media players for Linux.

Hold up, the OP was asking what the best media player is on *windows*? Well, I'll answer that one with another question - if you run linux but still have windows, why on earth are you using it to watch videos and play music? ;)
