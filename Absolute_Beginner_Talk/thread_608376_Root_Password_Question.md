You set a root password? Congrats, you just made your system less secure. If I wanted to break into your computer, I know you have a root account and I can sit here for months brute forcing it. But, I can't brute force a locked root account and I can't brute force your other user accounts because I don't know the user names. You can return your root account back to its locked state by running:

```
sudo passwd -l root
```

Anything you need to do as administrator of an Ubuntu system can be done via sudo, kdesudo, or gksudo. Logging in to X as root is most likely to cause very serious trouble.

### Post by dwhitney67 on 2007-11-10
Basically with Ubuntu, you are supposed to perform sys admin duties "locally".

To answer your last question, you would need to copy the file from "wherever" to your Ubuntu system, then login to the Ubuntu system to perform sys admin duties.

$ scp /etc/motd sudo-user@ubuntuSystem:
$ ssh sudo-user@ubuntuSystem
$ sudo mv motd /etc

Yep, it awfully ridiculous to have to go thru so many steps; but that is what Ubuntu offers "out of the box".  In my opinion, it would be easier to enable your root account and also install "denyhosts" daemon (thus guarding yourself against brute-force attacks).

If your Ubuntu system is not on the open network (i.e. you have not enabled your router to forward any port-comm to it), then you really should have no worries about enabling the root account.

While ardchoille42 does have a valid point, there are ways to protect your system from brute-force attacks.  Start by assigning non-dictionary word passwords.  It is safe to write a cryptic password on a piece of paper and keep it next to your PC.  Nobody on the web will be able to read it (that is, the piece of paper).  If someone breaks into your domicile and "procures" your system, it is unlikely that they will pay attention to the scrap piece of paper with your password.  And if they do, it's really no big deal.  Either way, your PC will be gone.

P.S.  One does not need a password to view the files on your system.  All one would have to do is connect the HDD to an external HDD-enclosure, and voila mount every partition you have.
