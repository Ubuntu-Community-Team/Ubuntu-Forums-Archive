The critical thing to bear in mind when looking through this file is that each section gets defined, and then tied together at the end. So you'll have a section for each video card, a section for each monitor, a section for each "screen" that defines the display on one of the monitors from one of the video cards, and then a layout section at the end.

For each of the video cards, you'll need to know the Bus ID. There is a command to find it, but I can't remember what it is at the moment. Possibly **lspci -X | grep VGA**. You'll also need to put in which driver you'd like to use.

For each of the monitors, you'll need to find out which resolutions and refresh rates they can do. Either read the manuals or read the manufacturers' websites. Putting the wrong values in could break your monitors, or lead you to only having 640x480 available.

The Screen section just ties one video card to one monitor, defined by the name you gave each in their respective sections. The ServerLayout section describes how you'd like your two screens laid out.

It is all complicated, in that it involves editing a fairly hefty config file, but each section is quite straightforward. Just take your time, and make backups for if (when?) it goes wrong. There are other options, like Xinerama, but I didn't like them for reasons that are probably mostly due to my particular configuration. This setup means that you can move the mouse between the screens, and files, but not windows. It's also very cool. I'm very pleased that I took the time to set this up, and hopefully you will be too.

Good luck.

Pretty much, I think. As I said, take it slow. **man xorg.conf** would be a better page to read than the one I said earlier. Scroll down a bit for the useful information. And you must must must make a backup of your xorg.conf before you start. If it goes wrong because you've made a typo somewhere it's much easier to copy the backup back than to edit it back without a graphical display

In case you want to write the commands down on a piece of paper before you start, assuming you can only get online from that computer, ```
sudo cp /etc/X11/xorg.conf /etc/X11/xorg.conf.backup
``` will make a backup file of your current settings. When it all goes hideously wrong and you can't get an X display, **Ctrl-Alt-F2** will take you to a virtual terminal where you can log in and do command-line stuff. ```
sudo cp /etc/X11/xorg.conf.backup /etc/X11/xorg.conf
``` will copy your backup over the edited one. **Ctrl-Alt-F7** will take you back to your graphical screen. **Ctrl-Alt-Backspace** will kill (and hopefully restart) your X server so that you can try out your settings. Save everything first, since it's a bit abrupt.

Good luck again.
