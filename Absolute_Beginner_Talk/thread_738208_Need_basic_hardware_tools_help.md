### Post by cmnorton on 2008-03-30
First, you need at least one test (scratch/junk/experimental) system. Two would be even better. These would be systems that you have not specially configured with sound, your favorite text editor, and set up everything just so. You should be able to install over them without a second thought.

It is on these systems that you will install, set up daemons to run, load and unload modules, rebuilt the kernel, write shell scripts and do a lot of general learning. Somewhere in your hardware budget you employer is probably replacing desktop systems. See if you can grab one or two when they are being replaced, if you cannot order a couple of cheap desktops.
 
Second, you will get different responses from different people regarding training material, all of which are valid, because they are from different individuals' personal experiences. I would suggest purchasing O'Reilly's Linux in a Nutshell and some book on bash. O'Reilly has a guide. Ken O. Burtch Linux Shell Scripting With Bash ISBN 0768663490 Prentice Hal is another good onel.

You can also purchase support in lieu of formal training. Canonical supports Ubuntu. If you set up Ubuntu server, that is probably close enough to its Debian roots, that getting help on Ubuntu would also help you with Debian. 

For years ago this month, I walked into my own personal Twilight Zone. I became responsible for two major and two minor Informix-based applications, all written in various forms of Informix 4gl, and they were running on SCO Unix, on a Pentium II, and on a 9 GB mirrored SCSI.  

Keep chipping away, and you will do well. You just have to stay sane long enough for your efforts to start paying off.

Good luck.
