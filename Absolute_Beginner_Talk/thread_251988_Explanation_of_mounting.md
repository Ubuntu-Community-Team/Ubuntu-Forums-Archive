### Post by benfindlay on 2006-09-06
"Mounting" a drive in linux is the same as what windows does when it gives a drive an assigned letter (e.g. c:\)

There are some important differences, such as the fact that linux (and Mac OS for that matter) mount the drive according to its name/description. This has the advantage (for *serious* powerusers) that you are not limited to 26 local drives/partitions. Somebody is bound to say "who the hell has more than about 6 drives on an average pc?". Trust me, it's been known to happen

While windows gets around this problem with networks by allowing you to assign remote drives the same drive letter (with a tag saying "on server" or "on laptop" etc, linux has the major advantage that every drive is uniquely identified by its name, leading to a theoretical infinite number of partitions being available.

But this is a bit off topic, all you really need to know is that drives, cds, usb pen sticks etc are "mounted" and will appear on your Desktop as icons, labelled with their description.

Hope this helps!
