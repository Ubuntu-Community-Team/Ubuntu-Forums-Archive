### Post by djchandler on 2007-08-22
After reading all your questions, there's probably no one Linux user that can answer your questions. It sounds as if you are asking for guarantees that what you might want to do in the future will work. As much as I hate to say this, I think you would be happiest with an Intel-based Mac if that's truly what you want, guarantees. Macs will run Windows. Linux will run on an Intel-based Mac. If you want it all, spend your money on a Mac.  And don't buy a cheap one. Get the best hardware you can afford.

If you want to experiment, sweat a little to make things work, do some research, become at least a little bit geeky, then Linux may be for you. With the questions you are asking, the second best solution to the Mac is dual booting Windows and Linux. Personally, I like one OS per computer. I use XP for some stuff on one computer, Linux for other stuff on another computer. They can network together, use a dhcp server on a commercially available router that fully adheres to TCP/IP protocol. I can run VNC and run both systems from one desktop. Back in the 80s, BYTE columnist [Jerry Pournelle]("http://www.jerrypournelle.com/") proclaimed "one cpu, one user." I'll take that one step further and say, "one cpu, one user, one os." Only you can decide what stuff you want to to run where.

Nvidia does supply drivers online. Most video cards are supported well enough to get you up and running. We follow a philosophy here in terms of what the default settings and software repositories are--it must be open source, without restriction. All of us decide at some point we need something that isn't fully open source, like RealPlayer, or full speed 3D support for our Nvidia graphics card. That becomes essentially your problem to solve; you can count on plenty of support here to get it done. Somebody here knows how to answer your question, just not all your questions at once.

The world is a risky place. Something disastrous, like an earthquake, could happen in Silicon Valley and wipe out the manufacturer of THE vital part of your system. Less risk costs more money. More risk costs less money, and maybe even some sweat.

We welcome you to our community. Make a plan, and we can help you refine it. Ask a question, and somebody can answer it. Make a decision, and we can help support it.

Thank you for visiting the Ubuntu Forum. We look forward to your future visits.

Regards,

### Post by RomeReactor on 2007-08-22
Hi. Yes, switching from one operating system to another can sometimes be a daunting task, but in a short while you'll see Ubuntu is easy to use. Now, as for the questions:

*  Programs and drivers. In windows we're used to hunt the web for drivers and programs, downloading them from sites. In Ubuntu, most driver installation is done during the actual OS installation. If a particular piece of hardware doesn't work right away, you can search/ask in these forums and more likely than not someone will point you in the right direction. This means installing a driver from the repositories, which are huge pools of software; you can use a graphical interface to draw programs and driver from these pools; in this case, Ubuntu fetches the programs/drivers and installs them for you. It's only when there are no drivers (or correctly working ones) specific to your particular piece of hardware, or the program you want isn't there, that you download them from a site. By default Ubuntu will install an open source driver for your Nvidia card. This driver is very good, but doesn't do 3D rendering, so you'll need to get the official Nvidia drivers. There **are** Linux drivers in [Nvidia's site]("http://www.nvidia.com/object/unix.html"), but unless you need the absolute newest version, you can get them through the repositories, meaning Ubuntu will download them for you and install them.

* To run windows programs (including games) in Ubuntu, you use a program called [Wine]("http://www.winehq.org/"). Note that not all windows games or programs run correctly through Wine; many will not run at all. Take a look  at their [application database]("http://appdb.winehq.org/") (browse the top rated games/applications, or search for a specific one) to see what does work and what doesn't. Also take a look at the [Gaming & Leisure]("http://ubuntuforums.org/forumdisplay.php?f=93") section of these forums to find out how people have managed to get certain games working. Wine is available from the repositories, so there's no need to download it from their site, unless you are looking for a specific version that offers compatibility with a particular game.

* As for the Wacom tablet, I can't say as I've never used one.

* For a very good drawing/coloring program, Ubuntu comes with [Gimp]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GIMP") installed. If you're not comfortable with Gimp, ask around here as there are other programs you could try. 

* For Instant messaging, there's Gaim (now called [Pidgin]("http://pidgin.im/")) also installed by default. It supports many protocols, including those you mentioned.

* As Hobo Joe mentioned earlier, for iTunes there's [Amarok]("http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amarok_%28software%29") or other programs.

* Desktop Effects (Compiz, Beryl, Compiz Fusion): Ubuntu by default comes with Desktop Effects (Compiz) installed--but not activated. I suggest you become comfortable with Ubuntu before trying to install Beryl or Compiz Fusion, as these are still beta software and can lead to problems. Once you enable direct rendering with the official Nvidia drivers, you can use the effects.

### Post by coolen on 2007-08-23
For you, I think a dual boot would be best.
Down to business:
There are ways to run Windows programs in Linux, to varying degrees of success. Some run flawlessly, some require a little setup, and others don't work at all. You can check on the status of a game/program at the website (the project is called Wine, or Cedega). Few will run as well as they would on a Windows platform.
For hardware, you'll be happy to know that there are drivers for that graphics card. In Feisty, there's a tool to painlessly install them, officially.
For MSN, I mostly use aMSN from Trevino's repository. It's a little rough around the edges, but has the best feature list of any Linux client. Gaim/Pidgin both work with MSN as well, although nudges and the like are missing, unfortunately. There are other projects using the GTK toolkit. Emesene is young, but looks rather promising.
Replacing iTunes will be a struggle. It depends what you used in iTunes. The default Ubuntu install uses separate applications for ripping, burning, and playing CDs/music. Banshee does all of these things, I hear. For me, Rhythmbox is perfect.

If your looking for an OS with lots of commercial games, then Linux may not be what you're looking for. There are many community developed and maintained games, you may have luck with Wine, but if the Mac doesn't have the gaming capabilities you need, I doubt Linux will satisfy you.

As I said, best dual boot. You may find other reasons to fall in love with the system. I have a dual boot on this machine, and I haven't gone to Windows in over a month now (nor have I reset ^_^).

Now, about Beryl:
Beryl is a community-based fork of Compiz, developed by Novell. Currently they're merging, bringing all of the extra plugins from Beryl into Compiz. This effort is called Compiz Fusion, and many are turning to it. To me, it has a more professional feel to it.
Whichever you decide on, there are many howtos out there. For the most part, you need to simply enable your drivers, fetch some packages, and fire it up.
