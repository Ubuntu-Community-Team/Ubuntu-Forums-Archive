### Post by aysiu on 2006-05-12
[QUOTE=yozef]
Thier internet website is one of the most confusing of any Linux Distro. I have never had the trouble of navigating for ISO's the way I had with their site.[/quote] I don't see what's so difficult about finding a Mepis ISO. See the attached screenshot.

I'll tell you the reasons people recommend Mepis to newcomers (and the reason I went straight to Mepis after trying Ubuntu last year--it was only after getting comfortable with Mepis that I was able to migrate to Ubuntu):

1. Until Dapper, Ubuntu has not had a point-and-click graphical installer. Mepis does.
2. Until Dapper, Ubuntu had separate live and installer CDs. Mepis has combined the two for a long time.
3. Mepis comes with a lot of popular proprietary codecs. On Ubuntu you have to install them yourself or use Automatix to install them for you.
4. Mepis comes with a graphical partitioning tool (QTParted). Ubuntu does not.
5. Mepis has a one-click way to reinstall Grub. Ubuntu does not.
6. In Mepis, all your partitions show up on the desktop. To mount them, you just click them. In Ubuntu, you have to jump through all sorts of command-line hoops to do this.
7. Mepis has a "browse as super-user" launcher to make root changes. In Ubuntu, you have to know to create your own *gksudo nautilus* or *kdesu konqueror* launcher.

Is that enough for you? I think in terms of a complete "out-of-the-box" experience, Mepis is a superior distro to Ubuntu. The fact that Mepis will base itself off of Ubuntu starting with release 6 will make Mepis even better.

I use Ubuntu because the support community here is more knowledgeable and responsive than the Mepis community.

### Post by aysiu on 2006-05-12
Well, now that I've gone on this long spiel about what's so great about Mepis, I'll tell you some things that aren't so great about it:

1. As I mentioned before, the community lacks knowledge.

2. The fonts are terrible, even when you try to turn on anti-aliasing--there's just something weird about Mepis fonts.

3. KDE is the only real supported environment. Here, you can get good support on Gnome, KDE, XFCE, and beyond.

4. Before its forthcoming move to being based on Ubuntu, it was drawing on Debian repositories, and there are just way too many Debian repositories--it's hard to know which ones to include in your /etc/apt/sources.list. In Ubuntu, there are the default repositories and only a handful of extra repositories, but they still have a lot of applications in them. Ubuntu: less confusion, still a lot of programs.

5. Just not as easy to customize. For example, there's no way that Mepis users know to get rid of the ugly pyramid bootsplash.

### Post by aysiu on 2006-05-12
[QUOTE=yozef]
Like other Linux distros the support is what fell short and for Linux our community of support is what sets us apart from the out-of-the-box Windows or Apple users. We prefer not to be drones but rather become active participants in our technological reality.[/QUOTE] It's kind of interesting the differences in approaches and values.

Linux distros are famous for having fewer graphical frontends for configuration, and I don't know if it is actually fewer, but what's more interesting is that the graphical frontends are different. 

For example, Windows has a graphical frontend for installing drivers. XFCE, however, has a graphical frontend for changing the menu icon. It's very difficult to change the Start Menu icon in Windows.

Likewise, in KDE and Gnome, it's pretty easy to configure the clock--give it different fonts, change it from AM/PM to 24-hour, show seconds, not show seconds. In Windows, pretty much all you can do with a right-click on the clock is change the time.

In Windows, there's very little in the way of a graphical tool for changing themes. I can go from Luna to Silver to Windows Classic. That's about it. If I want more, I have to pay money or do some serious hacking. In Gnome, I can just drag and drop a .tar.gz into the Theme Preferences window.

I can adjust keyboard shortcuts in preferences, too. I don't know how to do this graphically in Windows.

The GUIs Linux distros have that Windows doesn't tend to be more towards customization of appearance. There are fewer graphical frontends for configuring wireless or installing drivers or adjusting screen resolution. The irony is that Windows doesn't really need these things usually, as Windows comes preinstalled and third-party hardware manufacturers usually design their hardware to work with Windows and include drivers for Windows.

Recently, someone in our tech department at work gave me some Windows freeware for doing screenshots so that I didn't have to PrintScreen, open up MSPaint, paste it in, and save the file. In other words, I had to install third-party software to get basic gnome-screenshot functionality.

Well, just rambling...
