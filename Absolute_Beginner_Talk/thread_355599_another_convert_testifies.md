### Post by wmblowers on 2007-02-07
Hello everyone,
I just wanted to take a brief moment to share my GNU/Linux-Ubuntu experience to date.

My current computer (Windows XP) is almost 5 years old.  I had been waiting (and waiting... and waiting...) for Vista.  When I heard some of the horror stories about the 'security' and DRM issues with it, as well as the fact that if I wanted the 'Premium' version I'd pretty much have to buy a new (more expensive!) computer, I decided to look at other options.

A friend of mine has an iMac, and I played around with it for a little bit.  Pretty slick, but unbelievably pricey.  I still figured I was going to be stuck with Windows, but then I saw something mentioned in a forum about Ubuntu.  I sailed over and started doing some research.

I'm not exactly a total geek but I'm usually able to figure things out fairly well, so I started doing some research.  Not necessarily having a lot of time for tweaking, etc, I figured that maybe the Ubuntu thing might be a little bit over my head; so I decided to kind of let the idea go.  Then, I happened to go over to a friend's house and saw she had Kubuntu installed on her machine.  I couldn't believe how nifty everything looked; it was an older machine and it was running fast!  I decided to take a closer look.

I was still kind of apprehensive, but the thing that tipped me over was when another friend of mine had a 6-yr old Gateway that had a fried HD.  I bought it from him for $25, slapped another 256M of RAM into it (512M total), along with a new HD, a USB 2.0 card, and a DVD writer.  I also bought a splitter so I could use one monitor, keyboard and mouse on both my Win and Ubuntu machines.  How slick is that!  For just over $300 I've got a refurbed machine that's really fast; it boots in a THIRD of the time my Windows machine does.  Awesome.

One thing that totally impressed me about the install (Edgy):  NOT.  ONE.  ISSUE.  The install was FLAWLESS.  Amazing.  It loaded, got the updates, and was ready to run.  It found my DSL; all the drives work... I was flabbergasted.  Whenever I've done a Windows install I've ALWAYS had HOURS and HOURS of problems.  Not so with Ubuntu.

I've spent a lot of time getting up to speed on things; it's certainly a big change and I've had some issues to figure out, but I've either figured everything out or found suitable workarounds so far.  Last week I discovered Ultimate Ubuntu and man, is *this* something else... dropped it on, and got everything but the kitchen sink now.  I have had a few minor issues (a Win virus infestation in Wine and Picassa) but just got rid of those and now my machine's in good shape.

The only remaining issue I'm dealing with is burning DVDs... I've just about got it nailed.  The last one I burned had slight pauses between chapters.  Not sure what's causing this, but I still have my Windows machine I can use for that, so I really do have the best of both worlds here.

At any rate, let me just say that I very much appreciate what everyone connected with Ubuntu, Debian, and GNU/Linux is doing to further the cause of Open Source and Free software; and to break the grips of The Monopoly.  Although I'm not much more than a motivated user, (so I won't be much good at programming, et. al.) I  do intend to make financial donations from time to time to help further the cause.

Thank you all very much.  Keep up the good work!

WLB  :>)

### Post by Tanker Bob on 2007-02-07
While I'm here typing, let me add my 2 cents.

I attained and maintained status as a Windows guru, even holding positions managing Windows LAN shops.  My home Windows system is tweaked to the nth degree.  I saw no possibility of moving off of the Windows platform.

Then came VISTA.  While researching the upgrade, I read extensively on the DRM, Protected Video Path - Output Protection Management (PVP-OPM), Protected Video Path - User-Accessible Bus (PVP-UAB), Protected User Mode Audio (PUMA), and Protected Audio Path (PAP) components and plans.  In short, these involve encrypting hardware and software paths, adding cost and complexity to video cards, audio cards, and their software drivers.  These essentially give RIAA and MPAA control of hardware that YOU pay for without adding any value whatsoever.  It's even worse than that, but I'll stop there.  That was all too much for me.

I researched the Linux landscape and discovered the Ubuntu family of live CD packages.  I ran Ubuntu on a spare hard drive for a week and was very impressed.  It recognized and set up almost all my hardware (except for my Epson scanner, which took some work).  The NVIDIA drivers worked great once I figured out that routine.  The repositories are a super idea that greatly enhances usability.  After a week of testing, I was sold.  The only issue was that I wanted more control over the system than Ubuntu routinely provided.

So, I bought a new, larger hard drive and installed Kubuntu 6.10 to the naked drive, keeping my WinXP Pro setup as a dual boot initially.  I'm blown away by the extensive variety of useful applications that the package installs automatically.  I've also been able to find apps to do pretty much whatever I need--and they are all FREE!  The system is perfectly stable and faster than under Windows.  Memory use is much better, and security is great.  I was already using Firefox and OpenOffice.org while on XP, so there was no great adjustment there.  So far I've been able to solve most issues that I've encountered in "customizing" the system by searching the forums and HOWTOs here.  When that failed, folks in the forums here have been very helpful.

In order to preserve the few Windows programs that I cannot live without (syncing my Dell Axim, Logos Bible Software, et al), I decided to go with a VMWare virtual machine under Kubuntu.  That's the reason I needed a larger hard drive.  I've had some challenges with that, largely because I tried to "restore" my XP setup to the VM--big mistake.  I'm currently building a new VM from scratch, which is working fine so far.  This way, I can bring up my Windows apps in less than 2 minutes vice much longer in dual boot, plus all my Linux stuff is still instantly available.  This is working great for me.

I cannot say enough good things about the entire Ubuntu/Kubuntu/etc. team.  The downloadable live CD approach combined with a complete package concept is ingenious and goes a long way towards bringing Linux to the mainstream.  The repositories make it simple to find compatible software for specific uses.  While I still respect WinXP Pro as an OS, Linux is the future, not VISTA.  As Peter Gutmann has said: " The Vista Content Protection specification could very well constitute the longest suicide note in history."  Ubuntu/Kunbuntu has converted another Windows die-hard.  Long live Kubuntu!
