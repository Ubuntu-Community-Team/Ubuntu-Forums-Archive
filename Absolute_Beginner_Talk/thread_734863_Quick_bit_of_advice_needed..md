There’s no need to re-partition your drive and no risk of wiping out your existing data. When you boot into Ubuntu, the system reads and writes to the file as if it were a standalone drive. ### Post by bumanie on 2008-03-25
I initially found hardy very good, using alpha 6 release, until there was a kernel upgrade that made my system unbootable. So, yes it worked well, but whilst it is still in testing stage, things can happen with updates etc. that conflict with your particular hardware and you end up with an unusable computer. If you do use it, ensure you have a separate /home, so that if something goes wrong. at least all your saved data will be safe. Also it is not a good idea to use a testing/development version as your main OS due to potential instability.
