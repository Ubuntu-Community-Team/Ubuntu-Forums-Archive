If wireless is detected out of the box, you won't have to do anything. If you have an incompatible wireless card, you might have to use something call *ndiswrapper*, which is a hack that allows you to somehow use Windows wireless drivers in Linux. There is a GUI frontend called *ndisgtk*. Unfortunately, as of 7.04, Ubuntu no longer includes *ndiswrapper* on the CD. Your best bet would be to use a Ubuntu derivative like Linux Mint (which includes *ndisgtk* and a lot of other good GUI tools Ubuntu does not include by default).

### Post by Irihapeti on 2007-09-02
I can't answer all your questions, but I'm sure other people will fill in the gaps.

The different kinds of Ubuntu are the different releases, and the version numbers (7.04, 6.10) are the year and month of release. At the moment, feisty is the current version and gutsy is being developed.

Your best bet might be to get a liveCD and play with that. It boots off your CD drive and you can explore Ubuntu without having to put anything on your hdd. That gives you a chance to discover how well your hardware works.

The wordprocessor (OpenOffice) looks a bit different from MSWord and some of the commands are different. It can save files in Word format, except Word 2007. I recently used it to do an editing job for someone who sent me the original doc in Word 2003. It converted fine in both directions.

Command lines may seem scary at first, but a lot of it is in the mind. You do have the option of copying and pasting instructions. I have a text file or two with some common stuff that I use. I have to say that they've got me out of trouble a time or two.

Hope this helps

### Post by macogw on 2007-09-02
Re: the command line
You can just copy and paste that stuff in.  Don't worry about what it means unless you want to learn.  I wanted to learn it because I consider the keyboard to be much faster than the mouse, so I looked up the commands I was given to see what they do. 

The other "different kinds" of Ubuntu (if you didn't mean version numbers), are Kubuntu, Xubuntu, and Edubuntu.  Kubuntu uses KDE, and Xubuntu uses XFCE.  These are desktop environments.  They come with different default programs, but you can install KDE and XFCE on your (GNOME-default) Ubuntu, or just install programs made for KDE and run them with GNOME.  They can all run together, they just might not match perfectly as far as theming goes.
