### Post by lackhead on 2007-12-14
> **thef0rce said:**
> 
Just one more thing I would like to know. For people who already know how to use linux quite well, are there any restrictions on what you can do in Ubuntu? I used to use Slackware, which had a lot of room for configuration. I'm looking for a slightly simpler distro to use, with decent hardware support but still a great deal of flexibility

Then I would think Ubuntu is a fine choice, as is OpenSuSE (I prefer Ubuntu for all of the reasons posted on this thread).  

As to your question about simplicity vs. restrictions- pretty much any linux distribution will allow you to do whatever you want to it.  That's the nice things about an open operating system.  However, most linux distributions also aim towards making things simple, and they do so in their own ways (different package management, different setup styles, different philosophies).  So with that being said, the way I read your question is "How much tinkering can I do with the operating system without breaking the SuSE/Ubuntu/Slackware/Gentoo way?"  Well, I do most of my installs/config's from shell scripts that I write, and Ubuntu's been pretty easy to deal with. Even for some of the esoteric stuff I've wanted to do, with a little poking I've found a way to get the job done without compromising or bending over backwards.  SuSE was a bit more difficult in this regards, but certainly a far cry from M$!!  But in any case, the more you use a distribution the more you will know it and the more flexibility and ease you will have. 

I don't think you'd be going wrong with any of the major players- Ubuntu, SuSE, or Gentoo. Slackware is great, but it certainly is a minimalist approach and has a steeper learning curve. We use it on some of our embedded systems and it works fine, but I'd never personally put it on a desktop.  I've settled on Ubuntu for our server and desktop OS because of its philosophy, the community, and just because it matches the way I think and work better than any of the other distro's I've gotten to know well.  It certainly is a good place to start when trying to learn more about Linux.

Just my $.02,
