### Post by u.b.u.n.t.u on 2006-06-14
[QUOTE=sbuntu]I am talking about the one that you see from behind,the one that fans the whole cpu[/QUOTE]

I would leave the fan in there. You may want to clean the dust off it as that can slow a fan and cause extra noise. Use a cotton bud to clean each blade of the fan. make sure the power is OFF and the the power plug disconnected. (Better safe than sorry, especially as you seem new to working with hardware). Also don't bump your computer. Move it slowly, it isn't a football.

Under NO circumstances open the power supply or mess with it. Let it bet.

Also do NOT mess with your cpu fan unless you know what you are doing.

Your computer may have any number of case fans. These move air around the case to keep it cooler. If the computer came with a fan, you can bet that it needs it. Unless you can judge otherwise or get a tech friend to check it out.

Have you ever opened a computer before and worked on it? If not I suggest you ask a friend who has and learn.

You said you have a pentium 2. Is it a socket or slot? If it is a slot, does it have a fan on the heatsink? Some slots don't have fans and they are ideal for a noiseless box - excluding the PSU (power supply unit) which always needs a fan and should never be opened.

If a PSU every does stop working, bin it and get a new one - even second hand one is ok as long as you know the specs in terms of power output and leads you need, and design for case (some PSUs are very generic and some are designed for specific shaped cases).

I hope this helps.

### Post by Bartender on 2006-06-15
s -
Regarding the PSU fan, I don't share the alarm expressed by some of the other folks who've taken the time to respond.
I've replaced a couple of 'em.  It's not that big of a deal.  PSU's aren't radioactive.  The potential for harm comes from the large capacitors inside, which hold a charge for several hours and will happily discharge thru you if you give them a path to ground.
  
MaximumPC says to unplug the PSU and let it set for a day or two.  That allows them to discharge, minimizing the danger.  

If you've never done anything like this, get some tape and put a flag around the wire near each plug that goes to a part and label it so you don't get confused during reinstallation.  Draw a picture too.  Sometimes these plugs hold on pretty tight, so be patient and GENTLY rock them back & forth a little bit as you pull if necessary.  Motherboard, HDD, and CD drive plugs are keyed, but the floppy plug can be confusing so take a good look at it when you pull it.  Each plug usually has a little label on it which can help identify where it went.  If you can set the PC on its side you may not even have to unplug the PSU completely, just unscrew it and set it next to the PC, but if I'm going to this extent I'll blow the dust out so I want it away from the PC and out of the house.

There're usually four screws to get the PSU out, then four more to remove the  clamshell-like roof (often crimped to fit tightly into the other half).  Then you're in.  Hopefully the fan unscrews easily.  They're often hardwired (no plug) so you may have to snip wires.  Don't panic if your replacement fan has 3 or 4 wires and the PSU only has 2.  You only need black & red.  Use a soldering gun if you have access to one.  Make sure nothing can short out - heatshrink tubing is a worthwhile extra step.  Make sure the new fan is blowing the same direction as the old.

There's always the question of how much you want to spend on an ancient PC.  The most recent hand-me-down PC I got (described in sig) had a screeching PSU fan that I replaced with a Nexus.  Nexus fans aren't cheap but have the best reputation.

Also, the CPU fan was making a racket.  My PIII was a slot processor, probly like yours.  Prying these things apart is a bit of a challenge.  PM me if you want to know more.  I've got a few pointers but don't want to go on & on here.

### Post by Panhead Bill on 2006-06-15
If you are just looking to remove the dust from the fan (and the supply innards) remove the supply from the PC and get a can of compressed air or "Dust-off".   With short bursts of air, blast the supply from each opening and watch the dust fly.  After that careful use of dry cotton swabs can remove the dust and grime from the fan blades, followed with a few more bursts of compressed air.

The CRT in a monitor IS a large capacitor, it can store a nasty or even deadly charge (as can several capactiors in the case).  TV technitions work on them safely every day because they know how to discharge this charge safely.

A switching powersupply can hold a nasty jolt.  I believe that U.L. safety regs require bleader resistors to safely dissapate this charge over time.  If you know how to safely discharge the power supply you CAN work on it.  One method is to short the P.S. start leads togeather while the supply is unplugged from the AC outlet, but still connected to the motherboard.
Once the supply is discharged, fan replacement is not too hard.

If in doubt, learn more before before putting yourself at risk.
