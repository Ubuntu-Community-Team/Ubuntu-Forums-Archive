### Post by mivo on 2007-11-12
I've looked at, and bought, a number of Linux books, but they all seem to fall into one of two categories:

- Too general and too basic: These books cover ground you are already familiar with (what a distro is etc.) and touch everything else only superficially, so that you may obtain some rudimentary understanding of many things, but still feel rather clueless.

- Too advanced. These books have a specific topic, but assume quite a bit of knowledge and may go too much into depth so that they lose you after the introduction. They also tend to be rather expensive.

I agree with rbc that the forums here are an excellent resource, You can often find links to related resources and hop around and learn on topics that interest you. The Ubuntu wiki is also a good starting point.

I also agree that you will learn the most by having problems or specific goals, and then trying to fix/achieve them. I recently picked up a new desktop and now use my old box for experimenting. It is not always fruitful (trying to compile and package a .deb file didn't really work entirely well, but this gives me something else to learn about), but messing up that box won't affect my production machine. I'm actually really tempted to try something like Linux from Scratch, but I feel this may still be far too advanced for me.
