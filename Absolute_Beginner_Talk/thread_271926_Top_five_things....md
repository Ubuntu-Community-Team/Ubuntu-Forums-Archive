### Post by ewl1217 on 2006-10-05
Here are my reccomendations. None are required to use Ubuntu, but they do let you get the most out of it. Keep in mind that these are in no particular order.[LIST]
[*]the command line (see [http://linuxcommand.org/)]("http://linuxcommand.org/")
[*]apt (Ubuntu's package management system; accessed via Synaptic, apt-get, aptitude, etc.; no real link for this, just open Synaptic and explore)
[*]general understanding of a GNU/Linux OS (stuff like the Linux kernel, GNU utilites, X server, etc.)
[*]the forums here and the [Ubuntu Wiki]("https://wiki.ubuntu.com"), always a great help
[*]general understanding of computer hardware (hard drive, processor, RAM, video card, etc.)[/LIST]

I'm not so sure. The man pages don't make a good starting point - they assume a level of knowledge that a beginner isn't likely to have.

There is a gap in the market for a 'How to learn Linux' document I reckon. A kind of 'meta faq' on where to go, and what to learn first. There are plenty of tutorials, how-tos and examples on the web, but it's not immediately obvious where to start on the path to learning.

Here are a few things that come to mind on places to start learning Linux properly:

1. learn a text editor, preferably one that you can find on any linux box, and preferably one that works from the terminal, so that you can fix your X install when it gets borked. 

For me, that's Vim, because vimtutor is an excellent way to start finding your way around. Just fire up a terminal window (In Dapper, go to Applications > Accessories > terminal) and type 'vimtutor'.

2. Find a bash tutorial (bash is the default Ubuntu shell) that you like, make a coffee, and spend some time playing around with the basic bash commands -- ls, cp, mv, cd, cat, less, find (that's fun the first time!), grep etc. Google for 'basic bash tutorial' and you should find plenty to keep you busy! Be patient with yourself - it will take a while to get the hang of it. Here's one to get you started:

[http://www.linuxcommand.org/]("http://www.linuxcommand.org/")

Oh, and when you're playing, do it in your home directory, and with a 'normal' user account - not root!

3. Try to get a feel for the package management tool in your distribution of Linux, so that you are comfortable installing and removing software. In Ubuntu, that's APT and dpkg. Synaptic is a great tool, but it can go wrong, and often it's easiest to fix things at the command line. Here are a couple of links to get you going:

[http://linuxplanet.com/linuxplanet/tutorials/4161/1/]("http://linuxplanet.com/linuxplanet/tutorials/4161/1/")
[http://linuxformat.co.uk/wiki/index.php/Package_management_tools_(apt_and_rpm)]("http://linuxformat.co.uk/wiki/index.php/Package_management_tools_(apt_and_rpm)")

4. Add a few links to your bookmarks, and read whenever you get some spare time. Here are a couple of sites that might interest you:
[http://www.linux-tutorial.info/index.php]("http://www.linux-tutorial.info/index.php")
[http://www.justlinux.com/]("http://www.justlinux.com/")

5. There are loads more! I'll post them as I think of them.

I hope this helps. Have fun!
