### Post by Tundro Walker on 2007-04-17
I'm gonna sound like I'm riding the fence, but I agree that standardization is good, but also that freedom of choice is good.

Computers didn't really seem to take off much as an office or home solution until Microsoft standardized the software people were using.  This was a case where standardization really helped, since everyone knew you'd use MS Word to do documents, Excel for spreadsheets, etc.  Until then, it seemed like every other month at the software store, somebody was trying to re-invent the wheel by producing their own word processor or file manager or what-not, and the average person, who isn't into computers enough to care about making a choice between several, didn't really bother with it.  Instead, they'd just buy what they were using at work, and bring it home for use.  

Seriously, I get this, because it's like the average car driver.  When they go to get new tires, they could care less about what brand they get.  They just know that they have bald tires and need new ones.  However, they do understand quality and reliability, etc.  So, if they heard that brand X was better, they'd get brand X.  Other than that, if the tire companies make it difficult to compare, then consumers might as well just toss darts at a board to pick one.  This is why Consumer Reports magazine is still popular today...they make unbiased comparison easy.  Even then, the average user won't buy a Consumer Reports magazine to figure out what the best tires are.  They have more important things to do with their time (well, more important as in "more fun" things to do with their time...seriously, the average person doesn't think shopping for tires, or software for their computer, is fun...and before I get slammed with IM's...folks on this forum would probably be considered non-average, since we're using Linux/Ubuntu, and thus probably show more interest in our computers then the average user.  I, however, don't put as much interest into the tires on my car.  I just want a car that runs, and tires that don't go flat.)

But, with a standard set, you get monopolization and vendor lock-in, which wouldn't be such a bad thing if that vendor produced the best stuff, hands-down.  But, what usually happens is the vendor rests on their laurels, and starts getting sloppy.  In Microsoft's case, they were always a bit sloppy; they  always shipped some software with some bugs, but as they got more and more market share, they're attitude about shipping software that wasn't ready loosened, and that's why Win98, WinME, etc all shipped and caused such a stir, which had to be fixed with Win98 SE.  But, Microsoft could permeate the business market, because the MBA business management style of the 80's and early 90's was to go for immediate low-cost.  Note I didn't say lowest "Total Cost of Operation" (TCO).  80's & 90's MBA's were cut-throat, and usually showed up cost-cutting everything real quick so they could show quick profits and turn-around, then get promoted or what-not and leave before the **** hit the fan from all the garbage they just screwed up.  In Microsoft's case, this was a boon, because MS could sell their software at lower cost then others, and PC's were cheaper to buy then Apple's, because IBM had allowed 3rd party manufacturers the rights to produce knock-off hardware.  So, management bought up buggy MS software on PC's that were built from all kinds of different parts cheaply.  In the short-term, it cost less then setting up Apple's and other, more stable software.  But, in the long-term, it's cost them more to support, upgrade, etc those PC's and software.  But, they already made the initial investment and many are choosing the "you made your bed now lie in it" approach and  don't want to shell out tons of money to totally revamp their systems and software.  (From a TCO, you have to understand that it would cost a fortune...you'd not only replace machines, but OS and software, and you'd have to retrain folks on software, and rebuild custom apps to work on the new OS and software...a real big nightmare.)

I applaud Microsoft for boosting computers into the hearts and minds of the world by providing a standard computing platform.  But, it was a little sloppy to begin with (Win 3.0 & 3.11), got a little better (Win95), then when they got vendor-lockin, they got sloppy with Win98 and WinME, which took Win98 SE to fix.  (Won't go into the other fiasco's they've caused with their other software).  But, they've gotten their customer base so used to using buggy software, that they can get away with charging folks for tech support calls to support their buggy software, and are also now selling an ANTIVIRUS program for their OWN OS.

So, that's where branching out to other choices are good.  But, like you said, tons of other choices make it difficult for the average person.  They don't want to compare the nuances of 15 different things.  3-4 is more than enough.  In software sales, I can understand there being 15 different word processors to choose from, since each one is vying for your dollars.  And I sort of agree with your sentiment that in the open-source community, folks should pool thier resources to make single "products" better rather then making a new product to "compete" with others.

But, in open-source, some folks make things because they feel they can do it better.  Or, because they want to do it differently (EG: a minimalist version vs. a robust version).  So, you end up with 15 different versions of things, and it's confusing figuring out which one you want to use.  And this is actually good, because if all the programmers who wanted to work on "widget" (whatever it may be) pooled together, they'd all want to implement their ideas, and you'd end up with this uber-massive program with tons of really specialized features only very select folks would use, and the other vast majority of users saying "why don't you get rid of that crap, because I don't use it and it just slows down the program."  So, for now, you're stuck picking and choosing between 15 different types of bread.

However, SourceForge.net has provided a meeting ground for developers to do what you're talking about...pool their resources to provide a few robust apps instead of tons of individual ones.  OpenOffice.org has shown this works tremendously well, and can produce very robust applications.  But, currently, more and more developers are choosing to focus on plugin development, because more software focuses on that type of architecture.  Mozilla Firefox is a great example.  Firefox provides the core web browser system, and developers can make plugins to extend its capabilities.  So, you add-on what you like, and leave off what you don't.  However, the core application has what everyone wants...a good browser experience with good security.  This eliminates a main reason why some developers produced their own versions of the same software...because they like using product X, but like some of the features in product Y, so they decided to just incorporate it all into their own product Z.  With plugins, they can take the core system and simply expland upon it easily without having to reinvent the wheel.  It's one of the reasons why Firefox took off so well, it's one of the reasons people like using GNOME & KDE (due to plugins and extendable desktop software), and I think it will continue to be a growing reason why other software takes off in the future.  So, instead of 15 choices, you'll have 2 or 3, each with extendability.

Even the OS world operates like that.  Apple, Linux and Windows are seen as the top "competitors", and each can be seen as an extendable base...the OS just runs the hardware...the applications you extend it with are what make it customized to your taste.  However, I think the whole "distro" thing has made it difficult for some Windows folks, who are used to being spoon-fed, to get into Linux.  They're used to starting with a clean slate that they build upon, where as in Linux land, someone hands you a slate with lots of stuff already on it...  So, again, you're stuck having to decide between 15 different versions of something.  C'est la vie.

To answer another question that usually arises from this...eventually, like evolution, a "good" solution will be found for things through experimentation and adoption of standards.  EG: like folks using a media player on their computer to play movies and music.  But, of that solution, there may be different ones, each "best" in their own right depending on the circumstances ("best" is subjective). EG: Somebody may use a full-featured media player on a powerful computer, but a stripped down one on a less powerful computer.  They're both good.  And, as time goes by, maybe new developments will totally change how we use medial players, altering our concept of what's "good".  EG: we don't use Apple ][ E's anymore, because a terminal-style computer is so archaic.  We've moved on to GUI-based computers these days.  However, change doesn't just happen over night.  It's gradual.  All those "15 versions of the same thing" you're miffed  about having to choose from will each be different in subtle ways.  And folks will use them, and talk, and realize what they do and don't like between them.  And developers will take the feedback and merge it into next-generation "widgets", and so on, using more current architecture, more current interface design, and so forth.  Gradually, our idea of what's "standard" and "good" changes, and then we end up with 15 different versions all meeting that criteria while also trying out something new.  It's perpetual.

### Post by Tundro Walker on 2007-04-17
I wanted to add one more thing...

The question you're asking..."why are there so many versions of things", isn't as important as asking..."in the future, can I still access and use whatever I make with program XYZ today?"  Programs come and go, but you want the piece of mind to know that important things you work on, documents, music, etc, will still be accessible and useable in the future.

That's why a lot of folks still use .txt files for documenting things.  The ones I made in the 80's still open up in text editors today.  Some folks are picking up web editors as the new standard document transfer format, since HTML is an open standard that's easy to use, and can be looked at in a text editor as well as a web-browser or web editor.

My old PW (Professional Write...a DOS-based word processor) stuff...nope.  Old MS Office stuff can't get opened in newest versions of MS Office, either.  All the music projects I worked on in Sonic Foundry's Acid 4.0 when I was using WinXP..guess what...I can't use them in Linux.

So, that may help narrow down your choices for things.  If you use some program that has it's own proprietary format, or some archaic one that nobody else uses, you might want to give it up for a different program.  That's why the open-source movement has been trying to standardize certain formats as constant, so you'll have portability between OS, applications, etc.  Afterall, just because an applications I use to type up a document isn't cross-platform compatible doesn't mean my document shouldn't be cross-platform compatible either.   Open Office.org has done a good job of sticking with an open-source format for their stuff (documents, spreadsheets, etc), which will hopefully catch on with other OS's.  But in the meantime, OpenOffice.org supports proprietary MS formats as best it can (EG: MS Word).  They're doing their best to allow user content to transfer between OS, computers, etc, because not everyone that uses a Windows computer at work uses the same at home, or vice-versa.  If only Microsoft and Apple would catch on to this.  But, they're interests are in keeping users locked in, not giving them freedom.  I mean, if I knew all my hard work I did in MS Word and such couldn't transfer over to Linux, I'd be less inclined.  But it can, so I did.  While I pine over some of my work lost from Acid 4.0, I still have .wav and .mp3's for those projects, so I can hear them, but can't edit them.  My consolation to this was that Acid was a ($400!) buggy product that constantly crashed on me in MS Windows, so I certainly don't miss THAT.

However, for standardization what it comes down to is what gets used the most and adopted...sometimes a matter of being in the right place at the right time, not exactly what's "the best" (IE: my reference to Microsoft's buildup over the 80's & 90's).  For instance, everyone uses mp3's as the standard music format.  But, it's a proprietary format.  Eventually, somebody has to pay some money to someone else to use that format, be it a music software developer who wants to sell a program that allows mp3 generation, or an mp3 player manufacturer who wants to play (you guessed it) mp3's on their device.

Will mp3's be around in 5-10 years?  Who knows.  As pervasive as they are, there will probably still be things that play them, or at least there will come a time when converters will help convert that format into the latest format that has caught on (OGG?).

Anyways, just wanted to add that.
