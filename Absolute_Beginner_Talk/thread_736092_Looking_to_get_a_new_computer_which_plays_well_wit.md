### Post by shad0w_walker on 2008-03-26
The majority of chipsets should work fairly well with Ubuntu, the key ones you want to check are the motherboard chipset and the graphics card. My recommendations are a motherboard with an Nvidia chipset (Very good opensource drivers so supported properly out the box) and something Nvidia graphics card wise as they have good drivers both opensource and closed source (Opensource doesn't handle the 3d side of things yet)

The rest of the hardware shouldn't be a major thing as most systems come with all the major components built onto the motherboard now, so if the chipset is decently supported there then you should be good to go with most systems.

There are also plenty of places about now that are selling Ubuntu/General Linux prebuilt systems so you could always buy from them or just see what components they use to find decent compatible parts.

### Post by cmnorton on 2008-03-26
I have installed Ubuntu a few times, both on laptops and desktops. Whether you purchase a system pre-configured with Ubuntu or not depends on the kind of hardware. I have had little if no trouble at all installing Ubuntu, especially compared to Red Hat and Fedora, on desktop (non-laptop) hardware. The laptops are a different story (Acer and Thinkpad).

As to non-laptop hardware take a look at prices for kits from places like Tiger Direct and compare those against places like ZaReason, System 76, Eight Virtues PC Sales, Dell, and so on. It still might be better to get Ubuntu pre-installed. If a desktop falls in your lap, you've got nothing to lose by installing Ubuntu.

As to laptops, I would buy it pre-configured with Ubuntu. I learned a ton installaing Ubuntu 7.10 over an XP environment on a brand-new Thinkpad T61p. Learning new things is good, I know, but I'd have preferred to have spent that time programming, not fussing with hardware.
