### Post by Herman on 2006-10-13
The line tool in Gimp is called 'bezier select', and it is absolutley a fantastic tool once you start giving it a try.
It is far easier to use than you might expect, and you can do really advanced tricks with it too, even when you are only a beginner with it.
 
Simply press 'b' for the bezier select tool, and click where you want a line to begin, and click again where you want the line to end.
Now, you can move the line around until you get it perfect by clicking on the dot on either end and dragging it with the mouse. 
Isn't that neat! :D
You can even click on the middle of the line and bend it! Try it! :D
You can draw any shape with it.

If you make a mess, just press ctrl + z and undo your last changes. The GIMP remembers a very long list of changes, so you can go backwards step by step.

When you are happy with your line, or lines. just click 'Edit' --> 'Stroke path'.
A box will open to allow you top choose settings for exactly how you want your line stroked.  You can just leave it the way it is, or you can make GIMP do almost anything with your line. GIMP will remember your previous settings too, and offer you the same settings for the next line.

A little practice, like five minutes or so, and you will really begin to enjoy the bezier select tool.
If I only want perfectly or horizontal or vertical lines, I press 'r' to switch to the rectangle tool, and make my line with that instead, in the way that petersjm has already explained. You know, a straight line is also the same thing as a narrow rectangle. Or, if I do make a rectangle or square, there are also options for filling it in with a background color, foreground color, or even a pattern! :-D (You can have wood grain, leopard skin, marble tiled or even blue sky pattern!) (He-he). :D  

Just give all the GIMP tools at least five or ten minutes each. Play with them a bit and have some fun! GIMP is great! :D 

You can even use GIMP to make your own animations, quite complex ones too. For example, I used GIMP to make a model steam locomotive, [click here to see it]("http://users.bigpond.net.au/hermanzone/p3d/gold%20loco%20S.gif"),  It is very small, but if you look closely and watch it, it is actually reasonably mechanically correct, timing wise and all! (He-he).:D

You can do almost anything with GIMP, and it's great fun! Just don't give up without giving it a try, and read  [Grokking the GIMP]("http://gimp-savvy.com/BOOK/")a little, that will help you a lot.
I understand your frustration, I found it a little difficult to get started with GIMP too, but many new things worth doing seem a little awkward at first. Learning how to ride a bike, how to drive a car, it all takes practice and a little perseverance and patience. Soon you will be just flying along with it!

Regards, Herman :D

### Post by Herman on 2006-10-14
Easy, for a square,  just press 'r' for rectangle, click on your image and drag out a rectanglular selection.
If you want a square, (which is really a type of rectangle), you hold down your shift key while you drag out the selection.

It's very much the same procedure for a circle, which is really a special kind of ellipse. so press 'e' for ellipse, and hold down your shift key while you drag. If you don't hold down your shift key, you'll be able to make an ellipse of any shape you like.

Then click 'Edit', and fill with a color or a pattern, or stroke the selection with a color or pattern. :D
....................

Another really neat thing you can do with GIMP that you can't do with too many other drawing tools is to make a transparent layer, on top of another image, and trace shapes from the image, or draw something else on top.
It's a lot like using tracing paper.
Just go over to your 'Layers, Channels and Paths' window, and click on the 'New Layer' button.
Now, say you are a town planner or an engineer working on a map, a fisherman with a chart, or you are a farmer with a map of your farm. You can draw your water pipelines for example in on a new transparent layer over your map, and make them show or dissappear by clicking the little 'eye' icon, also in the 'Channels, layers and paths' box.
Another layer can be for fencelines, another for electrical wires, one for roads, and so on and on....you can show or hide each set of features, so your map can have everything on it, yet never be too cluttered, you can turn of the details you don't need at any time.
GIMP is lots of fun and it can be extremely useful and practical as well.

Regards. Herman :D
