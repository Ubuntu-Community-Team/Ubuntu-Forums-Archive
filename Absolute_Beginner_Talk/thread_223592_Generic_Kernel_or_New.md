### Post by xen on 2006-07-26
I suggest that if you do not know how the kernel works, you do not need to change from the generic one you are using.

It is possible that you could generate a performance increase by using your own custom kernel, but it's not the easiest process to perform. Whilst there are many good guides for doing this available on the forum, you'll often find device problems thereafter which are not covered beyond the scope of the article (such as wireless difficulties).

The kernel is the 'core' of Ubuntu, it wraps everything together and runs the show, essentially. If you wish to learn more about it, then I suggest you ignore what I said above and dive into one of the many good guides available!
