### Post by skippy81 on 2006-05-28
There are two reasons:-

Altuism - By making a linux distro easily availiable it encourages people to use open source software and thereby helps the wider community.   It also gives people who can't afford Windows and alike an equal (or stronger) footing.  

Money - Connonical Ltd (Umbrella company who 'own' ubuntu) provide support contracts to enterprise customers.  The enterprise market will only accept a linux distrobution which is well established and popular, so therefore increasing the total number of Ubuntu users increases Connonical's income.  I don't believe that Cannonical have broke even yet - I'm not even sure if they expect to.    

I'm not an expert on Ubuntu's history, but my understanding was that Mark Shuttleworth (google him, he gets in the news a lot) wanted to promote open source for primarily charitable reasons - although the hope is that ubuntu will eventually break even and become not just a charitable sucess but a commercial one also.  If Ubuntu doesn't make money though, I'm sure Mr Shuttleworth won't be crying himself to sleep :p He's south african (I think) and probably wanted to provide a professional and viable OS which could be used across the world.  Of course, although Mark Shuttleworth put up the initial $$$, there are numberous individuals working their balls off to make Ubuntu the best. 

It's not too much of a novelty how Ubuntu is run really, both OpenSUSE and Fedora are 'sponsored' by wealthy companies.  I do think however that the spirit of altuism is somewhat greater within Ubuntu though.  

Whether you agree that distrobuting free CDs is a good way to spend money or not is another matter.  I personally totally agree with it.
