### Post by CptPicard on 2007-06-12
I think it is quite a misconception to even assume that Linux would be on some kind of a world-dominating mission where everyone HAS TO be using Linux eventually, or that Linux enthusiasts would even be interested in aggressively seeking the lowest common denominator in order to gain market share.

This is not a competition over users. It's all about the freedom of choice, and the freedom to access and tinker with your system. You use what suits you best, and if it is Windows, all power to you.

The Beryl issue is a good demonstration about what Linux is and that people fail to understand the no free lunches consequences of its nature. In Windows you would probably never even have such a profound "desktop mod" as the whole  system is locked up by Microsoft. On Linux you can have it, but the tradeoff is that yes, to begin with it's experimental, and yeah, you aren't getting pretty widgets to click as implementing those is very much the last priority in a cutting edge development system.

Linux is essentially an open development platform with many participants, and this is why the shell is so crucial as a very flexible and programmable command interface that can accommodate the various components with relatively low extra development overhead for the component writer... the centralized GUI control applets do come eventually in distributions when stuff has matured and stabilized enough, but one shouldn't expect them sooner. And the command line will, even then, be the place where the new stuff happens. There is no getting around that.

That said, it IS a disgrace that Linux STILL makes a n00b edit xorg.conf way too often...
