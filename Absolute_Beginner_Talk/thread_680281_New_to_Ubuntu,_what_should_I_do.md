### Post by Bruce H. McCosar on 2008-01-27
Consider that you have known a hardware store.  Nobody enters the store and says, gee, what can I build?  They go to the hardware store with an idea already in play.  I'm going to build a gazebo.  I'm putting in a floor.  I've got to get that tree limb off of my car (Can you tell I live in Florida?)

When you were using Windows, your hardware store sold the following tools:

Cutting -- Scissors 7.6 Trial Offer.  For a $86 annual subscription, you get MyScissors(R)(TM)(E=mc2) which allow you to cut on objects that don't already have dotted lines preprinted on them.

WinHammer 13.4-- Free, but the power supplies cost $24 each, and go out every Tuesday.

Nailzz 11.3 -- Actually, you have these, but the driver fails to load.  The GUI box pops up and asks you to register your products; you do so; you finish and click ok.  When you reopen the box, the same prompt appears again, as if the preceding never happened.

Now, you have Linux.  Whatever your interest, there's probably a tool out there that's basically like the Millennium Falcon vs. a Soyuz Space Capsule.  Especially for tasks that were perfected somewhere back in the 1970s, but still goofy as a lard sandwich on Windows.  For example, programming (gcc).  Or writing large documents (LaTeX).  Or changing huge amounts of text at once, without having to hunt down individual errors and correct them, by a sophisticated pattern matching algorithm (perl, python, emacs, vim, etc.)

It's like Linux is the store that sells jigsaws, miter saws, circular saws, band saws, table saws, and so on.  Windows, well ... they do sell artificial beaver teeth (1.0, trial offer, etc., upgrade to Beaver Teeth Pro for $54, you know the drill.)

There's a bit of a learning curve, but then again, you aren't born knowing how to use a circular saw, either.  Still, it beats trying to cut wood by chewing through it.

So, in conclusion, I'd say go with what you love.  Let your interests determine your direction.  Mine, for instance, is music, so I'm heavily into Ubuntu Studio / Kubuntu (mainly the softsynths).  A week ago, I never imagined I'd have my Hammond organ, Yamaha keyboard, and Aeolus all wired together with MIDI and be sitting here playing pipe organ music most of the evening.

(I do need an extra pair of arms, though.)

Good luck!

### Post by Circus-Killer on 2008-02-06
my advice is simple: give yourself time.

it will take you a while to find your feet. you asked what you should be doing? well, whatever you want to be doing. nobody can tell you what to do. only you know what you need the computer to be doing.

what do you usually do in windows? take those tasks and see if there are equivilant ways of doing the same thing in ubuntu. google will be a great aid in this, along with these forums.

essentially, you can do what you want, but if you want a machine thats going to be telling you what to do, you came to the wrong OS. linux is all about letting you do what you need to do, instead of doing whatever the OS tells you to do.

as i said, give yourself time. over time you will figure out how to do more things, along with preferences to applications you run. don't always settle for what's the default in ubuntu, fiddle with other applications. for example, if you don't like rhythmbox, try one of the other audio players, such as Exaile! if you don't particularly like the gnome desktop, try out Kubuntu for a change in flavor.

essentially, it will take you time to get into the run of things. but you need to start off with a purpose. without purpose, a computer is useless. so ask yourself, what is it that you use your computer for. then start googling for ways to do it in ubuntu, and if you run into a road block, feel free to come ask around in the forums.

welcome to ubuntu!
