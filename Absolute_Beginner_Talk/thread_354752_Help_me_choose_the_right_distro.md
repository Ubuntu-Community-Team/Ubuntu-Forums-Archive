Let me say this:  There is much to be said for GNOME or XFCE as being "Not Windows."  

Newbies always think that they should go for the most familiar-looking DE.  The truth, I think, is a bit counterintuitive:  the best environment is the one that is not as familiar.  If you use a DE that is too Windows-like, you will be lulled--FALSELY--into believing that everything you did in Windows will work in Linux.  Again, this is NOT the case.

A new environment--GNOME, say--should be intuitive enough to be productive, but alien enough to drive home the impression that your old tricks may not work in this new environment.

The vast majority of help "issues" I've encountered are from users vainly looking for things that work *exactly the same* as Windows where no such analog may exist in Linux.  The more you perpetuate the illusion of "Windows without problems," the more likely you will feel that you're using "broken windows" instead.

### Post by cmost on 2007-02-06
I have to disagree a bit with L14UX_1NS1D3.  I've used KDE almost exclusively since converting to Linux four years ago.  KDE is infinitely more customizable than Gnome.  In fact, it's KDE's myriad of options and endless ability to customize its interface that adds to it's complexity for new users.  KDE can be made to look any way you like and you can add and subtract K applications, features, and applets to your heart's content.  The general goal of Gnome, on the other hand, is to provide a simple, clean professional system that contains only the most appropriate functionality for any given task.  One only has to read the Gnome design guidelines to see this is the case.  While it is possible to customize Gnome in a plethora of ways, it's not presented with heavy customization by default.  Based on this, I can make the following recommendation for you:

If you like bells and whistles, power, and flexibility and you want the ability to "have it your way" then choose KDE.  With KDE you can set up your system to look and act like Windows, Mac, or something in-between.

If you like bells and whistles, power, and flexibility but you cherish simplicity and an environment that "just works" without a lot of frills, then choose Gnome.  With Gnome, you'll still be able to set up your system to look and act like Windows, Mac or something in-between, but, it will be simpler to use in the long run.

If you like a minimalistic approach while still enjoying power and flexibility, then choose XFCE.  With XFCE, you'll find a fast, simple system that takes the best from Gnome's look and feel.

Good luck!

Trust me:  the most important change you make when you change operating systems has nothing to do with hardware or software.  It has nothing to do with the computer--and everything to do with the user.

Think of a migration like moving to a new country.

Say you've lived in the USA for your whole life.  You've never spoken anything but English.  All of a sudden, you need to move to China on short notice.

You try to get adjusted.  You shout at people, but no matter how loud you shout, you cannot make yourself understood.

Then you realize that nobody speaks English.  That puts you in a bind:  you need to learn enough of the local language to get by.  This is often quite a humbling experience.  You might have been a very eloquent person in your native language, but in this new language, you are barely functional.  

Still, after a few weeks and months, you make the transition.   You begin to think in local terms, reckon things in local money, eat local food, learn local landmarks.  You adapt.

Same thing with operating systems.
