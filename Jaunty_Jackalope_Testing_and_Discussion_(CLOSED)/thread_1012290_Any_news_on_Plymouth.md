### Post by gnomeuser on 2008-12-23
> **Slug71 said:**

It is fairly likely that he does not understand that to do a complete overhaul of the look and feel of the platform, we need these underlying features to be in place.

Take the new notifications e.g., that beautiful transparency depends on composition. To get that you need 3D accelerated graphics. to get in any kind of supportable way you need DRI2, KMS and Gallium3D. This foundation then allows proper accelerated drivers which we can support.

Till that work is in place one would have to shortcut to getting that support or have a fallback that does not depend onf compositing. Both are undesirable from a support and QA stance. The latter solution includes the problem that it likely will not look or perform as nice as the composited solution.

People think overhauling the theme is such a simple job, just take a mockup and turn it into reality. There is a lot of work that needs to go into that process.

Yep, but keep it semi stable. Like how opensuse offers the lastest snapshots (about once a week) of KDE unstable. Ubuntu could easily do the same thing with the whole OS being updated with new builds maybe every 2 weeks and then let testers have at it, once the features mature enough they are put into a pool that will be dumped into the next release.

Example: Plymouth and kernel 2.6.29 and the lastest version of Xorg and all released on this test repo. If some gets put in here (maybe a new version of networkmanager) and within a month it's fairly stable, someone can suggest it get moved up to the next release and not pushed back to the fall release as it seems fairly stable.

This addresses 2 issues we have right now, it makes testing all these apps easier for users (just one repo and even live cds) and developers don't have to push as hard to get people to test new features, It also makes it easier to determine if something can make a current release (hey we tested amarok 2.1 and it's not really that buggy and think it can make the spring release, or if it is just not ready (plymouth.)

### Post by gnomeuser on 2008-12-24

Not really, you would have to approach the Fedora Board to get the schedule changed. That being said Fedora and Ubuntu are aligned pretty nicely, Ubuntu is generally out a couple of weeks before Fedora.

The problem Shuttleworth is wanting to attack is that he doesn't have the manpower to pull of a proper enterprise ready LTS. It is thus in his best interest to get Novell and Red Hat to pull much of the weight. He envisions this happening by aligning all the major enterprise vendors on the same schedule. By doing this and agreeing to use the same basic kernel, toolchain etc. everyone would have a reduced support burden.

Now why would Red Hat and Novell do this, they already carry the majority of the burden doing backports and testing for their enterprise releases. They have their own tools and workflows built up around their existing solutions. They would gain very little short term and be giving up a whole lot of freedom to schedule (not to mention it would rapidly cut into their profits). 

Don't get me wrong long term more cooperation is definitely a good thing, but I would love to see a proposal like this come with an implementation in Free Software of the testing framework Mark envisions. Some kind of prototype, call it a gesture of good will if want, something to show that Canonical are serious and want to commit to this. Giving us a forum for even doing would be wonderful. This will not happen ovenight, and if it is ever to happen we need someone to sit down and build the tools in an open fashion. I think Canonical could do that quite well. Right now though, it's seeming to everyone who can help make this happen like Shuttleworth using his massive media presence to pressure everyone to do his bidding without him investing a dime. Everyone needs to feel like equal partners or it will never get off the ground, I believe his vision is good long term but his approach to making it happen is fatally miscalculated. 

I imagine that there might be a vanue to start a kind of OpenEnterprise.org companion to FreeDesktop.org, to create specs and tools that suit the enterprise. Tools we can collaborate around. I think that would be the way to unify us all a bit more around our common goals. Figure out what problems we need to solve and sitting down, solving them together as partners.
