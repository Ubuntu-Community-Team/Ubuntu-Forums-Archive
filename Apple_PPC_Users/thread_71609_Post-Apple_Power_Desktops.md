### Post by joshuapurcell on 2005-10-03
I never made it too much of a point to look into Power-based architecture in the past, but after Apple's announcement that they were switching to the x86 architecture I wanted to find out what the reason for this was. I have also had the chance recently to work with some different models of IBM PPC servers running Linux, and I needed to know a little about what was going on under the hood in order to get Linux installed/configured as well as work with the different hardware. I've been very impressed with what I've found so far about these types of systems.
What I've found out in the last couple of weeks has made me wish that I had the option to get one of the newest and upcoming Power-based processors in a computer that I could mess around with at my house. I'm not talking about the exact processors that are running in all the fastest supercomputers or the ones that are in the Mars rover, but just something that has the same architecture. If the architecture is good enough to warrant the most powerful computers in the world, then I figure they should be good enough for my home computer. I also buyin to the design philosophy behind these chips: they are made to do the most common processes the fastest while leaving the rest up to the software developer.
But why is this move to Power architecture not an easy one (other than Apple discontinuing its use of the processors)? Mainly because most software (namely Microsoft software) is x86-based, and it can't (or won't) be easily recompiled to run on PPC systems. Of course, Linux has no problem recompiling to run on this or other architectures (and neither does a majority of it's associated software). I just wish there was hardware readily accessible to individuals rather than just companies (or individuals with money).
If Apple wouldn't have moved away from this architecture for it's future product line then it would be an easy decision when I choose my next computer. Most of what I can gather from reading various takes on the subject is that Apple made this architecture switch based purely on design principles: Jobs wanted his computers to put more emphasis on [innovative design](http://www.macworld.com/news/2005/09/15/intelvsamd/index.php) while IBM refused to put that principle above quality of the chip. To translate, in order to make his future computers look like an ornamental vase, he needed a chip that used the lowest possible voltage while putting off the lowest amount of heat. While I understand that looks could play a part in choosing a home computer, I definately don't put that above performance.
So I wonder what my options are in getting a Power-based system? I've read about all the latest gaming consoles having this type of architecture, and the Playstation 3's Cell processor is the single biggest reason why there's no question which console I'll be getting. I just wish I had a fully-functional computer (rather than a console) with the same specs to go along with it. I doubt I would be able to by a used system as my next main computer, but I would end up doing that for a hobby system if that's my only choice. Has anyone heard of any future systems that will be using some of the future Power processors and at the same time plan to target the general public?
