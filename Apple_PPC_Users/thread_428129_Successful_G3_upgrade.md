### Post by tgalati4 on 2007-04-30
Sounds like a CRT problem.  You can search the web for a service manual.  There may be a tweak to get the display back in shape.  The display is made by LG (perhaps the entire computer as well).  As components age, the CRT control voltages drift and the image will tilt and expand and color shift.  Some of the adjustments are pots on the back of the CRT gun.  Others are digital tuning settings.

With some research, you can probably find the correct tuning procedures.  Whatever you do, mark your original settings and don't short anything with the case removed.  Those displays operate at higher than normal voltages.  They look great when they are in tune, but they also get out of tune more quickly than a standard CRT monitor.

To correct the tilt, you normally loosen the yoke (the large thumbscrew on the thin part of the glass gun) and rotate it a few degrees and then test it.  It's good to short the flyback with a screwdriver under the rubber cap to reduce a shock hazard.  Or let it sit unplugged for a day before making any adjustments.

Good luck.

### Post by ssam on 2007-04-30
before you open up you iMac

**DANGER DANGER HIGH VOLTAGE**

> 
CRTs operate at very high voltages, which can persist long after the device containing the CRT has been switched off and/or unplugged, sometimes for years. Residual charges of hundreds of volts can also remain in large capacitors in the power supply circuits of the device containing the CRT; these charges may persist. Modern circuits contain bleeder resistors, to ensure that the high-voltage supply is discharged to safe levels within a couple of minutes at most. These discharge devices can fail even on a modern unit and leave these high voltage charges present.

from [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube)

### Post by tgalati4 on 2007-04-30
No doubt that the warnings are there for a reason.  But these are relatively easy problems to fix:  rotating the yoke  to fix tilt, and adjusting a few pots to control screen width.

Most modern CRT monitors have external controls that the user can adjust.  The iMac has everything buttoned up tight.  Even case dissassembly is a major chore.  You can take it to a mac repair place.  It will probably cost $200.  More than what the unit is worth.
