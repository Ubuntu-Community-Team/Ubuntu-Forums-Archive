### Post by bodycoach2 on 2008-02-14
I have used all the current popular graphical systems: Windows, OSX, various Linux distros, Solaris and other Unix incarnations -yes, I know OSX is Unix/FreeBSD.

All the different systems have their pluses, minus, quirks, and things to love. The most consistent quirk of all the systems is how they handle USB drives. Windows want's you to click on a mysterious little icon in the task bar and 'safely remove hardware'. Mac wants you to drag the USB icon to the trash, or right click and 'eject'. Ubuntu wants you to 'unmount', as though your USB stick was a gymnast. 

As far as security goes, ALL systems have vulnerabilities; Mac, Unix and LInux included. If you have something valuable enough to get, a cracker will get it, no matter what system you're on. 

If I, or our Free Geek Central Florida organization, were to receive a Mac with OSX on it, I'd probably keep using it till it was too slow, then put a suitable Linux distro on it.

But, if I received a brand new laptop, I would [Dban]("http://dban.org") the hard drive and install Ubuntu on it. As far as usability, I like Windows. As far as security and business practices, I use it only when necessary, and feel pangs of guilt and regret when I do. I'm starting to feel that way about using Mac OSX now too. 

When you use Windows or OSX, you're 'borrowing' the system with a license. If you read the EULA's, they can take back the OS anytime they want. They can even turn if off remotely, if they so desire. 

 When you use Linux, it's yours.

### Post by bodycoach2 on 2008-02-15
I'd have to agree with this. Most people just want to get specific tasks done: writing, surfing, accounting, pic or video editing, etc. They OS isn't really important. The programs are important. 

I think too many people involved with Open Source tend to focus too much on the OS, and not on the applications. In the past, that was necessary to get the OS to work on hardware, but Linux is now able to work on more hardware than Windows or OSX combined. 

I'm glad to see focus going to specific apps: OpenOffice.org, GIMP, F-Spot, etc. Much more emphasis needs to be placed on those areas if they are ever going to be competitive with closed source applications. I'd prefer to use an open source application, even if I have to pay for it. But most of all, I want my applications to work well.

The Mac application developers have focused on content creation, and are leading the pack. In the very near future, education will depend greatly on content infrastructure, development, creation, and delivery. More focus needs to go on Open Source applications in that area.
