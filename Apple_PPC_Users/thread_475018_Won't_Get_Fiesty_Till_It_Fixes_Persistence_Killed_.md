### Post by jimwg on 2007-06-15
Greetings:

One drawback to being a techie is that you usually just don't understand why newbies "don't get" it or don't have the common sense in tech matters that's second nature for you. It's almost a quiet arrogence and the reason you need skilled PR pros translating tech issues between techies and newbies.

After a successful run of installing Edgy persistence on my flash drive, I decided to test it out with Fiesty Fawn. There were NO warnings about doing this on the Fiesty download pages as there are buried deep assorted Ubuntu pages. I did the live persistent thing and Feisty Fawn popped up in default live-CD mode, as though my previous Edgy prefs weren't even there. I rebooted with Edgy and ditto. My flash drive's Edgy persistent files were wiped out. Now I'm hearing on other forums similiar tales of woe -- and it'll get worst as more Edgy fans with persistence files try to upgrade to Fiesty. Yet Ubuntu took no measure to simply type a few lines of warning on the download page.

Look, I'm a Ubuntu supporter. I don't like to dump on anyone. I'm willing to accept that software can do bad things, especially cutting edge software. I wouldn't even blame the programmers (in fact I admire them). But for Ubuntu customer/user service folks to've heard and known that Feisty corrupts flash drives loaded with Edgy persistence files -- entailing hours of work in setting up Ubunti and mail and Firefox settings and passwords and new files and loaded programs now in smoke -- is nothing short of neglect for the user. Worst, the newbie whom you're trying to bring into the fold. There should've been clear warnings of this right on the download page, not buried on page 36 somewhere.

This isn't a quibble. When you read of many interested in trying out Ubuntu but are put-off by it some way or another, a bad experience creates another unfavorable opnion and rumor. You can head these off at the pass by acknowledging the problem then taking measures that limit anymore user and PR damage. Successful customer service or user relations always assumes the customer or user knows squat about a product and your job's to enlighten them. As a product Ubuntu is no different, and it behooves it to take heed when users keep mentioning a common malady.

This isn't an ego battle. Let's do the right user friendly thing, Ubuntu.

James Greenidge
