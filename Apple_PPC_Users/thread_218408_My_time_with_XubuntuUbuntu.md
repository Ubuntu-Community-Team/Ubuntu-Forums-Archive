### Post by Jagot on 2006-07-18
I've messed around with Fedora Core, Debian and SuSE, along with Ubuntu of course.

To be honest out of all of them Ubuntu has the most user-friendly package management system.  Along with Debian it uses Synaptic along with apt-get/aptitude to download from repositories.  Also, Canonical are making headway with stuff like the GDebi .deb installer.

There are other supposedly user-friendly distro's, the most well-known example I think must be Linspire.  Mepis is also supposed to be pretty good but I've never used them.

In terms of finding software, in terms of Ubuntu of course it's performed most easily with Synaptic.  If you're talking about in general then sites like [http://freshmeat.net/](http://freshmeat.net/) help.

I can't give a difinitive answer about whether you can install any app on and Linux system, but I should imagine the answer to be yes with various levels of fiddling, even to altering source code.

On PPC that's probably correct - my iMac only listed one resolution. On x86, I've got the official ATI drivers installed and I can change my resolution using the Screen Resolution control panel. PPC is the ugly-duckling of the computing world, unfortunately.

> **MrFrodo said:**
> And I spent an hour trying to download and install Opera browser. Why can't I just download an installer, run it, and be done?! Or at least just download a .deb file, double-click it and have the OS know what to do - and just do it?

Some programs have self-installers. Alien Arena, Google Earth, and Songbird 0.2 have those kinds of Windowsy-installers. Xfce even has a GUI self-compiler!

Because Ubuntu is so popular, there are now a bunch more .deb packages available than there used to be, and some sites have set up their own 3rd-party repositories. On Dapper, you can double-click a Deb and it will install; as you've probably realised, you downloaded the x86 one by mistake.

You can also convert RPMs to Debs.

But remember, PowerPC Linux is a fraction as popular as x86 Linux, so there are very few pre-built binaries around. Best to stick to the repositories as much as you can - you are using the repositories, right? (isn't Opera in the Dapper Commercial repository for PPC?)

The reason why self-installers are uncommon on Linux is probably due less to technical reasons than to the wishes of Linux users - I'd rather administer my programs through Apt than through a dozen independant systems, and I know I'm in the majority on that one!
