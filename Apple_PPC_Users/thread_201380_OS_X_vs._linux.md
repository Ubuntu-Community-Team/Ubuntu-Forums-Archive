### Post by wagerrard on 2006-11-02
System76 makes nice looking hardware, but the equivalent Apple product is often cheaper. E.g., to bump their Koala model up to the specs of the $799 Mac Mini you need to push the price above $900.

On the OSX-Linux comparison:  I've run both for years. Putting aside ideological concerns, OSX and third-party software is typically more polished, more focused on ergonomics and the user, and, in many cases, more innovative. (There's some great outlining/writing/personal data management software for the Mac that seems to  be absent entirely from Linux.)

As has been pointed out here, if you depend on specific software capabilities for your income, you should select the best software and then buy hardware it runs best on. You clients won't care about the wonders of open source if you miss their deadline.

Tools like Synaptic give a slight edge to Ubuntu in the software intall/update category.  But, it's also braindead simple on OSX: Drag an icon into an open folder. Updating an app is done the same. (The use of an "Applications" folder and the bundling of app binaries with their supporting paraphenalia is one of the architectural things that sets OSX apart from Linux and the BSD's.)

As has also been mentioned, Apple's Auto Update feature has workded flawlessly for me.  It's configurable so you can do upgrades when you wish. 

Now, on rare occasions I've seen Mac lock up, killing the mouse and keyboard.  I've seen it on Linux, too. Failing that, there are at least two different ways to kill an app from the GUI, and you can always open a shell and do a "kill...".

On the other side, Linux has always been faster and snappier on any given piece of hardware for me. Distributions like Ubuntu and Fedora have progressed to the point that ordinary mortals can use and maintain them happily.  A couple o years ago that wasn't the case.

Finally, the aesthetic thing is a matter of personal taste and how much you want  to spend to indulge you own.
