### Post by ntu_user on 2005-04-14
I got ubuntu installed and running nicely on a recent iBook G4. I managed to fix the sleep problem as well as the USB issues related to sleep (don't ask me how, I spent hours with google and ubuntu wiki and what not, I just don't remember the steps). The sound is still broken.
But I wanted to make a few points regarding this distro. I've been running Linux on both i386 (Slackware is my choice) and ppc (Debian and YDL) probably since the early versions of 2.x kernel so I have some experience both with linux and the crowd around it.
Ubuntu is generally fine as a distro but the support on ppc is limited at best. I see issues on this list encountered by lots of users and have yet to see/hear an ubuntu rep at least stating that they are looking into it and/or provide a solution. Without google linux would be dead. 
There is no FAQ for ppc or I couldn't find it and the wiki is, well a wiki, one can spend days trying to find some answers.
The way the forums are organized can also drive one crazy, for one new distro they came up with a whole page of forums with divisions and subdivisions.
Basically what I fail to understand is how much longer is the linux community (open or not) going to continue this game with their users of providing only half solutions. I guess as long as people are stupid enough or curious enough to listen them. Specially on ppc where OS X shines from most points of view, isn't there a sense of shame to consider Linux as an alternative? 
Yes, linux came a long way but because of that, the expectations are higher. Will anyone deliver?
Another issue I have is with the enormous quantity of software provided; with few exceptions, almost all apps are available is a number of versions. I agree with the posibility of choice but considering that each application SHOULD BE tested for proper functionality before making it available as part of the distro, I think everyone would be better served by a more limited but fully functional choice.
Nevertheless, I hope things will improve.

### Post by Rxke on 2005-04-15
You make some very valid points here.

But first of all, I've got to say Ubuntu is sparkling new, so give it some slack. (Ok, I'm very bad at puns, being non-English speaking...)

First, the way the forums are set up... Indeed it's awkward at best, surely from the point of view of a PPC user.
For instance: I've seen people posting about their laptop problems in the laptop section, but that section is in fact for non-ppc platforms, so they do not get an answer... Confusing if you're new  to Linux, or only have a vague understanding about differences in architectures.

Second, I think there should be some kind of newbie section, Ubuntu wants to be the Linux for everybody, and right now a lot of new people keep asking the same stuff over and over again (hey, I'm probably guilty too...) in the same places where other people ask 'tough' questions. For now the attitude is still one of friendliness, but i've seen the first instances of the dreaded Debian RTFM! syndrome popping up... IMO, that's a pity. 

Ubuntu has *a lot* going for it, it could become huge if some people (mods?) dedicated themselves to lead the hordes of new users gently upslope, WITHOUT those new users interfering with the more dyed in the wool crowds, that are now squishing bugs etc...

Third, and for us PPC users, most important: feedback from the developers re: PPC issues. Sometimes I cannot shake off the feeling 'we' are left on our own too much.
At the very least there could be a sticky with known PPC issues, regularly updated, so people don't have to go searching in the strange land of bugtracker et all. (I'm thinking about people fairly new to Linux here, you just cannot expect this from them, you can not.)

I shortly dabbled with Debian, when I was running OS9, and I was very impressed, except that it was a time of sleepless night spent at wikis, google, messageboards,... just to get, for instance,  DHCP up and running in a network, so eventually i got off again, too much 'real' work to do.

Then came Ubuntu, seemed like a dream come true, it's nothing but spectacular for a semi-computer-literate, but there are still ways to improve it.

Oh, and finally: you complain about the plethora of software packages... I think it's a rather good choice, Mr. Shuttleworth wants to cater for everyone, so I think it's good to have the choice. I clearly recall the frustrating search for affordable apps in OS9, being against piracy, because people just copy the big shots like photoshop, word etc... i think that's not a good thing, because it's false economy... Other good software does not get a chance this way, and when people eventually get a 'real' job, they of course end up buying the stuff they used in the past. Around me I see students using software, if bought legally, would've cost them thousands of Euro's, without giving it even a second thought... Professors the same. While there are other free packages around, sometimes not that good, but at least... That way there should be a drive towards improvement, not features nobody uses.

Uh-oh... this is becoming a rant. End Of File  ;-)
