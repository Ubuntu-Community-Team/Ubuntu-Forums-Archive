### Post by arbulus on 2007-06-06
I own an iMac G5 PPC.  I've been a big fan of Macs for qute a long time, and over the years have always felt like the PPCs have been superior to the x86 architecture.  But times have changed greatly.  Apple, the biggest producer and seller of PPC machines no longer sells them.  And something I've noticed since their switch from PPC to Intel is the sharp decline in support for the PPC platform.  It seems like the PPC is reaching the end much more quikly than any previous technology has.

My first experience with Linux was learning how to set up a dual boot on my iMac with Ubuntu.  It was a nerver wracking few days, but I was really happy that I did.  Sadly, however, that joy changed.  As I started to use Ubuntu more and more, I realized how little support for the PPC the Linux world has.  For example: no 3D accelerated video drivers (and an explict declaration that neither nVidia nor ATI would ever create them), no Adobe support for anything (Flash is the biggie.  The web is nearly useless without Flash), just spend some time in Add/Remove Programs and you'll see just how many things say that they aren't available for the PPC.

And this trend isn't just in the Linux world, it's in the OS X world as well.  Look around for third party apps for your Mac and you find that people are moving away from supporting the PPC at exponential rates.  They're not even building apps as Universal Binaries, only supporting Intel machines.

The move away from the PPC is getting faster and faster, and it's seriously depressing.  Thankfully I picked up an older HP box to install Ubuntu on.  Otherwise, I probably wouldn't be using Ubuntu anymore.  I haven't booted my iMac into the Ubuntu partition in a month or so.  It's a waste of time.  PPC is no longer a platform that can ever be considered a viable desktop Linux realm.  And with support for PPC fading so fast, it will only get worse.  I really lament that.

The thing that really bothers me is that there are still PPC users all over the world. The day before Apple dropped PPCs, thousands of people purchased brand new ones. A day later, those machines were immediately obsoleted.  The industry basically forgot that there are still folk everwhere who use their PPC machines everyday, and those people who bought their brand new machines a day too early got royally screwed.  I just don't understand the mindset that drives the industry regarding this platform. PPCs didn't disappear because Apple stopped selling them.  There's so many still out there and desparately in need of software to run, but they've been forgotten.

Anyway, I just wanted to rant a bit, and perhaps spur a little discussion on the issue.

### Post by johng4 on 2007-06-06
I stated this in a previous thread, but I'll post it here as well..

PowerPC is still in big time use.  Examples?

PlayStation3, XBOX 360, and I believe the Wii... but don't quote me on the Wii.
Cellphones, PDAs, and numerous other embedded enviroments.. mostly PPC.

PPC is not dying so much as its in transition.  IBM's new Power7 line of PPC CPUs is one of the most mind boggling superior single core CPU designs out there.  Minimum speeds on these will be in the 4-5GHz range, and I've read they scale up in the 7GHz range.

x86 CPUs will have to wait until the overall architecture catches up with the speed capabilities before we see another great shift in speed.  PowerPC needs a new vendor, and a new market. PPC is the beta of computer architecture... and beta never went away.  Most movies you watch, and almost all TV shows you watch are shot in Beta.  Give it time.

### Post by thedarky on 2007-06-07
The OP has made points that jibe with my experience in the past week.

Having a fixed income and so a fixed budget, I researched how to get internet connected at home for the absolute basics of email, news browsing and streaming public radio - - not even interested much in whether I can download video/multimedia.
It came down to Ubuntu-plus-second-hand-puter.
I was advised that although apple has stopped supporting "Gx" processor puters, their life is highly extendable through the very active Linux community that is producing bundles for us beginners to get basic networking services up and running.  The consensus was that linux works well on hardware designed to run unix derived systems.   I found the price of second-hand Macs made them as good a prospect as an intel puter with a fair video card, so I got an eMac G4 which is not so old and which didn't come with any operating system (ex school license stock).

I regret it now.
After a week, I've found that the core of my net use, RealPlayer, is most definitely not supported, either by ubuntu officially for the ppc or by the community.  Real advise (I emailed them) that there is definitely support for linux but that ppc support is a secondary item for development when they get time.   There are very many blind alleys here in the support community to chase up, all saying their recipe is the right one for ppc Realplayer installation.
Well, after I stumbled into gear with how to chase up manual installation skills (a very short sharp lesson in how not to try to use a new language is when a new user is fed cli code in web pages with a blithe "just cut-and-paste this" instruction ;-)) I found that all of these how-to's are pretty much out of date for feisty and that Realplayer users will probably have to wait quite a while for it to be looked at by the community.
Because I found another aspect of the linux community that hobbles development - - the  attitude that if it's not open-source then it should be.
Fact is that Real has partnered with most of the big public radio broadcasters in the world for decades to produce a stable low-bandwidth streaming standard that these broadcasters use widely and that Real supports with free software (I shan't talk about the MS alternative because it's not relevant here).  I am aware of Real's reputation for bad practises in the past but they support free public radio.  What's not to like if they freely distribute their codecs in their own player?
Plugins, in general, for Firefox are either not supported for straightforward installation or just don't work - - Java I need for a couple of work sites.
Flash I can do without I thought, but some tell me that it is used for demonstrations online that often can't be duplicated otherwise.
Funny thing is that I learned that Flash and ppc (OS X, I think is involved here somehow too) didn't really get along very well at the best of times.  So no wonder it's not a popular item on the agenda for ubuntu.

Most certainly I understand that there is a positive approach here in the apple ubuntu community (the main reason that I landed on the decision to get a mac for ubuntu) and some good users have given good help, but the overall impression I get is that there are not enough basic numbers of ppc users in the community to constitute a stable core of basic knowledge maintenance for stuff that's outside the official distro.

No offence to anyone here, but it's too steep a learning curve for a complete new user and so I shall stay with the very nice official installation that I've managed to get going, use the networking for email and browsing, wait until I can sell the Mac and see what I can do with an intel second-hand.


I have an idea that people with OS X experience would have more resources to cope with the thin patches I've experienced.  I'll check Synaptic and Apt every week to see if something useful gets done for Realplayer and thank the community for letting me have my say.
I'd suggest that a sticky thread about what plugins definitely come in the "community" distribution that ubuntu.com makes available as a port - and what definitely doesn't work yet, sort of like a "Read here to avoid misunderstanding" thread for new users, would be very welcome.
I've donated by the way.

all the best :-)
