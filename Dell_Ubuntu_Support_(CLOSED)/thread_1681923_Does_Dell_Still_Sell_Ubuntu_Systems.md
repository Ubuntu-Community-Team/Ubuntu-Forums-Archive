### Post by gbrainin on 2011-02-05
I bought my Ubuntu Dell back in '07 when they first came out.

Hardware Support: As good or better than Dell's hardware support in general, _provided_ you call the Ubuntu support line, _not_ the general support line.  Telling the general support people that you don't have a "start button" breaks their scripts.

Software Support: None promised, some minimally given in the process of diagnosing a hardware error (again, the Ubuntu support line).  For personal use, I have had no reason to use official support.  Between this forum and the internet in general, most questions are fairly easily answered.

Note that I have heard that the Dell Ubuntu support line has changed since I last used it; in particular, I understand it is no longer physically in North America.

Regarding hardware compatibility and drivers, these things don't work the same way that they do under Windows.  That said, it is not my experience that all the drivers are pre-loaded; in particular, many video chipsets have different driver versions, an open-source one and a closed-source one, and you need to take action to get the latter.  The action is not particularly complicated; as I recall, it's a window that pops up during your first session to ask if you want to load the other driver.

Similarly, various closed-source items you may wish to use (Microsoft fonts, flash animation, etc.) require installing the associated software.  This does not require deep programming knowledge, rather selecting them from a menu and clicking the "apply" button.

There is a little bit of a learning curve in switching from Windows to Linux (same as with any other switch, say to Mac), but the curve keeps getting gentler as time goes on.  And I think most people here would say that it's worth the effort (but of course we're self-selected to say that).

### Post by gbrainin on 2011-02-12
Windows-only software is generally the hurdle for anyone considering any alternate operating system, but it's rarely an insurmountable one.

The printer/scanner is most likely the least of your problems.  If you're concerned, you can try downloading Ubuntu and running it from a live CD/USB to see if it can print/scan.  Note that the live CD/USB environment is a lot slower than running from the hard drive.

There are plenty of scanning programs in Ubuntu.  I'm not aware of one that types forms, but there may be something like that.  Alternately, I'm sure you can simply insert text over the scanned image and print that.

PowerChurch I never heard of, but don't be surprised if it works under WINE or in a VM.  Again, Ubuntu and WINE can be test-driven without cost, so try it and see.

As for QuickBooks, I have not installed it on a VM myself, but I think it's likely that it's workable.  VMs in general require a bit of tweaking, I'm told, which means you must understand how the VM works in order to tweak it.  That, I assume, is what Intuit doesn't want to "support," and while I can't blame them for not wanting to deal with someone else's software, it also creates an additional (and often unnecessary) barrier to leaving Windows.
