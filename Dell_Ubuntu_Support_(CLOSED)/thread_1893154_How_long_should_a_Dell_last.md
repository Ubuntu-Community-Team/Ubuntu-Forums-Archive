These problems are always a little hard to troubleshoot remotely, but it looks to me as if the problem is either that your machine's setup options are incorrectly trying to boot from the wrong or device, hard-drive or CD, or else the hard-drive is corrupted. 

Try this: Power-on your machine and hit the F2 key (or DEL key, it's either F2 or DEL) to get into the machine's Setup menus. Once in Setup look to for the option to load the machine's default values. After loading the defaults try rebooting your to your hard-drive while no CD is installed. If that fails try installing your bootable CD and rebooting.

My guess is that your hard-drive has been corrupted. If after running Setup and loading the default options you can boot a known good installer CD, you could try re-installing Linux.

If your hard-drive is corrupted and CANNOT be reformatted and Linux re-installed, you might need to replace the hard-drive. But based on the messages you're getting I bet the rest of your machine's hardware is ok. 

So best case: Run Setup and reload the machine's default settings.
Second case:  Reformat the drive and re-install Linux.
Worst case:   Replace the hard drive, reformat and re-install.

### Post by xbxb on 2011-12-10
> **Janeleaper said:**
> Thanks for that.  

Set to defaults, the machine wants to boot from a floppy disc (there is no floppy drive). I've tried re-setting the boot priority to cdrom, but it is ignoring that and still looking for a floppy.

So, I guess it needs a new hard drive.  Can you advise me how I go about this?  Sorry to be so ignorant.  I know how to install Ubuntu on top of an existing system, but not raw, so to speak.

You shouldn't yet assume it is a broken hard-drive. Based on your original post, it looks like your machine tried to boot to the hard-drive, found a valid boot sector, etc, but then found a partially corrupted O.S. AFTER it started booting, i.e. the drive is mechanically sound but needs to be reformatted and the O.S. restored. 

In Setup you might be able to set the floppy drive to 'Disabled' to get it out of the way. Then with Setup's boot priority set to CDROM, and a known good _BOOTABLE_ CD such as your original Ubuntu installation CD (not all CD's are _bootable_), try booting the CD. If the Ubuntu CD boots try re-installing Ubuntu to the hard-drive. If the re-installation fails, then at that point would I begin thinking it might be a broken hard-drive.

For a general troubleshooting method: disconnect all peripheral devices (when power is off), then reconnect only one boot device at a time for testing. For each device, power on, go into Setup and load the correct options for that particular connected device and reboot. If using this method any device ever boots then that would indicate a particular corrupted or broken peripheral. If NO boot device will ever boot then the problem is more likely to be a motherboard/power-supply issue and replacing a particular peripheral (such as your hard-drive) won't fix it.

By the way: Even if all cards and connectors seem firmly seated, one of them might have, over time and use, developed a poor electrical connection. You should RE-SEAT everything. Unplug/disconnect then re-plug/reconnect everything.

### Post by Malkosha on 2012-05-11
We've been using Dells at my work place for decades. They seem to hold up rather well and we have some that are VERY old. Their support is far better than HP's, IMO, and if your buying a laptop, support is the one thing you should never scrimp on.

PC's require maintenance just like a car ... so to speak. Heat is the killer here and you must clean the dust from time to time. Also, as the PC ages, keep an eye on your CPU temps. After so long, you will have to replace the CPU thermal grease to keep things cool. Check for busted caps and if you find any ... even if your PC is running fine ... plan for a new one.

Caps would burst turning the MB into junk. Since then things have gotten far better and their lifespans have increased.

Dollar for dollar, its always better to build your own PC. This gives you total upgrade potential at a much cheaper cost than using a proprietary machine from HP or Dell, which limits your upgrades and eventually forces you to buy a new machine.
