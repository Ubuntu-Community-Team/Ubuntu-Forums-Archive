### Post by ugm6hr on 2009-01-31
I do not see why this could not be successful, but the "confusion" factor is an issue.

For example, the story of the student who bought an Ubuntu Dell *by accident* and then couldn't bet online, or use .doc files or complete her University degree rings alarm bells.

I have myself donated / sold a few older machines with Xubuntu on.  While most caused me no problem, I did sell a 8-year-old (Dell as it happens) laptop (Inspiron 5000e from memory) last year which was a less than pleasant experience.  Despite a very clear "Windows not included" and "Xubuntu Linux Operating System" in the advert, and demonstrating this in person, and having a discussion why I would not be installing Windows on it, I received an angry email and phonecall the next day asking for his money back on the basis that "You can't sell a computer without Windows".

Just be aware that as a private seller, it was not too much of an issue for me.  However, as an independent store, your customer relations are your biggest draw for future customers, and a single "bad" experience will be spread to 10 other potential future customers, which may impact on your main income stream from repairs etc.

Just something to consider: be prepared to support your customers for free for at least a month if you do this, and perhaps give refunds for those people who genuinely don't understand.

### Post by MatthewMetzger on 2009-03-09
Thanks for the questions, yther. You guessed right. 

I do only carry a few models that I've chosen specifically because I believe they are high quality. I carry the Dell Optiplex line (mostly GX260, GX270, GX280) and the Latitude line (D420, D620, D820). I try to keep to just those six models. I'll try out some of the newer models as they become affordable to purchase used.

I set up a computer exactly how I want it (with extra software like gnucash and inkscape), then make an image of it using CloneZilla. My CloneZilla images are on an ssh server and it takes under fifteen minutes to set up a new system (if there are no hardware problems). They install over a local network connection. I have separate images for the several of the different models, but the Linux kernel is great in that it will boot up on most anything, even if it was installed on very different hardware.

I want to create an open documentation project around the processes and business concepts that I use in my shop. Similar operations could be opened in every city with a population over 30,000.

A computer repair shop has to work on Windows computers, of course. But I'm doing just fine with only selling computers with Ubuntu (I do offer Windows, if someone requests it, but I haven't sold a computer with Windows, yet).
