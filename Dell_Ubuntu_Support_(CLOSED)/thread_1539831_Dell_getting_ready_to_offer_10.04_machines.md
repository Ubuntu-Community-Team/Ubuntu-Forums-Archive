Regarding your first point, I'm sure Dell does leverage other people's work. At the same time, I believe they were instrumental in getting better wireless drivers for Broadcom chips, which used to be a real pain to work with. So they contribute as well. Since they need hardware that works with Linux to sell Linux computers, this also gives hardware manufacturers to sell Linux compatible hardware in order to get Dell's business. 

Dell also provides Fluendo multimedia codecs on their computers. In the United States, this is the only legal way I know to be able to watch movies and listen to MP3s. This is great. I think a number of small businesses and other companies interested in having these codecs might be hesitant to buy Ubuntu computers without them. Shortly after they provided the codecs, I noticed Ubuntu started offering them for download on their store. I'm not in expert in everything the Dell/Canonical team has done but I believe these are two positive examples reflecting their accomplishments. I'm sure there are more.

Regarding your second point, as for your computer, I profess ignorance. I know that the initial Ubuntu computers Dell sold weren't perfect. One of the writers on Ars Technica wrote a review on a 14" Inspiron laptop awhile back when it was first offered as a testament to this fact. I don't think the argument should be about whether these Ubuntu computers work perfectly or not. Instead, I think the argument should be about how well these computers work. The fewer showstopper and the fewer minor flaws on Dell's Ubuntu computers, the larger an audience their computers will have.

For example, I might be interested in getting an Ubuntu desktop for my parents in a couple of years if the price was reasonable enough and there wouldn't be too much further tweaking needed. It wouldn't need to be perfect, and given Ubuntu's release schedule, I'm sure it's not easy for Dell to get a system with modern hardware working perfect. It would just need to be good enough for me to be interested.
