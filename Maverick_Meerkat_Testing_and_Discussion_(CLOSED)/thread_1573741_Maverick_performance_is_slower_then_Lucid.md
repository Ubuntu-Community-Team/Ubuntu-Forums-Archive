### Post by recluce on 2010-09-14
> **cariboo907 said:**
> How much power saving is there when running with ondemand, and if the cores are at idle or slightly above, how does that decrease cpu life? I have systems here that have been running at fullspeed for over ten years, so that shoots that argument in the foot.

A ten year old CPU had only basic speed stepping, if any. So that wouldn't proof anything, even if it weren't purely anecdotal.

> 
I can see running ondemand on a laptop, to save battery usage, but on a desktop, unless the rest of the system is setup to save power as well, it's a wasted effort.

If you are using a modern CPU (core i5 or i7 quad, for example) you will see the following benefits in **not locking** your CPUs into the highest speed step:

- the CPU has a thermal budget. Within that budget, it will automatically overclock on one or two cores, if the other cores are idle or at low loads. Boost speeds can add up to a full GHz on some CPUs. If the CPU runs at full load all the time, it will have no thermal budget to speak of. So you are actually loosing performance by not using dynamic speed stepping.

- Noise on desktop systems: unless you have passive cooling, more heat from the CPU means more noise from the fans.

- More heat in your room - great for me in winter (Canada), not so great in hot climates in the summer

- Idle desktop CPUs use less than 20 Watt power, a core i7 desktop CPU has a thermal design power of 80 to 130 Watts! Locking your CPU at highest clock rate will not reach that TDP, but get you close to it. Let's say you have one of the higher speed models with 130W TDP, than running it at highest clock rate should get you to at least 75% TDP - that is around 100W. Compared to 20W at idle, you are wasting 80W - or almost 2kWh in 24 hours or 730 kWh in a year. Granted, your system will not always be idle (but most of the time) and it may not be on all the time. Still, you can see the environmental and financial incentive in not running locked high

- If you need to consider an airconditioned room full of servers, these numbers multiply to a tidy amount of money.

Side notes: in theory, producing less heat will prolong CPU life. In practice, CPU failures are extremely rare, unless the cooling system is completely inadequate or suffers catastrophic failure. You will likely see the CPU go obsolete first. Desktop graphics cards have power management, too - limiting power consumption on CPU and GPU during low load situation will take care of the lion's share of your systems power consumption.

Bottom line: if CPU scaling should indeed not work correctly in Maverick, I would consider it not only a bug, but a **_show stopper!_**
