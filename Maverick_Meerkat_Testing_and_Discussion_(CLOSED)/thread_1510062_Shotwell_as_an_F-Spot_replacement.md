### Post by Mr. Picklesworth on 2010-07-05
> **ranch hand said:**

Actually, none of those. Did you try my Firefox example?

I just agree with choosing the right tool for a given job, and I think file management philosophy comes into play there, too. Operating systems are all about abstraction, and the file system is just one of those abstractions. The file manager, with its move and copy and rename commands, is another abstraction on top of that.
(The really dumb thing is that it's hard / pointless to use more than one file manager at once, even though there are lots of choices out there with their own individual benefits).

Think of the file system as paper. To me, your approach sounds like people should only have one kind of paper: blank paper. They should draw the lines on it themselves because it's Really Important (it is!) that everyone knows paper comes from trees without even that white colour. In addition, one can *potentially* use a sheet of blank paper for anything; grid paper has way fewer options.
On the other hand, I think people should be able to get paper with lines right out of the store. I like to pick the right paper for the job I'm doing and get on with it with the added helpfulness of that paper's special properties. I recognize that it means I need more kids of paper around, but the benefits seem to outweigh the negatives. (If they didn't, lined paper wouldn't be so popular).

It's about having the right guides available, making a given job (such as finding an application) easier. In the end we're still dealing with files, but the tool is making use of all that colourful metadata we have available in modern operating systems.

Files are great. Everything runs on files. They are powerful things.
So are transistors.
